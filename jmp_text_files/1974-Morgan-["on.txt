MORGAN'  Mathematical Institute, University of Kent, Canterbury, Kent, England  This paper is in two parts:  (1) A review of tests of Luce's choice axiom reveals a surprising lack of direct statistical tests. A simple statistical test  of the choice axiom is proposed and applied to two sets of data. Clarke's data, which originally suggested the axiom,  demonstrates a statistically significant departure from that axiom, though it must also be mentioned that Clarke's data was  pooled over individual subjects.  (2) A long-standing objection to the choice axiom is that it is unsuitable when choice is multistage. A number of propositions  are therefore given (and tested statistically) for a two-stage mechanism of choice, and the choice axiom adapted to such a  two-stage situation.  INTRODUCTION  Few psychologists and behaviorists do not, implicitly or explicitly, use Luce's Choice Axiom (CA) at some stage in their work.  The CA (see Luce, 1959; we shall henceforth denote this reference by: L) is also known to psychologists as Clarke's Constant  Ratio Rule, as it was independently proposed by Clarke (1957) as a means of predicting the acoustic confusions between letters  when only a fraction of the alphabet is being used as the stimulus set, and when the confusions between the letters, when the  entire alphabet was the stimulus set, are known. In such a case the choice is the selection of a response to the stimulus.  The CA is simply a formalization of a "law of irrelevant alternatives": it states that our preferences between specific  objects do not change when other objects are added to, or subtracted from, the overall set of objects. Luce states the CA as  follows (L, p. 6):  Let M be a set of objects, from a subset of which an object is going to be chosen. Naturally the CA is an attractive proposition as, employing it, we can reduce a multiple-choice problem to the far simpler  binary-choice problem; see, for example, Audley (1960) and Dawkins (1969a, 1969b). The CA is also implicit in many other  studies; see, for example, Hartigan (1968), Levelt, Van der Geer and Plomp (1966) and Morton (1969). The CA has attained a  venerable status over the years; for example, from Conrad (1964) we have "Clarke (1957) has amply justified the use of  deductions about smaller confusion matrices' made from a large one." Briefly, the CA  predicts CS from CM by deleting all the rows and columns of CM except those which also occur in CS, and renormalizing the  reduced matrix so that each row sums to one. Rather than make a direct test of the CA other researchers seem to have concentrated on testing predictions of the CA,  possibly following the lead of Luce (L, p. 19). Four out of ten subjects in Chipman's (1960) study, for example, were found to violate stochastic transitivity. If just one of the bicycles and the pony are  presented we choose the pony with probability 1; if all three are presented we choose the pony with probability a. Debreu's objection to the CA was also made by L. J. Savage in correspondence with Luce (see Luce and Suppes, 1965, pp.  334-335). Luce himself, however, was well aware of this objection (L, p. 132) and suggested that we cannot expect the CA to  hold for decisions that are divided in some way into two or more intermediate decisions.  Coombs (1958), Krantz (1967), Rumelhart and Greeno (1971) and Tversky and Russo (1969) are other writers who have examined the  CA through its predictions. Debreu's objection to the CA is that it ignores the effect of any possible similarity between the  elements being chosen and these studies examine the CA by means of specially designed experiments with "similar" elements. For  Krantz (1967) "choice" was the selection of one of a pair of presented colors as being the more similar to a standard and he  tested the prediction of a ratio scale. Four subjects were used and eight chi-square tests of the CA made. Of these tests only  two were significant at the 5 % level and Krantz concluded, somewhat strongly, that, "The statistical significance of the  deviations of observed from predicted (by the CA) frequencies is beyond question." Finally, he also sums his test statistics  over subjects, which is a dubious procedure as it ignores likely dependencies in the within-subject results. The work of  Tversky and Russo (1969) is similar to that of Krantz and, although their experiments reject the CA, they also conclude that  the CA, "...may still hold for more homogenous sets of pair comparisons." Rumelhart and Greeno (1971) obtain data from  paired-comparison experiments with a set of twelve elements, which were the names of three politicians, three actresses and  three athletes, so that again an appreciable amount of element similarity is present. They show that, "...stimulus similarity  can influence choice behaviour to such an extent that some existing models (the CA) are incapable of handling stimulus  similarity." But they qualify their findings with the fact that they have pooled over subjects. Coombs' results are discussed  by Luce (L, pp. 19-20).  Of course a direct test is more powerful than a test of predictions and from Luce (L, p. 12) we have, "Although he (Clarke)  does not explore the implications of his assumption, he does present the only direct empirical test of the CA that has been so  far published... ." While in the words of Clarke (1957): "Unfortunately, a meaningful statistical test of the observed  discrepancies between obtained and predicted (by the CA) cell entries is not available."  In the following section we present a simple direct statistical test of the CA that does not suffer from the dependency defect  of Tversky's test, and we shall apply this test to Clarke's data and also to the data of Egan (1957). Since the appearance of  Luce's book there have been several direct experimental verifications of the CA, and we shall mention these in the next  section, but these verifications have not been in the form of statistical tests. Such a  decomposition of T was originally suggested to the author by the cluster analyses (see Morgan, 1973) of the acoustic  confusions of the letters A-Z of the alphabet investigated by Conrad (1964). Morgan found the strong clusters:  CQTPBGV UED,  Z H X S F, (Z given a British and not American pronunciation) IRYL0MNAJK,  with W remaining an outsider. Confusion was high within clusters and low between them; possibly, in confusing a letter a  subject first of all selects a cluster and then confuses within that cluster.  It was such a two-stage choice situation that Debreu (1960), Luce (L, pp. 131-133) and Tversky (1972) considered as unsuitable  for the application of the CA. ON LUCE'S CHOICE AXIOM 121  CONCLUSION  We have found, from tests on pooled data, that the CA, while apparently a good predictor of proportions, is by no means as  reliable as has been previously assumed. It is, however, not simple to propose alternatives to the CA, as first of all basic  propositions concerning the nature of choice have to be made and as we have seen in the previous section this may be a  difficult task.  Finally, it is instructive to note how much choice and confusion must depend on extraneous factors external to T, as well as  the particular subset of T under consideration.
