The analysis of the structure and dynamics of networks is now recognised as a significant and valuable enterprise in many distinct fields, including the psychological, biological, social and physical sciences. For example, networks have been proposed to represent such varied phenomena as interpersonal relations and interactions in social groups, interactions among proteins, gene regulation, neural networks, food webs and the topology of power grids.

Despite the variety of applications, it is often valuable to regard networks as abstract graph theoretical structures and hence take advantage of the fact that abstract mathematical representations facilitate the potential application of analyses developed and understood in one domain of application to another. Formal mathematical representations also assist in analysing the extent to which cross-application is warranted.

Many such representations are algebraic in character as early applications of network analysis in the social and behavioural sciences illustrate. For example,  Luce (1956)  analysed the circumstances in which social relational structures give rise to mathematical groups;  White (1963)  characterised the kinship structures of Australian aboriginal structures as groups of permutations among kinship classes;  Boyd, Haehl, and Sailer (1972)  demonstrated how kinship structures in certain North American groups could be characterised as inverse semigroups; and  Boorman and White (1976)  and  Pattison and Bartlett (1982)  represented relational structures in more general terms as mathematical semigroups. In each of these examples, the constituent elements of the derived algebra include a designated set of binary relations describing relationships of interest in a group of individuals, for example, relationships of parenthood, marriage, or friendship. The application of relational composition to this set of binary relations yields a finite set of additional binary relations that represent the presence of indirect relations (such as friend of friend, or wife of son) among members of the group. The original and indirect relations, together with the relational composition operation, define a closed algebraic structure. Since relational composition corresponds to the tracing of labelled walks in a network, the algebraic structure generated by relational composition and an initial set of binary relations is a characterisation of the relationships among the labelled walks to which the set of binary relations gives rise ( Pattison, 1993 ).

In this paper, we extend this capacity to what we term here dynamic network structures, that is networks whose arcs or edges are not assumed to be universally present or absent but rather to exist over a finite number of closed time intervals. The purpose of this extension is to provide a framework within which the analysis of dynamic network structures may be set, and hence to assist in the construction of principled representations, measures and analyses for dynamic networks.

A valuable initial step in this direction was taken by  Moody (2002)  in his conceptualisation of a time-ordered path in a dynamic network. Moody defined a composition operation for dynamic networks in which each network tie was characterised by one or more time intervals over which the tie existed. Moody’s definition of composition and hence of network paths ensured that the endpoint of each link in the path did not precede the endpoint of the preceding link and thus that “flow” of some entity along the path was actually possible. More recently,  Holme and Saramäki (2011)  have reviewed the literature on dynamic networks and noted that the concept of a “time-respecting” path has emerged in a number of fields. We use this concept as a starting point for an algebraic representation of dynamic networks.

The paper is organised as follows. First, we review algebraic representations for static network structures, that is for network structures whose arcs and edges are assumed to be simply present or absent. We then introduce an algebra of temporal intervals, as an analogue of the Boolean algebra used to define operations in static networks, that can be used to both analyse the underlying dynamic network structures and build meaningful measures from them. We illustrate these constructions with an analysis of time-bound interactions in the form of mobile phone calls. We finish by sketching some developments enabled by these representations.

Efficient algorithms for computation of walk-based measures can be developed using graph-theoretic concepts ( Brandes, 2001; Harary, Norman, & Cartwright, 1965 ) or by taking the algebraic approach to path-finding problems ( Baras & Theodorakopoulos, 2010; Batagelj, 1994; Carré, 1971; Gondran & Minoux, 2008 ).

The algebraic representations described so far have entailed operations over networks in which ties take values from the set

The term “dynamic” here refers to the changing nature of social relations over time. This could refer to relations that are only current over finite intervals, or it could refer to discrete interactions or transactions. Interactions are played out sequentially, so that the order in which they occur needs to be considered (e.g. turn-taking in conversations,  Gibson, 2005 ). In particular, time order has implications for the way resources, attitudes, diseases, etc., might flow or diffuse through various kinds of temporal networks, and imposes a “time-respecting” ( Holme & Saramäki, 2011 ) constraint on network paths, and therefore on associated measures. As  Moody (2002)  clearly demonstrated, ignoring the time-order of relations results in a potential overestimate of reachability and hence impacts all other network measures that depend on reachability.

In what follows we explicitly represent temporal intervals over which social interactions are observed, and develop quite general interval algebras that allow consideration of the following features: the duration of the whole interval, the way in which intervals overlap, the duration of overlap and the length of time between disjoint intervals. In particular, we define two general operations for the composition of intervals into paths that are temporally conformable. Depending on the process we wish to model, we may choose to explicitly consider particular features in the composition operation. In this paper we consider two real world applications with distinct features: transmission of infectious diseases in which an individual may have several concurrent as well as sequential relationships; and potential transfer of information over one-to-one phone calls, in which we assume that the importance of information, and hence its transmission, decays over time. Some examples of an interval algebra for a different set of applications can be found in  Baras and Theodorakopoulos (2010)  and  Gondran and Minoux (2008) .

Insofar as the transmission occurs along a network path, the class of problems we consider can be described as network path problems ( Baras & Theodorakopoulos, 2010 ), in which paths are formulated in terms of weighted edges between two nodes. In our case, the edge “weights” are given by the interval or set of intervals over which direct interactions occur. Reachability in our context gives us an indication of potential flow from  
                         i 
                       to  
                         j 
                       along any path for which the aggregate weight of the path is a non-empty set of time intervals. For measures that depend on geodesic distance between the specified pair of nodes, we need to determine an appropriate definition of the length of a path in our set of problems. In calculating a temporal betweenness centrality measure, for example, we may either define shortest paths to be the ones with the least number of interconnecting nodes; or the ones that deliver the earliest possible transmission.

Any dynamic network which is of practical relevance has a finite number of nodes, and any cycle in this network contains a finite number of distinct nodes. Nevertheless, it is possible to construct cycles within cycles, and this raises the possibility of infinitely long cycles. We have shown that each cycle can only be traversed a finite number of times. In the process we have seen that at each transition from one node to the next, the resulting intervals are either translated by the  
                            δ 
                          that is added to their offset or they are constrained by smaller intervals with which they are composed. As the length of a walk increases so does the magnitude of the offset until eventually it gets absorbed by some smaller offset along the walk.

As we observed earlier, the ordering of interactions in a network of actors constrains the available pathways for the transfer of information or other goods. As a result, existing static network measures need to be generalised to incorporate temporally constrained walks. In the static case, the reachability of a node depends solely on the network structure; in the temporal case it also depends on whether there are paths that respect the constraints imposed by the ordering of interactions and the application of the endomorphisms. Consequently, we would expect that the reachability of an actor will be affected by time, so that less nodes are accessible.

Reachability is a measure of the extent to which information originating at a particular node can eventually reach other nodes. When the temporal ordering of interactions is taken into account, the number and extent of potential walks are limited by the temporal constraints. How might the temporal constraints imposed on paths impact on measures such as betweenness? How should betweenness be defined for dynamic networks? In the static case the calculation of betweenness relies on tallying up the shortest paths between a source and destination; hence, it is a path-based measure. In the dynamic case, should we consider shortest paths or paths of earliest potential transmission time, or some combination of both? The exact form of betweenness should reflect the processes at play in the network. For the purposes of this paper we merely wish to demonstrate that when calculating betweenness, as is done using the standard definition, the incorporation of the temporal constraints can significantly change the calculations.

The purpose of the above figures is not to fully document and understand the dynamics at this stage, but rather to demonstrate, firstly, a novel technique for calculating dynamic betweenness and secondly, to show that its dynamics can vary quite markedly from that of the static case.

In this paper, we have considered sets of time intervals in which each of a designated set of relations connects pairs of nodes, and considered several algebraic structures to which they give rise. The first is a left dioid structure based on Moody’s definition of time-ordered walks in dynamic networks. We showed how the algebraic structure of the left dioid may be exploited to assist in computing time-respecting paths and walks in the dynamic network. We then considered a generalised form of composition that allows for each form of transmission and/or relation to have a specific value of  
                         δ 
                      . We showed how a dioid of endomorphisms could be defined in this case and how, utilising this algebraic structure, paths and walks based on this generalised composition could also be computed. We applied these constructions to assess reachability and betweenness in illustrative observations on a dynamic network. The approach developed here provides the foundation for further development of dynamic network algebras and of measures for dynamic networks that are based on walks and paths in networks and hence on potential network flows. In future work, we will explore the structure of the algebras of dynamic networks to which these constructions give rise, and explore the further development of various measures capturing properties of dynamic networks.
