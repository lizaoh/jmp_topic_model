Stereokinetic phenomena, first discovered by  Musatti (1924, 1928–29, 1955) , are visual illusions of depth elicited by 2-D images undergoing periodic movements e/o deformations in the frontal plane of an observer.

In our previous papers ( Beghi, Xousa, & Zanforlin, 1997a, b ;  Xansa, Beghi, & Zanforlin, 1997, 2001 ;  Zanforlin, Beghi, & Xousa, 1997 ) we presented mathematical models of the stereokinetic phenomena known as the “oscillating tilted bar”, the “swinging gate” and the stereokinetic phenomenon generated by  a vertical line segment periodically contracting and simultaneously displacing itself  “from left to right”  in the frontal plane of an observer .

The criticisms of  Rokers and Liu (2004)  and  Liu (2004)  helped us in reformulating our mathematical models, eliminating discrepancies and ambiguities present in our previous papers. Characteristic of this new modelling is a clear definition of a multi-stage mathematical procedure matching basic requirements posed by the Visual System, as we know them from our experimental observations: in our previous papers we adopted the same multistage procedure without defining it explicitly.

The stages are the following:

Stage  1: Determination of the perceptual center  M  of the stimulus, decomposition of the image into two symmetrical parts and evaluation of the relative velocity distribution of the points of the stimulus with respect to  M . It comes out that the two symmetrical components have relative velocity distributions with opposite orientation ( antisymmetric property of the relative velocity distribution of the points of the stimulus) .

Stage  2: Introduction of a  minimality assumption,  presented here in a revised form. Its original version ( Zanforlin, 1988 ) was the following:

the visual system minimizes the relative velocity differences between the various points of the percept .

The revised version of the minimality condition that we present here is the following:

the visual system attributes to the points of an arbitrarily chosen symmetrical part of the stimulus an additional velocity component orthogonal to the frontal plane, in such a way as to minimize the differences between the lengths of the resulting 3-D velocity vectors .

This additional velocity component attributed to the generic point  Q  of the stimulus can be decomposed into the velocity of the midpoint  M  and the relative velocity of  Q  with respect to  M .

A relative velocity distribution orthogonal to the frontal plane and with opposite orientation is finally attributed to the points of the remaining part of the stimulus, thus extending the  antisymmetric property  to the 3-D space.

It should be underlined that this extension of the  antisymmetric property  is justified by the fact that the percept does not appear “broken in the middle”, with two symmetrical components originating from the midpoint.

Stage  3: With reference to the stereokinetic phenomena  of the oscillating tilted bar and of the swinging gate, a  transient phase  is now introduced: this phase characterizes the “virtual rising up of the stimulus” from the frontal plane during a finite time interval of amplitude Δ t . This rising up, not “seen” by the observer, leads to the definition of the percept's structure. The choice of the value of Δ t  will be discussed in the next sections.

Stage  4:  Steady-state phase , characterizing the appearance of the percept.

Stereokinetic phenomena are crucial for the understanding of the mechanisms underlying human visual perception. Their mathematical modelling is not an easy task, owing to the great interdisciplinary cooperative effort required. It seems that we achieved this goal, since our models evidence a fairly good qualitative agreement with the experimental results (see  Beghi et al., 1997a, b ;  Xausa et al., 1997, 2001 ; Zanforlin et al., 1988; Zanforlin, 1997).

We consider particularly important to recall here explicitly the main results concerning the stereokinetic phenomenon generated by a vertical contracting bar, since they evidence (theoretically and experimentally) a discrepancy from what should be expected according to the laws of Geometric Perspective, namely 
                         
                            (1) 
                            contracting vertical line segments with no lateral displacement appear to rotate around their midpoints for all contraction's time laws; 
                         
                         
                            (2) 
                            when contraction is combined with simultaneous lateral displacement, the percept appears to move in depth as well as to rotate around its midpoint; 
                         
                         
                            (3) 
                            although lateral displacement of the stimulus is necessary to induce an impression of movement in depth of the midpoint, the amount of depth displacement perceived depends mainly on the vertical contraction and very little on the amount of lateral displacement; 
                         
                         
                            (4) 
                            the amount of displacement in depth perceived is far shorter than what would have been predicted by the laws of perspective of rigid objects; 
                         
                         
                            (5) 
                            the amount of perceived rotation is not very different whether the line appears to rotate around its stationary midpoint or rotate while moving in depth (when Δ x  and Δ h  have the same magnitude).

when contraction is combined with simultaneous lateral displacement, the percept appears to move in depth as well as to rotate around its midpoint;

although lateral displacement of the stimulus is necessary to induce an impression of movement in depth of the midpoint, the amount of depth displacement perceived depends mainly on the vertical contraction and very little on the amount of lateral displacement;

the amount of displacement in depth perceived is far shorter than what would have been predicted by the laws of perspective of rigid objects;

the amount of perceived rotation is not very different whether the line appears to rotate around its stationary midpoint or rotate while moving in depth (when Δ x  and Δ h  have the same magnitude).
