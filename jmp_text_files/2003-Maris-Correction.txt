Meyer, Irwin, Osman, and Kounios (1988)  developed the speed-accuracy decomposition (SAD) technique for studying the course of information accumulation during stimulus processing. Their goal was to find empirical evidence for the discrete or continuous nature of information accumulation. The amount of information was measured by the so-called sophisticated-guessing probability, which is defined in the first section of this note. As shown by  Meyer et al. (1988) , under certain assumptions (see, The Parallel Sophisticated Guessing Model) this sophisticated-guessing probability can be calculated from the responses on so-called regular and signal trials (see, The Titrated Reaction Time Procedure). However, one of the formula's used by  Meyer et al. (1988)  is incorrect. In this note, the correct formula is derived.

In the first section, the SAD technique is described briefly. In the second section, the incorrect formula of  Meyer et al. (1988)  is presented and the correct formula is derived. Finally, in the third section, the sensitivity of the SAD technique to the use of the incorrect instead of the correct formula is examined.

The SAD technique involves three components: (a) an experimental procedure, the so-called titrated reaction time (TRT) procedure; (b) a model for the TRT data, the so-called parallel sophisticated-guessing (PSG) model; and (c) a data-analytic procedure in which the sophisticated-guessing probabilities are calculated from the TRT data, making use of the PSG model.

The TRT procedure consists of a random mixture of two trial types: regular trials and signal trials. Regular trials are the trial type one typically sees in conventional reaction time procedures: after a brief warning signal, a stimulus is presented to which the participant responds as quickly as possible without committing many errors.

Signal trials differ from regular trials in that they involve a response signal (e.g., a pure tone). The response signal is presented somewhere between the stimulus and the longest regular trial response times. If the participant has not yet responded to the stimulus by the time the response signal is presented, then upon detecting the response signal, he or she should respond immediately, giving his or her best guess about the correct response. If the partial information that has accumulated up until the response signal is detected, is available to the participant, he or she may use this partial information to make a sophisticated instead of a pure guess. Assuming that these sophisticated-guessing probabilities can be made observable, one can obtain information about the accumulation of partial information by varying the time lag between stimulus onset and response signal.

Meyer et al. (1988)  assume that part of the responses that are given after the response signal come from normal processing. That is, not all of these responses are sophisticated guesses. For this reason, the sophisticated-guessing probabilities cannot be directly observed. However, it is possible to correct the probability correct on the signal trials for the responses that come from normal processing. This correction formula is derived from the PSG model.

The PSG model is a race model for the responses on signal trials. It assumes that, on detection of the response signal, two processes race against each other: (a) the normal process and (b) the guessing process. The guessing process has access to whatever partial information has been accumulated by the normal process up until the response signal is detected. If the guessing process wins the race, a response is given on the basis of this partial information. In other words, a sophisticated guess is made. And if the normal process wins the race, the participant responds on the basis of an amount of information one typically accumulates on regular trials. It is assumed that the normal and the guessing processes are statistically independent.

Although numerical examples cannot give definite answers, the present results give some reason to believe that the results of the existing studies using the SAD technique are not substantially distorted because of the incorrect formula in  Eq. (4) . This belief is based on the following arguments: (a) there is only a distortion if the micro-trade-off is not flat (i.e., statistical dependence between accuracy and completion time of the normal process) and (b) when the micro-trade-off is extremely non-flat (a step-function), as in the numerical example, the distortion is probably small.
