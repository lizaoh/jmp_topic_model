JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY: 2, 219-241 (1963)  Stochastic Learning Models for Social Competition  SEYMOUR ROSENBERG AND MAX S. SCHOEFFLER  Bell Telephone Laboratories, Inc., Murray Hill, New Mersey  The paper extends a class of linear learning models for a response continuum to a repetitive two-person auction. Pairs of  subjects bid against each other on successive trials for prizes whose values were determined stochastically. Three  experimental groups of 10 subject-pairs were used, the groups being differentiated by the variance of the prize values.  Results indicated that (a) mean asymptotic bids were slightly lower than the mean of the prize values, and (b) response  variances were in accordance with model predictions. The implications of the data for the structure of the model were also  considered. The results support the use of certain random variables rather than constants as coefficients in the basic linear  difference equation of the model.  Mathematical learning theorists have devoted a good deal of effort extending quantitative learning theory to elementary forms  of social interaction in the laboratory (e.g., Burke, 1959; Estes, 1957; Hall, 1962; Rosenberg, 1962; Suppes and Atkinson,  1960; Suppes and Carlsmith, 1962). One class of social situations which has been particularly prominent in this work is that  of competition between two persons. Most of the early developments of stochastic learning theory took place within the  experimental context of the two-choice task. It is not surprising, therefore, that the initial applications of stochastic  learning theory to social competition have been restricted to situations involving a small set of responses. It has not been  possible to relax this restriction on the number of responses without introducing formidable computational problems. However,  the stochastic models which have recently been developed for the response continuum, i.e., for an uncountable set of ordered  alternatives (Anderson, 1961; Rosenberg; 1962; Rouanet and Rosenberg, 1964; Suppes, 1959, 1960) can also be extended to the  analysis of interpersonal situations in which each person selects from a large, finite, and ordered set of alternative  choices. The present paper describes the application of a class of continuum models (i.e., a general model and some of its  special cases) to a competitive laboratory situation involving a large number of ordered responses. The experimental task in  this study is similar to an iterated sealed-bid auction with just two bidders.  Quantitative theories of competition have also come from disciplines other than psychology. Solutions from game theory, for  example, prescribe how "rational"  219  220 ROSENBERG AND SCHOEFFLER  players ought to behave in certain competitive situations. Game theory was not intended to describe either the strategies  which players actually adopt or changes in strategy by players as they accumulate experience about a game. Nevertheless, the  relevance of game theory to actual human behavior has been extensively investigated in the laboratory (see review by Rapoport  and Orwant, 1963). The results of this work strongly suggest that experimentally naive subjects do not, in general, discover  and utilize a minimax strategy except for simple games with a saddle point. Even in simple games, only a fraction of the  subjects adopt the correct (minimax) pure strategy.  Economists have also formalized a variety of economically competitive situations. The economic literature of the early  nineteen thirties contains analyses of the duopoly. These models prescribe optimal pricing for the firm using the interrelated  variables of manufacturing costs and consumer demand as input information. These economic models, like game theory, are both  rational and static. That is, they do not Jescribe continual adjustments in response to changing inputs when the adjustments,  themselves, contribute to determining the inputs. In addition, these models are not intended to describe the market but,  rather, are possible guides for action for an entrepreneur. Economists have also developed dynamic models to describe economic  phenomena. In a recent paper on duopoly behavior, Ferguson and Pfouts (1962) explicitly postulate a learning mechanism to  describe temporal changes in the pricing decisions of two business competitors.  With few exceptions, economic models have not been subjected to experimental tests. Very likely the paucity of detailed model  testing is due to the inaccessible nature of a good economic "laboratory" from which to obtain satisfactory data. It should be  remarked at the outset that while our experimental situation is similar to a sealedbid auction, it has not been our purpose to  mimic the auction in detail. Although we have abstracted some of the basic features of an auction, we have also tried, in this  first study, to stay close to the familiar experimental paradigm of simple learning in a two-person situation. We have thus  interpreted successive auctions in a given market as the repeated trials of a learning task and have assumed a constant demand  for the commodity.  BASIC TASK  In order to coordinate a continuum model with events in the present experiment, it will be helpful to sketch first the basic  features of the experimental task. The task was developed from a one-person bidding situation previously used by Schoeffler  (1962). An experimental session consisted of a sequence of discrete trials. Each trial started with a signal to both subjects  to bid privately for an object of unknown value. The bids were in "Make-Believe Dollars" (MBD's). A specific object was not  actually displayed to the subjects but only its value in MBD's was indicated to both subjects  STOCHASTIC LEARNING MODELS 221  after they had bid on a trial. The other subject's bid on a trial was also revealed at the end of each trial. The net gain (or  loss) of the higher bidder on each trial was defined in the instructions to be the difference between the value of the object  and his bid on the trial; the net gain of the lower bidder on each trial was zero. During this experiment, subjects were in  separate booths and could not see or talk to each other.  The absence of an actual object at the beginning of each trial was a convenient simplification of the experimental task. This  simplified experimental task might be thought of as resembling an auction situation in which the quality of the objects  remains constant over time and the bids are made in terms of price per unit quantity, e.g., price per dozen. Or in the case of  sealed bids for contracts, one may think of a set of contractors who submit bids from time to time for jobs requiring  approximately the same material and manpower. Fluctuations in the value of the objects are introduced by the market at resale  or, in the case of contracts, by actual costs in material and manpower.  THEORETICAL ANALYSIS  The class of models which was applied to this auction task will be referred to as the general model. Special cases will be  identified as necessary, The general model is, in turn, a special case of the general linear model for the response continuum  which is described by Rouanet and Rosenberg (1964).  The general model will be described first within the context of a one-person simple learning experiment because the one-person  experiment is particularly well suited for an exposition of the basic structure of the model. The coordination of the general  model to the two-person auction task will then be described. The general approach which characterizes the applications of  learning models to interpersonal behavior is retained here, i.e., predictions concerning interpersonal behavior are derived  entirely from a precise formulation of psychological (intrapersonal) processes.  However, the present application departs in an important way from previous applications of learning models to two-person  interactions with determinate reinforcement. In the auction task the reinforced (correct) response is not explicitly  identified by the experimenter on each trial. In order to apply the model to the auction task it was assumed that the value of  the reinforced response on a trial was a linear combination of the value of the object and the other subject's response on  that trial. The rationale for this definition of the reinforced response was developed from the competitive orientation given  in the instructions to the subjects.  Basic Model. In simple learning the subject is given a signal at the beginning of each trial to make a response located on a  one-dimensional continuum. An outcome follows shortly and, in the determinate situation, informs the subject of the correct  response on that trial. The trial is then terminated. This experimental paradigm, which is analogous to the classical  Humphreys situation, has been used by  222  ROSENBERG AND SCHOEFFLER  Suppes and Frankmann (1961) with the circumference of a circle as the response continuum and by Rosenberg (1963) with a line  segment as the continuum. There are numerous other experiments in early studies of human learning and in the motorskills  literature which involve linear response continua but which do not utilize such models. In the typical learning experiment, the outcome on each trial contains a value which  the experimenter identifies for the subject as the correct response for that trial. In the auction task, the outcome on each  trial includes the value of the object, the other person's bid, and the net gain or loss to the subject. From this set of  observables, the experimenter must now infer the value of yn in the model. The definition of yn which we shall introduce for  the auction task is sufficiently different from the usual determinate learning situation that we shall refer to y,, as the  goal-response instead of the correct or reinforced response. This term should simply remind us that we have introduced a novel  definition of y,,.  It is in the definition of the goal-response that the competitive nature of the task enters into the analysis. In order to  understand how the goal-response was defined for this task, it is helpful to read first that part of the subject's  instructions which structured the experiment as a competitive situation. The subjects were told:  "Before we start let me emphasize that in this auction, you should try to do better than the other person. This is a  competitive situation so that the only thing that matters is that you beat the other person by as much as possible. The actual  amount of money you are gaining or losing is not important. You should simply try to gain more than the other person or lose  less than the other person. Assume that you don't know each other and that you will never see each other again. The other  person's feelings should not make any difference to you and assume that your feelings do not make any difference to him or  her. Just remember that you're out to beat the other person by as much as you can and that he or she is out to beat you."'  Consider the outcome event for one subject at a time. The apparatus sketched in Fig. 2 was designed so that the value of the object and the bid of the other subject were  revealed on each trial only after a subject had written down a bid for that trial. Each subject was provided with a  ring-binder mounted on a wooden board. The ring-binder held a set of response sheets and printed cards. Each printed card,  approximately half the width of the response sheet, contained the value of the object and appeared face-down at the beginning  of a trial.  A system of lights and mirrors was used to display each subject's bid to the other at the appropriate time. MI's are small  round mirrors mounted above and in front of each desk; M2 is a half-silvered mirror mounted on the wall in front of the booths  and visible to both subjects; L1's are desk lamps; L2 is a light-bulb mounted behind the half-silvered mirror. There were no  other lights in the room.  Each trial consisted of the following automatically timed sequence:  1. A response interval of 10.2 sec. (LI's were off and L2 was on.) With this lighting condition each subject could see only  her own ring-binder. Each subject wrote her bid (using a black grease-pencil) on the response sheet in the square labelled  "YOUR BID" and then flipped the card over as shown in the lower part of Fig. 2 in order to see the printed "VALUE OF OBJECT"  on the card. An experimenter monitored the subjects to assure that subjects followed this routine. The experimenter also  extended the response interval if either subject failed to bid within the allotted time.  2. An interval of 2.2 sec. (L1's and L2 were all off.)  3. A display and computation period of 7.1 sec. (LI's were on and L2 was off.) With this lighting condition and only with this  lighting, each subject could see the response sheet of the other by looking into the half-silvered mirror. In this interval  each subject also recorded her gain or loss on the left side of the response sheet. The first general remark about the response data is that the variance of the bids does not approach zero  with increasing trial number, that is, subjects do not adopt a pure strategy. Each subject displays variability in bidding  throughout the experimental session and our analyses of the bidding will be concerned with response means and variances and  with the values approached by these statistics.  The learning curve in terms of response means turns out to be relatively flat. This is probably due in part to the fact that  the subjects were told in advance that the value of the object would never exceed 60 MBD's-restricting initial bids to values  close to the value of the asymptotic response mean.  According to the model the response means of the two subjects in a pair, if they converge at all, converge to the average  value of the objects-which, as shown in Fig. 3, is 37 for all conditions in this experiment. This is a parameter-free  prediction in the sense that no parameters need to be estimated from data to derive this equilibrium value. However, certain  assumptions concerning the range of values of model parameters must be made to assert that the equilibrium is a stable point.  The derivation of asymptotic response means and the necessary and sufficient conditions for convergence are given in the  Appendix.  The response means for each subject in each consecutive block of 14 trials were first calculated to determine approximately  where this statistic reached a stable value. From these data, Trials 85-112 appeared satisfactory for estimating the  asymptotic response mean for each experimental group. The choice of Trials 85-112 was confirmed by an analysis of variance  test which yielded no significant differences between the response means from Trials 85-96 and the response means from Trials  97-112. INSTRUCTIONS TO SUBJECTS  The experiment in which you are about to take part is a study of how people bid in a certain kind of auction. For this  purpose, we have constructed a special laboratory situation which has many features of an auction and in which we would like  you to participate for about 45 minutes.  Imagine, first of all, that you are bidding for an object of a certain value. This is a special type of auction because both  of you will be bidding at the same time and privately. That is, you will bid for an object without knowing what the other  person's bid is. As usual, the object goes to the higher bidder for the amount that he or she bid. The lower bidder neither  buys the object nor has to pay anything. (Pause; all pauses are five seconds.)  Just to make things simple, we will not actually show you the object. We will just tell you the amount of money that the  object is worth. However, neither of you will know the amount of money that the object is worth until after both of you have  made a bid for it. (Pause.)  We will now give you a few examples to show you how this auction works. The way you bid is by writing a number on the small  white sheet of paper in front oyou. Just to get you acquainted with the mechanics of bidding, we have already written down a  number for each of you. These are practice examples and will not actually count. After both of you have bid, the light behind  the mirror in front of you will go off. (Demonstrate.)  Look at this mirror into the small round mirror in the next booth. In this way you should be able to see what the other person  has bid. (Pause.)  In this example, one person bid $81 and the other person bid $85. $85 is the higher bid so the person who bid $85 buys the  object. Now turn the small card over to find out the value of the object which was bought. The object was worth $87. So the  person who bid $85 buys an object worth $87 and pays $85 for it. His or her net gain is then $2. He or she then writes this  net gain on the left side of the white sheet of paper as you see in the example in front of you. The lower bidder neither pays  anything nor loses anything so that the net gain of the lower bidder is 0 and this is written on his or her white sheet of  paper.  When the light behind the mirror goes on again, you are to turn to a new sheet of paper and write a new bid for a new object  of unknown value. Turn the sheet over now. (Pause.) When each of you has written your bid, the light behind the mirror will go  off and you will again be able to see each other's bids. (Pause.)  In the new example in front of you, the two bids are $85 and $76. You may now turn the smaller card to see the value of the  object for which you were bidding that time. The lower bidder again  enters 0 as the net gain.  When the light behind the mirror goes on turn to a new sheet and make a new bid. You can turn the card over to see the value  of the object as soon as you have made your bid. (Pause.) As you see in this example, the two bids are the same. In case of a  tie neither of you buys the object and both of you would put a 0 for the net gain.  You should be told a few more things and then we can get started.
