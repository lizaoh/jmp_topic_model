JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 36, 32-67 (1992)  Measurement-Free Tests of a General State-Space Model of Prototype Learning  IN .TAE MYUNG AND .TEROME R. BUSEMEYER  Purdue University  A general state-space model of prototype learning is formulated which is used to organize and relate several special cases  such as Hintzman's (1986) multiple-trace model, MetcalfeEich's (1982) holographic model, and connectionistic models (Knapp and  Anderson, 1984; McClelland and Rumelhart, 1985). Three basic properties were defined in terms of this general model-linearity,  time-invariance, and non-interference. An experiment was conducted using a prototype production task in which subjects were  asked to produce a prototypic spectrum after observing a series of random spectral stimuli generated from a common chemical  compound. "Measurement free" tests of linearity, originally developed by Krantz (1973), were performed with few assumptions  about internal features that subjects may use to encode the stimulus information. The properties of linearity and  noninterference were reasonably well supported, but the property of time-invariance was violated in a complex manner. These  results corroborate previous findings by Busemeyer and Myung (1988) using more general "measurement free" tests. 1992  Academic Press, Inc.  If you look through the psychobiology chapter of almost any popular introductory psychology textbook, you will find a figure  showing the author's rendition of a prototypic neuron. Next to this, you may also find a photograph depicting a sample of  actual neurons, which vary tremendously in shape and size. Apparently, the prototype was abstracted from a large sample of  such photographs, and although the prototype resembles the instances in terms of essential features, it does not look exactly  like any one of them in detail. Thus, the prototype is not obtained by simply selecting one of the instances, and some type of  abstraction is involved.  Humans seem to possess a natural ability to abstract and reproduce a single image, called a prototype, from a myriad of  examples. How this abstraction occurs is the main issue of this article. In Section 1, we review the previous experimental  findings and earlier models of prototype learning. In Section 2, we present a general state space model of prototype learning  and we show how earlier models are related through this general theoretical framework. In Section 3, we present new  experimental tests of three basic properties of the general theory that are shared by many of the earlier models.  Reprint requests should be addressed to Jerome R. PREVIOUS RESEARCH AND THEORY ON PROTOTYPE LEARNING  Previous Research  One of the earliest studies of prototype learning was reported in Clark Hull's (1920) dissertation (Experiment L). Subjects  learned how to associate 12 different names (category labels) to 144 different Chinese characters (exemplars). Each name was  associated with a unique set of common features, which we call the objective prototype. Two different types of trials were  used. On categorization trials, the experimenter presented a Chinese character (an exemplar) and the subject was asked to say  the name that was most likely to be associated with the character (category label). On prototype production trials, the  experimenter presented a name (a category label) and the subject was asked to draw a Chinese character that best represented  that name.  The results of the prototype production task for two of the category names are reproduced in Fig. 1 (see Hull, 1920, Plate IV  on page 80 for the complete set of 12). The first character in each row is the objective prototype associated with one of the  names. The remaining characters show the evolution of a subject's prototype at various stages of training. As Hull (1920)  noted, the most striking property of these results is the gradual way that the prototype evolved during training.  Note that Hull (1920) used two different types of tasks-categorization and prototype production. In both tasks, subjects learn  to associate category labels with exemplars. In the categorization task, an exemplar is presented, a category label is  retrieved, and the main question is "Does abstraction occur?" In the prototype production task, the process is reversed-a  category label is presented, a prototype estimate is retrieved, and the major question shifts to "How does abstraction occur?"  Following the pioneering work by Hull (1920), most researchers have restricted their research on prototype learning to  categorization tasks. For example, in the landmark studies by Posner and Keele (1968, 1970), subjects learned to associate 16  two-dimensional dot patterns to four category labels. Each dot pattern was constructed by statistically distorting a centroid  (objective prototype) associated with a category. On each training trial, a dot pattern was presented, subjects produced a  category label, followed by feedback indicating the correct category label. Posner and Keele (1968, 1970) found that during transfer, subjects classified the objective prototype as accurately as the old  training stimuli and better than new dot patterns, and, furthermore, accuracy for the prototype persisted longer than that for  old training stimuli after a delay between training and test. Their study became a cornerstone of the prototype hypothesis  (Reed, 1972; Homa, 1984). This view assumes that people form a prototype as a central tendency of a category, and that they  use the prototype to categorize new exemplars.  The prototype model of categorization was soon challenged by an alternative, exemplar hypothesis, which assumes that subjects  store only the individual examplars that they have studied. A new exemplar is then classified into the category with  remembered exemplars most similar to the new one (Brooks, 1978; Hintzman, 1986; Medin & Shaffer, 1978; Nosofsky, 1986). Recent  evidence indicates that categorization performance depends on the variance and correlations between feature values as well as  the central tendency, and exempler-based models provide a better account of these effects (Medin & Schaffer, 1978; Nosofsky,  1986). Therefore, categorization tasks may tell us little about the cognitive processes involved in prototype abstraction.  Often the objective prototype is defined as the central tendency of a distribution of exemplars. Therefore, experiments on  intuitive estimation of central tendency may be more relevant for understanding prototype learning (see Busemeyer, 1991, for a  review), In a detailed study by Weiss and Anderson (1969), subjects were asked to estimate the average length of a series of  lines. In one experiment, an estimate was given after each successive line length within a series, and in another experiment  the estimate was given only once at the end of the series. The purpose of the study was to investigate a general averaging  model that we call the prototype updating model. In order to relate this work to subsequent research, we present a  multi-dimensional version of their model.' One important test of the prototype updating model is a test of additivity (Anderson, 1964): If the line length for serial  position k is manipulated while the lengths at the remaining positions are held fixed, then the simple main effect that  results from this manipulation should be independent of the fixed values. In other words, there should be no interactions  among the line lengths across serial positions. Weiss and Anderson (1969) tested this property and found only a few small  violations of this additive (superposition) property. Therefore, they concluded that their results, as well as previous  research, generally support the prototype updating model.  Given that the additive (superposition) property is supported, then the absolute weights can be estimated from the main  effects of each serial position (Anderson, 1964). For example, if the arithmetic average was computed, then the main effects  would be equal across serial positions. Weiss and Anderson (1969) found that when the means were estimated only once at the  end of a series, the absolute weights were an increasing function of serial position (producing recency effects). But when  successive estimates were obtained, the absolute weights were a U-shaped function of serial position (producing both primacy  and recency effects), although there were large individual differences.  It is important to note that the serial position effects described above for intuitive estimation are not the same as those  observed with memory recall experiments. In particular, Anderson and Hubert (1963) found that there was no correlation between  serial position effects obtained from intuitive estimation and serial position effects obtained from free recall, and similar  results were obtained by Dreben, Fiske, and Hastie (1979).  More recently, Busemeyer and Myung (1988) reintroduced the prototype production task using two-dimensional dot stimuli similar  to that used in earlier category learning experiments. This prototype production task can also be interpreted as a  multi-dimensional intuitive mean estimation task. Subjects were told that their task was to locate and draw the true positions  of four stars located in space on the basis of a series of telescopic photographs that were distorted by atmospheric  disturbances. The photographs were generated by statistically distorting the centroid (called the objective prototype).  Within the prototype production paradigm there are two different ways of presenting exemplars-blocked vs mixed. In the blocked  procedure, subjects learn a sequence of exemplar images associated with a single category label within a block of trials, and  after completing the block, they move to another block of trials with an unrelated category label. In the mixed procedure,  subjects learn exemplar images associated with different category labels, and the category labels vary randomly from trial to  trial. Busemeyer and Myung (1988) used a blocked training procedure in which subjects observed a series of photos (exemplars)  generated from the first star pattern (objective prototype) during the first block of trials, followed by a distractor task,  followed by another series of photos generated by the second star pattern for the next block of trials, and so on.  The purpose of the experiment was to test three basic properties derived from the following linear system model of prototype  learning (t > 0):  y(t+1)=Y,w(t,k)f(k), k=0,...,t, (2)  where y(t + 1) is a vector representing the prototype estimate after observing a total of t exemplars, w(t, k) is a (scalar)  serial position weight assigned to serial position k within a sequence containing a total of t exemplars, f(k) is a vector  representing the exemplar presented at serial position k for k > 0, and f(0) = y(1) represents the initial estimate.  One property is noninterference-the manipulation of the i th coordinate of the exemplar f(k) should have no effect on any  coordinate other than the ith coordinate of the prototype estimate y(t + 1). This follows from the fact that the serial  position weight, w(t, k), is a scalar. No systematic violations of this property were obtained.  A second property is additivity (superposition}-if we hold fixed the exemplars presented before or after serial position k,  and change only the exemplar presented at serial position k, then the effect of this change should be independent of the fixed  values for the other exemplars. This follows from the additive combination rule (superposition principle). Similar to Weiss  and Anderson (1969), small but statistically reliable deviations from additivity were found. But the linear system model may  be retained if we assume that these deviations are the result of a nonlinear S-shaped output function that squeezes the  prototype image into the bounded response plane. This explanation for the deviations from additivity was examined in the  experiment reported below. Previous Models of Prototype Learning  Recently, a variety of prototype learning models have appeared in the literature inclulding connectionistic memory models  (Knapp & Anderson, 1984; McClelland and Rumelhart, 1985, 1988, Chap. 4), holographic memory models (Metcalfe-Eich, 1982), and  multiple-trace memory models (Hintzman, 1986). Surprisingly, it turns out that despite the apparent differences, many of these  models are special cases of the linear system model (2) when applied to prototype production tasks.  All of the models described below assume that the exemplars and the category labels are represented by feature vectors. The  m-dimensional feature vector f is used to represent the exemplar image, and the n-dimensional feature vector g is used to  represent the category label. When unrelated category labels are used, any pair of category feature vectors, g; and g;,  assigned to two different category labels i and j, are assumed to be orthogonal, g Tg; = 0. It is convenient to collect both  the category and exemplar feature vectors into a p = n + m-dimensional vector, h, where h = [g If], called the exemplar  ensemble. One each trial, a retrieval cue is presented, which is represented by the feature vector v(t). It is assumed that a  fixed sequence is used so that v is a function of t. The retrieval cue may be a category label (e.g., v = g for prototype  production) or a new exemplar pattern (e.g., v = f for categorization). The retrieved image is represented by a q-dimensional  feature vector y.  Multiple-trace model. The multiple-trace model could be generalized as follows. First, the exemplar ensembles may be rehearsed, and the number of  times that an ensemble is rehearsed may be an increasing function of its age, producing primacy effects, denoted p(t-k).  Second, each exemplar ensemble may contain context features that change as a function of lag, producing recency effects,  denoted r(t-k). Added together, these two factors produce serial position weights, w(t - k) = p(t - k) + r(t - k), that would  be a U-shaped function of lag. Finally, the echo may be formed by averaging rather than summing the exemplars,' producing a  model identical to the exemplarbased averaging model (3), which is a special case of (2). Hintzman (1986) also pointed out that the multiple-trace model, could be extended by adding a nonlinear recursive output  model. Essentially, the output echo is re-entered as a probe input, and this recycling process is repeated N times. A similar  nonlinear recursive output model could be constructed for the holographic model by recycling the output.  Unlike the previous models, these nonlinear recursive output models do not satisfy the additive (superposition) property, and  consequently they are not special cases of (2). During the early stages of training, nonlinear hidden unit models will not satisfy additivity, and, consequently, they will  not mimic (2). However, when the learning rate decreases as a function of training (see White, 1989, for the precise  conditions), and as t then becomes large, W(t + 1) converges to the solution that minimizes the mean squared error between the  output y(t) and the target, x(t). When the exemplars are randomly and independently sampled from a fixed population, setting  the prototype estimate equal to the mean of the distribution (the objective prototype) minimizes the mean squared error. Thus,  at asymptote, the prototype estimate of the nonlinear learning model converges to the arithmetic averaging model; i.e., the  output converges to (2) with w(t, k) = 1/t.  Myung and Busemeyer (1989) proposed a general state-space model of prototype learning, which includes as special cases the  linear system model (1) and various types of nonlinear models. This general model is presented in Section 2 along with more  general definitions of the three basic properties of linearity, time invariance, and noninterference. Finaly, we describe how  these general properties were tested using measurement-free methods (Krantz, 1973) in Section 3.  2. GENERAL STATE-SPACE MODEL OF PROTOTYPE LEARNING  We begin by distinguishing between representations of images formed by the subject and those used by the experimenter. On each  trial, denoted t, an exemplar  image is presented visually (e.g., a photograph) or auditorily (e.g., a tone sequence). The experimenter records the exemplar  image by obtaining a k-dimensional vector of physical measurements denoted e(t).  Another m-dimensional vector, denoted f(t), is used to represent the subject's perceptual image of the physically defined  exemplar image e(t). The map relating the two is represented by f(t) = V[e(t) ], where the real-valued vector function V  represents the perceptual process.  In category or prototype learning, the exemplar ensemble consists of two components, the exemplar image, f(t), and a category  label. The n-dimensional vector g(t) will be used to represent the subject's interpretation of the category label (e.g., the  title of a picture). The ensemble produced by concatenating the category label vector and exemplar image vector produces a p =  m + n dimensional vector h(t), h(t) = [g(t) If(t)].  Each trial of a category or prototype learning experiment ends with the presentation of some type of feedback or target  provided by the experimenter. The feedback information provided on each trial is represented by a target vector x(t). For  category learning tasks, the feedback is the correct category label, e.g., x(t) =g(t), but for prototype learning tasks, the  feedback is a new exemplar image, e.g., X(t) = f(t).  All memory tasks involve some type of retrieval cue that is used to probe momory and retrieve an image. The retrieval cue is  symbolized by a vector v(t) and the output image retrieved by this cue is represented by the vector y(t). The nature of both  of these vectors will vary across experimental tasks. In the categorization task, the retrieval cue would be a novel exemplar  image, e.g., v(t)=f(t), and the output image is a category label; e.g., y(t) has the same coordinates as g(t). However, in the  prototype production task, the retrieval cue is a category label, e.g., v(t) =g(t), and the output image is a prototype  estimate; e.g., y(t) has the same coordinates as f(t).  The retrieved image, y(t), is still an unobservable internal representation. To complete the task, subjects must map their  internal image onto an observable response scale, which is recorded by the experimenter as the vector r(t). The map from the  retrieved image to the recorded response is represented by r(t) =J[y(t)], where the vector function J can be interpreted as  the judgment, decision, or response process. In the categorization task, the recorded response, r(t), is the final category  decision (a category name). For recognition tasks, the recorded response is a yes-no binary choice. In the prototype  production task, the recorded response r(t) is a k-dimensional real-valued vector with the same physical measurements used to  record the exemplar image, e(t). The stimuli  were mass spectra of fictitious chemical samples, consisting of seven vertical bars with different heights in different  horizontal coordinates, associated with fictitious chemical names as shown in Fig. 4. The horizontal coordinate represents  mass number, and the vertical coordinate represents abundance of molecular fragments in a sample preparation.  Mass spectrometry is one of the most frequently used techniques to identify unknown molecules. METHOD  Stimulus Presentation  Subjects were told to imagine that each pattern was a mass spectrum of a fictitious drug molecule, that each pattern was  distorted from what it should be because of variations in sample preparation, and that four mass spectra were obtained from  each drug. Subjects were asked to estimate the true pattern for each drug based on the four distorted patterns. They were also  told that different sets of four patterns were obtained from sample preparations of different drugs.  A blocked training procedure was used, which produced the following sequence of exemplar ensembles: (g, I f), (911f12), (g,  I f,s), (911f14), (911f2,), (92J22), (92I f23), (92I fz4), (g3 I f31), -- where gi represents the category label feature  vector for category i and f;; represents the image feature vector of exemplar j from category i.  Stimulus Design  Only 64 drugs out of 100 were manipulated for tests of the three properties described earlier. The patterns from the other 36  drugs were simply fillers designed to make subjects unaware of the stimulus design.  The main design of the experiment was based on the manipulation of three elements, e3, e4, and e5 in the patterns of the  regular 64 drugs. For convenience, let us assume that each stimulus pattern was contained within a bounded 10 x 10 plane in  arbitrary units for each axis with the coordinate value (0, 0) at the lower left corner. The value of e4 was assigned either  2.6 or 8.3 units on each pattern. For example, e4 could have a sequence of values (2.6, 8.3, 8.3, 2.6) across four sample  patterns from the same category. All 16 possible sequences of length four were included in the experiment. e3 was fixed at  either 3.8 or 7.1 units for all four patterns within a given drug, but this value varied across drugs. e5 was also fixed at  either 1.4 or 5.6 units for all four patterns within a given drug, and again this value varied across drugs. Four possible  combinations of e3 and e5 were crossed with 16 sequences of e4 to produce a 2 x 24 x 2 factorial design with 64 conditions.  Each subject observed all 64 conditions in a different random order.  The remaining elements, (e,, ez, eb, e,) for the 64 regular drugs and (e,, eZ, e3, e4, e5, eb, e,) for the 36 filler drugs  were generated as follows. First, the true value for each element was randomly sampled from a uniform distribution over a set  of {2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8}. Then the four disturbances across four sample patterns were randomly sampled from a normal  distribution with zero mean and a standard deviation of 1.0.  The horizontal coordinates for the seven elements were fixed within four samples for a given drug. For filler drugs, the  horizontal coordinate for each element was sampled from a uniform distribution, U[0.3, 9.7]. For regular drugs, the horizontal  coordinates for the first two elements, e, and ez, and the last two elements, eb and e,, were sampled from uniform  distributions, U[0.3, 2.5] and U[7.5, 9.7], respectively. The horizontal coordinates for the remaining elements, e3, e4, and  es, were sampled from uniform distributions, U[3.6, 4.0] for es, U[4.8, 5.2] for e4, and U[6.0, 6.4] for es.  Experimental Groups  An earlier study by Weiss and Anderson (1969) found the when estimates were made only at the end of the sequence, fairly  uniform recency effects were observed; but when subjects provided trial by trial estimates throughout the sequence, both  recency and primacy effects appeared with large individual differences. We investigated this effect with more complex stimuli  by using two different groups of subjects. In one group (Group A), subjects were instructed to observe each pattern  sequentially and to provide a drawing of their estimate after each pattern. The other group of subjects (Group B) was asked to  provide a drawing of their estimate only at the end of the fourth pattern.  Procedure  The experiment was conducted in a quiet room with all the procedures preprogrammed on a microcomputer. Subjects were shown  stimulus patterns in the upper half of a video screen and then they were asked to draw their estimate of the prototype in the  lower half of the same screen. The time sequence of the experimental procedure was as follows. At the beginning of each drug  molecule, the computer showed the name of the drug in the center of the screen for two and half seconds, and then after  clearing the screen, an empty square box displaying only the drug name and the sample number appeared in the upper half of the  screen. DISCUSSION  This experiment was designed to empirically test three basic properties of prototype learning models using the prototype  production paradigm. The results indicate that additivity and noninterference held reasonably well but time invariance was  clearly violated in a complex way. These results rule out both the exemplar-based averaging model (3) and the prototype  updating model (1), and all time-invariant models of prototype learning (Hintzman, 1986; Metcalfe-Eich, 1982; Knapp and  Anderson, 1984). These results confirm the previous conclusions of Busemeyer and Myung (1988) but with new stimuli and using  new feature-free and measurement-free tests. In the following, the implications of the present results for prototype learning  models are discussed.  Linearity. Recently, researchers working with neural (Grossberg, 1988) or connectionistic (Rumelhart, Hinton & Williams, 1986)  network models have stressed the importance of nonlinearity for modeling human cognition. Granted that some part of the  overall system is nonlinear, the next question is, Where is it located? Figures 2 and 3 suggest a number of possibilities (any  of the four functions V O, 'I1, U, and J). For example, the fact that humans can learn XOR types of category learning problems  (see, e.g., Nosofsky, 1989) forces us to conclude that a nonlinear transformation is involved at some point in the system, but  we do not know where. Is a nonlinear encoding of the physically measured stimuli sufficient (a nonlinear V, see, e.g., Gluck,  Bower, & Hee, 1989) or is a nonlinear learning model required (a nonlinear VI; Rumelhart, Hinton, and Williams, 1986)?  The present results indicate that for simple prototype learning tasks, it feasible to assume that nonlinearity is restricted  to the encoding (V) and output (J) maps, and the internal dynamics are linear. Clearly, these conclusions are restricted to  tasks that can be optimally solved by linear systems. For the prototype production task used in the present experiment, the  optimal input-output model (the model that minimizes mean squared error) is given by (2) with w(t, k) = 1lt. Perhaps nonlinear  dynamics play a more important role in tasks that require a nonlinear solution. However, this remains to be shown, and a lot  may be gained by seeing how far a linear learning model with nonlinear input-output maps can take us, since it is easier to  analyze than nonlinear learning models.  Time variable learning systems. Much of the previous work with adaptive network learning models has been based on the  assumption that the learning rate is fixed. There are at least two good reasons for this assumption. One is parsimony-it  avoids the problem of specifying how the learning rate varies as a function of training. Perhaps a more important reason is  that the network remains responsive to the environment for an indefinite amount of training. Although this may not be a  desirable feature for stationary environments (which probably only exists in artificial contexts), this may be a desirable  feature in a nonstationary environment (which is probably more common in natural environments).  In a stationary environment, a decreasing learning rate is needed to filter out  environmental noise and to stabilize the state vector. In fact, for the present task, an optimal learning model is given by  (1) with co(t) =1/t. More generally, convergence theorems used in stochastic approximation theory normally require the  learning rate to decrease with training (see Ljung and Soderstrom, 1985, chap. 4).  The problem is that if the learning rate decreases, but the environment suddenly changes, then the system will be sluggish in  responding to the change. One way around this problem is to build a mechanism for detecting change into the model, and adjust  the learning rate after a change has been detected (Robinson, 1964). But even this modification runs into trouble when the  environment changes gradually over time rather than in occasional spurts.  In the present case, the empirical facts indicate that the learning system is time variable, and the learning rate tends to  decrease with experience. However, the pattern of the time-invariance violation is complex and none of the previous linear  learning models provide a completely satisfactory account of the serial position effects that have now been obtained in three  separate studies (Fig. 1 of Busemeyer and Myung, 1988; Fig. 3 of Weiss and Anderson, 1969; and Fig. 6 of the present study).  One direct consequence of the above facts is that previous theoretical accounts of category and prototype learning (e.g.,  Hintzman, 1986; Knapp and Anderson, 1984; Metcalfe-Eich, 1982) are called into question. The simulations reported in this  earlier work were all based on the untenable assumption of no serial position effects, and these models need to be reassessed  using learning assumptions that are more consistent with the facts.  Utility of measurement free methods for testing dynamic systems. Much of the past research by psychologists on connectionistic  models has relied heavily on computer simulation. While this is often necessary due to the complexity of the subject matter,  these tests tend to be highly specific to the particular architecture and parameters that form the basis for the simulation.  If the model, as a whole, fails to fit the data, it is difficult to identify the specific reasons for the failure. The present  experiment is an example of an alternative approach, based on ideas borrowed from axiomatic measurement theory (for another  recent example, see Hubner, 1990). The main advantage of this alternative approach is that fairly general tests can be  conducted separately for each basic assumption, and violations directly point to the basic assumption that is at fault, thus  providing better guidance for model revision than a general lack of fit test of the model as a whole.
