JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 25, 51-81 (1982)  A Factorization Procedure for Finite Algebras  PHILIPPA E. PATTISON AND WARREN K. BARTLETT  University of Melbourne  A procedure is developed for decomposing any finite algebra into a minimal set of maximally independent simple homomorphic  images, or factors, of the algebra. The definition of admissible sets of factors is made in relation to the congruence lattice  of the algebra, and generalises the notion of an irredundant reduction in a modular lattice. An algorithm for determining all  possible sets of factors of a given finite algebra is derived and an index for measuring the degree of independence of factors  is defined. Applications of the technique to finite algebraic models within the social psychological domain are presented and  include factorizations for certain semigroups of binary relations and for a class of finite semilattices.  1. INTRODUCTION  The construction of discrete and algebraic models in psychology and related disciplines has been undertaken cautiously but, in  general, with a persuasive rationale (for example, Atkin, 1972; Boorman & White, 1976; Boyd, 1969; Friedell, 1967; Ginsburg &  Partee, 1969; Hamburger & Wexler, 1975; Hoffman, 1966, 1976; Lorrain, 1975; Lowe, 1969). The frequency of use of such models  is low, however, even though the arguments proposed to justify them often seem to apply outside the specific contexts in which  they were introduced (for instance, Arabie, Boorman & Levitt, 1978; Cunningham, 1978; Lorrain, 1975; Shepard & Arabie, 1979).  One reason for this reluctance to use discrete and algebraic models for data of psychological interest may be the difficulty  in manipulating algebraic structures. In contrast to models with familiar and computationally convenient metric structures,  algebraic models generally possess less familiar properties and rely on complex and, at times, ad hoc, principles of  calculation. The purpose of the present work was to develop a general analytic procedure for finite algebraic structures, and  so to extend the potential applicability of algebraic representations to psychological data.  One of the most useful features of representations involving multidimensional real spaces is that any such space may be  provided with a co-ordinate system reflecting an analysis of the space into a number of orthogonal real components. The  analysis of a finite algebra proposed in the present work is intended to bear a partial resemblance to this resolution of a  multidimensional real space into orthogonal unidimensional subspaces.  The anticipated correspondence between the two analyses is only partial, however, for two major reasons. First of all, it is  deemed useful to relax the requirement of strict mutual orthogonality of components and to emphasise, instead, their  simplicity, or irreducibility, as a defining feature. Secondly, the more general algebraic structures considered here need not  possess the inherent symmetry of real vector spaces, with the result that the choice of a co-ordinate system may not be so  freely made. Thus the proposed analysis has the property of yielding, for a given algebra, a collection of irreducible  components, which is often unique and whose members are maximally, rather than absolutely, independent.  The particular algebraic structures in terms of which the analysis was originally developed are the semigroup algebras  generated by collections of square binary matrices under Boolean multiplication (Lorrain & White, 1971; Boorman & White, 1976;  Kim & Roush, 1978). Such structures have been proposed to represent relational structure in systems of multiple social  networks and so to assist the description and comparison of different multirelational social systems (Arabie et al., 1978). A  proposition directing the analytic procedure developed below is that the usefulness of algebraic models of this kind depends  not only upon their representational faithfulness, but also upon the extent to which features of the often complex  representation which they construct are recognizable and permit comparison with representations of other systems of the same  kind. That is, it is contended that the value of the models is in part a function of the extent to which they submit to  algebraic "calculation."  The proposed decomposition procedure is elaborated for a general finite algebra. The finiteness restriction was imposed since  the decomposition algorithm was originally developed for application to finite semigroup algebras. In principle, however, the  procedures may be extended to the infinite case.  In what follows, definitions of universal algebraic and lattice-theoretic terms have been adapted from those of Kurosh (1963)  and Birkhoff (1967). In general, the collection of factorizations of an algebra is claimed to define a particularly useful class of subdirect  representations of the algebra. Any factorizing set of an algebra is sufficient to describe all of its structural content;  moreover, the algebraic conditions of the factorization definition provide an operation alisation for the intuitively useful  requirement that the factors be maximally independent of one another. (The factors are defined so as to be as simple as  possible, and so as independently realizable as possible.) Hence the structural complexity implicit in a full algebraic  representation, such as in the multiplication table of some semigroup, may be resolved by identifying collections of  structurally simpler components of the algebra which are known to combine in reasonably independent ways to produce the  original algebra. Such collections permit the structural content of the algebra to be manipulated, in a manageable way. Thus,  the identification of all factorizations of an algebra is put forward as a first step in reducing, and so dealing with, the  possible complexity of an algebraic description.  Like direct and subdirect representations of an algebra, the existence and form of factorizations are determined by its  congruence lattice. However, as for direct and subdirect representations also, the definition of a factorization is of the  existence type. C. Representing a factorization: The Reduction Diagram  The factors of any factorization of an algebra correspond to congruence relations which are irreducible. That is, each factor  of a factorization is subdirectly irreducible and possesses a unique (nontrivial) maximal homomorphic image. That maximal  image is either factorizable, or is itself subdirectly irreducible and, in the latter case, has a unique maximal homomorphic  image. One may systematically find the unique images of all factors appearing in a factorization, their factorizations or  unique images, and so on. The procedure results in a subset of the congruence lattice, O(A), and, together with the partial  ordering among its elements, it summarizes the structure of A. The diagram illustrating this summary statement of the  structure of 0(A) and hence of A is called a reduction diagram and the following conventions are adopted in its presentation:  (i) The elements of the reduction diagram of an algebra A are selected members of the lattice of homomorphic images of A, and  include A itself.  (ii) A single arrow directed from an element of the reduction diagram represents a mapping of that element onto a unique  maximal (nontrivial) homomorphic image. Other Decomposition Techniques  Factorizations of an algebra have been defined as maximally non-redundant subdirect decompositions and, as such, select a  useful decomposition solution from a potentially large number of such solutions. Other forms of decomposition are possible,  however, and indeed have been used to advantage in algebraic analyses of social, psychological and biological phenomena (Boyd,  1969; Boyd, Haehl, & Sailer, 1972; Krohn, Langer, & Rhodes, 1967; Rhodes, 1971).  Particular use has been made in these other applications of semidirect and Wreathproduct decompositions wherein component  structures are combined in certain nonindependent and serial manners. It has been argued by Pattison (1980) that these methods  are not of greatest general use in the analysis of semigroups of multiple, social networks. They have, however, proved useful  in some special applications, especially in the context of structural developmental hypotheses (Boyd, 1969). In such cases,  factorization may not assist the identification of useful components, although alternative decomposition procedures may be  brought to bear on the subdirectly irreducible components. More generally, though, care must be taken to seek specifically  other possible resolutions of a structure, where they may be of interest.  B. The role of Factorization in Algebraic Analyses  As the illustrative applications demonstrate, the factorization technique yields useful resolutions of an algebra into a  number of structurally simple components. In the case of the semigroups of binary relations to which the technique was  applied, the problem of "interpreting" the relational structure implicit in the complex semigroup of a relational system has  been transformed into that of "interpreting" relational structure of the simpler factors of that semigroup. The interpretation  of those simpler factors, however, raises additional questions. In general, where algebraic models are secondary abstrations  drawn from some particular data type (as they often are; for example, Boorman & White, 1976; Boyd, 1969; Mandel, 1978), the  question of the relationship between features of the abstract representation and the data from which it is generated, is  bypassed in the algebraic analysis. It is proposed by Pattison (1982), however, that it is precisely a technique of the kind  described which identifies the primary structural features as recognizable types and allows the question of the relation  between the abstract algebraic representation and the data from which it is derived to be efficiently addressed.
