In recent years, there has been an increasing interest in computerized learning games ( Ketamo, 2003; Lyytinen, Ronimus, Alanko, Poikkeus, & Taanila, 2007; Manske & Conati, 2005; Wilson et al., 2006 ). In these games, the driving principle for optimizing learning appears to be that if the number of mistakes is too high (or if it takes the student too long to complete tasks), the learning material is too difficult, and if the number of mistakes is very low (or it takes very little time for the student to complete tasks), the learning material is too easy ( Ketamo, 2003; Ketamo & Multisilta, 2003 ).

Thus, the adaptation logic typically follows some variation of an ad hoc algorithm that increases the difficulty of the learning tasks after correct answers and decreases it after incorrect answers. The algorithm is tuned so as to yield around 75%, 80%, or some such percentage of correct answers. While this simple logic may work reasonably well when the learning material has only one adaptable dimension of difficulty, it runs into trouble when the learning material is more complicated.

Wilson et al. (2006)  model multiple dimensions of difficulty of the learning material by applying ad hoc formulas to update estimates of the student’s success probability over the “learning space” with these dimensions. The adaptation logic aims at keeping the success probability around 75%. Although this is a welcome multidimensional generalization, the algorithm is relatively complicated with many tuning constants and is not based on a statistical model; the only explicit principle underlying the adaptation logic is still a certain prescribed success rate (even though the algorithm may implicitly implement other principles).

An average success rate around certain numbers may be necessary for efficient learning, but it is certainly not sufficient; for example, alternating exceedingly easy and exceedingly difficult tasks in 3:1 proportion, or always giving a multiple-choice task with three correct choices within four identical (unmarked) options, will yield a 75% success rate, but these tasks are clearly suboptimal for learning. To avoid such pathologies, the adaptation principles should be based on a full-blown statistical model of the student’s skills as well as the task rather than on simple estimates of success probability or response times which may not be sufficient indicators of the induced learning results.

Principled Bayesian student models are already becoming prevalent in the field (e.g.,  Manske & Conati, 2005 ), but they have mostly been used for offering individualized support for the student rather than actively choosing the most effective learning material. An interesting approach by  Stacey, Sonenberg, Nicholson, Boneh, and Steinle (2003)  suggests the use of cognitive conflict to facilitate learning, that is, choosing learning material that the student is likely to get wrong due to misconceptions. However, there is an obvious conflict with keeping the student motivated and therefore it is not immediately clear how this approach would generalize the simple principle of a certain average success rate.

In this paper, we propose general principles of adaptation that are applicable in any Bayesian student model. In particular, instead of assuming arbitrary constraints on the success rate of the student, we consider how to explicitly model the motivational cost of failures. Our goal is to present a proof-of-concept implementation of our proposed adaptation principles and to demonstrate how it provides a smooth learning experience for the student in practice. However, an objective comparison of the induced learning results to those of other adaptation methods is left for future work.

This paper is structured as follows. First, we describe Bayesian adaptive estimation theory and propose our principles of adaptation as an extension of it. Then, we present a Rasch-like example model and apply the principles to it using some novel computational techniques. After that, we evaluate the resulting algorithm using simulations and results from a pilot study with a real computerized learning game and real students. Finally, we present a generalization of the model for multidimensional skill of the student and difficulty of the learning tasks. We conclude with a discussion of the philosophy behind our proposed principles.

The ad hoc adaptation rules generally used in adaptive teaching systems are analogous to the staircase method of measurement (see  Treutwein, 1995 ), in which a sensory detection threshold is estimated by increasing the stimulus intensity after each correctly detected stimulus and decreasing it after each undetected stimulus. The staircase method is not very efficient for measurement, often wasting several trials before reaching the overall scale of the threshold and then moving randomly around the optimal measurement points.

Bayesian adaptive estimation can be much more efficient, as it can efficiently use the information provided by all earlier trials even when the information is too scarce to obtain meaningful estimates of the parameters. In measurement, the adaptation’s only goal is to optimize the measurement efficiency. In a teaching system, there can be several goals, but the most important is the optimization of the learning result.

Adaptive Bayesian methodology can be used to quickly find the desired difficulty level for the learning material in a manner similar to a binary search. The general framework automatically allows more than one “dimension of difficulty” of the content to be adapted simultaneously, making it relatively easy to enforce a certain success rate after enough information has been accumulated. However, whether a specific success rate should be aimed for, or if there is some more important principle for optimal learning, is open to question.

In fact, we emphasize the auxiliary goal of good measurement efficiency instead. Information about student’s skills is valuable for analysis and feedback, and at least some measurement information is necessary for optimization of the learning effects anyway. We assume that the trial contents that are good for measurement, which is highly desirable in its own right, would also be among those that are efficient for learning.

Indeed, in many models optimization of the expected information gain  (4)  of each trial appears to naturally avoid trial contents that are extremely demanding or extremely easy as the model can predict the results of such trials to be correct or incorrect and so the actual answers would yield little new information. This algorithm can be seen as a formalization of some aspects of the cognitive conflict approach pursued by  Stacey et al. (2003) : material that best differentiates between alternative hypotheses about the student’s conceptions is preferred.

However, there is one obvious flaw in pure optimization of the information gain as a general principle: the trial contents may be too difficult to keep students motivated, even though these contents might still be efficient for learning if the student would keep playing. In the following subsection, we propose a principled solution.

According to classical psychological theories of motivation, failure feedback lowers the learner’s outcome expectancies which in turn may induce avoidance toward the task (for more details, see, e.g.,  Förster, Grant, Idson, & Higgins, 2001 , and the references therein). Therefore, the more mistakes there are, the less motivated the student may become. In order to maintain motivation in a computerized teaching system, we propose the following adjustment of the pure measurement goal: instead of simply optimizing the expected information gain of each trial, we optimize the expected gain divided by the expected cost measured as the estimated probability of an incorrect answer. In other words, we try to obtain as much measurement information as possible for each failure of the student. Thus, instead of maximizing

However, a realistic model is bound to assume some probability of careless mistakes and therefore, even with this very simple definition, the expected cost should never be zero.

All distractors are assumed to be equally discriminable from the target so that we can ignore the specific answer and just model its correctness.

This model is a variation of Bayesian Rasch models (e.g.,  Swaminathan & Gifford, 1982 ). However, our application is different from the typical uses of Rasch models in that we use informative priors for the categories and apply the model to each student independently; idiosyncratic deviations from the nominal difficulties

                                  k

Furthermore, our implementation is more flexible in that the link function can be arbitrary; in particular, the lower and upper asymptotes can differ from 0 and 1, respectively, which is needed for modeling guessing and careless mistakes (see  Fig. 2 ). This property of the implementation is very useful since the nontrivial asymptotes seem to foil most attempts at using the analytic properties of the specific shape (logistic, cumulative normal, Weibull, etc.) of the link function.

Thus, in addition to small changes to the skill and difficulties, we expect that the student’s skill may occasionally jump to any value. That way the model can keep up if the student happens to learn (or forget) outside the game. Although the skill generally tends to increase, we prefer to use a noninformative prior with zero mean for the change to avoid biasing the estimates when there are only few trials in a session. A more complicated model could estimate a separate speed-of-learning parameter for the student. The reason we allow also the difficulties to change in time is robustness — it is conceivable that in some cases the difficulty order of the categories can change as the student learns. These transition densities can be efficiently applied by using the FFT convolution.

This complete description of our implementation of the model serves to show that the inherent computational complexity of the proposed principles can be overcome in a practical way. We do not claim that the design choices of this specific model are in any way optimal; many of them are almost the first ones that came to mind. However, we do expect the Bayesian principles to be generally robust as long as the relevant qualitative features are captured by the model.

In this section, we evaluate the example implementation of our proposed principles of adaptation. First, we evaluate how the two variations of the algorithm, Learner-Friendly and MaxInfo, achieve their abstract goals, the optimization of the measurement efficiency per mistake, and per trial. Then, we assess their resulting “learner-friendliness” based on the observed success rate of the student and discuss the benefits over an ad hoc adaptation algorithm used previously in our real-world application. However, at this point we do not have any empirical data on how our assessment of “learner-friendliness” relates to motivation or learning results in reality.

As both variations of the algorithm look only one trial ahead (greedy optimization) they are only approximations to the truly optimal strategy under their assumptions. Therefore, we compared the true measurement efficiencies of both algorithms under various session lengths. The results shown in  Fig. 5 
                             confirm that the learner-friendly variation indeed yields better estimation efficiency per the number of mistakes of the student while the pure information maximization strategy is more efficient per the number of trials.

Another pilot version of the game has been used for teaching the same material but with a different adaptation logic. That version used roughly the same categories of content as the present version, but presented only trials of one category within one session. Within each session, the difficulty was controlled by adapting the number of choices according to the ad hoc rules discussed in Section  1 . The game advanced to the next category only after a certain low percentage of mistakes was reached in the current category. The resulting success rates from real students are shown in  Fig. 6 . The early categories were too easy for many students, which yielded the large probability mass near the 100% success rate. Also, for some students some categories were exceptionally difficult within their position in the ordering, which resulted in the long left tail of the distribution as the students struggled through these categories. The Bayesian principles avoid all these problems by anticipating deviations from the difficulty order of the categories and by freely moving between the categories in each session. However, at this point we do not have an objective comparison of the induced learning effects between the different adaptation principles.

Learning is a cumulative process and so the specific effects of a single learning trial are difficult to assess. Although models of the induced learning effects have been proposed for different settings (see, e.g.,  Atkinson & Crothers, 1964 ), their usefulness in the relatively complicated setting of our real-world application is uncertain. In particular, the interrelations of different units of the learning material may be too complicated to be accurately captured by existing models (see, e.g.,  Chant & Atkinson, 1973  for a simple model of interrelated learning material). For now, we have chosen not to model any explicit effect of the presentation of certain content; that is, the true state of the student in the future is not assumed explicitly to depend on the contents of the past trials. We do anticipate the possibility of changes in the state of the student, but we do not expect that exposures to certain content would automatically trigger certain changes. Instead, we have adopted a less explicit but more easily quantifiable view on the induced learning effects.

Analogous to the fact that measurements of a physical system can alter its state, measurements of a student’s skills can alter her state. We assume that conducting the measurements where there is the most uncertainty about the student’s current skills would yield the best chances of the student learning. For example, given a knowledge space structure ( Doignon & Falmagne, 1985 ) for the learning material, if the prerequisites of a certain task have not been learned, there is little uncertainty about the task’s result and so its measurement would be useless; similarly, if a certain task is already mastered, there is little uncertainty about its prerequisites and so their measurement would be useless. This reasoning leads to our proposed principle of optimizing the measurement efficiency as a means of optimizing the learning results. Of course, any other quantification of the induced learning effects could be used equally well in the Bayesian framework.

Apart from suitable learning material, another important factor for learning is motivation. This is especially important for children who may develop avoidance towards challenging tasks due to too many failures ( Onatsu-Arvilommi & Nurmi, 2000; Ziegert, Kistner, Castro, & Robertson, 2001 ). Assuming that the chances of learning are proportional to the amount of measurement information given by the results of the learning tasks, and inversely proportional to the number of failures of the student, the mathematically optimal teaching strategy is to optimize the information gain divided by the number of failures.

This formulation exemplifies the philosophy behind our approach: instead of enforcing ad hoc constraints on content selection, we aim at modeling the phenomena behind the constraints so that the mathematical solution to the optimization problem yields the desired behavior without any post hoc adjustments. In our example application, instead of taking the high success rate of the student as an external constraint, our learner-friendly formulation derives it from the assumed cost of failures.

This principle of learner-friendly adaptation is general, applicable in any Bayesian model having the notion of a success or failure of the student (or other suitable definition of a cost) in each learning task. The example model we presented illustrates that the proposed principle can be expected to work generally in a sensible way without any tuning parameters, simply by following its implicit internal logic. It should be noted, however, that if the student model does not adequately capture the structure of the learning material, then the proposed principle cannot be expected to work very well. For example, if there is no prior information about the relative difficulties of different tasks, then the model has no way of knowing which ones might be suitable for the student without trying all of them in an arbitrary order until one is found. Thus, even though the principles themselves are completely general, they do not obviate the need to carefully develop an appropriate student model for the specific problem.

The example student model presented here has certain shortcomings. In particular, it only uses the correctness of the answers, assuming that all items within a given category are equally confusable to each other. In several aspects, it is the simplest model that could possibly work for this learning material. For work on more detailed models of the “Literate” game task, see ( Vilenius, Kujala, Richardson, Lyytinen, & Okamoto, 2007; Kujala, Richardson, & Lyytinen, 2008 ).
