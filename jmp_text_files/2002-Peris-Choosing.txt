Choosing among Maximals 

Josep E. Peris and Begon~ a Subiza 

University of Alicante, Alicante, Spain 

In a choice situation, it is usually assumed that the agents select the maximal elements. Nevertheless, there are situations in which the number of maximal elements is large, and a selection inside the maximal set is then required. This selection may be done in a random way or, as we propose in this work, by obtaining additional information from the preference relation that may provide a way of discriminating among the maximal elements and selecting the ``best'' ones. � 2001 Elsevier Science (USA) 

1. INTRODUCTION 

In choice theory it is usually assumed that preferences can be translated into choices and the way to do it consists of choosing the maximal elements. But, in many cases, a selection within the maximal set is required. For instance, there may be complete indifference about several items on the menu in a restaurant, but only one of them will finally be selected for dinner. 

The selection is sometimes done in a random way. In many cases, however, it is possible to use original preferences to obtain additional information that may provide a way of selecting within the maximal set, which is the case that interests us. Consider the following example (see Luce, 1956): An individual prefers a cup of coffee with 20 g of sugar ``approximately'' (because of a lack of sensitivity, he is unable to distinguish between two cups with a difference of 2 g of sugar). If there are six cups of coffee containing 20, 19, ..., 15 g of sugar, respectively, the maximal set is given by the first three possibilities, but these maximal elements are not ``identical'': they are physically different, and, in terms of preferences, the first one, with 20 g is preferred to the other three possibilities, whereas the maximal element with 18 g is only preferred to one of the possible alternatives.

4. MAJORITY MAXIMALS 

Total (linear) orders can be considered as the ``ideal'' preferences for an agent, since in such a case he would be able to completely discriminate between any pair of alternatives and choose just one of them from the feasible set A. But the lack of sensitivity, or the impossibility of keeping every option in mind, leads to weaker classes of preferences. When dealing with partial orders, a possible interpretation of such a preference would be to assume that the agent reveals strict preferences, but he only does so for some pairs of alternatives. What should the ideal preference (order) of the agent be? In general, it is possible to find different orders that could be considered as the origin of a preference relation. The notion of the compatible order formalizes this idea (see, for instance, Trotter, 1992, where compatible total orders are called linear extensions of a partial order P).
