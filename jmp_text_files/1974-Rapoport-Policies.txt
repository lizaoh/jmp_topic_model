When the task  commences, a decision maker (DM) is provided with some capital x (x > 0) which he is required to allocate over m (m > 1)  mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive alternatives, each of which occurs with probability p; (p: > 0, i = 1,..., m;  Em 1 pi = 1). The task  consists of N stages.  Defining risk in terms of the mean and variance of DM's bets, and assuming that the minimization of risk is DM's objective,  decision policies satisfying this objective are derived in closed form and their testable properties are briefly discussed.  In 1956 J. L. Kelly, Jr. of Bell Telephone Laboratories published a paper which attempted to attach a "value measure to a  communication system in which errors are being made at a non-negligible rate" (Kelly, 1956, pp. 917-918). Constituting one of  the earliest attempts to draw a connection between economic investment problems and information theory, Kelly's stimulating  paper has immediately attracted the attention of economists interested in providing economic interpretations to Shannon's  measure of information (see, for example, Arrow, 1971, Chap. 12; Marshack, 1959, pp. 92-95; Murphy, 1965). The reinvestment  feature of Kelly's example has also attracted the attention of mathematicians (e.g., Bellman and Kalaba, 1957, 1958), who  regarded it as a problem solvable by dynamic programming. Within psychology, Kelly's work was first referred to by Edwards  (1962), who realized its applicability to the study of human decision proccesses. Subsequently, Kelly's example and the work  of Bellman and Kalaba (1957, 1958) and Breiman (1961) led to the development of a research paradigm for studying gambling  behavior, the multistage betting game (MBG), which has been investigated both theoretically and experimentally by Rapoport  (1970), Rapoport and Jones (1970), Rapoport, Jones, and Kahan (1970), Rapoport and Stein (1972), and Gordon (1970).  1 This research was supported in part by PHS Research Grant No. MH-10006 from the National Institute of Mental Health and  University Science Development Program Grant No. GU-2059 from the National Science Foundation. At each stage (trial) of the game DM is required to allocate all of his  capital over the m alternatives, betting the amount yi on alternative i (yi > 0, Y7 n, yi = x). If alternative i obtains on a  given stage, DM's new capital for the next stage is yiri , where ri (ri > 0) is the return per unit bet on alternative i. It  is assumed that MBG is played for N stages (N > 1), that N is known by the DM, and that pi and ri are stationary. Without loss  of generality, assume that the m alternatives are ordered by the size of piri , such that p1rl > Y2r2 > *, * > P rm > 0, and  call alternative i the ith most favorable alternative.  The multistage betting game resembles the problem of portfolio selection, which has been studied extensively by economists  (e.g., Bickel, 1969; Markowitz, 1959; Hester and Tobin, 1967). In the portfolio problem, DM is assumed to have some finite  amount of capital which may change over time, and, in addition, he is assumed to allocate all of his capital over the m  securities (alternatives) on each stage of the process. The main differences between the MBG and the portfolio problem are  that only one alternative obtains on each stage in the MBG and the returns are assumed fixed, whereas all m alternatives  obtain on each stage in the portfolio problem, the returns are stochastic variables, and, typically, the distributions of the  returns are assumed to be correlated. Since the MBG problem has more structure than the general portfolio problem, one may  derive explicit solutions for betting policies maximizing various objective criteria rather than mere computational  techniques, as in the portfolio selection problem.  Models for Uncertain Outcomes  Two classes of models have been proposed for portfolio selection, namely, models assuming the maximization of expected  utility, and models that assume the minimization of risk, where risk is defined either in terms of certain properties of the  utility function (e.g., Arrow, 1971; Pratt, 1964) or in terms of certain properties of the distribution of returns (e.g.,  Markowitz, 1959; Tobin, 1958, 1965). Economists preferring the latter definition have proposed that the risk of a gamble with  monetary consequences is expressable in terms of the expected value and variance of the distribution of money, and that DM  chooses among alternative gambles so as to minimize risk. The main advantages of this proposal are its ease of applicability  even when m is large, the tractability of the variance as a measure of dispersion, specifically, the computability of the  variance of returns of a compound portfolio from the variances and correlations of the returns on the constituent portfolios,  and the compatibility of the definition of risk with expected utility theory when the utility function is quadratic.  Psychologists, who have been primarily interested in descriptive rather than  44 RAPOPORT AND STEIN  normative aspects of decision making, have also developed and tested these two classes of models. The simplest expectation  model assumes maximization of expected value; its failure as a descriptive theory has led to two major modifications, namely,  the independent transformations of money and objective probability into nonlinear utility and subjective probability,  respectively (Edwards, 1961; Luce and Suppes, 1965). About the same time that economists incorporated the concept of risk into  the theory of portfolio selection, several psychologists became aware that theories of individual decision making avoided this  concept despite its obvious psychological appeal. Coombs and his associates (Coombs, 1964; Coombs and Pruitt, 1960; Pruitt,  1962) proposed an alternative direction for the study of individual decision making based on the subjective notions of  riskiness and ideal risk level and explored the variables affecting the perception of riskiness of simple one-stage  two-outcome gambles and the relationships between the perceived riskiness of gambles and preferences among them. The evidence  that they collected "supports the hypothesis that variance is a strong correlate of risk in simple game situations, but in  more complex situations, the concept of risk is elusive and idiosyncratic" [Coombs and Meyer, 1966, p. 514]. For this and  other reasons, Coombs and his students (Coombs and Meyer, 1969; Coombs and Huang, 1970) have recently proposed and tested a  theory of risk preference, the so-called Portfolio Theory, which is "intrinsically independent of the definition of risk"  (Coombs and Huang, 1970, p. 317).  The expected value and variance have been chosen by economists as the two major components of risk primarily because of their  technical advantages and intuitive appeal. Pollatsek and Tversky (1970), pursuing a rigorous axiomatic approach, first showed  how the theory of extensive measurement (Krantz, Luce, Suppes, and Tversky, 1971) can be applied to the measurement of risk.  Suppose that A denotes a set of probability distributions over the real line, interpreted as lotteries. Pollatsek and Tversky  showed that if suitable axioms are satisfied, then there exists a real-valued function R on A and a unique B, 0 < B < 1, such  that for all f E A, which have finite expectations and variances,  R(f) = 9V(f) -I-- (1 - B) E(f),  where E(f) and V(f) denote the expectation and variance of the distribution f, respectively, and R is interpreted as its risk.  Thus, under certain axioms, the empirical adequacy of which has not been established, the risk of a lottery is expressable as  a linear combination of its mean and variance.  Following the work of Kelly (1956) and Bellman and Kalaba (1957, 1958), Rapoport and Jones (1970) and Rapoport et al. (1970)  presented and experimentally tested several expectation models for MBG, differing from one another in the assumptions made  about the form of the utility function. One of their expectation models, which assumes a logarithmic utility function, has  provided a first approximation to the mean betting data. Being a parameter-free model, however, it cannot account for  individual  MULTISTAGE BETTING GAMES 45  differences in betting behavior that have been shown to be quite substantial in subsequent studies (Gordon, 1970; Rapoport,  1970). Moreover, since assessment of utility functions is task dependent, the primary assumption of the logarithmic utility  model has remained untestable. To alleviate these problems, and to extend the theory of MBG to include the notion of risk, the  present paper proposes minimization of risk models where, following the axiomatic work of Pollatsek and Tversky, the risk of a  gamble is defined by a linear combination of its expected value and variance.' Two different cases will be considered,  depending on whether or not the expected value of DM's gamble is constrained. As will be shown below, these two cases result  in markedly different decision policies. Discussion  The betting policy derived above for the N-stage MBG has several desirable properties. First and most importantly,  minimization of the variance of the final capital subject to the constraints of Theorem 3 yields a proportional betting  policy, i.e., the proportion of capital bet on alternative i on stage v, yi*.,I xv , is independent of (i) the duration of the  multistage problem, N, (ii) the history of winning and losing in the problem, and (iii) the amount of capital available to DM  at the beginning of stage v, x. Proportional betting policies for MBG have been derived previously (Rapoport and Jones,  1970; Rapoport et al. 1970), assuming maximization of expected utility, where the utility was assumed to be given by either a  linear, logarithmic, or a power function. However, in all of these cases the proportion bet on the most likely alternative,  alternative 1, was limited from below by p, . This restriction does not hold when the variance of the gamble is minimized,  subject to the constraints of Theorem 3.  There is another testable property of the optimal betting policy. Lemma 2 together with Eq. (6) show that yi*, i = 1, 2,...,  j, is a piecewise linear function of k. Table 1 clearly demonstrates this linear relationship. This implication may be tested  with  52 RAPOPORT AND STEIN  individual or group data. Supposing that a subject participates in a sequence of independent MBG problems with the same values  of pi , ri , and m, but with possibly different values of N and x,. This research paradigm has been employed by all the MBG  studies conducted by Rapoport and his associates. The subject may adhere to the optimal model minimizing the variance within a  problem but change the value of his expectation level, k. The value of k for a particular problem may depend upon the size of  the subject's initial capital for this problem, the duration of the problem, or the amount of capital the subject has already  accumulated in the preceding problems. To test this implication, consider a yilx vs k two-dimensional plot, where ko < k <  k,,n_1 . Suppose a subject bets optimally within a problem but changes k from one problem to another. Then the problem means  of the proportions of capital bet on alternative i, (1 IN) YN 1 yi.vl xv , should fall on a piecewise linear function in this  plot. Similarly, consider a group of subjects playing the same MBG problem. Since attitudes to risk presumably vary from one  individual to another, different values of k may characterize the subjects. If all the subjects attempt to minimize the  variance of their final capital subject to the constraints of Theorem 3, the means of their proportions bet on a given  alternative i should also fall on a piecewise linear line in a yilx vs k plot.  Although the minimization of variance model has not been tested rigorously, recent MBG experiments (see, in particular,  Gordon, 1970, and Rapoport, 1970) have shown many subjects for whom yi,xdecreases as xincreases. These data violate the  expectation models and the minimization of variance model which result in proportional betting policies. To account for such  data, models yielding nonproportional betting policies are required. Such a model is presented in the immediately following  section.
