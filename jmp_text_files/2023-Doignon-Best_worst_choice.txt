Finn and Louviere (1992)  introduced the best-worst choice (BWC) paradigm, according to which the subject, when presented with a subset of the whole domain of alternatives, is asked to produce her best and her worst alternative in the subset (for a detailed report of the origins and early motivations of the model, see  Flynn & Marley, 2014 , or  Marley, Flynn, & Louviere, 2008 ). Many other papers also popularized the best-worse-choice paradigm, for instance  Marley and Flynn (2015) , and  Marley and Pihlens (2012) ; for a survey with the description of several applications, see the monograph ( Louviere, Flynn, & Marley, 2015 ).

To account for the variations in the observed choices,   Flynn and Marley (2014), Marley (2009)  and  Marley and Louviere (2005)  among others propose various probabilistic models. We are interested here in the “best-worst random ranking model” defined in  Marley and Louviere (2005)  (renamed “linear ordering model of best-worst choice probabilities” in Definition 7.21 of  Marley & Regenwetter, 2017 ). In Section  2 , we repeat a precise definition of the model, to which we simply refer as the  best-worst-choice model  (BWCM).

In a DIMACS meeting in May 24–26, 2006, Tony Marley presented to Samuel Fiorini, Mike Regenwetter, Reinhard Suck, and the author the problem of characterizing the prediction range of the BWCM. For each number  
                         n 
                       of alternatives, the prediction range is a certain (convex) polytope ( Grünbaum, 2003 ), the  best-worst-choice convex polytope  (BWCP); an affine description of the BWCP (by affine equalities and inequalities) would deliver at once a characterization of the model (that is, of the probabilities predicted by the model). The five-researcher team worked on the characterization problem from time to time over the years. Tony pushed for the publication of our partial results, but completion of a manuscript never occurred (our working notes were quoted by  Marley & Regenwetter, 2017 , and by  Colonius, 2021 ). As far as we know, an affine description of the polytope is still lacking in general; let us call the search for such a description the  Marley problem .
