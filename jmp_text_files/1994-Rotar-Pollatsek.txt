INTRODUCTION 

This paper offers remarks about, and a generalization of, the Pollatsek and Tversky (1970) theorem on a measure of risk: sec also Krantz, Luce, Suppes, & Tversky (1971, Chap. 3). (The papers are abbreviated below as PT and KLST, respectively.) In this paper we do not touch on a related (and al present more developed) branch of theory that concerns preferences rather than risk on a space of monetary gambles; some results and references may be found in Fishburn (1988) and Wakker (1989). 

The central construct of PT is a binary relation of comparative risk which satisfics two groups of conditions. The first consists of the monotonicity (or independence with respect to convolution) axiom and an Archimedean condition. These axioms ensure the existence of a functional which, being specified on a set of probability distributions, preserves the relation under consideration and is additive with respect to convolution. 

The second and more specific group of axioms implies that the value of the above functional for a gamble is a liunear combination of its mean and variance. 

The proof given by PT is based on a central limit theorem of probability theory. In our opinion, the approach taken in PT is very resourceful. Aithough the possibility of a normal approximation for sums of independent random variables is

On the other hand, by taking into account only the means and variances (which may be regarded as the crudest characteristics of distributions), the resulting criterion is not very flexible. This limitation was the source of criticism in later papers where, in particular, the PT measure of risk was shown not to agree with some empirical evidence; see, e.g., Coombs & Bowen (1971) and Keller, Sarin, & Weber (1986). 

It thus became clear that other non-moment factors appear to influence perceived risk, and models that integrate these factors into a measure of risk were proposed. It is worth noting here the papers of Luce (1980, 1981), Fishburn (1982, 1984), and Luce & Weber (1986). 

Here, as in PT, we restrict our attention to the criteria that are additive with respect to convolution. 

A crucial point in PT seems to be the scalar monotonicity axiom (for a definition, see below) that requires the rule of comparison to be, in some sense, the same in domains of “large” and “small” values of random variables. This seems unduly restrictive. We therefore weaken the scalar monotonicity axiom or replace it with another one in order to come to a more flexible form of the additive criterion. As is shown below, in view of the additivity property it may be and, under some conditions, must be a finite linear combination of cumulants of higher orders. We arrive at this form in two different ways. The first consists of a direct generalization of the PT scalar monotonicity axiom. The second appeals to a continuity condition that is stronger than the usual one. More precisely, when defining a continuity property for a functional, we pay attention to the behavior of the “tails” of the distributions, that is, the probabilities of large deviations. Afterward, we discuss some properties of the additive criteria and the independence axiom.

4. A REMARK ON THE INDEPENDENCE AXIOM 

We next give a simple example which demonstrates that we should be cautious about invoking the independence axiom, Axiom 1, even if we are ready, in principal, to accept it (so to speak, if we “believe in additivity”). In other words, when accepting this axiom, we somewhat restrict either a possible class of distributions or the possibility to invoke certain additional requirements. 

Consider the following axioms. 

Axiom A (A Weak Version of Independence). F>G=>FoF>GoG. 

Axiom B. For any non-degenerate r.v. X, there exists a (sufficiently large) number A such that ¥> A (A is less risky than X). 

Axiom C. If {X,}, {Y,} are two sequences of r.v.’s such that (X,/Y,) > +00 in probability, then Y, > X,, for sufficiently large n. 

The last two axioms do not iook unnatural in light of the rule “more means less risky” but, of course, they are not beyond dispute. Theoretically, it is possible to assume that there exists an amount A which is less risky than a given gamble X. However, it might be that for some people this amount of money is more than all the money in the world. Our goal, however, is not to discuss Axioms B, C but to show that the above three axioms are not always compatible.
