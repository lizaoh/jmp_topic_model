JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY: 4, 501-506 (1967)  Theoretical Note: A Fixed-Point Model for Short-Term Memory'  BENNET B. MURDOCK, JR.  University of Toronto, Toronto, Ontario, Canada  Most current models for short-term memory assume at least two separate storage systems. A unitary model for paired associates  that seems capable of accounting for at least the gross empirical phenomena is proposed. Transitions both into and out of the  recall state are possible, and the fixed point of the transition matrix determines the asymptote of the short-term retention  curve. Experimental evidence is presented to document the contention that a steady state is achieved by practiced subjects  after a single presentation of a list of paired associates.  Most of the current models for short-term memory (Atkinson and Shiffrin, 1965; Bernbach, 1965; Bower, 1966; Broadbent, 1958;  Evans and Dallenbach, 1965; Kintsch and Morris, 1965; Peterson, 1966; Sperling, 1963; Waugh and Norman, 1965; Waugh and Smith,  1962) are multiprocess models which postulate at least two separate storage systems. Thus, there is primary memory and  secondary memory, short-term storage and long-term storage, a recognition state and a recall state; the names differ but the  dichotomy does not. The purpose of the present note is to suggest that this retreat from parsimony may be premature.  For paired associates, a model based on a single storage system is consonant with at least the main experimental findings. The  essential phenomena to be explained are: (a) a marked recency effect with little or no primacy effect; (b) a stable asymptote  greater than zero; (c) a responsiveness of the latter but not the former to experimental manipulation; and (d) associative  symmetry. The exact nature and magnitude of these effects will vary slightly from experiment to experiment, but these effects  are sufficiently consistent and reliable to be generally manifest (Murdock, 1963a; 1963b; 1966).  An extremely simple stochastic model is as follows: An association can be in one of two states, al or a2 . If in state al it  can be recalled, but in state a2 it cannot be recalled. We reconsider the basic phenomena enumerated above in light of this model.  (a) As is self-evident from Fig. 1, a very marked recency effect is obtained. According to the model there should be no  primacy effect whatsoever; no advantage should acrue to the first pair in a list. We have never yet obtained any primacy  effect whatever with paired associates, though other investigators have (e.g., Peterson and Peterson, 1962; Tulving and  Arbuckle, 1963).  (b) The model predicts an asymptote, and is clearly one of its key features. (c) Responsiveness to experimental manipulation merely requires that one or more of the parameters of the model co-vary with  treatment conditions. To give one example, in a recent study (Murdock, 1963a) it was shown that the asymptote of the retention  curve varied predictably with presentation rate. One can label a a forgetting parameter and P a reminiscence parameter, and to  accommodate the rate effect all that would be necessary would be to let a or g be rate-sensitive. While intuitively it seems  more reasonable to associate rate effects with the forgetting parameter than with the reminiscence parameter, the main point  is that the model is not strained by the data.  (d) The model is neutral with regard to associative symmetry since "recall of a pair" is not adequately defined here.  While there is abundant evidence for an asymptote in free recall (e.g., Murdock, 1962) the experimental evidence for paired  associates is considerably weaker (see Murdock, 1963a). PROCEDURE  This study was originally conducted as an investigation of the length-difficulty relationship of Thurstone (1930) with paired  associates composed of common English words. A study-test method was used wherein each trial consisted of one presentation of  each of the pairs in the list at a presentation time of 2 sec/pair followed by a self-paced test trial. On the test trial, the  A word of each A-B pair was presented as the cue for recall. List length ranged from 9-100 pairs in square-root steps (i.e.,  9, 16, 25,..., 100), and each list was learned to a criterion of one perfect trial. One list was learned per day; each of the  two subjects (undergraduate women students) learned a total of 40 lists, five blocks of eight each (i.e., 9, 16, 25,..., 100)  with order randomized within blocks.  RESULTS  The learning data gave strong support to the square-root function (see also Carroll and Burke, 1965), but here the chief  interest is in the first-trial recall. As argued elsewhere (Murdock, 1963a), given an asymptote of the short-term memory curve  then there should be a fixed probability (under these conditions, p = .25) of recalling any given pair. Alternately, the total  number of pairs recalled should be a linear function of list length and the slope should be .25.  For each subject the first block of eight lists was discarded because of marked practice effects. DISCUSSION  The experiment has shown that, for practiced (and well-motivated) subjects, the steady state predicted by the model (and  characteristic of short-term memory data in general) is manifest in the study-test procedure of paired-associate learning.  Recall probability following a single presentation of common English words paired at random and presented for 2 sec/pair is  very close to .25 even for as many as 100 pairs which encompass a single-trial interval of several minutes. These results do  not of course demonstrate that any particular pair changes back and forth in state; they are simply consistent with the  fixed-point aspect of the model.  The absence of output interference in the experiment would be expected regardless of list length since it is clear (Murdock,  1963c) that such interference depresses performance only when the very last pair or two in the list is tested in the first or  second output position. Neither the primacy effect nor the S-shaped recency effect of free recall (Murdock, 1962) are  explicable in terms of this model. However, free recall requires emptying the store which is quite different from probing for  one pair; also, the model is presently restricted to paired associates. As Fig. 1 shows, forgetting should be extremely rapid,  so in all but the shortest lists most of the pairs should be near the asymptotic value. However, with lists of two or three  pairs (Murdock, 1963a), the model does not fit well.  The essential feature of this model, and one which distinguishes it from other models, is that items which cannot be recalled  at one point in time may in fact be recalled at a later point in time. Experimental evidence from other studies has already  been cited; in addition it might be noted that Arbuckle (1964) found the "reminiscence" effect to be invariant with list  length and presentation rate. Finally, the model would predict that, in an RTT paradigm, there should be an increase in NC's  (conditional probability of correct on TZ given incorrect on Tl) as the (filled) 7'1-T., interval increased. Significant  increases in these conditional probabilities have actually been found in two separate studies (Peterson et al., 1962;  Arbuckle, 1964). In other words, the number of pairs that will be successfully recalled following an initial incorrect recall  should increase over time.  REFERENCES  ARBUCKLE, T. Y.
