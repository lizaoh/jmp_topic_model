Applied Psychology Unit, Cambridge, England  This paper is in three major sections. The first provides corrections and additions to existing work on the fitting of  receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curves by the method of maximum likelihood. The second compares the predictions of  normal and logistic models. The third examines the minimum-logit-chi-square method of parameter estimation in the ROC context.  INTRODUCTION  This paper is a sequel to the papers by Abrahamson and Levitt (1969), Dorfman and Alf (1968, 1969) and Ogilvie and Creelman  (1968) and in order for the paper to be reasonably self-contained there will necessarily occur a certain amount of duplication  of previous results. The paper deals mostly with rating-method data, the exception arising in the last section of the paper.  The signal-detection paradigm consists simply of a subject successively choosing between signal present (with background  noise), SN, or no signal present (just noise), N. The model now usually assumed is that the stimulus sets up a response within  the subject that can be represented as a continuous, unidimensional random variable X with probability distribution function,  FN(x) were the stimulus noise  and  Fs,(x) were the stimulus signal and noise. If the subject is required to respond just Yes (signal present) or No (no signal present) then the model assumes that he tests  for the underlying distribution by setting up a criterion (z, say) and responding Yes if the response X is, say, greater than  z and No if X is less than z. One then has,  P(Hit) - P(Yes/SN) = 1 - F(bz - a),  P(False alarm) .- P(Yes/N) = 1 - F(z).  Varying z gives the ROC curve [a plot of P(Hit) against P(False alarm)], defined by a and b, that is taken to represent the  subject's performance.  If the subject only responds Yes or No then it is readily shown that one has no degrees of freedom to estimate b and typically  one then assumes b = 1, i.e., that the two distributions have the same variance. In order to estimate b, the experiment must  be elaborated, the two standard means of doing so being either a repetition of the above with different z values (obtained by  manipulating a payoff matrix, say) or by requiring that the subject grade his response (for example, Yes, sure or No, fairly  sure). It is the second elaboration that is considered here: by analogy with the above the model then assumes n criteria, zk ,  k = 1, 2,..., n (in addition it is convenient to define z, = - oo and z,,+1 = + oc) so that the subject makes the overt  response Ri , say, if the latent response X falls in the interval:  zi_1 - X < zi , i = 1, 2,..., n +- 1. This greatly reduces the amount of basic algebraic manipulation  required since we no longer have to obtain explicit analytical expressions for a large number of first and second order  partial differential coefficients-it is worth noting that many maximization programs involve the numerical calculation of the  matrix of second order partial differential coefficients, at least at a late stage in the iterative process, so that inversion  of this matrix immediately_ supplies the covariance matrix of the parameter estimates.
