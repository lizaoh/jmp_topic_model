INTRODUCTION  It is often a "working" assumption that psychological space is Euclidean. In recent years theorists have developed scaling  techniques which have relaxed the Euclidean assumption (Shepard, 1962a, b, 1974; Kruskal, 1964a, b; Carroll and Chang, 1970;  Beals, Krantz, & Tversky, 1968; Guttman, 1968; Lingoes, 1965). However, virtually all of the research has concentrated on  Minkowski metrics. Riemannian spaces, a natural extension of Euclidean space, have not been studied (Shepard, 1974).  That such curved spaces have psychological importance has already been noted by Luneburg (1947, 1950) and Blank (1953, 1958,  1959; see also Foley, 1972) in the binocular metric for vision, and by Shepard's (1974) reference to "horseshoe-shaped" (i.e.,  elliptic) structures in some scaling solutions. In general the quadratic form determines  both the local and global geometric structure of a Riemannian space.  An important parameter of a Riemannian space is its curvature at each point. Basically, the Gaussian curvature at any point is  defined as the product of the maximum and minimum curvatures of all geodesics (curves of minimum distance) passing through  that point. The curvature of a curve, at a given point, is IL 1 /r, where r is the radius of the circle that best fits the  curve at that point.  For the purposes of the definition, we can regard the space (or a small portion of it) as an m-dimensional manifold  (hypersurface) S in an (m + 1)-dimensional space T. The tangent space at point X E S separates T into two regions. The  curvature of a geodesic through X is arbitrarily called positive if its center of curvature (i.e., the center of the  best-fitting circle) is in one region; it is negative if the center is in the other region.  Thus, the Gaussian curvature at a point may be either positive or negative. Points with negative curvature lie in  saddle-shapped regions of S. Figure 1 illustrates such a region. The curve running from the front to the back of the saddle  (left to right in Fig. 1) has its center of curvature at the top of the figure. We ask the question: What Riemannian space of constant curvature  best fits the observed distances ? Piezsko, in essence, asks: How can we best describe the errors in Euclidean scaling by  assuming Riemannian spaces of nonconstant curvature ?  The most serious error in Pieszko's (1975) model is in the direct use of a finite difference approximation to Eq. (1). That is:  42ij = 91111 4x111 + 922ii iii + 29121, dx1iiX2U  where dxki; = xki - xki and xki , xk; represent the projections of the points i, j onto the axis k. This assumes that the  infinitesimal generator of the metric (Eq. (1)) holds in a finite difference representation (Eq. (5)). The fallacy of this  assumption is particularly evident when large differences are employed in his examples. The point is that a Riemannian space  does not have a locally Euclidean metric, nor can the infinitesimal metric (Eq. (1)) be employed, per se, in any finite  difference algorithm to determine the curvature, geodesic structure, etc., of the space. A fundamental theorem of differential  geometry states that the infinitesimal metric determines the global metric (Lipschutz, 1969). However, it does not do so by  simple substitution of finite differences for infinitesimal differences.  Pieszko's error is similar to Fechner's (1889). Both argue incorrectly that a differential equation can represent finite  differences (Luce & Edwards, 1958). Fechner coincidentally obtained correct results; Pieszko does not. The relationship in Eq.  (4), though determined by Eq. (1), is not itself a quadratic function. In Eq. (4) the Riemannian distance in S is an arcsin  function of the "quasi-Euclidean" distance in T. A glance at a graph of the arcsin function shows that it cannot be  approximated by a quadratic function unless the argument is small. By including a reference to Luneberg's hyperbolic space, Pieszko implies that his method will apply to such spaces. In fact,  hyperbolic spaces cannot be isometrically embedded in Euclidean spaces of higher dimensionality (Guggenheimer, 1963, Willmore,  1959). The embedded manifold is one half of a double hyperboloid whose axis of rotation is the imaginary  dimension (Fig. 2).  Pieszko calculates cross products from the original distances and then factors them to obtain the coordinates of his points.  The errors introduced by this procedure are small if the space is relatively "flat," but they increase with increasing  curvature of the space. DISCUSSION  Basically, this paper describes an extension of Torgerson's scaling method to curved spaces of arbitrary dimensionality. A  number of possible extensions of the method have not yet been explored, but their exploration is straightforward.  The extension to nonmetric methods is simple. Techniques for minimizing squared errors while maintaining monotonicity with the  measured distances or similarity scores are well known (Shepard, 1962a, b; Kruskal, 1964a, b). Nonmetric methods can be  applied like metric methods, with the addition of such techniques. Moreover, the transformations in Eqs. (4) and (7) would no  longer be necessary, and J K I could be arbitrarily set to 1, because only a monotonic fit is required.  Generalization to a Minkowski metric in the embedding space (i.e., letting the 8i; be a Minkowski metric) is an intriguing  possibility, although it is likely that the sine transformations (Eqs. (4) and (7)) will no longer result in a metric for  exponents other than two.  The possibility of local minima has been explored extensively with both real and imaginary data; so far no problems with local  minima have arisen.
