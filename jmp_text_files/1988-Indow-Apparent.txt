PARALLEL AND EQUIDISTANT ALLEYS IN THE HORIZONTAL PLANE  Suppose that two series of stimulus points Qi are presented on the horizontal plane at eye level and their positions are  adjusted under binocular observation according to two different criteria. Let us call the configuration IQiI a parallel alley  when they are adjusted so as to appear as two straight lines which are symmetric to the median line and parallel with each  other. Let us call {Qi} an (equi-)distant alley when they are adjusted so that respective pairs of Qi, symmetric to the median  line, appear to be separated by the same lateral distance. These will be denoted by P- and D-alleys. When P- and D-alleys,  which share the farthest pair of points in common, are constructed, they do not give the same result and predominantly { Qi }  for D-alley lies outside { Qi } for P-alley.  The discrepancy between P- and D-alleys is in agreement with the observational facts. When a P-alley has been constructed, the  two series of points each appearing straight are neither converging nor diverging toward the subject, but lateral distances  between Q's in closer pairs appear shorter than the lateral distance in the farthest pair, if the subject is asked to compare  these distances. This observational fact was first noted by Hillebrand (1902, p.264) who dealt with P-alleys only. Two kinds of alleys were first  systematically explored by Blumenfeld (1913). The discrepancy may not exclude by itself the following possibility. These two  {Qr}'s give rise to, under two different observing conditions, one and the same perceptual pattern: parallel and equidistant.  When constructing a P-alley, it is natural for the subject to scan the series of Q's along its direction. On the other hand,  when a D-alley is constructed, the main direction of eye movement would be lateral. If how { Q; } appears to the subject  depends upon scanning direction, it would well be possible that the discrepancy between P- and D-alleys is due to the  difference in scanning direction. However, the same discrepancy is obtained when a P- or D-alley is constructed by two moving  points instead of a stationary configuration { Q;}. Under this condition, scanning direction can be controlled by the  direction of movement and it was demonstrated that the discrepancy between P- and D-alleys has nothing to do with the  difference in scanning direction. For example, even if two points moved in opposite directions along the respective sides of  an alley, e.g., on the left approaching and on the right moving away from the subject, the result was still the same (Indow &  Watanabe, 1984a). It seems very plausible that being parallel and being equidistant really do not mean the same thing in  perception. Then, if its geometry is to be explicitly referred to, visual space under discussion is not euclidean.  Visual space (VS) is the final product of the long series of processes starting from stimulus in the physical space (X). When  the physiological process from retina to brain is completed, VS is generated as perception. Phenomenologically it is  articulated into individual objects, backgrounds, all appearing in front of the self. The self is a percept consisting of  visual and proprioceptive experiences. VS extends in three-dimensional directions and how it is articulated depends upon  stimulus condition in X. In complete darkness, there is almost no articulation and the self is surrounded by darkness which  starts from directly in front of the face and its thickness is indeterminate. Herein, the face is a percept as a part of the  self. If the eyes are stimulated by homogeneous light of sufficiently low intensity, VS consists of fog or mist of a certain  brightness surrounding the self, which is called the Ganzfeld phenomenon (Metzger, 1930; Avent, 1965). As the intensity of  light is increased, the mist appears to retreat and VS starts to be articulated. Under ordinary conditions, VS is fully  structured and there are percepts having geometrical properties, curves, straight lines, intersections, betweenness,  parallelness, distances, lengths, angles, surfaces, and congruence, etc., all appearing at certain distances from the self.  Percepts in VS are hierarchically related to each other including the self. Namely, a percept is localized with regard to  other percepts which act as its framework. The self is not the ultimate framework. On the contrary, it is localized with  regard to the floor and walls of a room, for instance. When visible, the ground and sky would be the largest framework. If VS  under a given stimulus condition is articulated in accordance with a geometry, the discrepancy between P- and D-alleys implies  that it is not according to the euclidean geometry. However, it would be worthwhile to ask what geometry, among well-established  geometries, is most appropriate to describe the structure of VS under discussion. Luneburg (1947, 1948, 1950) presented a set  of rationales for assuming that VS is a three-dimensional Riemannian space (R3) of constant Gaussian curvature K and showed  that, if { Qi } for P-alley lies outside { Q; } for D-alley, it is hyperbolic (K<O). His model is ultimately reduced to two  formulations: theoretical equations for perceptual phenomena formulated in the euclidean map (EM) of VS and the special  pattern of correspondence between EM and the physical space X.  EUCLIDEAN MAP, EM, AND FORMULATION OF ALLEYS IN EM  Riemannian space R of constant K includes three cases: elliptic (K> O), euclidean (K=0), and hyperbolic (K<O). Unless K=O, a  Riemannian surface RZ cannot be depicted on a sheet of paper without any distortion. The surface of a sphere of radius r is  used as a model for an elliptic plane of curvature K where K= 1/r' and a surface of saddle back shape as a model for a part of  a hyperbolic plane. In this type of representation, three-dimensional eculidean space, E3, is necessary to embed the  two-dimensional surface Rz. There are many different representations of R in E with no additional dimensionality. Lobell  (1950) reviewed various ways of representing the hyperbolic plane in the euclidean plane (E z). Perhaps, the most well-known  representations are the Klein's model and a model given by Poincare. The former is explained in detail in a standard book and  used to derive the hyperbolic trigonometry (Busemann & Kelly, 1953). To my knowledge, the latter and its relation to the  former are touched upon rather lightly in several places (Greenberg, 1973; Hilbert & Cohn-Vossen, 1932; Penrose, 1980; Rucker,  1977; Smart, 1978; Sommerville, 1958).' Luneburg as well as Blank (in Hardy et al., 1951) used the latter representation for R  including both hyperbolic and elliptic spaces and this is what was called the euclidean map. None of these articles and books  give any references and only a representation of two-dimensional Rz in two-dimensional EM' is discussed. Penrose (1980, p.90)  briefly refers to the possibility of same representation for R3, but no reference is given. APPARENT FRONTOPARALLEL PLANE, HP, AND ALLEYS ON HP  We will never come across in our daily life two parallel lines such as those epitomized by P-alley. Railway tracks which are  physically straight and parallel appear to converge at the horizon (the vanishing point) to the observer standing in the  center of the tracks. What is meant by a P-alley is that two lines are perceptually straight and parallel up to the horizon.  Of course, we see lines that appear straight and parallel, but they are not in this direction. Frames of a window are  physically straight and parallel in the horizontal as well as vertical directions, and they appear that way to our casual  observation. This is a pattern in three-dimensional visual space, VS'. Hence, it was attempted in a previous article (Indow,  1979) to derive equations in EM' for P- and D-alleys which extend horizontally or vertically and also appear frontoparallel  under the same constraint that VS' is of constant curvature K. An experiment was carried out to see, according to these  equations, which K is most appropriate for these alleys on an apparent frontoparallel plane, HP (Indow & Watanabe, 1984b).  Horizontal P- and D-alleys were constructed on each of three HP's at different distances from the subject. Small light points  in the dark were used because it is difficult to make the scaffold invisible otherwise. Two procedures were used, one based  upon the mapping functions (4) in which two parameters K and a were estimated and the other in which no a priori assumption on  mapping functions was involved so that only K was estimated. The result was somewhat ambiguous, but it suggested that the  geometry of this VS2, HP, in which Q's are not very different in x, is not too far from euclidean.
