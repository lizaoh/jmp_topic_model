Many multinomial processing tree (MPT) models have been developed for single trial learning and memory paradigms (see  Batchelder & Riefer, 1999  for details). In practice a number of these paradigms involve multiple trials where the same task is repeated on each trial. In such cases, behavior is likely to reflect earlier study and test trials and perhaps the passage of time leading to forgetting. Since MPT models are intended as tools to measure latent cognitive processes ( Batchelder & Riefer, 1999 ), it is important to consider ways to modify an MPT model so that the past trials may lead to changes in the parameter values operative on a given trial.

There are three possible approaches to the problem of modeling change in parameter values across trials for MPT models. First, one could apply the MPT model separately to data from each trial and then study trends in the parameter estimates by graphing them over trials and conducting hypothesis tests. Second, one could develop a detailed dynamic model for trial-to-trial changes in the parameters.  Batchelder and Riefer (1980)  contains examples of both of these approaches for an MPT model of pair-clustering in free recall (also see  Batchelder & Riefer, 1986 ). Further,  Batchelder, Chosak-Reiter, Shankle, and Dick (1997)  provides an example of the second approach by representing a Markov learning model for free recall as an MPT model, where the categories are trial ordered sequences of correct and incorrect recall of individual items.

The first method of applying the model to each trial separately has several disadvantages. For example, it ignores data within a subject (or subject-item) concerning changes over trials. In addition, it fails to capitalize on increased statistical power provided by a simultaneous multi-trial analyses of the data, and it is agnostic on important psychological processes of change due to repeated study and tests of the to-be-remembered material. On the other hand, the disadvantage of the second method stems from the fact that there is no satisfactory general approach to making an arbitrary MPT model dynamic. In certain cases it would require considerable theoretical commitment to a complex model, possibly masking basic information about latent processes obtainable from separate trial analyses.

There is a third, more natural approach to generalizing MPT models to multiple trials and it is the one we pursue in this paper. The approach follows naturally from the observation that in a multi-trial learning or memory experiment with repeated study trials, participants would likely improve performance on such cognitive skills as encoding, storage, retrieval, and clustering, all of which may be reflected in a suitable MPT model by separate parameters. Suppose  θ 
                   1  is a parameter reflecting such a skill and let  n  index trials in the task, then one might expect that constraints in the form of a linear order of the following sort would occur, namely: 
                      
                         (1) 
                         θ 1,1 ⩽θ 1,2 ⩽⋯⩽θ 1,n ⩽⋯⩽θ 1,N ,

On the other hand, if it was a pure forgetting paradigm (a series of memory tests following a single initial study trial), one might expect the reverse ordering of that shown in  Eq. (1) . The third approach we develop here attempts to incorporate non-overlapping linear orders (a given parameter can participate in at most one linear order) of constraints like  Eq. (1)  into the MPT modeling framework to handle multi-trial experiments.

The approach has two components. First, we show how to construct new parameters that are free to vary in [0,1] that can be used to reparameterize the original model with the order constraints. Then we show that the reparameterized model is an MPT model (with no parametric constraints). In other words, the class of MPT models is closed under the class of non-overlapping linear orders of parametric constraints. Our results serve three related purposes described in detail in the paper. First they contribute to the development of the mathematical structure of MPT models as a distinct class of statistical models for categorical data. In fact, the closure of the MPT class under reparameterizations representing non-overlapping linear order constraints proves to be very useful, and it is also an atypical feature of most familiar classes of statistical models for categorical data. Second the new parameters in the reparameterization enable the formulation of new hypotheses tests that can bear directly on the effects of trials on the changes in parameters of an MPT model. Finally, our results provide a way to extend the inferential machinery developed in  Hu and Batchelder (1994)  to the case of order restricted inference for MPT models.

The paper is organized into five basic sections. In  Section 1 , the nature of binary MPT models is reviewed. In  Section 2 , we show how parametric order constraints in any MPT model can be represented by simple reparameterizations, and further we show that the class of binary MPT models is “closed” (in a sense to be described) under these reparameterizations. In  Section 3 , we show how to interpret and formulate psychologically meaningful hypotheses about the new parameters generated in the reparameterization, and in  Section 4 , we briefly discuss other standard approaches to order restricted inference and relate them to the MPT class. In  Section 5 , we illustrate the reparameterization methods with real data from a free recall experiment involving pair-clustering in free recall.

Simple reparameterizations would be needed to match the formal definition given in  Hu and Batchelder (1994)  that allows more than two links to occur at any node in the tree. Parametric order constraints such as those exhibited in  Eq. (1)  create a nested model in a special way, namely they allow for parameters to lie in only portions of the original parameter space without reducing its dimensionality. For example, take two parameters,  θ 
                      1  and  θ 
                      2  each free to vary in [0,1]; the simple constraint that  θ 
                      1 ⩽ θ 
                      2  allows for solutions in a volume of only half the size of the original parameter space. The inclusion of order restrictions in a particular model does not pose a principled problem for statistical inference. There are a variety of iterative algorithms that can be used to maximize likelihood functions in selected regions of a parameter space, approximate posterior distributions and calculate posterior probabilities of the constrained region, or achieve other decision criteria subject to the constraints. In  Section 4  we will discuss briefly some of these methods.

The main purpose of this paper is to discuss an approach to order restricted inference at the level of the entire MPT class. We will consider the case of one or more non-overlapping linear orders on the parameters. Our main result is that the MPT class is “closed” under this class of parametric order restrictions. By “closed” we mean that for any MPT model under linear order constraints, one can reparameterize the model in such a way that the new model satisfies the MPT assumptions (all parameters are free to vary in [0,1]), it has the same number of parameters as the original model, and it is “statistically equivalent” to the original model with the order restrictions. By statistically equivalent it is meant that both the restricted model and the reparameterized model entail exactly the same set of probability distributions over the observable categories. This closure property is useful because if the reparameterized model is outside the class of MPT models, then it may not be possible to utilize the MPT model inferential machinery described earlier to handle order restricted inference. In fact, we will show that there are several algebraic ways to represent parametric constraints in the form of a linear order, and that in each case the MPT class is closed under such representations. These results not only further our mathematical understanding of the MPT class, but they provide a way to extend the software discussed earlier based on the EM algorithm to MPT models under linear orders of parametric constraints. That software is designed to analyze models where each parameter,  θ 
                      
                         s 
                      , is allowed to vary freely in [0,1], and the reparameterized MPTs also satisfy this property, while being statistically equivalent to the original model with the order restrictions.

The methods for dealing with order restrictions for MPT models have a drawback that the tree for a reparameterized model may have considerably more branches than the original model with the order restrictions. In any event, in order to use our approach the new tree would have to be constructed using the methods described in  Section 2 . If one was concerned with a single application of a particular model, one might try methods for dealing with order constrained inference for the model directly, such as gradient search methods to maximize the likelihood function within restricted regions of the parameter space. Bayesian methods are also possible, for example  Robertson, Wright, and Dykstra (1988)  includes a short section on Bayesian approaches for dealing with order restricted inference, and there has been considerable additional work of this type since that volume that follows the approach using Gibbs sampling in  Gelfand, Smith, and Lee (1992) . Bayesian approaches to order constrained inference are certainly promising; especially for particular models. Also there may be a way to work Bayesian methods into a general approach to order restricted inference for the entire class of MPT models. However we do not pursue it in this paper.

When several non-overlapping linear orders of restrictions are at play, the PAVA algorithm applied to unrestricted MLEs may not yield order restricted MLEs of the latent parameters. In particular, if two or more latent parameters each have trial-ordered restrictions as in  Eq. (1) , there may be tradeoffs in the two orders that prevent PAVA from yielding order-restricted MLEs. We have verified this observation with simulations involving order restricted joint estimation of the storage parameter,  c , and the retrieval parameter,  r , from the pair-clustering model described in Batchelder and Riefer (1986, the model is described in the next section). For this reason, efforts to apply PAVA in the context of MPT models will not be pursued in this paper. We would not rule out the possibility that there may be productive applications of some isotonic regression algorithms to order restricted inference in MPT models, for example an augmentation of the M-step in the EM algorithm.

To illustrate the added hypotheses that can be tested by the addition of parametric order constraints to a multitrial MPT model, an example will now be considered for a particular free recall model involving clusterable pairs. The model was first proposed in  Batchelder and Riefer (1980) , developed subsequently in  Batchelder and Riefer (1986)  and  Riefer and Batchelder (1988, 1991) , and applied to clinical data in  Riefer et al. (2002)  using some of the order restricted inference techniques formalized here. The model, called the pair-clustering model, attempts to separately measure the effects of cluster storage and cluster retrieval for pairs of words from the same category in a repeated trial free recall paradigm. A general overview of the methodology used in a pair-clustering experiment will be given next, then the model will be described, and finally it will be applied to a data set using the methods described in this paper.

In a pair-clustering experiment, subjects are presented with a list of items for study. The list consists of clusterable pairs, items that are both associated with the same category (e.g. oxygen, hydrogen; doctor, lawyer), and singletons, items that do not have a category pair in the list. The spacing between items in a clusterable pair may be controlled with members of other pairs and singletons separating them (see  Batchelder & Riefer, 1980 ). Following list presentation, subjects are asked to recall the word list. This study-test procedure may be repeated over a number of trials.

The data on the pairs from the free recall are then coded into categories using the following scheme:  E 
                      1 : both words of a pair were recalled consecutively;  E 
                      2 : both words of a pair were recalled, but were not recalled consecutively;  E 
                      3 : one and only one of the words in a pair was recalled;  E 
                      4 : neither of the words in a pair were recalled. The data on singletons are coded as  F 
                      1 : a singleton was recalled;  F 
                      2 : a singleton was not recalled. The model has three parameters:  c , r , and  u . The parameter  c  represents the probability that a category pair is stored and subsequently maintained in memory as a cluster, 0⩽ c ⩽1. The parameter  r  represents the conditional probability that a category pair is recalled as a cluster, given that it is stored and maintained as a cluster, 0⩽ r ⩽1. The parameter  u  represents the probability of storing, maintaining and retrieving a non-clustered item (e.g. singletons and non-clustered pair members), 0⩽ u ⩽1.

Over prolonged periods, severe alcoholism often results in deficits in cognitive functioning and even organic brain damage. A common deficit in such organic alcoholics is anterograde amnesia, in which the ability to remember new information is affected. While anterograde amnesia is well documented in organic alcoholics the cause of this amnesia has been widely debated (see  Riefer et al. (2002)  for a more detailed overview). There have been empirical results attributed individually to both storage and retrieval deficits. In fact, the same pattern of results have been interpreted as either storage or retrieval deficits by different researchers. Given this ambiguity,  Riefer et al. (2002)  analyzed data from both organic alcoholics and their controls using the pair-clustering model to separately measure storage and retrieval capacity in a memory task.

There were six study-test trials of pair data from organic alcoholics and their controls reported in  Riefer et al. (2002) . Half of the 42 participants had a staff diagnosis of organic brain syndrome associated with alcoholism. The other half of the participants were alcoholic controls not suspected of organic brain syndrome. A 40-word list consisting of 20 pairs of high association level words in  Battig and Montague (1969)  was presented to each group for a total of six study-test trials (the same list of words was used on each trial). During the study portion of the trial, words were presented for  4

These results indicate that the performance differences between the organic alcoholics and their controls can be attributed to differences in their ability to both store and retrieve items from memory; however, the retrieval deficits of the organic alcoholics, when compared to their controls, are more severe than their storage deficits. The results further indicate that the rate at which storage improves may be the same for the two groups, and that it is the initial (trial one) ability of the groups to store items that is different. Furthermore, while the alcoholic controls’ ability to retrieve items improves over trials, the organic alcoholics’ ability does not. Most important for our purposes, the sorts of hypotheses tested in this analysis of the data would be very difficult to carry out without the new parameters and corresponding MPT models designed to deal with the order restrictions that arise naturally in the multi-trial experiment.

This paper has shown that the class of MPT models is closed under parametric order restrictions in the form of non-overlapping linear orders. In fact, there are several ways to reparameterize these linear orders into new MPT models, as shown by Methods A through D, and all of these reparameterized models are statistically equivalent. These results are surprising in the sense that one would not expect a class of models to be invariant under functional transformations of the parameters such as  Eqs. (8), (9), (13), and (18) . This closure property contributes to the structural analysis of the MPT class. In this article, we have focused only on the case of non-overlapping, linear orders. It is possible to extend our analyses to other varieties of partial orders that allow some overlap in the linear orders; however, the details will be the subject of a future paper.

The inclusion of order constraints into the MPT class allows for many kinds of analyses for multi-trial data not previously possible with MPT models. For example, it is now possible to perform hypothesis tests not only on the latent parameters of the model over trials and between groups, but hypothesis tests can now also be performed on parameters representing the rate of change in the latent processes. More generally, MPT models allow for analyses not easily formulated with traditional methods of modeling categorical data such as with the classes of log-linear or logit models discussed in  Agresti (2002) . These models can show that there are differences between groups and attribute these differences to the factorial structure of the experimental design, but in general they do not offer methods to get at the substantive, underlying causes of the differences. In contrast, an MPT model embeds substantive assumptions derived from the underlying experimental task ( Batchelder & Riefer, 1999 ), and this allows one to test substantive hypotheses about the underlying basis of differences between groups. When parametric order constraints arise naturally from the task, our methods offer the advantage of incorporating into the model not only meaningful interpretations of the parameters themselves but also of their pattern of change over trials.
