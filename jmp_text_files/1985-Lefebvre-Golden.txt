INTRODUCTION  Within recent years researchers working in personality theory have been puzzled by a constant 0.62. It appears when subjects  are given a task to categorize themselves and others on bipolar dimensions. This constant is close to the value of (,~5 - 1)/2  = 0.618..., known in mathematics as "golden section": it divides a line segment into two sections such that the ratio of the  entire segment to its larger section is equal to the ratio of the larger section to the smaller one. In personality theory,  the constant 0.62 is the ratio of choosing positive poles to all choices. Adams-Webber writes:  It has been shown in a series of recent experiments that when subjects categorize acquaintances dichotomously on the basis of  bipolar constructs (for example, pleasant-unpleasant), they tend to assign these figures to the "positive" poles (for example,  pleasant), approximately 62 percent of the time (Adams-Webber, 1978, 1979a, b; Adams-Webber and Benjafield, 1973; Benjafield  and Adams-Webber, 1975, 1976; Benjafield and Green, 1978; Romany and AdamsWebber, 1981). This finding has been generalized  across a variety of bipolar constructs, different cultural groups, and alternative methods of measurement. It has also been  replicated with the same subjects on different occasions. According to this theory each person has his own unique system of dichotomous constructs which  serve as special axes for "projecting" the other person. The ratio of positive to all choices was 0.62.  Adams-Webber (1979) attracted attention to the work of Osgood and Richards (1973) who conducted an experiment on the semantic  determination of "and" vs "but." Osgood (1979) repeated this experiment  in 12 different languages; the frequency of "and" choices was 0.60. We suggest another hypothesis: human cognition has a special mechanism for modeling the self and others. It works as a  universal "inner computer" and creates the core of the images which later are "dressed" and "colored" with nuances. It also  conducts the process of choice in dichotomizing good vs bad. The constant 0.62 is a characteristic of this "computer."  We will show in this paper that if we suppose that the subjects' behavior corresponds to the principles described in the  algebraic model of ethical cognition (Lefebvre, 1977b, 1980, 1982, 1984), then the constant 0.62 appears as a consequence. Its  precise value is 0.625 under very general assumptions about inner computer performance; and its precise value is equal to ;zs  :0.6172 if we limit our analysis to a more constrained case. Under special conditions this value is precisely equal to the  golden section ratio.  2. AN ALGEBRAIC MODEL OF ETHICAL COGNITION  Main Assumptions  (1) A person possesses an inner formal mechanism for modeling himself and the others. This mechanism is universal and does not  depend on the particular culture to which a person belongs.  (2) The models of oneself and of another person are reflexive; that is, these models contain the models of oneself and of the  other which in their turn contain the models of oneself and of the other, and so on.  (3) The inner formal mechanism for modeling includes a calculating process, which is automatic and does not depend on a  human's will. This process predetermines a person's responses in a situation of choice between "good" and "bad" and generates  his inner feelings such as guilt and condemnation.  (4) The models of oneself and of an other also have this calculating ability, and this allows a person to model automatically  his own and his partner's inner feelings, which gives him information unavailable for direct observation.  Let us mention that we do not connect the work of this modeling mechanism with the chains of a person's verbal reasonings such  as "I think that he thinks that I think," etc. These chains are constructed on the basis of linquistic chunks and can  242 VLADIMIR A.LEFEBVRE  be of any structure and length. The function x- y=x+ y (where x c G and y e G) is called implication. Let us  agree to write it down in exponential form : x -+ y = yx. Then each formula representing an individual or a situation can be  considered as a formula of Boolean algebra expressing a Boolean function. The main principle of our further reasoning is that  we see in a formula both an individual and a Boolean function. The syntax of a formula allows us to compare it with the  structure of images enclosed into each other, and a function corresponding to this formula gives a description of an  individual's behavior.  Consider an individual A with images of himself and his partner and images of the images. Let A be a formula in Boolean  algebra of two elements { 1, 0}. For each pair of values a and b we can calculate a value of the formula. We interpret a = 0  as the "environment demand" on an individual A to accomplish an action marked "bad," and a =1 to accomplish an action marked  "good." We interpret b = 0 as the environment demand on an individual B to accomplish an action marked "bad," and b = 1 to  accomplish an action marked "good"; all of this is from A's point of view. The value of formula A is interpreted as an action  of individual A: if A = 0, an individual accomplishes an action marked "bad"; if A = 1 he accomplishes an action marked  "good." Therefore, for each pair of values a and b we can find out how A's response is marked. THE MAIN HYPOTHESIS  An analysis of experimental data shows that the constant 0.62 is not specially connected with a particular situation of  evaluating another person. The data rather give evidence that this constant appears each time subjects have a task of  evaluating anything as "good" or "bad," no matter whether the object for evaluation is another person or something else. Thus,  it is natural to hypothesize that the invariant 0.62 is a characteristic of any subject when he is functioning as a special  evaluating device. Our main hypothesis is that "inside" a subject there is a special algebraic mechanism at work, and it  transforms the evaluations "good" and "bad"  ALGEBRAIC MODEL OF ETHICAL COGNITION 297  in a way similar to the one described in a previous section. We suppose that a subject's  choice of the positive pole is made with a frequency equal to the frequency of positive responses of a corresponding formal  individual. In other words, this frequency is equal to an individual's ethical status. CONCLUSIONS  The results of this work can be summarized as follows:  The existence of the constant 0.62 is explained if we suppose that special formal mechanisms of inner feelings are actualized  in human cognition.  On the other hand, the fact that the algebraic model of ethical cognition is able to explain the existence of the constant  0.62 is evidence of this model's correspondence to real processes in the human being.  More generally these results argue in favor of Chomsky's hypothesis (Chomsky, 1965) that at the basis of cognitive processes  there are some computational procedures.
