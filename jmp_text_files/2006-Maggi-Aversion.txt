In decision theory various approaches aim to describe and to formalize the decision process. The most common approach is still the  expected utility theory  (EUT), even if it is demonstrated that it suffers from some relevant limitations (e.g., see  Kahneman & Tversky, 1979 ). In order to overcome the limits of EUT, different new approaches have been proposed (see  Starmer, 2000 ). Among them, the  cumulative prospect theory  (CPT) of Kahnemann and Tversky stands out, thanks to its tractability and strong psychological and behavioral evidences (e.g., see  Laibson & Zeckhauser, 1998 ). In  Section 2  we briefly introduce CPT. In order to describe the attitude of the individual facing the uncertainty, an important concept is  loss aversion .  Section 3  presents the main definitions of loss aversion. We remark that all of them, except the  Neilson's (2002)  weak loss aversion definition, depend on how probabilities are perceived, i.e. distorted, by the individuals. In  Section 4  we define the  
                         π 
                      -risk aversion ( 
                         π 
                      -RA) as the dislike for lotteries that are perceived as fair.  Theorem 4  shows that the  Neilson's (2002)  weak loss aversion is equivalent to  
                         π 
                      -RA. This result characterizes in behavioral terms the weak loss aversion property of the value function, without imposing any additional restriction on the probability perception. Finally,  Section 5  shows that the widely used power value function violates weak loss aversion, implying  
                         π 
                      -risk seeking for some mixed sign lotteries.  Section 6  concludes.

In CPT both rational and psychological factors enter solving the decision problem under risk or uncertainty. In order to model the different perception of improvements or deterioration from a reference wealth ( reference dependence ), CPT separately evaluates the gains and the losses, rather than considering the total final wealth (e.g., see  Fennema & van Assen, 1999 ;  Tversky & Kahneman, 1992 ). The empirical evidence suggests that losses matter more than gains ( loss aversion ) and that the perception of an incremental change in wealth weakens with the distance from the reference point ( diminishing sensitivity ). Diminishing sensitivity can be modeled by value (or utility) functions that are concave for gains and convex for losses: i.e., by  S-shaped  value functions. Moreover, the subjective perception and the evaluation of risk cause a distortion of the real probabilities, a phenomenon to be taken into account when making a decision, by properly weighting the probabilities. All these factors enter into CPT.

Theorem 4  shows that, in a CPT model, weak loss aversion is a condition equivalent to perceptual risk aversion for mixed sign lotteries. This result characterizes the role of weak loss aversion from a behavioral point of view. Thanks to  Theorem 4 ,  
                         π 
                      -RA deals only with the weak loss aversion property  (8)  of the function describing the perception of the value, and it needs no further assumption (beside the basic ones  (2) ) on the probability perception. In other words, this paper identifies a notion of loss aversion that can be defined in behavioral terms and that keeps separated the perception of value from the one of probabilities. This result gives an answer to the question of how to define loss aversion. In fact, weak loss aversion  (8)  is necessary and sufficient in order to get a risk averse behavior with respect to mixed sign lotteries. Indeed, if  (8)  does not hold, then there exist lotteries that are perceived as fair, and yet are preferred than the status quo.
