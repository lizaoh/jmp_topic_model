One of the primary tasks in mathematical psychology is to evaluate formal theories, or models, of mental processing. This model evaluation procedure is complicated because it requires a delicate balance between several relevant criteria ( Jacobs & Grainger, 1994 ), including descriptive and explanatory adequacy, generality, simplicity, and falsifiability. In this article, we focus on one particular criterion,  model generalizability , that has received considerable attention in the modeling literature. Furthermore, we will discuss two specific methods — the Bayes factor, and the generalization criterion — that assess different aspects of model generalizability.

The remainder of this article is divided into five sections (Sections  2–6 ). In  2 , we present an overview of Bayes factors, and their use in psychology. In  3 , we offer a simple numerical example to show how Bayes factors can be very sensitive to prior assumptions. In  4 , we discuss model generalizability, and the generalization criterion. In particular, we will show how model generalizability can be defined at different levels ( local  versus  global ). In  5 , we present various analyses of a dataset that involves retention functions, and apply some alternative Bayesian methods (other than Bayes factors) that may be useful for evaluating competing models. In  6 , we conclude by relating the present research to the broader statistical modeling literature.

Recently, psychologists have begun to embrace the Bayesian approach to model selection ( Lee & Wagenmakers, 2005 ). The most popular tool in psychology for Bayesian model selection is the Bayes factor ( Myung & Pitt, 1997 ), which is closely related to the Bayesian information criterion (BIC), and the Minimum Description Length (MDL) criterion. Together, these criteria have been used widely within mathematical psychology, in areas such as: information integration ( Myung & Pitt, 1997 ), multidimensional scaling ( Lee, 2001 ), categorization ( Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002 ), problem solving ( Vickers, Lee, Dry, & Hughes, 2003 ), memory ( Lee, 2004 ), decision processes ( Lee & Cummins, 2004 ), individual differences ( Lee & Webb, 2005 ), and response time distributions ( Rouder, Lu, Speckman, Sun, & Jiang, 2005 ). Clearly, an investigation of the assumptions underlying Bayes factors (and related methods) has significant implications for researchers across several domains. We attempt such an investigation in this article. Specifically, we conclude that Bayes factors can be very sensitive to prior assumptions, and that this prior sensitivity is related to a specific form of model generalizability.

Before we define the Bayes factor, it is worthwhile to clarify what we mean by a  model . The term “model” is commonly used to denote a set of sampling distributions that are related in some way (e.g.,  Grünwald (2007) ,  Wasserman (2000) ). Strictly speaking, this set is not a model, but a model class ( Su, Myung, & Pitt, 2005 ), also known as a model family ( Dempster, 1997 ), a model structure ( Domingos, 1999 ), or a model type ( Wagenmakers, Ratcliff, Gomez, & Iverson, 2004 ). A model class consists of a collection of models in which each model corresponds to a distinct sampling distribution. Our definition of “model” here corresponds to what others have called a “point hypothesis” or “simple hypothesis” ( Grünwald, 2007 , p. 15). Although we will sometimes, for convenience, use “model” when we actually mean “model class”, the reader is advised to keep this distinction in mind.

The Bayes factor is an extension of the standard likelihood ratio, where the likelihood is defined as the probability of the observed data, given a model with specified parameter values. Whereas the likelihood ratio compares two models by their respective likelihoods, the Bayes factor compares two model classes by their respective marginal likelihoods. The marginal likelihood for a model class is a weighted average of the likelihood, and is computed across all the parameter values represented by the prior distribution. In brief, a Bayes factor is a likelihood ratio for two model classes.

A Bayes factor comparing two model classes is typically interpreted as quantifying the extent to which the data support, or provide evidence for, one model class over another ( Lee & Wagenmakers, 2005; Wagenmakers & Grünwald, 2006 ). We suggest that this interpretation can sometimes be misleading; a Bayes factor merely indicates which model class,  averaged over its prior distribution , is more likely to have generated the data. In particular, a Bayes factor does not indicate which model class is more likely to contain the true model that generated the data. To clarify this distinction, we offer a simple numerical example.

The evaluation of competing models is typically reported in a familiar format comprising four progressive stages: (1) model specification, (2) parameter estimation, (3) model selection, and (4) sensitivity analysis. In the following example, we will describe how the Bayesian approach can be applied at each of these four stages. We will emphasize, in particular, the importance of sensitivity analysis: a task in which researchers check whether conclusions derived from their initial assumptions would hold under different assumptions.

The uniform, Jeffreys, and Haldane priors are often regarded as “non-informative”, because they all express a specific form of ignorance about the true rate. We briefly mention some of the arguments that have been put forward for each prior. The uniform prior is the unique distribution that assigns equal prior probability for each of the possible counts: in this example, ranging from 0 to 100 ( Geisser, 1984 ). The Jeffreys prior distribution — Beta(0.5, 0,5) — is invariant to reparamaterization; for example, if the data are transformed to a different scale, the Jeffreys prior does not change ( Navarro, Pitt, & Myung, 2004 ). The Haldane prior distribution — Beta(0, 0) — is a uniform prior on the log-odds scale ( Zhu & Lu, 2004 ), and is often regarded as the prior that represents “complete ignorance” about a true rate ( Lee & Wagenmakers, 2005 ). Because the Beta(0, 0) distribution is improper (i.e., does not integrate to a finite value), the marginal likelihood is undefined, so we used the Beta(0.05, 0.05) distribution as a proper approximation to the Haldane. Finally, we also investigated an informative prior distribution — Beta(3, 2) — which expressed the belief that rates over 0.5 are more probable than rates under 0.5.

In many cases, this assumption may diverge significantly from the researcher’s prior beliefs (see  Weakliem (1999)  for discussion). An advocate of the BIC may argue that this criterion assumes a plausible, if conservative, non-informative prior ( Raftery, 1999 ). Although such a prior may be plausible under certain conditions, this argument alone cannot be used to justify the interpretation of the resulting Bayes factor. As we demonstrated earlier, each of the non-informative priors (the uniform, Jeffreys, and Haldane) could be considered plausible, but each of them produced a different Bayes factor.

Another argument for the use of BIC is that it is relatively simple to compute. Although this is an important pragmatic consideration, there are many existing criteria, such as the Akaike information criterion (AIC), that are just as simple to compute ( Wagenmakers & Farrell, 2004 ). Indeed, we suggest that whenever BIC is presented for model comparisons, researchers could also calculate other information criteria such as the AIC (see  Penny, Stephan, Mechelli, and Friston (2004)  for an example). In the model selection literature, it is well-known that BIC tends to favor simpler models, whereas AIC tends to favor more complex models ( Kass & Raftery, 1995 ). Reporting both BIC and AIC may therefore serve as a crude form of sensitivity analysis: if the two criteria select the same model, one can be more confident that the final decision was not overly dependent on an assumed prior.

The dependence of the Bayes factor on the prior suggests that some  objective  or  reference  prior should be useful as a baseline measure (e.g.  Bernardo & Rueda, 2002 ). This “Objective Bayesian” approach ( Berger, 2006 ) does not preclude the use of more informative priors, and allows the additional effect of subjective information to be readily assessed. Many sophisticated rules have been proposed for determining objective priors ( Kass & Wasserman, 1996 ), as well as variants of ordinary Bayes factors that do not require subjective specification of priors, such as the  intrinsic  Bayes factor ( Berger & Perrichi, 1996 ), and the  fractional  Bayes factor ( O’Hagan, 1995 ). Although we find the Objective Bayesian approach appealing, one is still left with the “subjective” dilemma of choosing between the many formal rules available for specifying priors. This issue is still actively debated in the Bayesian statistics literature ( Berger, 2006 ), but is beyond the scope of the present article.

The sensitivity analysis revealed that the effect of priors on parameter inferences is different from their effect on Bayes factors. In obtaining posteriors, priors can usually be conceived as representing some minimal amount of information, whose influence will be outweighed as more data are observed. When the data are highly informative or precise, the posterior density converges to the likelihood, and the effect of the prior may be negligible. In contrast, the marginal likelihood is highly sensitive to prior assumptions ( Aitkin, 1997, 2001; Kass, 1993; Sinharay & Stern, 2002 ). The divergence between posterior distributions and Bayes factors is sometimes known as the Jeffreys–Lindley paradox ( Lindley, 1957; Robert, 1993; Shafer, 1982 ). The pervasive nature of this divergence means that one is often forced to choose between inferences by posterior distributions, or inferences by Bayes factors ( Kass & Raftery, 1995 ).

The example that we chose here was elementary, and involved only a single parameter. Models in psychology are typically more complex, and involve many more parameters. In these cases, the sensitivity of marginal likelihoods to the assumed joint prior distribution of the parameters becomes even more critical. We repeat the advice of  Myung and Pitt (1997)  that whenever Bayes factors are presented for model comparisons, they should be calculated, ideally, for a wide range of plausible priors. Such a sensitivity analysis may be difficult to implement in complex cognitive models, but is nonetheless important for any definitive conclusions to be made. To our knowledge, this advice is rarely followed in the field of psychology.

In the next section, we will examine how this prior sensitivity relates to model generalizability.

The distinction between local and global model generalizability provides some insight into Lindley’s paradox. Whereas parameter inferences are concerned with the true model that generated the observed data, Bayes factors are concerned with the distribution of models from which the true model was sampled. For example, when the prior distribution is improper, the marginal likelihood is not well defined, and global model generalizability cannot be meaningfully assessed. In contrast, parameter inference (and local model generalizability) is possible with an improper prior, as long as the resulting posterior is proper.

In previous discussions of the local-global distinction, researchers have raised concerns about the use of local methods. For example,  Pitt et al. (2006)  argued that local methods are limited because they focus on model predictions at (or around) a particular point in parameter space, whereas global methods are preferable because they focus on model predictions across all parameter values. We suggest that these characteristics could also be viewed as reflecting an advantage for local methods, and a disadvantage for global methods. Global (but not local) methods are critically dependent on regions of the prior parameter space that could have generated the observed data, but did not (i.e. yield negligible likelihood); in some scenarios, researchers will be interested  only  in those parameter values that could have generated the observed data. We will give some examples of such scenarios later in this article.

In the following sections, we briefly mention two other methods that, like Bayes factors, assess global model generalizability. The first method is the  normalized maximum likelihood  (NML) criterion, which is derived from the MDL principle ( Myung, Navarro, & Pitt, 2006; Rissanen, 2001 ). The second method is the  accumulative prediction error  (APE) criterion, which is derived from prequential analysis ( Dawid, 1991; Wagenmakers et al., 2006 ). It is interesting that these three methods (Bayes factors, NML, and APE) can be made formally equivalent in terms of prediction error for future data, even though they were motivated by different principles (see  Grünwald (2007)  for further details). This formal equivalence suggests that it may be useful to view all three methods as assessing global model generalizability.

So far in this article, we have compared nested models on hypothetical datasets. In the next section, we compare a set of non-nested models on a real dataset involving retention (or forgetting) functions. In particular, we will contrast the conclusions from Bayes factors with those from a number of alternative criteria that assess local model generalizability.

One of the long-standing questions driving memory research is how to characterize the time-course of forgetting or retention of information. Historically, attempts to evaluate candidate retention functions have relied solely on goodness-of-fit measures (e.g.,  Rubin and Wenzel (1996) ,  Wixted and Ebbesen (1997) ). More recently,  Lee (2004)  used Bayes factors to compare five standard models from this literature for the 210 published datasets collated by Rubin and Wenzel. In the modern statistical toolbox, the Bayes factor is but one of many Bayesian methods available for model comparison. For example, a number of methods have been developed in which the object of interest is the posterior, rather than the prior, distribution. (It is beyond the scope of this article to provide a detailed description of these methods, but all are commonly used in the applied statistics literature). Such methods include: (1) the posterior predictive loss criterion (( Gelfand & Ghosh, 1998 ), see also,  Karabatsos (2006) ), the deviance information criterion (( Spiegelhalter et al., 2002 ); see  Myung, Karabatsos, and Iverson (2005)  and  Karabatsos and Batchelder (2003)  for applications in psychology), and (3) the posterior likelihood ratio ( Aitkin, 1997; Aitkin, Boys, & Chadwick, 2005; Congdon, 2005; Dempster, 1997; Fox, 2005 ). The common rationale underlying these Bayesian methods can be summarized as follows: Given that the posterior, rather than the prior, distribution of a model is ultimately reported and used for further inference, it is the posterior distribution that should be evaluated for the purposes of model comparison. All of these alternative methods are related to the generalization criterion in that they are designed to assess local model generalizability.

Aitkin et al. (2005) , proposed that models can be compared by the posterior distribution of the likelihood ( Aitkin, 1997; Dempster, 1997 ). The posterior likelihood approach may be regarded as a compromise between the maximized likelihood and the marginal likelihood approaches. On one hand, the maximized likelihood represents an upper bound, and so is necessarily an overestimate of the evidence in favor of a model class. On the other hand, the marginal likelihood underestimates the evidence in favor of a model class, because it is averaged over regions of prior parameter space that yield near-zero likelihood. Rather than maximizing or integrating the likelihood to produce a single-value summary, we can make an inference about the true likelihood (or equivalently, the true deviance) in the usual Bayesian fashion by examining its posterior distribution. In this way, only those models that could have generated the data are compared.  Fig. 13 
                             plots the posterior deviance distribution for the five retention functions, based on 10 000 posterior samples. These distributions exhibit the same preference ordering as that of the generalization criterion.

The logarithmic and hyperbolic models can be directly compared as follows. Suppose we draw a sample from each model posterior, and compute a likelihood ratio or a deviance difference. This single deviance difference is merely a sample from the  posterior distribution  of the deviance difference. By repeating this process, we can obtain the posterior distribution to estimate a credible interval. For example, the 95% credible interval for the deviance difference in favor of the logarithmic model over the hyperbolic model is (0.8, 12.7), which indicates anything from a very weak preference to a strong preference for the logarithmic model (see  Kass and Raftery (1995)  for interpretation of deviance differences). In  Table 4 
                            , we report the corresponding credible intervals for the deviance difference between the logarithmic model and the other four models.

Like the posterior predictive loss criterion and the DIC (but unlike the Bayes factor), the posterior likelihood ratio favors the logarithmic model over the hyperbolic model. In addition, the posterior likelihood ratio also provides an interval of uncertainty regarding this preference, which is fully compatible with the intervals that would be reported for model parameters.

In this article, we have investigated the assumptions that underlie two model selection criteria: the Bayes factor, and the generalization criterion. Although both criteria are said to assess model generalizability, we have shown that they can yield divergent conclusions. This divergence can be attributed to the distinction between two kinds of model generalizability: local and global. The definition of model generalizability in Eq.  (7)  allows for various interpretations, depending on how a researcher defines  
                         
                            
                               Y 
                            
                            
                               
                                  rep 
                               
                            
                         
                      , the future datasets of interest. Thus, local and global definitions of generalizability will be equivalent only when there is no parametric uncertainty (e.g., comparing two fully-specified models), where  
                         
                            
                               Y 
                            
                            
                               
                                  rep 
                               
                            
                         
                       is unambiguous.

We are not the first to propose that the generalizability can be defined at various levels. For example,  Spiegelhalter et al. (2002)  suggested that in hierarchical models, there is ambiguity in how the likelihood is defined (the “focus” of the model), depending on whether the random effects are integrated out. Choosing the model focus, they argued, depends on whether the researcher wishes to make future predictions for the upper-level units or for the lower-level units. This issue is currently an active area of research in Bayesian statistics ( Celeux, Forbes, Robert, & Titterington, 2006; Trevisani & Gelfand, 2003 ). We do not wish to claim that one level of analysis is more appropriate than than another, but rather that the local-global distinction — following ( Pitt et al., 2006 ) and ( Wagenmakers et al., 2004 ) — is one that deserves wider recognition in the mathematical psychology literature. Which level is more appropriate in any given context depends on the researcher’s goals.

As we pointed out earlier, the conflict between Bayesian model selection and parameter inference (known as Lindley’s paradox) means that researchers are sometimes forced to choose one or the other. For example, some Bayesian statisticians prefer the latter approach, in which “models are compared not by posterior probabilities but rather by their predictive accuracy for intended purposes” ( Gelman & Rubin, 1995 , p. 172). In this approach, the focus is on model building and model checking, rather than on model selection. One of the consequences of this posterior predictive approach is that with increasing data, the contribution of the prior distribution to posterior inferences gradually diminishes. This contrasts with the prior predictive approach: “[t]he difference between the posterior and prior predictive replications is that the former treats the prior distribution as an outmoded first guess, whereas the latter treats the prior distribution as a true ‘population distribution’ ” ( Gelman, Meng, & Stern, 1996 , p. 756). In fact, Bayes factors play no role in this approach because they are thought be a “distraction” to the information contained in the posterior distributions ( Gelman, Carlin, Stern, & Rubin, 2003 , p. 185). We suggest that the inherent conflict embodied by Lindley’s paradox is not between Bayesian model selection and parameter inference, but between global and local model generalizability: model selection methods that assess local generalizability are largely compatible with this model building approach.
