ZINNES AND RONALD KURTZ2  Indiana University3  Bloomington, Indiana 47401  Five different models for matching judgments are described. Each one is applied to an experiment employing both matching and ordered discrimination tasks, three payoff matrices, and three levels of stimulus discriminability. Under the matching instructions, subjects indicated whether two patterns of lights had the same or a different number of lights. Under the ordered discrimination instructions, subjects indicated which one of two patterns had more lights. One of the models consistently gives a better account of the individual and the pooled group data than the others, but even for this model the goodness-of-fit chi-squares are generally too large.  The experiment reported here involves what has been called both ordered and unordered discrimination judgments (Luce and Galanter, 1963). Ordered discrimination judgments refer to the usual forced choice task in which the subject is required to determine which one of a pair of stimuli is the larger, heavier, etc. Unordered discrimination judgments refer to a rarely used task in which the subject is asked merely whether the pair of stimuli match (are the same) or not. Such matching judgments have been used in the studies by Galanter (1956), Hefner (1958), Kalish (1958), Kinchla (1967), Rothkopf (1957), Sorkin (1962), and Walk (1966). Evidently the reason for the sparcity of experimental work with this task is based on the very old belief that the "same" or "equal" category in discrimination experiments is too unstable, too easily influenced by the subject, and as such gives a poor measure of a subject's optimum discriminability.  Our interest in this task here stems from its formal or conceptual equivalence with the task a subject implicitly performs in a simple generalization experiment. Since, he does not have to decide whether the test stimulus is larger or smaller than the training stimulus, the ordered judgment is not relevant in this experiment. Nor is it correct (at least in principle) to conceptualize the generalization task as an identification task, since the subject does not have to determine which stimulus is presented on test trials, but merely whether it differs from the training stimulus. Because of this formal similarity between unordered discrimination experiments and generalization experiments, we believe a deeper understanding of the generalization experiment and of its relationship to ordered discrimination experiments can be obtained by studying the matching judgment. A detailed treatment of the matching judgment might help to explain in precise terms why, for example, generalization occurs to stimuli that are clearly discriminable and why generalization and discrimination gradients do not always correspond (Guttman and Kalisb, 1956).  In addition to using two types of discrimination tasks, this experiment also involves the explicit manipulation of payoff matrices differing in asymmetry. In the context of the matching judgment, an asymmetric payoff matrix refers to a condition in which the outcome following an incorrect response of "same" does not equal the outcome following an incorrect response of "different." Asymmetric payoff matrices are of interest to us because of their implicit or explicit use in most generalization studies.. In generalization experiments, the occurrence of the conditioned responses in the absence of the training stimulus is generally not as undesirable as the nonoccurrence of the conditioned response following the presentation of the training stimulus. Since the effect of such an asymmetric payoff matrix is to produce a strong conditioned response bias, it would help to explain, at least in qualitative terms, why the conditioned response occurs to test stimuli that are clearly discriminable from the training stimulus. Our aim here is to be able to spell out and account for these properties of the payotl matrix in more detail.  MODELS FOR MATCHING JUDGMENTS  Other than the algebraic, nonprobabilistic models of Galanter (1956) and Goodman (1951), the earliest model explicitly developed to account for matching judgments appears to be the one described by Hefner (1958). This model may be thought of as one possible extension of Thurstone's model for comparative judgments. Closely related to Hefner's model is the one described by Sorkin (1962), using the terminology and framework of the Tanner and Swets (1954) theory of detection, and one used in a somewhat different context by Greenberg (1965). Although both Hefner and Sorkin claim some success with this model, it is difficult to draw any conclusions concerning  394 ZINNES AND KURTZ  its adequacy because of the very inefficient estimation procedures used and the lack of any tests of goodness-of-fit in their studies.  There are a number of other possible and plausible extensions of the Thurstonian judgment model. Two such extensions are considered here. In what follows, model 1 refers to the Hefner model and models 2 and 3 to two other Thurstonian-type models for matching.  In addition to extending the Thurstonian models to the matching experiment, it is also possible to generalize other models as well. Two such generalizations are considered here. The involve the detection models of Atkinson (1963), Atkinson, Bower, and Crothers (1965), Atkinson, Carterette and Kinchla (1962), Atkinson and Kinchla (1965), and Luce (1963a, b). Although there are many other possible models for matching judgments, the five considered here seem to be the simplest ones. It should also be pointed out that each one of these five models can be made considerably more complicated by adding more parameters, for example, to account for possible temporal or spatial biases. The versions described here are in most cases the simplest ones.  Before describing these five models for matching judgments, it will be helpful to have in mind certain general aspects of the present experiment. In each one of the conditions of this experiment, two stimuli, light patterns, were presented sequentially on each trial. For each group of subjects only two types of stimuli were available, although for some groups of subjects matching judgments were required and for others discrimination judgments. (Here and in what follows discrimination judgments will refer to ordered discrimination judgments.) In those groups in which the matching judgments were required, the two types of stimuli determined four types of trials. These may be designated as: (S,, S;), (S;, So), (SO, So) and (S;, S;), where, for example, (S,, S;) indicates a trial in which stimulus So is presented first and S; second. The subscripts on S are intended to indicate that one of the two stimuli, So , was common to all groups while one stimulus, S; , differed for different groups. For those groups in which discrimination judgments were required, there were only two types of trials: (S; , So) and (S,, S;). Group Versus Individual Analyses  Although the previous models have been described in terms of the judgments of individual subjects, we are interested in the degree to which these models fit group data, data obtained by pooling the observed proportions over individuals, as well as how well they fit individual data. These are two separate but related issues. It is possible for a model to hold over group data and not over individual data, and conversely, to hold over individual data but not over group data. In other words, the failure of a model to fit one type of data does not preclude its succeeding very well with the other type. Therefore, in the analyses that follow, in most cases each model is applied to both group and individual data.  Some remarks about the existence of subject differences may be necessary. If a model fits both individual and group data it may do so in one of two ways: either the parameter values are equal for all subjects in the group, or these parameter values are distributed or constrained in such a way that the expected value of the observed proportions over subjects satisfy the model. For example, it can be shown that if the discrimination parameter d; in model 1 is normally distributed over subjects, the model will fit the group data if it fits the individual data. Consequently, the existence of significant subject differences is not sufficient grounds for concluding that a particular model cannot fit group data based on these subjects. A few subjects were obtained from other courses by paying them for their participation in the experiment.  Apparatus. The stimuli presented on each trial consisted of patterns of lights, formed from a 9 x 9 matrix of lights. The panel on which the lights were mounted was 13 in. square, and the distance between adjacent lights was 1.5 in. Each subject viewed the light patterns individually from a distance of approximately 7.5 ft. in a darkened room that prevented the subject from seeing the unlighted light bulbs.  There were four types of light patterns or stimuli, S9 , S,9 , S, S,z , differing in the number of lights they contain. Stimulus S9 , for example, refers to a set of 54 light patterns, each of which contains 9 lights. Presenting stimulus S9 on a given trial, therefore, means randomly selecting and presenting one of the 54 light patterns containing nine lights. Similarly, S10, SJ, and S,. each refer to a set of 54 light patterns containing 10, 11, and 12 lights, respectively. Out of the 81 lights on the 9 x 9 matrix, 52 lights appeared in one of the light patterns.  All relevant experimental time intervals were determined automatically by electronic timers. On each trial and in all conditions, two stimuli were presented successively to the subject. Each stimulus was exposed for 0.5 sec. The interstimulus time on each trial was one sec. and the intertrial time was two sec. The intertrial time refers to the time between the subject's response and the onset of the first stimulus of the following trial. After the second stimulus of each trial had been presented, the subject was allowed to wait as long as he wished before making his response.  Each subject's response consisted of pressing one of two keys, and the key pressed was recorded on one of several counters. The programming source for most of the experimental conditions was an IBM key punch. The sequence of stimulus patterns presented during ark experimental session was determined by the information punched on the IBM cards. Since it was necessary to rerun certain experimental conditions when the key punch was no longer available, some of the data reported here were obtained by converting the information on the IBM cards onto paper tape and using, instead, a Western Union tape reader as the programming source. However, all relevant time intervals were preserved when this change was made.  Procedure. There were 12 experimental conditions, differing with respect to type of stimuli presented on each trial, task required of the subjects, and payoff schedule employed. Subjects were run individually for either 300 or 200 trials and each subject participated in only I of the 12 groups. Nine of these groups are referred to as M groups and the remaining three to D groups. In each group, two stimuli were presented successively on each trial. In the M groups, subjects were asked to indicate whether the two light patterns presented seemed to have the same or different number of lights. In the D groups, they were asked instead to indicate whether the first or the second light pattern seemed to have more lights.  There were four types of trials in each of the M groups, and these are designated by (S9 , S;), (S; , S9), (S9 , S9), and (S; , S;). In group M., , S; was equal to S10, that is, the subjects in this group were shown only stimulus S9 and S,o throughout the 300 trials; in M., and M.,, S was Sand S,., respectively. The three groups M.,, M.,, and M.3 therefore differ with respect to the discriminability of the pair of stimuli presented.  For the D groups, there are only two types of trials and these can be described as either (S9, S;) or (S;, S9) trials. The value of j in the three D groups, D,, D= , and D,, was equal to 10, 11, and 12, respectively. The subjects in D, , for example, were exposed only to stimuli S, and S,,. The subjects in these M groups did not receive trial-by-trial information as to the points they had won or lost on each trial or whether they were correct or incorrect. The trials were run in blocks of 100, and at the end of a block, subjects were informed of their score for that block of trials. To avoid large negative scores, subjects were told at the start of each block of trials that we would assume that they had 100 points initially. The score reported at the end of each block of trials was, therefore, based on adding their wins and substracting their losses from 100. They were told that the maximum possible score for each block was 200.  All subjects in the three D groups received a symmetric payoff matrix. The subjects in the D groups were told that they would be informed of the percentage of correct responses that they had obtained after each block of 100 trials.  All groups with the exception of D2 and D3 were given 300 trials in three blocks of 100 trials. Groups D2 and D3 were given only 200 trials because subjects' performance in these groups was so high that it was difficult to sustain their motivation over three trial blocks. Between blocks of trials there was about a 5-min rest period. Following each trial block, each subject was reminded of his score on prior trial blocks and was asked if he could better his previous performance on the next block.  Each block of 100 trials for the M groups contained an equal number of S, and Sd trials, and for the D groups, an equal number of (S9 , S) and (S,, S9) trials.  All M groups were run with the IBM key punch as the programming source. The problem is that the large number of observations involved in these data makes the power of the chi-square test approach one.  There is another reason for not dismissing model 1; its relatively poor fit is not atypical of the models in the phychophysical domain. Many studies that have reported excellent fits have been able to do so primarily because they did not calculate appropriate chi-square, goodness-of-fit tests. In those cases where we have calculated an appropriate chi-square on the basis of the published data (for example, Shepard, 1958; and Swets, 1959) these chi-squares have been invariably quite large and quite significant. Consequently, the large chi-square of model 1 should not be viewed as unusual. The procedure adopted in this paper involves comparing the goodness of fit of several models, rather than attempting to accept or reject any model. SUMMARY AND COMMENTS  The problem of understanding the relationship between generalization and discrimination experiments, it has been suggested here, is related to the simpler problem of understanding the relationship between discrimination and matching experiments. But even for this simpler case, a completely satisfactory model was not obtained here.  Of the five models considered, model 1 seems clearly to be best in its ability to account for the group and the individual data. The limitations, however, of this model are obvious. Only for discrimination condition 1 did it fit the individual data reasonably well. The chi-squares for the other two discrimination conditions were large and  420 ZINNES AND KURTZ  increased with stimulus discriminability. Why the fit of this model (and the others) should become worse with increasing discriminability is not clear. It cannot be attributed merely to large subject differences, since subject differences tended to increase with the asymmetry of the payoff matrix, rather than with discriminability.  In terms of the group data, the main limitation of model 1 was its poor fit to a single data point, that associated with p, and group M13 . Since there is a great deal of subject differences associated with this point (this may be seen from Table 2) this condition needs to be replicated.  REFERENCES  ATKINSON, R. C.
