SMITH  Regional Science Department, University of Pennsylvania, Philadelphia, Pennsylvania 19104  Two representational criteria for probabilistic choice models are studied. These criteria, called scalability and weak  scalability, are shown to be characterized by behavioral axioms designated, respectively, as pairwise dependence and weak  pairwise dependence. The former is shown to be equivalent to the order independence axiom of Tversky. In addition, structural  conditions on the choice alternatives are established under which all axioms are equivalent.  1. INTRODUCTION  The scalability hypothesis for modeling individual choice behavior has been investigated by Krantz (1964), Tversky and Russo  (1969), and Tversky (1972), among others. Tversky (1972) has shown that a testable axiom called order independence completely  characterizes this representational hypothesis. In this note we offer a formal definition of scalability and show that this  condition may equivalently be characterized by an axiom designated as pairwise dependence. This axiom serves to point up the  essential binary nature of scalable choice behavior. Moreover, the representation theorem for this axiom shows that the  appropriate scale functions for such models may always be chosen to be the binary choice probabilities with respect to any  fixed "base-point" alternative. In addition, we propose a slightly more general form of scalability called weak scalability.  This weaker criterion is shown to have consequences for the "independence of irrelevant alternatives" problem of scalable  choice models. Moreover, this criterion is also characterized by a weak pairwise dependence axiom which shares all of the  essential binary properties of the pairwise dependence axiom. Hence the appropriate scale functions are identical in both  cases.
