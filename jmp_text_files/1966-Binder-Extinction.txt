TEREBINSKI  System Development Corporation, Santa Monica, California  The experiment involved a three-person game in which the members voted for a leader; the designated leader then made a  decision for the group. The proportion with which each member was reinforced for his decisions as leader was under  experimental control. Earlier work with this game had reported a reasonably good fit between observed voting behavior and that  predicted from a Markov model based on the reinforcement probabilities. Voting choices showed learning trends and asymptotic  states which were predictable to a significant extent from the model. In the present experiment, a variation was introduced in  which a shift from differential to equal frequency of reinforcement among the members occurred mid-way through the game. The  subjects showed sensitivity to the shift by the extinction of previous voting preferences. The Markov model proved generally  adequate at asymptotic predictions.  Binder, Wolin, and Terebinski (1965) have shown, in a leadership-selection game, that subjects learn to vote for leaders in a  manner directly related to the decisionmaking success of the leaders. Various specific aspects of voting behavior were  accurately predicted from a mathematical theory by using the reinforcement probabilities for leaders' decisions as parameters.  However, in the experiments thus far completed there has been no investigation of the extinction of the learned voting  preferences.  In the present experiment, reinforcement schedules like those of the earlier experiments were used but these were altered  after a fixed number of learning trials. The sensitivity of the subjects to changed reinforcement conditions, the process of  extinction, and the degree of conformity with expectations from a Markov model could all be investigated.  1 This research was supported by grant GS-507 from the National Science Foundation to the System Development Corporation. Apparatus  The apparatus is identical to that described by Binder et al. (1965).  Procedure  Each trial in the experiment was divided into a voting phase and a decision phase. During the voting phase each member of a  three-man group voted for one of the other two members for group leadership. That member who received the most votes among  those eligible became leader; a member was eligible on a given trial only if he voted. Where there was a tie, leadership  choice was random.  The decision phase involved a prediction on the part of the leader as to whether an A or a B was the correct response for the  trial. Reinforcement of the leader's prediction was on a pre-arranged schedule and independent of the particular prediction.  Voting and decision choices were indicated by the pushing of buttons on a console, and stimulus information was supplied to  the subjects on a cathode-ray scope activated by a computer. During a voting phase, subjects were informed of the correctness  or incorrectness of the leader's prediction in the preceding phase. During a decision phase they were informed of the leader's  identity (a digit between one and three). And during both phases they were given the team score (as "Group oo Right") and the  member's own number in the group. The scope also displayed "Choose Leader" or "Predict A or B" as appropriate during the  trials.  Detailed descriptions of scope format, instructions, subject-arrangement in the experimental room, trial and phase times, and  methods of motivation may be found elsewhere (Binder et al., 1965).  The subjects were run for a total of 600 trials, with a rest period of ten minutes after the first 300 trials. During the  first 300 trials a 951 reinforcement condition was used, meaning that one member of the group was reinforced (scope displayed  "Ldr. Right") with a probability (v) of .9 for his predictions when leader, another member was reinforced with Tr = .5, and  the third with rr = .1. Starting with the 301st trial and continuing to the 600th, all three players were reinforced with a =  .5.  Reinforcement schedules were randomly determined in advance under the constraint that each proportion hold exactly in blocks  of ten trials. Four such schedules of 300 trials each were prepared with .9 blocking, four with .1 blocking, and 16 with .5  blocking. For the first 300 trials, each group was assigned one each of the .9, .1, and .591 sequences with the  restriction that no two groups had more than one sequence in common. The element of, say, member X is  conditioned to Y or Z and he votes for that other member to which it is conditioned. (Self votes are not permitted.) From the  viewpoint of a given member, the leader on a trial may be the individual for whom he voted, the individual for whom he did not  vote, or the member himself. Moreover, the leader on the trial may or may not be reinforced. The model assumes that the  probability the pattern will shift, after a trial, from the individual to whom it is attached to the other individual is a  function of the six contingencies defined by the product of these two sets of conditions. The probability of shift is assumed  to be c when the individual for whom the vote was cast became leader, but was not reinforced, or when the individual for whom  the vote was not cast became leader and he was reinforced. The probability of shift was assumed to be zero under each of the  other contingencies. (See the first two columns and the last column of Table 5.)  These axioms led to an eight-state Markov model with states XjY;Z, where X, Y, Z represent the members voting and i, j, k the  members for whom they voted. The possible values for the subscripts are as follows: i = Y, Z; j = X, Z; k = X, Y.  134 BINDER, WOLIN, AND TEREBINSKI  An example of a state for the 951 reinforcement condition is 955919 , in which case the ar = .9 member voted for the 7T = .5  member, .5 voted for .9, and l voted for .9. Let us now consider the implications of data for axiom structure. The characteristics of possible discrepancies between  predicted and obtained results are useful in determining directions of productive modification of the structure. First, we  turn to the postulated shift probabilities and the obtained relative frequencies of shift from trial n to trial n + 1 for the  contingencies in Table 5. Those probabilities which are assumed to be zero in the model have obtained values of .079, .184,  .179, and .187 (see Table 5). Similar differences from a zero value were found with the previous  LEARNING AND EXTINCTION OF LEADERSHIP PREFERENCES IN SMALL GROUPS 135  groups. Although the zero assumption seems to be a reasonable first approximation, it obviously ignores such factors as  gambler's fallacy, the operation of strategies, a personal desire to be leader, and idiosyncratic reactions to frustration.  The model assumes that the probability of a vote shift of a member from trial n to n + 1 depends on the member's choice on  trial n and the success or failure of the leader's decision, and is independent of the particular voting selections of the  other members. While the experimental method was so devised that the particular choices of other members were not revealed to  a given member, it was possible to infer certain patterns on the basis of miscellaneous information available to each member.  Prior to giving a few examples, it will be helpful to point out anew that an "R" appeared on the scope whenever all members  voted and the vote was tied (in which case the leader was selected randomly by the computer). The R did not appear when the  computer chose randomly on the occasion that one or more members failed to vote. Now for the first example, if X voted for Y  and Y became leader without the appearance of an R, X could conclude that Z did not vote for him (X). As a second example, if  X voted for Z and the R appeared, X could conclude that Y voted for X and Z voted for Y.  If a pheriomenon like mutual sociometric choosing were operative in the leadership context, one would expect a subject to use  whatever sociometric choice information there was available on a given trial to determine his vote on the following trial. To  test for this phenomenon, alternate sets of conditions were specified for each member on the basis of his vote and the pattern  of stimulus information presented to him. For one of the two sets listed for each of the three possible leadership results (X,  Y, or 7, becomes leader), the member of interest had information to the effect that one (or both) of the other members showed  a bias, pro or con, in the voting behavior relative to him. And for the other of the two sets, the member had information  indicating the absence of this bias or a bias in the other direction.  For this analysis, it is necessary to consider the particular voting patterns on trials in which one or more members failed to  vote, despite the fact that the states of the Markov process ignored vote failures. Even though these did not occur  frequently, vote failures proved to be sources of specification, in some contexts, and of ambiguity, in others, from the  viewpoint of sociometric choice. To specify a failure to vote by a given member, say X, the subscript n shall be used; thus,  for example, XnY,,Z.y specifies the condition whereby X failed to vote while Y voted for X and Z for Y. One possible path toward overcoming the shortcomings of the model in rate of change and lengths of leadership runs would be to  allow a shift parameter, to be estimated from the data, in place of the assumed zero values (see Table 5). This would  presumably slow down the learning process and decrease the expected average number of trials over which the same member is  leader. Of course the same result could also be achieved by a more drastic type of model revision toward one that allows for  sampling among a number of patterns.  DISCUSSION  The acquisition curves obtained with the present data show interesting differences from both predicted results and those  obtained in the earlier experiment (see Figs. 1 and 2). During the middle portion of the first 300 trials, for a total length  of over 150 trials, the .1 member was leader more often than the .5 member. Clearly no such tendency was found before. It was  not until after trial 210 that the increase started for .5's leadership which eventually led to more frequent selection than  .1. Much of the increase in the proportion of leadership for .5 was at the expense of the .9 member; after reaching the  predicted asymptote and remaining there for over 30 trials, .9 decreased as leader after trial 210 and remained below  prediction to the end of acquisition. The resulting discrepancies between obtained and predicted results are reflected in  Table 1.  The differences between earlier and present experiments in consistency of trend of acquisition curves make it difficult to  guess the course if the 951 reinforcement condition were continued after trial 300 with the present subjects. But it is clear  that they were quite sensitive to the shift to 555 reinforcement rates: Between trials 300 and 330 there was a marked drop in  the proportion of .9 leadership accompanied by a corresponding increase in .5 and .1 leadership. The change thereafter was  more gradual. Although the obtained extinction curves do fluctuate about the predicted curves somewhat (Fig. 1), they clearly  converge toward them. The earlier results differ mostly because of the unexpectedly  strong preference of the .9 member for the .5 rather than the. 1 member (see Table 4). A similar tendency is evident in the  present results, but to a lesser extent.  That the earlier preferences were not entirely extinguished over the final 300 trials is shown in the consistent tendency of  Table 4 for all members to prefer, over trials 521-600, the member with the higher previous reinforcement schedule. That is,  over trials 521-600, .9 votes for .5 more than .1, .5 more for .9 than .1, and .1 more for .9 than .5.
