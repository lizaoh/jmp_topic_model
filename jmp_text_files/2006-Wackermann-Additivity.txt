The above-described  duration reproduction task  ( drt ) is one of the principal methods of research in ‘time perception’ ( Bindra & Waksberg, 1956; Woodrow, 1951; Zakay, 1990 ) and frequently employed in experimental studies ( A.Eisler, 2003; Richards, 1964; Vierordt, 1868; Woodrow, 1930 ).

                            In all our experiments, and most of those reported in the literature, the subjects were instructed to not use helping techniques such as subvocal or ‘mental’ counting, foot tapping, etc. These strategies are known to increase precision of temporal judgement ( Grondin, 2001 ), but they may also distort the result of the study. Of interest is the subjects’ perception of ‘raw’ durations, that is to say, unstructured time intervals; not their sense of rhythm. 
                          However, the interpretation of  drt 
                         response curves remains a pertinent problem. Several models of psychophysical processes underlying duration reproduction have been proposed, e.g., the ‘parallel-clock model’ ( H.Eisler, 2003 ), and the ‘dual klepsydra model’  dkm 
                         ( Wackermann & Ehm, 2006 ;  Wackermann, Ehm, & Späti, 2003 ). Due to large dispersion of responses  r  for a given stimulus duration,  s , ( Fig. 2 ), estimates of individual response curves are subject to considerable errors. The usual empiricist strategy—fitting data to model-based theoretical response functions and comparing residual variances—is only of limited value for a qualified decision between competing models. A move beyond merely empirical criteria is thus required: we have to study our models in terms of analytical properties of the response functions.
