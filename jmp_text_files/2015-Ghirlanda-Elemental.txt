The distinction between elemental and configural models is a central one in associative learning theory, with considerable theoretical and empirical effort devoted to comparing the two approaches ( Ghirlanda, 2002; Harris, 2006; Kinder & Lachnit, 2003; Pearce, 1987, 1994; Pearce & Bouton, 2001; Vogel, Castro, & Saavedra, 2004; Wagner, 2003, 2008 ). The main point of contention regards the fundamental unit of learning. In elemental models, each perceptual element can acquire associative strength (the potential to cause a response) independent of other elements. In configural models, associative strength accrues to percepts as wholes.

The fact that existing elemental and configural models often make contrasting predictions ( Pearce & Bouton, 2001; Vogel et al., 2004 ) reinforces the intuitive belief that the distinction is an essential one. At the same time, many authors have noted that it appears often possible to construct an elemental model mimicking a configural one, and vice-versa.  Pearce (1994) , for example, remarks that some predictions of his configural model can be approximated by the  Rescorla and Wagner (1972)  elemental model, if the latter adopts a suitable stimulus representation. Similarly,  Wagner and Brandon (2001)  introduce an elemental model approximating, under some conditions,  Pearce’s (1987)  configural model. Moreover,  Kinder and Lachnit (2003)  have shown that adding a parameter to  Pearce’s (1994)   model enables it to interpolate between predictions considered typical of configural and elemental models. Elemental models that may behave like Pearce’s configural ones have also been proposed by  Harris (2006)  and  McLaren and Mackintosh (2002) . This literature suggests that elemental and configural models may not be as different as usually thought, but it has the limitation of having considered only specific models and particular experimental designs.

The nature of the relationship between elemental and configural models is also relevant to linking formal learning theory and neural processes. Elemental models are easily interpreted as a decision neuron that reads out information from distributed representations of stimuli ( Enquist & Ghirlanda, 2005; Haykin, 2008; Minsky, 1969 ), a mode of operation that appears biologically plausible (for instance:   Abbott, Rolls, & Tovee, 1996; Hung, Kreiman, Poggio, & DiCarlo, 2005; Salinas & Abbott, 1994 ). Configural models, on the other hand, seem to assume (sometimes explicitly:   Pearce, 1994 ), that each stimulus pattern is ultimately represented by a single neuron (or group of neurons with identical response properties). That is, configural models seem to rely on a localistic representation of stimuli similar to the “grandmother cells” that most neuroscientists reject ( Levy, Hasson, & Malach, 2004 ,  Quiroga, 2013 ,  Rolls, Treves, & Tovee, 1997 , but see  Bowers, 2009  and  Roy, 2013 ). Configural models would receive a more biologically plausible interpretation if one could show that the computations they assume can be performed based solely on distributed representations, which would be the case if one could derive equivalent elemental models.

Within this context, my aim is to investigate the relationship between the elemental and configural approaches in generality. In Section   2 , I define the two approaches formally. In Section   3 , I present four theorems:

I have attempted to understand whether the elemental and configural approaches have inherently different abilities, or whether they are, in fact, equivalent. I showed that we can always construct a configural model equivalent to a given elemental one, provided generalization functions are not restricted to measures of similarity, and that we can construct an elemental model equivalent to a given configural one provided the latter has a positive definite generalization function. Existing configural models ( Estes, 1973, 1994; Kinder & Lachnit, 2003; Pearce, 1987, 1994 ) satisfy this condition, and positive definite functions appear sufficient to describe stimulus generalization ( Ghirlanda & Enquist, 2003; Jäkel, Schölkopf, & Wichmann, 2008b ), yet we cannot conclusively rule out that non-positive definite functions may prove useful in associative learning theory.

These results have two main implications. First, the existence of elemental equivalents to configural models implies that the abstract computations assumed in configural models can be performed in simple neural network models without resorting to localistic stimulus representations (see the Introduction). This means that there is no  a priori  reason to consider the configural formalism less biological plausible than the elemental one. Second, the elemental and configural approaches cannot be distinguished empirically, at least when we restrict configural models to using positive definite generalization functions. Even if the two approaches can be considered equivalent, however, the elemental–configural distinction does not become meaningless. Rather, both approaches may retain heuristic value because intuitions that come naturally when thinking elementally may be difficult to reach when thinking configurally, and vice-versa. The notion of element replacement, for example, is easily grasped from an elemental perspective, yet corresponds to the unintuitive generalization factors in Eq.  (33) . Conversely, reproducing  Pearce’s (1987)  simple generalization function in Eq.  (47)  in an elemental model appears to require unintuitive fine-tuning of the number, pattern, and value of stimulus elements (we cannot rule out, however, that representations simpler than the one considered above may exists).

In conclusion, a succinct summary of this paper is that the historical elemental–configural controversy may be reinterpreted constructively as the quest to understand how nervous systems represent stimuli, or, equivalently, how they generalize. Because we still ignore a lot about the principles that underlie stimulus generalization and representation, it seems fruitful to be able to entertain both configural and elemental viewpoints. The results presented here assist us in relating the two approaches, and hopefully to integrate insights from both.
