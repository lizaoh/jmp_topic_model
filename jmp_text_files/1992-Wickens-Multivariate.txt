JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 36, 213-234 (1992)  Maximum-Likelihood Estimation of a Multivariate Gaussian Rating Model with Excluded Data  THOMAS D. WICKENS  University of California at Los Angeles  This paper describes the maximum-likelihood fitting of a bivariate (more generally, multivariate) Gaussian signal-detection  model for rating data. The model applies whenever pairs of detection ratings are recorded under several stimulus conditions.  The underlying representation is a bivariate Gaussian distribution with orthogonal cutpoints determining the responses. Each  stimulus condition is potentially represented by a separate Gaussian distribution, but the same cutpoints are used throughout.  Parameters of the Gaussian distribution can be equated across stimuli and linear hypotheses about their values tested. The  estimation algorithm applies to incomplete tables, thus permitting certain cells to be excluded from influencing the parameter  estimates. The procedure combines methods drawn from (1) conventional rating-scale detection experiments, (2) a general  multidimensional recognition model, (3) the analysis of the polychoric correlation coefficient, and (4) estimation in an  incomplete table. Since its inception (see especially Green & Swets, 1966, and papers in Swets, 1964) this  model has provided procedures that integrate the results of different detection conditions and that summarize performance in a  small number of parameters. The model is sufficiently well known to require only brief comment here. Essentially, it describes  the detection or discrimination of noisy signals. Different stimuli are assumed to evoke different random distributions of  sensation on a latent dimension. The observer makes decisions regarding the signals by adopting criterion cuts along this  dimension. First, the observations are treated as mutually independent. Sequential dependencies among them or  associations between stimulus types at the response level are not accommodated. Second, the observations are treated as  probabilistically homogeneous. The presence of practice or fatigue effects or of between-session differences in a multiday  experiment violates the model. Similar sampling assumptions underlie most statistical treatments of data of this type.  The effect of violations of the sampling assumptions depends in part on the use that is to be made of the model. Violations of  homogeneity act to increase the variance of the responses, thus attenuating the differences in mean among groups (which are  measured relative to the standard deviation of the reference group) and possibly decreasing the correlation. Minor violations  of independence probably do not greatly alter estimates of the model's parameters. These violations, particularly those of  independence, have a more substantial effect on results that depend on the purported sampling variability, such as standard  errors of the parameter estimates and hypothesis tests of their values. When one questions the sampling assumptions, these  tests should be treated with caution. The risks of violation are considerably lowered when practiced observers are used.  2. PARAMETER ESTIMATION PROCEDURE  Closed formulae for estimates of the parameters of the multivariate Gaussian detection model do not exist. Estimates must be  found using iterative search methods. When this happens it may be necessary to select the group with the largest range of responses as a  reference category and for each other stimulus to piece together the relative means and variances from categories where both  tables have positive values. Of course, as the responses to different stimuli cease to overlap at all, both the model itself  and its starting point become indeterminate.  The Newton-Raphson procedure can behave erratically and diverge when the initial point is very poor. The point obtained from  the steps above is usually accurate enough to work. However, it is not adjusted to omit the influence of any excluded cells.  When these cells substantially violate the model that fits the remaining data, the initial point may be so bad that the  algorithm diverges. One can invent ways to make better initial estimates, but it is easier to modify the x;ik in the excluded  cells temporarily so that they are roughly consistent with the balance of the data. A better initial point is obtained and the  Newton-Raphson algorithm works properly. Because only the excluded cells are changed, the final estimates are unaffected. Specific results for the bivariate model are not available, but simulation studies  indicate that when some individual cell frequencies are less than five, the goodnessof-fit tests tend to reject a correct  single-response Gaussian model too often (Metz, 1989). In general, goodness-of-fit tests with large tables that contain both  many observations and small expected frequencies are likely to fail, even when the deviations from the model are small. This  tendency limits their usefulness. CONCLUDING REMARKS  The multivariate latent Gaussian model presented in this paper is very general. The description of a categorization as the  partitioning of an underlying continuous distribution is conceptually simple and attractive, as is the interpretation of the  differences among several conditions as differences in the parameters of several such distributions linked through a common  response mechanism. Substantive differences among conditions can be expressed as constraints on these distributions and tested  as null hypotheses about their parameters.  The latent Gaussian model is not restricted to the visual-detection task used to exemplify it here. Several other applications  of this type of model were referred to briefly in the introduction of this paper. As an extension of the signal-detection  models, the model can interpret psychophysical data whenever both stimuli and responses are multidimensional. Indeed, it can  be used whenever associated ordered categorical responses have been collected under several conditions, be they ratings or not.  The estimation procedures presented here separate the Gaussian distribution functions from the functions that link the  parameters of these distributions across the response sets (the stimulus types in the present example). Without substantially  modifying the maximum-likelihood approach, algorithms similar to the present one can fit models with structural connections  among the stimulus conditions that are far richer than parameter identity. Such extensions allow a large and complex set of  theoretical descriptions to be applied to multivariate categorical data.  REFERENCES  ASHBY, F. G., & TOWNSEND, J. T. (1986).
