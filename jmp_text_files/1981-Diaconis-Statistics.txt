INTRODUCTION  As part of an investigation of visual perception, Julesz performed the following kind of experiment. Generate two different  random patterns, place them side by side, and show them to subjects. Certain patterns are easy to distinguish, for example, if  one is dark and the other light, or if one is homogeneous and the other has clusters. Julesz introduced the "first- and  second-order statistics" to describe darkness and homogeneity, respectively. A mathematical definition of these terms will be  given below.  The "Julesz conjecture" is that if two random patterns have the same first- and second-order statistics, they cannot be  visually distinguished. An early overview of the evidence can be found in Julesz (1975). Some random patterns with the same  first- and second-order statistics, but different higher-order statistics, are described in Rosenblatt and Slepian (1962);  also see Julesz, Gilbert, Shepp, and Frisch (1973). Recently, some methods were discovered for generating patterns which have  the same first- and second-order statistics, but are visually different. These counterexamples are reported in Julesz,  Gilbert, and Victor (1978) and Gilbert (1980). Recent surveys on  Research was partially supported by NSF Grants MCS-77-16974 (P. The idea behind  the construction, suggested by David Aldous, is simple: divide the plane up into random regions and put an independent piece  of a 0-matrix into each region.
