In knowledge space theory (KST;   Doignon & Falmagne, 1985, 1999; Falmagne & Doignon, 2011 ) the knowledge state of a student is the collection of all problems that she/he masters in a given field of knowledge, and a knowledge structure is the collection of all possible knowledge states in a given population of students. The basic local independence model (BLIM;   Doignon & Falmagne, 1999; Falmagne & Doignon, 1988 ) is a probabilistic model applied, in KST, for the stochastic assessment of knowledge, and for the empirical validation of knowledge structures.

This model has lately received some attention ( de Chiusole, Stefanutti, Anselmi, & Robusto, in press; Heller, under revision; Schrepp, 2005; Stefanutti & Robusto, 2009 ), particularly concerning its identifiability. At the moment, there is still incomplete knowledge about this fundamental issue of the BLIM. Notwithstanding, a few results have been recently obtained in this direction. For instance,  Spoto, Stefanutti, and Vidotto (2012)  have shown that the BLIM is not identifiable for two broad classes of knowledge structures named, respectively, forward-graded and backward-graded. Moreover,  Stefanutti, Heller, Anselmi, and Robusto (2012)  developed a procedure for testing the local identifiability of the BLIM with arbitrary knowledge structures on sets of items having a moderate size. Furthermore, some connections between the BLIM and latent class models, including identifiability issues, have been pointed out by  Schrepp (2005)  and  Ünlü (2011) .

While it is known that the identifiability of the BLIM strictly depends on the specific knowledge structure to which it is applied, it is still not clear how to separate all knowledge structures, on a finite set of items, that make the BLIM unidentifiable, from the remaining ones. In other words it is not known, in general, how to read the identifiability of the BLIM, directly from the combinatorial properties of the knowledge structure to which it is applied. At the moment this knowledge seems to be restricted to the aforementioned forward- and backward-graded cases. Moreover, what to do when the BLIM is not identifiable for a particular knowledge structure is a sensible question, for which there is no ultimate answer at the present time.

This article can be essentially divided into two main parts: the first one concerns theoretical results on the lack of identifiability of the BLIM for certain classes of knowledge structures that frequently occur in practical applications. This material draws upon theoretical results, obtained in  Spoto et al. (2012) , on two broad classes of knowledge structures called forward-graded and backward-graded. The second part is a collection of simulation studies whose aim is to provide evidence supporting a particular conjecture on how to restore local identifiability of the BLIM, when it is applied to an arbitrary (not necessarily forward- and/or backward-graded) knowledge structure.

The BLIM is a particular case of latent class models ( Lazarsfeld & Neil, 1968 ). Although identifiability problems are a well-known issue in latent class analysis ( McHugh, 1956 ), the BLIM deserves separate investigation for various reasons. In the first place it introduces the restriction of local independence and the link between conditional probabilities of the response patterns and error rates. Furthermore, as pointed out by  Spoto et al. (2012) , identification of the BLIM strongly depends on the specific knowledge structure to which the model is applied. This is a specific feature of this model that is not shared by latent class models in general. Given a finite fixed set  
                         Q 
                       of items, the collection of all knowledge structures on  
                         Q 
                       could, in principle, be partitioned into two subsets: one containing knowledge structures for which the BLIM is identifiable and the other containing knowledge structures for which the BLIM is unidentifiable. Two important types of knowledge structures for which the BLIM is not identifiable have been studied by  Spoto et al. (2012)  and they are the subject of the next section. It has to be mentioned

Theorem 8  suggests a procedure that could be applied to solve the BLIM’s unidentifiability issues with FG and BG structures, namely adding equally informative items. The use of equally informative items in the BLIM, or similar models, is not new. For instance,  Ünlü (2006)  applied equally informative items for the estimation of response error rates.

Stefanutti et al. (2012)  provided a procedure for testing local identifiability of the BLIM for an arbitrary knowledge structure and an arbitrary point in the parameter space. The aim of the simulation studies described in this section is to provide evidence in favor of the conjecture that the introduction of equally informative items is sufficient for restoring the  local  identifiability of the BLIM, not only in the case of FG and BG structures, but with respect to any knowledge structure. Although rather counterintuitive, this conjecture is based on the following consideration: when local identifiability concerns one of the error rates of the BLIM, it is implied that this error parameter cannot be separated from the probability of one or more knowledge states; more precisely there will be a tradeoff between this parameter and other parameters in the model. If  
                         p 
                       and  
                         q 
                       are two equally informative items they belong, of course, to the same knowledge states, but not necessarily to the same response patterns. What accounts for response patterns containing one of the items, but not the other, are the error rates and not the knowledge state probabilities. Thus, the introduction of equally informative items should allow for a separation of an error rate from a knowledge state probability.

In all the simulations, the local identifiability of the BLIM is tested by means of a MATLAB function named BLIMIT ( Stefanutti et al., 2012 ). Thus, our results are restricted to this specific type of identifiability.

Drawing upon the local identifiability concepts described in Section   3 , the BLIMIT function computes the Jacobian matrix of the prediction function of the BLIM for a given point  
                            θ 
                          in the parameter space of the model. If the matrix is full rank, then the model is locally identifiable. If not, by analyzing the null space of the Jacobian matrix, the BLIMIT function detects tradeoff dimensions, that is sets of parameters for which a specific tradeoff exists. A tradeoff among a set of parameters occurs whenever a change in one of them is compensated by a change in the others, while keeping constant the prediction function.

The results showed that the BLIM for all the structures in which each item has an equally informative item, was locally identifiable. Therefore, at least for all the structures on domains of three and four items, the introduction of equally informative items solves local identifiability issues.

We systematically tested the two conditions for all structures of three and four items and concluded that they hold true for all those knowledge structures. Therefore, at least for these knowledge structures, whenever a single error parameter is contained in one tradeoff dimension, this can always be explained by the forward- or backward-gradedness of the structure.

The aim of the second simulation study was to test our conjecture on larger structures. We used structures on a domain of five and six items respectively. Even with this relatively small cardinality of the domain, the number of potential structures is huge and computationally intractable. Thus we performed the simulation on a subset of the set of structures on five and six items.

A random construction of structures was performed by uniformly choosing among all the possible structures on five and six items. The selection was uniformly stratified on the basis of the number of states contained in each structure. Within the structures on five items, 25 strata were created by varying the number of states between four and 28 states. For each stratum 42 structures were randomly selected (thus, the number of simulated structures was 1050). For the structures of six items, the number of states varied between four and 60, again 42 structures were selected for each stratum (the total number of simulated structures was 2394). We tested local identifiability for each structure, then, using the same procedure described in the previous simulation study, we introduced equivalent items and tested again local identifiability.

Out of the 1050 tested structures on five items, for 397 (37.8%) the BLIM was unidentifiable. After introducing the equally informative items, for none of the 1050 resulting structures, the BLIM was unidentifiable. Even for 387 (16%) out of the 2394 structures on six items the model was unidentifiable. The introduction of equally informative items solved unidentifiability issues for all the unidentifiable cases. From a descriptive perspective, it has to be stressed that, in both cases, most structures with identification problems presented either very low or very high cardinality. Our results further supported our conjecture and encourage further studies on this topic.

In the present article, it was shown that many different instances of knowledge structures turn out to have problems with identifiability. Specifically, in all the structures described in the first part of this article, unidentifiability of the BLIM is caused by a kind of gradation, namely backward- and/or forward-gradedness.

The importance of the identifiability issues becomes higher when we consider that most of the structures described here have clear pedagogical, psychological, and computational interpretations and applications.

The introduction of equally informative items can be a reasonable solution of identification problems. From a practical point of view, the question of producing pairs of equally informative items arises. In this direction, two levels can be distinguished: the level of problem types and the level of instances of problem types. Although interesting, this aspect is beyond the theoretical scope of the present article.

Our simulation studies strongly suggest that the introduction of equally informative items (either for every item of the domain or for a specific item) solves any local identifiability issue for all the tested structures. This result encourages further research in this direction, and demonstrates a deeper relationship between unidentifiability and forward- and backward-gradedness.
