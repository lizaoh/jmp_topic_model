The situation which we consider is that a number (n) of quantifiable judgements X1, X21 ..., xare synthesized into the  quantity f (x, , XZ,..., x. This problem occurred in the applied theory called the analytic hierarchy process (Saaty 1977,  1980, 1982) to deal with decisions involving a large number of alternatives, criteria and decision makers. The analytic  hierarchy process is a multiattribute approach to decision making. It is being used widely today in industry and in  government. For example, the staff of Woods Gordon, a large Canadian consulting firm, has used it in nearly a dozen  applications between 1981 and 1983 to health, industrial, strategic, and other problems. In this process a number of people  participate to provide pairwise comparison judgements. In comparing two elements with respect to an attribute in the adjacent  upper level of the hierarchy one answers the question: which element is perceived to have the given attribute more and how  much more? A scale of relative strength has been developed which the participants could apply uniformly.  When several people are involved, they usually justify their judgement in a reasoned debate. Often consensus may not be  reached and one must synthesize the judgements. The synthesis procedure is first explained to the participants, the judgements  are elicited and then synthesized. In general, the process of judgement involves making pairwise comparisons between alternatives according to a given criterion.  The individual judgements are made by comparing an object, say A, with another, say B, according to the given criterion and  these individual judgements are synthesized into a single judgement. Another example of a similar procedure is in  psychophysics: scaling by magnitude estimation or ratio estimation. Marley (1972) points out that a subject calculates a  ratio, perhaps subconsciously, when he or she compares two components. So, here too, the judgements are ratios. If the  individuals had compared the second object, B, with the first, A, these ratios would be replaced by their reciprocals. It is  natural to assume that the synthesized judgement also changes to the reciprocal. The synthesizing function f, enabling one to  develop a rule for group judgement, will be assumed to satisfy this reciprocal property. The second author has been using the  geometric mean for this purpose and the question was whether a more general type of function existed which satisfied the  reciprocal property.
