Given a set  Q  of problems (or items), a  knowledge state  is the subset of all problems in  Q  that some student is capable of solving. A structure  (Q, K ) , in which  
                         K 
                       is the collection of all knowledge states on  Q  (thus,  
                         K 
                       is a subset of the power set 2 
                         Q 
                       of  Q ) is called a  knowledge structure . If such a collection is closed under union, and contains at least the empty set ∅ and the full set  Q , then it is called a  knowledge space . These are the essential concepts of knowledge space theory of  Doignon and Falmagne (1985,1999) . Various approaches can be found in the literature for constructing a knowledge space in practice (see, e.g.,  Koppen & Doignon, 1990 ;  Koppen, 1993 ;  Dowling, 1993 ;  Lukas & Albert, 1993 ;  Albert & Held, 1994 ;  Theuns, 1998 ). One of them—perhaps the most common—is  expert query . A human expert is queried with questions of the form “Does failing all items in a particular subset  P  of  Q  entail failing some other item  q  from  Q ?”. When responses to all questions of this form are obtained, the pairs ( P , q ) for which this response is positive constitute a subset of 2 
                         Q 
                      × Q , thus a relation between the power set of  Q  and  Q  which is called an  entail relation .  Koppen & Doignon (1990)  have shown that a knowledge space can always be recovered from an entail relation, on the basis of a 1–1 correspondence between the family of all knowledge spaces on  Q  and the set of all entail relations for  Q .

A naive expert query procedure obtains from an expert a response for each single question ( P , q ) in 2 
                         Q 
                      × Q  with  q ∉ P . Although correct, this approach is unfeasible in practice since the number of questions increases exponentially in | Q |. As an example, with 20 items, the order of magnitude is 10 million questions. An entail relation is characterized by certain properties, like some form of “transitivity” (see  Koppen & Doignon, 1990 ). A non-naive query procedure exploits these properties to produce inferences: a question is not presented to the expert (or the consistency of the expert's response is checked) whenever the response to this question can be inferred from the responses to previously presented questions (see, e.g.,  Koppen & Doignon, 1990 ;  Koppen, 1993 ;  Dowling, 1993 ). In the sequel, the term  query procedure  refers to non-naive query procedures for constructing a knowledge space.

Existing query procedures assume that the domain  Q  of questions is known in advance (i.e., at the start of a query), and that it is fixed during the whole query process. The outcome of the procedure is an entail relation, which by the above-mentioned 1–1 correspondence is converted to a knowledge space  
                         K 
                       on  Q . If a new question  q ′∉ Q  is added to the domain, thus obtaining  Q ′= Q ∪{ q ′}, the existing procedures do not provide a way to use the information stored in  
                         K 
                       to build the new space  
                         K ′  on  Q ′. This means that either these procedures should be adapted or  
                         K ′  should be constructed from scratch even when a considerable amount of information is, indeed, available. Clearly, this turns out to be an inefficient way to extend a knowledge space to new items. The subject matter of this paper is a query procedure for constructing a knowledge space incrementally. If  Q  is extended to  Q ′, such a procedure provides an explicit way to use the information stored in  
                         K 
                       for constructing  
                         K ′ , and avoids to ask questions that can be inferred from the entail relation corresponding to  
                         K 
                      . The new knowledge space  
                         K ′  is called an  extension  of  
                         K 
                      .

In the next section an algorithm for extending a knowledge space to a larger domain of knowledge is introduced and shown to be correct.  Section 3  deals with a special question that is characteristic for an incremental query procedure: the order in which items are added to a domain of knowledge. The basic idea underlying this section is that the efficiency of the incremental query procedure is affected by (1) the order of insertion of the items, and (2) the order of presentation of the questions. Here, the problem to find an optimal order is posed. Finally,  Section 4  illustrates a series of tests of the proposed algorithm against other existing query algorithms.

In this section an algorithm is developed which, given a knowledge space  
                         K 
                       on a domain of items  Q  and a new item  z  not in  Q , specifies a query procedure for determining the knowledge space on the extended domain  Q ∪{ z }. The queries will be of the form 
                         “Given some specific subset  B  of items and another single item  x , does failing all of the items in  B  entail failing the item  x ?”,

A positive response to this question amounts to saying that for any state  L  in the knowledge space on  Q ∪{ z },  L ∩ B =∅ implies  x ∉ L . As  Koppen and Doignon (1990)  have shown, such queries produce the  entail relation  on  Q ∪{ z } that is in 1–1 correspondence with the knowledge space.

In an actual application of this procedure a decision has to be made concerning the  insertion order  of the items, i.e., which item is added to the domain at each step. This choice has practical consequences, since, as will be clear from the previous section, at each step finding the extension of the current knowledge space is partly based on inferences, and the number of inferences that can be made will generally vary for different insertion orders of the items. Since the number of potential queries to the expert grows exponentially with the size of the domain, it is of some practical significance to try and find an optimal insertion order, one that maximizes the total number of inferences to be made and thus minimizes the actual number of queries the expert is exposed to.

With this idea of optimization in mind it becomes also relevant that in the algorithms of  Sections 2.1 and 2.2  (or 2.3) the “Choose” statements leave unspecified which subset  K , respectively which pair ( K , q ), is to be chosen at each point. Here also, there may be consequences for the number of inferences to be made. Item ordering and query ordering can be combined in order to specify a particular setting under which the incremental procedure is applied. To this end the following notation is introduced. An ordered triplet of the form [ xyz ], where  x , y , z ∈{A,D}, denotes a  query strategy . The symbol ‘A’ stands for “ascending order”, and the symbol ‘D’ means “descending order”. So, for instance, [ADA] stands for the query strategy in which the order of the items is  ascending , and the order of presentation of the queries is  descending  in the first subprocedure and  ascending  in the second subprocedure. Thus, in the whole there are eight query strategies. The question how these query strategies compare with respect to efficiency is addressed by a simulation study reported below.

This section presents four studies in which the incremental query procedure (henceforth,  iqp ) is compared with other query algorithms. In principle, eight different query strategies [ xyz ] might be used in  iqp , but it was decided to run  iqp  with strategy [AAD] only, which in the previous section showed the best performance (in terms of number of questions asked). This version of  iqp  will be denoted  AAD . This algorithm is compared with Koppen's standard  query  algorithm (henceforth,  KSQ ), rewritten in C++, but mimicking the original algorithm in all essential features.

Study  3.  Test on  “ random ”  spaces : In Study 3 knowledge spaces were generated by “random experts”. In its simplest form, a random expert is one whose response to every query is governed by a single parameter  π ∈(0,1), fixed throughout the query process, specifying the probability of a positive response. In a more sophisticated version of such a random expert the order of insertion of the items might be considered. In an incremental procedure, given a space  
                         K 
                       on  Q  and a new item  z ∉ Q , any query ( P , q ) posed to the expert involves  z : either  z = q  or  z ∈ P . Given an ascending, respectively descending order,  z  is roughly judged to be more, respectively less “difficult” than all the old items, and it is reasonable that this is reflected in the probability of a positive response.

This idea is implemented by introducing two parameters governing the responses of the expert to a query ( P , q ): a probability  α  of a positive response given that  z ∈ P , and a probability  β  of a positive response given that  z = q . Thus, with an ascending insertion order,  α  is the probability that the response of the expert is “inconsistent” with the difficulty order in the sense of indicating that failing an additional “more difficult” item entails that an “easier” item is failed, while  β  is the probability that the response of the expert is “consistent” with the difficulty order in the sense of indicating that failing some set of “easier” items entails that a “more difficult” item is failed. With a descending insertion order, “easier” and “more difficult” switch rôles, and so do “consistent” and “inconsistent”.

That is, each of the four procedures was applied to (a simulated expert whose responses were based on) each of the 80 random spaces.

In all simulations reported here the  iqp  algorithm does better than the incremental adaptation of  query  and, except for one case, it even outperforms standard  query  on the whole domain. Simulations show that this difference is not negligible especially for the incremental version of  query . In fact, especially with large domains, and at least with the kind of spaces considered here, a considerable amount of questions can be saved by the  iqp  algorithm.

Regarding a general comparison between query on the whole domain and incremental query, Study 3 shows that the performance of the incremental query procedures tends to get worse as the cardinality of the space becomes large.  Fig. 5  suggests that, with larger spaces, the performance ratio of the  iqp  might slope down to negative values. However, in these cases alternative query strategies might be considered (e.g., the [DDD] strategy), moreover, citing  Koppen (1993, p. 17) , “since our objective is to use the knowledge spaces in knowledge assessment procedures, we have to assume a certain amount of structure anyway to make the whole enterprise worthwhile. Such a space will be workable in practice only if the number of states is a tiny fraction of the number of subsets, and only in this case will the restrictions embodied in this space produce a real gain in efficiency”.

In conclusion, the results obtained in this series of studies suggest that we may capitalize on the information given by a preliminary linear order on the items for saving queries to the expert. In all probability, the difference in performance observed between the incremental version of  query  and the  iqp  algorithm may be explained by the fact that the latter has an explicit way to deal with this kind of information (see, in this connection, the results obtained in the comparison of the eight query strategies of the  iqp ).
