Suppose a patient tells you that she is indifferent between living 40 years in poor health and living 20 years in full health. If she is also indifferent between living 30 years in poor health and 15 years in full health, then she has exhibited behavior consistent with the assumption of constant proportional time tradeoff. More generally, under constant proportional time tradeoff (CP-TTO), if for some health states  a  and  b , we find  ax ∼ by  is an indifference expressed by the patient between living  x  years in a health state  a  and  y  years in a health state  b , then for all  c >0, such that  cx , cy ⩽ M ,  a [ cx ]∼ b [ cy ].

Under mild conditions, CP-TTO implies the necessary assumptions for additive conjoint measurement and a multiplicative representation of riskless value. Because risky utility represents the same preferences over outcomes as riskless value, risky utility is a strictly increasing function of riskless value ( Keeney & Raiffa, 1993 ). This fact will allow us to identify several useful characteristics of utility functions for which we know that preferences satisfy CP-TTO. Following a similar argument to that of  Maas and Wakker (1994) , we show that CP-TTO together with a few assumptions of risky choice imply specific parametric forms of the utility of duration, including in one case a model that is inconsistent with utility independence. We also find that CP-TTO simplifies axiomatizations of quality-adjusted life years (QALY) representations in the sense that if CP-TTO is among the assumptions of a QALY axiomatization, other standard preference assumptions, e.g., double cancellation or utility independence, become redundant and can be dropped from the axiom system. This line of argument is similar in spirit, if not in the formal details, to arguments in  Wakker (1989) ,  Ebert (1988) , and  Miyamoto and Wakker (1996) .

It is well known that under CP-TTO, when utility independence is invoked, the utility function is a member of the log/power family. Characterizing the utility of survival duration reduces in this case to the problem of estimating a single risk parameter ( Pliskin, Shepard, & Weinstein, 1980 ). We show that invoking constant relative risk aversion (CRRA) along with CP-TTO also implies a log/power utility function. Thus, CRRA and utility independence imply each other when CP-TTO is invoked (This equivalence occurs when the health domain satisfies a restricted solvability condition. When restricted solvability is not assumed, CRRA appears to be a stronger condition than utility independence (see Theorem 4.1)). When constant absolute risk aversion (CARA) is then added, the utility function becomes linear and the riskless value function represents risky choices as well. In another line of development, we show that the utility function is an exponentiated linear model when CARA and CP-TTO are assumed and utility independence is dropped. Under this QALY model, estimation of a risk parameter for one health state in combination with a riskless health value elicitation in relation to any other health state, determine the curvature of the utility function. The exponentiated linear QALY model (or expo-linear QALY model), is the only QALY model that is known to violate utility independence, but is tractable for decision analysis. Keeney and Raiffa (1993, p. 259, Eq. (3.69)) first put forth this model for use in cases when utility independence is violated, but until now it has not received an axiomatic treatment.

QALYs are the most important outcome measure in medical decision making. Therefore, the study of how best to represent and elicit preferences using QALY models deserves attention.  Maas and Wakker (1994)  explored ways to minimize the role of risky choice in the axiomatization and elicitation of QALYs by introducing riskless value conditions. A reduction in the role of risky choice is desirable for several reasons. Risky choices are often unreliable and subject to bias ( Johnson & Schkade, 1989 ). Moreover, they involve probabilities that can be difficult to convey and difficult for laypersons to understand (see  Maas & Wakker, 1994 ). Maas and Wakker begin by assuming an additive representation of riskless value and a weakened version of utility independence. From these conditions they deduce that the QALY model is multiplicative and the utility of duration is exponential. Thus, utility curvature is characterized by a single exponential parameter. Thus, risky choice plays what is probably the unavoidable role of determining the degree of utility curvature.

Our approach is similar in spirit to  Maas and Wakker (1994) , but differs notably from their approach in a couple of ways. First, we extend QALYs to a case where utility independence is violated. Second,  Maas and Wakker's (1994)  use the Thomsen condition to guarantee additive conjoint measurement (see Definition 3, Section 6.2.1 of  Krantz, Luce, Suppes, & Tversky, 1971 ). The Thomsen condition does not imply but is implied by CP-TTO. Despite the fact that it is a weaker condition, we do not use the Thomsen condition explicitly in our characterization of QALYs. Instead, we use CP-TTO. The only rigorous statistical test of the Thomsen condition that is currently known ( Falmagne, 1976 ) is not feasible in the domain of health. In contrast, empirical tests of CP-TTO are straightforward in the domain of health and as we will show, CP-TTO constrains the form of the utility function in ways that the Thomsen condition alone does not.

Several published studies have evaluated CP-TTO (e.g.,  Bleichrodt & Johannesson, 1997 ;  Stalmeier, Bezembinder, & Unic, 1996 ;  Bleichrodt, Pinto, Abellan,  in press). However, published studies, thus far, have tested CP-TTO on group data as opposed to replicating observations within subjects and testing CP-TTO using an individual subject analysis. The individual subject approach can detect violations of CP-TTO on a case-by-case basis whereas in a group analysis such evaluations of individual subject data are not possible.

Because CP-TTO is a less general test of an additive representation than the Thomsen condition some subjects may not satisfy CP-TTO, but may still have an additive representation of riskless preference. This stands as a limitation to adopting CP-TTO as a riskless value condition. This limitation would pose a significant problem if the CP-TTO axiom were often violated in empirical studies. However, the majority of empirical studies have supported CP-TTO, particularly for durations greater than 5 years ( Bleichrodt & Johannesson, 1997 ;  Miyamoto, 1985 ;  Stalmeier et al., 1996 ). Not all studies have obtained clear positive results, however.  Sackett and Torrance (1978)  found negative results. This study involved durations less than 5 years and was confounded by a change in unit of the survival duration stimuli.  Bleichrodt et al.  (in press) report equivocal results for the validity of CP-TTO. In this study, the CP-TTO hypothesis was retained under a parametric analysis but was rejected under a nonparametric analysis in an experiment that employed a conventional assessment strategy. Also, in this study CP-TTO for an unconventional elicitation exercise was rejected, suggesting that perhaps such tradeoffs are not procedurally invariant. However, whatever the empirical status of CP-TTO in the aggregate may be, it remains quite easy for a decision analyst seeking to represent a client's health utility function to evaluate the descriptive accuracy of CP-TTO for any client through a simple experiment.

In  Section 3 , we present notation and structural assumptions, and show that CP-TTO under these structural assumptions implies an additive value function for the riskless preference relation. In  Section 4 , we relate these results to QALY models for preference under risk. When combined with our results for riskless preference under CP-TTO, simple assumptions for preference under risk yield different parametric QALY models. Interestingly, the latter developments do not require utility independence assumptions.

Thus, far we have limited our discussion to better-than-death health states. This is largely because, as discussed below, CP-TTO applies only between health states that are either all better-than-death or all worse-than-death. It is logically possible to test CP-TTO in the domain of worse-than-death health states. However, subjects may find such exercises unsettling, or at the very least unusual and unfamiliar. Our results for better-than death health states could easily be extended to worse-than-death health states under a separate value function. The value function for better-than-death health states is then positive, for worse-than-death health states it is negative, and for equal-to-death health states it is 0. The two value functions can be pieced together into an overall continuous value function with singularity at zero duration ( Miyamoto, Wakker, Bleichrodt, & Peters, 1998 ). A sufficient solution to piece together the value functions would be to include a certainty equivalent for a ‘mixed’ gamble (i.e., one outcome better-than-death and the other outcome of the gamble worse-than-death) to determine the relative difference in steepness between the two value functions. In practice, health states worse-than-death are difficult to elicit and are less commonly included in decision analyses. Therefore, we do not pursue the issue of health states worse-than-death any further.

In this section, we extend our results to situations involving decisions under risk. We begin by assuming binary rank-dependent utility theory. This theory constitutes the framework of assumptions regarding preference under risk within which extensions of the preceding theorems will be developed. Next we postulate a condition that is self-evident in the domain of health (the zero condition). Under the aforementioned assumptions, we show that simple preference conditions concerning risk posture directly imply specific multiattribute parametric forms for QALYs.

It is worth mentioning when maximal health states arise. Even though a person may consider themselves in full health they may be able to entertain a situation with an infinite number of health improvements and no maximal health state. Being able to run or swim faster and faster or, experiencing a series of smaller and smaller incremental reductions in chronic pain, but never achieving full relief are two examples that illustrate such a situation. In each case, no maximal health state exists. However, in the practice of decision analysis, maximal health states  always  exist. This is because decision models (decision trees, Markov models and influence diagrams) admit only a finite set of health states into the decision problem. Weak ordered finite sets have maximal element(s). The common practice in medical decision analysis is to define full health as “health without symptoms or problems” and treat this state as maximal in any applied health decision analysis. This is typically quite reasonable, because clients can imagine this state and medical technology is geared toward the relief of symptoms and problems.

If a maximal health state satisfies CARA and CRRA, a CP-TTO structure guarantees that every health state satisfies these two conditions. Constant proportional time trade-off together with mild conditions implies an additive riskless value function and places powerful constraints on the functional form utility when well-known risk posture conditions hold. In this paper, we have outlined how CP-TTO in combination with risk posture conditions leads to several different QALY representations.

By invoking CP-TTO in the context of risk posture conditions, the role of risky choice in QALY elicitation is reduced to risk parameter estimation for a single health state. This is true even in the case of the expo-linear model where utility independence fails. Finally, when a maximal health state is a member of the health state set and CRRA ( Definition 4.1 ) and CARA ( Definition 4.4 ) are observed to hold for the maximal health state, then they must hold for all health states. Thus, for practical elicitation, invoking CP-TTO minimizes the scope of the health states for which these risk posture conditions need to be tested.

The results above imply additional strategies for eliciting utilities, when one invokes assumptions. If one assumes CP-TTO holds for all health states pairs for which there is a utility elicitation, then a single time tradeoff for each health state with respect to full health is sufficient to elicit the riskless value of the health states. If one assumes the linear QALY model holds, the elicitation is complete since risky utility is a linear transformation of risky utility in this case. If one does not assume the linear QALY model necessarily holds, but assumes either CRRA ( Definition 4.1 ) or CARA ( Definition 4.4 ) hold, then a single risk parameter need only be estimated. This can be accomplished a number of ways, the most common being certainty equivalents or probability equivalents. However, of these methods certainty equivalents may be less subject to bias and framing effects ( Hershey & Shoemaker, 1985 ). Further, the practice of eliciting certainty equivalents to establish utility curvature is common with medical patients ( McNeil, Weichselbaum, & Pauker, 1978, 1981 ;  Miyamoto, 1988 ;  Maas & Stalpers, 1992 ).
