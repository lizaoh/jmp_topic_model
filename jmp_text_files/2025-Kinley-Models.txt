Humans and other animals exhibit a near-universal tendency to subjectively devalue future rewards as a function of their delay, a phenomenon known as “delay discounting”. The steepness with which future rewards are discounted has been implicated in a range of psychiatric disorders ( Amlung et al., 2019 ) and associated with addictive ( Amlung, Vedelago, Acker, Balodis, & MacKillop, 2017; MacKillop et al., 2011 ) and otherwise unhealthy ( Story, Vlaev, Seymour, Darzi, & Dolan, 2014 ) behaviours.

Given a discount function, it still remains to be specified how an individual will arrive at a given choice. In practice, individuals will sometimes make decisions that do not adhere to D1 and D2. This is generally assumed to indicate inattention rather than genuine preference, and opportunities to deviate from these desiderata are sometimes built into experiments as attention checks ( Athamneh et al., 2021; Craft, Tegge, & Bickel, 2022; Lin, Saunders, Hutcherson, & Inzlicht, 2018; Pope et al., 2019; Stein et al., 2018 ). However, it is important to critically evaluate this assumption before building D1 and D2 into a model of discounting. Indeed, one can conceive of rationally intelligible reasons not to adhere to D1 and D2: for example, perhaps I know I would likely spend $100 that I received in a year on something harmful that will only become available then, or perhaps the disutility of waiting for 1 year more than outweights the utility of $100, and so I instead opt for nothing. Conversely, perhaps I want something that costs $100 and only becomes available in a year and I know I would likely spend $100 too early if I received it now, or perhaps I know I will enjoy anticipating receiving $100, and so I opt to postpone the reward ( i.e ., I choose $100 in a year over $100 now). Thus, another goal of this study is to examine the validity D1 and D2. If they are indeed valid desiderata for a descriptive model of discounting, participants who do not adhere to D1 and D2 should tend to show signs of inattention.

First, if deviations from D1 and D2 reflect genuine preferences, these preferences would appear to be in opposite directions: choosing $0 now over some nonzero amount later (deviating from D1) would suggest extreme delay aversion, whereas forgoing some amount now in order to receive it later (deviating from D2) would suggest extreme delay tolerance, even a preference for delays. Thus, we would expect participants who deviate from D1 not to deviate from D2 and vice versa. On the other hand, if deviations from D1 and D2 reflect inattention, participants who deviate from D1 would be more likely to deviate from D2 and vice versa.

Second, if deviations from D1 and D2 reflect inattention, participants who tend to make such deviations should exhibit noisier decision making overall. We measured decision stochasticity based on whether participants exhibited consistent indifference points: suppose participants chose between $100 in a month and immediate rewards in increments of $10 ( e.g ., $40, $50, $60, etc.). If a participant never chose immediate rewards of $40 or less and always chose immediate rewards of $50 or more, this would reflect consistent decision making; the participant switched only once from preferring the delayed to the immediate reward. On the other hand, if the same participant rejected an immediate reward of $60, this would reflect inconsistent decision making; the participant switched from preferring the delayed reward back to preferring the immediate reward. For each participant, the numbers of such “switches” at every delay were summed and modelled using negative binomial regression (chosen over Poisson regression due to overdispersion concerns  Venables & Ripley, 2002 ).

Third, if deviations from D1 and D2 reflect inattention, this should be reflected in reaction time patterns both at the participant level and perhaps also at the trial level. At the participant level, participants who make these deviations should exhibit overall shorter reaction times. At the trial level, participants who are equally inattentive across all trials would not necessarily show shorter reaction times for decisions deviating from D1 or D2, whereas participants who deviate from D1 or D2 due to a temporary lapse in attention would show selectively shorter reaction times on these trials.

Fourth, if deviation from D1 and D2 reflect inattention, participants who tend to make such deviations should show non-systematic discounting according to the Johnson & Bickel criteria ( Johnson & Bickel, 2008 ). These criteria are, first, that no indifference point should be higher than the previous one by more than 0.2 (monotonicity criterion) and, second, that the final indifference point should be lower than the first by at least 0.1 (flatness criterion). This was recently examined by  Almog, Vásquez Ferreiro, Berry, and Rung (2023) , who found that participants deviating from D1 tended to show more non-systematic discounting. Our overall pattern of results support the view that deviations from D1 and D2 tend to reflect inattention rather than true preferences: participants who deviated from D1 were more likely to also deviate from D2, even though these seem to imply opposite preferences. Moreover, participants deviating from D1 or D2 often made decisions more quickly, more noisily, and less systematically.  Almog et al. (2023)  similarly found that deviations from D1 are associated with non-systematic discounting, per the Johnson & Bickel criteria ( Johnson & Bickel, 2008 ). Interestingly, however, they argue that the association is not strong enough for these deviations to serve as a valid attention check. They quote one of their participants:  
                            I accidentally clicked the choice ‘$0 in …’ in one of the questions, but I can’t go back. I hope my HIT [crowdwork assignment] doesn’t get rejected by that 
                          ( Almog et al., 2023 ) note that this participant had a lapse of attention on the trial in question but was attentive enough in general to realize their mistake, meaning it is unwarranted to reject the rest of their trials. The apparent frequency of such cases means that trials offering the opportunity to deviate from D1 are ineffective as attention checks. Nonetheless, this does not imply that it is unwarranted to ignore such trials themselves. Indeed, it is telling that this participant found it so hard to imagine preferring $0 over a nonzero option that they correctly assumed the trial was an attention check.

Ultimately, the most decisive test would be to simply prompt participants to explain any choices that deviate from D1 and D2. Generally inattentive participants would likely not respond to these prompts, whereas participants who made an isolated mistake or who genuinely preferred the option they chose could explicitly say so. Although this test has not been carried out to our knowledge, the evidence we have reviewed suggests that cases of genuine preference would be rare.

On balance, across a range of datasets and performance criteria, fixed-endpoint models clearly outperformed free-endpoint models. This was most evident in the case of the linear models, where the difference between those with and without fixed endpoints was always highly significant ( Table 7 ). In the case of the nonlinear models, for all but the proportion correct criterion, fixed-endpoint models always numerically outperformed free-endpoint models ( Table 6 ), although in the smallest of the 3 datasets these differences were not always significant. For the proportion correct criterion, the fixed-endpoint models did not significantly outperform the free-endpoint models, and in fact in dataset 1 they performed significantly worse than the free-endpoint models. The proportion correct criterion may have produced inconsistent results because it is a relatively coarse measure and yielded ties between fixed- and free-endpoint models in a majority of cases (1086/1802 participants across all datasets), perhaps leaving too few to discern a meaningful pattern.

In the analyses presented here, we have taken care not to assume that a single discount function or choice rule is appropriate for all individuals, as there is often considerable variability between decision makers ( Franck et al., 2015 ). However, it may not even be warranted to assume that a given model is appropriate across all trials for a single individual ( e.g ., individuals’ decision making processes may change through learning over the course of an experiment;  Szollosi & Newell, 2020 ). Modelling trial-to-trial variability is beyond the scope of the current study, and thus the imposition of a single, static model for each individual decision maker represents a limitation of the current results.

Moreover, the fixed-endpoint models discussed here may be limited in their direct applicability to real-world decision making: the “endpoints” in question arise only in a rather contrived experimental paradigm designed to yield data suitable for fitting psychometric curves. Ultimately, the value of fixed-endpoint models likely lies in their application to estimating parametric models of discounting (which are in turn indirectly associated with real-world outcomes;  Amlung et al., 2019 ; but c.f.  Bailey, Romeu, & Finn, 2021 ; rather than in their direct applicability to decisions outside the laboratory.

There are several ways that future work might extend on the results presented here. First, a Bayesian implementation of these models (similar to  Vincent (2016) ) would provide measures of uncertainty in the estimated parameters and allow for rich inferences to be drawn using limited data.

Finally, it will be interesting to determine whether the current results could be extended to models of probabilistic and social discounting ( Jones & Rachlin, 2009 ). “Probabilistic discounting” refers to the phenomenon in which probabilistic rewards are subjectively devalued compared to guaranteed rewards ( e.g ., a 50% chance of $200 is subjectively less valued than a 100% chance of $100). In this case, desiderata similar to the ones we introduced earlier appear to hold: one should always prefer something over nothing, analogous to D1 ( e.g ., a 50% change of $100 over a 100% chance of $0), and for equal reward values, one should always prefer the likely over the unlikely reward, analogous to D2 ( e.g ., a 100% chance of $100 over a 50% chance of $100). “Social discounting” refers to the phenomenon in which the subjective value of rewards declines as a function of social distance ( e.g ., one might be indifferent between $100 for oneself, $90 for a socially close other, and $50 for a more socially distant other). In this case, it is less obvious that desiderata analogous to D1 and D2 should hold; one might not always prefer something for someone over nothing for anyone ( e.g ., one might choose $0 for oneself to prevent any reward going to an adversary). Similarly, for equal reward amounts, it is not clear that one should always prefer to reward the self, particularly if the other person in question is in need. Thus, we would expect the present results to extend to probabilistic but not social discounting. It remains to be seen whether this expectation is borne out by future research.
