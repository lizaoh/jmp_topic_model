Foundations of Measurements, Vol I  ( Krantz, Luce, Suppes, & Tversky, 1971/2007 ) opens with this quote from Plato:  
                         
                            What measure is there of the relations of pleasure to pain other than excess and defect, which means that they become greater and smaller, and more and fewer, and differ in degree?

Therein is captured the core challenge of measurement in psychology: How to measure the not directly observable sensation that intervenes between physical stimuli and behavioral responses?

A variety of measurement techniques to measure psychological attributes have evolved. However, unlike a measurement of length in meters, the current understanding of the scale properties of the measures these tools produce as well as those of the attributes they measure is often limited. For instance, in the ubiquitous ratings paradigm, a person may be asked to provide a rating of mood using numbers

The well-known works of Helmholtz, Fechner, and Weber are examples of early efforts towards placing measurements in psychology on a sound scientific basis, but by the first third of the 20th century the problem of measurement remained unsolved. In 1932, a committee of the British Association for the Advancement of Science was established and asked to “consider and report upon the possibility of ‘quantitative estimates of sensory events’–meaning simply: Is it possible to measure human sensation?” ( Stevens, 1946 ). The committee debated for 7 years without reaching a common conclusion. Stevens characterized the essence of the disagreement as one about the inherent meaning of measurement. Acknowledging the potential semantic ambiguity in the question, Stevens observed that measurements have scales and these scales fall into certain definite classes. Each class is defined by formal (mathematical) properties, which, i.a., define permissible (statistical) operations on them. Stevens’ summary conclusion was “[p]araphrasing N. R. Campbell (Final Report, p. 340), we may say that measurement, in the broadest sense, is defined as the assignment of numerals to objects or events according to rules”. ( Stevens, 1946 ).

Stevens and colleagues proceeded to put these measurement principles into practice. This led them to the development of the magnitude estimation/production methods. They collected data for numerous sensory domains demonstrating that human subjects can produce judgments of intensity in a consistent fashion (Stevens referred to these intensive continua as prothetic). They aggregated responses over respondents and found across domains that these data were well-fit by power functions. These observations culminated in the proposal that physical intensities were related to psychological sensations by a power function, which they suggested be given the status of a law—the power law. This multi-decade effort is summarized in  Stevens’ (1975)  monograph.

Despite the considerable success of Stevens and colleagues, the results did not constitute a theoretical basis for measurement in psychology. The authors of  Foundations of Measurement, Vol I–III , wrote that by the 1960s, they saw what constituted a mathematical foundation for measurement in science consisted of results “[s]cattered about the literatures of various fields lacking coherent structure”. ( Krantz et al., 1971/2007 , introduction).

It had long been understood that the methods of measurements reduce to three primitives (see  Fig. 1

Measurement in physics evolved over time and by the 1960s, many results seemed so accepted as to constitute a coherent philosophy, but these results did not constitute a foundational measurement theory. Furthermore, these techniques proved inadequate for answering most questions about measurements of sensations: e.g., “How to construct a ‘tape measure’ for loudness?”; “What are the underlying scale properties for brightness?”; “Are loudness and brightness actual measurable dimensions?”. With the aim of remedying this situation, Luce and colleagues (Krantz, Suppes, & Tversky) embarked upon building a mathematical foundation for measurement. Their efforts culminated in the three volume series  Foundations of Measurements I–III  ( Krantz et al., 1971/2007; Luce, Krantz, Suppes, & Tversky, 1990/2007; Suppes, Krantz, Luce, & Tversky, 1989/2007 ) (FOM I–III).

The treatment of fundamental measurement in FOM I–III provides an approach to building axiomatic theories of measurement. Their approach, the representational theory of measurement, treats measurement as a mapping between two relational structures, an empirical one and a numerical one. For a given empirical system to be measurable, it needs to fulfill the properties of a formal system. In practical terms, the approach consists of formulating qualitative (testable) axioms involving ordering (see  Fig. 1 ) that together imply a numerical, vectorial, or geometric numerical representation. Then it is shown how manipulating the ordered attribute in two different ways (e.g., operations) lead to two representations for each of the two separately. To establish how the two representations are related, they are linked in two ways by qualitative linking axioms, often by a “distribution law”.

Example: In algebra, the two operations of addition and multiplication are defined, each of which manipulates associated attributes, and the two are linked by the well-known distributive property

Similarly, at the heart of the theory development outlined in this article are two operational primitives (Sections   2.2.1 and 2.2.2 ) of matching and productions, a representation is established for each one separately (Section   2.2.3 ), and the two are linked by behavioral properties containing both operations (Section   2.2.4 ) which are quite similar to arithmetic distributive properties.

The axiomatic approach is typical of classic inductive theory formulation in physics. Inductive theories fair well when it comes to making novel predictions and for the cumulative expansion of their scope. The authors of FOM I–III saw their work as building a foundation for applying the successful theoretical modeling approach of physics to measurements in psychology.

Although, FOM I–III is celebrated as a major achievement, the representational theory of measurement has not come to dominate in psychology.  Luce and Narens (1993)  commented on this “non-revolution” of the representational theory of measurement. They reiterated the powerful argument for the axiomatic approach and suggest some reasons and challenges for the apparent lack of its proliferation. One possible obstacle is that quite rigorously formal algebraic approach on which FOM I–III rests. As a consequence, the text has not been found easily accessible to non-mathematically trained readers.

The main topic of this paper is to describe Luce’s development of a comprehensive application of representational measurement theory to the percepts of intensity. The ideological roots are in what Fechner termed  outer psychophysics  ( Fechner, 1860/1966 ). Outer psychophysics encompass the view that psychophysics should mimic the approach of physics to measurement. That is, from a defined physical stimulus, a mathematical formalism maps–or connects–responses in a way that forces a numerical measurement scale. Arguably, the tools necessary for this have only recently matured to a point where the topic explored could be based on a sound theoretical basis.

Luce initially formulated his theory to solve problems in utility theory ( Luce, 2000 ). While he continued to evolve the utility theory ( Luce, 2010b , and references there), he realized that it was feasible to reformulate the theory and employ an axiomatic approach for modeling cognitive/global perceptual phenomena. The application to perception gave rise to a sustained period of incremental theoretical development, often in tandem with empirical work involving several coauthors as well as garnering the interest of other researchers. What has arisen is a framework for a unified theory of all intensive domains with real-world applications.

The article is organized as follows:

Section   2  summarizes the present form of  Luce’s (2004, 2012b)  theory of global psychophysical judgments of intensities. The account begins with an axiomatic theory of magnitude production, showing the necessary and sufficient conditions for the method to deliver ratio scaled measures of intensity. Next is the model of intensity judgments, which includes the representations and the axioms that imply them along with the plan for empirically evaluating the theory. The section concludes with an account of several predictions derived from the theory.

Section   3  reports the empirical work that had been reported for various portion of the theory. This work is most extensive for the theory of ratio productions and the 2-D theory, as well as some of the derived predictions. Missing and desirable additional work is outlined.

Section   4  summarize the main elements of the article along with a brief discussion of its relation to the introductory elements and possible future directions.

Luce’s (2002, 2004, 2008)  psychophysical theory of intensive continua followed the script of the axiomatic measurement approach envisioned in FOM-I. He constructed a behavioral model composed of several descriptive (=testable) axiomatic invariances from which he derived numerical representations—a measurement scale and its properties. In subsequent papers, he both incrementally expanded the scope of the theory and derived testable predictions from it, often some that answered outstanding questions. Many parts of this theoretical edifice have been favorably supported empirically, some await additional work.

It is notable that the numerical representation is a solution to functional equations for which the axioms form a necessary and sufficient basis. Therefore, to the extent the axioms are found to hold, the representation (the model) follow as a matter of mathematical logic. The representations are purely descriptive entities and, in particular, even though they describe and predict behavior, they no inherent psychology meaning nor any necessary relation to biological workings of the perceptual system. In this respect, these theories are similar models in classical physics, which capture relationship between variables without capturing any of the underlying aspects of the universe that give rise to the data these theories describe. For this reason, no attempt is made to relate the axioms or the several parameters of the models to underlying psychological mechanisms.

Luce (2012a)  realized that his theory differed materially according to whether it concerned pairs of sensors that nature had designed to work interactively–e.g., the two ears–or senses not so paired—e.g., shock, saltiness, and preference over money. The paired organs he called binary or two-dimensional (2-D) attributes; the others he called unary or one-dimensional (1-D) sensations.

Together, the 1-D and 2-D theory is complete in the sense that human evolution has (apparently) endowed us with only unary and binary senses. There can be some debate about whether, say, odor and taste are 1-D or a 2-D sense: there are two nostrils and two sides of the tongue project ipsilaterally to the cortex Conversely, even though duration of a tone is judged by sounds that enter the two ears, it is not obvious that such judgments depends on their combined sense: while loudness is sharply attenuated if one ear is blocked, the percept of tone duration is not similarly influenced.

Humans do not have a third arm or additional eyes–whether these might be desirable is decidedly beyond the present scope–but we may ponder whether, say, the simultaneous touch of the skin in three or more places does not contradict the assertion that the 1-D and 2-D theory is complete? The skin is generally considered a single organ but perhaps more importantly, touch in multiple areas beyond what Helmholtz called sensory circles is not obviously experienced as a unitary percept. If accepted, these spatial arrangements do not present sensations beyond 2-D, any more than spatial arrangement of circles is seen as a single circle with some combined size of the constituent separate circles. Thus, in practice it does not seem we need to consider cases beyond 2-D.

That matter of the equality of reference points involves repeated applications of  (21) . It is suspected that by a trivial reinterpretation of dimensions, here exemplified as physical frequency, the second attribute can be interpreted as perceptual domains or individual respondents. This means that  (21)  may be applied to answer questions about possible commonality of scales between domains, e.g., between loudness and brightness, as well as between two individuals within or across domains ( Luce et al., 2010; Steingrimsson, Luce, & Narens, 2012 ). The fact of the power form being generally a good fit to 2-D data raises the intriguing possibility of a common ratio scale for some or all 2-D intensive dimensions. Conversely, because the empirical literature consistently reveals individual variability, it is not expected that individuals share a common scale of intensity judgments.

The behavioral properties/axioms are not domain specific. Thus empirical work begins with interpreting the primitives in a specified domain. For instance

If the properties of the theory are found empirically supported, the representations follows as a matter of mathematical logic and thereby constitute a descriptive model of the perception in the given domain. No data fitting is involved.

The statistical evaluation is of a series of invariances, which means exploring support for a null hypothesis. This is typical of inductive theories. While this is routine in the physical sciences, it has not been typical of mainstream psychological research. Behind this matter lies a considerable debate touching on deep issues regarding the nature of modeling in psychology. This debate is beyond the scope of the present article. The many empirical papers that are cited provide often detailed discussion of the non-trivial methodological and statistical challenges involved in the empirical evaluation of this type of model.

Torgerson’s (1961)  conjectured that respondents are behaviorally unable to distinguish between subjective differences and subjective ratios. Empirical evaluations of the conjecture have produced mixed results, some favoring it and others rejecting it ( Birnbaum, 1980; Ellermeier, Narens, & Dielmann, 2003; Rule, Curtis, & Mullin, 1981; Schneider & Bissett, 1988; Veit, 1978 ).

Luce (2012b)  derived predictions for equisections  (28)  and fractionations  (28)  from his production representation  (7)  and showed the conjecture implied that the function  
                               W 
                             was the identity function. This is rejected by the failure of the multiplicative property  (18)  (Section   3 ).

If the conjecture implies an empirically false consequence, why is the empirical record mixed?  Steingrimsson (in preparation-d)  shows that in several cases, stimuli that were argued to allow respondents to distinguish between judgments of differences and ratios only did so when examined in their physical units. However, when examined in psychological space, using plausible instantiations of the summation representation  (6) , they appeared ill chosen.

Further, data were collected on the basis of the representations for equisections  (28)  and fractionations  (28) . These data showed that respondents could produced responses that differed for ratio and differences judgments, thus violating the conjecture. However, they did so based on individual and task dependent strategies that were seemingly unaffected by the ratio/difference instructions.

These data thus suggest a refinement of the conjecture to include the ability to perform both ratio and difference judgments but that respondents cannot surface these judgments simultaneously. Instead, they shift between the two scenarios in a yet unspecified manner. A happy consequence of this seemingly novel characterization of the conjecture, is that the data appear to simultaneously give a plausible account for the theoretically derived evidence against the  Torgerson’s (1961)  conjecture based on the  
                               W 
                             function ( Luce, 2012b ) as well as of the inconsistency in the empirical record ( Steingrimsson, in preparation-d ).

When respondents match one signal to another signal they do, in fact, not usually produce the same physical signal. We call these non-equal matches (NEM). Special cases include the time-order error (TOE) in audition and the space-order error in vision.  Steingrimsson and Luce (2012)  derived predictions from the production representation  (7)  for respondents’ behavior in experimental tasks involving intensive stimuli presented either sequentially or displaced in space. They showed that several distinctly different behaviors were consistent with the model, and that these could account for phenomena such as the time-order error. They found empirical support for different behaviors which have important implications about the methodology of psychological experiments in general. In particular, it adds to the caution about averaging data over respondents because even in these types of tasks, important individual differences can lead to incorrect conclusions made on the basis of aggregated data.

The 2-D model has had most of its elements evaluated in several domains, whereas the 1-D model is largely unexplored empirically. The following summarizes the studies carried out to date. The experiments evaluating behavioral axioms have in common that the data are analyzed by individuals rather than aggregated over respondents (any exception is justified by the properties of the data).

Aggregating individual data and then making assertions about individual behaviors on this basis is a common practice. However, it is a long-standing observation that this is not scientifically justified unless individuals relate by an underlying relationship that is at most linear ( Luce, 1995 ). Examples abound: this is, e.g., not the case for the functional form of power, types of individual behavior under risk ( Luce, 2010a ), or individual behavior in some 2-interval forced choice tasks ( Steingrimsson & Luce, 2012 ).

This observation is consistent with the theoretical observations that individuals may differ on the level of model parameters, which allows for a great deal of individual variations, while describing behavior within a unified theoretical framework. This is typical of the axiomatic approach. The experimental methods and the statistical methods employed often present non-trivial challenges. These details are left out in this summary.

Ratio scalability for both 1-D and 2-D domains is established if any of commutativity  (1) ,  Luce’s (2004)  production commutativity  (12)  or cross-dimensional commutativity  (21)  is found to hold. It has been favorably evaluated in the 2-D domains of loudness ( Ellermeier & Faulhammer, 2000; Luce et al., 2010; Steingrimsson & Luce, 2005a; Zimmer, 2005 ), brightness ( Steingrimsson, 2009; Steingrimsson et al., 2012 ), perceived contrast ( Steingrimsson, submitted for publication ); and in the 1-D domains of size of circles ( Augustin & Maier, 2008 ), pitch intervals ( Kattner & Ellermeier, 2014 ), and short-time duration ( Birkenbusch, Ellermeier, & Kattner, 2015 ). In summary, magnitude production has been found to produce ratio scale measures for brightness, loudness, perceived contrast, size, pitch interval, and short-time duration. It follows that these domains have an underlying ratio scale representation.

This article summarizes: (1) Luce’s theory of global psychophysical percepts for all intensive domains. (2) His axiomatic approach to evaluate the possible forms of the free functions appearing in the theory. (3) Several model derived predictions. (4) The empirical evidence to date.

Luce’s (2004, 2008)  theory concerned subjective intensity judgments of inherently binary (2-D) stimuli, e.g., loudness and brightness.  Luce (2012a) , concerned a general theory and representation for the inherently unary (1-D) signals, e.g., utility of money, weight, odor intensity. These theories share the  
                         p 
                      -additive and magnitude production representations. However, the property of bisymmetry plays a different role in the two theories, leading to different predictions for the psychophysical function in the two cases. Included is a general theory of ratio scalability both for stimuli varying in one or two attributes.

The empirical evidence supports the 2-D theory in loudness, brightness, and perceived contrast. It also supports a power function form for the psychophysical function and a power or Prelec form for the weighting function. The 1-D theory has only been partially evaluated. The evidence favors the production representation and a power form for the weighting function.

In a lecture at University of Michigan ( 2006 ),  Luce  numerated 20th century movements in psychology. Some have come and gone (e.g., radical behaviorism) others grown and become impactful (e.g., psychometrics), but none has achieved the cumulative success of physics. Luce expressed the view that identifying lawful relations (invariances) as in physics, had to be an inevitable part of the answer to the problem of theory in psychology. This view consistently informed his body of scientific work in psychology.

His psychophysical theory is a large scale application of representational measurement theory to capture perception of intensive continua. It is complete in the sense that, in principle, it extends to all such continua. The evolution of the theory demonstrates the strength of the axiomatic approach in a variety of ways. For instance, it shows that the theory can cumulatively be expanded, that novel predictions follow as a matter of mathematical logic, and the theory is empirically supported by evaluating only behavioral invariances.

The empirical support for the 2-D theory shows that domains typically studied and modeled independently are unified on the level of description. The predictions about temporal distortions, the “regression effect”, and the Torgerson’s conjecture show long-standing questions can be given a satisfying theoretical account.

Much empirical work remains: this includes, but is not exclusive to, evaluating the 2-D theory in additional domains, the 1-D theory in all domains, and several of the model predictions; and numerous cross-modal predictions remain to be tested as does the possibility of a common ratio scale across some or all of the 2-D domains.

The axiomatization of magnitude production proved that the commutative property is equivalent to ratio scaled psychological measures. It would clearly be desirable to extend this axiomatization to other methods of measurement. An example is the ratings paradigm. A principled theory of reference signals does not exist.

Although not discussed in any detail in this article, numerous empirical challenges remain, including the development of generally accepted statistical methods for empirical evaluation of invariances.

In Section   1 , the core challenge of measurement in psychology was characterized that of measuring the not directly observable sensation that intervenes between physical stimuli and behavioral responses. The theory of magnitude production (Section   2.1 ) is an example of how the axiomatic approach can provide a solution to this difficult problem and, in general, the Theory of Global Psychophysical Judgments of Intensities (Section   2 ) is a demonstration of how such a tool of measurement can be leveraged to provided empirical evidence in support of the underlying properties (Section   3 ). The result constitutes the most comprehensive and large scale application of the representational theory of measurement to psychophysical problems to date. This demonstration stands in some contrast to the earlier discussion of the “non-revolution” of the representational theory of measurement ( Luce & Narens, 1993 ) in that it stands are a demonstration of the promise the authors of FOM I–III saw in their measurement foundational work—it can certainly not be claimed that the theoretical work cannot be brought to bare of long-standing practical problems in psychophysics.

Another important observation is that while the work of Stevens’ and colleagues demonstrated the plausibility of the power function as a good candidate for a psychophysical function, it is by way of invariance property  (16)  that the form becomes a testable prediction. A parallel to physics is Kepler’s function fitting to data that gave rise to his laws of planetary motions that later became derived predictions of Newtonian physics (there is no implied equivalence of import). This along with the may derived predictions from the theory (Section   2.4 ) is another demonstration of how the successful approach to model construction in physics is applicable to problems in psychology.

The model itself is also noteworthy for another feature of models in physics, namely, it brings a unification on the level of description of multiple sensory domains, such as loudness and brightness, that are typically studied separately and have not been thought to have a shared underlying structure.

The work described here fulfills a vision of scientific psychology built on the same foundation that have made the physical sciences successful. This is a story that begins with the building of a theoretical foundation necessary to actualize this vision for psychology. Over years, tools and techniques matured. The psychophysical theory starts as an adaptation of an axiomatic work in utility theory but evolves and expands in tandem with empirical work. At its present state, the theory provides a complete description of judgments of intensive continua. Much empirical work remains and the theory itself should be expanded. There is no doubt that Luce wished to see his work continued. My hope is that this article will aid the actualization of this desire.
