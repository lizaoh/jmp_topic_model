Busemeyer and Townsend’s Decision Field Theory (DFT) ( Busemeyer & Townsend, 1993 ) introduced stochastic processes to preferential choice situations. DFT applies to binary choice options and accounts simultaneously both for choice frequencies and choice response times. Its generalization to three choice alternatives (Multialternative Decision Field Theory, MDFT ( Roe et al., 2001 )), which was mainly designed to account simultaneously for three context effects (attraction  Huber et al., 1982 , similarity  Debreu, 1960; Tversky, 1972 , compromise  Simonson, 1989 ) led to various competitive approaches, with a focus on choice probabilities (e.g.,  Usher & McClelland, 2004; Wollschläger & Diederich, 2012 ) and ( Howes et al., 2016; Wollschläger & Diederich, 2020  for an overview).

In a recent paper ( Mallahi-Karai & Diederich, 2019 ), we proposed another dynamic stochastic model for preferential decision making with multiple alternatives, called  cube model , without any emphasis on any of these effects. The special feature of the cube model is that it assumes  accept  and  reject  boundaries and also incorporates the ideas of having subprocesses for each attribute characterizing the choice options ( Diederich, 1997 ). Using the theory of Wiener processes in higher dimensions, the cube model’s state space is presented by a cube and each facet of the cube is itself a cube in a lower dimensional space. This allows us to define an iterative process during which a) either an alternative is (immediately) chosen (i.e. hitting an accept boundary) or (b) alternatives are subsequently eliminated (i.e. hitting reject boundaries) until a choice is made or (c) all but one alternative is eliminated from the choice set. In the latter case, the remaining alternative is taken by default.

Despite its intuitive appeal, the model is difficult to analyze analytically. For instance, finding the joint distribution of the time and the location of hitting a reject boundary is already a difficult problem. Because the distribution of the hitting point cannot be expressed in closed form, one cannot hope for a usable analytic formula for the reaction time and the choice probabilities. Fortunately, as the underlying process is a simple transformation of a family of independent Wiener processes, one can simulate the process and aim for numerical results.

The aim of the current theoretical note is twofold. First, we propose and study a closely related model in two dimensions where the state space of the underlying process is atwo-dimensional disk. Our analysis makes essential use of the rotational symmetry of disk, which makes it possible to obtain analytic solutions in closed or semi-closed form for choice probabilities and mean reaction times. The rotational symmetry manifests itself through the simple expression of the Poisson kernel which governs the hitting distribution of the Wiener process. As already mentioned above, analogous expressions for cube model do not have closed forms.

Second, we will define and discuss a notion of equivalence for models defined by seemingly different state spaces. We will show that, up to a random change of time, the disk model is equivalent to the two-dimensional cube model. The discrepancy in the level of difficulty in analyzing these two models is closely related to the fact that the equivalence between the models is determined (essentially uniquely) by a transcendental map between the disk and the square. This is why analytically simple expressions in the disk model turn into prohibitively difficult ones for the two-dimensional cube model.

For demonstration, we consider a choice situation with three alternatives of which one is special: A so-called status quo alternative for which the decision maker is not required to act but maintains the current or previous decision ( Samuelson & Zeckhauser, 1988 ). Research on status quo options has been done in many areas of marketing, consumer research, but also for health, law, environmental, financial decision making and more (e.g.  Boxall et al., 2009; Hartman et al., 1991; Kahneman et al., 1991; Meyerhoff & Liebe, 2009 ).

This note is organized as follows: In Section  2  we review the basic definitions and set the notation for the remaining of the paper. Section  3  summaries the basic features of the cube and disk models. In Section  4  we describe the disk model in detail and obtain formulas for the choice probabilities and the mean reaction time in the absence of drift. In Section  5  we discuss equivalence between these models. In Section  6  we briefly compare the model to the approach by  Smith (2016) .

In this section we will discuss the relation between the disk model and the two-dimensional cube model and draw conclusions about the question of equivalence of models. As it will be seen, the two-dimensional cube model and the disk model can be viewed as equivalent models. One of our goals is to provide an explanation for this seemingly paradoxical situation.

As indicated in the introduction, our goal in this paper is twofold. Our first goal is to introduce the disk model and investigate some of its basic properties. As indicated above, a desirable property of this model is that its state space, the unit disk, has a rotational symmetry. This rotational symmetry allows for a simple analytic expression for the Poisson kernel which, in turn, can be utilized in deriving explicit formulas. Analogous formulas do not exist in closed form for the unit square. We also explained that using a different set of ideas, Smith has studied a problem with similar mathematical challenges. Both approaches effectively use the rotational symmetry of the disk. While our methods are general enough to deal with Wiener processes without drift starting at arbitrary points, Smith’s approach works best with processes with arbitrary drift, but starting at the origin. In this regard, the two toolkits can be considered to be complementary. It is conceivable that by combining ideas from Smith’s work, Girsanov’s theorem, and techniques introduced in this work, one can also analyze the disk model in the most general case. The disk model can also be extended to a model with more than 2 main alternatives. In fact, by a partition of the unit circle into  
                         
                            2 
                            k 
                         
                       arcs viewed as accept and reject boundaries for  
                         k 
                       alternatives, one can design and study more general choice situations. Note, however, that since each reject boundary borders on two accept boundaries, after rejecting each alternative only two of the remaining  
                         
                            k 
                            − 
                            1 
                         
                       alternatives become available. We hope to pursue this in a future work.

As it was shown, the Riemann mapping theorem guarantees that as far as the choice probabilities are concerned, the disk and the two-dimensional cube model are weakly equivalent, and thus provide similar predictions for choice probabilities. Nevertheless, interest for closed analytic expressions makes the disk model more desirable to work with. The question of studying strong and weak equivalence of models raised in the previous section is of intrinsic interest and we hope to come back to it in future.

Finally, one reviewer suggested that we connect our model to optimal decision theory, most accurate for a given speed or fastest for a given accuracy ( Bogacz et al., 2006 ). This is certainly interesting but difficult to realize for preferential choice for which we do not have correct and incorrect responses.
