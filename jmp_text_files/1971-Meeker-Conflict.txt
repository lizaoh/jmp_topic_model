JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 8, 389-403 (1971)  Value Conflict in Social Exchange: A Markov Model*  B. FOLEY MEEKER  Division of Behavioral Sciences, National Research Council, National Academy of Sciences, Washington, D.C. 20418  A Markov model is developed to describe the process of decision making by a subject who must make a series of choices between  reciprocating the helpful acts of a partner and maximizing his own payoff, in a situation in which the other cannot retaliate.  The theory from which the model is derived assumes that the probability of the subject's choice is a function of which of two  unobservable states ("values") he occupies, and that his choice in turn influences the probability that he changes his value  state. An experiment with three conditions of conflict between reciprocity and "rationality" provides data from which the  parameters are estimated, using a procedure that involves assuming that the unobservable states are observable, estimating the  error in that assumption, and reiterating the original estimate.  This paper presents a stochastic model dealing with a social-psychological process that could be called "value conflict." The  process occurs when a person must make decisions in a situation in which he holds simultaneously two or more values which,  when applied to the alternative courses of action available to him, make incompatible prescriptions for his behavior.The  specific kind of value conflict with which this model deals arises in social exchange situations; that is, situations  involving an exchange of rewards and/or costs between two or more people (Homans, 1961; Blau, 1964; Thibaut and Kelley, 1959).  Some such situations require that people choose between getting something objective that they value such as money and  conforming to some socially defined norm or value such as reciprocity (Gouldner, 1960), equity (Adams, 1965), or social  responsibility (Berkowitz and Daniels, 1963).  Some Examples of Exchange Value Conflicts  This kind of dilemma apparently confronts subjects in some social-psychological experiments which are examples of social  exchange situations. One example of conflict between self-interest and other values occurs in two-person game experiments such  as the Prisoner's Dilemma. These games have been extensively studied by social  * This research was supported by NSF grant No. 1928, and was carried out at the University of California, Irvine and at the  University of Washington.  389  psychologists (for some early examples, see Deutsch, 1958; Loomis, 1959; Minas, Scodel, Marlow and Rawson, 1960; Solomon,  1960) and typically require each member of a pair of subjects to make a series of decisions, each of which has monetary  consequences for himself and the other subject. In addition to trying to maximize his own gain in such a game, a subject may  try to maximize the difference between his own and his opponent's gain (that is, compete), try to match the behavior of the  other player, try to control the behavior of the other player, or try to maximize the joint gain of the two players (Messick  and McClintock, 1968; Rapaport, 1968; Terhune, 1968). A subject who wants both to help the other person and to get as much for  himself as he can finds himself unable to do both, or he may find that these values, while compatible with each other,  conflict with some other value he has. These games thus pose an intrapersonal as well as interpersonal conflict.  Another set of questions raised by two-person games concerns the effect of behavior on behavior. Two common results of game  experiments are (1) that the choices made early in the game or even before the game begins influence later choices sometimes  outweighing other factors such as personality (Terhune, 1968; Tedeschi, Lesnick and Gahagan 1968; Swingle, 1968; McClintock  and McNeel, 1967), and (2) that the two subjects influence each other; usually, both eventually do the same thing (the  "lockingin" effect discussed by Rapaport and Chammah, 1965). It appears that subjects may become committed to a course of  action such as competition through the processes of decision-making and interaction operating independently of values or  motives.  THE MODEL  Consider the following hypothetical situation: There is an actor P who is engaged in intersection with another actor 0. P can  perform one of two acts. One of these acts helps 0 and one does not. The act that helps 0 always results in a lower payoff to  P than the act that does not help 0. In addition, 0 has helped P in the past, and 0's future acts are not contingent on P's  present behavior. An experiment that meets these conditions is described later.  The condition that 0's future acts are not contingent on P's present behavior is imposed in order to locate the conflict  entirely within P; he need not reciprocate 0's help in order to motivate 0 to continue to help him. In fact, the only  "rational" thing for P to do is to choose the act that will not help 0 because that results in higher reward for P at no cost.  On the other hand, if P believes that reciprocity is a universal requirement of social behavior, he is in a state of conflict  between getting as much as he can of something he wants and conforming to the norm of reciprocity which he also values. In  addition, there are other norms he could consider; competition or maximizing the difference between his own and 0's outcomes  (which would require that he not help 0), altruism or social responsibility (which would require that he help 0), and  VALUE CONFLICT IN SOCIAL EXCHANGE: A MARKOV MODEL  391  maximizing the group outcome (which might require either, depending on the relative gains to the group from helping himself  and helping 0). There is a set of values consistent with each act, and while some may be consistent with both sets, others are  in conflict.  The set of values that is consistent with one act will be called a value state. In the hypothetical example just described,  there are two value states, one consisting of reciprocity, altruism, and possibly group orientation, and a second consisting  of selfinterest and competition. For convenience, the first will be referred to as the Reciprocating State, the second as the  Rational State.  The exact psychological nature of value states is not specified; they may be thought of as values, norms, motives,  expectations, or utilities. The concept probably comes closest to the sociological concept of norm because a value state is an  idea about what the right behavior is in a given situation, but does not necessarily describe actual behavior.  Value states are assumed to have the following properties:  Axiom 1. There are two states, Reciprocating (referred to as C) and Rational (referred to as T). If P is in State C on Trial n, he will give response H on that trial with probability p: (p > 1 - p):  P[Hn I C.] = p.  Axiom 2b. If P is in State T on Trial n, he will give response NH on that trial with probability r: (r > 1 - r):  P[NH. I T.] = r. (4)  Axioms 3a and 3b make use of some general ideas from the set of social-psychological theories known as balance (Heider, 1958),  or dissonance (Festinger, 1957). In these theories, it is assumed that an individual faced with some kind of cognitive  conflict or inconsistency experiences discomfort, and is under pressure, therefore, to change some element of his cognitive  structure. In the present situation, the inconsistency is between P's value state (which says to do one thing) and his  behavior (which he observes to be the opposite thing).  392 MEEKER  Since P experiences discomfort when his behavior is inconsistent with his value state, he may change his value state to be  consistent with his own past behavior. This means that if P is forced  to make a series of decisions between helping and not helping, he will show a tendency to repeat the same choice over and  over, but if he changes once from, say, helping to not helping, he may initiate a series of nonhelping choices. EXPERIMENTAL METHOD  An experiment was designed to explore the theoretical problems for which these statements are a mathematical model. It uses a  type of two-person game situation.  Procedures  Two subjects were seated one on each side of a screen each facing a small panel with four switches and two lights. At the  beginning of each trial, they had 10 sec in which to indicate both a "guess" (as to what the other person's choice was going  to be) and a "choice" by turning on two of the four switches. After both subjects had indicated a choice, a feedback light  went on for one subject showing him what the other subject's choice had been. After 3 sec, the subjects were instructed to  turn off all their switches and the next trial began. Data are reported from the choices.  The experiment was not introduced to the subjects as a "game," to avoid inducing a competitive orientation. The subjects were  told that the study concerned decision-making in situations in which people have a limited amount of information about their  decisions. At the beginning of the experiment, each subject was given $1.20 and told that money would be added to or  subtracted from his score, with the amount depending partly on his own decisions and partly on the other person's decisions.  The rewards and costs in the situation were established by a scoring sheet which the subjects looked at during the experiment.  There were three different scoring sheets; two of these involved a prisoner's dilemma type of payoff matrix2:  Subject 2  H NH  Subject 1 H + 20, +24 -3 +3 NH + 30, -30 -20, -24  2 The scoring sheets the subjects saw were not in matrix form, but rather in the form of statements: "If I choose A and he  chooses A, I gain 20 and he gains 2" etc.  394 MEEKER  and in the third the subject's decision had no effect on his own score:  Subject 2  H NH  Subject 1 H + 2 +20, -I-2 NH -;- 2e, 0 0, 0  In one of the two prisoner's dilemma conditions, the H choice was labeled "For the Group" and the NH choice was labeled "For  Myself," and the emphasis on group score was repeated in the instructions in which the experimenter pointed out that the  group's score was higher if both subjects chose "For the Group." In the other prisoner's dilemma condition and the no-cost  condition, the choices were merely labeled "A" and "B," and no hints were given as to which choice was more appropriate. This  provided one condition in which there was neither a cost associated with the H choice, nor a group orientation; this was the  no-cost individual condition (NCI). In one of the prisoner's dilemma conditions, there was a cost associated with the H choice  and no group orientation; this was the prisoner's dilemma-individual condition (PDI). In the third condition, there was both a  cost associated with the H choice and a group orientation; this was the prisoner's dilemma-group condition (PDG). Each pair of  subjects made 50 choices in one of the three conditions.  Subjects  The subjects were junior college students, recruited through the student employment office, and were run in same sex pairs.  Males and females were randomly assigned to one of three conditions and data for both sexes were combined in the analysis.  Although one subject in each pair received feedback and one did not, only the data for the subjects who received feedback are  reported here. Responses for 44 subjects are reported; 9 in NCI, 20 in PDI and 15 in PDG.  Meeting the conditions of the model  The conditions imposed on the hypothetical situation for which the model was developed are met as follows: (1) "The act that  helps 0 always results in a lower payoff to P than the act that does not help 0"; this is true in the PDI and PDG conditions,  since the H choice has less value to P than the NH choice no matter whet 0's choice is. In the NCI conditions, H and NH have  equal value to P. (2) "O's future acts are not contingent on P's present behavior"; this is accomplished by having one-way  feedback of the decisions; P knows what 0's decision has been on each trial, but 0 is never informed about P's decisions.  Since P knows that 0 is ignorant of his choices, he cannot make a helpful choice with the intention of motivating 0 to help  him. (3) "0 has chosen to help P"; the feedback P receives about 0's choice is prearranged, and P is always informed that 0  has chosen H. DISCUSSION  A value state is a hypothetical construct, and initially no assumptions were made about its properties except that it  influences behavior and may change after inconsistent behavior. These assumptions produce a model that makes sense when  applied to data from an experiment designed to meet the conditions of the theory, and we are now in a position to add more  content to the hypothetical construct.  In the first place, consider the parameters p and r (probabilities of behavior consistent with value state) in the three  conditions (see Table 4). These parameters indicate the influence the value state has on behavior. In all three conditions, p  remains about the same while r varies. This indicates that one property of State T (Rational State) is that the influence of  state on behavior will change with changes in the cost of the behavior and with experimental instructions, while a property of  State C (Reciprocating State) is that the influence of value state on behavior is not affected by these changes. In this  sense, State T is indeed more "rational" than State C. It is also reasonable to think of State C as primarily oriented to the  behavior of the other person, since the behavior of the other subject is the same in all three conditions. In this sense,  State C is more "reciprocating" than State T. It is also clear that the two states are different in the way they influence  behavior, not merely opposite in effect. This intepretation of State C leads to the testable hypothesis that variations in the  behavior of the other subject would have more effect on the parameter p than on r.  The parameters B and 0 (probabilities of change of state after inconsistent response) measure the effect of behavior on value  state. The strongest effect of behavior on State C occurs in PDG, the weakest in NCI. If a high value of B may be taken as an  indication of high conflict between behavior and value state, NH conflicts more with State C when there is a cost attached to  NH (that is, a cost to the other person) and when there is a group orientation. For State T, the greatest conflict between H  48o/8/3-7  occurs in PDI, that is, when there is a cost (to the subject) attached to H and no group orientation. The similar effects of H  on T in NCI and PDG might be interpreted as meaning that the addition of a group orientation to the instructions decreases the  "irrationality" of the H response.  This analysis of the four parameters indicates that perhaps the effect of values on behavior and the effect of behavior on  values are independent of each other; there may be, for example, a strong "temptation" to behave in a manner inconsistent with  the value state, and either great or little effect of that behavior on the value state.  Another thing this analysis shows is that the NCI and PDG conditions, which in the mean number of H responses are alike, are  really quite different; the PDG condition has relatively high intrapersonal conflict. It may be hypothesized that factors that  tend to increase the importance of "group" values (for example, an increase in the cohesiveness of the group) should increase  p and B, and decrease 0. Increases in both cost and cohesiveness simultaneously ("escalation" in the exchange of favors, for  example) should produce a result like the difference between NCI and PDG-more internal conflict and higher probability of  changing value states.
