A probabilistic multidimensional unfolding model is proposed in which the coordinates of each stimulus and each ideal point are allowed to have different variances and covariances on each dimension. The model permits the specification of isotropic or anisotropic variance structures, independent or dependent sampling conditions, and internat or external analyses. Properties of the model are described and comparisons are made to other probabilistic and deterministic unfolding models. © 1995 Academic Press, Inc.

Depeneat samp ling ne used here, refers to a person drawing one sample instead of two, from the ideal point distributionbetween two when stimuli. that This person conceptualization is attempting of to the choose choice process follows Thurstone’s (1927) framework, in which a subject’s choice or expression of preference for one stimulus over another is modeled as the outcome of a sampling process. With independent sampling, two samples are drawn from the ideal point distribution, one for each of the two stimuli being considered. Dependent sampling seems to be a more plausible process when the stimuli are presented simultaneously to a subject. Unlike independent sampling, the dependent sampling assumption makes it possible to estimate separately the variance structure of both stimuli and ideal points. (Dependent and independent sampling for the one-dimensional case is discussed more fully in Zinnes and Griggs, 1974.) 

The present model differs from the wandering idea! point model (Bockenholt & Gaul, 1984; De Soete, Carroll, & DeSarbo, 1986), which assumes that only the ideal points are probabilistic; the stimulus points are deterministic. This may be an appropriate assumption for some data sets, but for many interesting data sets, involving complex, manydimensional stimuli, our expectation is that the variances of the stimuli will be larger than that of the ideal points.

Maximum likelihood (ML) estimation requires good initial estimates of the parameters. Initial estimates were provided using the isotropic space procedure described in Zinnes and MacKay (1987). The ML estimation then proceeded by sequentially reestimating the variances holding the location estimates fixed and then reestimating the location estimates holding the variances fixed. Use of this alternating estimation procedure alleviates the bilateral indeterminacy of scaling (Kruskal, 1978) and results in’ solutions that are as good or better than those obtained in one-stage approaches. This alternating estimation procedure continued until there was no improvement in the likelihood function. Two to three sets of iterations were all that were needed in the analyses reported here.

7. DISCUSSION 

FIG. 16. Maximum likelihood solution for 12 residences and 20 ideal points using an anisotropic dependent sampling model with separate variance estimates for stimuli and ideal points. Labeled points represent the residences and unlabeled points represent the ideal points. 

; The models we have looked at are based on some very simple and intuitive extensions of Thurstone’s Case V comparative judgment model and Coomb’s unfolding model to

multidimensional spaces. More complex models, such as those involving unique variances on all stimuli, dimensional weights, and more general psychophysical functions relating judgments and distance ratios appear to be relatively easy to implement, but are not developed here. Instead, our emphasis has been on trying to understand the implications of anisotropic spaces for the simple unfolding model. 

From. a psychological process point. of view, the proposed model ; is based upon the assumption that the perceptual effect . . : : of a stimulus is fixed on a single trial but random over trials. Unidimensional models based upon this assumption go well back into the last century. Similarly, this model assumes that a persons’ ideal point is also random over trials. This is a more recent assumption, but one that seems to have been . well established for at least 30 years (Coombs, 1958). This paper has discussed only two of the many numerical analysis issues that arise when working with ratios of quadratic forms in normal variables distributions. Some of the numerical operations, particularly the integration and - oe . . “ optimization phases, can be implemented in very different ways. Parameter estimates are sensitive to the specific algorithm selected. Finding the best algorithm is a continuing effort, as new algorithms are constantly appearing. 

Another area of rapid change concerns our mathematical understanding of quadratic forms and the ratio of quadratic forms ae . a : . distributions. Interest by mathematicians in these distributions increases each year. However, our knowledge of particular forms, such as the ratio of general quadratic forms, is still very limited.
