INTRODUCTION  In most applications of mathematical learning theory the experimenter tends to ignore the structure of the stimulus set as  well as possible contextual contingencies. That is, it is usually assumed that all items are the same and that the sequence of  presentation is immaterial (the homogeneity and stationary environment assumptions, respectively). These assumptions are  sometimes justified by randomizing over presentation items and by allowing for warm-up effects. There is ample evidence,  however, that these assumptions are invalid in cumulative learning situations and for performance tasks which are context  dependent.  Stochastic finite-state theory, which has been developed in recent years, provides a new conceptual apparatus to deal with  both context and contingencies. In general, the theory consists of models of finite-state systems which admit and emit certain  input and output, respectively, at discrete time intervals. The behavior of the system is usually defined by specifying three  finite sets and a conditional probability, namely, the input, output and internal-states sets, and the probability p(y, s; ~  x, si); this is the probability of the system going to state s; and emitting the output symbol y given it had been in state si  and fed with the input symbol x.  The names "stochastic sequential machines," "probabilistic automaton" and others have been used to refer to various stochastic  finite-state models which may be structurally distinct. As a  consequence, several formal relations have been established between various models which exhibit equivalent external behavior.  That is not to say that the choice of a particular model for a specific application is irrelevant in a class of externally  equivalent models. The choice should depend, especially in psychological paradigms, on the application and on a realistic  interpretation provided by the descriptive power of the model.  Suppes (1969) proved the theorem that there is an asymptotic isomorphism between a stimulus response model and a finite  automaton. His formal development was restricted to one representation, a recognition automaton (Rabin, 1964). Later in the  paper he proposed a two-state probabilistic automaton as an example of a performance model of an individual, solving column  addition problems.  The purpose of this paper is to investigate another representation for the same task considered by Suppes. Since arithmetic  problems possess a simple structure and their solution involves a well-defined algorithmic flow, they provide a natural  subject matter for exploring models of information processing.  In the next section we describe Suppes' model and list some problems. In Section 3 we introduce a general model of stochastic  sequential machines. In Section 4 we apply the model to describe the behavior of young students performing addition problems.  In Section 5 we investigate the adequacy of the model with respect to some actual data.  2. A plausible explanation to  this phenomenon is the following. The probability that a student generates a spurious carry is negligible as long as previous  columns did not have a carry. In the notations of Section 2, P(Si = 1 I ci = 0 for all i) = 0; errors in column i occur  because of addition difficulty only with probability y = P(Zj 0 (mi + ni + si_1) mod 10), which is the same error probability  as in the first column. If, however, a carry were already encountered in at least one previous, column then P(Si = 1 I ci = 0  and 3c; = 1 for j < i) is approximately equal to 9 % as can be calculated from Table 2. In other words, a carry has a residual  effect by causing spurious carries in subsequent columns.  Of course, we cannot be sure that our interpretation of this phenomenon is the correct one. However, detailed investigation of  the error columns in the data revealed responses which were greater by one than the correct answer. This effect was not  confined to columns within one problem; spurious carries were produced beyond problem boundaries.  The value of our approach is in its capacity to describe the process parsimoniously; greater prediction was gained by  switching two entries only in state so of A(xl).
