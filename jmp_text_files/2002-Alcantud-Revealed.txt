Revealed Indifference and Models of Choice Behavior 

J. C. R. Alcantud 

Universidad de Salamanca 

This paper introduces an approach to the study of rational choice behavior which is based on the concept of subjective indifference, as well as its relationships with both the classical preference-based approach and the choice behavior approach. With regard to the first relationship (which has already been studied in Alcantud, 2001, as a purely topological and order-theoretical problem) new considerations are now put forward in the decision theory context. In short, if a decisionmaker has some indifference classes defined on a choice set, then we know the conditions under which his/her tastes can be modeled by a continuous preference yielding these indifference classes. The second problem is posed and tackled here and gives place to a theory of revealed indifference, where both the decisive, and indecisive cases will be considered. © 2002 Elsevier Science (USA) 

Key Words: subjective indifference; preference; revealed indifference; choice structure. 

1. INTRODUCTION 

With regard to modelling individual choice behavior, there are two main distinct approaches which refer to different methodologies. The first one considers the decisionmaker’s tastes as the primitive concept, and they are typically summarized in a binary relation of a certain type. In particular, individual agents’ (strict) preferences are usually modeled through asymmetric and negatively transitive binary relations. The term ‘‘preference’’ will be henceforth applied to this type of order, which reflects strong rationality assumptions on the agent. The other approach considers choice behavior as the primitive characteristic and makes assumptions directly on it. These are intended to impose elements of consistency on the choice behavior. 

A central issue in pure decision theory is to explain the relationship between these two approaches. This may be found, e.g., in Mas-Colell, Whinston, and Green (1995), especially in Sections 1.C, 1.D, 3.H, and 3.J. In short, the preference-based approach is, in general, more restrictive than the choice-based approach, and the circumstances under which they are equivalent are well known when we refer to consumer demand behavior. 

This paper considers the possibility of departing instead from the knowledge of another important feature in the line of the first approach, the agents’s indifference. Considering this model favors practical applications. Indeed, it seems more plausible that an agent expresses his/her indifference classes rather than his/her preferences with the coherence required by the respective models. Situations that reflect such a possibility typically arise in social decisionmaking, as will be shown in Example 1 in Section 4. It is our purpose to conduct a study that will complement the aforementioned. Our aim is, therefore, twofold. On the one hand, we intend to recall necessary and sufficient conditions under which this alternative way to model individual choice behavior leads to that by preferences of certain significant types. For this we shall refer to a study that was started in Alcantud (2001). New interpretations will be derived in this general framework. On the other hand, the relationship of this alternative approach with respect to the observational approach will be tackled here. 

With regard to the first topic we take up our research in (2001), where we answered the following question: If we study an agent’s choice behavior by taking his/her indifference on the set of options as the primitive concept, can we assume that his/her tastes may be modeled by a continuous preference? The proof we developed there is highly technical since it makes use of tools that we produced in order to study topologies intrinsic to spaces ordered by preferences. However, Section 3 includes a sketch of an alternative proof that will provide a relevant interpretation of the underlying process through intuitive reasonings in well-known terms. Another related topic, the determination of a continuous preference by its induced indifference, will also be briefly addressed in order to complement the exposition (cf. Alcantud & Gutiérrez, 1999). 

The second topic concerns the relationships between modelling the indifference of an agent on pairs of options and his/her choice behavior. It gives place to a theory of revealed indifference which will be presented in Section 4. For that purpose we introduce an axiom of revealed indifference. It is intended to impose rationality on choice behavior with regard to declaring that subjective indifference between pairs of elements behaves rightly in accordance with choices (in senses that will be hereby proposed). For the sake of the exposition, that section will also refer briefly to the theory of revealed preference, that is, to relationships between the approaches by preferences and by choice behavior.

3. THE APPROACHES BY INDIFFERENCE AND BY PREFERENCES 

In this section we explore issues regarding the relationships between modelling individual tastes through preferences and modeling by indifference classes. 

Of course, it is obvious that the knowledge of an agent’s preference permits him/her to obtain his/her indifference. Furthermore, we have provided conditions under which the original information contained in the preference is uniquely determined by the indifference. This holds if the set of options is a connected topological space and we impose continuity on the class of preferences in which we are interested (cf. Alcantud et al. , 1999). 

Consider the converse situation. If we depart from a given indifference then we may be interested in constructing a preference of some particular type (e.g.,

continuous) that induces it. Such a possibility would explain the indifference as arising from a richer model, and therefore it would permit us to appeal to results stated in that context. In this case, such indifference must be necessarily an equivalence relation. The problem is trivial if preferences are not required to satisfy any further axioms. However, uniqueness cannot be obtained in this simple framework, since in fact any linear ordering of the indifference classes yields a solution. 

Therefore, let us consider now the case where our interest is focused on the relevant class of continuous preferences; for this, a topological structure on the space is assumed. We have proved in Alcantud (2001) the following characterization of the equivalence relations on topological spaces that may be explained as indifferences arising from continuous preferences. 

Theorem 2. Let (X, y) be a topological space and let R be an equivalence binary relation on X. The following statements are equivalent: 

(a) There exists a preference on X which is continuous with respect to y and whose indifference coincides with R. 

(b) There exist two nests of open sets of y whose union generates a topology y Œ such that R saturates y Œ and the equivalence classes by R are closed in y Œ . 

Thus, if we depart from a model of individual choice behavior in which indifference is a primitive concept, then we know under which conditions this is compatible with assuming that there are underlying continuous preferences explaining such behavior. Again, uniqueness is guaranteed in the event that the space is connected. Recall that the upper and lower contour sets obtained from a continuous preference O are two nests of open sets whose union generates yor( O ) . The equivalence classes by the associated indifference ’ are closed in yor( O ) , and ’ saturates yor( O ) . Therefore, the fact that (a) implies (b) through this process means that the sufficient condition (b) is not artificial in this context. 

To end this section, we now proceed to give a significant observation that does not derive directly from that statement. Theorem 2 allows us to show that if one can find some sets that behave as contour sets , in the sense given by (b) in the theorem, then they are upper/lower sets of a preference that solves the problem , and they also somehow generate the contour sets. This fact might be better understood in view of the following sketch of an alternative proof that differs significantly from that given originally in Alcantud (2001) and the remark below. We give that proof here because it is based on intuitive reasoning with concepts arising from pure decision theory and thus may help to clarify what underlies the whole process.

4. THE OBSERVATIONAL APPROACH AND REVEALED INDIFFERENCE 

This section is devoted to studying relationships between the observational approach and the model by indifference. In order to give a unified view of the topic, we shall address issues regarding connections between the observational approach and the model by preferences, too. 

A choice structure is intended to be a device that reflects all the situations that are posed to a decisionmaker (which are captured in a collection B of subsets of a primitive set of options X ), as well as the alternatives that he/she might choose in each possible instance (the acceptable alternatives, as expressed by a choice rule). 

Suppose that an agent has established his/her choices on some different choice situations (i.e., subsets of the set of options X ) and that these are captured by a given choice structure ( B , c) . In this section we are concerned with the possibility that the agent has a well-defined indifference with appropriate properties regarding such choices . We shall introducethese reasonablepropertiesin forthcomingdefinitions.

Now, since we expect some amount of consistency in choice behavior, its representations should satisfy certain rationality restrictions. Much has been written on that topic, and assumptions such as congruence or revealed preference axioms among others have been widely studied in contexts involving choice structures (see, e.g., Richter, 1971, and Sen, 1971). Those conditions appeal, in different senses, to facts that depend on the idea of having elements that are revealed preferred to other ones. We introduce here an alternative approach, which is based on the idea of having the notion of a subjective underlying indifference instead. Next we study the properties that we might reasonably expect from this model. We then wonder whether an observed choice behavior can be explained through that axiom. 

In the model we are currently considering, it seems sensible to request that the following two properties hold whenever the agent possesses a well-defined subjective indifference, in the form of a certain binary relation ’ of some type, on the set X : 

(i) For any B ¥ B and x, y ¥ B such that x ’ y, x ¥ c(B) if and only if y ¥ c(B) . 

(ii) If there is a B ¥ B such that x, y ¥ c(B) , then x ’ y . Observe that (ii) implies reflexivity of ’ provided that {x} ¥ B for all x ¥ X . Besides, the symmetry of ’ is implied by (i) and (ii) if B contains all the subsets of X with two elements. Indeed, in this case x ’ y yields y ’ x since either x ¥ c({x, y}) (and therefore y ¥ c({x, y}) by (i)) or y ¥ c({x, y}) (and therefore y ¥ c({x, y}) by (i)). In each case, Condition (ii) implies that y ’ x .

However, in our framework the primitive concept is the choice structure. Thus we have a different problem: Under which conditions can we find preferences or indifferences that are compatible with the choice structure? 

It is the subject of the theory of revealed preference to search for conditions under which choice structures may be rationalized ; that is, when one may explain the choices modeled by a choice structure ( B , c) as a result of maximizing preferences, according to the process shown in Example 2. In this case the preferences are obviously compatible with ( B , c) . In particular, the following two models are especially fruitful. 

Model 1 . In the general case (that is, no assumptions are made on B), SARP yields rationalizability provided that choices are univalued (i.e., c(A) consists of a single element for each A ¥ B). This can be found e.g., in Mas-Colell et al. (1995, Proposition 3.J.1 and the comment after it). The converse is immediate, and therefore SARP characterizes rationalizability in the present context. 

However, uniqueness cannot be obtained because the agent does not necessarily reveal his/her preferences on each pair of options. This obliges us to extend the
