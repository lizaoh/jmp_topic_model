Tversky's ((1979) Prospect theory: An analysis of decision under risk, Econometrica, 47, 263-291) prospect  theory postulates that the subjective value of a gamble is determined by different combination rules depending on the outcomes  of the gamble. The theory distinguishes regular from irregular gambles, and in addition, distinguishes two different kinds of  irregular gambles. The representations of value for these classes of gambles are formally analogous, but they differ in the  way that gamble probabilities are integrated with the worth of gamble outcomes. The present work identifies properties of  preference, restricted betweenness and equivalence under interchange, that play a central role in justifying the use of  different combination rules for different classes of gambles in prospect theory. ^ 1987 Academic Press, Inc.  Unlike expected utility theory and subjective expected utility theory, Kahneman and Tversky's (1979) prospect theory  postulates that the subjective value of a gamble is determined by different combination rules depending on the outcomes of the  gamble. In particular, gambles are classified as regular or irregular depending on the magnitude of gamble outcomes relative  to a neutral reference level. The weights of complementary events can sum to less than one in the so-called regular gambles,  but they always sum to one in the irregular gambles.  I will argue that the distinction between different classes of gambles in prospect theory is motivated by three  considerations. First, the prospect theory analysis of the Allais paradox implies that the weights of complementary events  must sum to less than one in some regular gambles. Second, a "betweenness" property will be described that forces the weights  of complementary events to sum one in all irregular gambles. Third, I will point out a symmetry condition, called equivalence  under interchange, that requires a further division of the class of irregular gambles into subsets with different  representations. Taken together, these analyses show why prospect theory must apply different representations to three classes  of gambles.  Please address all correspondence and reprint requests to John. M. GAMBLE CONSTRAINTS IN PROSPECT THEORY 411  REGULAR AND IRREGULAR PROSPECTS  Prospect theory postulates that outcomes are perceived as gains or losses relative to a neutral reference level. In gambling  for money, a natural reference level is a 0 outcome in which one neither receives nor pays out money. For convenience, I will  assume here that 0 is the reference level, although some other outcome could be identified as the reference level without  essential change to the analysis. Thus, the outcome x is a gain if x > 0, and a loss if x < 0.  Prospect theory is formulated with respect to gambles of the form  (x, p; y, q), (1)  signifying that one receives x with probability p, y with probability q, and 0 with probability 1 - p - q, When q = 1 - p,  gamble (1) is equivalent to a two-outcome gamble between x and y. I will follow Kahneman and Tversky in calling such gambles  prospects. A prospect is said to be irregular if p + q = 1 and either x > 0 and y > 0, or x < 0 and y < 0. In other words, an  irregular prospect is a two-outcome gamble whose outcomes are either both gains or both losses. A prospect is said to be  regular if it is not irregular, i.e., if p + q < 1, or p + q = 1 and either x > 0 > y, or x < 0 < y. Kahneman and Tversky (1979) explain the difference between regular and irregular prospects in terms of differences in  information processing during the editing stage of the choice process. Regular prospects are evaluated simply as a weighted  combination of a gain and loss component. The processing of irregular prospects is described as follows.  In the editing phase such [irregular] prospects are segregated into two components: (i) the riskless component, i.e., the  minimum gain or loss which is certain to be obtained or paid; (ii) the risky component, i.e., the additional gain or loss  which is actually at stake.... [The] value of a strictly positive or strictly negative prospect equals the value of the  riskless component plus the value-difference between the outcomes, multiplied by the weight associated with the more extreme  outcome. (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979, p. 276).  Equations (2) and (3) stated above formalize the processing differences between regular and irregular prospects. SUMMARY AND CONCLUSIONS  Three arguments have been presented here. The prospect theory analysis of the Allais paradox implies that a3(p) + /33(1 - p) <  1 for some values of p. Restricted betweenness and an innocuous continuity assumption imply that ak(p)+fk(1 - p)= 1 for every  p when k= 1 or 2. Thus the analysis of the Allais paradox, restricted betweenness, and the continuity assumption combine to  imply that the probability coefficients of regular and irregular prospects must be different, at least for some values of p.  Equivalence under interchange and the analysis of the Allais paradox imply that there must be p such that a,(p) =Aa2(p) and  /31(1 - p) 0 /32(1 - p). Thus different probability coefficients are needed for irregular gambles of the first and second kind.  Restricted betweenness and equivalence under interchange are normatively compelling principles of preference. I have assumed  in the present analysis that they are also descriptively valid, largely because their normative status is very transparent.  The continuing discovery of nonnormative preference behaviors, however, suggests that one should be cautious on this point.  The normative analysis of the behavior of the Allais paradox remains a matter of some controversy (Allais, 1953; Savage, 1954;  Tversky, 1975), but the fact that this preference behavior occurs is well documented (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979; MacCrimmon &  Larsson, 1979; Slovic & Tversky, 1974). It is interesting to note that the Allais paradox is used in motivating the  distinction between regular and irregular prospects, and also the distinction between the two kinds of irregular prospects.  The central role of the Allais paradox in motivating distinctions in the prospect theory representation of gambles has not  previously been recognized.  418 JOHN M.
