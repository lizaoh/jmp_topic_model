A TAXONOMY OF LOCAL NETWORKS 

There are a variety of procedures used to construct local networks which affect the way in which the network describes an individual’s social locale (see Walker, Wasserman, & Wellman, 1993). Five important aspects of local network data are the following: 

1. Restrictions on the Number of Relations. Restrictions are often placed on respondents in a network study limiting the number of relations for which ties are reported. For example, interest may center on just a small number ofrelations, such as “To whom do you go for support?,” “Who are your friends?,” and “With whom do you have important discussions?.” Such restrictions are usually imposed by the design of the study. We will refer to the measured relations as primitive and denote them by the labels 7,, 7), ..., T,. The ego and the individuals named by ego (the alters) are denoted by the elements of the set ¥ = {1, 2, 3, ...,n}, and we write 7,,(i, 7) = 1 if there is a link or a relational tie from itoj of type T,, (and T,,(7, 7) =0, otherwise). Ego will always be node 1. 

2. Restrictions on the Directionality of Ties. Local network studies also differ in their treatment of the directionality (Wasserman & Faust, 1994) of network tes. In some studies, ties are regarded as directional, and information is sought for each relation Tr, about a possible tie from node / to node /, as well as a possible tie from node j to node i. In this case, each relation T;, may be seen as a directed ego-centered graph in which loops (that is, ties from a node i to itself) are usually forbidden. In other studies, ties are viewed as nondirectional and a single tie of type T,, between nodes i and j is recorded. Where ties are nondirectional, each relation T;, may be seen as an ego-centered graph (also usually without loops). 

3. The Source of Local Network Data. Local networks can also be distinguished in terms of the source from which information about ties in the network has been obtained. In some sampling schemes, ego is the source of all relational information (e.g., Burt, 1984); in others alters are asked about their outgoing ties; in yet others, an observer is the source of all relational information (see Walker, Wasserman, & Wellman, 1993). 

4. Restrictions on the Outdegree. In many survey studies (e.g., Burt, 1984), the number of individuals to whom a given person is connected by a given type of relation is restricted to be no greater than a fixed integer N. Typically, such restrictions arise from the use of “fixed choice” format for questions eliciting network information, such as “Name up to five people to whom you feel close.” NV is usually small, such as 5 (Burt, 1984; Fischer, 1982) or 9 (Kadushin, 1982). Restrictions on N are usually imposed only for practical reasons and, where the structural effects of restriction have been studied, the practice has not been recommended (e.g., Holland & Leinhardt, 1973). 

5. Restrictions on the Order of a Network. In a local network, the members of ego’s social circle may all be direct associates of ego {in which case, the local network comprises 2 first-order zone for ego) or they may be indirect associates. For example, a second-order zone contains (a) individuals in ego’s first-order zone as well as members of the first-order zone of each of those individuals, and (b) all of their interpersonal links. Thus, a second-order zone comprises individuals connected to ego by a walk (defined below) of no more than two links, together with all their interrelations. More generally, we may obtain a kth-order zone by assessing relations among all individuals to whom ego is connected by a walk of no more than k links. 

The notion of a walk is the basis of characterising differd similariti lati f local k ences and similarities among relations for a local network. 

DEFINITION. A walk U,U,---U, of length k in a local network is a sequence /,, f5, ..., i, 4, of network members such that i, is related to i, ,, by the relation U,,, for each h=1, 2,..., K. 

Note that nodes may occur any number of times in a walk; for example, i, could be the same as i;. Ego can cour any number of times in a walk. Note also that cycles are permitted, as is usually the case with algebraic models (eg., Boorman & White, 1976; Boyd, 1991; Pattison, 1993). 

For example, in Wellman’s (1979) study of personal networks in East York, respondents were asked to name the persons outside the home to whom they felt closest, and then to indicate which pairs of these individuals were close 14 one another. Thus, the order of each local network was one, ties were nondirectional and ego was the source of data for a single Closeness relation. No restrictions were placed on the number of persons that could be named by ego, although the number varied only between 3 and 6. 

The General Social Survey network items (Burt, 1984) obtain relational information for a maximum of five persons named by ego. The local network generated by these items also has ego as the source of relation information and is of order 1. ALGEBRAIC MODELS FOR LOCAL NETWORKS 

Algebraic models are useful representations of the structure of network relations because the relational operations from which they are constructed—the partial ordering and composition of collections of network walks—have 

meaningful, natural-language interpretations in terms of network relations. For instance, a statement of the form “one is usually acquainted with the close friends of one’s close friends” has an approximate representation in terms of the partial ordering CC < A, where C andA denote the relations of close friendship and acquaintance, respectively, and where CC is a relation representing network walks of two C relations. Algebraic models have been argued to possess two important properties. Before describing an algebra for a local network, we may note that a local network can be presented in the form of one or more # xn square matrices (see Table 1), with one matrix for each type of primitive, simple, or generator relation in the network. We use the label 7, to refer to both the relation as well as its matrix and we adopt the convention of using the first row and column of each matrix for the relations of ego. The collection of primitive relations defining the network is denoted by T={7), 7,..., T,}. The relational ties in a local network may either be dichotomous or valued, although in the interests of simplicity we shall assume dichotomous data here (for discussion of algebras for the valued case, see Pattison, 1993). Thus, a “1” entry in cell (i, /) of a matrix for a relation indicates the presence of a relational tie from actori to actor /.

Choosing an Algebra for a Local Network 

For any given local network, we have presented a series of algebraic structures that may be used to represent it. We could construct the partial local role algebra of order 1, or of order 2, or of order 3, and so on (if appropriate); we could also construct the local role algebra as we initially defined it. A natural question to ask is how these various structures are related to one another and how should we select a particular one for a given application. 

First, we may observe that for some sufficiently high value of m, the partial local role algebra O°” of order mis identical to the local role algebra Q. (This follows from the fact that the full set of relation vectors for an element ofa local network has only a finite number of distinct relation vectors, so that there is some walk length m beyond which no new distinct relation vectors are generated). 

The result is helpful in choosing amongst the alternative algebraic structures, since it guarantees that the choice set is finite, namely {Q", 9, ... 9°}. From there, the choice depends on the kind of methodological restriction on walk length in the local network under consideration, as well as largeon its number size. For of instance, distinct relation vectors, if a local network then gives computation rise to a of its local role algebra may be difficult. It may then be convenient to deal with representations of bounded sizes, purely on computational grounds. We illustrate this point in the example presented above, where we compare Tables 3 and 4 with Tables 5 and 6. Note that, while the algebraic precision of the “full” local role algebra is lost in the partial representation, it is nonetheless the case that many of the relationships captured in the “full” version are present in the partial one (such as the inclusion of C in F). A suitable order for the partial local role algebra may then be chosen as a value A for which the number of distinct relation vectors Qo” is not too large, and the number of equations and inclusions is not too small. Mandel (1983), for example, considers walks of length 3 or less in many of his illustrative applications of role algebras. 

The effect of methodological restrictions on walk length on the choice of an algebra is a little more complex. If the network has no restrictions on walk length, then the local Tole algebra Q is a natural choice, provided that it is not too large. However, in the case ofa local network constructed as the kth-order zone of some network member, the partial local role algebras of orders k and k + | can be argued to have some special significance. Constructions based on the truncated set of relation vectors of order k may be interpreted with some confidence, since the truncated set pertains to all walks of length & or less in relation to all persons reachable by such walks. The truncated set of order k + 1 is l!so a defensible construction, since it should permit adequate comparison between walks of length k + 1 or less from ego to members of his/her Ath-order zone. As mentioned earher, though, for walks of length k +2 or greater, there is no guarantee of adequate comparison among walks, since there may be walks of length k + 2 from ego to members of the Ath-order zone which pass through individuals outside the kth-order zone. Such walks are therefore omitted from representation. 

It is important to note that restrictions both on walk length and on the outdegree of each network element in a local network influence the adequacy of the (partial) local role algebra which can be constructed from it. Restrictions on outdegree clearly place a restriction on labelled walks in a local network, and these restrictions lead in turn to effects on the local network algebra. Restrictions on walk length also affect labelled walks beyond a certain length, and we have argued that it is wise to use a partial role algebra of order k +1 or less for a local network constructed from a k th-order zone.

CONCLUDING REMARKS 

In summary, we have outlined a scheme for constructing algebraic representations of an individual’s social role that is based on statistical comparisons among “short” network paths. The statistical comparisons are made in a manner that is sensitive to some of the methodological constraints imposed by methods for collecting network data. Clearly, these constructions need to be evaluated in a variety of substantive investigations, but they do appear to hold some promise in circumventing the practical difficulties associated with currently available methods.
