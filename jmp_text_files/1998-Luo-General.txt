A General Formulation for Unidimensional Unfolding and Pairwise Preference Models: Making Explicit the Latitude of Acceptance 

Guanzhong Luo 

South China Normal University, Guangzhou, China 

Probabilistic unfolding models for direct responses of persons to statements are characterised by single peaked response functions. The range in which a positive response is most likely is termed the latitude of acceptance, a well known but little researched concept in the modelling of attitude measurement. This paper derives a general form for probabilistic unfolding models in which a natural parameter characterises the latitude of acceptance. It is shown that a number of already known models for unfolding can be reexpressed in this general form with different operational functions and that, in doing so, the implied latitude of acceptance for these models is identified. It is also shown that other unfolding models can be generated readily by specifying the operational function of the general form. A general form for pairwise preference models is also presented. A discussion on the latitude of acceptance parameter as a scale parameter is also provided. � 1998 Academic Press 

Key Words: measurement of attitude; probabilistic unfolding; Rasch model; single-peaked function; latitude of acceptance; hyperbolic cosine model. 

1. INTRODUCTION 

Single peaked response functions were implied in Thurstone's approach to the measurement of attitude. They were implied in the step in which the persons responded to statements that were already scaled onto a latent trait continuum according to his law of comparative judgement (Thurstone, 1927, 1928). Thurstone referred to these scales, to which a person responded by either Agree or Disagree, as the ``maximum probability'' type (Thurstone, 1959, p. 214). Coombs (1950, 1964) elaborated these response functions and introduced the term unfolding to characterise the process of identifying the direction of the scale in the presence of responses

which were only a function of the distance between the locations of the person and the statement, irrespective of direction. Within a deterministic framework, Coombs (1964) adopted the concept of latitude of acceptance from Young and Falk (1956) and Hovland, Harvey, and Sherif (1957) to denote the interval within which a positive response was given. More recently, substantial progress has been made in developing probabilistic models for unfolding for both direct responses and pairwise preferences (e.g., Bechtel, 1968; Sixtl, 1973; Davison, 1977; van Schuur, 1984, 1988; DeSarbo 6 Hoffman, 1986; Andrich, 1988, 1989, Hoijtink, 1990, 1991). The basic form of these models is the single peaked response function. A distinctive model amongst these is the hyperbolic cosine model (HCM) described in Andrich 6 Luo (1993), Luo (1994), and Andrich (1996). (An equivalent model was also developed independently in a different form by Verhelst 6 Verstralen, 1993). The distinctive feature of the HCM is that, in addition to location parameters for the person and the statement, it has an explicit parameter, termed the unit parameter, from which a range for a positive response to be most likely can be defined. Using the HCM as a primitive model, this paper provides a synthesis for probabilistic unfolding models. Three steps are involved in reaching this synthesis. First, the HCM is reparameterized in order to make explicit the parameter which defines exactly the range on the continuum within which the response of a person to an statement is more likely to be positive than negative. Second, a general form of unfolding models is abstracted from the reparameterized form of the HCM and it is demonstrated that a number of previously proposed unfolding models, including the HCM, can be viewed as special cases of this general form. To consolidate this conclusion, it is shown that these models have an implicit latitude of acceptance parameter which can be made explicit. In most of these models, the implicit latitude of acceptance parameter is a constant. Third, it is shown that various new unfolding models can be constructed readily from the general form, and three such models are constructed. In addition, by an analogous procedure, a general form for pairwise preference models is also introduced.

The general form (9) requires that the value of the latitude of acceptance parameter must be not less than 0. It implies that the probabilistic curve for a positive response is required to cross with the probabilistic curve for a negative response. When the two curves do not cross, the latitude of acceptance parameter is undefined. In this case, a person is more likely to give a negative response than a positive response to a statement even if the person is at the location of the statement. This structure is then considered ill-conditioned because of the dominance of the random effect on positive responses.

7. SUMMARY AND DISCUSSIONS 

It is well known that the response function of an unfolding model is single-peaked. However, the general mathematical structure of the response function has not be explored in the literature. This paper provides a general formulation for unidimensional unfolding models. With this general formulation, two structural factors of an unfolding model��the operational function and the latitude of acceptance parameter��are identified. By specifying different operational function of the general form, different unfolding models can be generated. This general formulation also makes explicit the role of the latitude acceptance parameter in shaping an unfolding model. In general, the latitude of acceptance parameter is not an arbitrary scale parameter except under a very special condition of the operational function. 

The general formulation of unfolding models described in this paper reveals that the required properties of any particular operational function for an unfolding 

414 

GUANZHONG LUO 

model are (i) nonnegative (P1), (ii) monotonic (P2), and (iii) symmetric (P3). With this general formulation, other subsequent properties that any unfolding model will hold can also be examined. For example, it is evident that no sufficient statistics exist (except the raw data matrix) for statement location and person location parameters within the unfolding models (10), (12), and (14). With the general form of (9), it can be seen that no sufficient statistics exist for statement location and person location parameters within any unfolding model. This critical property distinguishes the unfolding models from the cumulative models, within which there exists a class of models (Rasch models) for which the sufficient statistics are readily available (Rasch, 1961). 

It is noted that with the HCM, there is a monotonic correspondence between the latitude of acceptance and the maximum probability for a positive response. That is, the probability P[xni=1 | ;n=$i , \i ] increases as the value of the latitude of acceptance parameter \i increases. This is considered consistent with a intuition in Andrich 6 Luo (1993) by referring to the role of units in the process of linear measurement. This phenomena can also be observed with the generalised SSLM and some other models. However, this property is not essential for the general form (9). In fact, the extended PARELLA model, which is also a special case of the general form, has the maximum probability 1.0, regardless of the value of the latitude of acceptance parameter. It is a empirical question whether the monotonic correspondence between the latitude of acceptance and the maximum probability is observed in any particular data set. 

Another issue raised here is whether different models with different operational functions would yield virtually the same fit for a given data set. Theoretically, this is not the case. This can be examined from the perspectives of data generation and data analysis. From the former, the operational function of (9) employed to generate the data will determine the structure of the data. For example, if the data are generated according to the PARELLA model, then a person with location ;n will give a positive response to the statement at his�her ideal point ($i=;n) with certainty. However, if the data are generated according to the HCM, the probability that this person gives a positive response to the statement depends on the value of the latitude of acceptance parameter \i. In this respect, different models will generate different data. On the other hand, in estimating parameters for a given data set, all statements and persons involved are to be located onto the same scale governed by the value of the latitude of acceptance parameter, which is the property of the data. This simultaneous calibration of parameters will detect misfit between the specified model and the true structure of the data. The sensitivity with which the misfit will be detected will increase as the sample size increases. Using the general form (9), it can be anticipated that a general computer program for unfolding analysis can be developed with which users can specify different operational functions 9 according to their understanding of the data, as long as 9 holds the properties (P1), (P2), and (P3). On the same data set, the program could estimate the parameters according to different operational functions specified, and the user can then identify the model that best fits the data. 

The latitude of acceptance parameter has been a well known but little researched concept, in part because it has not been readily formulated in unfolding models. The general form of the models, together with the understanding of the implication

Finally, a general pairwise preference model can be constructed readily from the general unfolding model for direct response of persons to statements. This means that for any specific direct response unfolding model, there is a pairwise counterpart. It seems to the author that the pairwise preference models studied so far have had the constraint that all statements have the same latitude of acceptance. Whether this constraint is essential in the applications of pairwise preference models is to be explored.
