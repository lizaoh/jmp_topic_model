We provide an axiomatic foundation to semi-lexicographic and uniformly semi-lexicographic preferences. We use two new axioms in our characterization. One of them requires the weak preference order

Maximizing along with a stronger version of PAD characterizes uniformly semi-lexicographic preferences. Uniformly semi-lexicographic preferences are not transitive. We show that the transitive uniformly semi-lexicographic preferences are lexicographic.

The lower contour sets of a continuous preference are closed subsets in the topology of the set of alternatives. In fact a continuous preference is completely characterized by its lower contour sets. Such topological characterizations of discontinuous preferences are not possible since the lower contour sets of discontinuous preferences are not closed. Take-the-best or the lexicographic heuristic has been applied in many fields of decision making, see Gigerenzer and Goldstein  Gigerenzer and Goldstein (1996) , Gigerenzer and Gaissmaier  Gigerenzer and Gaissmaier (2011) , Pachur and Marinello  Pachur and Marinello (2013)  and  Graefe and Armstrong (2012) . In this paper we try to provide an explanation for such a widely used heuristic from an axiomatic point of view where the set of available alternatives is large and hence it takes a long time and patience for a consumer to go over each alternative, i.e., from the perspective of a consumer the space of alternatives is large. An example of a large confusing space of alternatives is the space of different alternatives of pasta sauce discussed in  Schwartz (2004a) . The questionnaire in  Willman-Iivarinen (2017)  considers questions that inquire whether a participant is a maximizer. In particular, the participants were asked whether they tried to choose the best of all possible alternatives. The maximizing behavior in  Willman-Iivarinen (2017)  is called ‘maximizing accuracy’. The paper finds positive correlation between maximizing and choices made lexicographically. Further, the paper finds positive correlation between maximizing and rational choice and negative correlation between maximizing and satisficing behavior. Rational behavior in  Willman-Iivarinen (2017)  is defined as a choice made according to a careful deliberation system i.e., in which a consumer deliberates carefully all the alternatives and compares their properties. The formalization of maximizing in this paper can be explained with the help of the following thought experiment. We imagine a consumer contemplating over two alternatives out of a large set. The consumer then asks herself, if she would like to see more alternatives. If she says no, then she sets the best of the two alternatives as her standard. If she says yes, then the consumer contemplates over two alternatives that are better than the best of the two, i.e., the consumer is not satisfied with the best of what she has contemplated. For a maximizer this process of contemplation is unending. A satisfier will stop at some finite number of iterations as most likely after some finite number of iterations a satisficer will come across an alternative which is good enough and set it to be her standard.  Simon (1956)  provides an example of a similar satisficing behavior on the part of organisms while making choices.

Further, we emphasize that in our formalization a consumer is a maximizer not because there are preferred alternatives outside the unhappy set (which always happens by definition), but because there are preferred alternatives outside the unhappy set which are better than the best that is obtained by combining the attributes of the two alternatives and that the best of the two lies in the unhappy set. We also note that our paper is different from  Fishburn (1971) ,  Borie (2016)  and  Blume, Brandenburger, and Dekel (1990) . In these papers, the space of alternatives is a space of lotteries. Before ending this section we make a remark about the local continuity property of PAD.

PAD is a local continuity property. Any continuous preference, i.e. a preference whose upper and lower contour sets are closed, satisfies PAD and SPAD. The converse is not true as lexicographic preferences satisfy PAD and SPAD and lexicographic preferences are not continuous. However, the line of demarcation between a continuous and a discontinuous preference is thin in the sense that if we also assume that PAD holds for non diagonal alternatives, then the preference is continuous.
