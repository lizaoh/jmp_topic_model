MARLEY2  University of California, Berkeley, California 94720  A simple choice experiment is one in which a subject is asked to select among several alternatives according to some specified criterion, and a ranking experiment is one in which he is asked to rank order the alternatives, again according to some specified criterion. When decisions are governed by a probabilistic process, various connections are possible between simple choice and ranking behavior. In this paper we consider a set of conditions that assumes that the ranking probabilities can be expressed as a "natural" function of the simple choice probabilities. Under these conditions the binary choice probabilities on a finite set A determine the simple choice and ranking probabilities on every subset of A, and explicit forms are given for the functions relating the simple choice and ranking probabilities to the binary choice probabilities.  INTRODUCTION  Luce and Suppes (1965, pp. 256-257) classify asymptotic theories of preference according to three binary distinctions, two of which are relevant to the present study. If we suppose that the responses that a subject makes to stimulus presentations are governed by a probability mechanism, then a theory is algebraic if it requires that these probabilities be 0, z, or 1, and probabilistic otherwise. We consider probabilistic theories. A simple choice experiment is one in which a subject is asked to select among several alternatives according to some specified criterion, and a ranking experiment is one in which he is asked to rank order the alternatives, again according to some specified criterion. We consider theories for both classes of experiments.  When decisions are governed by a probabilistic process that generates probabilities other than 0, 2, and 1, various connections are possible between simple choice and ranking behavior. Theoretical ideas concerning such relations have been presented by Block and Marschak (1960), Luce (1959, pp. 68-74), Luce and Suppes (1965, pp. 351358), Mallows (1957), Marschak (1960), and Pendergrass and Bradley (1960). We may assume either that the simple choice probabilities can be expressed as some "natural" function of the ranking probabilities, or that the ranking probabilities can be expressed "naturally" in terms of the simple choice probabilities. In this paper we concentrate on the latter approach, and develop a set of ideas that recurs frequently in the earlier work, but which has not previously been studied intensively.  The following example illustrates these ideas. Suppose that a three alternative set A = {x, y, z} is presented many times and that, on each occasion, the subject is required to rank order the alternatives from superior to inferior according to some specified criterion. The subject might order the set by first selecting what strikes him as the superior alternative, according to the criterion, and assigning it rank one; this alternative is then discarded, and he selects the superior alternative from the remaining pair, and assigns it rank two; and, of course, the single remaining alternative is assigned rank three.
