Knowledge Space Theory (KST;  Doignon & Falmagne, 1985, 1999; Falmagne & Doignon, 2011 ) has been proved to represent a very useful framework for computer-assisted adaptive knowledge assessment and technology-enhanced personalized learning (see, e.g.,  Anselmi, Robusto, Stefanutti, & de Chiusole, 2016; Falmagne & Doignon, 1988a, 1988b; Heller & Repitsch, 2012; Heller, Steiner, Hockemeyer, & Albert, 2006; Hockemeyer, 2002 ). It follows that KST is a valuable methodological approach for the development of intelligent tutoring systems (ITSs), which are computer-assisted learning environments designed to follow the practices of expert human tutors ( Woolf, 2008 ). ITSs are interactive and adjust to learners with tasks or steps suited to their individual characteristics, needs, or pace of learning ( Shute & Zapata-Rivera, 2007 ).

Some KST-based ITSs are ALEKS, RATH, APeLS, and Knowlab. The first two developed under “traditional” KST that focuses on the solution behavior exhibited on the items. The last two developed under the competence-based KST ( Doignon, 1994; Düntsch & Gediga, 1995; Gediga & Düntsch, 2002; Heller, Ünlü and Albert, 2013; Korossy, 1997, 1999 ), an extension of KST that takes into account the cognitive abilities (skills, competencies) responsible for the observable solution behavior. ALEKS (see, e.g.,  Falmagne & Doignon, 2011 ) is an internet-based, automated system which adaptively assesses knowledge in mathematics, economics, statistics, and chemistry. Created in 1994 thanks to a grant obtained by the National Science Foundation, ALEKS is currently one of the most widely used ITSs in the USA, and it has been found to be as good as traditional classroom teaching ( Fang, Ren, Hu, & Graesser, 2019 ).

RATH ( Hockemeyer, Held, & Albert, 1998 ) and APeLS ( Conlan, Hockemeyer, Wade, & Albert, 2002 ) are adaptive tutoring systems that support the navigation of the learner by presenting only those links to documents for which she fulfills all prerequisites for understanding them. A difference between RATH and APeLS is that the latter distinguishes between actual documents and abstract skills, thus allowing more flexibility with respect to changing documents. RATH has been applied to the field of elementary probability theory ( Hockemeyer et al., 1998 ).

Developed at the University of Padua, Knowlab ( de Chiusole, Stefanutti, Anselmi, & Robusto, 2020 ) consists of an  assessment module  and a  learning module . The former adaptively assesses the skills a learner masters (the competence state) and provides her with a detailed report of her knowledge based on the principles of formative assessment. The latter guides the learner in a learning process that is customized to her knowledge. In both modules, the learner can be presented with automatically generated item instances ( de Chiusole, Stefanutti, Anselmi, & Robusto, 2018 ). The assessment module and the learning module interact with each other in a continuous exchange of information about the learner’s current competence state. An application of Knowlab for learning basic statistics (Stat-Knowlab) has been used by more than 20.000 students at the University of Padua during basic statistics courses, and it has been found to be useful for monitoring their learning processes ( de Chiusole et al., 2020 ). Thanks to a grant from the EU, Knowlab has been translated in English and extended for its use in an international context. One of the aims of the project is the development of new courses to be offered through the Knowlab system.

The learner navigates through the pages of the ITS in order to try to achieve her learning goals. While the navigation behavior of the learner can be directly observed, modeling this behavior usually requires taking into account the latent state of the learner. For instance, the competence state of the learner will influence her concrete actions, such as visiting certain learning objects instead of others. Conversely, the visited learning objects will affect her competence state. There are theoretical developments in KST where learning processes have been modeled in the discrete time. For instance,  Heller, Augustin, Hockemeyer, Stefanutti and Albert (2013)  and  Heller, Keenoy, Levene, Mat Hassan, Hockemeyer, and Albert (2004)  described a hidden Markov model capable of predicting the observable navigation behavior across learning objects and the underlying learning process, as well as their mutual dependencies. In this model, the indicator of time is increased by one unit whenever the learner moves from a learning object to another.  Wang, Tang, Li, and Hu (2018)  used a hidden Markov model to describe the learning behaviors (e.g., asking for explanation, practicing after making an error) of students navigating ALEKS. Four possible actions could be performed by the students (receiving an explanation prompted by ALEKS, requesting an explanation, answering correct, answering incorrect), and the analyzed data consisted of sequences of these actions.  Robusto, Stefanutti, and Anselmi (2010)  and  Stefanutti, Anselmi, and Robusto (2011)  proposed the gain-loss model, a probabilistic model that evaluates learning processes and the effectiveness of learning objects in promoting the attainment of new skills and the maintenance of skills already mastered. The gain-loss model applies to learning processes with two test occasions, namely a pretest and a posttest. Bayesian knowledge tracing ( Corbett & Anderson, 1994; Yudelson, Koedinger, & Gordon, 2013 ) is an approach for modeling the learning process that is not based on KST. It models students’ knowledge as a latent variable in a hidden Markov model. The probability that a student masters specific skills is continuously updated on the basis of the correctness of her responses to items that require those skills to be solved. Applications of the aforementioned models can be found in  Anselmi, Robusto, and Stefanutti (2012, 2013), Corbett and Anderson (1994), de Chiusole, Anselmi, Stefanutti and Robusto (2013), Heller et al. (2004), Heller, Levene, Keenoy, Albert, and Hockemeyer (2007), Robusto et al. (2010) , and  Wang et al. (2018) . All these models are in the discrete time. As such, they take into account neither the sojourn time within the learning objects nor the time elapsed between two assessments. The influence of these aspects on the learning process is not negligible. For instance, visiting a learning object for a few seconds is expected to have a totally different impact on learning compared to studying it for some hours.

The present work deals with modeling learning processes in the continuous time. The bivariate Markov processes (BMPs) described by  Ephraim and Mark (2012)  consist of a pair of stochastic processes in the continuous time, one observable and the other latent, which are jointly Markov. BMPs have been successfully used in several applications such as speech, written character, and image recognition, queuing theory, biomedical engineering, meteorology, portfolio theory, option pricing, and risk management (see, e.g.,  Ephraim & Mark, 2012; Ephraim & Merhav, 2002; Ortobelli Lozza, Angelelli, & Bianchi, 2011 ). In the present article, the navigation behavior and the learning process of a user of a web-based tutoring system are jointly modeled as BMPs constrained by assumptions that are coherent with the concepts of competence-based KST. Such constraints are expressed as formal assumptions about the web-based system and about the nature of the learning process. Scenarios are considered where the observed process is the navigation of a learner through the pages of an ITS, whereas the latent learning process consists of transitions among the competence states.

The article is organized as follows. Section  2  provides a brief overview of KST and BMPs. Section  3  shows how probabilistic models based on BMPs can be used for describing learning processes in the context of KST. Section  4  presents the results of a simulation study aimed at investigating the goodness-of-recovery of the parameters of a model based on BMPs. Section  5  illustrates an application of the model to the analysis of real navigation data pertaining to Stat-Knowlab. Section  6  elaborates on what is presented in the previous sections and proposes some suggestions for future research.

A BMP consists of a pair of stochastic processes that happen to be  jointly Markov . Only one of them is observable, whereas the other one is latent. The two processes are not stochastically independent and neither of the two need be a Markov process. A thorough exposition of the theory of BMPs can be found, for instance, in  Ephraim and Mark (2012) , and the more general theoretical framework of continuous-time Markov chains is provided by  Todorovic (2012) . The family of BMPs is rather wide, and the different existing models can be classified according to assumptions about the nature of the states of the process (finite, countable infinite or uncountable) and about time (discrete vs. continuous). A rather famous class in the family is, for instance, that of the hidden Markov models (finite states and discrete time). Other instances in the class are the different types of diffusion processes, like those arising from Brownian motion (continuous state space and continuous time). Here, the focus is on BMPs in the continuous time, with a finite state space. Unless differently stated, in the sequel we will refer to them as BMPs.

The study aims at evaluating if a BMM for continuous time can be used for modeling learning processes in the context of KST. A scenario is outlined in which learners navigate through the pages of an ITS meant for promoting the attainment of some skills. The goodness-of-recovery of model parameters is investigated under conditions that differ for the relation between the skills, the type of navigation through the ITS and noise on the item responses, and the sample size.

BMPs ( Ephraim & Mark, 2012 ) provide a valuable general framework for describing continuous time learning processes in the context of KST. In both a simulation study and an empirical application, BMPs have been used for modeling the navigation behavior of a learner through the pages of an ITS. The results of the simulation study showed that the parameter values are well-recovered by the estimation algorithm. The results of the empirical application showed that the proposed approach provides useful insight into the learning processes of students navigating an ITS like Stat-Knowlab. The following elaborates on what has been presented so far, and proposes some suggestions for future research directions.

The approach outlined in the present work is rather general and flexible in modeling learning processes with different assumptions. In the simulation study and in the empirical application, the assumption was made that the learners could only move forward along the learning paths (i.e., once learned, a skill could not be lost). Such an assumption is not always reasonable. Loss of the skills might occur, for instance, when their attainment is not consolidated, when large time intervals are considered (months, years), or when old basic skills are replaced with new advanced ones (see, e.g.,  Siegler, 2005 ). The aforementioned assumption can be easily released to allow for the loss of the skills.

The proposed approach is general enough to be applied to arbitrary competence structures. However, important advantages from a pedagogical perspective follow when well-graded competence spaces are considered ( Cosyn & Uzun, 2009; Falmagne & Doble, 2013 ), as in the simulation study and in the empirical application of the present work and in other works (e.g.,  Anselmi, Stefanutti, de Chiusole, & Robusto, 2017; de Chiusole & Stefanutti, 2013; de Chiusole, Stefanutti, Anselmi and Robusto, 2013; de Chiusole et al., 2020 ). Finally, well-gradedness plays a relevant role in those cases in which the attainment of new skills cannot be unequivocally established from the item responses (in this respect, see  Heller, Anselmi, Stefanutti, & Robusto, 2017; Heller et al., 2015; Heller, Stefanutti, Anselmi, & Robusto, 2016 ).  Stefanutti and de Chiusole (2017)  showed that, when the competence structure and the skill function (i.e., a function associating to each item the skills that are relevant for solving it) satisfy particular properties, the collection of minimal (by set inclusion) competence states delineating the same knowledge state is well-graded. Under this condition, a learner can make tangible progresses along the knowledge structure by learning one skill at a time.

It is worth noting that the proposed approach can be easily adapted for the use with ITSs having different architectures. For instance, in the ITS outlined in the simulation study the learners accessed the tests from the home pages, whereas in Stat-Knowlab they accessed the tests from the learning objects. Other architectures can be easily dealt with. Just to mention some of them, the learners could be required to take a first test when entering the ITS, to take intermediate tests straight after visiting specific learning objects, and to take a final test when exiting the ITS. The BMPs described in the present work can be used to model learning that occurs when navigating other KST-based ITSs (e.g., ALEKS, RATH, or APeLS). There is another class of BMPs that consists of continuous-time BMPs with a continuous state space ( Ephraim & Mark, 2012 ). These processes could be used to model learning in ITSs which are based on frameworks that are profoundly different from that covered in the present work, such as item response theory.

In the simulation study and in the empirical application, conditions were considered in which there were only a few skills, competence states, and learning objects. Even in these small examples, the number of parameters to be estimated was considerable. Concerning the simulation study, for instance, there were 21 transition rates in the most restrictive condition where skill  
                         a 
                       was a prerequisite of skill  
                         b 
                      , navigation through the ITS was guided, and there was no noise on the item responses. If follows that large data sets might be required to reach valid and reliable parameter estimates even in simple scenarios. We note in passing that large data sets are not uncommon because the ITSs are usually made available to the learners on a continuous basis so that new data are collected every time new or old learners access them. At least two pathways for future research may prove fruitful. With respect to the first, the transition rates that are not informative about possible changes in the latent competence state (e.g., the rate pertaining to the transition from the home page to a test page, or the rate pertaining to the transition from the home page to the exit page) could be considered as nuisance parameters, and the attention could be directed towards obtaining valid estimates for those transition rates that pertain to changes in the competence state. With respect to the second pathway, equality constraints can be imposed on the estimation of specific transition rates so as to reduce the number of free parameters (e.g., a constant transition rate could be considered for all cases in which a skill  
                         s 
                       is learned). The validity of these constraints must be tested empirically.

The goodness-of-fit of the BMMs has not been tested in the present work. Well-known model selection criteria, like theAkaike information criterion (AIC) or the Bayesian information criterion (BIC), can be used to compare BMMs which are based on different assumptions about the learning process. Future works could be devoted to investigate which goodness-of-fit tests are suitable for empirically testing the different assumptions of these types of models.

In the present work, the variant of the EM algorithm described by  Ephraim and Mark (2012)  was used to compute maximum likelihood estimates of the parameters of the BMPs. Such a variant assumes that the initial probabilities of the BMP-states are known. Adapting the EM algorithm to compute maximum likelihood estimates of these initial probabilities could be the subject of future works.

Model identifiability is crucial to interpretation of parameter estimates. Drawing upon the results on the identifiability of BMMs that are available in the literature (e.g.,  Buchholz, Kriege, & Felko, 2014; Larget, 1998; Ryden, 1996 ), future works could be devoted to investigate the identifiability of particular BMMs which are meant to model the navigation behavior of learners through the pages of an ITS.
