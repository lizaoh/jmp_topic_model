JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 25, 163-174 (1982)  Maximum Likelihood Estimation for Luce's Choice Model  WIM L. J. VAN PUTTEN  University of Leiden  A terminology for general choice models based on the choice axiom is given. It applies to all kinds of choice experiments,  such as confusion choice experiments, paired comparisons, triadic comparisons, directional rankings, scores on binary test  items, and others. Maximum likelihood estimation for such general choice models is considered. Conditions for the uniqueness  of maximum likelihood estimates are given, and it is shown that the estimates can be derived by iterative proportional  fitting. This offers the opportunity of a general test of the choice axiom for all kinds of choice experiments using the  likelihood ratio. The estimation and testing procedure is applied to data from a form recognition experiment, reported by W.  A. Wagenaar (Nederlands Tijdschrift voor de Psychologie, 1968, 23, 96-108).  This paper is concerned with maximum likelihood estimation of the parameters in choice models based on Luce's choice axiom  (CA; see Luce, 1959). Objections against the CA or the empirical validity of the CA will not be considered here. See Luce  (1977) for a discussion of these topics and for more references. In the first part of the paper conditions for the existence  and uniqueness of the maximum likelihood (ML) estimates are given. The second part is devoted to an estimation procedure based  on the principle of iterative proportional fitting (IPF). The procedure is a generalization of Ford's (1957) algorithm for  binary comparisons. The estimation procedure is very simple and may be applied to data from any "pick 1 from n" type of choice  experiment. This offers the opportunity of a simple direct statistical test of the CA with the likelihood ratio in all kinds  of choice experiments. This way of testing the CA is more general than the likelihood ratio test of equality of two transition  matrices, used by Morgan (1974), to test the CA for confusion choice experiments. The third part of the paper is devoted to  the application of the estimation procedure and the likelihood ratio test to data from a form recognition experiment. The stimulus set consisted of four dot patterns.  Pattern 1 consisted of dots equally spaced on an equilateral triangle; in pattern 4 the dots were equally spaced on a circle. At this point it is interesting to compare the test of the CA suggested here with  Morgan's (1974) testing procedure. Morgan described a test of the CA only for confusion choice experiments. The underlying  idea, however, applies to more general choice experiments as well and may be described in our terminology as follows. Consider  a choice experiment in which one of the choice sets is the master set V itself. The CA implies that, for each choice set w and  each object i E w, piw=pilyi,wpj. In words and in terms of frequencies this means that the expected relative frequencies of  choices of objects from choice set w are equal to the expected relative frequencies of choices of the same objects from master  set V. For each choice set w ~ V this results in a different hypothesis. Morgan's suggestion is to test each of these  hypotheses with the likelihood ratio test.  172 WIM L. J. DISCUSSION  It has been shown in this paper that ML estimation of Luce's choice model and testing of the CA can be done very easily for  all kinds of choice experiments. One restriction may be that the number of objects and the number of choice sets should not be  too large. Such is the case for most confusion choice experiments, paired comparisons and triadic comparisons. Computational  problems may arise if the data consist of directional rankings. J. VAN PUTTEN  The linear rate of convergence of the IPF algorithm is too slow for large problems. For example, comparison of the rates of  convergence of the IPF algorithm (suitably adapted) and the algorithm of Wright, Mead, and Bell (1979) for unconditional  maximum likelihood estimation of the item and subject parameters in the Rasch model shows that the IPF algorithm is  considerably slower. For problems of moderate size, however, this is not very important. For example, the number of iterations  for each of the estimations in the previous sections varied between four and seven. The simple IPF algorithm is quite  satisfactory for small problems. For large problems, however, it may be more appropriate to use a general purpose fitting  routine such as STEPIT (Chandler, 1969) or to develop a special purpose fitting routine based on a modified Newton method.  In a strictly statistical sense maximum likelihood estimation is appropriate only if the underlying probabilistic model is  true. In most cases, however, the CA is not valid; it is too simple a model to describe the choice behavior of individuals or  groups correctly. Nevertheless, even in such cases the CA and maximum "likelihood" estimation may still be used as reasonable  instruments, for ranking and scaling of objects.
