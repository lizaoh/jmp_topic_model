The concepts of storage and retrieval are essential for an understanding of memory and the changes that occur in the memory retention rate. For example, if there is a decrease in the rate of memory retention, as measured by a significant decline in the proportion of correctly recalled items, is that change due (a) to a change in memory storage, (b) to a change in the retrieval success rate, or (c) to a combination of changes in both storage and retrieval? It is difficult to see how progress can be achieved in the development and assessment of a unified theory of memory if this question is not tackled. Initially some memory researchers (e.g.,  Belmont & Butterfield, 1969; Buschke, 1974 ) attempted to utilize strictly an experimental approach to learn about storage and retrieval processes, i.e., these investigators attempted to link some independent variables with the fundamental memory processes of storage and retrieval. However, those claims were unconvincing and a number of theorists have argued that a strictly experimental analysis cannot measure latent memory processes. For example,  Brainerd (1984)  argued that any storage/retrieval measurement approach will be futile without the delineation of a measurement scale by means of a mathematical model. Brainerd stressed that storage and retrieval are not directly observable and are not linked to any single behavioral recording. Yet these latent memory processes can be measured by utilizing mathematical models that characterize how the various independent variables, study conditions, and conditions of test are linked to psychological processes.  Chechile and Roder (1998)  have also criticized a strictly experimental approach for learning about fundamental memory processes and argued for the necessity of model-based measurement.

A number of models have been developed for measuring storage and retrieval processes. All of these models are linked with rather specific experimental tasks and testing procedures.  Batchelder and Riefer (1999)  reviewed many of these models within the context of a larger review of multinomial processing tree (MPT) models in psychological research. One such storage/retrieval model is the  Batchelder and Riefer (1980)  MPT model that is coupled to a free recall, pair clustering task.  Riefer and Rouder (1992)  and  Rouder and Batchelder (1998)  also developed MPT models for storage/retrieval measurement that was tied to a testing procedure consisting of a combination of free and cued recall.  Chechile (1998)  and  Chechile and Soraci (1999)  generated MPT models for storage/retrieval measurement linked to a testing procedure that has an initial free recall test that is followed by a series of forced-choice recognition tests.

One of the first set of MPT models for storage/retrieval measurement was developed by  Chechile and Meyer (1976) . These models were designed for a novel testing task that featured a random mixture of recall tests along with yes/no recognition tests that were followed by confidence ratings. The Chechile and Meyer task has been used in a wide range of memory studies. The task was used by Chechile (1987, Exp. 5),  Butler and Chechile (1976) , and Chechile and Butler (1976) to explore proactive interference in short-term memory.  Chechile and Roder (1998) ,  Gutowski and Chechile (1987) ,  Larner (1979) , and Gerrein and Chechile (1977a) used the task to examine differences in clinical groups. The task has also been used to study the acoustic similarity effect ( Chechile, 1977b ), the effects of serial position ( Skoff & Chechile, 1977 ), retroactive interference effects ( Chechile & Ehrensbeck, 1983 ; Chechile, 1987), memory development ( Chechile & Richman, 1982 ;  Chechile, Richman, Topinka, & Ehrensbeck, 1981 ), and the generation effect ( Wakeford, 2002 , Exp. 3). Moreover,  Chechile (1978, 2002)  demonstrated how the data from this task can be analyzed in terms of signal-detection models rather than MPT models. Consequently, the Chechile and Meyer task is a rich experimental context that supports the analysis of memory data from a number of competing theoretical frameworks. However, it will be argued here that there are problems with the  Chechile and Meyer (1976)  models.

In this paper, the original  Chechile and Meyer (1976)  MPT models will be reviewed in  Section 2 . Those models are critiqued in  Section 3 . In  Section 4 , a new model (the 6P model) is developed to circumvent the problems raised in the critique. It will be shown in  Section 5  that the 6P model is identifiable. In  Section 6 , the maximum likelihood estimates (MLE) for the parameters of the 6P model are provided. In  Section 7 , an alternative method for fitting the model parameters for multinomial data, which is called population parameter mapping (PPM) (see  Chechile, 1998 ), is applied to the 6P model. Although the parameters of the 6P model can be estimated by either MLE or PPM methods, the relative accuracy of these estimates are not equivalent. In  Section 8 , the relative accuracy is explored for experiments of various sample sizes ( n ). It will be demonstrated via Monte Carlo studies that the two methods converge for large  n  experiments, but for smaller  n  experiments, the PPM estimate has a smaller absolute value error for the storage parameter. In  Section 9 , a statistical test is described for evaluating a key assumption of the 6P model. Two seven-parameter models (7A and 7B) are also developed in  Section 9  for use if the 6P model fails to satisfy the test. Both MLE and PPM estimates are developed for the two seven-parameter models. Also, Monte Carlo studies are conducted that assess the accuracy (as a function of  n ) of the PPM method to select correctly between the two seven-parameter models. In  Section 10 , the new models are used to reanalyze some existing data in order to illustrate the differences between the old and new models and to provide validation evidence for the new models. Finally, an overall discussion of the new models is provided in  Section 11 .

The Chechile and Meyer task is characterized by the testing method where subjects are given a random mixture of recall, old, and foil recognition trials. The purpose of the random intermixing of the test trials types is to justify a later modeling assumption that the same value of storage is applicable on both recall and recognition trials. This assumption is reasonable provided that the participants do not know how they will be tested until the test probe is presented. Clearly, if the participants know in advance the type of test probe that will be used for a particular target, then they might utilize different encoding and memory retention strategies for recall and recognition trials.  Hall, Grossman, and Elwood (1976) ,  Balota and Neely (1980)  and  Neely and Balota (1981)  did find that participants are more successful on a recognition test if they anticipate that the test will be a recall test. The random intermixing of the test probes over the testing session circumvents differential encoding and retention strategies for the recall and recognition trial. Moreover, on the recognition test trials, the participants are required to provide a three-point confidence rating. Participants are instructed to use a “3” rating if they are certain of their yes/no recognition response, a “2” rating if their response is an “educated guess”, and a “1” rating if their response is a pure guess. The purpose of the confidence ratings is to provide information about guessing processes. Experimental evidence supports the utilization of the three-point confidence rating. For example, in  Chechile and Meyer (1976, Exp. 1)  the conditional probabilities for a correct response increased with the confidence rating. For old recognition at the longest retention interval, the conditional probabilities are

The testing protocols of the Chechile and Meyer task can be employed in a number of different designs for memory studies.  Chechile (1973) ,  Chechile and Meyer (1976) ,  Chechile and Butler (1975) ,  Butler and Chechile (1976) ,  Gerrein and Chechile (1977) ,  Larner (1979) , and Chechile (1977, 1987) used the Chechile and Meyer testing method in studies of short-term memory that assessed the retention of single targets via the Brown–Peterson experimental paradigm.  Skoff and Chechile (1977) ,  Chechile et al. (1981) , and  Chechile and Richman (1982)  used the Chechile and Meyer test procedure in serial probe experiments.  Chechile and Ehrensbeck (1983)  assessed paired-associate learning and interference by using the Chechile and Meyer task.  Gutowski and Chechile (1987)  and  Wakeford (2002)  used the Chechile and Meyer test protocols in a continuous memory experiment. Finally,  Chechile, Anderson, Krafczek, and Coley (1996, Exp. 2)  and  Krangel (2002)  used only the recognition part of the test procedure for list learning experiments.

The definitions of storage and retrieval measures come from an analysis of the recall trials. In  Fig. 1

It should be pointed out that the aforementioned experiments that utilized the Chechile and Meyer task have used noninformative cues for the recall test trials, e.g., a test cue of a “X” or the word “recall.” The purpose for such test cues is to justify a modeling assumption that there is a trivially low chance of correctly guessing the target on a recall test. From  Fig. 1  process tree, there is only one way to be correct on a recall test, i.e., the target must be both stored and retrieved. The experimental implementation of a recall test requires the design of the target and test probe. In a poorly designed recall test, it might be possible for the participate to guess the target even without a memory representation. Suppose that a target were a high associate of a cue word, e.g., FAST–SLOW. In such cases the presentation of the first member of the pair is not a suitable recall probe because the participant could be correct even if they had no explicit representation of the pair based on the learning that occurred in the experiment. Consequently, Chechile and associates took the precaution to avoid memory targets that could be guessed. Given a well-designed recall test, the memory target can only be correctly recalled if there is sufficient storage of the item at the time of test and if there is successful retrieval of that representation. Insufficiently stored items, i.e., targets with no memory representation at the time of test or just a memory fragment, will result in incorrect recall. Note that Chechile and associates required exact reproduction of the target in order to be scored a correct recall. Partial recall and an omission were both scored as incorrect recall. The  Chechile and Meyer (1976)  models for recall and recognition appear to be inconsistent with the finding of recognition failures of recallable words. This effect has been explored by Tulving and associates ( Flexser & Tulving, 1978 ;  Tulving, 1974 ;  Tulving & Thomson, 1973 ;  Tulving & Watkins, 1977 ;  Wiseman & Tulving, 1976 ). In these studies, subjects are provided with weakly associated target pairs (A–B). The condition that resulted in a lower recognition rate is the recognition testing of the B items in the absence of the A items. The encoding task is to learn the A–B pairs. Yet, the recognition test is not a yes/no recognition test of the A–B pairs versus nonpresented control pairs. Instead, the recognition context is changed from the encoding context, i.e., the A item is removed. The A context, however, is provided in the recall test. Furthermore, the B items have a preexperimental association to the A items. When the subject already has an association between A and B, it is not clear that the reporting of the B item is the recalling of a memory acquired from the study phase or simply a guess. Thus, both the recall and recognition test procedures used by Tulving and associates are departures from the test procedures that were carefully constructed so as to learn about the underlying storage and retrieval processes. Consequently, the recognition failure of recallable words effect is considered here as a consequence of these altered test procedures and is not relevant to the Chechile and Meyer testing procedures.

The  Chechile and Meyer (1976)  models have had some success. From the outset, those models have found changes in both storage and retrieval processes. Independent variables have been identified that selectively influenced either storage or retrieval. For example, Chechile and Meyer (1977b) found that the degree of intra-item acoustic similarity only influenced the storage measure; whereas  Chechile and Meyer (1976, Exp. 2)  found that increased search time at test only influenced the retrieval parameter. Consequently, there is evidence that the two multiplicative components of the correct recall proportion correspond to separate processes. Moreover, the search time study supports the labeling of the  
                         
                            
                               θ 
                            
                            
                               r 
                            
                         
                       parameter as the retrieval component of the correct recall proportion. Despite this supportive evidence, there are several reasons for developing revised models for the recognition trials of the Chechile and Meyer task.

Although four models were developed in the  Chechile and Meyer (1976)  paper, only Models 3 and 4 have been employed in subsequent experimental work. These two models have a very large number of parameters—12 parameters in the case of Model 3 and 13 parameters for Model 4. The total degrees of freedom for the three types of trials is 11, i.e., 1 degree of freedom from the recall binomial, 5 degrees of freedom from the old recognition multinomial and 5 degrees of freedom from the foil recognition multinomial. Clearly these models are not identifiable in the usual sense, and the parameters cannot be estimated via classical inferential methods such as maximum likelihood estimation (MLE).  Chechile and Meyer (1976)  and  Chechile (1977a)  showed, however, that it was possible to obtain model identification and to estimate the parameters by means of a Bayesian analysis. Without data from recognition test trials, the posterior probability distribution given only recall data is ambiguous, i.e., the same distribution for both the storage and retrieval parameters. The model for recall has two parameters, and there is only one degree of freedom. However, there are recognition memory data, and one can consider a two-step process in the Bayesian analysis. The first step is to obtain a storage probability distribution that is based on the recognition memory data prior to considering the recall data. There is no such distribution for the retrieval parameter because that parameter is not involved in the recognition trials. Consequently, the “final” posterior distributions after examining the recall data are different for the storage and retrieval parameters. The differential pre-recall prior distributions disambiguate the storage and retrieval measurement. See  Chechile and Meyer (1976)  and  Chechile (1977a)  for more details about the identification of Models 3 and 4. Thus, with Bayesian statistics it was possible to estimate the model parameters and to bypass the necessity of making questionable simplifying assumptions. For example, Models 1 and 2 in the Chechile and Meyer paper are simplified models that were classically identifiable; however, it was not clear which one of these models was superior. Hence, the more complex models, i.e., Models 3 and 4, were utilized in subsequent experimental research. Yet there are major costs associated with the use of complex models that are not classically identifiable. For Bayesian analyses with more parameters than degrees of freedom,  Chechile (1977a)  pointed out that the variance of posterior distribution does not approach 0 as the sample size increases to infinity, i.e., the estimates are not consistent. Furthermore,  Chechile (1998)  has developed a new Bayesian method for estimating the latent model parameters for multinomial data—the method is called population-parameter mapping (PPM). The PPM method does not require the evaluation of numerical integrals. Also, PPM estimation has a valuable probability estimate of the coherence of model itself. PPM estimation thus has advantages over a standard Bayesian estimation for the parameters of a multinomial process-tree model.However, PPM estimation cannot be done for models that have more parameters than degrees of freedom. Consequently, there is a need to revise the models for the Chechile and Meyer task in order to obtain consistent estimates that enable both MLE and PPM estimates.

Although it is advantageous to have a more simple process-tree model for the Chechile and Meyer task in order to estimate the model parameters from a variety of statistical perspectives, there is an even more compelling psychological problem with all four of the models in the  Chechile and Meyer (1976)  paper. The difficulty pertains to the model of foil recognition trials (see  Fig. 3 ). That tree is very similar to the old recognition tree ( Fig. 2 ). The model for old recognition is very sound. It is assumed in old recognition that the participant will respond correctly and have highest confidence if the target is stored sufficiently. It is reasonable to assume that the re-presentation of the target in the same context as in the original learning will function as an excellent retrieval cue, i.e., it will reactivate a sufficiently stored encoding if it exists. The old recognition probe is presumed to initiate a bottom-up, data-driven re-elicitation of a sufficiently stored target when it exists. Note that this conceptualization of old recognition differs from a view of recognition as involving a top-down search of the entire memory system, e.g., the  Forster and Bednall (1976)  model of word recognition. Given the scanning rates observed in the literature for visual search and memory search (approximately 40 ms per item, viz.  Sternberg, 1966 ), the speed of old recognition is many orders of magnitude faster than would be expected if there were a search of the entire memory system. Consequently, even theorists who initially advocated a strict top-down search of the memory system, abandoned that notion as untenable. For example,  Forster (1976)  and  Becker (1980)  restricted the role of search in old recognition to the scanning of a short-list of candidates items that were generated directly from the perceptual encoding of the probe. Consequently, the assumption of an error-free, high confident response in old recognition when the target is sufficiently stored is consistent with the prevailing conceptualization of old recognition. The view is also consistent with the global memory models (e.g.,  Estes, 1994 ) that regard recognition as driven by a similarity-based activation. Hence the  Fig. 2  tree is reasonable.

Although the above models for recall and old recognition trials are reasonable and consistent with existing memory data, there is a serious difficulty with the foil recognition model. It is questionable to assume direct access for foil recognition as portrayed in  Fig. 3 . A foil item is dissimilar to the target, so it is not likely to reactivate the target encoding. Yet the  Chechile and Meyer (1976)  foil recognition models assumed that the rate of knowledge-based foil recognition is the same as in old recognition. This assumption is problematic and needs to be revised. Nevertheless, the subject may sometimes be able to recover some component of the target during a foil recognition trial. For PPM inference, the following definitions from  Chechile (1998)  are essential:  
                         Definition 3 
                         
                            I Mapping : An I mapping (identifiable mapping) is a set of order pairs of vectors such that no two pairs have the same domain vector. 
                      
                      
                         Definition 4 
                         
                            UI Mapping : A UI mapping (unique identifiable mapping) is a set of order pairs of vectors such that no two pairs have the same domain vector or the same range vector.

I Mapping : An I mapping (identifiable mapping) is a set of order pairs of vectors such that no two pairs have the same domain vector.

UI Mapping : A UI mapping (unique identifiable mapping) is a set of order pairs of vectors such that no two pairs have the same domain vector or the same range vector.

Note that both I and UI mapping are proper functions that take a point in the domain vector space to a single point in the range vector space and thereby avoid the ambiguity of a one-to-many mapping. Nevertheless, it is possible that an I mapping has more than one point in the domain space that maps to the same range space point, i.e., it is possible for an I mapping to be many-to-one. However, for UI mappings each point in the domain space is mapped to a different point in the range space. For many writers a UI mapping is simply called one-to-one. Yet  Chechile (1998)  introduced the term UI mapping to deal with a type of one-to-one mapping that does not satisfy the “onto” property where all the points in the range space have a mapping from the domain space. Hence, the one-to-one mapping (with the “onto” property) satisfies more stringent requirements than the UI mapping, which in turn satisfies more stringent requirements than an I mapping. The mapping operation itself is denoted by the symbol “ 
                         ⟶ 
                      .”

MLE values for the parameters of the 6P model can be obtained by using existing general purpose software for MPT models, e.g., the GPT.EXE software discussed by  Hu and Phillips (1999) . However, it is advantageous to also have a direct solution for the 6P MLE equations in order to support later Monte Carlo simulations designed to compare two different systems for fitting the model, i.e., specific MLE software for the 6P model can be embedded within other Monte Carlo software.

The first step for obtaining the 6P MLE values is to substitute the RHS expressions from ( 2 )–( 11 ) into the appropriate terms of ( 1 ) in order to obtain the likelihood function in terms of the 6P model parameters. The maximization of the likelihood itself can be determined via a search of all parameter value combinations. However, this exhaustive search approach is computationally costly. Alternatively, the partial derivatives of the likelihood function can be found with respect to all six of the model parameters. These derivatives are set to zero in order to determine the MLE values. For the 6P model, this second approach results in a set of equations that do not have a closed form solution; although the equations can be solved numerically. However, a hybrid approach, i.e., a search along a single dimension with constraints imposed from setting the partial derivatives of  L  to zero for five of the model parameters, results in a highly efficient procedure for maximizing the likelihood function. This hybrid approach was selected for obtaining the MLE values.

The method of maximum likelihood estimation was introduced by R.A. Fisher and has been widely employed because of its reputed optimal properties. However,  Le Cam (1986)  observed that “In view of Fisher's vast influence, it is perhaps not surprising that the presumed superiority of the method is still for many an article of faith promoted with religious fervor. Chechile and Meyer (1976)  introduced a novel experimental task and also provided MPT models for that task. The models were designed to measure underlying storage and retrieval components of the correct recall rate. The models assumed whenever the memory target was sufficiently stored, the subject would be able to reject a foil and have high confidence in the response. This assumption is problematic on theoretical grounds. The foil is not likely to directly activate a stored target representation. It is also possible that a subject might be able to reject a foil on the basis of fragmentary information. Both of these considerations indicate that the  Chechile and Meyer (1976)  modeling of foil recognition was flawed. Hence, a new set of MPT models for the Chechile and Meyer experimental task is provided. For these models, a new  
                         
                            
                               θ 
                            
                            
                               k 
                            
                         
                       parameter is introduced to represent the probability of enough target knowledge during foil recognition testing to reject the foil with high confidence. A longer retention interval in the Brown–Peterson experimental paradigm results in a lower rate for correct recall, but that effect is shown in  Section 10.1  to be due to only a decline in the storage parameter (

However, an increase in the time deadline at test in the Brown–Peterson paradigm results in a higher recall rate, but that effect is only due to an increase in the retrieval component of correct recall (see  Section 10.2 ). These two results indicate (a) that the correct recall rate has been factored in two different probabilities that reflect different psychological processes, and (b) the labeling of the retrieval parameter is justifiable in light of the deadline experiment.  Gerrein (1976)  also showed that highly similar foils to targets resulted in an increase in the false alarm rate. A valid measure for target storage should not be influenced by the properties of nonpresented items. In order to provide for identifiable models in this paper, some assumptions were made about the confidence rating probabilities. For model 6P the same parameter ( 
                         
                            
                               θ 
                            
                            
                               1 
                            
                         
                      ) is used for the probability to give a high confidence rating in all cases of recognition guessing. This model is treated as the default model and a chi-squared test is provided to assess the suitability of the model assumption for any given experimental condition. If the results indicate that this assumption is not warranted, then two seven-parameter models are reasonable alternatives (i.e., models 7A and 7B). These models are also identifiable and can be estimated with either MLE or PPM approaches. Models 3 and 4 from  Chechile and Meyer (1976)  were not identifiable at the level of the likelihood function and could not be fit via the MLE approach; although those earlier models were identifiable from a Bayesian framework. Consequently, the new set of models for the Chechile and Meyer task have an important technical advantage because the parameters can be estimated from different statistical frameworks. Widely available software tools, such as GPT.EXE (see  Hu & Phillips, 1999 ), can be used to obtain MLE fits for the new set of models. Alternatively, specific MLE algorithms are provided here for each of the new models.

The new models can also be estimated by utilizing the PPM method developed by  Chechile (1998) . In this paper, the PPM method was shown to have at least four advantageous properties. First, Monte Carlo simulations demonstrated generally smaller errors for the PPM estimates in comparison to the error found with the MLE. Although MLE and PPM errors converged for the case where

                         P

Moreover, the accuracy of the model selection decision between the seven parameter models was even excellent when the sample size was relatively small. Fourth, PPM, as a variant of a Bayesian analysis, results in posterior probability distributions for each of the model parameter. Consequently, hypotheses about the model parameters can be directly assessed via probability statements.

Finally, the Chechile and Meyer task provides a rich set of memory data that can be analyzed by a wide variety of models. Besides the original  Chechile and Meyer (1976)  MPT models and the three new MPT models, the data from this task can also be examined by means of signal detection theory (see  Chechile, 1978 ;  Chechile, 2002 ). Moreover, the task has been employed to examine a diverse set of issues in the memory literature (see  Sections 1  and  2 ). The MPT models developed in this paper correct a flaw in the earlier MPT models of foil recognition trials. It remains to be seen if the new models substantially change the conclusions reached in the published papers that employed the older MPT models.
