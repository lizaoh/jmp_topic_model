HAIGHT  University of California, Los Angeles, California  Four word association tables recently fitted to the Yule distribution by W. J. Horvath are analyzed more fully by (a)  comparing them with two other theoretical alternatives, (b) illustrating various methods for parameter estimation, (c)  illustrating the usefulness of the Smirnov goodness-of-fit test, and (d) using a conjecture of Zipf to derive a stochastic  model leading to the zeta distribution.  From the statistical point of view, word association data, such as that given by Horvath (1963), presents certain very  difficult features: the lack of any convincing model for the phenomenon, the curious shape, and in particular the very long  tails, of the distributions, the variability between different stimulus words and the need to characterize this variability,  and finally the variability between subject populations. Not only is the problem difficult-it is also extremely interesting.  The following list, partly taken from Simon (1955) shows the variety of other phenomena which have in common with word lists a  fundamental characteristic: rank listing (the Zipf approach) and frequency listing (the Yule approach).  1. Words in a given book  2. Scientists by number of papers published 3. Automobiles by make or by color  4. States by number of counties they have 5. Elements by their abundance on earth 6. Cities by size  7. Individuals by income  8. Congressional speeches by length 9. Genera by number of species 10. Countries by area  11. Surnames by frequency  There are, of course, certain classical statistical situations bordering on some of this data. The distribution of size of  collections or size of objects (like grains of sand) is usually lognormal, provided the sizes are continuous variables.  Moreover, any  217  sample can be ranked, and the study of such ordered data is a respectable branch of statistics. However, considerations such  as these do not entirely resolve the difficulties in terms of traditional theory. Cities listed by size cannot be considered  an ordered sample unless the sample is supposed to be the whole population. The frequency of the elements can hardly be  considered a sample at all, and this case appears more deterministic than stochastic. Indeed many of the items listed above  should be regarded as only quasi-stochastic in nature. What they do have in common is an intrinsic qualitative ranking. The  color of automobiles is a purely qualitative characteristic, and is quite independent of sampling.  From the point of view of parameter estimation and comparison of theory with observation, it makes very little difference  whether the underlying causes are assumed to be deterministic or stochastic. The sequence of values pm which define a  probability distribution can equally well describe nonprobabilistic phenomena arranged into appropriate categories. As an  example of this, consider the newly devised area code system for long distance telephone calls. Omitting Alaska and Hawaii,  and treating Prince Edward Island as part of Nova Scotia, the whole of the United States and Canada has been divided into 57  "Area Codes." These are arranged so that no area code contains part of two states of provinces. Therefore we can count the  number of area codes per state or per province, and form a tabulation, similar to a statistical distribution, of the number of  states or provinces having n area codes. THE HARMONIC DISTRIBUTION  In looking for an improved model for word association data, we will go back to the "Zipf conjecture" that, in the ranked  tabulation, the relative frequency of the neh most frequent reply is proportional to n- where a is a constant. To introduce  the theory, we begin by consideration of the case a = 1, so that the ranked data form a harmonic series. According to Zipf  (cf. Simon, 1955, p. 437), values of o nearly unity are obtained in listings of city sizes. Rather than dealing with  approximate results; i.e., empirical data, we consider the theoretical model, i.e., a table of reciprocals of the integers. If  we form a tabulation with the first category consisting of the number of integers whose reciprocals are closest to one, the  second category those integers whose reciprocals are closest to two, etc., we find that there are two values, namely one and  two,' in the first category and none in any other. This trivial array can be made more meaningful if we multiply the  reciprocals by a constant Z before beginning the tabula; tion. Table 10 shows the result for Z = 1, 5, 10, 20, 30, 100, and  1000. Since the total number of entries in every  column of Table 10 is 2Z, the proportion of weight in the first category is, for any Z of respectable size, practically 3. In  other words, the harmonic distribution is, taking the first few dozen categories, practically speaking not a one-parameter  family of distributions, but a single distribution, corresponding to Z ---- oo.
