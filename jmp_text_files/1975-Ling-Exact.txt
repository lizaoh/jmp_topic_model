LING  University of Chicago, Chicago, Illinois 60637  Recently, several authors have proposed the use of random graph theory to evaluate the adequacy of cluster analysis results.  One such statistic is the minimum number of lines (edges) V needed to connect a random graph. Erd6s and Renyi derived  asymptotic distributions of V. Schultz and Hubert showed in a Monte Carlo study that the asymptotic approximations are poor  for small sample sizes n typically used in data analysis applications. In this paper the exact probability distribution of V  is given and the distributions for some values of n are tabulated and compared with existing Monte Carlo approximations.  INTRODUCTION  Recently, several authors have proposed the use of probability theory of random graphs to aid their assessment of the  legitimacy or adequacy of results obtained from cluster analysis or other techniques of discovering data structure from a  given similarity or dissimilarity matrix. For a review of cluster analysis techniques, see, for example, Cormack (1971). A  number of researchers have considered the use of one statistic, the minimum number of lines V needed to connect a random  graph, to evaluate the hypothesis of randomness in the data structure. Abraham (1965) attempted a theoretical derivation of  the probability distribution of V but concluded with erroneous results. Lingoes and Cooper (1971) gave an expression for the  approximate probability of the minimum number of deletions of lines (in a random graph G) to induce a disconnected graph.  Their formula is related to, but would yield very poor approximations for, the probability distributions of V. Fillenbaum and  Rapoport (1971, 1972) used an asymptotic formula of Erdos and Renyi (1959, 1960) in their applications involving small  samples. Schultz and Hubert (1973) demonstrated, via Monte Carlo simulation of sets of 1750 random graphs, that the asymptotic  results of Erdos and Renyi were indeed poor approximations for the distributions of V for small n, thus casting doubts on the  validity of some applications which made use of those approximations. As an illustration, there are 105! different  sequences for adding the lines to a graph of 15 points. Obviously, an exhaustive enumeration is almost impossible and thus, so  is obtaining an exact distribution for V.  It will be shown in a following section that it is in fact straightforward to derive, though rather cumbersome to tabulate,  the exact distribution of V under the random graph model considered. Comparisons of the exact distributions of V to those  empirical distributions tabulated by Schultz and Hubert will show that the empirical results are adequately accurate for the  purposes for which they were intended. However, the existence of exact results will alleviate the need for further Monte Carlo  studies of the type performed, which are very expensive, not to mention the question of numerical accuracy.  In this article, a tabulation of Pr(V < v) is given for n = 3(1) 16. These results correspond to the cases for which Monte  Carlo results are available in Schultz and Hubert (1973, 1974). The tabulated values were obtained from an eight-line computer  program coded in APL, which did not strive for computational efficiency, yet required only seconds of CPU for the cases  presented. However, such a computer program would not be practical for large values of n as both the computational complexity  and the CPU time required grow exponentially as n increases. At the time of writing this article, work has been in progress on  an efficient computing algorithm, coded in FORTRAN, which will provide distributions of V and other related statistics.  Extended tables of the distributions of V and FORTRAN listings are available from the author on request. Schultz and Hubert performed two sets of Monte Carlo simulations on the cumulative distributions of V. The first set  (including n = 9, 12, and 15), which was reported in their 1973 article, was based on 1750 samples. The other set (n = 6, 7,  8, 10, 11, 13, 14, and 16) was based on 1000 samples and was reported in (1974, Table 1). The accuracy of their approximations  is surprisingly good. In fact, the maximum discrepancies between their values and the exact values for the cases n = 9, 12,  and 15 are 0.013, 0.015, and 0.011, respectively (which are well within the bound of 0.04 they gave). For n = 6(1) 16, the  maximum discrepancy is 0.032 (in the case n = 16), while most of their other probabilities are accurate to at least two  decimal places.  Therefore, for the cases tabulated, the empirical approximations appear to be more than adequate for their use in data  analysis. One may even make the parallel, though somewhat juxtaposed, observation that the empirical results serve as a check  of the correctness of the theoretical distributions given by (2). However, the advantages of having Eq. (2) over the necessity  as well as the product of Monte Carlo simulations seem obvious.  98 ROBERT F.
