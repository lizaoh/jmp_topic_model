Derivations for the Chunking Model 

BENNET B. MURDOCK 

University of Toronto 

The chunking model is an extension of TODAM which uses multiple convolutions, n-grams (autoconvolutions of sums of item vectors), and chunks (sums of n-grams) to explain serial organization in a distributed memory. This paper presents the necessary derivations to compute signal-to-noise ratios in terms of the standard TODAM parameters. Quantitative applications of the model to a variety of memory tasks involving recognition, recall, and redintegration are now possible, and a number of implications of the analysis are briefly discussed. © 1993 Academic Press, Inc. 

The chunking model (Murdock, 1992b) is an extension of TODAM designed to account for chunking and serial organization. It will also account for many of the serial-order effects described by the chaining model of Lewandowsky and Murdock (1989). Starting with the basic TODAM framework of random vectors, convolution and correlation, and probabilistic encoding, the model uses multiple convolutions, n-grams, and chunks to provide some structure or organization in a distributed memory. This paper provides the necessary derivations so the model can be applied to experimental data. 

We start with a brief review of TODAM, and then summarize the chunking model. We next provide some preliminaries which are necessary for the derivations. We need to be able to compute d’ values as a function of the model parameters in order to make predictions or fit data on recognition and recall, and these require the mean or expectation and variance of the resemblance distributions. We then derive such expressions, first for the variance and then for the expectation. We conclude by listing a number of principles which follow from the analysis.

For paired associates (Murdock, 1982), item information and associative information are stored in a common memory vector whereas for seriation (Murdock, 1983) a chaining model is used so the current item and its association with the prior item are stored in a common memory vector. The item vectors in TODAM are assumed to be arbitrarily large zero-centered random vectors (vectors whose elements are random variables). The center N elements (N odd) are random variables and they are flanked by zeroes. After Anderson (1973), these random variables are assumed to be normally distributed with a mean of zero and a variance of P/N where P is the power (or square of the length) of the vector. Generally we assume P is 1.0 so the item vectors are normalized to 1.0.

Six parameters (N, a, p, 4, ¢, and p) are needed to fit the model to data. The first, N, is the dimensionality of the space or the number of non-zero elements in the item vectors, and N may vary with the type of material. The second, «, is a forgetting parameter and is probably the simplest possible way of generating forgetting in a distributed memory model. The third, p, is for probabilistic encoding; each element in an item vector is encoded with probability p or not encoded (set to 0) with probability 1—p. Without probabilistic encoding learning would not occur (Murdock & Lamon, 1988). The fourth, 4, is an attentional parameter; it provides a way of representing the division of attention where there are multiple sources of information to be simultaneously encoded. The fifth, ¢, is an habituation parameter (in Murdock, 1989.) It models a possible diminution in attention with repeated presentation. Habituation is probably the simplest form of learning (Kandel, 1976). The sixth and last, g, is a similarity parameter. This parameter specifies inter-item similarity (e.g, Murdock, 1989), but will not be included in the present paper. However, chunking is only part of a serial-order memory system that can recall, recognize, and redintegrate single or multiple items from strings of items that have been presented in sequence. Serial-order effects (Estes, 1972; Lewandowsky & Murdock, 1989) are probably the most complex effects in the human memory area, and the chunking model must explain them as well. 

These serial-order effects include forward and backward serial recail, probe recognition and recall, serial-position effects, similarity effects, item and order errors, performance in Brown-Peterson tasks, and recall of missing items to say nothing of serial learning, transfer, and retroactive and proactive inhibition. These topics have been extensively studied for the past 30-100 years. The chunking model is an attempt to encompass these effects as well as chunking in a distributed memory model. 

The basic concepts of the chunking model are multiple convolutions (a convolution of several item vectors), n-grams (the n-way autoconvolution of the sum of n item vectors), and chunks (the sum of x-grams 1 to n). Multiple convolutions, n-grams, and chunks endow TODAM with considerable flexibility and power. The present paper provides the derivations needed for quantitative tests of the model.

The model assumes that people form chunks to group sequentially presented information. DISCUSSION 

Strictly speaking, the results of this analysis only apply to the case where a fixed order of presentation is used, and experimenters usually randomize the presentation order. However, in a similar analysis of item and associative information (Murdock, 1992a), simulations showed that the results could be generalized to a randomized presentation order because the simulation results for the variance were quite close to the analytic results, . In one simulation with SO replications, with a randomized presentation order with N= 229, «=0.90, 8 =0.80, m=2, n=3, R=S=H=T=8, Py =0.9, 4, =08, and ¢,=0.7, the obtained variance was 0.00093 and the predicted variance (assuming a constant presentation order) was 0.0010 (¢=0,345). Consequently, these derivations may also apply to a randomized presentation order. 

There are a number of principles which emerge from this analysis.

(1) The variance of the product (or dot product) is the product of the variances for random variables, random vectors (single items), multiple convolutions, n-grams, and chunks.

(4) The variance of the dot product of an m-way and n-way autoconvolution is approximately mm! #! times the variance of the dot product of m- and n-way multiple convolutions. This increase in noise variance with chunk size could explain why optimum group size in short-term memory tasks is only about three or four (Ryan, 1969; Wickelgren, 1964). 

(5) As with single items, the variance of the dot product of multiple convolutions, n-grams, and chunks is inversely proportional to N, the dimensionality of the item vectors, but the expectation is independent of N. 

(6) Probabilistic encoding works the same way for multiple convolutions, n-grams, and chunks as it does for single items. Specifically, the within-repetition variance is proportional to p but the covariance between repetitions is proportional to p’.
