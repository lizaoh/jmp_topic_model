WOLFF  Psychology Department, Indiana University, Bloomington, Indiana 47401  A probabilistic, multidimensional Thurstonian model for treating same-different judgments was tested in an experiment in which  subjects made single-dimensional and two-dimensional same--different judgments. Strong asymmetries in the data required adding  temporal bias parameters to the model. The model, with bias parameters, acobnrtted for the single- and two-dimensional  judgments reasonably well, but the parameters *ere not invariant between the different solutions. The two-dimensional  judgments were more variable than expected on the basis of the single-dimensional judgments.  The goal of the present study is to give an adequate account of both one-dimensional judgments and multidimensional judgments,  using the same type of model in both cases and using, where appropriate, the same parameter values. The procedure adopted here  is to state the model at the outset in its simplest form and then to add appropriate bias parameters as needed. Whether or not  they are needed and whether or not the model adequately explains our data we believe are solely determined by the goodness of  fit of the model, and not by prior conceptions of what constitutes an attractive or meaningful multidimensional configuration.  The experiment reported here contained stimuli varying in two dimensions and a task requiring same-different judgments. The  model used at the outset to analyze the experimental data is a multidimensional extension of Thurstone's case 5 model and was  first stated by Hefner (1958). A single-dimensional version of this model applicable to same-different judgments was explored  previously in an experiment involving only single-dimensional same-different judgments (Zinnes & Kurtz, 1968). Of the five  models considered by Zinnes and Kurtz, this model (called model 1 in their study) fitted their data best. In this paper we are  interested in determining how suitable this model is for treating both single- and multidimensional same-different judgments.  PROBABILISTIC, METRIC MODELS  The Thurstonian model used here is both probabilistic and metric. It is probabilistic because it explicitly assumes that a  random element underlies the choice process on each trial and it is metric because it explicitly gives the function that  relates the observed choice proportions to the parameters of the model.  * We wish to thank Harold Lindman and Stephen Edgell for their helpful comments on an earlier draft of this paper. ISSN 0022-2496  SAME-DIFFERENT JUDGMENTS 31  The standard argument against this class of models can be summarized by Shepard's (1966) criticism that these models make  strong and therefore vulnerable assumptions. But that is the whole point. Strong models are more vulnerable than weak models  in the sense that they can be more easily tested and rejected if necessary. This means simply that tests of strong models tend  to have more power than those of weak models, and so it is easier to find out if one's model is wrong.  Another argument in favor of nonmetric models is that they give meaningful multidimensional pictures without having to make  strong assumptions. If one can get the same multidimensional picture using fewer or weaker assumptions, then it looks as  though one has been able to buy more for less. However, this position places emphasis on the meaningfulness of the  multidimensional configuration obtained, and not on whether any significant amount of data can be accounted for by the model  or on whether anything significant has been learned about the judgment process.  This point can be illustrated with the data here. Figure 6 gives the Kruskal solution (Kruskal, 1964) obtained from the data  to be described in more detail later. By comparing Fig. 6 to a plot of the same set of stimuli in terms of their physical  coordinates (Fig. 1), one can conclude perhaps that the configurations obtained are meaningful, because the nonmetric plot of  the stimuli in psychological space resembles in most respect the plot of the same stimuli in physical space. But this is all  that one can conclude and it is not a very exciting conclusion. It hardly justifies the experimental work and the numerical  analysis that are required to obtain it. There are, however, a number of more fundamental things that one can conclude from  these data by examining in detail their properties and by attempting to account for these properties by an explicit model. At  least that is what we attempt to do here. The results reported here we feel give substantially more insight into the subjects'  judgmental processes and into the limitations of a certain class of multidimensional models than can be obtained by routinely  grinding out a nonmetric solution.  The probabilistic aspect of the Thurstonian model used here involves a somewhat separate issue than does its metric aspects.  One could attempt to work with a metric, nonprobabilistic model, but such a model is bound to fail very quickly as soon as one  makes a serious attempt to apply the model to a given set of data. The difficulty is that, except for the most trivial kind of  data, or except for the crudest kind of response measure, the responses of subjects are not particularly consistent. It seems  necessary, therefore, to use a random element at some point in the model, if the model is to have any chance of survival. One  could, of course, object to the specific way in which the random element is introduced into the Thurstonian model, but this is  a wholly different matter. It involves the question of how well the model works and this is precisely the question we ask in  this study.  The real value othe probabilistic assumptions is that they make it possible to test the model in a relatively unambiguous  way. Questions of the goodness of fit of the model, the invariance of the parameters, or in the specific case of  multidimensional models, the number of dimensions required, can be answered by making use of standard statistical machinery.  Instead of working with measures of stress (Kruskal, 1964), that do not take into consideration the number of parameters  estimated and therefore have little meaning,  48o/i6/1-3  32 ZINNES AND WOLFF  it is possible to work with statistics whose distributional properties are well known. The same kinds of criticisms can be  made about a variety of other procedures that are commonly found in the literature: plotting or correlating observed versus  predicted values, calculating some dubious measure of "percent variance accounted for," or inspecting various curves for  elbows. In all cases better procedures exist that more accurately reflect the various questions of goodness of fit or  parameter invariance, provided one is not predisposed in favor of deterministic, nonmetric models.  THEORETICAL EQUATIONS  The basic assumptions are the Thurstonian ones generalized to r dimensions. Associated with each stimulus is an r-dimensional  random vector, the components of which are normally and independently distributed. In addition, we make the generalized case 5  assumptions, namely that the variances within and across dimensions are equal.  The assumption of equal variances across dimensions has no empirical significance at all. It simply sets the unit on each  axis. It does not, for example, require the stimuli to be equally discriminable on different dimensions. In fact, the  parameter values that are obtained later on clearly indicate that the experimental stimuli are substantially less  discriminable along one dimension than they are along the other.  The assumption of equal stimulus variances within each dimension is more serious. It could be questioned if something like  Weber's law is valid for the type of stimulus and the type of judgment used in the present experiment. There is, however, a  direct experimental test of this case 5 assumption, which is described at the end of this section. Estimation of Parameters and the Noncentral X2 Distribution  Maximum likelihood estimates of the unknown parameters were obtained using an iterative search strategy and parabolic  interpolation. In the present context at least, there appears to be very little that can be gained by constructing more  optimum maximum likelihood estimation procedures. (The details of this program are available elsewhere (Chandler, 1969).  The normal distribution functions in Eq. (7) were evaluated using the approximations given by Hastings (1955). Evaluating the  noncentral X2 distribution in Eq. (4) is another matter. Many approximations are available for the noncentral X2 distribution,  but there does not appear to have been a thorough study of their relative accuracies or speeds. Since speed is crucial for the  iterative process for estimating the parameter values to be feasible, we made some preliminary studies of four of the simpler  approximations (Abdel-Aty, 1954; Patnaik, 1949; Pearson, 1959; Sankaran, 1959) for two degrees of freedom.  The Abdel-Aty and Sankaran approximation (referred to by them as their first approximation) both make use of the modified cube  root transformation of Wilson and Hilferty to transform the noncentral X2 distribution to a normal distribution. The Sankaran  approximation differs from the Abdel-Aty approximation by its use of a variable exponent, its values lying between and ' ,  rather than the fixed cube root transformation used by Abdel-Aty.  The Pearson and Merrington approximation (given in Pearson, 1959) and the Patnaik approximation (his first approximation) both  attempt to transfer the noncentral X2 distribution into a central X2 by equating the first two moments of the distributions.  These two approximations differ by their treatment of small X2 values. The Patnaik approximation, for example, gives the  correct value when the noncentral variable is equal to zero, while the Pearson and Merrington approximation does not. Subjects  METHOD  There were 49 subjects, 17 in one group and 16 in the other two groups. The subjects were drawn from the elementary psychology  course at Indiana University. They were run individually or in pairs, depending on the scheduling possibilities.  Apparatus  The stimuli presented on each trial consisted of four lights in the shape of a diamond, selected from a 9 x 9 matrix of  lights. The distance between adjacent lights on the 9 x 9 matrix was 1.5 in. and the distance between the subject and the  light matrix was about 7 ft. The experimental room was darkened so the subjects were not able to see the unlighted light bulbs  in the 9 x 9 matrix.  There were eight distinct stimuli, differing with respect to the separation between  36 ZINNES AND WOLFF  their horizontal pair of lights or their vertical pair of lights or both. It was possible to vary the exact location of each stimulus on the 9 X 9 matrix by using different sets of four lights. In  fact, each stimulus was presented in one of four locations, these locations differing by either a horizontal displacement of  one light or a vertical displacement of one light or both. Thus trials containing "identical" pairs of stimuli did not  necessarily consist of two stimuli located in the same place on the 9 x 9 matrix of lights. On each trial and in all groups,  two stimuli were presented successively to the subject. Each stimulus was exposed for 0.5 sec. The interstimulus time on each  trial was 1.25 sec and the intertrial time was 2.25 sec. The intertrial time refers to the time between the subject's response  and the onset of the first stimulus of the following trial. On each trial the subject was allowed to wait as long as he wished  before making his response.  Procedure  There were three experimental groups. The Group H subjects were asked to determine whether the pair of stimuli presented  successively on a given trial had the same or different widths. They were asked to ignore the vertical pair of lights and to  base their judgment solely on the distance between the horizontal pair. The Group V subjects were asked to make their  judgments of same or different with respect to the vertical pair of lights and to ignore the horizontal pair. The Group HV  subjects were asked to consider both the horizontal and the vertical distances and to make their judgments of same or different  6  SAME-DIFFERENT JUDGMENTS 37  on the basis of both of these. A same response by the subjects in this group indicates that the two pair of stimuli presented  appeared to be identical in both their horizontal and their vertical distances.  The subjects were told that they would receive one point on each correct trial and lose one point on each incorrect trial.  They were instructed to try to obtain as many points as possible. The subjects received feedback on each trial. Immediately  following each response, a green or red window, containing the number 1 and located immediately adjacent to the response  buttons, was illuminated and remained illuminated as long as the subject held down the response button. The green light  indicated a correct response, the red light an incorrect one.  Each subject received 300 trials, in groups of 100 trials separated by 5-min rest periods. After each block of 100 trials, the  subject was informed of the total number of points he had won or lost during the preceding block.  The exact sequence of stimuli presented on each trial for all groups was determined by random permutations of a basic list of  300 trials, although the exact location of each stimulus on each trial was determined by a random selection procedure. This  meant that each subject or pair of subjects in a given group was presented the stimuli in a somewhat different order, but that  each subject in that group received the same set of 300 stimulus pairs. Subjects in Groups H and V received equal numbers of  same (Es) and different (Ed) trials, 150 of each, but those in Group HV received a slightly smaller number of ES trials, 144  rather than 150. (An EB trial for Groups H and V is a trial in which the stimuli of the pair presented have identical values  on the relevant dimension, but not necessarily on the other.) Matched subjects or matched pairs of subjects across groups  received, insofar as possible, the same random permutation schedule of stimuli. Each subject participated in one group only. DISCUSSION  The main purpose of the present study has been to try to account for single- and multidimensional judgments using the same  type of model in both cases. The model we have used is a multidimensional version of the Thurstonian model in the sense that  it associates with each stimulus a multivariate normal distribution. This model implies that the square of the distance  between pairs of stimuli has a noncentral X2 distribution, the degrees of freedom being equal to the dimensionality of the  space. A secondary purpose of the study has been to demonstrate the feasibility of obtaining maximum likelihood estimates of  the stimulus coordinates in this multidimensional version of the Thurstonian model.  The major results are as follows.  (1) There were strong asymmetries in the data for all groups. These asymmetries were dealt with by introducing temporal bias  parameters in the model. Without these bias parameters, the fit of the model to the data was completely unsatisfactory.  Asymmetries in one form or another probably occur in most multidimensional studies, but they are usually glossed over by  multidimensional analyses which attempt to eliminate them by averaging them out. Some recent exceptions are Nakatani (1972)  and Townsend (1971). These results suggest to us the need to look for these asymmetries and, in many cases, the need to  incorporate them directly into the multidimensional model.  (2) There was no detectable context effect. We were not able to show that the singledimensional judgments were influenced by  the irrelevant dimension. Perhaps this may be taken as an indication that the stimuli in this study were nonintegral or  analyzable. Krantz and Tversky (1975), using somewhat similar stimuli, rectangles instead of lights in the form of a diamond,  found strong interaction between dimensions. It may be that their stimuli were more integral than the stimuli used here. There  are other possible explanations. Their subjects and those here made quite different responses. Their subjects rated each  stimulus pair on a dissimilarity scale from 1 to 20, while those here made only same or different responses. The rating  response is undoubtedly more complex and undoubtedly makes more demands on the subject. On the basis of their results, Tversky  and Krantz conclude that the properties of "interdimensional additivity" and  SAME-DIFFERENT JUDGMENTS 49  "intradimensional subtractivity" must be rejected. They also conjecture that interaction between dimensions is probably  widespread. This conclusion may be too strong. The model used here with some degree of success implies both of the above  properties.  (3) The model accounted, for the single-dimensional judgments of the horizontal component of the stimuli quite well, but did  not do as well with the single-dimensional judgments of the vertical component. We could find no explanation for this,  although a number of variations of the model were tried. However, it should be emphasized that the poorness of the fit of the  model to the Group V data is relative. It looks poor only in comparison with the Group H fit. Considering the large numbers of  observations involved, a goodness-of-fit p value o.016 is not particularly discouraging. However, it is clear that there is  something different about judgments of vertical distances as compared with judgments of horizontal distances.  (4) The model accounted for the multidimensional judgments better than it did the single-dimensional judgments of Group V, but  less well than it did the single-dimensional judgments of Group H. It appears as though the model fits the multidimensional  judgments about as well as can be expected on the basis of its fit to the two single-dimensional judgments. The limitations of  the fit to the multidimensional data seem almost certainly due to its difficulty with the vertical component of the stimuli.  (5) The parameters were not invariant between the single- and multidimensional solutions. The parameters for the  multidimensional data were smaller than those for the single-dimensional data. It appears that the multidimensional stimuli  were more indiscriminable than expected on the basis of the single-dimensional data. This we see as the major negative results  of the present study. It is evident that further assumptions are required, to tie together the single- and multidimensional  solutions. Cliff and Young (1968) report good agreement between single-dimensional and multidimensional judgments, but their  analyses consist mainly of correlating two sets of interpoint distances. They do not test for parameter invariance. A  correlation of the distances of the singledimensional configuration and the multidimensional configuration obtained here would  undoubtedly be very high. Green and Carmone (1971) do not obtain good agreement between the single- and multidimensional  cases, probably because their statistical analysis does not depend solely on correlations.
