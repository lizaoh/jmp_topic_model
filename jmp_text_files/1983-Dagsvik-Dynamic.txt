DAGSVIK  Central Bureau of Statistics of Norway  The present paper deals with the extension of two well-known static discrete choice theories to the dynamic situation in which  individuals make choices at several points in (continuous) time. A dynamic version of Luce's Axiom, "independence from  irrelevant alternatives," is proposed and some of its implications are derived. In the static case Yellott (J. Math. Psych.  1977, 15, 109-146) and others have demonstrated that an independent random utility model generated from the extreme value  distribution exp(-e-nx-b) becomes equivalent to Luce's Axiom. Yellott also introduced an axiom called "invariance under  uniform expansions of the choice set," and he proved that within the class of random utility models with independent  identically distributed utilities (apart from a location shift) this axiom is equivalent to Luce's Axiom. These results are  extended to the dynamic situation and it is shown that if the utility processes are expressed by so-called extremal processes  the corresponding choice model is Markovian. A nonstationary generalization is proposed which is a substantial interest in  applications where the parameters of the choice process are influenced by previous choice experience or by time-varying  exogenous variables. In particular, it is demonstrated that the nonstationary model is Markovian if and only if the joint  choice probabilities at two points in time have a particular form. Thus, the paper provides a rationale for applying a  specific class of Markov models as the point of departure when modelling mobility processes that involve individual discrete  decisions over time.  1. Examples of this kind are choices about where to live,  transportation mode, labor force participation and level of education. In these applications the traditional theory of  consumption is not obviously applicable. Until recently, probabilistic choice theories covered only experiments at one point in time. However, in many applications the  interest is focused on the structure of choices made over time (cf. Heckman, 1980, 1981).  In the present paper we are concerned with the extension of static choice models to the dynamic situation where individuals  make discrete decisions at different points in continuous time. Formally, we consider a population of decision-makers who  consistently face a set of discrete alternatives, called states. An individual will remain in a state (areal zone, occupation  category, family size, level of education, transportation mode) as long as the attractiveness of other states are inferior to  the attractiveness of the state occupied. The individual choice process is described by the time intervals (holding time)  between each choice and the transitions between the states at the times when choices are made.  In principle, the study of intertemporal choice behavior could be handled within the framework of the static models by  treating each choice history as one alternative. Unfortunately, this approach does not automatically provide appealing model  structures. For instance, a direct application of Luce's IIA Axiom (independence from irrelevant alternatives) yields models  where choices at different times are independent and for many applications this is not an acceptable restriction a priori.  A more fruitful approach seems to be the one followed by Heckman (1981). He extends the random utility idea by representing  each alternative by a random utility Gaussian process. Thus, an individual decides to move whenever the utility of the state  occupied becomes less than the utility of any other state. Yet, this approach is somewhat unsatisfactory, since the choice of  normally distributed utilities is not motivated on theoretical grounds.  The most widely known static choice model is the conditional logit model derived from Luce's IIA Axiom. This model combines  computational simplicity with a clear behavorial content and therefore offers an appealing tool to the analyst. Accordingly,  it is a natural point of departure for a generalization to the dynamic case. In this paper intertemporal extensions of IIA are  proposed and the implications for the corresponding choice model are investigated. The strongest of these assumptions leads to  a particular choice model with the Markov property.  The close connection between the random utility model suggested by Thurstone (1927) and Luce's choice theory (1959) became  apparent when Holman and Marley (cf. Luce and Suppes, 1965) showed that if an independent random utility model has extreme  value distributed utilities it is equivalent to the Luce model. The natural problem to ask next was whether the extreme value  distribution is the only one consistent with the Luce model. This problem was resolved by McFadden (1973) and under  considerably less restrictive assumptions by Yellott (1977) and Strauss (1979). Yellott also gave a characterization of the  Luce model by an axiom which he calles "invariance under uniform expansions of the choice set" (IUE). This axiom is weaker  than Luce's IIA Axiom but implies Luce's Axiom when the choice model is assumed to be an independent random utility model. Consequently, this extends the results of Yellott's (1977) paper to the dynamic situation. MOTIVATION AND SUMMARY OF RESULTS  A common feature of a variety of social, psychological and economic phenomena is that they are generated from discrete  decisions made by individuals at several points in time. Thus, in the intertemporal context the choice alternatives are  sequences of decisions (choice careers). We imagine the decision process as follows: Initially, the individual plans the  optimal career throughout the planning horizon. However, he is likely to be uncertain about future preferences as well as  about future characteristics of the environment. Future preferences may alter as a result of experience and learning, and he  may also receive new information making him revise his previous plans. Even if the decisionmaker is certain about his  preferences and operates in an environment of perfect certainty he may still seem uncertain to the analyst. This is so because  many variables that affect the preferences and the environment, while known to the individual, are unobserved by the analyst.  Consequently, the individual's realized career may differ from the expected career, and therefore, from the observer's point  The most well-known static discrete choice theories are Luce's constant utility and Thurstone's random utility models. An  extension of Luce's theory to the dynamic situation would consist of postulating restrictions on the choice probabilities when  the choice alternatives are decision careers. Since there may be various degrees of "similarity" between different choice  careers it is not obvious how the Luce model  4 JOHN K. DAGSVIK  should be extended. Intuitively, this problem might perhaps be more easily understood within the random utility framework. The  extension of Thurstone's random utility model to intertemporal choice settings is to represent the choice alternatives by  random utility processes { Uj(t) }, where Uj(t) represents the attractiveness of the jth alternative at time t. The individual  maximizes utility at every point in time which means that j is occupied during a time interval if Uj(t) is not exceeded by the  utility of any other alternative in that interval. The variable Uj(t) is interpreted as the highest level of utility given  that state (alternative) j is occupied at period t and given that future choices are made optimally.  If the residual processes are not autocorrelated the joint probability of making a particular sequence of decisions will  simply be the product of the marginal choice probabilities at each point in time and thus the conventional static theories can  be applied. Similarly, in case the deterministic parts of the utilities depend on the choice history the joint choice  probabilities can be readily calculated provided there is no autocorrelation in the residual process.  Unfortunately, conditioning on realized choice history is not always sufficient to remove autocorrelation in the disturbances.  In several contexts it may be plausible to assume that current choices depend on previous relative preference evaluations.  This effect, which we may call habit persistence, stems from temporal stability in the unmeasured factors that influence  preference. In general, both habit persistence and effects due to previous choice experience may be present.  In an interesting paper, Heckman (1981) has suggested a dynamic extension of the Thurstone model. This model has the advantage  of permitting time-varying explanatory variables and general correlation structures between utilities at different times and  for different alternatives. However, when the choice set contains three or more alternatives the computational difficulties in  estimating this model reduces its applicability. Moreover, this model is formulated in discrete time which is unnatural since  the real processes usually develop in continuous time. The inconvenience with discrete time representations is that model  parameters for one sampling interval unit are not in general comparable to the parameters defined for other interval units.  However, more fundamentally, Heckman's choice of Gaussian utility processes is not justified from choice theoretic arguments.  Ginsberg (1972) discusses how the parameters of his semi-Markov model can be related to Luce's choice theory. However, his  study is somewhat limited since only transition probabilities, given a transition, are obtained from the IIA Axiom while the  holding time distributions are not derived from behavioral considerations (except for the decreasing failure rate assumption).  A similar objection applies to other works; see, for instance Moss, (1979), and some of the references therein.  The present paper is an attempt to generate probabilistic models for discrete dynamic choice from behavioral axioms. We start  by proposing a plausible extension of Luce's IIA Axiom to the dynamic situation (Section 4). As far as we know there seems to  be no similar attempt in the literature except for Luce's (1959) demonstration that whenever holding times satisfy IIA the  corresponding distribution must be exponential. The implications of the Axiom (Axiom 1) for two and three  DISCRETE DYNAMIC CHOICE 5  points in time are explored and an additional axiom (Axiom 2) is introduced to obtain a further characterization of the  stochastic dependence between choices at different times. An experiment with choices at two points in time involves choice  sets B, and BZ at these times, respectively, from which the decision-maker chooses. Since these choice sets may be disjoint or  partially overlap, choices at different times show increasing degree of dependence with increasing overlap of B, and Bz. Thus  when stating Axiom 1 choices of alternatives belonging to B, r1 Bz must be treated separately from choices of alternatives  that are not contained in B, r) Bz. One way to view Axiom 1 for choices at two points in time is that an individual selects a  subset A = (A, , AZ) of the most attractive choice careers. Subsequently, he selects the most attractive career from A. In the  last step the decisionmaker acts as if A is the choice universe, i.e., the alternatives outside A are "irrelevant." However, A  is not an arbitrary subset of the choice set: The elements of A have the property that the choice alternatives at different  times are different. When the choice sets B, and B, are different there are further restrictions on A.  In Section 5 we introduce the class of dynamic generalized Thurstone models (DGTM), i.e., models that are generated from  independent random utility processes and similarly to the static case we investigate the uniqueness properties of DGTM.  Yellott (1977) proved that for complete choice experiments with at least three alternatives the extreme value distribution is  the only one that is consistent with IIA provided the characteristic function of the utilities never vanishes. Recall that a  complete choice experiment with n alternatives is one in which choice probabilities are determined for every subset of the  choice universe where the universe contains n states, or alternatives. If n is infinite the condition on the characteristic  function is not necessary.  In the dynamic case it turns out that a similar relationship exists: Specifically, the class of DGTM with utility processes  that have multivariate extreme value distributed marginals satisfy the above axioms (see Section 5.) The multivariate extreme  value distribution is the asymptotic distribution for the maximum of independent identically distributed random vectors and  consequently one would expect Yellott's Axiom "invariance under uniform expansions of the choice set" (IUE) to be closely  related to this distribution. The dynamic version of IUE introduced here (Axiom 3) states that the joint choice probabilities  at arbitrary points in time remain invariant when each alternative is replicated k times, where k is arbitrary. In Section 6  we prove that the DGTM satisfy Axiom 3 if and only if the utility processes have multivariate extreme value distributed  marginals. Accordingly, Axiom 3 implies Axiom 1 for DGTM. Moreover, we demonstrate that Axiom 2 together with Axiom 1 implies  Axiom 3, for choices at three points in time.  In Section 7, we turn to the problem of whether the class of DGTM generated from the multivariate extreme value distribution  contains Markovian models as special cases. An axiom (Axiom 4) stating that the utility processes are nondecreasing (except  for a trend component) is introduced and behavioral arguments are provided to justify this axiom. An example of a  nondecreasing process with multivariate extreme value marginal distributions is the extreme Markovian stationary process  JOHN K. DAGSVIK  (EMS). Assuming that the utilities are EMS processes it follows that the corresponding holding times are exponential and hence  that the choice model is Markovian. The EMS process was defined by Tiago de Oliveira (1973) and apart from a trend component  it belongs to the class of pure jump processes. The Markovian nature of the choice model generated from EMS processes relies  on the particular property that the difference between two independent EMS processes has exponential-distributed excursion  times. (Recall that excursion times are the time intervals the process lies below a given level.) As far as we know this  result has not been proved before and it should be of independent interest.  The results mentioned above are obtained under the assumption of stationary utility processes. In a wide range of applications  this restriction is not acceptable. For instance, the deterministic utility components may depend upon time-varying exogenous  variables. However, the generalization to nonstationarity is not immediate since the Heckman approach to let the deterministic  utility components depend on time while the residual processes remain stationary does not seem to yield tractable solutions  for the type of processes considered here. Instead we suggest another and more convenient extension to nonstationarity. An  interpretation of this regime is that nonstationarity is thought to arise from temporal variations in collections of  independent "basic" random utility components from which the original utilities are obtained by taking the maximum over these  collections of basic utilities. In other words, the original states are interpreted as aggregated from "small" basic states. MARKOVIAN GENERALIZED THURSTONE MODELS  An important question is whether the class of dynamic generalized Thurstone models satisfying Axiom 3 contains Markovian  models. In this section we introduce an additional behavioral assumption and we investigate its implication. It turns out that  the Markov property of the choice model is closely related to utility processes of a particular type, namely, the extreme  Markovian stationary processes (EMS) defined by Tiago de Oliveira (1973). EXTENSION To NONSTATIONARITY  For the model framework to be interesting in a variety of applications it must be sufficiently flexible to account for  time-varying explanatory variables. For instance, individual or household characteristics like age, education and family  composition may be relevant exogenous variables that affect the preferences and these variables usually change over time.  Furthermore, the alternative-specific variables may also fluctuate over time. Examples are found in migration studies where  regions are characterized by time dependent economic indicators. Similarly, in urban travel demand analysis transportation  alternatives may have different attractiveness at different times as a result of changes in costs, operating hours and  frequence.  Nonstationarity may also arise as a consequence of past choice experience. For instance, Heckman (1980) has studied the effect  of past labor market experience upon actual labor supply propensities. In some contexts it may be plausible to make the  assumption that the occupied state has an additional utility component due to the individual's "adhesion" propensity which is  explained by the costs and the psychological stress connected with a change of state.
