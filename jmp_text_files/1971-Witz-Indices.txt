JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 8, 44-57 (1971)  Collaboration Indices in Structural Role Theory  KLAUS WITZ AND GORDON O'BRIEN  University of Illinois, Urbana, Illinois 61801  In this paper a central aspect of organizational structure-interposition collaboration -is defined using the concepts of  structural role theory. Four indices for measuring different forms of structural collaboration are defined; two of these have  been used in several empirical studies. The bulk of the paper is devoted to the problem of finding maxima for two of the  indices under suitable conditions. Finally some ways in which the indices could be used to investigate the relationships  between collaboration and organizational processes are discussed.  Ever since Durkheim's study of the dependence of social relationships upon the division of labor, social scientists have been  concerned with the effects of organizational structure upon interpersonal behavior and organizational productivity. One of the  main difficulties confronting workers interested in these problems, however, has been the development of a method describing  organizational structure which was both general and precise. Definitions of social and group structure have often been general  enough to allow classification of a large number of different groups and institutions but, unfortunately, have not provided  ways for measuring precisely the dimensions of structure which supposedly differentiated organizations. Conversely, a number  of methods of measuring organizational dimensions have been developed which are not suitable for classifying structures  because they deal with only a few aspects of the total structure.  Recently, a concept of organizational structure was advanced which is suitable for generating a general taxonomy of  organizations yet is, at the same time, amenable to quantitative analysis (Oeser and Harary 1962, 1964; Oeser and O'Brien  1967). The purpose of this article is, firstly, to define a central component of organizational structure, namely,  interposition collaboration-a form of structural coordinationusing the concepts of Oeser's structural role theory; and,  secondly, to develop a number of indices which can be used to measure the amount of collaboration required by any given formal  organization. Our collaboration refers to the interdependences between the task system of an organization and the positional  structure. The particular kind of collaboration employed depends on the allocation relationships that order positions and  tasks, and the precedence relationships that order the elements of the task system. Because the definition of collaboration  must be in terms of a number of elements and  44  COLLABORATION INDICES IN STRUCTURAL ROLE THEORY 45  relationships of the total organizational structure, it has been a particularly difficult concept to quantify.  1. DEFINITIONS OF COLLABORATION  In structural role theory (Oeser and Harary 1962, 1964; Oeser and O'Brien 1967), a formal organization W includes a set of  positions P = {pl ,..., p}, a set of tasks T = ft, ,..., to}, and two relations R2 and R4 which order positions and tasks.  R4 is the task allocation relation, R4 C P x T, and R2 the precedence relation between tasks, R2 C T X T. We define an atom of  position-task collaboration to be an ordered quadruple (p; , p; , tk , tl) such that (p; , tk) E R4 , (p; , t,) E R4 , (tk ,  ti) E R2 , and pi p; . Each atom of collaboration (p; , p; , tk , tl) corresponds to a configuration like the one in Fig. 1 in  the diagram of S and expresses the fact that the tasks allocated to pi and p; have to be performed in sequence (one directly  after the other). DISCUSSION  Three forms of collaboration have been defined and explored. Much mathematical work needs to be done, especially on branching  task systems and more restricted classes of allocation relations. However, simple sequential task structures are found in a  wide range of actual work organizations, and in such cases the indices, together with the results in Sec. 4, can be used to  investigate the relationships between collaboration and a number of social relationships. Specifically the following problems  for future research may be listed:  1. The effect of various forms of collaboration upon communication relationships. Increasing the amount of structural  collaboration will lead to greater regulation of the interaction between persons who are assigned to given organizational  positions. By studying a range of organizations which vary in both the amount and form of collaboration employed it is  possible to map the communication relationships which are generated by various collaboration patterns. Determination of the  communication patterns is likely to be a necessary step for understanding the nature of the affective relationships between  workers in a given organization. A number of studies conducted in different organizations, including factories (Vroom and  Mann, 1960), classrooms (Byrne, 1961), college dormitories (Newcomb, 1956), and housing settlements (Festinger, 1953) have  supported the hypothesis that affective relationships are significantly related to the amount of communication occurring  within the organizational content. These studies have demonstrated that interpersonal relationships are significantly  determined by communication patterns but so far no one has been able to predict the actual pattern of communication which is  likely to occur nor have studies been performed which sought to establish the functional relationships between collaboration,  communication, and effective relationships.  2. The effect of various forms of collaboration upon informal influence relationships. Just as different position-task  structures may generate affective relationships so may they produce influence relationships. Because a task is highly  collaborative some positions, perhaps those with tasks at the beginning and end of the task sequence, will be able to have  more control over the task flow and this influence may not be of the same magnitude as these positions have in virtue of their  location in the formal authority chart. Also, some positions may have greater levels of influence simply because they share a  number of tasks in the precedence line. A study is being conducted at present to identify the task structures in an accounting  firm which produce influence relationships between positions which may not be conducive to high productivity.  3. The effect of various forms of collaboration upon organizational productivity. Many studies of group and organizational  productivity have related productivity to structural factors other than the task system. Generalizations have been made about  the effect of sociometric and status structures upon outputs which have generally  COLLABORATION INDICES IN STRUCTURAL ROLE THEORY 57  assumed that the task structure across organizations is constant. Further research should aim at identifying the type of  collaboration which optimizes performance on a range of tasks. If it is found that the optimal collaboration patterns for  creative planning tasks, for example, are not the same as optimal patterns for physical manipulative tasks, then research on  productivity will have to ask slightly different questions. Instead of asking questions about the effect of member  composition, status, and sociometric patterns upon productivity the questions will be phrased in terms of the interaction  effects of those variables with organizational variables such as collaboration.  The preceding problems have been stated in other places but very little systematic research has been directed at their  solution. Now that indices of organizational structure are available it is hoped that more research will be conducted.  REFERENCES  BYRNE, D.
