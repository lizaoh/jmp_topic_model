Consensus theory is an extension of latent-trait models that assumes that the correct responses to a set of questions – the answer key – are unknown (e.g.,  Anders & Batchelder, 2012; Batchelder & Romney, 1988; Romney, Weller, & Batchelder, 1986 ). Consensus theory includes the answer key as a set of model parameters which are estimated along with the latent trait. Originally, the method was proposed for applications in Anthropology (e.g.,  Romney et al., 1986 ): Researchers who question people about an unknown culture initially do not know the answer key. In such an application, estimating the answer key – that is, details about the unknown culture – is typically the primary goal. Another application includes the eyewitness testimony of multiple witnesses. Consensus theory offers an estimate of what happened during a crime – the answer key – while accounting for inter-individual differences in the witnesses’ ability to recall the crime  (Waubert de Puiseau, Aßfalg, Erdfelder, & Bernstein, 2012; Waubert de Puiseau, Greving, Aßfalg, & Musch, 2017) .

                         Strictly speaking, consensus theory provides an estimate of the consensus between witnesses. This consensus typically overlaps with but is not necessarily identical to the ground truth in mock crimes  (Waubert de Puiseau et al., 2012, 2017) .

Unfortunately, applications of consensus theory are limited by the lack of models that account for mixed response formats within a test. Extant models account for dichotomous (e.g., yes vs. no), multiple choice, ordinal, and continuous response formats  (Anders & Batchelder, 2015; Anders, Oravecz, & Batchelder, 2014; Batchelder & Anders, 2012; Batchelder & Romney, 1988; Batchelder, Strashny, & Romney, 2010; Karabatsos & Batchelder, 2003) . However, currently, it is not possible to mix these response formats and estimate the latent trait based on the combined responses. Consider again the example of an eyewitness interview. Extant models can be inadequate for eyewitness testimony: yes–no questions, for example, are a poor match for the complexity of eyewitness recollections (e.g.,  Waubert de Puiseau et al., 2012 ).

Mixed response formats are well known in latent-trait analysis  (Moustaki & Knott, 2000; Moustaki & Papageorgiou, 2005; Sammel, Ryan, & Legler, 1997) . Generalized latent trait models allow a mixture of response formats within the same test. Here, I propose the  variable-response model  (VRM), an analogous approach for consensus theory in which the answer key is unknown. Unfortunately, generalized latent trait models are not readily adapted to consensus theory. Instead, I propose an approach for the VRM that is based on well-known latent-trait models for dichotomously scored items  (Birnbaum, 1968; Rasch, 1961) . In these models, the  item characteristic curve  describes the probability of a correct response as a function of the model parameters. The VRM extends the concept of the item characteristic curve to all permissible responses of an item. This approach includes the answer key in the model as a latent parameter.

In the following sections, I begin with a brief introduction of the 2-parameter logistic model for dichotomously scored items  (Birnbaum, 1968) . This model forms the core of the VRM. By extending the 2-parameter logistic model to all permissible responses – independent of the hypothesized response distribution – the model core can be flexibly combined with any response probability distribution that meets some minimal requirements. I formulate the requirements for these distributions and introduce two examples. For the parameter estimation, I further propose a Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) procedure along with model selection and model check indices. Finally, I present the results of a simulation study and the application of the VRM to the empirical data of a Geography test.

Arguably, the most well-known latent-trait models account for items that are scored dichotomously as either correct or incorrect  (Birnbaum, 1968; Rasch, 1961) . These models assume unidimensionality—that is, a single latent trait underlies the responses of a respondent. In consensus theory, a similar concept is referred to as the  common truth  or  single culture  assumption and requires that there is a single answer key for all respondents (e.g.,  Batchelder & Romney, 1988; Karabatsos & Batchelder, 2003 ). For present purposes, I assume unidimensionality and a single culture for the VRM as well.

I propose a  Metropolis–Hastings  (M–H) algorithm  (Hastings, 1970; Metropolis, Rosenbluth, Rosenbluth, Teller, & Teller, 1953)  for most model parameters and Gibbs sampling for parameters with readily available conditional distributions. This approach is known as  M–H within Gibbs  and serves as the estimation algorithm for the VRM  (Fox, 2010; Patz & Junker, 1999) .

The M–Hwithin Gibbs algorithm requires the definition of prior and candidate distributions for the model parameters. I rely on weakly informative or uninformative priors to prevent biased results  (Fox, 2010) . I further rely on a hierarchical model structure in which the parameters of the prior distribution are sampled from a hyperprior distribution which itself depends on hyperparameters. These hyperparameters account for the fact that important information about the prior distribution of a first-order parameter (e.g., item difficulty) – such as the mean and precision of the distribution – is typically unknown  (Fox, 2010) .

Most parameters in the VRM require an M–Halgorithm and, consequently, a candidate distribution. With too narrow candidate distributions the Markov chain takes a long time to explore the posterior distribution whereas too wide distributions yield high rejection rates for the candidate parameter and render the Monte Carlo sampling inefficient  (Hastings, 1970; Metropolis et al., 1953) . A practical approach is to select the parameters of the candidate distributions in such a way that the acceptance rate of the candidate parameters reaches an acceptable level.  Gelman, Roberts, and Gilks (1996)  suggest acceptance rates of about 50% for efficient sampling.

Fig. 4  includes the results of this simulation. I first determined the dominant answer key for each data set. I defined dominance in terms of three criteria: (1) the mean absolute distance (MAD) between the true and estimated continuous answer keys, (2) the correlation between the true and estimated continuous answer keys, and (3) the proportion of matching true and estimated categorical answer keys. I considered key 1 – the answer key of group 1 – as dominant if all three criteria were better (i.e., lower MAD, higher correlation, higher proportion of matches) for key 1 compared to key 2 – the answer key of group 2 – and vice versa. I considered none of the answer keys dominant if there was no consensus between the three criteria about which key performed better. As a final comparison measure for the VRM, I computed the answer-key estimation accuracy of the average respondent and of an equal-weights aggregation rule. Compared to the VRM and other consensus-theory models, equal-weights aggregation neglects person and item characteristics during aggregation.

Fig. 5  includes the results of the simulation. Independent of the data generating model and the response format, latent trait estimates were always more accurate if the fitted model had also generated the data. This effect was particularly pronounced for continuous responses where the VRM and CRM failed to account for data they did not generate themselves. With regard to answer key estimates, the results were more mixed. For categorical responses, the VRM tended to deliver the most accurate estimates. The only exception were dichotomous responses generated with the GCM2: Here, both models estimated all answer keys correctly. Conversely, for continuous responses, the model generating the data outperformed the competing model. This was true for data generated with the VRM and data generated with the CRM. Interestingly, both models performed well with regard to the empirical data of the Geography test. This suggests that problems to handle the data of the other model may be restricted to only the most complex variants of the models used in the present simulation. Overall, the simulation did not reveal any systematic disadvantage of the VRM over other consensus-theory models.

The VRM builds on well-known latent-trait models for dichotomously scored items. Consequently, the VRM can be flexibly applied to all response distributions which preserve the rationale of the item characteristic curve. Here, I introduced implementations for continuous and discrete responses. I further proposed a MCMC algorithm to estimate the parameters of the VRM. In a simulation study, this algorithm recovered the data-generating parameters well. Applied to the empirical data of a Geography test, the VRM also provided the most accurate answer-key estimates among the tested methods. Finally, if researchers have access to small data sets with different response formats, I found that combining these response formats for analysis with the VRM is superior to several separate analyses.

Currently, the VRM also includes a number of notable limitations. The analysis of the Geography data revealed that, if data sets are relatively large (i.e., hundreds of respondents and items), separate analyses of each response format can yield superior answer key estimates compared to a combined analysis of all responses. This issue might be resolved by modeling multiple latent traits, similar to extant latent-trait models (e.g.,  Reckase, 1997 ). Future extensions of the model could further try to improve the applicability of the VRM, for example, by introducing a version of the model for ordinal data or by identifying multiple consensus cultures (i.e., answer keys) in a data set (e.g.,  Anders & Batchelder, 2015 ). Finally, compared to consensus-theory models that are specifically tailored to a response format (e.g.  Anders & Batchelder, 2015 ), the common latent-trait structure of the response formats in the VRM (Eq.  (2) ) is perhaps less suited to model the specific cognitive processes contributing to a response. However, the extent of this potential disadvantage is currently unclear given the sparsity of attempts to experimentally validate the postulated cognitive processes in consensus-theory models (but see  Aßfalg & Erdfelder, 2012; Waubert de Puiseau et al., 2012 ). The primary evaluation criterion of consensus-theory models remains the estimation of the answer key. In that regard, the VRM performed well with regard to both simulated and empirical data.

The VRM improves on extant consensus-theory models by providing a flexible framework for different response formats. For this purpose, I introduced some minimal requirements to include new response formats in the model. By accounting for different response formats within the same model, the VRM further adds to the flexibility of applied research in areas such as Anthropology or eyewitness testimony. Researchers can now combine response formats within the same analysis or estimate the answer key or the latent trait for different response formats with the same model.
