JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY: 2, 363-370 (1965)  Contingent Gains and Losses in a Risk-Taking Situation'  JEROME L. MYERS, MARY M. SUYDAM, AND BLASE GAMBINO  University of Massachusetts, Amherst, Massachusetts  On each of 300 trials, subjects were required to choose between a known payoff and a risky option. The value of the known  payoff was +1 on half of the trials, -1 on the other half. There were two between group variables, the level of risk and the  probability that risk taking would result in a gain. This probability was contingent upon the value of the known payoff. At  all three contingent probabilities, when the known payoff was + 1, more risks were taken at the high than at the low risk  level. When the known payoff was -1, less risks were taken at the high than at the low risk level. The results were considered  in terms of two models for choice behavior.  Several experiments involving the choice between a "sure thing" and a risky option have recently been performed (Myers and  Sadler, 1960; Katz, 1962; Myers and Katz, 1962). The payoff matrices for these experiments are of the form  El Es El E2  Al w w A1 w w  A2 11 1 1 and A2 1-1 -11 ,  where El and E2 are equiprobable events and A1 and A2 are the predictions of those events. The major findings are that (a) the  asymptotic probability of an A1 response, P(A,), is always greater when the payoff associated with an A2 is -1 than when the  payoff is + 1, and (b) as the absolute value of the level of risk, w, increases, P(A,) increases when the A2 payoff is + 1,  and decreases when the A2 payoff is -1. The first finding is clearly predictable; subjects are more willing to gamble when not  gambling results in a loss than when not gambling results in a gain. The finding that level of risk and value of the sure  thing interact is of considerably more interest, since the shape of the interaction runs counter to the intuitive notion that  increased risk should result in more intelligent behavior. In the studies under discussion, the subject can ensure a high  average payoff by always gambling when the sure thing is -1, never when it is + 1; subjects increasingly deviate from that  strategy as the amount risked increases. These probabilities have been made contingent upon whether the "sure thing" was a -~- 1 or a -1. One  purpose of these manipulations is to permit the determination of empirical relationships among level of risk, value of the  "sure thing" and event probability.  A second purpose of the study is to discriminate between two models of choice behavior. Myers, Reilly, and Taub-(1961) have  presented data that suggest that choice probabilities are a function of the differences in expected values associated with the  choices. Edwards (1956) and Taub and Myers (1961) have found that choice probabilities are a function of the relative losses  associated with responses, where loss is defined as the difference between the obtained payoff and the maximum payoff  obtainable given that the subject knew the correct response for that trial. Suydam (1965) has obtained rating data for 45  payoff matrices that are well fit by a model based on the relative loss hypothesis. For the treatment combinations included in  the present experiment, the expected value and relative loss models yield different predictions. The present experiment should  therefore discriminate between these two models of choice behavior.  METHOD  Materials. Six decks of 24 x 32 in. white cards were prepared. Decks 1, 3, 4, and 6 contained 50 cards and decks 2 and 5  contained 60 cards. The known payoff (sure thing) was printed on one side of each card and the unknown payoff (risk) was  printed on the reverse side.  Procedure. Subjects differed with respect to which deck was used. Each deck represented one of six combinations of risk and  contingency. Two levels of risk were employed: a low risk (decks 1-3) which contained values from -6 to +6, but not including  -1, 0, or +1 and a high risk (decks 4-6) with values from -16 to -}-16, but not including values from -11 to -}-11. Each  unknown payoff appeared an equal number of times in each deck. The three decks at each level of risk differed with respect to  their level of contingency. Contingency as used in this study refers to the probability that gambling would result in a gain,  contingent on the occurrence of the + 1 sure thing. For one deck, the contingent probability of a gain, 'r+ , was 0.80; i.e.,  gambling would be rewarded on 8011) of the trials on which the sure thing was -I-1. The remaining levels of n+ were 0.50 and  0.20. Associated with each deck was also a value of ir_, the probability of a gain for gambling when the sure thing was -1.  The decks were so constructed that a_ - 1 - or+; e.g., if 7r+ = 0.80, -rr_ - 0.20. Subjects tested with this deck had an 80o  probability of a rewarded gambling response when the alternative was a sure -f-1, and a 20% probability of a rewarded gambling  response when the alternative was a sure -1. Within each deck the value of the sure thing was either -1 or -{-1 on each trial.  Each value appeared an equal number of times.  Each subject was instructed that he could either accept a sure thing of -I-1 or -1, or reject the sure thing and accept the  risk which would be some positive or negative value greater than 1. The experimenter turned each card over as soon as the  subject made his decision, exhibiting the outcome of the gamble regardless of whether or not the subject gambled. The  experimenter shuffled the deck at the start of the game and before each new pass through the  CONTINGENT GAINS AND LOSSES 365  deck until the subject had seen 300 cards. Poker chips were used as payoffs, and each subject was given a stake of 200 chips  to begin the game. A bank of additional chips was available to the subject from which he removed additional winnings and  deposited his losses as the game progressed.  Subjects. Ninety male undergraduates enrolled in the introductory psychology course at the University of Massachusetts were  randomly selected and assigned to the six groups. There is only one notable qualitative trend in the data that the ELR values do not  reflect. As risk increases, P generally decreases more on -1 trials than it rises on + 1 trials, but the changes in ELR values  are equal in magnitude. This might be accounted for if a constant, representing the utility of gambling, were added to the  entries in the upper row of each payoff matrix, or if losses could be computed on the basis of utilities rather than actual  payoffs.  CONCLUSIONS  While the dEV and ELR models both describe the effect of Value at each level Of -rr+ , only the ELR model accounts for the  significant interaction of Risk and Value, an interaction observed at all three 7T+ levels in this study and at Tr+ = 0.5 in  four previously published experiments. Furthermore, Edwards (1956) and Taub and Myers (1961) have demonstrated the adequacy of  this model to describe asymptotic probability learning data, and Suydam (1965) has obtained excellent fits to rating data in a  choice situation. The last three named papers provide extensive discussions of the model and why it works.  A recent experiment by Taub (1965) provides further support for the hypothesis that choice behavior is more strongly  influenced by regret, or loss than by expected value. Taub's subjects were briefly shown slides containing several letters of  the alphabet. Certain letters on each slide were designated as low value targets; their correct detection resulted in a  one-point gain. The other letters on each slide were designated as high value targets; the high value varied over experimental  groups (2, 4, 8, or 16 points). An EV approach would yield the prediction that percent correct detections of high value  targets would increase as the high valu -, increased; percent correct detections of low value targets should be constant over  groups since the low value is always one point. An EL approach would yield the prediction that percent correct detections of  high value targets would be constant as value increased from 2 to 16; regret is zero for all groups whenever the high value  target is detected. However, percent correct predictions of low value targets should decrease as the high value increases; the  regret associated with a one-point gain increases as the alternative value increases. Taub's results are clearly consistent  with the EL formulation.  Apart from the question of goodness of fit, the ELR model is not entirely satisfactory. It is a static model and therefore is  not capable of describing trial-to-trial changes in behavior. Furthermore, it is doubtful that the ELR model precisely reflects  370 MYERS, SUYDAM, AND GAMBINO  the psychological processes involved. Certainly the subject does not calculate ELR's on each trial. On the other hand, it is  quite clear that the ELR model yields qualitatively correct nontrivial predictions about choice probability. At the very  least, we have a parameter-free description of some aspects of choice. At the most, there is the strong suggestion that loss,  or regret, is a major factor in decision behavior, that the subject is influenced not by his payoff, but by his payoff  relative to what he might have won.
