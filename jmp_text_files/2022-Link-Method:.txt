S. S. Stevens popularized the use of “direct” measures of stimulus magnitude and argued that feelings of stimulus magnitude were characterized best by a power function of physical stimulus intensity. Consequently, urged Stevens, the long-accepted theory of a logarithmic relation between sensation and physical stimulus intensity, developed by the creator of Psychophysics, Gustav Fechner, should be abandoned. Also, in a direct attack on  Thurstone ’s ( 1927a, 1955 ) newer psychophysical methods, Stevens argued that measurements of stimulus differences based on a measure of inherent error variability were inherently flawed. And, to illustrate the advantages of direct methods,  Stevens (1966)  applied the “direct” methods to previous psychophysical measurements, such as  Thurstone ’s ( 1927b ) measurements of the seriousness of crime. The use of the direct measures, Stevens argued, provides an accurate assessment of perceived stimulus magnitude.

The Wave Theory of Difference and Similarity ( Link, 1992, 2020 ) provides insight into the engine that generates results supporting both points of view. A single theory shows how both traditions viewed the same mental phenomena through different eyes. Wave theory shows how a paired comparison choice and a magnitude of stimulus difference derive from a single measure that is both logarithmic and a power function. Furthermore, and perhaps most significant, a small sample of paired comparisons from a single observer can now provide a basis for a complete prediction of the paired comparison probabilities for that observer. Individual choice behavior ( Luce, 1959 ) is predictable.

This new method of directly measured stimulus differences (DMSD) unites the two major features of each psychophysical tradition. On each experimental trial observers are to compare two stimuli with respect to an attribute and, on that basis, to choose one of the two stimuli. The observers are then to indicate the magnitude of the difference between the two stimuli. In previous research in my laboratory, the method of indicating the magnitude of comparative difference employed a pair of carefully calibrated dynamometers with outputs fed continuously to the computer controlling the presentations of stimuli ( Karpiuk, 1994 ). Later work, described below, shows that observer-generated horizontal line lengths displayed on a video screen provide very accurate measurements of the subjective magnitude of comparative difference. The use of visually created measures of magnitude allows for a widely usable method.

Before explaining the theoretical details behind these judgments, an experiment and its outcomes are presented.

Jobs and their salaries are important topics for college-aged students. Both the relevance of jobs, and the fact that the salaries of jobs are numerical values, were important considerations in the selection of jobs as stimuli. Also, previous research by  Snow (1981)  provided guidance on the use of jobs as stimuli. The job names are pointers to the salary the student believes is characteristic of the job. Moreover, the salaries associated with jobs are not known exactly, there is always a range of possible values. Yet the student must somehow construct a belief about the remunerative value of a job. Also, this particular paired comparison experiment, in which jobs are compared on the basis of which has the larger salary, provides for an application of ideas drawn from experiments on numerical comparisons ( Azzarello, 1985; Garbus, 1985; Link, 1990, 1992 ).

A set of job descriptions taken from the Bureau of Labor Statistics (URL: http://stats.bls.gov/soc ) were shown to McMaster University undergraduates to determine those jobs that were easily identified and/or learned by the students. This initial evaluation led to the selection of 20 jobs spanning a large salary range. Each of 20 McMaster University undergraduate student volunteers, ranging from 21 to 25 years of age, earned $10 to participate in the experiment. Each student was tested for corrected 20–20 vision in each eye. If the observer met this simple vision requirement, then the observer received an instruction sheet describing the experimental task. The instructions included a list of 20 occupational definitions from the United States Bureau of Labor Statistics. After reading the instructions the observer discussed with the experimenter any questions about the meanings of the job definitions or the experiment itself. The 16 jobs used for experimental trials were Accountant, Bartender, Bus Driver, Chemist, Computer Programmer, Dentist, Engineer, Farmer, Flight Attendant, Lawyer, Physician, Police Officer, Psychologist, Secretary, Teacher, and Welder. If necessary, the instructions were clarified. The student was then shown to a dimly illuminated experimental room and adapted to the dimness for one minute. Students were shown how to make responses and how to change them if necessary. Then, the experimenter explained the purpose of the keyboard arrow keys. The horizontal line length, L, used to indicate the magnitude of stimulus difference began as a yellow dot in the center of the black computer-controlled display screen. The experimenter showed how to extend and contract the length of the line. The experimenter stayed present for a few practice trials to answer any further questions and then left the experimental room closing the door. During the session a 50db white noise was presented continuously from speakers in the ceiling of the room.

Each observer received 10 practice trials and 120 experimental trials. Although the trials were observer-paced, the timing of events for each trial was controlled by a DEC (Digital Equipment Corporation) IBM style computer. A Turbo-Pascal program controlling the presentation of stimuli and measurement of responses was written by Peter Karpiuk, Stephen Link, and Fernanda Francisco. All times and timing of events were measured according to a clock routine that incremented a counter every 900 counts of a 16 MHz internal clock. Timing accuracy was within one millisecond.

Each trial was self-paced and initiated by the student pressing the “Enter” key on the computer keyboard. After a delay of 50 ms the computer displayed horizontally and centered at the top of a display screen the question “WHICH IS LARGER?” in capital red letters. Then “Yearly salary of a judge?” appeared in yellow letters, horizontally centered but about 1/3rd of the way below the upper edge of the display screen. The comparison occupation appeared in yellow letters in a symmetric position 1/3rd of the way up from the bottom edge of the display; “The yearly salary of a waiter/waitress?” A yellow dot appeared in the very center of the display screen.

When the student chose a job title by pressing either the up arrow or down-arrow key, the corresponding statement, such as “The yearly salary of a judge?” changed to a blue color. Reversing choices resulted in a return to the yellow color for the unselected title and a change to the blue color for the new choice, such as “The yearly salary of a waiter/waitress?” At the very bottom of the display screen appeared the message, in red capital letters, “CHOOSE TOP OR BOTTOM”.

After making a choice the student used the right-arrow key to increase the yellow dot in the center of the screen into a horizontal yellow line length corresponding to the student’s believed difference in salary between the two jobs. Pressing the left-arrow key effected a decrease in the length of the line. A line grew symmetrically with respect to the center of the display screen. At the end of a trial the student pressed the “Enter” key to complete the trial and advance to the next trial. Other keyboard keys were covered to prevent accidental depression.

The two streams of development, the long-standing Fechnerian approach including both Thurstonian Theory and Signal Detection Theory, and the  Herbart (1812, 1824)  and  Stevens (1975)  approach to the measurement of stimulus intensity, provide two windows through which we see a common underlying mechanism. Stevens’ method of direct measurement had a solid empirical basis and a theoretical development greatly advanced by  Baird (1997) . The method of paired comparison traces its theoretical origin to  Fechner ’s ( 1860 ) extension of the  Gauss (1809, 1962)  theory of error in physical measurements to variability characteristic of a sensation (a Vorstellung)

The method of paired comparison ( David, 1969; Thurstone, 1927a ) requires comparisons between a pair of stimuli drawn from a stimulus set of size n. The full paired-comparison design requires that all stimuli be compared against each other with respect to a particular attribute. For example, among a list of crimes, a randomly selected pair of crimes may be compared with respect to seriousness. Or a set of physical stimuli may be compared with respect to feelings such as heaviness, brightness, dullness, loveliness, and so forth.

Comparative judgments are usually binary choices between stimuli, sometimes with attendant measures of response time and response confidence ( Ascher, 1974; Cartwright, 1942; Link, 1978b, 1992; Link & Tindall, 1971 ). However, the magnitude of the comparative difference giving rise to, or perhaps resulting from, the process of choice is not measured. The reason for not measuring the magnitude of difference, it seems to me, is the lack of theoretical guidance about the value of such a quantity. Also, the rift between two areas of psychophysics that followed different empirical traditions, the Fechnerian and Stevens’ approaches, tended to isolate each experimental method from the other.

In this method of directly measured stimulus differences (DMSD) the two major features of each tradition are united. On each experimental trial observers are to compare two stimuli with respect to an attribute(s) and, on that basis, to choose one of the two stimuli. The observers are then to indicate the magnitude of the difference between the two stimuli. In previous research in my laboratory, the method of indicating the magnitude of comparative difference employed a pair of carefully calibrated dynamometers with outputs fed continuously to the computer controlling the presentations of stimuli ( Karpiuk, 1994 ). Later work, described below, shows that observer-generated horizontal line lengths displayed on a video screen provide very accurate measurements of the observer’s magnitude of comparative difference.

A fundamental assumption of mathematical biophysics and psychophysics is that a stimulus receptor acts as a trigger to shoot a quantum of neural energy through a neural pathway. When more specific assumptions are made about the probability distribution of stimulus amplitudes more exact predictions occur. Under assumptions characteristic of neural cell processes, such firing is Poisson distributed ( Rashevsky, 1938, 1960 ) and the basis for various neural counting processes ( Grandell, 1998; McGill, 1964; McGill & Teich, 1991, 1995 ).

Link (1992)  also characterizes stimulus intensity during a unit of time as a Poisson distributed amplitude. Viewed over units of time these amplitudes form a Poisson waveform with mean amplitude

Variability is not due to error. Variability is an inherent part of the Poisson characterization of neural signals.

A comparative judgment between two stimuli begins by subtracting a value obtained from one Poisson waveform from another waveform during a unit of time. These differences are not used singly but are summed until one or the other of two response thresholds is first exceeded. This sequential theory of comparative judgment ( Link, 1975; Link & Heath, 1975 ) provides the original calculations for the class of such comparative difference random walk models with general assumptions about the underlying waveforms. The Poisson waveform characterization of stimulus intensity used by  Link (1992, 2010, 2020)  leads to more powerful, easily falsifiable, predictions as well as new predicted relations between measurable response variables, such as choice, response time ( Link, 1978a ) and confidence.

Predictions of response probabilities, response times and stimulus difference magnitudes depend on both the characteristics of the process and its parameters. First, the unbiased response thresholds that must be exceeded for a response to occur have values of A and –A. As shown in  Fig. 4  these response thresholds represent the amount of stimulus difference that must be accrued before a decision occurs. The parameter A is a measure of the observer’s resistance to respond “Greater”, in judging one stimulus to be of greater magnitude than the other. The threshold at –A is the amount of stimulus difference needed to respond “Lesser”.

Apriori information concerning the response to be made may bias the decision process toward one or the other response threshold. Setting the value of this bias equal to B, in units of stimulus difference, permits its representation on the axis of accumulated comparative difference. When the value of the bias parameter is unequal to zero the amount of positive stimulus difference needed to respond is reduced to (A-B). To make the alternative response the negative stimulus difference needed equals

By manipulating these two parameters, A and B, observers can respond to task demands imposed by the experimental conditions and produce many different forms of Receiver Operating Characteristics ( Link, 1975, 1992, 2020 ). Judging from the data in  Fig. 2  there is no response bias toward the upper or lower response choices. Thus, the bias parameter B can be set to zero equal for this experiment, simplifying the response proportion equations.

However, the interpretation of these probabilities is different in part because the measure of centrality is the geometric mean. From the standpoint of traditional psychophysical theory, this observation provides an explanation for the often observed “constant error”. Plotting the marginal response proportion from a paired comparison experiment as a function of the physical stimulus values creates a psychometric function. Gaussian psychophysical theory would assume the response proportion of 0.50 to occur at the arithmetic mean of the stimuli. But, from Eq.  (11)  the value 0.50 occurs when a stimulus equal to the geometric mean is presented. The geometric mean is smaller than the arithmetic mean. Thus when the response proportions are plotted as a function of physical stimulus magnitude, the psychometric function would appear shifted toward negative values. Such a shift is often interpreted as indicative of a “constant error”. Given these theoretical results the appearance of a “constant error” is a necessary consequence of plotting the response proportions as a function of physical stimulus magnitudes.

To examine the ability of individual students averages of DMSD to predict individual student’s choices, a matrix of DMSD values similar to  Table 2  was constructed for each individual student. A remaining question concerns the ability of an average of these individual estimates to predict the response proportions in the paired comparison choice matrix for a group of subjects. If the predictions for the individual are accurate then the predictions for the group may also be accurate. Naturally, each student may contribute a different ordering of the stimuli leading to irregularities, such as intransitivity, in the observed response proportions. In this regard,  Narens (2002)  provides insightful examples of the difficulties encountered when averaging across students.

Having available the predicted paired comparison choice matrices for each student facilitates computing a predicted overall choice matrix. There are 20 students and 120 independent cells per matrix. By adding together the individual student matrices, cell by cell, the total number of predicted choices equals 2400, 120*20. The total number of incorrect predictions, that is deviations from the observed results, equals 108. On this basis, the probability of a correct prediction equals 0.955.

These many results suggest that directly measured stimulus differences provide a useful measure of the student’s impression of stimuli. This method provides a new tool for the prediction of binary choice behavior. Applications of this measurement technique to other types of comparative judgments such as in aesthetics, and its obvious extension to multidimensional scaling of DMSDs, provide for a deeper understanding of mental mechanisms and the stimulus representations on which much behavior is based.

The fact that student-produced line lengths can be used to predict response probabilities for the choice between two stimuli establishes a previously unsuspected link between the direct scaling methods of Stevens and the comparative difference ideas due to Fechner and Thurstone. The Poisson basis for paired comparisons using the Wave Theory ideas advanced by  Link (1992, 2020) , extended the sequential comparative difference model ( Link, 1975; Link & Heath, 1975 ), and advanced Fechner’s amazing theory of subjective choice. Now the Poisson based theory suggests using stimulus difference, as measured by observer-generated horizontal line lengths, as the basis for choices between stimuli. The obtained correlation of 0.99 with 99% of the variance accounted for by the linear relation between estimates of process parameters and directly measured DMSD’s opens the door for many new psychophysical investigations into our deeper mental processes. Direct measures of stimulus difference have a theoretical basis that permits prediction of individual choice behavior. Fechner and Stevens are united.
