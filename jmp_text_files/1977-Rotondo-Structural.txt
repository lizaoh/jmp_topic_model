JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 16, 95-120 (1977)  Discrete Structural Models of Organization in Free Recall  JOHN ANDREW ROTONDO  Department of Psychology, University of Virginia, Charlottesville, Virginia 22901  A method is developed for expressing and testing organizational hypotheses which account for patterns of co-occurrence among  to-be-recalled items. Each organizational hypothesis may be represented as a system of structural equations. A model defined  by such a system reflects a hypothetical organization of the items into higher-order memory units or "chunks." One set of  model parameters characterizes the internal structure of each chunk. Another set characterizes the processing of each chunk as  a molar unit. An iterative procedure is described for obtaining maximum likelihood estimates of model parameters and a test of  the model's goodness of fit.  In a critical review of organization theory Postman (1972) concluded that the identification of functional units of memory is  one of the organizational theorists most pressing problems. The present research seeks to identify and characterize normative  functional units using a comparative model-fitting approach. Unlike earlier work in the category clustering tradition, which  focuses upon the order in which items are recalled, the models developed here attempt to account for patterns of co-occurrence  among the to-be-recalled items. Thus, the choice of a dependent variable for identifying functional units is shifted from the  order properties of recall to the covariance structure of the set of to-be-recalled items. Items which have a tendency to  co-occur in recall and exhibit positive covariation of a certain form will be identified as a functional unit or chunk.  EXPRESSING ORGANIZATIONAL HYPOTHESES WITH DISCRETE STRUCTURAL EQUATION SYSTEMS  The structural models to be presented exhibit hypothetical properties of chunks which cannot be detected through an analysis  of recall order but which are well known to organizational theorists. Foremost among these properties is the tendency of items  within a chunk to occur in an all-or-none manner. The strength of this tendency appears to vary considerably with the nature  of the encoding task. From an inspection of recall patterns from categorized lists Cohen (1966) concluded that items belonging  to the same category are recalled in a some-or-none fashion, i.e., the items have a weak tendency to co-occur. In the  binary-octal recoding experiments described by Miller (1956) the recovery of three binary digits from a one-digit octal  representation occurs in an allor-none manner. The tendency of items within a chunk to co-occur is a property that may be  placed on a continuum which ranges from no tendency to co-occur to perfect  95  Copyright C 1977 by Academic Press, Inc. In the models to be presented chunks may exhibit any one of a continuous range of behaviors from some-or-none  to most-or-none to all-or-none, depending upon the value of several chunk parameters. The models also allow that an item may  be a member of more than one chunk at the same time and that the probability of an item's recall increases with the number of  chunks in which it is a member.  Each chunk is characterized by a model parameter called chunk strength which reflects the probability with which the chunk is  recovered as a whole. Each item is characterized by a set of model parameters called membership strengths. Membership strength  reflects the probability that an item is recovered from a chunk given that the chunk itself has been recovered. There is one  membership strength parameter for each chunk in which the item is a member. It is possible to obtain maximum likelihood  estimates of the chunk strength and membership strength parameters and a test of the model's goodness of fit. The marginal  probabilities of item recall and inter-item correlations may be estimated as functions of chunk strength and membership  strength. Thus the models attempt to account for both the observed frequency of item recall and the frequency of co-occurrence  in recall.  The conceptual advantages of this approach to the study of organized recall must be weighed against two practical  disadvantages inherent in the current formulations of the models, estimation and testing procedures. Presently, the models  incorporate no individual difference parameters and require relatively large sample sizes to ensure stable parameter estimates  and sufficiently powerful test procedures. COMPARISON WITH OTHER METHODS FOR TESTING ORGANIZATIONAL HYPOTHESES  In this section the use of discrete structural equation systems (DSES) to test organizational hypotheses will be contrasted  with the use of category clustering measures. Measures of inter-trial consistency of item recall order such as SO (Tulving,  1962) and ITR (Bousfield & Bousfield, 1966) have been excluded from the comparison for two reasons. First they are primarily  used for the detection of idiosyncratic organization whereas DSES and category clustering methods are used for the detection  of normative organization. Second, measures of inter-trial consistency in recall order fail to address the major problem for  which the other methods were developed; the identification of normative functional memory units. Large values of SO or ITR  indicate that there is some stable organizational structure present in recall but do not identify the functional units  responsible for the large observed values or the composition of those units in terms of the to-be-remembered items. What makes  these measures flexible enough to detect idiosyncratic organizations is also what makes them unsatisfactory analytic devices  for solving the identification problem when the basis of organization is normative.  All measures of category clustering are based on a principle of adjacency (Johnson, 1970). The principle states that items  belonging to the same functional unit or chunk will have a tendency to occur adjacently in the item recall order. Most  category clustering measures are functions which increase with total number of repetitions, the number of times an item  belonging to a category or hypothetical chunk is followed by another item belonging to the same category or chunk, e.g., RR  (Bousfield, 1953), O-E (Bousfield & Bousfield, 1966), MRR (Bower, Lesgold, & Tieman, 1969), and ARC (Roenker, Thompson, &  Bown, 1971). Another approach is that of Frankel and Cole's (1971) whose measure, Z, is a function of the observed number of  runs, i.e., the total number of sequences of items belonging to the same category. They define clustering as the presence of  significantly too few runs.  There are, however, several theoretically important situations in which adjacency breaks down as a valid principle for  inferring the existence or identity of functional memory units. The first of these situations is when output order follows  FREE RECALL 103  In this case any clustering can be alternatively explained by the principle of contiguity and without invoking the existence  of functional units or chunks. Neither can the alternative explanation of clustering by a contiguity argument be eliminated  when input order is unknown or unobservably under the subject's control since in these cases it is unknown whether or not  output order follows input order. Functional units successfully modeled by DSES are not subject to an alternative explanation  by a contiguity argument even if output order follows input order or input order is unknown. This is due to the fact that the  fit of a DSES model is independent of item recall order and that contiguity does not predict the specific inter-item  covariance structure necessary to confirm a DSES organizational hypothesis.  The second situation in which the principle of adjacency breaks down occurs when one or more items are members,  simultaneously, of more than one chunk. It is easy to construct examples of chunk structures in which it is impossible to  recall items belonging to the same chunk next to one another in output order. Consider the hypothetical chunk structure in  Fig. 2a. If the items belonging to chunk 1 (professions) are recalled adjacently then it is impossible to recall the items of  chunks 2, 3, and 4 (the associative pairs) adjacently. Conversely, if the items belonging to each of the associative pairs are  recalled adjacently then it is impossible to recall items in the professions category adjacently. More troublesome than the  foregoing situation is one in which it is possible for items belonging to different chunks to be recalled adjacently, giving  the appearance of a single chunk. Consider the hypothetical chunk structure in Fig. 2b. There are several possible ways to  recall these items which would satisfy the principle of adjacency for both chunks, e.g., plumber jockey doctor medicine  hospital. However, a clustering measure which was computed on the assumption that these five items  PROFESSIONS  ASSOCIATIVE UNITS  Chunk 2 Chunk 3 Chunk a  Chunk 1  FIG. 2. DSES models are not subject to these limitations since their fit is independent of item recall order and since  single chunk and multiple overlapping chunk models require different observed inter-item covariance structures for model  confirmation.  Testing organizational hypotheses with DSES models has several additional advantages. When a model fits well the experimenter  gets a clear picture of the internal structure of each chunk through the parametric values of the membership strengths and  chunk strengths. Unlike clustering methods a DSES organizational model is the "null" hypothesis instead of the alternative.  Thus the experimenter can directly assess a model's goodness of fit and can compare the fit of several models. In clustering  analyses the null hypothesis is that items belonging to the same category or chunk occur adjacently at the chance level. When  a clustering measure attains a value large enough to permit rejection of chance level clustering one is still left with the  question of exactly which member of the composite set of alternatives to the null hypothesis was responsible for the  rejection. Hence it could be argued, from a statistical point of view, that normative functional units are not unambiguously  identified by clustering analyses.  There are five principle disadvantages in using DSES to test organizational hypotheses. First, relatively large sample sizes  are required. Owing to the fact that the number of possible response patterns increases dramatically with the number of  to-be-recalled items (2a response patterns for q items) large numbers of observations are required to obtain stable estimates  of the probability with which a particular pattern occurs. Second, the models will not fit if organization is idiosyncratic.  Third, the experimenter must have some plausible models in mind before using the method since the testing is a confirmatory  procedure rather than an exploratory one. Fourth, the models must be made mathematically identifiable which can often be a  difficult task, although the simpler models are readily identified. Fifth, ceiling and floor effects must be avoided. If an  item is recalled by every subject or no subjects its estimated variance will be zero making meaningful estimation of its  covariance with other items impossible. A program developed by Rotondo and Pankey (1975) performs the estimation of all model parameters and tests the model's  goodness of fit. Once a DSES organizational model is specified it can be input to the program in the form of a design matrix  in which the user specifies fixed free and constrained parameters. Output includes:  (1) the observed response patterns and their frequency of occurrence;  FREE RECALL 109  (2) Bayes estimates of the frequency of each response pattern (a smoothing procedure which replaces observed frequencies of  zero with positive values);  (3) estimates of the Chi's and P,'s, the membership strengths and chunk strengths, respectively;  (4) the estimated marginal expectations of the to-be-recalled items under w, i.e., the model, and under 0;  (5) the marginal residuals (estimated marginals under d2 minus estimated marginals under w);  (6) the estimated correlation matrix of the items under w and under 0;  (7) the item correlation residuals (the item correlation matrix under 0 minus the correlation matrix under w);  (8) the estimated probability of each response pattern under w and S2;  (9) the value of the X2 goodness-of-fit test, degrees of freedom, and observed significance level.  The smoothing procedure in (2) assumes a conservative prior distribution in which all response patterns have equal  probability. The observed frequencies of the response patterns are then used to modify the prior distribution. The modified  frequencies which result are input to the estimation and testing routines of the program. In this way the chance of serious  over- or underestimation of the probabilities of response patterns is reduced. The properties of such procedures are discussed  in Bishop, Feinberg, & Holland (1975). Discussion of Experiment I  It is clear that for a sample as "small" as the one used in Experiment I the distribution of the test statistic is still far  from its asymptotic distribution when the model is true. Thus the reported p values cannot be used to draw firm conclusions  about a model's absolute fit. Despite these problems the pattern of results preserves the utility of the notion that recall in  categorized lists is a two-level process consisting of the retrieval of a chunk followed by independent retrieval of items  from the retrieved chunk. Judging by its performance in this initial test the single chunk model seems to merit further  investigation. It is also consistent with Slamecka's (1968) theory that items are stored independently along with a general  representation of the categorical structure of the list chunks arising because different parts of the retrieval plan affect  different subsets of items as wholes. Items in the same category are linked to a common superordinate unit or plan rather than  directly to each other. This view is inconsistent with an  114 JOHN ANDREW ROTONDO  associative growth hypothesis which identifies learning with the development and strengthening of inter-item associative  bonds. There is also evidence that chunks are retrieved independently of each other. Discussion of Experiment II  The results indicate that each block is functioning as an organization unit and that blocks are retrieved independently of  each other. However, the current lack of a satisfactory small sample testing procedure permits only tentative conclusions  about the internal structure of each block. It appears likely that the structural form of organization differs from block to  block. In some blocks subjects may be sensitive to the overlapping associative structure and use it as the basis of  organization. In other blocks items may be treated as though they were members of the same taxonomic category and exhibit a  single chunk structure. It is also possible that organizational structures different from either the single or overlapping  chunk models are being used or that there are large individual differences in the way a particular block is organized.  Adequate assessment of goodness of fit is a problem that will require further investigation. However, the conceptual  advantages of the present approach may make such  118 JOAN ANDREW ROTONDO  investigation worthwhile. Any final conclusions about the adequacy of these models will have to await the results of large  sample studies of the development of suitable small sample testing procedures.
