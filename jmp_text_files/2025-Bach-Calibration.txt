Experiment-based calibration is a technique for validation of measurement instruments. The conceptual idea is to evaluate measurement accuracy by comparing the outcome of a measurement method or instrument against a  standard , defined as the predicted outcome of a well-defined experiment. This is a canonical technique in the physical sciences ( Phillips, Estler, Doiron, Eberhardt, & Levenson, 2001 ). For example, the measurement of physical length can be evaluated against a standard based on the speed of light generated by a helium-neon laser.

Recently, a similar approach has been introduced to validate the measurement of latent variables in experimental psychology, such as memory, attention, or perception ( Bach, Melinscak, Fleming, & Voelkle, 2020; Bach, Rigdon, & Sarstedt, 2025; Bach et al., 2023 ). For example, the measurement of perceived lightness can be evaluated against standard values derived from physical luminance together with a formal theory of lightness perception (e.g., the CIECAM02 model).

Experiment-based calibration was introduced to physical quantities in the mid-20th century, long after the laws of physics were already well understood. In consequence, there is little doubt in the physical sciences whether, and how, a calibration experiment generates the quantity that one would like to measure (i.e., the  measurand ). For example, there is no uncertainty that the helium-neon laser generates light that travels with an invariable speed, and the predicted outcome of an experiment can be very accurately computed. In contrast, uncertainty about the effect of an experimental manipulation is likely higher in psychology and social sciences. Sometimes, we can be quite sure that an experiment impacts on the measurand. Physical luminance of an object influences perceived lightness, and the CIECAM02 model is a very good quantitative model of this impact–but even this model does not numerically capture all aspects, such as the Helmholtz–Kohlrausch effect ( High, Green, & Nussbaum, 2023 ). In other instances, the relation might be even quite loose or speculative. For example, when manipulating the number of practice trials in a learning task, this will on average affect resulting memory, but the numerical relation of trial number to memory cannot be precisely specified.

This raises two key questions. (1) If the relation between experimental manipulation and the latent variable is quite loose, can calibration still yield information on measurement accuracy? (2) If yes, and if several latent variables are loosely impacted by the experimental manipulation, then how can we be sure which one we are actually measuring? For example, changing the luminance of an object changes its perceived lightness but might also affect its aesthetic perception, which we can think of as an “experimental confound”. How can we ensure, in a calibration experiment, that we are evaluating the measurement of lightness perception and not of aesthetic value?

As shown in the next section, retrodictive validity depends not only on measurement error but also on experimental aberration. In psychological experiments – in contrast to calibration in physics – experimental aberration will presumably depend on the specific sample and experimental minutiae in ways that are often not understood in detail. Thus, it might differ between two similar calibration experiments. Hence, previous work has proposed to pitch two measurement methods against each other  within the same experiment  ( Bach et al., 2020 ). In this case, experimental aberration is constant for all tested measurement methods. This is particularly simple when different measurement methods consist of different data transformations, applied to the same  observables  (e.g., reaction times). However, there are also many applications in which different observables can be acquired simultaneously for comparison. Examples are key presses for which response time and response force can be observed simultaneously ( Stahl & Rammsayer, 2005 ), or the simultaneous recording of skin conductance and electrocardiogram ( Mancinelli et al., 2024 ).

While aberration is constant for all measurement methods calibrated simultaneously, retrodictive validity depends also on the covariance of aberration and measurement error, which is different between methods. This is why previous work has analysed the covariance conditions under which retrodictive validity is informative on measurement error ( Bach et al., 2020 ). In result, if measurement error is uncorrelated with experimental aberration, then a method with higher retrodictive validity is guaranteed to have smaller measurement error variance. The previous sections defined a calibration space as a tupel of a probability space and a set of real-valued random variables with minimal constraints, and analysed their interrelation. Researchers will often be interested in modelling or simulating how these variables come about ( Bach, 2023 ). The goal of this section is to analyse how data-generating relations can be embedded in the calibration concept, and under which conditions they result in a standardised calibration space. Throughout, we assume orthogonality, noting that we can always construct a non-orthogonal calibration space from an orthogonal one and vice versa.

This generative calibration model is based on two key ideas. The first is to cast the data-generating relation as a three-stage probabilistic process with a starting distribution for

To render the concept applicable in practice, the second key idea is to separate systematic bias from random imprecision (see  Bach, 2023; Bach et al., 2020 ). Thus, we specify deterministic non-linearities for systematic aberration and error, and conditional probability distributions for their random components. This allows formulating a more intuitive and still quite general calibration model, together with sufficient conditions that are easy to verify.

Experiment-based calibration is a relatively novel approach to psychometrics in experimental fields ( Bach et al., 2020 ), with several examples of practical application ( Bach & Melinscak, 2020; Bach et al., 2023 ) and simulations ( Bach, 2023 ). The current work generalises and expands the previous work into several directions and thus contributes to laying a rigorous groundwork for this research program. First, I show that previously derived boundaries under which retrodictive validity can be informative on measurement accuracy hold for any distribution of the experimental standard values. Second, I show that the same boundaries apply in the assessment of convergent validity in classical psychometrics. Third, I dissect the role of confounding variables, i.e. variables that covary with the latent variable of interest. When a single method is analysed, if this method relates more closely to latent variable  
                         
                            
                               T 
                            
                            
                               2 
                            
                         
                       than to latent variable  
                         
                            
                               T 
                            
                            
                               1 
                            
                         
                      , then  
                         
                            
                               T 
                            
                            
                               2 
                            
                         
                       will have a less close relationship to the standard values. When multiple methods are compared under ideal conditions, then calibration will converge on the latent variable that has the closer relationship to the standard values. This underlines the importance of the experimental standard. Finally, I lay out a generative calibration model and derive identities that can be used for analytical and numerical modelling and simulations ( Bach, 2023 ).

Experiment-based calibration requires that substantive theory allows predicting standard values in an experiment, and is thus suitable for consolidated research areas. This reflects the approach of theory-based measurement, whereby any measurement of latent variables should be motivated and defined via substantive theory rather than by the measurement instrument (including its latent variable model) ( Borgstede & Eggert, 2023 ).

Interestingly, calibration can also help solving a problem well-known in measurement theory: that there is an (unknown) non-linear mapping from the latent variable to the measured score. Such non-linearity renders, for example, interpretation of non-crossover interaction effects in linear models ambiguous: what appears as an interaction at the level of the measured score might have been caused by a non-linear mapping from latent to measured score, when in fact there is no interaction at the level of the latent variable ( Loftus, 1978; Wagenmakers, Krypotos, Criss, & Iverson, 2012 ). While statistical techniques have been developed to identify such interactions ( Dunn & Anderson, 2024 ), calibration could aid solving the problem in a more fundamental manner. Non-linearities in the mapping from latent to measured score reduce trueness of the measurement — and thereby, retrodictive validity. If this non-linearity were known, it could be embedded in the measurement model, thus rendering the mapping from latent to the resulting measured score linear. This would, in turn, increase retrodictive validity. In consequence, including various candidate non-linearities into the measurement model and selecting the one with highest retrodictive validity will, in the limit, minimise non-linearities. As one result, this will improve the interpretation of interactions in substantive experiment using this measurement method.

As any tool, experiment-based calibration comes with limitations. Most notable is the orthogonality assumption, i.e. that aberration and error are uncorrelated ( Bach et al., 2020 ). In the present work, I show that violations of this assumptions correspond to a mis-specified substantive theory. As another limitation, calibration does not cleanly separate trueness and precision. Previous work has identified surrogate metrics that can be used for this purpose ( Bach et al., 2020 ), and alternatively, calibration can be complemented with test-retest reliability assessment, which directly assesses precision ( McDonald, 2013 ). Ultimately, it might be possible to derive clear metrics of uncertainty on psychological measurement ( Rigdon & Sarstedt, 2022; Rigdon, Sarstedt, & Becker, 2020 ). With the present work, I hope to aid the modelling work that will put this approach into practice.
