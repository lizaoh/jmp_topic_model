BADGIO  University of Pennsylvania  AND  YAACOV RITOV  Hebrew University of Jerusalem  To demonstrate the feasibility of defining impulsiveness (or its opposite, overcautiousness) as a departure from an optimum  amount of time, we gave subjects a specified number of points (e.g., 100) for a correct answer to an anagram problem, a  specified cost per second for working on each problem (e.g., 4 points), and a specified probability that each problem was  insoluble (e.g., 0.25). At any point in time after each problem was presented, the subject could give up working on it. To  find the optimum stopping point, we fit a curve representing the probability of solving an as-yet-unsolved, soluble problem as  a function of time spent working on the problem. From this curve, under certain assumptions, we could calculate (for each  condition) each subject's optimum stopping point, the time at which the subject should give up. Subjects' optimum stopping  points, calculated in this way, dit not correlate with their actual mean stopping points (for giving up without a solution).  Although subjects spent more time than their respective optima in some conditions, they were insensitive to increases in the  optimum that resulted from decreases in the probability of the problem being insoluble, decreased cost of time, or increased  payoff resulting from repetition of a problem later under conditions of high payoff and high cost. Hence, in these conditions  with longer optima, subjects tended to spend too little time. Subjects' stopping points did not approach their respective  optima over trials, but subjects were sensitive to false feedback about whether problems were insoluble when they gave up.  Individual differences in mean stopping point were predicted by ability to solve problems in the first few seconds but not by  ability to continue to solve problems with further effort after the first few seconds. Individual differences in departures  from optimum were not related to individual differences in confidence.  ;i, 1991 Academic Press, Inc. Baron, Badgio, and Gaskins  (1986) have pointed out that individuals may differ in the position on this function that is optimal, given each person's  costs and benefits of spending additional time. Some people who appear to be impulsive by the traditional measure might be  near their optimum: for example, extra time might not do them any good. Baron et al. have urged that impulsiveness (as  distinct from impulsivity, the traditional measure) be defined as giving up (or guessing) before spending the optimum amount  of time. In principle, impulsiveness could be ascertained for a single subject in a single task, although comparison of  individual subjects to their respective optima could be used to study individual differences as well.  As an example of the distinction between definitions in terms of speed-accuracy tradeoff and in terms of optimal stopping,  consider the finding (Baron et al., 1986) that training children to "take time to think" increased the time they spent on  arithmetic, logic, and perceptual matching problems (before giving up or guessing), but the training increased the accuracy of  only those children who had high IQs or who were judged by their teachers to be highly impulsive. By our definition in terms  of optimal performance, those children who slowed down but did not improve in accuracy cannot be said to have been impulsive  at the outset, regardless of how fast and inaccurate they were. They were not impulsive because they did not benefit from  spending extra time.  Of course, problem solving is only part of a broader class of situations involving optimal stopping, including speeded  classification (Edwards, 1965) and waiting in queues (Wing, 1977). However, problem-solving is unique because of the  possibility that premature stopping can impair both academic performance and learning (Baron et al., 1986). Moreover, the  uncertain event of interest-a solution to the problem-is perceived as highly dependent on the subject's ability at the task.  In the present paper, we examine departures from optimal stopping in an anagram task. Anagrams are convenient, but they are  also like many other problems in that they require the use of knowledge (i.e., spelling) in a novel way. Performance at  solving anagram correlates with other measures of ability (Gavurin, 1967).  In order to determine an optimum stopping point, we need to manipulate (or measure) the costs and benefits of the time spent  working on a problem. We do this here by giving subjects explicit information about the number of points to be gained by  solving a problem correctly, the number of points to be lost per second spent working on the problem, and the probability that  the problem actually has a solution. Given this information, we estimate an optimum stopping point for each subject in each  experimental condition, on the assumption that subjects follow instructions to try to maximize average points per problem. We  examine the determinants of deviations from this optimum. If we assume that much of the error in our estimated optimum is  constant across conditions and subjects, then our conclusions about differences among conditions and subjects may be taken more  OPTIMAL STOPPING IN ANAGRAMS 43  seriously than our conclusions about whether subjects are impulsive or not in a given condition.  On each trial of our experiments, the subject is presented with a single five-letter anagram. He can work on it as long as he  likes, or give up at any time. He is told in advance that there is a certain probability, e.g., 0.25, that the anagram will  have no solution. A changing display informs him of the time he has spent so far and the payoffs he might receive. He pays a  cost for each second of working, e.g., two points. If there is an answer, and if he finds the answer, he earns some payoff,  e.g., 100 points. He does not get the payoff for giving up when a problem is insoluble; this is like many real-world problems,  where the only benefit of giving up on an insoluble problem is in saving time. He is told to maximize his average points per  trial, and he is told what this average is after each trial (excluding the practice trials).  We turn now to the potential determinants of departures from optimal stopping that we consider in these experiments. (Baron et  al., 1986, and Baron, 1985, Chap. 4, provide a more thorough review of the possible determinants of deviations from optimal  stopping.) For simplicity, we discuss these determinants for the case of premature stopping or impulsiveness rather than  overcautiousness; however, both deviations from the optimum may occur.  One possible determinant is overconfidence that the problem is insoluble, after some time is spent on it without its being  solved. In the extreme, if the subject is certain that the problem has no answer, he will stop regardless of the payoffs. If  his confidence is too high, he will stop too soon. We assess overconfidence in most of these experiments by asking the  subjects to state the probability that the problem is insoluble at the time they give up. A subject is overconfident to the  extent that the mean value of this subjective probability is higher that an objectively estimated probability that the problem  is insoluble at the time the subject gives up. In one experiment, we manipulate overconfidence by giving false feedback,  telling the subjects that the problem is insoluble whenever they give up.  A second possible determinant of impulsiveness is that the subjective probability of success in the near future is lower than  the objectively estimated value. We call this low efficacy. Note that overconfidence concerns the work that has already been  done on a problem, and low efficacy concerns the work to be done if the subject continues. Thus, it is possible for a person  to have both overconfidence and low efficacy. For example, he might believe that he is very good at solving an anagram in the  first few seconds; if he does not get it by then, it is probably insoluble, but even if it is soluble, it is beyond his  ability to solve. We assess efficacy in some experiments by asking the subject, when he gives up, to state the probability  that he could solve the problem in 10 more seconds if it were soluble.  Overconfidence or low efficacy may result from a subject's mistaken beliefs about how his probability of solving a problem  depends on the time spent so far, his "subjective hazard function." High confidence, and hence overconfidence, may be produced  by a belief that success (in solving a soluble problem) is likely in the first few seconds. Thus, if the subject has not  solved a problem in a few seconds, he  44 BARON, BADGIO, AND RITOV  believes that it is insoluble, since, if it were soluble, he would have solved it. Low efficacy may be caused by a belief that  the probability of success falls off quickly with time, so that solutions become essentially impossible after a few seconds  even if the problem is soluble.  Alternatively, subjects might be simply insensitive to their ability, as suggested by Ferrell and McGoey (1980) for other  types of tasks. This would result in subjects' actual stopping points being uncorrelated with their optimal stopping points.  A third possible determinant of impulsiveness is underweighing the value of success. In the real world, this seems to occur  most often when the effect of success is delayed (Ainslie, 1975). Consider a student who solves problems while studying for an  examination, with the expectation that the problems might appear on the exam. These problems have a clear immediate cost  (e.g., the lost opportunity to watch TV), but their payoff is in the future (on the exam and beyond). In two of the present  experiments, we mimic this situation by telling subjects (truthfully) that some problems will be repeated (only) five trials  hence under conditions of heavy time pressure (high cost) and that solving the problems the first time they are presented  essentially guarantees that they will be solved quickly the second time as well. If subjects are sensitive to future value,  they will spend longer on these problems than they otherwise would spend before giving up.  METHOD: ALL EXPERIMENTS  Subjects  Subjects were recruited by a small sign on a major campus walkway, which directed them to our laboratory. When they arrived,  if a computer was available, they were asked whether they wanted to do a problem-solving experiment with anagrams. If they did  not, other experiments were available. Most subjects were university students, about evenly divided by sex.  Stimuli  There were two types of stimuli, soluble anagrams and insoluble ones. All had five letters and were presented with the letters  in alphabetical order. (This created a wide range of difficulties, and therefore the conditions for the model described in the  appendix were favorable.) The soluble anagrams had only one solution, according to the tables of Olson and Schwartz (1967).  All of these were familiar English words, in the judgment of J. B., and they included no plurals, no third-person singular  verbs formed by adding -s to another word, and no proper nouns. The items with mean percent correct (for subjects tested in  these experiments and others) below 10% were aaflt, cliry, innou, eomrw, acenp, orsuy, finuy, imopu, and beruy. The items with  mean 100% correct scores for the sample were agrvy, ejlly, ehllo, iorvy, emssy, ittwy, ejnoy, ahppy, and ennpy.' The insoluble  anagrams were actually soluble anagrams drawn from the same list, but the words were sufficiently rare as to be completely  unknown to J. B. (and often to the makers of frequency tables as well): Only a couple of subjects (out of hundreds run with  these stimuli)  OPTIMAL STOPPING IN ANAGRAMS 45  noticed any solutions (and no subject noticed more than one), and these trials were omitted from all analyses. (The use of  these items avoided the difficulty of determining that an anagram was truly insoluble.) For all stimuli, items with unusual  combinations of letters (e.g., four vowels) were avoided, as were items with only single vowels, so far as possible. An  attempt was made to match soluble and insoluble problems on such factors as number of vowels and presence of unusual letters  (such as X or Q), factors that might influence a subject's judgment of how likely a problem was to be soluble. Stimuli were  presented in a different random order for each subject, except in Experiment 1, where the same random order was used for all  subjects.  Procedure  Each trial began with a display on a computer screen, which indicated the conditions that held for the ensuing problem. The  top line said "cost per second = C," where C is the cost per second, in points (e.g., 5). The next line said, "probability of  being insoluble = U," where U is the probability of insolubility (e.g., 0.25). The next two lines read: "P points for correct  answer" and "0 points for incorrect answer," respectively. Quantities (C, U, and/or P) that changed from trial to trial were  highlighted so that the subject would attend to them.  When the subject pushed "return," the anagram was presented below this display. The display remained on the screen during the  trial, and the displayed numbers of points received for correct and incorrect answers were decremented by C points every  second after the anagram was presented. Thus, if C = 5 and if these values started out at 100 and 0, after 1 second they would  read 95 - 5, then 90 -10, and so on. This display called attention to the fact that one could always obtain P more points by  solving the problem than by giving up, even if one would lose points either way. This display was close enough to the anagram  so that the subject could be aware of its changes while looking at the anagram.  To end the trial, the subject pushed the "return" key, if she thought she knew the answer, or the "?" key, if she did not  think so. If the subject pressed "return," she would then be prompted to type in the answer (followed by the return key).  Subjects were shown how to correct typing errors, and if the subject typed an answer that was not five letters long, the  prompt would recur. Subjects were instructed to type "xxxxx" when they realized that their initial answer was incorrect.  When the subject typed the ? key, the computer would ask one or two questions about confidence before going on. First, the  subject was asked "% confidence in no ans." This meant that the subject should type in her percent confidence that the problem  was actually insoluble. The second question (asked only in Experiments 1 and 2) was "% conf. solvable in 10 sec. if ans." This  meant that the subject should type in her confidence that she could solve the problem in 10 more seconds, if it  ' The answers to the difficult items are: fatal, lyric, union, mower, pecan, yours, unify, opium, and buyer.  46  BARON, BADGIO, AND RITOV  were soluble. Next, the display presented the answer (or "no answer," if there was none) and the number of points won or lost on that trial.  Following the first 20 (practice) trials, the display also provided the average of the number of points earned per trial so  far. This encouraged subjects to be concerned with their averages rather than their totals. (At the end of the experiment,  most subjects were quite interested in how their averages compared with others'.) The subject could terminate the entire  display and move to the next problem by pressing "return" again.  Subjects were instructed carefully, typically for about 10 min. The experimenter sat next to the subject while she did several  trials, and he made sure she understood the sequence of conditions, the sequence of operations to be done on each trial, the  nature of the stimuli, and the point system. He questioned her if her confidence ratings were as low as the stated probability  of problems being insoluble. He emphasized the importance of considering the average number of points rather than the total,  the importance of minimizing losses as well as maximizing gains, the fact that no points were given for correctly indicating  that a problem was insoluble, and the importance of pressing "?" rather than "return" when no answer was found.' THE MODEL: PARAMETER DEFINITION AND ESTIMATION  Our main analyses concern the deviation from optimality in the time that each subject spends before giving up; his stopping  point. We derive a normative model under the following assumptions:  OPTIMAL STOPPING IN ANAGRAMS 49  1. The time to solve a soluble anagram is a random variable distributed with density f(t) and distribution function F(t). We  shall not estimate F(t) directly, but rather the hazard function h(t)= f(t)/[1 -F(t)], the rate (probability per second) of  solving soluble problems not yet solved.  2. h(t) does not depend on the proportion of insoluble anagrams, U, the payoff for a correct answer, P, or the cost per  second, C. It is assumed that h(t) declines so fast that defective probability distributions are produced. This is consistent  with the fact that some problems cannot be solved by the subject.  3. No additional information about a problem's solubility or difficulty accumulates during the solution process; a problem is  known to be soluble only when it is solved. This assumption will be checked by comparing performance on soluble and insoluble  problems.  4. Each subject sets (on the basis of the practice trials) a policy to stop at a certain Deadline D if a solution has not yet  been found. Although subjects may not consciously set policies to stop at a certain time, they effectively establish such  policies through the precedents they set for themselves.  5. Each subject's policy is to stop at the subject's mean stopping point (Dm) in a given condition. Actual subjects, of  course, would not adhere to their policies exactly, so there would be some variability around this mean. In Experiment 3, we manipulate  beliefs about ability to do well in the first few seconds, and persistence changes accordingly. When the manipulation is  removed, performance returns to a level unaffected by the manipulation, indicating that subjects continue to monitor their  performance. Adherence to this rule of behavior makes subjects insensitive to feedback from actual payoffs. If this rule is  followed more generally, it could work against the development of expertise in areas in which an individual is strong.  Arguably, to develop expertise, a person should persevere the most in just those areas in which she performs best, and her  best indication of performance is how well she does early on in each unit of the task. The rule that people used here seems to  work in the opposite direction.
