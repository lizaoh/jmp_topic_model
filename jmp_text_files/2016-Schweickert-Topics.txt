Multinomial processing trees are successful models of many cognitive phenomena. Topics include perception ( Ashby, Prinzmetal, Ivry, & Maddox, 1996 ), memory ( Batchelder & Riefer, 1986; Chechile & Meyer, 1976; Xi, 2014 ), and social cognition ( Klauer & Wegner, 1998 ). For reviews, see  Batchelder and Riefer (1999)  and  Erdfelder et al. (2009) .

Typically, a tree model is proposed based on intuition and experience, and evaluated by its goodness of fit to data. Here we discuss an alternative, Tree Inference, for starting with data from a factorial experiment and constructing a suitable tree ( Schweickert & Chen, 2008; Schweickert & Han, in preparation; Schweickert & Xi, 2011 ). We demonstrate by constructing a multinomial processing tree for data on immediate serial recall by  Vitevitch, Chan, and Roodenrys (2012) . We then consider an issue that arises when constructing a tree by combining information about pairs of vertices with a procedure called the Transitive Orientation Algorithm. For those interested in technical details at this point, the uniqueness of a tree constructed from information about which pairs of vertices are on a path together and which are not depends on certain subsets of vertices called partitive sets (or modules). We show how the parts of a partitive set are arranged in a directed tree.

This paper has two distinct parts. In the first we show by example how results from a single experiment with two factors can determine the form of a multinomial processing tree accounting for the data. In the second we consider a technical question that arises when considering how information from several such experiments could be combined to form a single multinomial processing tree. More than one such tree may be possible.  Schweickert and Han (2016)  showed that more than one is possible if and only if there are two or more vertices in series, as we will explain here later. Vertices in series are a special case of sets of vertices called partitive sets (e.g.,   Golumbic, 1980 ), and we show here that the form of a partitive set is relatively simple.

The inquiry in the second part of the paper arises by noticing that trees have now been constructed from several experiments on immediate serial recall, each experiment manipulating two or three factors (see  Schweickert, Fisher, & Sung, 2012  for examples). Looking ahead, suppose results from small experiments accumulate to the point where we have data for every pair of processes involved in a task. For each pair, suppose at least one multinomial processing tree has been constructed, and we know whether the vertices representing the pair are ordered or unordered, the trees being consistent about this. It may be possible to represent the order information of the separate experiments in a single multinomial processing tree. If one is possible, it can be constructed with the Transitive Orientation Algorithm, a procedure developed in graph theory (see, e.g.,   Golumbic, 1980 ). Although the procedure is well known, questions remain. More than one tree may be possible. The possibilities are determined by certain sets of vertices called partitive sets. We explain when the tree is unique, and consider the form of partitive sets in a directed rooted tree. The two parts of the paper are about the best understood situations, when information is known about one pair of vertices, from one two factor experiment, and when it is known about all pairs, from many two factor experiments. The second part of the paper is unavoidably technical.

We turn now to explaining how a multinomial processing tree can be constructed in one of the well understood situations, through an experiment that manipulates two factors. As mentioned earlier, there are two ways two distinct vertices can be arranged in a multinomial processing tree. If one vertex precedes the other, the vertices are  ordered  (or  comparable ). If neither precedes the other, the vertices are  unordered  (or  incomparable ). The problem of distinguishing ordered and unordered processes in a multinomial processing tree is analogous to that of distinguishing serial and parallel processes ( Townsend, 1972 ); for reviews see  Logan (2002)  and  Townsend and Wenger (2004) .

We can discover how two vertices are arranged by finding experimental factors that selectively influence the processes the vertices represent.  Sternberg (1969)  pioneered the use of selectively influencing processes to analyze reaction times. Effects on accuracy of selectively influencing processes were investigated by  Schweickert (1985) ,  Jacoby (1991)  and  Hu (2001) . The difficult problem of selectively influencing processes that are stochastically interdependent was investigated by  Townsend (1984)  and  Dzhafarov (2003) ; see  Dzhafarov and Kujala (2014)  for recent discussion.

Consider a task performed by executing processes in a multinomial processing tree. Suppose changing the level of an experimental factor changes probability values associated with arcs descending from a single vertex, leaving all else invariant. The factor is said to  selectively influence  the process represented by the vertex. (Factors changing probabilities on arcs descending from more than one vertex are discussed by  Schweickert and Xi (2011) , but are beyond the scope of this paper.) If two factors selectively influence two different processes, each represented by a vertex, the data will satisfy certain conditions explained below (following Eqs.  (1) and (2)  in Section   2 ). The conditions differ depending on whether the vertices representing the two processes are ordered or unordered, and the conditions determine both the form of a multinomial processing tree and values of its parameters. There are only three possibilities. If the conditions required for ordered vertices are met, a multinomial processing tree of the form illustrated in  Fig. 1  exists, in which ordered vertices represent the processes selectively influenced by the factors. If the conditions required for unordered vertices are met, a multinomial processing tree of the form illustrated in  Fig. 2 
                       exists, in which unordered vertices represent the processes selectively influenced by the factors. If the conditions are not satisfied, no multinomial processing tree exists in which the factors selectively influence different processes, each represented by a vertex ( Schweickert & Chen, 2008 ).

When factors selectively influence processes, a psychological interpretation of the processes can often be based on what the experimental factors are; for example, it is reasonable to interpret manipulations of similarity as influencing a discrimination process. A variety of factors selectively influencing processes in trees are discussed by  Jacoby, Begg, and Toth (1997)  and  Schweickert et al. (2012) . It is possible for an investigator to start with only data and proceed directly to a multinomial processing tree. In practice, of course, data are usually supplemented with theoretical and empirical results from previous work.

In an immediate serial recall task, a subject is presented with a list of items and soon after presentation attempts to recall them in order. Typically, an item is scored as correct if it is recalled in its proper serial position. There is now considerable evidence supporting multinomial processing tree models for the task. For example,  Tse (2009)  found that serial position selectively influenced the probability the trace of a to-be-remembered item is intact and semantic relatedness selectively influenced the probability a degraded trace could be reconstructed, using a multinomial processing tree model of  Schweickert (1993) . More examples are in  Schweickert et al. (2012) .

Experiment 3 of  Vitevitch et al. (2012)  is about the effects of a phonological network measure on immediate serial recall of words. From a lexicon of words and their phonetic transcriptions (e.g.,   Luce & Pisoni, 1998 ), a phonological network can be made by representing each word with a vertex (a point) and connecting two words with an edge (an undirected line) if they differ by addition, deletion, or substitution of a single phoneme (e.g.,   Luce, Pisoni, & Goldinger, 1990 ;  Roodenrys, Hulme, Lethbridge, Hinton, & Nimmo, 2002 ;  Vitevitch, 2008 ). For example, vertices representing the words  bit  and  bet  are joined by an edge, while vertices representing  bit  and  gate  are not.

A word in the network can be described by its values on various network measures, and values of the measures are related to performance on various cognitive tasks. For reviews see  Roodenrys (2009)  and  Vitevitch (2008) . One important measure is the  degree  of a vertex, the number of vertices it is connected to, each by an edge (i.e., the number of its  neighbors ). In  Fig. 3

However,  Goh and Pisoni (2003)  found words from large neighborhoods to be recalled worse. These conflicting results are thoughtfully discussed by  Roodenrys (2009) . Among mechanisms discussed is the idea that there is stronger activation of a word with more neighbors but with more competition during redintegration. Redintegration is reconstruction of a degraded memory trace. The upshot, after consideration of many details, is “neighbors both support the presented word and compete with it in recall” (p. 186). We return to these opposing effects below.

Chan and Vitevitch (2009)  found that performance on lexical decision and word recognition was better for words with higher clustering coefficient.  Vitevitch et al., 2012  found that performance on long term and short term memory tasks is related to clustering coefficient values of the to-be-remembered words. Here we look at the short term memory data of their Experiment 3.

In the experiment, subjects were presented with lists of 6 words through headphones and asked to recall them aloud immediately in order. Subjects were instructed to say “pass” if a word in a particular position could not be remembered. In half the lists, all words had high clustering coefficients and in the other half all words had low clustering coefficients. Phonological neighbors were not allowed in the same list. The high and low clustering coefficient word pools were approximately equal on several lexical and network measures, including word frequency and degree (each measure considered separately). The two factors manipulated in the experiment were serial position and clustering coefficient.

In an Analysis of Variance,  Vitevitch et al. (2012)  found significantly more words recalled on average when the clustering coefficient was high. They also found a significant interaction between serial position and clustering coefficient, with larger effects of clustering coefficient toward the end of the list. They point out that at the first serial position somewhat more words were recalled when clustering coefficient was low than when high.

When discussing their results,  Vitevitch et al. (2012)  draw insight from a spreading-activation model of  Chan and Vitevitch (2009) . Briefly, representations of items in the midst of the list are more degraded than those at the beginning and end, and degraded representations can sometimes be redintegrated to yield correct responses ( Hulme et al., 1997; Schweickert, 1993 ). When a word is presented, it activates its own representation in the phonological network and also those of its neighbors (e.g.,   Roodenrys et al., 2002 ). Activation is circulated among the target word and its neighbors in a concentrated fashion when clustering is high, due to the many completed triangles. Activation is circulated diffusely when clustering is low ( Chan & Vitevitch, 2009 ). At recall, activation of a target word and its neighbors contributes to redintegration. Consequently, redintegration, and hence recall, is better with high clustering coefficient.

The natures of the factors selectively influencing the processes provide a start for explaining the results of fitting the tree. The process selectively influenced by the clustering coefficient operates on the phonological network; it must be a long term representation, because words not presented (i.e., neighbors of a presented word) have an effect on the process. It is commonly assumed that almost all information in long term memory is ordinarily inactive; so to be useful a long term memory representation must be activated. On the other hand, the process selectively influenced by serial position must be a short term representation, because serial position information is temporary.

For further insight, we draw on previous models. The book edited by  Thorn and Page (2009)  provides useful and extensive discussion of the interaction of long term and short term memory, although no model we are aware of predicts all details found by fitting the tree. We found the clustering coefficient to have two opposite effects, in line with a proposal by  Thorn, Frankish, and Gathercole (2009)  that long-term knowledge contributes to immediate recall in more than one way, initially, by affecting activation “across a network of phonological units” and also through redintegration (p. 213).

Roodenrys (2009, p. 191)  says “the more neighbors the [presented] word has the more activation the word will ultimately receive and the more likely a correct response”. This is provided “the remaining information uniquely specifies the correct word… the more neighbors a word has the more likely it is that a preserved fragment will not uniquely identify the word and so words with more neighbors will suffer from more neighbor intrusions”. For more discussion, see  Roodenrys et al. (2002) .

Looking ahead, questions arise. As results about more pairs of factors accumulate, can they be organized into a single multinomial processing tree? Would a large experiment with many factors be needed to discover the possible forms such a tree would take? What determines how many possible forms there are?

In the next part of the paper we describe a well known procedure, the Transitive Orientation Algorithm, for combining information about pairs of ordered and unordered entities to produce a single structure for all. Conditions are known under which a structure in the form of a multinomial processing tree is possible. More than one may be possible, and we discuss uniqueness. For an introduction to the Transitive Orientation Algorithm, see  Golumbic (1980) .

The Transitive Orientation Algorithm is a procedure for constructing a partial order from the information about which pairs of elements are ordered and which are not (see, e.g.,  Golumbic, 1980 ). It is useful when there are more than a few processes and information is known about all pairs. If information about one pair of processes is missing, it may be useful to apply the algorithm twice separately, once assuming the pair is ordered and once assuming it is not. One might learn, for example, that a multinomial processing tree is possible with one assumption but not with the other, so that an experiment testing the pair would be decisive. The algorithm is not likely to be useful if information about many pairs of processes is missing.

In the second part of the paper we explain how the algorithm can be used to combine information about all pairs of processes to construct a tree structure for all vertices, if one is possible. We also discuss when no tree is possible, exactly one, or more than one. Most of the second part of the paper is about partitive sets. These are certain set of vertices that determine the uniqueness of the outputs of the Transitive Orientation Algorithm (see  Golumbic, 1980 ). The main result, which to our knowledge is new, describes the form of partitive sets in a multinomial processing tree. The form is relatively simple; to give a look ahead, a partitive set is illustrated in  Fig. 4

The Transitive Orientation Algorithm begins with the information about which pairs of vertices in a putative directed acyclic graph are comparable and which are incomparable. The directed graph is otherwise unknown. The goal is to construct a directed acyclic graph consistent with the information (there may be more than one) or demonstrate that none is possible. The information is represented in another graph, an undirected one. In the undirected graph two vertices are connected by an edge if and only if the two vertices are comparable. (Note that no edge connects a vertex with itself, because precedence is irreflexive.) If one draws an arrow on an edge, directing it from one of its vertices to the other, the edge is said to be  oriented . (It is changed from an edge into an arc.) Suppose every edge could be oriented in such a way that the vertices are partially ordered. Then the unoriented graph is called a  comparability graph  (for introductions, see  Golumbic, 1980  and  Roberts, 1976 ).  Figs. 6 and 7  illustrate different transitive orientations of the same comparability graph.

(The procedure will stop no matter what arbitrary orientations have been selected.) The graph is not a comparability graph. If at some step all edges have a single orientation, then the resulting orientations represent a partial order. ( Golumbic, 1980 , gives a good explanation of these matters.)

We note that a model may consist of two or more separate trees, one for recall, one for recognition, and so on. The Hasse diagram resulting from the Transitive Orientation Algorithm will not be connected. Here we assume there is a single tree, so the Hasse Diagram is connected.

A multinomial processing tree not only has vertices and arcs, but has with each arc a probability. In each separate experiment, arc probabilities may have been estimated from data. These estimates are not used by the Transitive Orientation and it does not produce new estimates. If arc probabilities for the tree that results from the algorithm are desired, they must be produced in some other way. It is an advantage of the Transitive Orientation Algorithm that parameter estimates are not needed as input, because obtaining accurate estimates by combining data from different laboratories would be difficult.

To summarize so far, an experiment with two factors can test whether each factor selectively influences a different vertex in a multinomial processing tree. If so, we learn whether the two vertices are comparable or incomparable (i.e., ordered or unordered). Suppose we have such information from several experiments. Suppose there is an arborescence capable of representing the comparability information. That is, a factorial experiment indicates that a pair of vertices  
                            a 
                          and  
                            b 
                          are comparable if and only if representatives of those vertices are comparable in the arborescence. If information about all pairs of vertices is available, a suitable arborescence can be constructed with the Transitive Orientation Algorithm. The hypothesis that a single arborescence is capable of representing the comparability information may be false, we can test this. We turn now to the question of whether more than one arborescence is possible.

For a given comparability graph there may be no possible orientation as an arborescence, exactly one, or many. In this section we consider the uniqueness of an arborescence produced by the Transitive Orientation Algorithm. A comparability graph with exactly two vertices,  
                            a 
                          comparable to  
                            b 
                         , can obviously be oriented to form two different arborescences; in one  
                            a 
                          precedes  
                            b 
                          and in the other  
                            b 
                          precedes  
                            a 
                         . The two vertices  
                            a 
                          and  
                            b 
                          cannot be oriented uniquely as an arborescence because there is nothing outside of  
                            a 
                          and  
                            b 
                          to provide information about their order.

A subset of the vertices of an arborescence  
                            T 
                          is  in series  if every simple path from the source of  
                            T 
                          to a terminal vertex of  
                            T 
                          containing one of the vertices of the subset contains all vertices of the subset.  Schweickert and Han (2016)  showed that a comparability graph  
                            G 
                          with a transitive orientation whose Hasse diagram is an arborescence has only one such orientation if and only if the arborescence has no nonsingleton set of vertices in series. The proof is straightforward. Nonuniqueness arises when a set of vertices is in series because a vertex comparable to one of them is comparable to all of them, so even if there exist vertices outside the set in series, those outside put no constraints on the order of those inside. Vertices in series in an arborescence can be reordered without changing any comparability relations, making other arborescences possible.

Vertices in series are a special case of partitive vertices. A set of vertices in a directed graph is  partitive  if a vertex that is not in the set and that is comparable to one vertex in the set is comparable to all vertices in the set. Partitive sets are sometimes called  modules  (e.g.,   McConnell & Spinrad, 1999 ).

Uniqueness of the partial order produced by the Transitive Orientation Algorithm, and the number of possible partial orders if there is more than one, depend on the partitive sets of the comparability graph (see  Golumbic, 1980 ). Hence it is useful to know what form the partitive sets take.  Shevrin and Filippov (1970)  described how the parts of a partitive set are arranged in a comparability graph, in their Lemma 8. Partitive sets can also be situated in a directed acyclic graph, and  Schweickert (1983)  described how the parts of a partitive set are arranged in a directed acyclic graph that has a single source and a single sink. Here we provide such a description for the special case of a directed acyclic graph that is an arborescence, and allow multiple sinks. The form of a partitive set in an arborescence is simple, described in  Theorem 10 , which to our knowledge is a new result. To reach this result, we prove a number of propositions. They are stated and organized here in such a way as to lead to  Theorem 10 . Although many of the propositions are elementary, we present proofs here because we did not find results in print in a form useful for our purposes.

Theorem 10  shows that vertices in a partitive set in an arborescence are sorted into two types of subtrees. Subtrees consisting of vertices in series precede subtrees that consist of a source vertex and all vertices that follow it. Vertices in the former type of subtree cannot be ordered through comparability information. All pairs of vertices in a subtree of the latter type can be ordered through comparability information, except for pairs in series. In short, in a partitive set of vertices, those vertices whose order surely cannot be determined precede those whose order might be determined.

An experiment with two factors can establish whether the data can be accounted for with a multinomial processing tree in which the two factors selectively influence two different processes. If so, the data can establish whether the two processes are ordered or unordered, and if they are ordered, the data can sometimes establish which process precedes the other. Data from an immediate serial recall experiment by  Vitevitch et al. (2012)  were analyzed here as an example. The results can be accounted for by a multinomial processing tree. In it, a process selectively influenced by the clustering coefficient of a to-be-remembered item preceded a process selectively influenced by the serial position of the item.

If one knows for every pair of vertices whether the pair is ordered or unordered, a partial order can be constructed, if one exists, with the Transitive Orientation Algorithm. Conditions have been reported in previous literature, summarized in  Golumbic (1980) , under which a partial order is possible in the form of an arborescence. More than one partial order may be possible for given order information; the possibilities depend on partitive sets of vertices. We show that the partitive sets of an arborescence have a simple form.

Comparable vertices within a partitive set can only be put in order by knowing their comparability with respect to other vertices within that partitive set. Vertices outside the partitive set convey no information about those within. Although the subgraph induced by a partitive set of vertices in an arborescence may be a rather complicated disconnected forest, the arrangement of the partitive vertices in the arborescence itself is clear cut. The subgraph is made of subtrees of two types. In subtrees of the first type, all vertices are in series. There is no way order of vertices in this type of subtree can be determined with comparability information; vertices inside of it and outside of it are uninformative. A subtree of the second type consists of a source vertex and all vertices on a path from the source to any terminal vertex. Order of all vertices within this type of subtree is determined by comparability information with respect to other vertices within the subtree, with the sole exception of vertices in series within the subtree, if there are any.

Results are reassuring for the uniqueness of Multinomial Processing Trees commonly in use. A Multinomial Processing Tree does not ordinarily have two or more vertices in series, albeit such might arise in special circumstances. So, vertices in commonly used Multinomial Processing Trees can be ordered in such a tree in only one way.
