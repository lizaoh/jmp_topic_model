INTRODUCTION  The observation that choice behavior is often inconsistent has led psychologists and economists to develop probabilistic  choice models. Mainly because of the complications involved in estimating parameters of models for representing first choices  out of feasible sets consisting of more than two alternatives, many researchers have resorted to studying pairwise choices,  using the method of paired comparisons as experimental procedure [for an extensive bibliography, see Davidson & Farquhar  (1976)]. THE WANDERING IDEAL POINT MODEL 29  Several experimental studies (Becker, DeGroot, & Marschak, 1963; Coombs, 1958; Krantz, 1967; Rumelhart & Greeno, 1971; Tversky  & Russo, 1969; Tversky & Sattath, 1979) have shown that often pairwise choice proportions systematically violate strong  stochastic transitivity because the choice proportions are not only influenced by differences in utility between the choice  objects, but also, to some extent, by the similarity or comparability of the alternatives. Subjects tend to be somewhat  indifferent between very dissimilar stimuli, even when the stimuli substantially differ in utility. Similar stimuli, on the  other hand, tend to give rise to extreme choice proportions, even when the difference in utility is not that large. In a  series of studies, Sjoberg (1975, 1977, 1980; Sjoberg & Capozza, 1975) found a monotonic relationship between the discriminal  dispersion in Thurstone's (1927) model of comparative judgment and the dissimilarity between the choice alternatives.  Because of these findings, so-called moderate utility models (Halff, 1976) have been proposed which assume only moderate  stochastic transitivity (e.g., Carroll, 1980; Coombs, Greenberg, & Zinnes, 1961; Edgell & Geisler, 1980; Heiser & de Leeuw,  1981; Marley, 1981; Sjoberg, 1967; Strauss, 1981; Takane, 1980; Tversky, 1972a, 1972b; Tversky & Sattath, 1979). Denoting the  probability of preferring a to  b by p(a, b), moderate stochastic transitivity states that   if p(a, b) > 1/2 and p(b, c) > 1/2,   then p(a, c) > min {p(a, b), p(b, c)}; (1)  whereas strong stochastic transitivity requires that   if p(a, b) > 1/2 and p(b, c) > 1/2,   then p(a, c)>max{p(a, b), p(b, c)}. (2)  Several of the recently developed moderate utility models are inspired by Restle's (1961) set-theoretic choice model and  require a priori information about the feature structure of the stimuli in order to be applicable.  A moderate utility model that does not rely on any a priori knowledge of the stimuli is the wandering vector model proposed by  Carroll (1980) and further elaborated by De Soete and Carroll (1983). In the wandering vector model, which is based on  Slater's (1960) and Tucker's (1960) well-known vector model for analyzing choice data, each object is represented by a fixed  point in a multidimensional space, while each subject is represented in the same space by a vector emanating from the origin  with a terminus that follows a multivariate normal distribution. When a subject is presented a pair of stimuli, he or she  samples a point from this distribution and chooses the object which has the largest orthogonal projection on the vector from  the origin in the direction of the sampled point.  In this paper, a new moderate utility model is developed which is an unfolding analogue of the wandering vector model (and  therefore called the wandering ideal point model).  30 DE SOETE, CARROLL, AND DE SARBO  2. THE WANDERING IDEAL POINT MODEL  General Formulation  The wandering ideal point (WIP) model is based on a probabilistic interpretation of Coombs' (1964) unfolding model. In the WIP  model each subject i (i=1, N) is represented as a random point Yi in an r-dimensional Euclidean space. It is assumed that Yi  follows a multivariate normal distribution  Yi-N(tti, Yi) (3)  and that the distributions of the N subject points are independent of each other, i.e.,  Covar(Yi, Yi)=0 (4)  for i, i'= 1, N and i 0 i'. Each stimulus j (j =1, M) is represented as a fixed point xj in the same r-dimensional space.  Since Hefner's (1958) pioneering work, it has become a common practice to develop a probabilistic spatial model by assigning  multivariate normal distributions to points in a multidimensional space. However, the WIP model differs from Hefner's model in  two respects. First of all, the Li need not be identity matrices. Second, multivariate normal distributions are assigned only  to a subset of the points, viz, the subject points.  According to the WIP model, a subject i samples a point yi from Yi each time he or she is presented a pair of stimuli (j, k).  Following Coombs' unfolding model, stimulus j is preferred to k whenever yi is closer to xj than to xk, or whenever  d(Yi, Xj) < d(Yi, Xk), (5)  where d(-, - ) denotes the Euclidean distance function, i.e.,  d2(yi, Xj) = (yi - Xj),(yi - xj). ILLUSTRATIVE APPLICATION  Rumelhart and Greeno (1971) obtained pairwise preference judgments from 234 undergraduates about nine celebrities. Of these  nine celebrities three were politicians (L. B. Johnson, Harold Wilson, Charles De Gaulle), three were athletes (A. J. Foyt,  Johnny Unitas, Carl Yastrzemski), and three were movie stars (Brigitte Bardot, Sophia Loren, Elizabeth Taylor). Subjects were  treated as replications of each other, so that the case N=1 applies.  These data have been analyzed before according to a variety of choice models. Takane (1980) fitted Thurstone's (1927) model of  comparative judgment Case V as well as the two-dimensional factorial model of comparative judgment to these data using maximum  likelihood estimation. The factorial model of comparative judgment has been proposed by Takane (1980) and Heiser and de Leeuw  (1981) and amounts to Thurstone's model of comparative judgment in which the matrix of covariances between the stimulus  utilities is constrained to have a prescribed rank. DISCUSSION  Although several unidimensional stochastic unfolding models have been proposed in the literature (Bechtel, 1968; Coombs et  al., 1961; Sixtl, 1973; Zinnes & Griggs, 1974), only two multidimensional probabilistic unfolding models have been developed.  The first one by Schonemann and Wang (1972; Wang, Schonemann, & Rusk, 1975) is based on the well-known Bradley-Terry-Luce  model and consequently assumes strong stochastic transitivity. In the multidimensional unfolding model proposed by Zinnes and  Griggs (1974), it is assumed that the coordinates of both the subject and the object points are independently normally  distributed with a common variance. [Note that recently this assumption has been relaxed in Zinnes and MacKay's (1983)  approach to probabilistic multidimensional scaling.] Unlike in the WIP model, Zinnes and Griggs assume that for each element  of the stimulus pair a subject independently samples a point from his or her ideal point distribution. The Zinnes-Griggs model  is defined by  Pik=Prob{F" (r, r, d ., d,k)< 1 } (23)  where F"(v,, v2, ti,, A2) denotes the doubly noncentral F distribution with degrees of freedom v, and v2 and noncentrality  parameters A, and A2, and d j (respectively d?k) the Euclidean distance between the mean point of subject i and the mean point  of object j (respectively k). Due to the occurrence of the doubly noncentral F distribution in (23), it is very difficult to  derive the transitivity conditions implied by the Zinnes-Griggs model when r > 1. However, in a Monte Carlo study reported in  Appendix II, we found that choice probabilities generated according to the ZinnesGriggs model never violated strong stochastic  transitivity. Therefore, it seems that (23) implies strong stochastic transitivity. Thus, both the Schbnemann-Wang and the  Zinnes-Griggs models appear to be strong utility models. The wandering ideal point model on the other hand is a moderate  utility model. Consequently, among these models, the WIP model appears to be the only probabilistic multidimensional unfolding  model requiring only moderate stochastic transitivity.  Further research is needed to ascertain the conditions under which the WIP model provides an adequate description of paired  comparisons data. More specifically, it would be interesting to investigate which restrictions might be realistically imposed  on the E; in the case of multiple ideal point distributions. APPENDIX II  Transitivity Conditions Implied by the Zinnes-Griggs Model  In one dimension the model defined by Eq. (23) can be shown to imply strong stochastic transitivity (Bossuyt, personal  communication). In order to investigate whether this result holds when r > 1, a Monte Carlo study was performed.  Configurations were generated by randomly sampling the subject and object mean point coordinates from the uniform distribution  on [0, 5]. For each configuration, the choice probabilities were calculated using the method for approximating the doubly  noncentral F distribution recommended by Zinnes and Griggs (1974, pp. 337-338). Strong stochastic transitivity was verified by  checking the substitutability condition:  Pjr % Pki  if and only if pjk >, 1/2,  which is equivalent to the strong stochastic transitivity condition (cf. Tversky & Russo, 1969). A violation was counted  whenever pjk >, 1/2 but Pjr - Pkr < -2e, where e = 0.0105 is the accuracy of the approximation to the doubly noncentral F  distribution.  A total of 20,000 configurations were generated: 10,000 configurations of 10 object points and one subject point in two  dimensions, and 10,000 configurations of 15 object points and one subject point in three dimensions. None of the 20,000 sets  of choice probabilities was found to violate strong stochastic transitivity.  40 DE SOETE, CARROLL, AND DE SARBO  REFERENCES  AKAIKE, H. (1977).
