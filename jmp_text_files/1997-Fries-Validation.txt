Empirical Validation of a Markovian Learning Model for Knowledge Structures 

Stefan Fries 

Universita�t Potsdam 

Falmagne (1994) introduced three Markovian learning models for knowledge structures. Within these models the probability distribution over the knowledge states of a knowledge structure at one point in time (or the joint distribution for t points in time) is explained as the result of a nonobservable learning process on the knowledge structure. In this paper a first test of the applicability of such a model is described. Estimations for stochastically simulated data show the explanatory power of the model. Reasonable fits to the data and to the original parameter values are found. The fit deteriorates drastically if the knowledge structure differs from the knowledge structure for which the data were simulated. An application of the model to two empirical data sets shows that the predicted data deviate significantly from the empirical data. This suggests that modifications of the model are necessary. ] 1997 Academic Press 

INTRODUCTION 

Doignon and Falmagne (1985) offer an approach to the formal description of knowledge structures on sets of dichotomous items. The knowledge structure consists of the different knowledge states that can occur within the members of a population. A knowledge state is a collection of those items that a subject is capable of mastering. Empirical knowledge structures can be of immense value for real-life applications such as adaptive knowledge assessment procedures. This is due to the fact that they allow restriction of the number of questions to be asked. 

More precisely a knowledge structure can be defined as a pair (Q, K). The elements of Q are the items, while Q is called the domain of the knowledge structure. K is a family of subsets of Q. These subsets are called the knowledge states. K always contains < and Q. Since � K=Q it is sufficient to denote the knowledge structure by K. Knowledge structures can have different properties. Before empirical knowledge structures are used in applications they have to be validated. Villano (1991) offers an approach to the validation of empirical knowledge structures. Villano's model assumes that the probabilities of the knowledge states in the knowledge structure are given by one parameter for every state. For a domain of high school arithmetics he succeeded in refining and extending an expert structure so that it fitted to empirical data. Nevertheless, there are several drawbacks in Villano's procedure. The most striking one is the need for too large a number of parameters. 

Falmagne (1994) proposed three Markovian learning models in which the probability distribution over the knowledge states is explained by the assumption of a discrete nonobservable learning process. In contrast to Villano's approach these models only need a fairly small number of parameters in order to explain the data. Until now the applicability of these models has not been tested. In this paper an empirical test of the second of the three Markovian learning models is presented. 

2. DESCRIPTION OF THE MARKOVIAN LEARNING MODEL 

The Markovian learning model under scrutiny assumes that learning takes place in unobservable, discrete steps. In any step, a subject can learn at most one item. The state space Z of the Markovian learning model is identical to the knowledge structure on which the learning takes place. Therefore, it is sufficient to denote the states of the Markovian learning by K. It is assumed, that the probability of a transition within one step from a state K to a state K _ [r] with r # S(K ) is equal to some learning parameter gr . This Markovian learning model is restricted to the probability distribution on the collection of the knowledge states. The model can predict the probabilities P(K ); however, for modeling empirical data this is not sufficient. For example, a wrong answer to a question could as well reflect the fact that the subject might just have produced a careless error. In the case of a multiple-choice format, a person might also guess the right answer. Hence, for a given set of questions, a person's response pattern R is not necessarily identical to the knowledge state K of that person. In order to model this situation, additional parameters are needed. As proposed in Falmagne (1989), we assume that there is a parameter ;i modeling the probability of making a careless error on item i and a parameter 'i modeling the probability of guessing the correct answer to item i. For example, the probability of answering some item i correctly given that i is in the knowledge state of the subject equals 1&;i .

Given a sufficiently large set of observed responses to the items we can now try to fit the general probabilistic model to data. When we apply the model to some data set, where subjects have been tested once on n items, we have to estimate n transition probabilities gi , n error parameters ;i , n guessing parameters 'i , and the number s of steps the Markov chain has taken up to the testing point. This totals a number of 3n+1 parameters for the general probabilistic model. In the data, there are up to 2[n] observation classes. For a data set of two testing points only one additional step parameter s2 predicting the number of steps up to the second test is necessary. In general, for a domain of n items and t testing points only 3n+t parameters are needed. Note that the number of the observation classes grows much faster with increasing number of testing points. For n items and t testing points there are up to (2[n] )[t] observation classes in the data. 

3. SIMULATION STUDY 

By now the major aspects of the Markovian learning model and its embedding into a general probabilistic model have been outlined. The behavior of such a complex model when confronted with empirical data is difficult to predict. A first question arising is whether the parameters of the model can be estimated for given data. A further problem occurs if someone wants to use the model in order to validate an empirical knowledge structure. The structure to validate might be close but not identical to some optimal structure. It is not clear how robust the model will be in such a case. 

For these reasons a simulation study was done. The basic rationale of this procedure is to first stochastically simulate the behavior of subjects. In the second step the parameters have to be estimated for the simulated data. With this procedure it can be shown whether the estimation routine leads to parameter values sufficiently close to the parameter values used in the simulation. An introduction to simulation methods can be found in Rubinstein (1981). 

A general distinction is made for the simulation study. In the first part we simulate only the Markov chain. In this case, the simulation gives us frequencies over the knowledge states of the knowledge structure. In the second part the subject's answering behavior, modeled by ;i and 'i , is also 

simulated. Hence, all the possible response patterns R are the observation classes of the simulation. This distinction is made in order to separate the exploration of the Markovian learning model from the exploration of its applicability to empirical data. 

Following the simulation of data we perform parameter estimates. As a minimization routine the function PRAXIS (Gegenfurtner, 1992) is used. The function PRAXIS is based on the method of a conjugent gradient search as proposed by Powell (1964). The function minimizes a /[2] -value. 

Several computer programs are needed for performing the simulations and estimations. The programs were written by the author in the programming language C.

4. EMPIRICAL DATA 

After the Markovian learning model has been explored in the simulation studies, the next step is to apply the model to empirical data. Two data sets were at hand. The first data set was used by Villano (1991). These data consist of the response patterns of 60,000 pupils who took the Regents Competency Test in Mathematics (RCTiM) in New York in 1987. From these data the same 10 items that Villano used in his study were chosen. The second data set was collected in a project at the University of California, Irvine by Taagepera and Potter on the teaching of chemical concepts. It consists of the answers of 1,777 pupils from

elementary school and high school, who worked on five problems involving the chemical density concept before and after an instructive session. Thus, for these problems two testing points are available. 

The knowledge structures to validate were determined using the technique of item tree analysis (ITA) as proposed by VanLeeuwe (1974). This technique results in a quasiordinal knowledge space by inspecting empirical contingency tables for all pairs of items. Knowledge structures of this type correspond to a quasi-ordering of the items and they are closed both under union and under intersection. 

In the estimations for the RCTiM data the guessing parameters were a priori set to zero. This was done because the chosen items have a free answering format so that no guessing can occur. In the estimations for the RCTiM data no fit could be achieved. The /[2] -values decrease with increasing step sizes, but for the largest step size s=500 used the /[2] was still 35,229.41 for 785df. This value is highly significant. Since the gain from increasing the numbers of steps got smaller and because of the high computational time needed, no higher numbers of steps were used. 

For the data on the chemical density concept the proportion s1 �s2 was estimated to be close to a value of 1.55. Nevertheless, searching the absolute values of the step sizes revealed no fit to the data. The smallest /[2] -value observed was 662.01 for s=500 and s2=775. This value is highly significant for df =161. 

5. DISCUSSION 

The results of the simulations and estimations show that the Markovian learning model is capable of explaining numerous different probability distributions over the potential observation classes. In particular, data on more than one testing point can be explained by a fairly small number of parameters. Nevertheless, the application of the model to empirical data was not successful. There are several reasons for this latter result. 

First, the failure might be due to the technique used to determine the knowledge structure. The item tree analysis does not offer a knowledge structure without uncertainty about its correctness. Especially the fact that the structures given by ITA are all quasi-ordinal puts a severe restriction on them. If the underlying structure is not quasi-ordinal it cannot be revealed by use of this technique, and as we have seen in the simulation studies, deviations from the original structure have a severe impact on the fit to the data. Thus, the bad fit might be a result of deviations of the assumed knowledge structures from the true structure. Nevertheless, the reason for the bad fit to the empirical data might also be due to the properties of the model. The model might simply be too restricted to model empirical data. 

A major critique of the model is that within the model the learning of a new item does not depend on the actual 

knowledge of the learner. However, a new item should be easier to learn if the learner already has a lot of knowledge about the domain of interest. In some aspect, the independence of the learning from the actual knowledge state is contradictory to the assumption of a knowledge structure on the domain. The reason is that knowledge structures, except for the trivial case of the power set, formulate dependencies over items. 

Another critical aspect is the lack of individual parameters in the model. In particular, the assumption that all subjects took the same number of learning steps up to some testing point is very restrictive. 

Further research should focus on models where learning depends on the actual knowledge of the subject and these models should include some individual parameters. Falmagne (1994) proposed a different Markovian learning model where the transition probabilities depend on the item to be learned, but also on the last item learned by the subject. With such a model chances are much better that for some empirical knowledge structure a fit to empirical data can be achieved. Aside from the Markovian learning models, Falmagne (1989, 1993) also introduced more complex models on continuous time that do not require the Markovian condition. In these models he introduced the concept of an individual learning rate *. Such a parameter incorporated into the models allows an appropriate modeling of individual differences.
