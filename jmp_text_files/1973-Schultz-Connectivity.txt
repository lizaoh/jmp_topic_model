JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 10, 421-428 (1973)  Data Analysis and the Connectivity of Random Graphs  JAMES V. SCHULTZ AND LAWRENCE J. HUBERT  The University of Wisconsin  This paper presents empirical information on the adequacy of an asymptotic approximation developed by Erd6s and Renyi dealing  with the minimum number of lines needed to connect a random graph. It is shown that the approximation is poor for several data  analysis applications of the result previously published in the behavioral sciences.  During the last few years a number of new data analysis procedures have been developed and used extensively by researchers in  the behavioral sciences. In particular, the clustering schemes advocated by Johnson (1967), the multidimensional scaling  routines first suggested by Shepard (1962), and the graph-theoretic techniques proposed by Fillenbaum and Rapoport (1971) now  offer the substantive expert a fairly rich set of data reduction methods that are not tied to the rather uninformative  framework of classical inferential statistics. At the same time, however, there may be a tendency to accept these new  developments too readily and without asking for the mathematical rigor that can be invoked to justify the more common  statistical techniques used in the behavioral sciences.  This note offers empirical data on the legitimacy of one particular graph-theoretic method developed by Fillenbaum and  Rapoport (1971, 1972). The first section below presents their graph-theoretic technique and its purpose; a later section  discusses evidence that suggests a theoretical constraint which should have been observed in previous applications.  BACKGROUND  The study of semantic structures undertaken by Fillenbaum and Rapoport (1971) is one of the more elegant and probably the most  extensive application of the recently developed data analysis techniques. Consequently, since this short note cannot hope to  do justice to their complete work, the reader is merely referred to the original source for a thorough presentation of the  methodology. Our aim is to concentrate on only one of their techniques; therefore, the following discussion is somewhat  limited in scope.  Copyright 1973 by Academic Press, Inc. For instance, the items could be the color  names or members of the HAVE family of verbs (Rapoport and Fillenbaum, 1972). Either a hierarchical clustering scheme or a  multidimensional scaling method seems to provide an appropriate way of investigating the semantic structure of the set S;  consequently, the problem is now one of obtaining some type of proximity measure between each object pair in S that could  serve as the basic input to either of these two methods. With this aim in mind, Fillenbaum and Rapoport suggest an  experimental paradigm in which each subject rank orders all object pairs from 1 to n(n - 1)/2 in terms of proximity. The mean  proximity values for the group then serve as the data used in either a multidimensional scaling or a hierarchical clustering.  Before the mean proximity values are incorporated into any further analysis, however, it would be of value to know that some  structure actually exists in the data. Or in other words, it should be possible to reject the contention that the subjects  have assigned the proximity values to the object pairs at random. As a way of introducing a reasonable procedure for testing  this hypothesis, some helpful elementary concepts from graph theory are introduced below; the interested reader should consult  Marshall (1971) for a more extensive and rigorous presentation.  GRAPH THEORY  An (undirected) graph G can be defined as a set of points and a set of lines connecting various pairs of points. For  convenience, it is assumed that only one line can exist between each pair of points and no point is joined to itself by a  single line. Furthermore, a graph is said to be connected if it is possible to start at any one point and reach any other by a  sequence of contiguous intermediate lines.  For our purposes, there are n points in G corresponding to the lexical items and a line will exist between each pair of points  if that pair has an associated proximity value less than or equal to some number c. For notational purposes, it is assumed  that smaller proximity values correspond to more similar object pairs. In general, G, will denote the graph corresponding to  the threshold proximity value c, which could either be a particular mean proximity value when group data is used, or  alternatively, a particular integer rank assigned by a single individual.  If a subject gives integer ranks to the n(n - 1)/2 object pairs at random, then this assignment can be equated with a sequence  of random graphs. Specifically, as the threshold c increases in unit steps, each new link added to the graph is chosen at  random from all the lines that are not present. Thus, it should be possible to choose some statistic based upon each subject's  performance, and then compare the sample distribution of this statistic to the expected distribution derived from an actual  random assignment of integer ranks to the object pairs. In  particular, suppose there are N subjects and the minimum rank needed to connect the graph for subject i is denoted by Vi . The  sample observations Vl ,..., 17, can be compared to the distribution of V obtained by enumerating all [n(n - 1)/2]! equally  likely possible assignments of the integer ranks to the object pairs. If the population distribution and the sample  distribution are statistically different using, say, a Kolmogorov-Smirnov test, then randomness in the subjects' responses can  be rejected.  A computational difficulty exists, however, in generating the distribution of V. As an illustration, there are 105! different  sequences for adding the lines to a graph of 15 points. Obviously, an exhaustive enumeration is almost impossible and thus, so  is obtaining an exact distribution for V. As a way out of the problem of enumeration Fillenbaum and Rapoport rely upon an  asymptotic result developed by Erd6s and Renyi (1959, 1960), even though an application of the asymptotic formula is not  justified unless additional evidence is available on the accuracy of the approximation for finite n. The next section deals  with this problem of approximation for 9, 12, 15, and 20 points in S; the case of 15 points corresponds to several of the  examples given by Fillenbaum and Rapoport. As one final point, we mention briefly another possible use of the results on connectivity for random graphs in picking an  appropriate hierarchical clustering technique. To be consistent with the previous discussion, this application assumes the use  of an approximate (or exact) distribution for V and not an inadequate asymptotic formula. Suppose a researcher wishes to apply  the single-linkage or the complete-linkage clustering technique as discussed by Johnson (1967) and his choice is limited to  these two alternatives. Because of the graph-theoretic reinterpretations of these two clustering methods in terms of connected  and complete subgraphs, respectively, it seems justifiable to apply the single-linkage procedure when the mean proximity  values suggest a series of clusterings or partitions that consist of loosely interrelated subsets of objects, and, conversely,  to apply the complete-linkage procedure whenever very "tightly-knit" and homogeneous subsets of objects exist. In fact,  Cattell and Coulter (1966) have characterized these two basic types of clusters within a more general psychological framework  (also, see Hubert, 1973). In short, a decision must be made as to the type of structure to impose upon the set of proximity  measures and whether this particular structure is best elicited using single-linkage or, alternatively, complete-linkage.  If the number of lines needed to connect a graph based upon a specific set of  428 SCHULTZ AND HUBERT  proximity measures is less than what would be expected when the proximity measures are assigned randomly, then an intuitive  argument could be made for the use of the single-linkage hierarchical clustering method. In other words, the actual proximity  values suggest that the graph is being connected very early in its evolution, and, consequently, tightly-knit structures are  not present in the data. Conversely, if the graph is first connected by more lines than expected under a random assignment,  then this fact may be interpreted as evidence for the presence of tightly-knit clusters in S. Consequently, it is more  reasonable to impose the complete-link solution and attempt to elicit this particular structure directly.  The use of random graph theory obviously has potential as an aid in interpreting the output from the current data analysis  techniques. It is also clear, however, that care must be exhibited in applying a particular mathematical result that may be  true only under very specific conditions, e.g., asymptotically, and very inappropriate when these conditions do not hold.  Although this admonition seems too obvious to mention, it can be forgotten early in the excitement surrounding the development  of another new analytical tool.
