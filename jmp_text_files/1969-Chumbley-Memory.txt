The subjects behave in accordance with the all-strategies-at-once strategy selection theory on trials on  which they make a correct response but try to use short-term memory on error trials to avoid completely restarting the  focusing process. The theory is tested by examining its ability to describe the way subjects restrict their choice of a  hypothesis on blank trials after receiving a limited amount of information about problem solution.  Early formalizations of cue, strategy, or hypothesis testing theories of concept learning (Bower and Trabasso, 1964; Restle,  1962) assumed that subjects do not use memory of information presented on previous trials when resampling on error trials. The  results of several experiments testing these theories (Bower and Trabasso, 1963, 1964; Trabasso, 1963; and Trabasso and Bower,  1964) have supported this assumption. However, other experiments incorporating misinformation (Merryman, Kaufmann, Brown, and  Dames, 1968), multiple concurrent problems (Restle and Emmerich, 1966), simple problems (Levine, 1966), or nonreversal shift  procedures (Trabasso and Bower, 1966) have produced data irreconcilable with the "no-memory" assumption of the Restle (1962)  and Bower and Trabasso (1964) theories.  Recently two theories have been proposed which attempt to deal with memory processes in concept learning. One theory (Trabasso  and Bower, 1966) assumes that a subject recalls stimulus-reinforcement pairings which are used in conducting consistency  checks on dimensions. The other theory (Levine, 1966), proposed in outline form, assumes that subjects remember and manipulate  sets of hypotheses to limit the size of the functional hypothesis set. Data have been presented supporting both approaches but  neither theory seems to be completely adequate in its present form. When the stimulus is presented on  trial n of the problem, the subject partitions his functional hypothesis set, H, into two subsets: A, hypotheses leading  to an A response on trial n, and B, leading to a B response. Equations 2 a 3 permit a powerful test of the HM theory when performance levels are experimentally manipulated. These  equations assert that as the proportion of subjects tracking the correct hypothesis on the blank trials changes, the  proportion of subjects tracking each of the other hypotheses change in a predetermined manner. For this reason, two procedures  which have been effective in changing the learning rate in concept learning were used to test the HM theory.  Bourne, Guy, Dodd, and Justesen (1965) have shown that the intertrial interval, the interval between the offset of information  feedback on one trial and the onset of the stimulus for the next trial, is important to concept learning. Since calculations  involving sets of hypotheses are quite complex operations, increasing the length of the intertrial interval should, as Levine  (1966) suggests, allow the subject to perform the operations more successfully. It can therefore be expected that t, the  probability of a successful recovery of discarded hypotheses following an error, will increase with an increase in the  intertrial interval until some optimal point is reached. Intertrial intervals of 5 and 15 sec were used in the present  experiment. The 5-sec interval, which is well below the optimal time (Bourne et al., 1965), was as short as possible given the  apparatus used. The 15-sec interval is somewhere near the optimum for problems like the ones used according to presently  available data. If a  subject is required to solve several independent problems concurrently as in the studies by Restle and Emmerich (1966) and  Erickson and Zajkowski (1967), proactive interference from the functional hypothesis sets of other problems may make it  difficult for the subject to retrieve the discarded set of hypotheses or the old functional hypothesis set in his attempt to  recover following an error. Thus increasing memory load should decrease t. Memory load was varied in the present experiment by  using the method of Restle and Emmerich (1966). Some subjects were required to work on three separate problems, the test  problem and two filler problems, with trials intermixed. Two trials on other problems with other dimensions intervened between  successive trials of the test problem. Subjects in the normal condition worked on the test problem alone.  METHOD  The four experimental conditions produced by the factorial combination of memory load and information processing time were:  Group 15-3 (15-sec intertriat interval and 3 concurrent problems) ; Group 15-1; Group 5-3; and Group 5-1.  The pattern of hypothesis choices made by the subjects was determined by using a modification of the Levine (1966)  "hypothesis-tracking" method. After four trials on a concept identification problem, which had its trial of logical solution  on Trial 3, the subject was given a sequence of seven stimulus presentations without informative feedback. These blank trials  were arranged so that any simple hypothesis would yield a unique sequence of choice responses. Levine used only four trials,  and found a certain fraction of uninterpretable sequences which he attributed to random errors. The seven hypothesis-tracking  trials in the present experiment introduced enough redundancy so that one-trial variations from a simple pattern of responding  Subjects. College students fulfilling an introductory psychology course requirement served as subjects. Each subject was  randomly assigned to one of the experimental conditions. The data from some subjects had to be discarded because of apparatus  failures and procedural errors, leaving the data from 245 subjects for analysis. There were 56 subjects in Group 5-1, 58  subjects in Group 5-3, 71 subjects in Group 15-1, and 60 subjects in Group 15-3.  Apparatus. The experimental apparatus was essentially the same as that used by Restle and Emmerich (1966). Event sequencing  and timing control were accomplished using electronic timers and relay switching circuitry. An IBM Summary Card Punch was used  to program stimuli and reinforcements and to record responses. The stimuli were 2 x 2 slides projected by a random access  projector with a remote control shutter.  Subjects were run in groups of four whenever possible, but in many cases only one, two, or three subjects were run at a time  since the group size was determined by the number of subjects volunteering to participate at a given time. The subjects were  seated at chair desks in a semidarkened room facing a wall-mounted projection screen. They responded by means of two push  buttons mounted on a sloping panel box mounted on the desk top in front of them. Information feedback was provided by white  lights mounted directly above the buttons. On  534 CHUMBLEY  training trials one of the white lights would come on immediately after all subjects had responded. On blank trials, of  course, neither light came on.  Stimulus Materials. Seven different sets of stimulus materials were used. Five of these sets were stimuli similar to those  used by Restle and Emmerich (1966). A sixth set of stimuli was constructed for this experiment. These six sets of stimuli had  three binary-valued dimensions and were used for pretraining the subjects and for the filler problems for the groups solving  three concurrent problems. The filler problem stimulus sets were chosen because they were known to be the basis of difficult  concept-identification problems. This was done to insure that the subjects would not solve the filler problems very rapidly  and thus reduce their effect on memory load.  The seventh set of stimuli consisted of 16 of the stimuli used by Merryman et al. (1968). The four binary-valued dimensions of  these stimuli were: number of figures (one or two); color of the figures (red or blue); form of the figures (square or  circle); and type of background (white or white with blue stripes). These stimuli were used for the test problems.  Stimulus Sequences. Each problem in the experiment consisted of 11 stimulus presentations. The particular stimulus shown on  each trial of the pretraining and filler problems was chosen randomly prior to the beginning of the experiment and was the  same for all subjects.  The stimuli and the sequence in which they were shown were specially selected for the two test problems. The internal  structure of each of these problems was shown in Table 1. Information feedback was presented on all 11 trials of each of the  pretraining problems but was omitted on the last 7 trials of the filler problems so the subjects would not be made aware of  the special nature of the test problems.  Procedure. When all of the subjects had been seated in their individual booths they were given fairly detailed instructions  about the nature of the task, the modes of response and information feedback, and examples of the type of rule they should use  to sort the stimuli. All of the subjects were pretrained on both one problem at a time and three problems at a time. Following  pretraining, the subjects in Groups 5-1 and 15-1 solved the test problems and the subjects in Groups 5-3 and 15-3 solved the  test and filler problems. Before the start of each problem or set of problems the dimensions and their values were listed.  Before the test problems, instructions on the blank trial procedure were given. These added instructions were: "You will be  shown the correct category for the first four pictures (of each set) and then will be tested to see if you have identified the  correct rule(s). You should sort the pictures on the basis of your best guess as to the correct rule (for each set) throughout  the test series."  On each trial the event sequence was: presentation of the stimulus until all subjects had responded or 30 sec had elapsed,  whichever time was shorter; presentation of the stimulus and information feedback (on outcome trials) for 1 sec; and a 5- or  15-sec intertrial interval during which the subjects' responses were recorded and a new slide and reinforcing event selected.  The intertrial interval was 5 sec long for all subjects on the blank trials. TESTS OF THE HM THEORY  The goodness-of-fit tests of the HM theory were conducted by combining predictions of the theory and the observed data to  generate chi-squares for each group and each test problem. Each chi-square was then minimized using a computer iteration  routine, STEPIT (Chandler, 1965), which varied the value of t between 0.00 and 0.99 until a minimum value of chi-square was  produced. The 0.99 upper limit on the value of t was required so that none of the predicted frequencies would be zero.  The observed and predicted proportions of subjects selecting hypotheses from each category, the values of t used to generate  each set of predictions, and the chi-squares resulting from the minimization procedure are presented in Table 3. The largest  chisquare is for Group 5-3, Test Problem 2, and it is not even marginally significant (Xzas = 2.308, p > .30).  The fact that t is definitely greater than zero for Group 15-3 (the standard deviation of the parameter estimates was about  0.10) calls for a slight redefinition of t. A subject solving three problems concurrently probably has trouble retaining three  functional hypothesis sets in memory. In fact, he may find it unfeasible to attempt to check all hypotheses simultaneously and  may resort to testing one hypothesis at a time as in the Restle strategy selection theory. Apparently the subjects in Group  5-3 were doing this since t ,. 0.00. If a subject has sufficient time, he may be able to rehearse functional hypothesis sets  enough between trials to enable him to check all hypotheses simultaneously with some success. This could account for the  relatively large difference in t values between Groups 15-3 and 5-3. To incorporate this explanation into the HM theory, the  definition of t must be slightly modified to: t is the joint probability of recovering the discarded hypotheses following an  error and retaining the functional hypothesis set in memory from the time it is formed on one trial until the time it is  reduced in size on the next trial of the problem.  This redefinition of t indicates that the one parameter of the HM theory may reflect more than one retention process. This  does not seem to be a serious deficiency in the model since when experimental operations are devised to separate these  processes,  538 CHUMBLEY  t can easily be replaced by two independent parameters. Until these operations are devised and more empirical knowledge  accumulated, it seems better to leave the HM theory in its present form than to speculate about several alternative forgetting  processes.  Another problem, with possibly greater implications for the experimental design than for the HM theory, arose in analyzing the  training trial data. These data are not presented because they seem to be seriously affected by an experimental artifact. The  blank trials data were of primary concern when the experiment was conducted and the instructions, quoted above, emphasized the  way the subjects should behave on the blank trials. It appears that many subjects emphasized their test series performance to  the exclusion of meaningful choice behavior on the training trials. For example, the proportion of right hand button presses  was much higher throughout the training series than would be expected by chance. This finding and the fact that some subjects  did not even make responses on the training trials yet tracked the correct hypothesis perfectly on the blank trials made a  thorough analysis of the training trial data of doubtful value. This does not necessarily mean the HM theory is wrong, for the  HM theory assumes that commitment to one subset of hypotheses may retard learning when that subset does not contain the  correct hypothesis. This result does mean subjects must be carefully instructed and pretrained to insure that outcome trial  data reflect the covert behavior of the subject.  It is clear that the HM model fits the data very well. It could be asked whether there are other models which could fit as  well. The answer, of course, has to be yes. However, there are some models which will not fit and others which might fit data  from the present experiment but not other published data. There is one class of models for which this is especially true.  Some models of concept learning assume the subject makes some sort of consistency check on hypotheses or stimulus dimensions  following an error, eliminating from the sampling pool those hypotheses which fail the consistency check. Some of these models  assume only local consistency checks, e.g., Bower and Trabasso (1964), while others assume checks on the  stimulus-reinforcement pairings of two or more trials, e.g., Trabasso and Bower (1966). It is assumed in some of the models  that hypotheses eliminated in a consistency check are eliminated only until the next error trial (Bower and Trabasso, 1964)  and in other models the number of trials for which dimensions (and the hypotheses based on them) remain eliminated is a random  variable (Trabasso and Bower, 1966). From even this brief description of this class of theories, it is apparent that there are  a large number of theories which could be generated using consistency check procedures. Chumbley (1967) tested two of them and  found them inadequate. Other data indicates that the consistency check on error trials approach may not be worth pursuing.  Levine (1966) and Richter (1965) found that performance in a human discrimination learning situation is a monotonic decreasing  function of the number of errors  MEMORY IN LEARNING 539  made by a subject. Consistency check theories predict that performance should be an increasing function of the number of  errors made by the subject (with the exception of the zero error case). This is because the subject depends upon error trial  consistency checks to eliminate hypotheses. The more errors he makes, the more hypotheses he will eliminate. In other words,  the empirical finding is that errors retard learning, whereas consistency check theories assume errors are the occassion for  learning. The HM theory is in agreement with the empirical findings and Chumbley (1967) showed that it provides a reasonable  fit to the data of Levine and Richter.  It is evident from the above discussion that a concept learning model is inadequate if it relies solely on the use of  consistency checks and hypothesis elimination to increase efficiency. On the other hand, the HM theory, relying only on the  manipulation of hypotheses, does a good job of describing the data obtained under a wide range of conditions. From the  standpoint of parsimony, mathematical tractability, and psychological adequacy, the HM theory is superior to consistency check  theories. The process assumed by the HM theory produces optimal information processing when t = 1.0, all-strategies-at-once  strategy selection when t = 0.0 as in Group 5-3, and fits the data of groups performing at several intermediate levels of  efficiency in the present experiment. The HM theory appears to be well worth the further development, revision, and testing it  requires.  REFERENCES  BOURNE, L. E., Guy, D. E., DODD, D. H., AND JUSTESEN, D. R.
