GEISLER  University of Texas at Austin  A random utility model of choice was developed by combining the basic ideas of the well-known theories of Thurstone and  Restle. The new model has exactly the same number of free parameters as Tversky's Elimination-by-Aspects model. Furthermore,  both models were found to fit, with equal accuracy, the data reported by Rumelhart and Greeno, and Tversky. It was concluded  that although the two theories are not identical, they may be difficult to discriminate empirically.  The most well known and widely applied probabilistic models of choice behavior are Luce's model, which is based on his choice  axiom (Luce,1959), and case V of Thurstone's random utility model (Thurstone, 1927a, 1927b, 1945). There are, however, many  experimental results that neither model can predict (e.g., Hohle, 1966; Rumelhart & Greeno, 1971). Some of the weaknesses in  these models are often demonstrated by considering all the pair-wise choices that can be constructed from sets of alternatives  such as: $20, $21, and a ticket to the Cotton Bowl. It is plausible that an individual might prefer the Cotton Bowl ticket  over either sum of money about 50% of the time. The models could predict this result by assigning about equal utilities to all  three alternatives. However, the models would then incorrectly predict close to even preference for the two sums of money.  In order to handle this sort of problem, Restle (1961) proposed a modification of Luce's model. Although Restle's idea of discounting aspects common to the alternatives is  intuitively reasonable, it is not easy to devise reasonable models which apply this idea to choices among more than two  alternatives at a time. However, Tversky (1972a, 1972b) succeeded in developing such a model (the EBA model) which reduces to  Restle's model for choices among pairs of alternatives and reduces to Luce's model if there are no overlapping aspects among  alternatives.  The models of Luce, Restle, and Tversky are each equivalent to some random utility model (Tversky, 1972b), but they were  developed with a fundamentally different rationale than Thurstone's model. In Thurstone's model the utilities are assumed to  vary from moment to moment, and the decision process consists of the simple fixed rule of picking the alternative with the  largest momentary utility. In the models of Luce, Restle, and Tversky the utilities are assumed to be fixed, and the decision  process is probabilistic.  This paper describes a tractable version of Thurstone's model that incorporates Restle's and Tversky's set-theoretic view of  choice alternatives. Although not previously developed, this general approach has been suggested by Glen Carr, as reported in  Restle (1971), and by Tversky (1972b). Other random utility models that allow for correlation between the utilities of  alternatives have recently appeared (McFadden, 1977; Hausman & Wise, 1978; Strauss, 1979). Thus, the larger the average utility for a set of aspects, the more variance or uncertainty there is in  its perceived value. It seems intuitively reasonable that, under many circumstances, one would have more uncertainty in  placing a value on something considered quite valuable than on something not considered to be valuable. For example, suppose  that the utility of each aspect is measured cognitively by adding up subjective utility units, each having the same expected  value. If the values of all the subjective units are either independently varying or positively correlated, then the  variability of an aspect (or alternative) must increase monotonically with its mean. Note that Axiom IV would accurately hold  if the values of the subjective units varied independently (although it would probably hold only approximately if the values  of the units were positively correlated). Similarly, Axiom IV would accurately hold if all the alternatives were subsets of a  larger set of nearly equal-valued and independently varying aspects. As a more concrete example, consider assigning utilities  to collections of objects. The utility (or value) placed on the art collection of a large art museum will, in general, be  larger than that of a smaller museum due to the greater number of pieces in the large museum. Furthermore, the total value of  the large collection will probably vary more from month to month than that of the small collection (even after factoring out  inflation). This is because each art piece will tend to vary independently in value, thereby causing the variability of the  total value to be greater for the larger collection.  THEOREM I. Variations on the present model can be easily obtained by substituting other probability densities for the normal. The easiest  to work with would be those closed under convolution; for example, the gamma, poisson, binomial and negative binomial  densities. The parameter c might not cancel  out for these distributions, as it does for the normal, and thus there would be one additional parameter. Also, there most  likely exist densities which are closed under convolution and, although Theorem I still holds, are not reduced to a one  parameter family due to the nature of their parameters. EMPIRICAL TESTS  In this section, the predictions of the present model are compared with those of Tversky's EBA model (Tversky, 1972a, 1972b).  It should be kept in mind that Luce's model is a special case of the EBA model, that Restle's model and the EBA model are  identical for choices from two alternatives, and that the present model is a subset of Thurstone's case I model.  Rumelhart and Greeno's Data  Rumelhart and Greeno (1971) measured choice probabilities for all possible pairs of choices from nine alternatives. The  alternatives consisted of three groups of three  CHOICE MODEL 273  alternatives, such that the alternatives within each group were very similar. Edgell, Geisler, and Zinnes (1973) fitted  Restle's model (and equivalently the EBA model) to these data. They assumed that all the p.'s were zero except for pi (i =  1,..., 9), P11a , tL45B, and PM .s Using STEPIT (Chandler, 1969), an iterative search routine, those authors reported a X2  goodness of fit value of 30.00 (with 25 df). Clearly, the two models fit the Rumelhart and Greeno (1971)  data equally well.  Tversky's Data  Tversky (1972a) ran three studies with eight subjects in each study. Each study involved three alternatives presented to the  subjects in both pairs and triples. In the first study subjects were asked to choose the pattern with the most dots. In the  second they were asked to choose the best college applicants on the basis of a two dimensional profile. In the third they were  asked to choose the best gamble. The three stimuli in each study were structured so that there should be some shared aspects  between two of them, but neither of these two should share any aspects with the third (except for aspects shared among all  three which are irrelevant to both Tversky's EBA model and the present one). Tversky fitted his model to his data by  estimating pi , ps , p.2 , and pie . The other two overlap parameters, p.2a and were assumed to be 0.
