FRIEDMAN, TOM TRABASSO, AND LUDWIG MOSBERG  University of California, Los Angeles, California  This research was concerned with the overlap problem of discrimination-learning theory-how discriminations among stimuli with  overlapping cues are learned. One hundred and twenty subjects learned a paired-associates list in which the overlap among the  compound stimulus members of the items was varied to produce a wide range of component cue validities and intratask transfer  effects. After learning the list, subjects were tested on the component cues alone and on a series of novel compounds which  overlapped the training compounds. An analysis of these transfer tests indicated that subjects tended to respond on the basis  of the validity or relevancy of the component cues, and that frequency of presentation of the cues during learning was not a  critical variable. An extended version of a mixed model of stimulus-sampling theory yielded a good quantitative account of  acquisition and transfer performance.  The research and theoretical analysis reported here is concerned with how discriminations among stimuli with overlapping cues  are learned. The experimental context is a paired-associates task with confusable stimuli and known responses. Two empirical  relationships are of concern: transfer during learning and transfer to novel situations after learning. The theoretical issue surrounding these empirical findings has been termed the "overlap problem" of discrimination learning  theory (Estes, 1959; 1962). Two alternative solutions have been proposed: one involving the adaptation of common irrelevant  cues (Bush and Mosteller, 1951; Restle, 1955) or the observing of relevant cues (Atkinson, 1960); a second based on the notion  of encoding-either by the selection of recall strategies which permit discriminations to be made (Restle, 1963) or by stimulus  patterning (Estes, 1959; Friedman and Gelfand, 1964).  The first approach, exemplified by the discrimination-learning model of Bush and Mosteller (1951), assumes that two distinct  psychological processes operate simultaneously in learning to discriminate stimuli with overlapping cues. First, there is a  conditioning process, in which the elements of a stimulus become associated with  318 FRIEDMAN, TRABASSO, AND MOSBERG  the reinforced response; second, there is an adaptation process, in which the organism learns to ignore the overlapping cues.  For example, in learning different responses to the compounds xy and xz, the subject learns the discrimination by associating  the appropriate responses with the dii'erential elements y and z and ignoring the common element x. A recent analysis of these  "dual-process" models by Bush (1965) has pointed up certain conceptual difficulties associated with them. For example, for the  list given in Table 1, these models fail, for there are no irrelevant cues to adapt. The subject must learn the task by  associating, in most instances, the pattern or compound with the correct response. Because these models seem clearly  inappropriate for the task of the present study, they will not be pursued further here.  As an alternative to models based on adaptation of cues, Restle (1963) has presented a "strategy-selection" theory for  paired-associates learning. In this theory, the subject learns an association by selecting a recall strategy or mnemonic  device. If the recall strategy leads to a correct response, it is maintained; if it leads to an error, alternative strategies  are considered until a relevant strategy is selected. For example, in discriminating between the items xy-R, and xz-R2, a  subject may first select the strategy "x is Rl" which leads to a correct response for xy but not xz. Later, after committing  an error in xz, the subject learns a supplemental strategy "x plus z is RZ" in order to discriminate the two items. One  problem with Restle's theory is that there is no obvious way of relating the stimulus structure to the recall strategies. In  order to make predictions concerning intea-task or inter-task transfer, additional assumptions are required. These assumptions  would be complicated by the fact that there are a number of acceptable recall strategies which allow discrimination between  items. For example, for the items xy-R, and xz-R2 , some recall strategies are: "x plus y is Rl , z is RZ"; "x is Rl , z is  R2"; "x is Rl , x plus z is R2 ." Depending upon which strategies are used, different patterns of transfer effects would  result. The problem of relating strategies to the stimulus situation used in the present experiment is even more complicated.  A more specific sort of encoding scheme, and the one considered in detail here, is based on the notion of patterning.  Basically, this means that in discriminating the item xy-R, from xz-R, , the subject associates the pattern of cues with the  reinforced response. In a mixed model employed by Friedman and Gelfand (1964) and Friedman (1966) it was assumed that learning  occurs in terms of patterns but transfer between learned and unlearned patterns and to novel situations was mediated by  responding to the component cues. The specification of the rules which govern transfer (the response rule) is crucial for the  mixed model in the same sense that the specification of recall strategies is crucial for Restle's strategy selection theory.  And, as with Restle's theory, there is no obvious way to specify the response rule.  One main purpose of the present research was to determine the critical variables in the reinforcement history of the cues  which control transfer and thus specify the response rule. Research by Binder and Feldman (1960) suggested that frequency  TESTS OF A MIXED MODEL FOR PAIRED-ASSOCIATES LEARNING 319  (how often a cue was presented) was an important variable. However, in a recent experiment by Friedman (1966), frequency was  found not to be a critical variable, and it was suggested that relevancy (how many responses a cue was paired with) was  important. The experiment reported here includes transfer tests which compare relevancy and frequency.  The experiment also included a series of tests on the component cues of the training patterns. The results of these tests  should help answer questions about how the items were coded.  METHOD  The experiment consisted of a training or acquisition phase followed by a test phase. In the training phase, subjects learned  nine-item paired-associates lists using the " Study- Recall" or Reinforcement-Test method. The stimulus members of the  paired-associates were two-cue compounds and the responses were the color names red, green, and blue. On study trials, each of  the nine stimuli was presented for three seconds while the experimenter gave the correct response verbally. Each study trial  was followed by a recall trial in which the stimuli were presented for three seconds and the subject attempted to give the  correct response. Study and recall trials alternated until the subject reached the learning criterion of two successive  correct recall trials on the list. After each subject reached the learning criterion, he was tested on a series of 13 novel  two-cue compounds. The cues comprising the novel compounds had previously appeared in the training list. Each test stimulus  was presented for three seconds and the subject attempted to give the appropriate response. A recall trial on the training  list followed the test series. This was followed by another series of 11 test stimuli consisting of single cues which had  appeared in the training list.  Training and test lists. THEORETICAL ANALYSIS  The particular model which will be applied to the results is an amplification on the mixed model presented by Friedman and  Gelfand (1964).  324 FRIEDMAN, TRABASSO, AND MOSBERG  STIMULUS SAMPLING ASSUMPTIONS  The basic stimulus sampling (coding) assumption of the model is that the pattern of cues in the item is associated with the  reinforced response. If a pattern is associated with a response, each component is also associated with that same response. I a component appears in patterns associated with different responses, then that component is conditioned to all those responses.  LEARNING ASSUMPTIONS  An item is assumed to be in one of three learning states: an unlearned state (U), a partially learned state (P) or a learned  state (L). All items are assumed to be in state U before the first study trial. If an item is in state U on a given recall  trial, the probability that it is in state P on the next recall trial is x, and the probability that it is in state U on the  next recall trial is I - x. If an item is in state P on a given recall trial, the probability that it is in state L on the  next recall trial is y, the probability that it returns to state U is z, and with probability 1 - y - z it remains in state P.  Once an item is in state L, it remains there with probability one. The transition probabilities among states between  successive recall trials may be represented by the following matrix:  State on Recall Trial n + 1  State on Recall Trial n  L P U  L /1 0 0  P Iy 1-y-z z  U 10 x 1 -x  The probabilities of being in states L, P, and U on the first recall trial are assumed to be 0, x, and 1 - x respectively. It  is assumed that all items on the list are learned independently according to the same process with the same parameter values.  RESPONDING  Responding to an item on a recall trial depends both on the learning state of that item and on the learning state of all other  items with which it overlaps. The response process may be interpreted in terms of the retrieval of associations.  If an item is in state L, there is perfect retrieval of the association and the probability of a correct response is one.  If an item is in state P, it may be confused with similar items also in state P and an error may occur through imperfect  retrieval of the association. More specifically, it is assumed that if an item is in state P and there are no "conflicting"  items in state P at that time, then the probability of a correct response is one. Two items "conflict" if they have common  cues and are assigned different responses. For example, the items ab and ac do not conflict, but ac and be do conflict. If an  item is in state P. and there is at least one other conflicting item also in state P, the retrieval of the association may  TESTS OF A MIXED MODEL FOR PAIRED-ASSOCIATES LEARNING 325  be imperfect, and the probability of a correct response to that item is p. The probability of each of the alternative error  responses is (I - p)/2.  If an item is in state U, the pattern is not associated with any response and responding to the pattern is based on selecting  some conditioned component of the pattern. Sometimes a relevant or unambiguous component is selected which is associated with  a single response; at other times, any component which is associated with at least one response is selected. If no component  of the pattern is associated with any response, then the response is simply guessed. More specifically, conditioned cues and  the recall strategies operating on these cues are defined as follows: If a pattern is in state P or L, we say that its  components are conditioned to the response associated with that pattern. If a component appears in more than one pattern in  states P or L, then the component is conditioned to all responses associated with the different patterns. The two recall  strategies are called the "relevant cue search" strategy and "conditioned cue search" strategy. The "relevant cue search"  strategy represents a tendency to respond to constituent cues of unlearned patterns which are unambiguous, i.e. are associated  with only one response. The second strategy, called the "conditioned cue search," represents a tendency to respond to any cue  which is conditioned, relevant or not. It is assumed that an item in state U on a recall trial sets off a relevant cue search  with probability r and a conditioned cue search with probability I - r. In a relevant cue search, a relevant cue is chosen at  random and the response associated with it is given. In a conditioned cue search, a conditioned cue is chosen at random and  the response is then chosen randomly from the set of responses associated with that cue. If a relevant cue search is initiated  and none are found, then a conditioned cue search is tried; ia conditioned cue search is initiated and there are no  conditioned cues, then the response is chosen from the set of allowable responses, Rl , R2 , and R3 with corresponding  probabilities of gl , gz , and gs .  It should be pointed out that for the above response rule, the relevancy of a cue on a given recall trial depends on the state  of learning of the patterns containing that cue. For example, suppose that on a given recall trial, the only pattern  containing c which is in states P or L is cd. Therefore, c would be conditioned only to R3 and would be a relevant cue, and  could be selected in a relevant cue search on that trial. However, as soon as other patterns containing c which are assigned  different responses are learned, c would no longer be a relevant cue.  As a further illustration of the application of the response rule, consider a recall trial on which the unlearned pattern be  is presented for recall. The patterns which overlap be which are in states L or P are ab, bd, and cd. Thus b is conditioned to  RI and R2 , and c is conditioned to R3 and is a relevant cue on that recall trial. If a relevant cue search were initiated, c  would be selected and R3 would occur. If a conditioned cue search were initiated, one of cues b or c would be selected (each  with probability 0.5), and the response chosen randomly from responses conditioned to that cue. THEORETICAL ANALYSIS OF ACQUISITION DATA  The test of the model as applied to the acquisition data was its ability to account for some of the major summary statistics  of the data: the mean and variance of trials to criterion on the list, and the mean and variance of the total errors, trial of  first correct, and trial of last error for the various items. Since the derivation of analytic expressions for these scores  would have required a transition matrix with 39 states, the model was tested by simulating the experiment and comparing Monte  Carlo results with the observed results. A program was written for the IBM 7094 which simulated the experimental procedure and  applied the assumptions of the model. The program generated 100 Monte Carlo subjects for each set of input parameters and  computed the appropriate summary statistics.  Parameter Estimation  It will be recalled that the model assumes the same values of learning and response parameters for all items of the list; the  differences between items in terms of observed outcomes result from transfer between items which are in different states of  learning.  In fitting the model, the response rule parameter, r, the probability of a relevant cue search, was set at 0.50, the value  obtained from the transfer test results. Three types of variations on the  basic model were considered: One type  330 FRIEDMAN, TRABASSO, AND MOSBERG  considered alternative response rules, a second type considered variations in the definition of learned and unlearned items,  and a third type considered other learning assumptions. Since the alternative models yielded a large set of outcomes, only.  the important qualitative findings are discussed. In all of the alternative models, the all-or-none predictions for the  control items were preserved.  Alternative Response Rules  One alternative model assumed the following simple response rule: "To an unlearned item, select a component cue at random and  give any response which is conditioned to that sampled cue. If there are no responses conditioned to the sampled cue, guess."  All other assumptions of this model were the same as the basic model. The parameters y and p were estimated from Monte Carlo  runs as described earlier. The differences between predictions for this model and those shown in Table 5 for the basic model  were slight.  In order to determine the importance of the parameter r in the basic model in generating predictions for the acquisition data,  a series of simulations were run in which the value of the parameter r was systematically varied from 0 to 1 in steps of 0.10.  The net result was no appreciable change in the accuracy of the predictions over the range of values used.  These results, together with the results of unreported explorations of other response strategies, make it clear that the  summary statistics in Table 5 can be fit by a number of different response rules. The assumption common to all of the  different strategies is that the subject samples components of unlearned patterns and responds on the basis of responses  conditioned to them. Of course, the response rule of the basic model has the added virtue of being able to describe the  transfer test data as well as the acquisition data, whereas these alternative response rules work only for the acquisition  data.  Transfer to Unlearned Patterns from Conditioned States  In the basic model, unlearned patterns received transfer from overlapping patterns in both states P and L. One alternative  model which was considered allowed transfer only from overlapping patterns in state P. The notion behind this assumption was  that items in state P could be considered as only partially discriminated from overlapping items in state U, while items in  state L were completely discriminated from overlapping items. This assumption is similar to that implicitly contained in  Restle's (1963) discrimination model for paired associates learning and in Feigenbaum's (1961) EPAM model. Otherwise, all  assumptions were the same as in the basic model. The parameters y and p were estimated from Monte Carlo runs as described  earlier. The general result was that the summary statistics of acquisition could be fit nearly as well by this model as by the  basic model. However, for the cd item, this alternative  TESTS OF A MIXED MODEL FOR PAIRED-ASSOCIATES LEARNING 331  model predicted too few errors and the predictions for the of item were no better than the predictions of the basic model.  Alternative Learning Assumptions  One simple alternative to the basic model which was considered was to allow a direct transition from the unlearned state U to  the learned state L, but preserving all other assumptions of the basic model. One reason for considering this alternative was  that if the of and fg items spent less time in the intermediate state P, they would have fewer opportunities to mutually  interfere with each other and the error rate on those items would be decreased. Such a result would improve the basic model's  predictions on those items. In this alternative, the probability of remaining in state i'' was kept at 1 - x = 0.66 but the  probabilities of going from U to state P or L were systematically varied. This restriction preserved the good fit of the  learning of the control items. In general, all variations other than the basic model yielded poorer predictions. Those  parameter values which yielded good fits for the mean errors on the various items failed to predict the variances and gave  very poor predictions for the overall learning on the list.  Two models which introduced associative interference into the learning process were also considered. In both models, the  notion of conflicting items was linked to the transition among the learning states. The first model allowed for proactive  interference by assuming that if an item in state U had a possibly conflicting item in state P, then the probability of the  unlearned item going into state P was decreased. The second model allowed for retroactive interference by assuming that if two  conflicting items were in state P, there was a certain probability z that one of them or both of them would regress from state  P to state U. All other assumptions were the same as the basic model. In attempting to fit these models, the parameters x and  r were set at the values used in the basic model, and 1Vlonte Carlo runs were made gridding the parameter space to estimate  the additional learning parameters. For neither of these alternative models were we able to locate parameter values which  yielded reasonable simulations to the obtained data.  DISCUSSION  The basic model performed well in describing acquisition and transfer performance in a fairly complex list of  paired-associates. We wish now to relate some of the specific assumptions of the model to the more general issues of the  overlap problem.  STIMULUS SAMPLING (CODING)  The model assumed that the pattern of cues becomes associated with the correct response. The single cue test results (see  Table 4) indicate that subjects are able to  332 FRIEDMAN, TRABASSO, AND MOSBERG  give at least one response associated with all of the component cues in the list, including the redundant cues of the control  items. These results may be taken as support for the patterning assumption. However, simple patterning will not serve as a  general solution to the overlap problem. The many recent studies on stimulus selection in paired-associates learning (see  Blough and Millward's (1965) discussion of these studies) make it clear that patterning is not always the rule; likewise, our  results make it clear that neither is stimulus selection a universal rule. The real question iswhat are the variables which  control how an item is coded or remembered? We suggest that the way a list of items is coded is largely determined by the task  requirements (including the instructions). It seems reasonable to assume that subjects adopt a consistent coding strategy  which they can apply to all of the items on the list. In the list we used, the only way to learn some of the items was to  associate the pattern of cues with the correct response. Thus, subjects adopted a patterning code for all of the items on the  list, whether or not particular items (such as of fg, hi, and jk) could be coded by selecting a single cue. Similarly,  instructions play an important role in determining how an item is coded. For example, in most of the recent work on stimulus  selection, evidence for cue selection comes from single cue tests after acquisition, as was done in the present study. The  instructions used in these studies generally ask the subject to learn the items as quickly as possible, and do not mention  that they will be tested on the component cues. We would expect that either instructing or training the subject to learn to  associate all the components of the stimulus pattern with the assigned response and that he would be tested on these  components later, would tend to yield a patterning result on the single cue tests.  RESPONSE PROCESSES  The idea of a response rule or a way of responding to the component cues of unlearned or novel patterns is important to the  mixed model. But again, the specific assumptions of the present model may not serve as general solution to the overlap  problem, Obviously, the structure of a list will determine what is learned and, how subjects will respond on transfer tests.  There is very little data on exactly how the structure of list determines transfer, but quite enough to know that the answer  will not be simple; see for example the results of Friedman (1966) on transfer in patternvs.-component discrimination tasks,  and the results of Binder and Estes (in press) on the effects of "relative novelty" of responses on transfer.  LEARNING PROCESSES  In the present model, the assumption that each item was learned independently worked out reasonably well. Yet there is much  ;evidence (Shepard et al., 1961, for  TESTS OF A MIXED MODEL FOR PAIRED-ASSOCIATES LEARNING 333  example) to suggest that the appropriate learning unit may be either a single attribute or a group of attributes, depending on  the assignment of responses to the stimuli in the task. The unit of learning may vary from a simple-response rule involving  only a single component, as in the concept identification experiments of Trabasso (1963) and Bower and Trabasso (1963), to the  learning of each stimulus pattern, as in the present research. The results on memory in the concept identification task  (Trabasso and Bower, 1964) indicate that immediate memory for attributes of patterns is very poor, although subjects have  learned the rule for classifying all patterns correctly. In concept identification tasks, rule learning predominates and  pattern learning is both absent and unnecessary to the requirements of the task.  REFERENCES  ATKINSON, R. C.
