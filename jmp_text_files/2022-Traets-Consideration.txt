Over the past decades many choice models have been developed, stemming from different frameworks. A distinction can be made between single-stage and two-stage choice models. In single-stage models, the decision process is modelled by linking the features of the choice environment (i.e, the choice set) to the choice outcome by a single function. In two-stage models, the decision process is split up into an initial evaluation of the choice environment, followed by a conditional choice process. In the present paper we develop a two-stage model and compare its properties with single-stage utility models in the context of discrete choice experiments (DCE). In the latter type of experiments, a decision maker is presented with several choice sets which consist of two or more alternatives. Each alternative consists of a combination of attribute levels. The decision maker is then asked to indicate his or her preferred alternative for each choice set. The researcher thus only observes the final choices, which generally do not reveal much about the decision process itself. In what follows we briefly highlight some of the central ideas present in both types of the models which we will compare via simulation and on empirical data in the remainder of this paper.

Due to this inherent flexibility, the RUM framework has proven that it is eminently suited to analyze and forecast choice behaviour. Not surprisingly, the most widely used choice models by practitioners, such as the multinomial logit (MNL) and the mixed logit model (MIXL), belong to this framework. For a more in depth reading on the history of the RUM framework, the reader can consult  Heinrich and Wenger (2002), Manski (2001), McFadden (2001) .

Nevertheless, the behavioural realism of some of the core assumptions of RUM models is being questioned frequently (for an overview see  Hess et al. (2018), Thaler (2016) ). Due to the axioms of completeness, transitivity, continuity and independence, on which utility theory is founded, RUM models are said to be normative and presume a rational decision maker, rather than that they reflect how decisions are truly made by human decision makers. For example, the fact that utility models are fully compensatory (i.e., positive features can compensate for negative features) seems to contradict with evidence showing that decision makers often avoid making trade-offs and use non-compensatory rules instead ( Araña et al., 2008; Jedidi & Kohli, 2008; Kahneman & Frederick, 2002 ). Advocates of RUM often argue that deviations from rational behaviour can still be consistent with RUM and as such they provide evidence in favour of using RUM models to describe and predict data ( Thaler, 2016 ).

In a seminal paper,  Simon (1956)  expressed the need for a choice mechanism that represents the desire of organisms to “satisfice” rather than to optimize. When satisficing, a decision maker searches through the set of alternatives in any order until an acceptable threshold or “aspiration level” is met ( Gigerenzer & Gaissmaier, 2011; Simon, 1955 ). The satisficing idea of Herbert Simon is present in a variety of choice models which make use of thresholds and heuristics. The two-stage model developed in this paper can be motivated from this framework of bounded rationality ( Simon, 1955 ), in which emphasis is placed on the cognitive limitations of human decision makers. It is presumed that the information present in a choice scenario can be initially too complex to process, which leads to the use of simplifying non-compensatory heuristics to screen the environment. During this screening phase some alternatives are immediately eliminated, which reduces the initial choice set to a set of “considerable” alternatives.

While research interest in the formation of consideration sets has never declined, and theoretical and empirical evidence for such screening behaviour has accumulated ( Bettman, 1979; Bremer et al., 2017; Ford et al., 1989; Hauser, 2014; Hauser et al., 2010; Roberts & Lattin, 1997; Shocker et al., 1991 ), identifying such heuristics based on discrete choice data remains difficult. We hereby briefly list some of these difficulties.

A first problem concerns the identification of specific heuristic screening rules. In  Table 1  different approaches are listed that have been reported by decision makers in order to eliminate alternatives from a choice set.

While the heuristics reported in  Table 1  are theoretically different, they cannot always be empirically distinguished, even if we know which alternatives are considered and which are not. For example, if there is a lexicographic rule consistent with the consideration set, there is also a conjunctive rule consistent with that set. The same applies for a deterministic elimination by aspects rule, which degenerates to a conjunctive heuristic as highly ranked attribute levels become equivalent to acceptable attribute levels ( Hauser, 2014; Manzini & Mariotti, 2014 ). It is possible to distinguish conjunctive rules from lexicographic rules by asking respondents to rank alternatives. In a typical discrete choice experiment, the researcher however only observes the final choice.

A second problem, closely related to the previous problem, is that decision makers can apply different screening heuristics, or use the same heuristic but differ in the attribute levels they find acceptable. Thus, different from single-stage compensatory choice models, not only the coefficients can vary between respondents, the screening rule itself may also vary. Identifying such consideration heterogeneity given the limited information in choice data imposes an additional challenge.

Lastly, a third difficulty for two-stage models is to disentangle the consideration process from the choice process. To overcome these difficulties, two-stage models often rely on auxiliary data such as scanner data, consideration data or consider-then-rank data ( Chiang et al., 1998; Wu & Rangaswamy, 2003; Xun, 2014 ), impose carefully selected assumptions that restrict the number of possible consideration sets ( Honka et al., 2017; Manzini & Mariotti, 2014 ), or assume a functional relation between the consideration probability and the choice probability of an alternative ( Kohli & Jedidi, 2005; Martínez et al., 2009 ). This paper aims to investigate to what extent insights about screening behaviour can be derived from choice data alone. After developing a two-stage model that takes consideration heterogeneity into account, we examine the parameter recovery of our model in a simulation setting. In addition, we test to what extent it is possible to identify the true data generating model if data is either generated with the proposed two-stage model, or with a compensatory mixed logit model. Lastly, we compare our model on empirical data with different single-stage models which focus on modelling preference heterogeneity rather than consideration heterogeneity ( Leong & Hensher, 2012 ).

To study whether the true data generating mechanism can be retrieved given choice data, we estimated both models on the LCCL and MIXL datasets. To estimate the MIXL model the simulated likelihood approach implemented in R-package “mlogit” ( Croissant, 2020 ) was used. As there are 8 dummy coded attribute levels and one opt-out alternative specific constant, the model requires to estimate 9 population means as well as 9 population variances. Afterwards, the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) was calculated for both estimated models for each dataset. To compare both types of models, we subtracted the BIC value of the LCCL model from the BIC value of the MIXL model for each dataset. The boxplots for those values are presented for each type of generated data (the x-axis) in  Fig. 3 .

As can be seen in  Fig. 3 , for each of the 100 LCCL datasets the BIC value of the fitted LCCL model was smaller compared to the BIC of the fitted MIXL model. Likewise, for each MIXL dataset the fitted MIXL model resulted in a better BIC than the fitted LCCL model. According to the rule of thumb by  Raftery (1995) , we can speak of “very strong evidence” in favour of the model with the lowest BIC when the difference in BIC values is at least 10, which was the case for each simulated dataset. These results thus clearly indicate that the pattern in the choice data generated by one type of model, cannot be fully grasped by the other model.

A discrete choice experiment was conducted as part of a larger survey to gain information about cinema preferences of Flemish students ( Heyrman, 2020 ). The choice experiment consisted of 12 choice sets containing 5 alternatives including an opt-out option. The design was obtained using an efficient design approach implemented in R-package idefix ( Traets et al., 2020 ). The attributes, together with their attribute levels, are listed in  Table 2 . From the initial 148 respondents who started the survey, 103 made it to the end. In the analysis that follows, we omitted the choice data of the participants who did not complete all choice sets.

The attribute “Price” denotes the price of a regular ticket and could take values 15 euro, 11 euro and 7 euro. The levels of attribute “snacks” indicate whether snacks are allowed or not. The size of the cinema could be either large (with more then 4 rooms) or small (with 1 to 3 rooms). The projection format indicated whether the movie was presented with Standard (Dolby digital), 3D, or IMAX technology. A brief explanation of all projection formats was provided to each participant before the choice experiment. The attribute “seat specific tickets” indicated whether tickets were seat-specific or not. Lastly, the hypothetical cinema’s could differ in the possibility to buy tickets online or not. In  Table 3  an example of a choice set can be seen.

In each choice set participants were asked to indicate their most preferred option, which also included the opt-out option. As can be seen in  Table 3 , a choice set with 6 attributes and 4 options contains a lot of information to process. It is thus not unlikely that participants use non-compensatory heuristics to narrow down the choice set.

In  Fig. 5 , the opt-out responses are indicated in black for each of the 103 participants who responded to twelve choice sets. As can be seen, 19% of the participants indicated the opt-out option at least once as their preferred option.

In the present paper a two-stage model was developed that accounts for consideration heterogeneity by allowing decision makers to make use of individual thresholds for attributes. Via simulation we showed that we can retrieve the parameters of the LCCL model accurately from choice data. Afterwards the model was compared with the standard mixed logit model that accounts for preference heterogeneity. It was shown that the data pattern produced by one model cannot be properly captured by the other model. Furthermore, we compared the performance of the model with a variety of other models on empirical data and concluded that the LCCL model should be selected based on AIC and BIC. Importantly, the insights gained from LCCL could not be provided by any of the other models. According to our model, the data suggests that 73% of the respondents applied a screening heuristic before making a final choice. Furthermore, we were able to identify groups of respondents that share a non-compensable requirement for particular attributes. This is in line with  Gilbride and Allenby (2004) , who reported that 92% of the participants in their sample used a conjunctive screening rule.

In the introduction we briefly elaborated on the ideas central to single-stage utility models and two-stage models. As mentioned, the neoclassical utility framework is often challenged by empirical evidence of behavioural phenomena that seem to violate the assumptions of rational decision making inherent to the RUM framework. Incorporating non-compensatory screening rules in choice models can be motivated from the perspective of bounded rationality, in which such heuristics serve as cognitively less demanding solutions to reduce choice complexity. Therefore, models that incorporate heuristics (or thresholds) can link context dependent information such as task complexity ( Payne, 1976 ), or the emotional state of the decision maker ( Araña et al., 2008 ), to the use of threshold-based screening behaviour. It is often argued that the preference for an alternative should indeed be evaluated in relation to the other alternatives in the choice set, and cannot be determined independently from that choice set ( Noguchi & Stewart, 2014 ). The most well-known observed phenomena that demonstrate context dependence are the similarity effect, the decoy effect, and the compromise effect. While it is known that standard utility models are unable to properly capture these effects due to invariance assumptions ( Kivetz et al., 2004; Rooderkerk et al., 2011; Tversky & Simonson, 1993; Wollschlaeger & Diederich, 2020 ), dynamic-cognitive approaches such as multi-alternative decision field theory (MDFT) ( Roe et al., 2001 ) have been successful in explaining such effects. As MDFT is also able to emulate the elimination-by-aspects (EBA) heuristic, it could be interesting to test whether the LCCL model is also able to capture such contextual effects. We briefly explain how this could be studied with the LCCL model, using the example of a choice set containing two alternatives (A and B) based on two attributes (price and quality) presented in  Fig. 6 .

To even further increase the contextual dependence in the LCCL model, the decision stage could also be modelled with alternative approaches. A popular counterpart to Random Utility Maximization is for example Random Regret Minimization (RRM) ( Chorus, 2010 ). In the latter type of models, alternatives are evaluated via an attribute-based comparison process which can either result in anticipated regret or rejoice. It is hereby hypothesized that anticipated regret from a loss outweighs the anticipated rejoice from an equal gain. As such, RRM models feature a reference-dependent type of decision-making that takes loss-aversion into account ( van Cranenburgh et al., 2015 ). An interesting approach to include RRM would be to allow the comparison between attributes to be based on all initial alternatives in the choice set, instead of only on the ones in the consideration set. Such an approach might capture what is known as the phantom decoy effect ( Trueblood & Pettibone, 2017 ), in which alternatives that are removed from a choice set still influence the final choice. Furthermore, as loss-aversion has been previously proposed as an explanation for contextual effects ( Tversky & Kahneman, 1991 ), and plays a central role in well-known models such as the leaky competing accumulator model (LCA) ( Usher & McClelland, 2001, 2004 ), it could be studied whether such models could benefit from taking a consideration stage into account in which loss-aversion results from the screening process.

Lastly, it can be seen that the proposed two-stage approach shows close resemblance with decision making frameworks such as general recognition theory (GRT) ( Ashby & Townsend, 1986 ). The latter theory also presumes that a decision process can be split up into an initial evaluation phase followed by a conditional decision stage. As this theory is developed in the context of perceptual stimuli, the dimensions of the stimuli are continuous rather than discrete attribute levels. In a DCE however, it could be that the number of attribute levels

This could for example be done by modelling the joint continuous distribution, as is done in  Cantillo and Ortúzar (2005) . The commonality between stochastic GRT ( Ashby, 2000 ) and two-stage models is that in both models stimuli (alternatives) are classified (considered) with some probability. While the error in categorization is attributed to perceptual error in GRT, no specific interpretation is given to the error parameter
