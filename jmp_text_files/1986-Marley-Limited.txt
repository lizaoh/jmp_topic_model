Finally, we indicate what we see as the advantages of our rehearsal model over these prior models. Several theories have been advanced to deal with these magnitude estimation (and related absolute identification) results.  Summaries of these theories are now presented followed by details regarding the aspects of magnitude estimation data that they  are able to adequately handle.  The attention band theory (Luce, Green, & Weber, 1976; Green et al., 1977; Baird et al., 1980; Green et al., 1980; Luce et  al., 1980; Luce et al., 1982) when applied to acoustic stimuli assumes that the subject has a roving attention band about 10  to 20 dB wide and that a stimulus falling within this band is represented more accurately than one falling outside the band.  The subject is assumed to have a tendency to track the previous stimulus so that the attention band tends to be centered on  the previous stimulus, and also to have a tendency to focus on the end stimuli. Sequential effects then occur due to the  tendency of the attention band to track the previous stimulus, and end effects occur due to the tendency to focus on the end  stimuli.  Ward (1976, 1977, 1979) proposes a fuzzy judgement approach that assumes category decisions and a guessing or biasing  heuristic. In particular, the internal representation of the presented stimulus is assumed to be a fuzzy subset (of the set of  possible sensation levels) which is categorized by comparing this representation to each category prototype (also assumed to  be fuzzy subsets). The overlaps between the representation and the various prototypes are assessed with the possible category  responses then being those categories for which the overlap of the internal representation and that category's prototype  exceeds a certain threshold. If more than one category exceeds this threshold then the final category response is determined  using a biasing heuristic. According to this heuristic, the subject chooses the category response that is the same as, or  closest to, the category response on the previous trial. This category response is then mapped onto the response continuum and  a response made that is close to the response prototype chosen. Sequential effects, in particular assimilation of the current  response to the previous response, occur because of the biasing heuristic that is used. Petzold (1981) also explains  sequential dependencies by a model with a guessing strategy that is influenced by the previous response.  Criterion setting theory (Treisman, 1984, 1985; Treisman & Falkner, 1984; Treisman & Williams, 1984) assumes that a subject's  response criteria change from trial to trial. A "tracking mechanism" moves the response criteria so that the subject is more  likely to make the same response on the next trial, and a "stabilizing mechanism" moves the response criteria closer to the  previous stimulus representation. The effect of the tracking mechanism is of larger magnitude but decays more  342 MARLEY AND COOK  quickly than the effect of the stabilizing mechanism. The subject's final response is then determined by the response criteria  that have been adjusted by both these mechanisms. There is considerable evidence from magnitude estimation and absolute  identification experiments that sequential effects, such as assimilation of the current response to the response and stimulus  on the previous trial, can be interpreted in terms of such criterion shifts (Jesteadt et al., 1977; Lockhead & King, 1983;  Ward & Lockhead, 1970, 1971; Purks, Callahan, Braida, & Durlach, 1982). However, there is also some evidence for small  increases in discriminability, i.e., d', when stimuli on adjacent trials differ by a small amount (Luce et al., 1982).  The perceptual anchor model of context-coding (Braida, Lim, Berliner, Rabinowitz, & Purks, 1984) has many similarities with  the rehearsal model that we develop in this paper'. The basic idea of that perceptual anchor model is that an observer codes  the sensation arising from a given stimulus presentation by determining its position relative to one or more internal  references or perceptual anchors. The "distance" of the current stimulus from such anchors is "measured" in terms of multiples  of noisy "units." Both the mean and the variance of these units are determined by the current context. This perceptual anchor  model is the most recent in a long history of similar ideas (e.g., Gravetter & Lockhead, 1973). Although it is similar in  several respects to our own rehearsal model, we do not discuss it further in this paper as it has not yet been applied in  detail to the magnitude estimation results presented above.  We now discuss the ability of the attention band, fuzzy judgment, and criterion setting models to fit the previously listed  magnitude estimation results (focusing on magnitude estimation of intensity of pure tones, although most of the results have  been replicated for other dimensions).  The fact that the mean magnitude estimate is (approximately) a power function of intensity is usually a direct consequence of  assuming mappings between physical intensity and sensory (or response) processes that yield such power functions. The fact  that the exponent is inversely proportional to the stimulus range is not usually considered-but see Teghtsoonian  (Teghtsoonian, 1973; Teghtsoonian & Teghtsoonian, 1978).  The results on the variability of the log response and of the response (Properties 3(a) and (b)) have not received much  attention in earlier theories, although they are discussed in Luce et al. (1980). Those authors assume the existence of a  reference stimulus at the upper end of the range, an idea that is carried over to our (upper anchor) rehearsal model.  The attention band model explains the coefficient of variation data (Property 3(c)) by the fact that for small separations the  stimuli will both tend to fall within the attention band where they are more precisely represented, and thus will have a lower  coefficient of variation (Baird et al., 1980). The dependence of the average correlation coefficient on stimulus separation (Property 4) refutes the response ratio  hypothesis (Luce & Green, 1974) which predicted a constant correlation coefficient near unity. The attention band model  (qualitatively) explains the correlation data by assuming that the response ratio approach is only used when both stimuli fall  within the attention band, otherwise the response is based entirely on the current stimulus. The fuzzy judgement approach  handles the correlation data by the fact that for large separations the stimuli tend to be at the ends of the range where  there are usually fewer response categories from which to choose and therefore less opportunity to use the biasing heuristic  (which gives the response dependencies) (Ward, 1979). The criterion setting approach deals with the correlation data by having  the magnitude of the changes in the response criteria decrease as one moves away from the presented stimulus (Treisman, 1984,  1985; Treisman & Williams, 1984).  None of the above models has been applied in detail to the changing range data (Property 5), but Green et al. (1980) conclude  that in its present form the attention band model is unable to account for these results.  Although the focus of this paper is magnitude estimation, we briefly mention some results on cross-modality matching and  mixed-modality matching which must be satisfactorily handled by a complete theory. In contrast to the magnitude estimation  results, in cross-modality matching the coefficient of variation plot does not have a pronounced V-shape, instead being more  or less constant across stimulus separations and about 5 to 10 times larger than for magnitude estimation (Baird et al.,  1980); however, the correlation plot continues to have an inverted V shape, although with a lower maximum than for magnitude  estimation. These are important results, showing that the inverted V shape of the correlation coefficient data is not  necessarily linked to the V shape of the coefficient of variation data, at least for cross-modality matching. These  cross-modality matching results have not been adequately explained by any of the current theories.  In other experiments involving magnitude matching tasks (magnitude estimation for two different modalities presented  alternatively, here sound and light) the correlation coefficient is found to have the same inverted V-shaped pattern for the  correlation of log responses on successive light-sound or sound-light trials, but also on successive same modality  trials-sound-sound or light-light-separated by an intervening other modality trial (Ward, 1981, 1982). For these same four  cases the coefficient of variation does not show the usual V-shaped pattern. These results show that responses depend on  previous responses regardless of whether they are for the same modality and so a theory that modeled magnitude matching as two  separate magnitude estimation tasks could never fit the data (without specific response dependencies). The fuzzy judgement and  the changing response criteria approaches seem promising in this area although neither has been applied in detail to these  data.  344 MARLEY AND COOK  In summary, there are currently three well-developed theories applicable to various magnitude estimation results. The  attention band theory has been the most productive in terms of generating interesting predictions, which its developers have  then tested; however, its theoretical predictions have tended to be phrased and tested in relatively qualitative fashion. The  fuzzy judgement and criterion setting approaches have focused mainly on the response components and have been shown to  qualitatively predict certain aspects of the data; however, neither has been explicitly tested on real data. Thus none of  these theories has presented adequate quantitative fits to the items presented earlier, except for item 1, mean magnitude  estimates are approximately power functions of intensity. In this paper we present such quantitative fits of the rehearsal  model to all items of the list presented earlier, with the exception of item 2, the dependence of the exponent on the range.  (The Teghtsoonian (1973, 1978) explanation of this phenomenon is compatible with the way that the exponent is determined by  the rehearsal model.)  A general description of the rehearsal model is now presented, followed by its application to magnitude estimation, with  detailed fits to some of the data in that area. A general discussion of the rehearsal model's adequacy and its potential to  fit (with or without changes) the remaining data that have been summarized here is then presented, with some comparisons of  its assumptions with those of the three other models introduced above.  THE REHEARSAL MODEL  The rehearsal model assumes that in experiments such as magnitude estimation, absolute identification, and cross-modality  matching, the subject must rehearse the (psychological) frame or continuum in which the presented stimuli occur, and in some  cases must rehearse a similar frame for the response continuum. The subject's rehearsal capacity is limited and thus perfect  retention of these frames is impossible (for the experiments typically conducted). The subject's responses are based on the  total amount of rehearsal activity between the location of the presented stimulus (perceived fairly accurately on the frame)  and the various possible anchors. The rates of rehearsal and of forgetting (decay) determine the extent to which the frame is  accurately maintained, which in turn determines the response accuracy. Thus it is assumed that although the presented stimulus  is fairly accurately represented on the subject's stimulus rehearsal frame, the subject can only determine its location within  the frame by measuring the amount of activation between it and various. known anchors, i.e., the location can only be  determined in the context of the entire stimulus range and not in any absolute way.  It should be noted that, while the rehearsal model most definitely has a memory (or rehearsal) component, it is not a memory  model in the usual sense of having the subject remember the actual values of previously presented stimuli, and then making a  response based on comparisons of these remembered values with the value of the current stimulus. This type of memory model  implies that performance will  REHEARSAL MODEL FOR MAGNITUDE ESTIMATION 345  deteriorate as the length of time between stimulus presentations is increased, whereas the rehearsal model does not have such  an implication. Luce et al. (1982) have shown with their yoked small-step (3) experiment that performance (in absolute  identification) does not depend on time in this way, so that the usual kind of memory model is inadequate. Standard memory  models also cannot explain the result that sensitivity is usually independent of the preceding stimulus (Purks et al., 1980),  whereas this is what one obtains using the rehearsal model.  A simple analogy to illustrate some of the major elements of the rehearsal model is that of trying to identify the horizontal  position of points presented on a cathoderay tube (CRT). Suppose that the CRT is such that only a thin horizontal slit can  emit light. Single points (of light) are presented at any given time along this slit and one must identify their horizontal  position. If one is in a dark room then although one can readily see the point one cannot known its position unless one has  available the context of the slit. Suppose that one had an electron gun that could be fired at the CRT slit to activate or  light up that portion of the slit that it hit. The gun has a limited firing rate and the activation of the slit decays or  fades with time. Thus one would be able to rehearse the context of the slit with this electron gun and base one's response on  the amount of activated slit to the left and/or right of the presented stimulus or point (provided the presented stimulus had  enough contrast with the background stimulation to be perceived). Performance would of course not be perfect unless the  limited firing rate was high enough to prevent any noticeable decay from occurring.  We now present the corresponding mathematical model applicable to a set of stimuli that vary along some dimension such as  acoustic intensity. (This has already been introduced in Marley and Cook, 1984, and the following discussion has been taken  almost directly from that source.) After this presentation we comment on certain aspects of the model.  The rehearsal model assumes that the input (sensory) dimension is monotonically mapped onto a linearly ordered set of sensory  elements; thus when a particular stimulus is presented it stimulates one (or more) of these elements. Associated with each of  these (psychological) sensory elements is a memory element that can be activated by the rehearsal process, which for  simplicity is assumed to be a Poisson pulse process of total rate ;.1. This total rehearsal capacity can be shared among the  relevant memory elements in such a way that each element is stimulated by a Poisson process of rate e, e < A,, but with the  total rate over all such elements being (less than or) equal to A,. Furthermore, each memory element is assumed to be a leaky  integrator that decays exponentially over time at rate a,. Each input pulse from the rehearsal process increases the level of  activity in the stimulated memory element by "one" (memory) unit; this activity begins to decay immediately. In the case of  magnitude estimation, it is also assumed that the response dimension must be rehearsed. Similarly to the stimulus dimension,  the response dimension is monotonically mapped onto a linearly ordered set of psychological elements, thus each element (or  set of elements) corresponds to a particular response. Associated with each of these psychological elements is a memory  element that can be  346 MARLEY AND COOK  activated by the rehearsal process, again assumed to be a Poisson pulse process of total rate Ao, that can be shared among the  relevant memory elements in the same fashion as that for the stimulus dimension. Similarly, each memory element is assumed to  be a leaky integrator that decays exponentially over time at rate ao. Several aspects of the model are worthy of comment. First, a major assumption of the model is that even though the sensory  stimuli are coded relatively accurately on the input array their location is not directly available without use of the  rehearsal frame. This is not a parsimonious assumption but we have to handle the result that absolute identification  performance is considerably poorer than one would expect on the basis of discrimination performance. Thus, something important  happens as one goes from small range to large range psychophysical experiments, and we are saying that the results are due to  the existence of a limited capacity rehearsal system-in fact one of the attractive aspects of the rehearsal model is that the  limited absolute identification performance is a natural consequence of the fixed rehearsal ability.  One might ask why the subject does not measure the location of the current stimulus by using the instantaneous rate of the  Poisson process over the relevant intervals. A somewhat unsatisfactory answer is that the model would not then yield responses  that covary across (adjacent) trials. A more reasonable answer might be based on the idea that the total rehearsal capacity is  small (as our later parameter estimates show), and that integrating the pulse process gives more reliable judgment (we have  not actually performed the calculations to follow through on this suggestion).  In our application of the model to magnitude estimation we assume rehearsal on the response dimension, whereas in our earlier  work on absolute identifcation (Marley & Cook, 1984) no response rehearsal was assumed. One can develop the current results  without assuming rehearsal on the response dimension (Marley, 1981) since such rehearsal only affects the interpretation of  the rehearsal parameter, ~, in Marley's formulae. However, since we deal with a sensory representation that is in logarithmic  form, it is also natural to deal with the response dimension in logarithmic form. The latter is reasonable when one assumes  rehearsal on both  348 MARLEY AND COOK  dimensions but is less so when no rehearsal is assumed on the response dimension. (We have tried to develop the whole model in  terms of ratios, rather than differences, but have been unsuccessful in thinking through the form of the rehearsal process in  such a formulation.) Finally, although we do not do so in this paper, we wish to also apply the model to cross-modality  matching. Subjects in such experiments do not appear to use intermediate numerical responses (Baird et al., 1980) and so the  symmetry of the two dimensions in such a case makes it reasonable to assume rehearsal on both dimensions.  We have not yet studied the decay characteristics of the rehearsal process in detail. However, since the decay rate  [partially] determines the size of the coefficient of variation and correlation of responses on successive trials, we predict  that such data depend on the intertrial interval. We also assume exponential decay although Treisman and his coworkers  (Treisman, 1984, 1985; Treisman & Faulkner, 1984; Treisman & Williams, 1984) argue for linear decay in their model (we think  their model might be reinterpretable in our framework in terms of local rehearsal on the response dimension). REHEARSAL MODEL FOR MAGNITUDE ESTIMATION 373  DISCUSSION  It is clear from the results presented in this paper that the rehearsal model can adequately deal with many of the observed  results in magnitude estimation, and our other work shows its applicability to absolute identification results (Marley & Cook,  1984; Cook & Marley, 1984). We therefore now present some general comments, and speculations, regarding its ability to fit  other data.  A standard result in magnitude estimation (and absolute identification) is assimilation of the current response to the  previous response (Jesteadt et al., 1977; Ward & Lockhead, 1970, 1971; Purks et al., 1980; Staddon, King, & Lockhead, 1980).  In fact, it was reanalysis of such effects that first lead Green, Luce, and their coworkers to present magnitude estimation  data in the form of correlation plots (Jesteadt et al., 1977). However, given the rather different nature of the data analyses  that demonstrate the correlation effects, and those that demonstrate assimilation, it is not clear that a theory, such as  ours, that can deal with the correlation effects will necessarily also predict the assimilation effects. Our intuition is that  the rehearsal model as currently presented does not predict assimilation-this is partly based on the fact that many related  sequential effects have been explained by trial-to-trial criterion changes (Purks et al., 1980; Treisman, 1984, 1985; Treisman  & Williams, 1984), and our current version of the rehearsal model assumes fixed criteria. However, it is unclear whether  interpretations of data via such criterion changes are independent of the associated theoretical framework, and so further  theoretical and simulation work is required to determine how to explain assimilation in our theory.  The effects of various stimulus range manipulations on sequential dependencies in magnitude estimation (Green et al., 1980)  can probably be explained in terms of the rehearsal model. However, it should be noted that the data in that paper suggest  that different subjects adjust their rehearsal range for changing ranges by differing amounts-some subjects, such as BO in  this paper, showing considerable effect of stimulus range on rehearsal range, with other subjects, such as BK, showing  somewhat less effect. The rehearsal model is compatible with such subject effects, since its present formulation does not  predict how the subject will adjust the rehearsal range under different experimental conditions. Depending on one's attitude  this can be considered a weakness in the formulation of the model or a necessary flexibility to deal with the strategies of  individual subjects. The following are, therefore, post hoc but reasonable explanations of some of the data from the gap,  large step, and small-step experiments of Green et al. (1980)-these data were summarized in the previous section.  When signals are selected from a 50 dB range under the constraint that all differences of successive intensities be small  (Green et al., 1980), the subject has the opportunity to rehearse a local range around the previous stimulus. To the extent  that he makes use of that opportunity, and to the extent that rehearsal and decay rates are high, the coefficient of variation  and correlation data for this experiment will be similar to that of a corresponding small range experiment. This is, in fact,  374 MARLEY AND COOK  true for subjects NP and BO of Green et al. (1980). Note that these are the subjects we chose to analyze in this paper because  the slope of their mean magnitude estimation function did not change significantly with changing stimulus range. Since the  slope, B, equals the ratio of the (log) response rehearsal range and the (log) stimulus rehearsal range, i.e.,  N*lko B - M*lkl,  this slope constancy indicates that these two subjects were adjusting their response rehearsal range, as a function of their  stimulus rehearsal range, in a way similar to that suggested by the Teghtsoonians (1973, 1978). This is, perhaps, indirect  evidence that these subjects are sensitive to opportunities to vary performance by changing rehearsal ranges.  When signals are selected from a 50 dB range under the constraint that all differences of successive intensities be large,  then to take advantage of this constraint the subject has to be able to center the (reduced) rehearsal range at the expected  distant location. However, one of the major ideas behind the rehearsal model is that the subject can only locate stimuli in  terms of a rehearsed frame, and thus the new location of rehearsal has to be "measured" relative to the current location using  the current rehearsal frame. Thus the subject needs to allocate some rehearsal activity to the whole possible stimulus range  in order to then center rehearsal on the new (distant) location, and it is not clear that performance in such a case can be  improved over that obtained in the regular random step experiments where all the rehearsal activity is always allocated to the  full stimulus range. (Similar arguments are used by Cook and Marley, 1984, to explain the small-step (3) and small-step (5)  absolute identification data of Luce et al., 1982.)  The gap experiment (Green et al., 1980) involves a stimulus set covering the usual range, but with two widely separated  clusters of stimuli. Using arguments similar to those just given for the large step experiment, the subject has to be prepared  both for small-steps (within the current cluster) and large-steps (across clusters), and thus probably must simply rehearse  the whole possible range of stimuli.  In summary, performance in the (standard) random step, the large step, and the gap experiments will be similar, with  performance in the small-step experiment yielding lower coefficient of variation, and higher correlation, of response ratios  to the extent that the subject can take advantage of the small-step constraint.  The rehearsal model has not been applied to cross-modality matching or magnitude matching. The natural extension to these  experiments appears to involve assuming a rehearsal dimension associated with each of the sensory dimensions, plus a (common)  rehearsal dimension for the response dimension in the magnitude matching case. We would then assume that in cross-modality  matching there is a direct matching between the two sensory dimensions, whereas in magnitude matching each sensory dimension  is mapped onto the common response dimension.  REHEARSAL MODEL FOR MAGNITUDE ESTIMATION 375  We then need to discover whether appropriate allocation of rehearsal capacity to the various dimensions, and appropriate decay  values on these dimensions, can be found to fit the data from these experiments (such data was summarized in the Introduction).  The results for magnitude production are essentially the same in terms of coefficient of variation and correlation coefficient  as those for magnitude estimation (Green et al., 1977). The rehearsal model would therefore model magnitude production in more  or less the same way as it models magnitude estimation, matching rehearsal amounts on two dimensions (input and output) to get  the similar results.  CONCLUSION  Overall the rehearsal model is capable of adequately predicting many of the observed results in magnitude estimation (this  paper) and absolute identification (Marley & Cook, 1984; Cook & Marley, 1984), and seems to have the potential to explain the  magnitude production results, and perhaps some of the magnitude matching and cross-modality matching results. Also, although  this paper has only been concerned with the application of the rehearsal model to auditory psychophysics, it is in no way  limited to the auditory domain. The rehearsal model does not at present obviously handle all trial-to-trial sequential  dependencies, but will perhaps be able to handle these with alterations such as incorporating ideas from Ward's (1976, 1977,  1979) fuzzy judgement and Treisman's (1984, 1985) criterion change approaches. In some versions, such as those used to fit the  smallstep (3) absolute identification data (Cook & Marley, 1984), the rehearsal model is similar to the attention band model  (Luce et al., 1976; Green et al., 1977; Baird et al., 1980; Green et al., 1980; Luce et al., 1980; Luce et al., 1982), both  having the assumption of a roving range (or band) in which performance is augmented-on the one hand by stronger rehearsal  which better maintains the range and so more accurately describes the stimulus location (rehearsal model); and on the other  hand by the assumption of a more accurate stimulus representation (attention band).  Finally, the different versions of the rehearsal model presented here correspond to different strategies that might be used by  different subjects (or by an individual subject) under different experimental conditions. The next challenge is to attempt to  predict conditions under which each strategy will be used by subjects, or to design experiments that control such possible  subject strategies.
