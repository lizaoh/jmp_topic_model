JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 36, 592-597 (1992)  Theoretical Note  A Note on the Multiple-Trace Memory Model without Simulation  CHING-FAN SHEU  Northwestern University  The multiple-trace memory model, MINERVA 2 (Hintzman, 1984, 1986, 1988), has usually been examined using the computer  simulation method. This article presents an analytic treatment using probability theory, leading to closed form solutions for  the probability mass functions of the model distributions. The expected values of such distributions are found to relate to  the model parameters in a straightforward manner. However, variances of the distributions do not appear to take on simple  forms. Exact quantitative predictions of the model are greatly facilitated by the analytical results. (0 1992 Academic Press,  Inc.  The multiple-trace model, MINERVA 2 (Hintzman, 1984, 1986, 1988), has been applied to a variety of memory paradigms. In many  cases (e.g., recognition, frequency judgment, and categorization) it fits the qualitative effects found in the human data.  Although the model is fairly simple and makes only a few basic assumptions, explicit expressions of the distribution functions  of the theory have not been derived and examination of the model so far has been carried out using simulation. This note  presents methods to calculate probability mass functions of similarity, activation, and echo intensity distributions as they  occur in recognition memory. In some cases approximation formulae are obtained for the moments of the distributions in terms  of the model parameters. The probability mass functions may be computed with numerical routines that run faster than  simulation by at least several orders of magnitude. The results of this note greatly facilitate quantitative comparisons of  MINERVA 2 to experimental data. AN APPLICATION  Ratcliff, Sheu, and Gronlund (1989) have been investigating predictions of several global memory models for recognition  experiments and have collected empirical ROC (receiver-operating-characteristics) curves under various experimental  manipulations. Comparisons of MINERVA 2 performance with data are greatly facilitated by the ease in generating the model  predictions for different values of the parameters. An interesting finding is that for shorter list lengths the ratio of old  to new activation variance implies asymmetrical ROC curves. However, the curves tend to symmetry as the number of items in the  list increases. This is due to the sum of new activation variances reaching the magnitude of the old activation variance.
