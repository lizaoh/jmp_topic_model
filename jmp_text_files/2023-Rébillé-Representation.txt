The elaboration and reflection on utility theory can be traced back to early economic theory ( Hervés-Beloso & del Valle-Inclán Cruces, 2019 ), but the topic is definitely multidisciplinary (e.g. psychology, cognitive sciences, chemistry, physics, etc.). For instance, the problem of utility representation has been addressed on connected separable topological spaces in  Debreu (1954, 1964)  and  Eilenberg (1941) . Interval orders received a clear status as objects of research through Fishburn’s contributions ( Fishburn, 1970a, 1970b, 1973, 1985  and  Monjardet, 1988 ). Numerical representations of preferences beyond utility functions have known a strong interest that remains actual and recent ( Bosi, Campión, Candeal, & Induráin, 2007; Bosi, Candeal, & Induráin, 2007; Bosi, Estevan, Gutiérrez García, & Induráin, 2015; Bridges, 1985, 1986; Bridges & Mehta, 1995; Chateauneuf, 1987 ). Interestingly some partial strict orders already appeared in the early scientific works of  Wiener (1914) . These works dealt with binary relations over temporal events. This fact is acknowledged in  Fishburn and Monjardet (1992) . A natural question was to know under what circumstances did a binary relation admit a bi-utility (or a multi-utility) function representation. In our study we have focused on an important class of preferences which consists of interval orders. These preferences have provided interesting features such as the precedence and succession binary relations. These have turned out to be strict weak orders and thus a bi-utility representation could be considered. Wiener’s condition has allowed us to obtain a positive answer to the question of the representability by a bi-utility function. Interestingly, strict orders that satisfied Wiener’s condition were necessarily interval orders, but interval orders did not necessarily have to satisfy Wiener’s condition in general. A related line of research is to characterize bi-weak orders and obtain their representation theorems.
