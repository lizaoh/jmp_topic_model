INTRODUCTION  The notion of a "psychometric function," a device which focuses on the local variations of a subject's discrimination or  detection ability, has been part of the psychophysicist's vade mecum since Fechner and Wundt, and is still very much in the  mainstream of empirical research. It is somewhat surprising then, that this notion has prompted exceptionally few theoretical  investigations. A notable exception is Levine's work (1971, 1972, 1975).  The most familiar case of a psychometric function arises when we consider, for a fixed stimulus b, the probability Pb(a) that  stimulus a is judged as exceeding b from the viewpoint of some sensory dimension. (Both b and a are in some real interval  representing a physical scale.) A graph of an idealized function Pb which is consistent, in its main features, which many  data, is displayed in Fig. 1. Such a function Pb is traditionally referred to as a psychometric function. This term is also used in a different situation,  when Pb(a) denotes the probability of detecting stimulus a when embedded in some (possibly noisy) background b. Occasionally,  we encounter the term in a much broader context, when the empirical measure under investigation is not a probability of  discrimination or detection, but some other variable, such as a reaction time, or a neural spike count. Our discussion will  cover all these cases in one general theory.  In view of the length of this paper, an outline of its content may be useful. We shall proceed from a rather abstract  definition of a family of psychometric functions. (Considering the diversity of usage, it could handly be otherwise.) In this  general case, the index b in Pb(a) denotes the "background" which may be-but need not be-an object of a nature different from  that of the stimulus, a. To emphasize this, we shall actually change the notation to Pb(x). This case will be referred to  generically as detection. We shall then gradually strengthen the definition to encompass a variety of paradigms, models and  constraints which have been found of interest by practitioners and theoreticians. In particular, the case in which the  variables a and b in Pb(a) take their values in the same domain will be referred to as discrimination. Discrimination is thus  seen here, from a formal viewpoint, as a special case of detection. Of particular importance in our developments are the two  representations:  Pb (x) = H[x, y(b)],  Pb(x) =F[u(x) - S(b)],  both of which are closely related to some of Levine's work. The connection between our results and Levine's will be made clear  in due place. These examples pave the way to a general definition of a family of psychometric functions, in which the background (or index)  is assumed to vary in some subset of a mathematical space, say, a vector space over the real numbers, equipped with a suitable  distance function. The term "suitable" is to be interpreted in two different ways. First, the distance function must be  compatible with the vector structure, in the usual sense of topological vector spaces. Various vector operations must be  continuous (e.g., vector sum, multiplication of vector by scalar, etc.). Second, the distance function must also be compatible  with the psychophysical aspects of the situation. For example, if two backgrounds a, b are close enough, they should be  expected to have similar effects on the detection of the stimulus. In particular, the domains of the psychometric functions  should overlap (cf. Theorem 5). In this paper, only the metric properties of the set I of backgrounds will be used. Our  definitions will be framed in such a way that the psychometric functions arising in discrimination will appear as a special  case of those obtained in detection, the latter being the general case.
