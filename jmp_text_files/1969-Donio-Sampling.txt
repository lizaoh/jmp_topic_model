JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY: 6,240-257 (1969)  Stimulus Sampling Learning Models Described as Continuous-Time Stochastic Processes  JEAN DONIO'  Stanford University, Stanford, California 94305  In a previous work, an extension of the stimulus sampling theory to continuous-time learning models was introduced. A  noncontingent reinforcement schedule occurring according to a Poisson process was then considered. The present article  thoroughly investigates first properties of four continuous-time models which differ in their reinforcement schedules. Two are  noncontingent and two are simply contingent; two occur according to a Poisson process, the other two are equally spaced in  time. The response process is always two-valued and continuous in time. Fundamental equations are derived from the axioms,  conditional probabilities computed and estimation procedures provided for the parameters.  The present paper considers a class of stimulus sampling models that involve time as a continuous parameter. This development  is based on the writer's dissertation (Donio, 1964) to which the reader is referred for a more detailed analysis of many of  the following results. The basic axioms have been discussed in Suppes and Donio (1967), and the present article attempts to  develop the theory sufficiently to permit detailed analysis of data.  To fix ideas, it is helpful to consider the following illustrative experimental paradigm (Suppes and Donio, 1967; Wyckoff and  Sidowski, 1955). The subject is in front of a screen, and small targets appear briefly on the left or right of the screen at  random times. The subject predicts on which side the target will next appear. In this task, the subject is actually making one  or the other response at all times. Thus, though there are only two responses, the response process itself is continuous in  time. The appearance of a target at any position on the screen is considered as a reinforcer of the response of predicting  that position. Thus, the reinforcement process may vary both in time and space. MATHEMATICAL CONSEQUENCES OF THE AXIOMS  As yet, we only have a static view of the model and we now consider its dynamics. Between times t and t + h, for a small h,  three types of events may occur (Suppes and Donio, 1967). First, a new stimulus sampling occurs with probability Ah in the  following way. The held stimulus is returned to the set of all stimuli and instantaneously a new stimulus is sampled with  probability 1 /N and held. Of course, if the conditioning of the new stimulus is different from the previous, the response is  changed. This type of event will be called Event a. Second, a reinforcement occurs. This can modify only the conditioning of  the stimulus being held. This will be called Event b. Third, nothing happens. This will be called Event c. ESTIMATION OF THE PARAMETERS UNDER A POISSON REINFORCEMENT PROCESS  Throughout this paper, three quantities have played a central role and appeared in all the formulas: A, the stimulus-sampling  rate; c, the effectiveness-of-conditioning parameter; and N, the number of elements. To test the validity of the models, we  must therefore estimate these quantities. For simplicity, we shall consider the Poisson-type noncontingent reinforcement  process. Of the three parameters, A and c are easier to deal with, whereas N is rather difficult to estimate. The general idea  which comes first is to write down the likelihood function derived from an actual experiment, maximize it with respect to the  parameters, thereby getting the needed estimates. This may be done in one and only case: the one-element model. If we assume  that N = 1, we are left with only two parameters, A and c. In addition, since there is only one stimulus, whether or not a new  stimulus is sampled from time to time is quite irrelevant, since a new stimulus sampling has of necessity to bring back the  same stimulus. This is emphasized by the fact that in the formulas corresponding to the one element case, the parameter A does  not appear. Thus, in the one-element model, only one parameter is important and has to be estimated, c.  The one-element model has however an undesirable feature. At most, one change in the switch position is allowed between  consecutive reinforcements due to the effectiveness of the conditioning. This is the type of deterministic behavior which was  emphasized by Suppes and Atkinson (1960). Therefore, we must consider the N-element model. We can no longer compute a  likelihood function which would still be manageable for computational purposes. Therefore, we shall replace the likelihood  function by a pseudolikelihood function, a first order approximation, and from then on, proceed as before.  In fact, the process will not directly yield estimates of A, c, N but an estimate of the quantity  u=  m1em'T - m2em2T  mi - ms  in which ml and m2 are previously described functions of A, c, N. T now represents the period between consecutive observations  of the response process. A set of three estimations of u for different T's will then yield the estimates of A, c, N. This is  computationally difficult. In an attempt to simplify these calculations, we may choose T small enough to justify an expansion  in terms of T, to the first-order. CONCLUSION  We have attempted to point out some of the mathematical difficulties which appear when one investigates the N-element model in  a continuous-time situation. Some of the results which were found, the one-element model with Poisson-type reinforcement for  example, were predictably logical extrapolations of the usual discrete-trial models. Other results show clear-cut differences  from the corresponding discrete models. For example, in the N-element model, the mean learning curve is expressed by a sum of  two exponentials depending on time, instead of a power of the trial number. In addition, the number of stimuli N will be  hidden in the exponents in a very involved fashion. The investigation for the mathematical difficulties and some simple  results has been completed for the very basic models considered in this article. However, computational difficulties become  rapidly overwhelming, even though it is not impossible to obtain rather precise results for some distributions or statistics.  These models are subject to criticism. For example, the axioms suppose that a reinforcement  STIMULUS SAMPLING LEARNING MODELS 257  becomes effective instantaneously. This is of course a physical impossibility. There will be some latency between a  reinforcement and its action. Nowhere in the article was this fact accounted for, except in a negative manner. The estimation  methods carefully avoided using the instantaneity of effectiveness of a reinforcement as starting point for an estimation  procedure, as would have been possible. Possibly, further work will account for that. In the meantime, experimentation will  tell in which direction the models have to be perfected.  REFERENCES  DONIO, J.
