FISHBURN  The Pennsylvania State University, University Park, Pennsylvania 16802  This paper discusses two models for describing an individual's binary choice probabilities between gambles whose outcomes are measured along a unidimensional scale on which a larger outcome is preferred to a smaller outcome. The models are based on a presumed underlying von Neumann-Morgenstern utility function. They view each pair of gambles in an interlocking fashion rather than as two separate entities. The first model is an incremental expected utility advantage model which is proposed for the context in which gambles are presented to the individual in terms of their outcome probabilities without specific reference to chance events. The second model, an expected utility loss generalization of Edwards' RELM rule, is intended for contexts in which outcome probabilities are tied to designated chance events. The models are proposed in a tentative spirit. Data from previous studies lend some support to the models as descriptors of binary choice probabilities in gambling situations.  1. MODEL 1: INCREMENTAL EU ADVANTAGE  Throughout this section G denotes the set of all simple probability distributions on a subset X of the real line, so that p E G iff p(x) > 0 for all x e X, p(x) > 0 for at most a finite number of x E X, and Yx p(x) = 1. As stated in the Introduction, functions in G will be viewed as simple gambles over the outcomes, presented to an individual in the probabilistic mode without reference to specific chance events, and it is assumed that an individual prefers x to y if x > y.  Numerous studies have shown that various parameters of gambles p and q, such as means, variances, higher moments, loss probabilities, and so forth, affect an individual's  BINARY CHOICE 103  choices between such gambles to a greater or lesser extent. It is also clear from a large number of studies that individuals do not typically satisfy the deterministic expected utility prediction that a gamble with larger expected utility will be chosen with probability 1. It has been suggested, however, that an individual's choice probability for p over q may be related to the magnitude of the difference E(u, p) - E(u, q) between their expected utilities. In a less sophisticated form, this proposal relates P(p, q) to the difference between the expected values (means) of the two gambles as discussed, for example, by Myers et al. (1961) and Lee (1971a, b) in studies geared to the presentational mode of our second model.  However, the difference in expected utilities cannot be the key to an adequate descriptor for P(p, q) since, for example, it might be true that E(u, p) - E(u, q) = E(u, p') - E(u, q') > 0 with p and q having zero variances and p' and q' having sizable variances, in which case we would anticipate P(p, q) = 1 but P(p', q') < 1 if p' and q' are sufficiently intertwined. SUMMARY  The purpose of this paper has been to present and analyze two expected utilitybased models for describing an individual's binary choice probabilities between gambles with monetary outcomes. The first model, phrased in terms of incremental expected utility advantages, is proposed for cases in which the individual compares gambles in their "pure" probabilistic forms. The second model, which is a dressed-up version of Edwards' RELM rule, is based on expected utility losses in presentational formats which use specific chance events to determine outcomes. Both models in their general forms satisfy moderate stochastic transitivity and versions of strong independence conditions.  Forms of the models which use power indices of individual perspicacity were  BINARY CHOICE 121  suggested as reasonable specific forms of the general models. The specific loss model was fit to data from four prior experiments. The fits seem to be good enough to justify further analysis of the loss model. Because a certain type of payoff matrix in the loss model context gives the same advantage and loss ratios (see Eq. (13)), indirect support was obtained for the advantage model.
