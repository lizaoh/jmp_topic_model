Most models which assume a simple (Difference or  Ratio) resolution rule operating on independent sensations require judgment time to depend on either ratios or on differences  but not on both. By definition, when two stimuli are supraliminally different, response probability does not provide information about  relations between the members of the pair. Many authors have suggested that response latency may remain sensitive to  supraliminal differences, but the range of this sensitivity is uncertain. One purpose of the present experiment is to  investigate the extent to which response time is useful in scaling supraliminally different stimuli. In particular we sought  to determine the amount of visual extent stimulus information which remains coded in (pair-comparison) judgmental latencies  after the more overt response measure fails.  Since a pair of stimuli with extensive measures, x and y, can be completely characterized by their ratio, r = xly, and their  difference, d = x - y, it is natural to study the separate effects of d and r on decision latency. Further, such an approach  provides a powerful test of various simple analyses of psychophysical discrimination. These simple analyses, which assert that  some response measure R(x, y), on a pair of stimuli x and 17, is given by R(x, y) = F[O(x), qf(y)], have long been popular. METHOD  Subjects  Four female students between the ages of seventeen and nineteen served as subjects. All were experimentally naive volunteers  enrolled in the introductory psychology course at the University of British Columbia. Subjects were paid an hourly rate and,  in addition, were offered an incentive bonus for following instructions emphasizing speed or accuracy.  Stimuli  Nine isosceles triangles with constant base and varying height were used as stimuli. These triangles had the following  properties: 1) the ratio of the heights of triangles k ordinal steps apart was fixed; 2) these ratios monotonically increased  with k; 3) the differences between heights of triangles at each ratio increased as the heights increased.  RESOLUTION TIME 93  The physical ratios of height to base were .560, .660, .778, .917, 1.080, 1.273, 1.501, 1.769, 2.085, for stimuli 1 through 9  respectively. The heights of triangles one, two, and three steps apart in the ordering were in the ratios 1.178, 1.389 and  1.638 respectively. Pilot work indicated that all stimulus pairs could be errorlessly discriminated.  The triangles were constructed from 1/16 inch black drafting tape placed on a white matte background. Triangle pairs were  photographed to produce 35 mm slides for stimulus presentation. Pairs were formed by placing the triangles side by side with  their bases on the same horizontal line, separated by a distance equal to the base. The vertical position othe base varied  from pair to pair so that the taller triangle was always centered in the vertical field. The stimulus slides were projected  onto a smooth wall in a dimly illuminated room. Upon projection, the stimulus slide covered an area 2.4 m square with the  shortest triangle being 161 mm high. The subject viewed the stimuli from a distance of 1.8 m.  Apparatus  A Beckman 5230 EPUT counter was initiated when a beam of light from a Leitz Pradovit projector activated a photocell. The  subject's response terminated the timer and cycled the projector. Subjects responded by pressing one of two microswitches  which were mounted 50 mm apart in a response panel fixed to a table.  Design and Procedure  On each comparison trial, the stimulus slide was presented two second after a verbal ready signal, and remained continuously  available until the subject made her response. Ten seconds later, the next ready signal was given. On one-half of the  comparative judgment trials, subjects selected the "shorter" (S) triangle; on the other one-half, subjects selected the  "taller" (T) triangle. One subject under each orientation made directional judgments in the order STTS during odd numbered  sessions, and in the order TSST during the even numbered sessions. For the remaining two subjects these orders were reversed.  A total of 2,160 comparative judgments were obtained from each subject over the course of 15 experimental sessions. During  each session, a subject compared each of the thirty-six stimulus pairs four times. Order of stimulus presentation within each  replication of comparison judgments was randomized subject to the restrictions devised by Ross (1934). A different random  order was used on each replication. Each stimulus in the pair appeared equally often on the right and left under each response  direction during a session. Two subjects were randomly assigned to a speed orientation and two to an accuracy orientation.  Each subject in the relative speed orientation was offered a bonus, the size of which, depended on her response speed in  relation to the subject with which she was matched. For the relative accuracy orientation, subjects were told to take as much  time as necessary to select their response. Each subject in the accuracy orientation was offered a bonus, the size of which  depended on the relation between her error rate and that of her matched subject.  94 PETRUSIC AND JAMIESON  To familiarize subjects with the stimuli, each triangle was presented twice in a semirandom order on the initial experimental  session. The subject was then required to categorize each triangle as "tall" or "short" by pressing one of the two buttons,  which had been preassigned to categories. DISCUSSION  The analysis in Figure 1 shows that for substantial range of difference, response times decrease monotonically as stimulus  differences increase. Confirmation of these results is available in early judgment-time studies. Henmon (1906), requiring  observers to indicate which of two simultaneously-presented horizontal lines was longer, held r = 1.20 constant and varied d =  l, 2, 3, 4 mm. Munsterberg (1894), using the chain reaction method, required observers to indicate whether a comparison line  was "longer," "shorter," or the "same" length as a standard line. Within a given series he held constant the ratio of the  lengths of the comparison and standard lines, and varied the differences. Replotting the data from these early experiments in  the form of the Figure 1 analysis, in Figure 2, confirms the results othe present experiment.  The dependence of response time on stimulus ratio and difference requires broad classes of popular, long-standing, and  desireably-simple models for psychophysical discrimination to be rejected. Both the Ratio model with a power psychophysical  function and the Difference model with a Fechnerian psychophysical function are rejected because response times decrease  monotonically as stimulus differences increase. As well, these data are incompatible with the judgment time models examined by  Welford (1961), since each of those models requires response time to depend on ratios, but not on differences. . RESOLUTION TIME 101  Positive Difference Structure (PDS)  We used two sets of data in our tests of the PDS axioms, and in subsequent analyses. One analysis was based on summary  judgment time statistics for the stimulus pairs, for individual subjects and collapsed across S2, S3, and S4. The second  analysis was based on a matrix of pairwise `similarity judgments' inferred from individual response latencies with various  pairs. This latter measure is discussed in greater detail in the Transitivity section, below.  Krantz et al. (1971) state the requirements for the construction of a PDS in the form of six axioms. Two of these axioms, the  Solvability and Archimedian conditions, are untestable, but they are not unreasonable in the present context of vertical  visual extent. Two other axioms, Closure and Transitivity, assert that an ordering of inter-point distances can be  established, and are satisfied by the judgment time response measure. The major tests come from the remaining two axioms,  Positivity and Monotonicity, and from tests of Transitivity using individual judgment times as implicit similarity judgments. CONCLUDING COMMENTS  Our data, together with those of Henmon (1906) and Munsterberg (1894) demonstrate the direct sensitivity of judgment time to  physical relations, defined on supraliminally different visual extents. For the range in which judgment times are sensitive to  physical differences between stimuli, Weber's law must be rejected. Interestingly, we find that the domain of Weber's law thus  reduces to two very different situations: one well-known range in which stimuli are very similar and confusable and a second  situation, seen in the flat late portions of Figure 1, in which stimuli are extremely different and non-confusable! Between  these two extremes, Weber's law fails to apply.  These data also force rejection of several long-standing, and commonly-applied conjunctions of simple psychophysical functions  with the Ratio and Difference decision rules.  106 PETRUSIC AND JAMIESON  Among those models which remain acceptable is a power psychophysical function with < 1, conjoined with a Difference rule for  stimulus comparison time.  The present results also show that latencies for directional judgments with pairs of simultaneously-presented visual extents  can be well-represented in terms of a Positive Difference Structure, extending the demonstrated range of PDS application in  psychophysics from loudness (Schneider, Parker, and Stein, 1974). Further analyses determine the psychophysical function to be  a power function with P slightly less than 1. Overall then, the results of two very different sets of analyses, examining  quite different aspects of the present data, show remarkable convergence to the conclusion that P < 1 for visual extent.  Among the most intriguing aspects of the present data is the remarkable sensitivity of the judgment times to the physical  relations between the two stimuli of the various pairs. An interesting conjecture is that such exquisite sensitivity may  ultimately demand the development of relational models for psychophysical tasks such as the one considered in the present  paper.
