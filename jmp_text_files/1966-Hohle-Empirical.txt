Procedures are presented for obtaining maximum likelihood scale values from pair  comparison and ranking data for both models, and a goodness-of-fit statistic for testing either model is derived.  Application of tests of the models on six sets of data indicated that (a) neither model provided uniformly satisfactory  representations of the data, and (b) all six sets of data were more accurately represented by Model 11 than by Model 1. PROCEDURE  Model I and Model II scales were derived from six different pairwise proportion matrices. Four of the matrices were obtained  from direct pair comparison judgments; the other two were inferred from repeated rankings of the stimuli (pairwise proportions  were obtained from ranking data by determining the ratios of the number of times each stimulus was ranked higher than each  other stimulus to the total number of presentations of the set of stimuli for ranking). The matrices obtained from rankings  were from judgments of two of the sets of stimuli for which direct pair comparison judgments had also been obtained. Three of  the sets of pair comparison data and one set of rank data were taken from published studies; the remaining data were collected  by the writer. The data analyzed included the following:  Lifted weights (Guilford, 1931). Seven weights, weighing from 185 to 215 grams, were used as stimuli. A single observer was  presented each pair of weights 100 times and was asked to make judgments as to which weight was heavier (no "equal" judgments  were allowed).  Vegetable preferences (Guilford, 1954). Preference judgments were obtained from 148 observers for each pair from nine common  vegetables.  Handwriting samples (Hevner, 1930). In this study, 20 specimens of handwriting were selected from a pool of 320 such specimens  so that a wide range of quality was  180 HOHLE  represented. All 190 pairs of specimens from the set of 20 were presented to 370 observers for comparisons with respect to  quality. The same 20 handwriting specimens were also presented simultaneously to the 370 observers with instructions to rank  them from "best" to "worst." From these data the number of times each specimen was ranked higher than each other specimen was  tabulated, yielding a proportion matrix comparable to that obtained from the direct pair comparison judgments.  Numerousness. Six 3 X 4-inch cards containing 16, 20, 25, 31, 37, and 43 spots were judged for numerousness by 15 preschool  children. In one session the cards were presented in pairs and the observer was asked which card he thought had more spots on  it. Each of the 15 pairs of cards was presented four times to each observer so that a total of 60 judgments was obtained for  each pair. In another session each observer was presented all six cards simultaneously, and was asked to select the card  containing the most spots. The selected card was then removed and the observer chose the card with the most spots from those  remaining, this process being repeated until only one card was left. The entire precedure was repeated 12 times for each  observer. The spatial positionings of the cards as they were presented over the 12 replications were arranged so that each  stimulus card appeared twice in each spatial position, and appeared adjacent to each other card four times, twice on each  side. The resulting 180 rankings of the stimulus cards were then tabulated to yield a pairwise proportion matrix.  An IBM 7040 computer was programmed to solve Eqs. 5 and 6, in which the methods described above were used, to obtain Model I  and Model II scales for each of the six sets of data. In solving for the Model I scale values, the iterative process was  continued until no scale value following the nth iteration differed from its values after the (n - 1)th by more than .0005.  The number of iterations required to meet this criterion ranged from four for the lifted weights data to six for each of the  sets of handwriting samples data. Equations 6 were solved successively for the Model II S-scale values until, for additional  iterations,  s?n+l) - SZn) IISin) .0005  for i = 1, ..., m.  This required from 14 to 179 iterations, with the vegetable data requiring the fewest, and the handwriting samples (ranking)  data requiring the greatest number of iterations. The origin of the Model I scale was fixed in each case by translating the  scale such that the smallest value was zero; the unit of each of the Model II scales was fixed by multiplying each scale value  by the reciprocal of the smallest value, thus making the smallest value equal to one.  The chi-square statistic, Y, was computed for each of the models, for each set of data, and, as an additional indication of  goodness of fit of each model, the mean absolute discrepancies between the observed proportions and those derived from the  fitted scale values were computed. The relatively low mean discrepancies between observed and derived pairwise proportions suggest that both models provide at  least approximate representations of these data. The goodness-of-fit statistics indicate that neither model is uniformly  acceptable, however. If the .01 significance level is adopted as the criterion for rejecting the models, then four of the six  sets of data require rejection of Model I, and three require that Model II be rejected.  One possible interpretation of this apparent failure of both models to account for several of the sets of data is that some  experimental dependence among the pairwise judgments other than that due to the constraints implied by the models was  introduced in obtaining these data. Such dependence among the judgments could be present whenever the stimuli to be judged are  readily identifiable such that the observer may be aware of how he has judged particular pairs of stimuli. If he has chosen A  over B, and B over C, for example-and he remembers these choices-he might choose A over C simply to be consistent in ordering  the stimuli, even though he might occasionally have chosen C over A had this pair been presented first. This source of  dependence is inevitably introduced, of course, when pair comparisons are inferred from rankings of the timuli. Consistent  with this interpretation is the fact that the two sets of data that do not provide any evidence for rejecting either model  (the lifted weights data and the direct pair comparison numerousness data) did not involve judgments of easily identifiable  stimuli, and since the pairs were presented separately, there was little opportunity for the observers to attempt to order the  stimuli.  Table 2 also provides data for comparing the two models. For each of the six sets of data, the goodness-of-fit (chi square)  statistic is smaller for Model II than for Model I. In other words, the likelihood ratio was uniformly greater when Model II  was assumed. Similarly, all the average discrepancies between observed and derived proportions were slightly lower for the  Model II scales. The statistical significance of this apparent superiority of Model 11 may be assessed by considering that, if  the two models provided equally good representations of the data, the probability that all six sets of data would favor one  model is 1/32.  Some writers have indicated a preference for the Bradley and Terry-Ford-Luce model (i.e., Model 11) over the Thurstone model  (Model I) because the former is mathematically less complex and entails fewer assumptions. The present study seems to give  this model a slight edge on empirical grounds also, though the range of conditions under which the model is applicable may be  somewhat less than has been generally assumed.
