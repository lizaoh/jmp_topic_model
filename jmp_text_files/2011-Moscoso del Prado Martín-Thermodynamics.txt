Ever since its introduction by  Donders (1869)  in the very early days of experimental psychology, reaction time (RT) has been among the most widely used measures of cognitive processing in human and animal behavioural experiments. In general, following Donders’ seminal work, the logic underlying the analysis of data in RT experiments is that, information processing takes time, thus the average time taken to initiate or complete a task reflects the duration of the mental process(es) that are involved in the task. Therefore, if certain types of stimuli, tasks, or groups of subjects elicit longer RTs than others, it is generally inferred that the former involve more cognitive processing than the latter. In this study, I propose a qualitatively different perspective on the understanding of RTs. Rather than focusing on whether some experimental conditions elicit shorter or longer RTs than others, I investigate whether different conditions elicit RT distributions with different degrees of  complexity , in the sense of entropy. I argue that an increase in the complexity of the RT distribution constitutes an indirect measure of the amount of information processing that has been performed by the system. For this, I take a psychologically  naïve , model-free, approach. I draw inferences on the neural/psychological processes by studying only the complexity of the RTs. This new approach enables moving from studying how much information is contained by stimuli or tasks, to directly investigating the amount of this information that is in fact processed.

Information Theory has a long history in the study of behaviour. Very soon after Shannon’s development of information theory in telecommunications ( Shannon, 1948 ), psychologists were applying it to the study of human RTs (see  Miller, 1956 , and  Laming, 1968 , for extensive reviews of these early arguments). Following this early enthusiasm, serious problems were detected with the basic ways that the information-theoretical concepts were being applied. As a consequence, several influential researchers have since become less optimistic about the value of information-theoretical techniques for the study of cognition ( Baddeley, 1994; Laming, 2001; Luce, 2003 ). Notwithstanding these, in recent years there has been a growing interest in the application of information-theoretical techniques to the study of behaviour. An important innovation is that the researchers have begun to use the information-theoretical tools to address not only the properties of the stimuli, as the early approaches did, but also the responses themselves. In fact, these techniques have become common in the analysis of cognitively relevant neurophysiological signals including single cell recordings (see  Borst & Theunissen, 1999 , for a review), ECoG data (e.g.,  Freeman, 2008 ), deep electrode and EEG recordings (e.g.,  Staniek & Lehnertz, 2008 ), and some researchers have successfully applied information-theoretical tools to behavioural responses ( Allen, Kaufman, Smith, & Propper, 1998a,b; Allen, Murphy, Kaufman, Groth, & Begovic, 2004; Cavanaugh, Mercer, & Stergiou, 2007 ).

One of the early products of the introduction of information theory in psychology is still one of the few robust laws of experimental psychology: The time it takes to make a choice is linearly related to the entropy of the possible alternatives. This is now referred to as Hick’s Law ( Hellyer, 1963; Hick, 1952; Hyman, 1953 ). Some exceptions to this law have been noted in specific experimental situations ( e.g. ,  Kveraga, Boucher, & Hughes, 2002; Longstreth & Alcorn, 1987  and  Longstreth, El-Zahhar, & Alcorn, 1985 ; but see also  Welford, 1987  for a view that disputes the conclusions of Longstreth et al. ), that have been referred to as “boundary conditions” ( Wagenmakers & Brown, 2007 ). Beyond these, the main empirical claim of this law is still widely accepted, and it is of great importance for models of cognitive processing (e.g.,  McMillen & Holmes, 2006; Usher & McClelland, 2001  and  Usher, Olami, & McClelland, 2002 ), research on cognitive ageing (e.g.,  Allen et al., 2004 ), or Human–Computer Interaction (see  Seow, 2005  for a review). More recently, a direct relation between the perceptual information content of stimuli and RTs has been found in psychophysical studies ( Norwich, 1993; Norwich, Seburn, & Axelrad, 1989 ). Interestingly, it is suggested that the rate at which information is collected could vary with the intensity of the stimulus ( Norwich, 1993 ). Indeed, for higher cognitive functions–language in particular–evidence has been presented recently that the rate of information processing is not constant, rather it is linearly related to the average informational load of the stimuli ( i.e. , words) in a particular experimental context ( Kostić, 1995 ). These proposals go against the spirit of Hick’s Law in that information processing speed might not be constant within an experiment after all. In this study I will investigate the implications of a generalization of Hick’s Law, with the focus shifted from the amount of information that is  carried  by the stimuli, to the amount of information about them that is actually  processed .

This study introduces a new approach to understand behavioural data using insights from information theory and thermodynamics. I focus on lexical stimuli, for which the cognitive relevance of informational measures has been shown (e.g.,  Altman & Carter, 1989; Baayen & Moscoso del Prado Martín, 2005; Filipović-Đurđević, 2007; Kostić, 1991, 1995; Kostić, Marković, & Baucal, 2003; McDonald & Shillcock, 2001; Milin, Filipović-Đurđević, & Moscoso del Prado Martín, 2009; Moscoso del Prado Martín, 2007  and  Moscoso del Prado Martín, Kostić, & Baayen, 2004 ). Sections  2 and 3  provide the formulation of the theory, and the practical computations involved. Section  4  introduces an empirical test of the theory. This makes use of two large sets from the English Lexicon Project database (ELP;  Balota et al., 2007 ) of English visual lexical decision and word naming data. The empirical evidence consists of four analyses. The first one investigates the relationship between the temporal entropy variable ( i.e. , complexity of RTs described above) with the traditional average RT (magnitude of RTs) interpretation of response latency data. This serves as a first test of the plausibility of the approach, and it reveals its relation to Hick’s Law. The second part of the analysis tests the power of the method to distinguish between the Visual Lexical Decision and Word Naming tasks, despite the great similarity of their average RTs. The third part provides a direct test of the theoretical development (expressed by Eq.  (2)  below) for particular properties of the stimuli. Finally, the fourth part goes to further depth about the implications of the relationship between mean RT and temporal entropy, and how these implications provide strong evidence against a generalization of Hick’s Law. Further, in the Discussion, I argue how the relation between the temporal entropy and the mean RT can provide a sort of ‘phase diagram’ of a particular task across conditions. This diagram provides indications of possible phase transitions reflecting the change in psychological mechanisms employed in a task.

The cognitive system can be considered a system in the thermodynamical sense of the word. In particular, it is an open system that exchanges energy (and information) with its environment. Performing an experimental task involves an exchange of information with the environment. The experimental instructions and the presentation of stimuli are a source of information. Performing the experimental task requires the processing of this external information, and information processing is costly in energetic and informational terms. As discussed by  Brillouin (1956) , the acquisition of information by any part of a system must be offset with a decrease of information somewhere else. In Brillouin’s terms there is a balance between gained and lost ‘negentropy’, that is, information. Having received energy and information, the stimulus is processed and a response is initiated. Once more, this process involves a further exchange of negentropy and energy with the environment. An ideal system with perfect efficiency could perhaps achieve a perfect balance between the received, and the spent negentropy. However, as the efficiency is never perfect, some negentropy will be lost in the process. In other words, information processing constitutes an adiabatic process, that is, an irreversible process ( i.e. , as information has been lost, one cannot just return to the previous state of the system), and these are bound to increase the entropy of a system. Eventually, in the case of the cognitive system, this loss of negentropy can be compensated for by a supply of energy, normally by metabolic means, that would enable the system to return to its ‘resting’ state. In short, the processing of experimental stimuli should temporarily increase the entropy of the cognitive system by an amount directly proportional to the amount of information that has been processed, corresponding to the negentropy that was wasted in the process. In essence, a measure of the increases in the entropy of the cognitive system elicited by different experimental conditions or stimuli would provide an estimate of the amount of information that has been processed (see  Kirkaldy, 1965 , for a physical perspective on neural information processing).

Measuring the overall state of entropy of the cognitive system might not be an easy task, as it would involve a quantification of the uncertainty in the state of all the microscopic units in the system. However, collateral measures of the ‘noise’ emitted by the system should reflect increases in its state of complexity. This is to say, if the system is in a higher state of complexity, the noises it emits will also increase in their complexity. For instance, it has recently been reported that the entropy of the adjustments in body posture while subjects are performing a dual cognitive task is larger than that measured when they are performing a single task, reflecting the increased amount of cognitive information processing that is involved in dual tasks ( Cavanaugh et al., 2007 ). Likewise, the random variability of times at which responses happen in a particular experimental condition can be considered as part of the noise emitted by the system. Therefore the uncertainty ( i.e.,  entropy, in its statistical sense;  Shannon, 1948 ) of the distribution of RTs can be taken to reflect the state of the system that generated them. A similar argument has been exploited recently by Allen and his colleagues, who have repeatedly shown that–taken together with the mean RTs–the entropy of the probability of correct and incorrect responses in behavioural experiments is an indicator of the amount of cognitive processing that has been performed ( Allen et al., 1998a,b, 2004 ). Similar to Allen’s, but potentially enabling a finer resolution, my hypothesis is that one can measure the entropy of the distribution of the RTs themselves in a particular condition, and make inferences about variations in the entropy (in its physical sense) of the underlying system. In short, an increase in the informational entropy of an RT distribution should be directly proportional to the amount of information that has been processed.

In common with most previous work on RT distributions, I assume that the RTs constitute a stationary sequence. By stationary it is meant that the properties of the RT distribution remain constant through a sequence of RTs. Furthermore, I also assume the sequence of RTs to be ergodic.

Under these assumptions, the probability distribution of the RTs constitutes what is called a natural invariant measure, and its Shannon entropy coincides with the dynamical Kolmogorov–Sinai (KS) entropy of the system (cf.,  Beck & Schlögl, 1993 ). The KS entropy is a fundamental quantity in the Theory of Dynamical Systems; it is an unambiguous metric of the intrinsic complexity of a dynamical system. My hypothesis is therefore that the entropy of the RT distribution–the KS entropy of the sequence of RTs–to which I will refer as the  temporal entropy  provides an index of the complexity of the RTs, and by implication of the system that generated them. Interestingly, this reasoning is remarkably parallel to a current direction in the field of evolutionary biology. In Directionality Theory (see  Demetrius, 1997, 2004 , and references therein) it is shown that the entropy of the distribution of lifetimes of individuals of a particular species or group provides an intrinsic index of their metabolic rates.

The main proposal of this study is that individual trial RTs from psychological experiments can be analysed in terms of their  temporal entropy , that is, the differential entropy ( Shannon, 1948 ) of their distribution. In practice, this shares a central part of the methodology with many more traditional techniques, inasmuch as the analysis concentrates in estimating the mean of a quantity across different conditions and/or groups of participants. In principle, one could just compute the self-information separately for different experimental conditions and/or subjects, and then apply one’s favourite regression technique or ANOVA to estimate effects on the mean, as one would typically do for untransformed or logged RTs. However, in a typical experimental situation, the number of RTs obtained for each individual subject in each experimental condition is–save perhaps for exceptionally large experiments–rather sparse. This would lead to dangerously imprecise estimates of the probability density function, and this error would be propagated and magnified in the subsequent analyses. This would be even more problematic in regression designs where the manipulated condition is a continuous variable; it would force us to also define a discretization bin for the predictor variable, and then estimate the densities separately for each bin. One can however take advantage of the  extensivity  of Shannon’s entropy, the fact that the entropy of a combination of two independent random variables is just the sum of their respective entropies. This implies that one can estimate the self-information for all RTs in the experiment using one single grand histogram across all subjects and conditions. This grand histogram represents the overall probability of an RT in the experiment, and as it will be based on a much larger sample than any individual subject or condition, it will be much more accurate. By extensivity, the entropy of the overall histogram will be the sum of the entropies of the variables ( e.g. , subjects, items, conditions, …) that influence the RTs. One can then use the regression analyses to separate the additive contributions of subjects, items, and experimental conditions using the fixed and random effect estimates of the regression. Notice that this also enables the contribution to the temporal entropy of different continuous regressors to be assessed without any need for discretizing them.

In this study, I take advantage of the reasoning above to estimate the temporal entropies for different items, and the contribution of different regressors to the entropy, using a mixed effect model technique (MEM; see  Baayen, 2007; Baayen, Davidson, & Bates, 2008; Bates, 2005 , for recent introductions to using this technique). An important advantage of MEMs is that they estimate explicitly the additive adjustments to regression provided by random effects such of subjects or items, thus enabling one to estimate the individual contribution of each of these to the temporal entropy in one single combined analysis. In particular, the theoretical model that I am proposing ( i.e. , the reasoning behind using the temporal entropy as a measure of cognitive processing), provides a very concise and constrained MEM formulation (see  Appendix A  for a detailed derivation). I retrieved from the ELP database ( Balota et al., 2007 ) the individual lexical decision RTs and word naming latencies to 1986 nouns and verbs. The set of selected words corresponds to those words used in a previous study that compared the lexical decision and naming tasks ( Baayen, Feldman, & Schreuder, 2006 ). The selection of only a subset of the words enables us to keep the models computationally tractable. All responses that were not marked as correct in the database were excluded from further analysis. In addition, I also excluded all responses equal or longer than 4000 ms, as beyond this limit responses appear truncated in the ELP database. This left a total of 64,087 responses (from 816 different participants) in the lexical decision dataset and 53,403 (from 445 participants) in the naming one. Along with the individual RTs, the surface frequency of the words (extracted from the CELEX database;  Baayen, Piepenbrock, & Gulikers, 1995 ) and their length in characters was also recorded.

To investigate the relationship between temporal entropy and mean RTs (Sections  5.1, 5.2 and 5.4 ), I fitted three mixed effect models (MEMs) to the data (using models identical to those illustrated in  Appendix C  for a smaller dataset). In all three cases, the models included an intercept, and two random effects, one of word and one of subject, as predictors. The first two models had the RT [in either untransformed or logarithmic scale] as dependent variable (so that the average predictions of the model correspond to the mean RTs), and the third model had the self-information of the RTs as independent variable (thus as argued above, the average predictions of the model correspond to temporal entropies). The individual estimates of either mean RT or temporal entropy for each word were computed as the sum of the corresponding model’s intercept with the particular random effect adjustment for that word. 
                            6 
                         
                         
                            6 
                            All models shared–to greater or lesser degrees–the problem that the distribution of the residuals deviated from normality, which is a precondition for the Gaussian family MEM used here. To ensure that the estimates obtained were robust despite this violation, all analyses were repeated using a range of distribution families, and the results were consistent in all cases.

To investigate the predictions on the informational content of stimuli, I performed MEM regressions on the temporal self-information and on the log RTs in each task. As before, these regressions included random effects of both subject and word. In addition, I also included fixed effect covariates measuring the lexical self-information and the average information content of its letters. An additional factor needed to be considered in these MEMs: The measure of the informational content of the letters in a word refers to the average case. However, different words will contain combinations of letters that contain more or less information than the average ( i.e. , they are either more frequent than usual or rarer than usual). Therefore, the effect of the information of the letters can vary with respect to word identity. To address this problem, the MEM models included the possibility of a random slope making the effect of letter-based information variable for different words. Similarly, the experience that different people have had with different words varies in both quantitative and qualitative terms. This was accounted for by introducing a random slope that enables the variation of the lexical self-information effect across individual participants. 
                            7 
                         
                         
                            7 
                            All MEM regressions were fitted using a restricted maximum likelihood algorithm, as implemented in the  R  package  lme4  ( Bates, 2005 ).

The first question that arises is how do temporal entropies relate to the traditional reading of the RTs being short or long. I investigated the relationship between both measures for individual words. For this, I used MEM regressions both on the plain (and logged) RTs, and on the RT self-information. The relation between the random effect adjustments for individual words between models (corrected by the corresponding general intercept) summarized the relationship between the temporal entropy and RT.

Fig. 2  plots the relation between the self-information for individual responses and the corresponding RTs. Note that this relation is non-trivial; for the bulk of the responses, it follows a non-linear U-shaped pattern, as argued above, it is non-monotonic. Therefore one cannot directly assume a simple linear relation between both measures. In contrast,  Fig. 3 
                          compares the estimated mean RT with the temporal entropies for each word in each of the tasks. The dashed lines plot the best fitting linear regression between both measures. The figure suggests that the temporal entropies are linearly related to the average RTs. This apparently linear relation is very strong, with explained variance values of 64% in Word Naming and up to 81% in Visual Lexical Decision. At first sight, this finding could be understood as a stronger restatement of Hick’s Law ( Hellyer, 1963; Hick, 1952; Hyman, 1953 ): The law states that the average RT to a stimulus is directly proportional to the informational content of the stimulus. The current results seem to suggest that the amount of information processing caused by the stimuli is also directly proportional to the average RT. This would indicate that there is a constant–possibly task-dependent–information processing speed of the cognitive system. This would go one step further than Hick’s Law by saying that the average RT would be directly proportional to the amount of information that has been  processed  (rather than just contained by the stimulus). Although this would be a suggestive interpretation, some caution needs to be taken before accepting this conclusion. In Section  5.4 , I will provide a more in-depth study of the issue of information processing speed and its implications for the plausibility of generalizing Hick’s Law. I now turn to investigate the effect of the informational contents of stimuli and task complexity on the amount of information processing.

I now return to the relationship between mean RTs and temporal entropy, illustrated by  Fig. 3 . In Section  5.1 , I asked the reader to delay judgement on the shape of that relation, despite the seemingly linear appearance of the plots. In this study, I have introduced the temporal entropy measure hypothesizing that it should provide an index of the amount of processing performed by the cognitive system. Analyses II and III have indicated that this may actually be the case, temporal entropy enables to distinguish the responses to two tasks that are otherwise extremely similar, and informational aspects of the stimuli are reflected in temporal entropy. On the other hand, mean RT is perhaps the oldest measure of the amount of cognitive processing, with an extremely large collection of experiments indicating that mean RT is sensitive to all kinds of cognitive manipulations. The question that arises is then, what is the relationship between these two measures?

Note here that the question above should have been asked  before  looking at the data, that is  Fig. 3 . To that question, based only on the frail theoretical premises on which I have based my argument, the only honest answer that one could have given is: I do not know at all. However, and this is the important point, the interrogation does not end here. Having acknowledged my ignorance, the next question that I need to ask myself is: So, what is the most probable shape of that relation based only on my ignorance? It may seem unintuitive that one could make any kind of inferences based only on one’s own lack of knowledge, but that is precisely what Jaynes’ fundamental Principle of Maximum Entropy ( Jaynes, 1957a,b ) showed (for an accessible introduction to the Principle of Maximum Entropy from the statistics perspective see  Beck & Schlögl, 1993  and  Sivia & Skilling, 2006  from the point of view of thermodynamics). The point of doing this theoretical analysis before actually looking at the data is that, in order for any statistical induction to be possible ( i.e. , for instance, saying that the relation in  Fig. 3  is linear), one  always  needs to have a  prior  distribution ( Jaynes, 1968, 2003 ). The Principle of Maximum Entropy, combined with the Transformation Groups argument of  Jaynes (1968)  to ensure consistency ( i.e. , to guarantee that two different ignorant observers should arrive at the same prior), offers the method for constructing such priors.

To apply the Principle of Maximum Entropy, we evaluate the knowledge we do have. First, we know for sure that RTs will be positive, as we have no way of measuring negative time. Second, our data question concerns the mean RT, which is after all a variable for which a lot of previous knowledge does exist; I could have even obtained rather good estimates of the individual mean RTs for each item in my set by just taking the data from  Baayen et al. (2006) . Therefore, I can assume that I will know a priori an estimate of the mean RT for each item (

This study has introduced a novel interpretation for RT experiments. The conventional approach is to consider how long responses take to occur. Instead, I proposed to investigate whether the temporal distribution of responses is more or less  complex . I argued that this complexity of the reaction time distribution reflects the underlying state of complexity in the cognitive system, and the empirical evidence has supported this view. This enables a shift from studying how much information is contained in stimuli or tasks, to directly investigating the amount of this information that is actually processed.

As evidenced by the comparison between Visual Lexical Decision and Word Naming, the temporal entropy measure is remarkably more sensitive than the traditional RT magnitudes–whether in untransformed or logarithmic scale–in distinguishing tasks with different properties. There has been a growing interest in techniques that enable going beyond the mean in the description of RT data (see  e.g. ,  Balota, Yap, Cortese, & Watson, 2008; Heathcote, Popiel, & Mewhort, 1991; Holden et al., 2009; Ratcliff, 1978; Rouder, Lu, Speckman, Sun, & Jiang, 2005; Van Zandt, 2002 , and references therein for some of the more current approaches, and the monograph of  Luce, 1986  for an extensive review of earlier attempts). These proposals can be broadly clustered in two groups. The first consists in applying non-parametric techniques for inspecting the shape of the probability distribution, with the current preference being the use of quantiles (or their ‘vincentile’ variation), or estimators of the hazard function (see  Luce, 1986  for review, and  Holden et al., 2009 , for a more recent instance). These approaches share with the one proposed here that no particular distributional shape needs to be assumed, and thus the results do no rest crucially on such assumptions. Hazard rates are particularly useful, as the shape of their right tails can distinguish between different groups of distributions, and the psychological processes they represent. However, two serious difficulties are found when working with hazard rates. The first, is that they are notably difficult to estimate reliably, and the estimates obtained are often too coarse grained to support strong claims on the distribution that generated them. The second, that is shared with all the approaches that follow, is that they need to be fitted separately for different subjects and conditions, and they do not provide a natural mechanism for studying the effect of continuous predictors (these require binning and plotting different hazard functions for each). As a consequence the results loose accuracy due to the reduced number of points that is often found when one considers single subject and/or conditions separately, and a single pass linear model-like analysis is not possible. Instead, one has to proceed in multiple steps, with the error of each step compounding. Also, no mechanism is provided to enable the description of the change in shape of the hazard functions across a set of experimental conditions in  one single  representation. In a similar spirit, quantile-based approaches present the advantage of being notably simple to compute. However, their interpretation may not be straightforward as one often has many conditions that differ on several quantiles, but it becomes difficult to attribute meaning to each difference. As was the case with hazard rates, this approach is also constrained by having to make the computations separately for different subjects and/or conditions.

The parametric approach, which has gained considerable momentum during the last decade, relies on assuming  a priori  that the RTs are well approximated by a particular probability distribution family. The distributions employed in this kind of techniques vary from distributions that implement the predictions of a particular cognitive model, as is the case of the broadly used Drift–Diffusion Model (DDM;  Ratcliff, 1978 , and subsequent studies), the reciprocal Gaussian distribution implementing Carpenter’s LATER model ( Carpenter, 1981 , and subsequent studies), the distribution of responses in the related Linear Ballistic Accumulator model (LBA;  Brown & Heathcote, 2008 ), or in distributions that do not ascribe to any particular model, but are empirically thought to capture RT distributions relatively well, as is the case with the popular Ex-Gaussian distribution ( McGill, 1963 ; see  Balota et al., 2008 , for a recent example). As compared to the non-parametric methods, the parametric approaches present the advantage of providing a few parameters that index the effects of different cognitive manipulations (note however that, in some cases such as the more recent version of the DDM, the number of parameters can grow considerably, making the fitting of the model and the interpretation of results relatively difficult). As a further advantage, in some cases a theory-driven interpretation can be given to changes in the values of those parameters. One evident inconvenience is however that the validity of the analyses ultimately rests on the RT distribution being well approximated by the specific parametric model that was assumed. The more model-driven methods, such as LATER, LBA, or the DDM, usually circumvent this problem by setting boundary conditions on the type of experiments and RT magnitudes on which the models are valid, but this comes at the cost of not providing a general mechanism for making inferences on RT data in general. Another problem is that, as was the case with non-parametric models, normally the models require separate fits for individual subjects and conditions, forcing again the two step analysis. 
                         12 
                      
                      
                         12 
                         Important progress has recently been made in this direction with parametric regression techniques that enable simultaneously fitting multiple parameters of a distribution as a function of the predictors. A promising approach in this direction is the GAMLSS technique developed by  Rigby and Stasinopoulos (2005) , notably including the Ex-Gaussian distribution. Although the current implementation still has problems dealing with multiple random effects and large datasets, much can be expected from upcoming developments.

The approach I have introduced in this study shares properties with both the parametric and the non-parametric methods. On the one hand, the method is distribution-free, and therefore does not depend on the validity of any assumptions on the shape of the RT distribution. On the other hand, the method provides one single index–the temporal entropy–on which the amount of cognitive processing can be assessed, taking the advantage of the parametric models to its single parameter extreme. As I have described, this index can be accurately estimated by just computing a histogram lumping together subjects and conditions, and then performing a plain regression analysis, as one would do with the traditional central measures of RTs such as the arithmetic or geometric mean. In brief, the method consists only in a non-monotonic transformation of the individual trial RTs. This is only slightly more complex than computing the logarithm or any other monotonic transformation of the type that are widely used. From there, the analyses can proceed using basic regression or ANOVA techniques. However, I strongly recommend the MEM methodology employed here, for it enables one to take advantage of the entropy’s additivity to explicitly separate the different contributions of multiple subjects, items,  etc . In my opinion, this is considerably simpler than working with the full distributional shape using either parametric or non-parametric methods, both in terms of practical implementation and of interpretation of the results. Yet the measure is remarkably powerful, as evidenced by the results in Section  5.2 . Having said this, it is important to clarify that the methodology proposed here is not so much an alternative to model-driven parametric distributional analyses, as it is a complement to it. As I detail below, the new approach enables a macroscopic consideration of the cognitive system, which is useful for making theoretical arguments across models and mechanisms. However, for a microscopic description of the important details of the system, parametric model-driven techniques such as LATER, LBA, or the DDM remain necessary.

An interesting finding of this study is that, for both tasks studied, a remarkably consistent relation (see  Fig. 3 ) was observed between the mean RT to a word and the corresponding temporal entropy. Furthermore, in both cases the relation could be accurately described by a simple linear relation including the mean RT, the mean log RT, and the mean square log RT, given in Eq.  (8) . For the data studied this relation appears to be relatively monotonic, save perhaps for the small hook at the left part of the curve for the word naming task. However, if one takes Eq.  (8)  to be an accurate representation of the data, one should notice that, for the range of parameter values estimated in Section  5.4 , this makes two interesting predictions. The first is that the relation between mean RT and temporal entropy, for the shortest mean RTs, should reverse in direction, a trace of which is indeed indicated by the hook mentioned above. The second, which is also visible in the smoother for the Word Naming task in  Fig. 3  is that the very long mean RTs, the relation should be significantly dampened. In short, these curves predict that the relation between mean RT and temporal entropy has at least three basic regimes. An approximately linear regime covers the midrange of mean RTs, indicating that in this region one should expect to observe the type of relation that would be predicted from generalizing Hick’s Law, with mean RT directly proportional to the mean temporal entropy. However, in the left part of the curves, this relationship should be fully reversed, causing mean RT be negatively related to the temporal entropy. This later prediction is rather counterintuitive. If one interprets the temporal entropy as an indicator of the amount of information processing, this would mean that in that region an increase in information processing is associated with a decrease in processing time. Finally, in the longer RTs, the relation should be stronger than a linear relation, this is, an increase in processed information corresponds to more than a linear increase in mean RT–a ‘super-Hick’ relation.

A main point being made in this study is that the curve represented by Eq. To understand what it is meant by this, it is useful to swap the axes in  Fig. 3 .  Fig. 6

This can be thought as a sort of phase diagram of the task; it indicates what change in mean RT one should expect to observe in association with a change in amount of processed information. The first thing that jumps out of the picture is its markedly biphasic nature (with a further third phase corresponding to the change of slope). The points labelled  B  and  C  both correspond to an equivalent amount of cognitive processing, yet the mean RT for  C  is remarkably longer than for  B . Arguing that the overall amount of information processed in both cases is the same suggests therefore that the  mechanism  processing the information is not the same in these two cases. These three ‘phases’ go in parallel with the “boundary conditions” that have been described to affect the existing empirical laws for RTs, such as Hick’s Law ( Longstreth et al., 1985 ) and the linear relation between the mean and the standard deviation of the RTs (MSD; 
                      Wagenmakers & Brown, 2007 ). For instance, Wagenmakers and Brown list three conditions that determine that the MSD relation deviates from linearity. The very fast RTs can correspond to situations when multiple mechanisms are simultaneously at work, and thus the DDM does not apply to this ‘mixture’ situation. As reviewed by  Longstreth et al. (1985)  it is also in very fast tasks having strong stimulus–response associations that Hick’s Law breaks down, Longstreth and colleagues cite using of oral responses among the cases that are problematic for Hick’s Law, and indeed, our study shows the breakdown of the relation more clearly in the Word Naming dataset than in the Visual Lexical Decision one.  Kveraga et al. (2002)  reported similar results for eye movement tasks. They showed that fast saccades having strong stimulus–response mappings also fail to exhibit Hick’s Law. On the other hand, very long RTs may correspond to situations in which exhaustive serial processing happens (e.g.,  Sternberg, 1966; Thornton & Gilden, 2007 ). In these situations, both the MSD relation and Hick’s Law break down. In the very long, allegedly serial RTs, Wagenmakers and Brown note that the MSD becomes quadratic ( i.e. , linear with variance), and Hick’s Law becomes closer to a power-law ( Longstreth et al., 1985 ) or an exponential ( i.e. , RT becomes proportional to the number of alternatives, rather than to its logarithm). If one now reconsiders the three phases in  Fig. 6 , one finds that, for the short mean RTs, Hick’s linear relation actually reverses in direction. In contrast, for the very late RTs, the relation between temporal entropy and mean RT becomes steeper than linear, as would be predicted by the serial processing hypothesis. Finally, in between these two phases, the relation is well described by a straight line. The advantage of the model-free property of the temporal entropy becomes now apparent, as one can use it as a sketch of the different psychological processes and mechanisms that are involved in task under different conditions. I believe that this diagram can be a very useful tool for disentangling the mechanisms that govern cognition.

It is also useful to consider what happens at the point labelled  A . As discussed above, the graph suggests that two different cognitive mechanisms co-exist for the task. At points  B  and  C  one of these two mechanisms dominates the behaviour, but at point  A , the influence of both is roughly balanced. Therefore, increasing the amount of information processing in  A  will have different consequences depending on which mechanism deals with the information that was added. This suggests that there may be different  types  of information that are processed differently. An important implication is that Hick’s Law cannot be directly generalized to processed information. Strictly speaking, the law proposed by Hick and others (e.g.,  Hellyer, 1963; Hick, 1952  and  Hyman, 1953 ) concerned only the relation between information contained in the stimuli and mean RT. Crucially, in this study, I extend Hick’s argument to the information about the stimuli that was processed, this is to say, the relevant information. In this case, the relationship between processed information and mean RT is not linear, although for a particular range, it might seem linear. Maximum Entropy theoretical analysis suggested that the generalization of Hick’s Law as a linear relation was unparsimonious, this is to say, a non-starter. The analysis suggested that one would likely find an effect of the mean log RT, and this was indeed confirmed in the empirical data. Note that collectively these results strengthen the argument considerably: The shape of the non-linearity is not just obtained from a fit to the data, but was theoretically predicted  a priori . Although the adjustment might seem small on the average entropies, inspection of  Fig. 5  makes it clear that the non-linear relation allows the information processing rate to increase considerably in some contexts. Interestingly, as was discussed above, the non-linearities of the generalized version of Hick’s Law actually go hand in hand with the cases where the traditional formulation of the law is known to break down. In other words, the results do not question the empirical validity of Hick’s Law for a particular range of tasks, instead, it suggests that a more general version of the law, given by Eq.  (8) , might account both for the linear regime, and for the exceptions that have been noted. This however does call into question the theoretical statement of the law, which should now be regarded within a larger frame of possible mechanisms. In the case of lexical processing reported here, the analyses suggest an adaptive system where the processing load is dynamically adjusted to the task demands. In a way, it is a ‘lazy’ system in the sense of  Zipf (1949) , with a possible reversal if one pushes the system to is faster zone. Not surprisingly, the increase of information processing rate with stimulus complexity is consistent with the findings of  Kostić (1995)  for Visual Lexical Decision, even in the estimates of the information processing rate. In a Visual Lexical Decision task using Serbian words, Kostić estimated that, depending on the average informational content of the particular stimuli in an experiment, the information processing rate ranged from about 30 to about 100 bit/s. This is consistent with the maximum rate ( i.e. ,  
                         
                            
                               
                                  
                                     r

                               ⋆

The difference between both estimates are possibly due to the difference between information contained in the stimuli that Kostić studied, and information that is processed about them that has been studied here. Notice also that Kostić’s estimates refer to the aggregated averages over full experiments. In contrast, the technique proposed here enables obtaining estimates for individual stimuli.

The principal contribution of the present study is that the entropy of an RT distribution provides an index of the amount of information processing that has taken place. Stated alternatively, changes in the entropy of RT distributions reflect changes in the underlying state of the cognitive system. I have estimated that, in the tasks under study, a minimum of around 10% of the increase in the entropy of the cognitive system is reflected in an increase of temporal entropy ( i.e. , the  
                         
                            
                               k 
                            
                            
                               ⋆ 
                            
                         
                       estimates). This finding provides a handle by which RT data can be directly linked to its metabolic counterparts, as was proposed in the seminal study of  Kirkaldy (1965) . From the thermodynamical argument, as is the case with animal (and human) lifetime distributions (cf.,  Demetrius, 1997, 2004 ), I predict that this entropy measure should index the amount of energy consumption by the system, a direct measure of which can be obtained from the BOLD response data that is provided by fMRI studies of cognitive activity. In this respect,  Koechlin, Ody, and Kouneiher (2003)  have reported that the BOLD signal is related directly to the informational content ( i.e. , entropy) of stimuli, but it remains to be seen whether the same holds for the informational content of responses. Furthermore, from a less theoretical side, the characterization of behavioural responses in terms of information-theoretical measures can enable the consistent treatment of behavioural and neurophysiological signals using the same theoretical tools (cf.,  Borst & Theunissen, 1999 ). These issues will be investigated in further studies.

The  R  ( R Development Core Team, 2005 ) function below provides a histogram-based approximation of the self-information associated with the RTs. Its parameters are:  
                         
                            • 
                            
                               x : A vector of positive RTs 
                         
                         
                            • 
                            
                               base : Base of the logarithms used for the self-information. Defaults to 2 so that they are measured in bits. Set to  exp(1)  to obtain results in nats. 
                                  14 
                               
                               
                                  14 
                                  
                                     nats  are dimensionless information units measured on a natural logarithmic scale, equivalent to what  bits  are on a binary logarithm scale. For practical reasons, when dealing with continuous as opposed to binary variables, the nat is the more commonly used unit of information. 
                               
                            
                         
                         
                            • 
                            
                               eps : Small value to add to zero valued bins in the histogram. Defaults to the minimum possible double float that can be represented in the machine. This is used to avoid the presence of log(0) in the computations. To not use it, set  eps=0 . 
                         
                         
                            •
