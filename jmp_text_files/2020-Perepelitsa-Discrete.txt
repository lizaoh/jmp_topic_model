The problem of choice is one of the fundamental problems in psychology, economics, and behavioral biology. The second half of the last century saw a rapid growth of theoretical works on this subject, as well as increasing amounts of experimental data.

In economics, the field was dominated by expected utility theory (EUT), with its critique based on experimental evidence and its ramifications. EUT was put forward by  Von Neumann and Morgenstern (1947)  as a mathematical formalization of what one can call rational preferences between contingent prospects. EUT is an axiomatic theory that starts out with postulates of completeness, transitivity, continuity and independence (substitution) axioms. The theory derives a utility function,

While the first three axioms of EUT are generally accepted, the last one, the independence axiom, drew a significant amount of critique from the experimentalists, starting with  Allais (1953) . Random utility theory of  Thurstone (1927) ,  Marschak (1959) , and  McFadden (1973) , describe situations when the utility is subject to random, unobservable shocks. The utility maximization principle together with empirically estimated distribution of shocks leads to a probabilistic criterion for a particular alternative to be selected over the others. There seems to be an unanimous agreement about general principles of reinforcement learning and, naturally, one can implement them for constructing models of individual choice as was done, for example, by  Cross (1983) ,  March (1996) ,  Erev and Barron (2005) , and  Denrell (2007) . In contrast with axiomatic approaches, one postulates a model of learning which serves as a “decision automaton” that generates a series of choices. The process is analyzed for convergence as the number of learning periods increases. The convergence does not need to imply that an agent learns a single alternative from the list of choices. Rather, it implies that the probability to select an alternative becomes fixated, i.e. the learning process converges to a non-trivial (non-constant) stationary stochastic process.

The present paper addresses the formal mathematical treatment of this approach to learning in models of discrete choice. We consider a generic situation with arbitrary learning priors, not restricted by LCA axioms, and finite memory span for accumulated experience. We obtain closed form equations, and in some cases the formulas for the limiting choice probabilities, that quantify the deviations of the learned decision making from the decision based on initial priors.

As an application of this theory, we consider the decision making model that results from the learning with one period memory span and learning priors selected according to EUT. This is done by introducing a weak stochastic order on pairs of lotteries. The model shows substantial deviations from EUT in a wide range of the parameters. Generically, the preferences are not transitive and violate independence axiom. Intransitivity, in particular, implies that the choice probabilities from which the binary preferences were derived, violate Luce’s choice axiom. We show that the binary preferences are characterized by the framing effect, with risk averse preferences for gains and risk seeking for losses.

As another application of this theory we introduce a parametric model for deviations from EUT and test it on the classical problem of demand for insurance.

All reinforcement learning models have three ingredients in common. The stimulus presented to a subject as a result of actions taken, the response, as a function of the stimulus, and the choice probabilities depending on the response strength, that model a particular reinforcement mechanism. We will follow the approach of  Luce (1959) , according to which a subject has a mental record of responses to each choice alternative and updates them according to the realized stimulus for a corresponding alternative. Then, the subject implements choice through a subject-specific function that selects an alternative with a probability proportional to the response strength for this choice.

We considered a model of choice that is built on general psychological principles for the relation between stimulus and response, when there are short-term memory effects for storing of the acquired experience. The model describes the statistics of persistent behavioral patterns that arise in the process of experimentation with available choices. Equivalently, the model can be viewed as a description of the process of adaptation to static environment.

While it cannot be considered as the definitive model, it proves to be a worthy alternative to the axiomatic approach as it indicates that some well known deviations from EUT can be ascribed to experimentation and adaptation on part of the participating agents. Other characteristics of decision making, such as the attitude toward risk, can also be explained in the framework of this model. As we showed in the last section, the decision making based on reinforcement learning leads to a range of attitudes toward risk, i.e. the decision making can be characterized as risk seeking or risk averse, which is in line with the earlier results of  March (1996) ,  Erev and Barron (2005)  and  Denrell (2007) .

The model has its limitations and several interesting extensions can be deliberated here.

As human agents have capacity for learning, the long term experimentation may lead to emergence of internalized, mental model of available uncertain choices and decisions that will be utilized the next time a decision is to be made. A part of this approach is the representation of subjective probabilities that quantify the uncertainty. We might conjecture that those are also learned, and short-term memory effects will play a measurable role in appearance of significant distortions of the corresponding objective probabilities.

It is possible that the lotteries presented to an agent as choices might have a more complex structure, for example, having a random waiting time between the moment a choice is made and the stimulus is delivered to the agent. It might also happen that responses to different alternatives are correlated. These extensions can be incorporated into the presented framework and will be the subject of further investigation.
