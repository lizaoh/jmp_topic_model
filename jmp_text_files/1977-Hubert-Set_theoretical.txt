JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 15, 70-88 (1977)  A Set-Theoretical Approach to the Problem of Hierarchical Clustering  LAWRENCE HUBERT*  Department of Educational Psychology, University of Wisconsin, Madison, Wisconsin 53706  A set-theoretical formalization is developed for the problem of generating hierarchically organized collections of subsets, or  to use a phrase common in the applied substantive literature, for the problem of hierarchical clustering. A number of terms  are introduced to characterize those clustering methods that attempt to limit the size of the overlap between each pair of  subsets constructed at a specific "compactness" level. Several examples, motivated primarily by graph theory, are discussed  briefly to illustrate the various settheoretical concepts presented.  INTRODUCTION  In recent years a great deal of literature has been developed in the behavioral sciences and elsewhere that deals with the  data analysis task of constructing a hierarchically organized collection of subsets. Computational procedures with this  explicit purpose are generally referred to as hierarchical clustering techniques and are designed to produce a system of  subsets that will represent parsimoniously the empirically observed interrelationships within a group of objects. Data  reduction is the primary goal of these methods, and consequently, a major portion of the relevant literature presents  alternative strategies for obtaining an adequate representation that fits the data "well" and at the same time has convenient  mathematical properties to facilitate substantive interpretation. For a thorough discussion of applications the reader is  referred to Hartigan (1975), Sneath and Sokal (1973), and Jardine and Sibson (1971).  The intent of this paper is partially expository and discusses a set-theoretical approach to the problem of generating  hierarchical subset systems. In this way a general context is presented for the type of clustering method developed in detail  by Jardine and Sibson (1971), Cole and Wishart (1970), Rohlf (1974), and others. The basic motivation for the paradigm  advanced by Jardine and Sibson is topological and many of the more important concepts they present are discussed extensively  only in terms of one particular clustering technique, even though the basic concepts themselves have much broader  applications. The  initial function h is then modified as follows. Proceeding sequentially through the list, if the maximum value of h on the  three pairs in an object triple is attained uniquely, then redefine h on this unique pair to be the second largest value on  the triple. If the list of triples is scanned repeatedly until no further changes in h occur, and a 1-clustering function is  defined by condition (i) in Proposition 4, a procedure equivalent to single-link hierarchical clustering is obtained (see  Jardine & Sibson, 1971, for an extended discussion of this algorithm). Alternatively, if the maximum in a triple is unique and  the redefinition step changes h on the second largest value up to the maximum on the triple, then another type of hierarchical  clustering method is defined. (It should be noted that this second option is not equivalent to complete-link clustering as  defined by Johnson, 1967, and as yet has unknown data analysis possibilities.) In the latter case, however, there could be an  ambiguity in what to redefine whenever the maximum on a particular triple is unique but the two other values on the triple are  identical. A similar problem of tied proximity values occurs continually in the clustering literature and is discussed by  Johnson (1967), Sibson (1971), and others.
