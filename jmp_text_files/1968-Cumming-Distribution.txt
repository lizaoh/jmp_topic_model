CUMMING  Monash University, Victoria, Australia  Bregman (1966) presented subjects with a sequence of stimulus-response pairs. Later he required them to order the responses to match a random ordering of the stimuli, and then to reorder those responses incorrectly matched with stimuli at the first ordering. If associations in paired-associate learning are all-or-none, second matches made when first matches are incorrect should be correct only with the frequency expected by chance. Bregman used a Monte-Carlo method to estimate the probabilities of obtaining by chance various numbers of correct second matches. In this study, the theoretical probability distribution of the number of correct second matches is derived exactly and useful values tabulated.  1. INTRODUCTION  One experimental design using paired associates requires presentation of stimulusresponse pairs to the subject, then rearrangement by the subject of a randomly ordered. list of the responses to match as closely as possible a randomly ordered list of the stimuli. The number of correct matchings made by a subject is then a measure of the amount of learning which has occurred. All-or-none learning theory implies that no learning occurred for an incorrectly placed response item, but that for a correctly placed item either learning was complete or no learning occurred and the correct placing was made by chance. Assuming that subjects give a randomly chosen permuta-, tion of the items for which no learning occurred, the number of correct placings expected by chance may be calculated and is given by Eq. 4.1 of Feller (1957, p. 97). The average number of correct placings expected by chance is one, whatever the number of items for which no learning occurred, so subtraction of one from the number of correctly placed items gives an estimate of the number of items for which complete learning occurred. Consequently, if subjects are required to make a second ordering of those items at first incorrectly placed, performance should be only at the chance level. Binford and Gettys (1965), and Bregman and Chambers (1966) are examples of studies examining the all-or-none theory in which subjects were required to make a second choice when the first response choice was incorrect. To test the hypothesis that second choices are random, the probability distribution of the number of correct second choices expected by chance must be known for the particular experimental design used.  Bregman (1966) presented his subjects with a sequence of stimulus-response pairs on a memory drum. Then he gave them the list of all stimuli presented, in a random order, and the list of all responses presented, also in a random order. Subjects were required to form a permutation of the responses, trying to match correctly as many as possible with the corresponding stimuli. All correctly matched stimulus-response pairs were then discarded, and the subjects instructed to reorder the remaining responses, again trying to assign as many as possible to their correct stimuli. Subjects were not allowed to repeat any incorrect match, consequently all responses had to be assigned to different stimuli. Bregman used a computer and Monte-Carlo methods to estimate the probabilities of obtaining various numbers of correctly positioned responses in the second ordering, under the assumption implied by all-or-none theory that subjects were behaving randomly when forming their second ordering. The purpose of the present study is to find the theoretical probability distribution and to tabulate values likely to be of use.  2.
