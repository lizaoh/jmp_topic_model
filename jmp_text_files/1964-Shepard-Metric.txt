JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY: I, 54-87 (1964)  Attention and the Metric Structure of the Stimulus Space  ROGER N. SHEPARD'  Bell Telephone Laboratories, Incorporated, Murray Hill, New ,jersey  Three experiments were performed in an investigation of how differences in size and inclination combine to determine the over-all similarity between otherwise identical visual stimuli. Similarity was defined both in terms of direct subjective judgments of over-all resemblance and in terms of the frequencies with which the stimuli were actually confused during identification learning. The results were incompatible with the static, Euclidean metric assumed by multidimensional scaling models. Apparently, when the stimuli vary along perceptually distinct dimensions, the psychological metric changes as subjects shift their attention more to one dimension or the other. The interstimulus similarities for any one state of attention, however, appear to conform with a Minkowski metric somewhere between the Euclidean and "city-block" varieties.  Owing to the continuing predilection of psychophysical investigators for stimuli that vary along but a single physical dimension, relatively little is known about the rules that govern how differences along two or more dimensions combine, psychologically, to determine the over-all dissimilarity between two stimuli. Yet in situations of practical interest the stimuli for which differential responses are required typically vary along more than one dimension, and, surely, any comprehensive theory of choice behavior must include some explicit machinery corresponding to rules for combining differences along these underlying dimensions. True, the methods of multidimensional scaling, which are now being applied to an increasing range of substantive problems, all rest (whether explicitly or implicitly) upon certain assumptions about these rules of combination. But, despite the highly specific character of these assumptions, their adoption has largely followed the dictates of mathematical convenience-not those of any conclusive empirical evidence.  t I am greatly indebted to Mrs. Jih-Jie Chang who, under my direction, prepared the extensive stimulus materials and collected and analyzed the data for the major experiments (I and III).  54  ATTENTION AND THE METRIC STRUCTURE OF THE STIMULUS SPACE SS  TWO-DIMENSIONAL PSYCHOPHYSICS AND THE  "RULES OF COMBINATION"  As one step in the extension of psychophysics toward the multidimensional case, then, the present experiments are concerned with stimuli that differ along just two physically specifiable dimensions. Each such stimulus can be uniquely represented by a point (a, b) in a two-dimensional plane with Cartesian coordinates a and b. If the stimuli are pure sinusoidal tones, for example, (a, b) might designate the tone with amplitude a and frequency b. Presumably any two such stimuli will have some definite degree of psychological similarity that could be operationally defined in terms of direct judgments of subjective resemblance or, perhaps preferably, in terms of the frequency with which those stimuli are actually confused in some identification (or paired-associate) task. Furthermore, any such measure of the psychological similarity between two stimuli would be expected to increase as those stimuli are made to approach each other physically (i.e., by simultaneously reducing the differences between them along both physical dimensions). But this is about as far as the relation between psychologically defined similarity and physically defined separation can be specified on purely intuitive grounds. For it is notoriously the case that psychological similarity is not an invariant function of physical separation but, in general, varies markedly from one location or direction to another in the physically defined space.  This lack of invariance leads directly to what might be considered the central psychophysical problem, namely, the problem of discovering the transformation that, when applied to the physically defined space, will convert it into a "psychological space" that does possess the required invariance. In unidimensional psychophysics this amounts essentially to finding the monotonic function that will stretch or shrink each small region of the one-dimensional physical scale just enough so that two points separated by a fixed distance on the resulting transformed scale will always represent equally similar stimuli, regardless of the location of the pair on the original physical scale. For the purpose of predicting human reactions to these stimuli under various conditions, such a psychological scale is obviously more useful than the original physical scale.  In the one-dimensional case this psychophysical problem is considerably simplified by the finding that, at least for two stimuli (S, and S2) that are not too greatly separated along the underlying physical scale, most kinds of measures will yield essentially the same values for the similarity of S, to S, and for the similarity of S, to S,. That is, although the psychophysical transformation must remove the initial dependence of similarity upon location along the underlying physical scale, it does not also have to correct for an initial dependence upon direction. This is precisely the respect in which the multidimensional case becomes more complicated. For, even if a separate psychological scale has been constructed for each of the underlying physical dimensions, this still does not provide any information about the similarity between two stimuli that  56 SHEPARD  differ along some arbitrary direction (i.e., along more than one dimension in the physically defined space).  For example, given a standard stimulus (a, b), the two one-dimensional psychological scales could perhaps be used to pick two other stimuli (a, b + db) and (a + da, b) that differ from the standard by psychologically equal amounts but along entirely different dimensions (e.g., frequency and amplitude). Consider, however, another stimulus (a + rda, b + rdb) that differs from the standard (a, b) by a variable amount r, but along both dimensions at once. Clearly, the two separate psychological scales do not by themselves say anything about how large r must be in order that the resulting multidimensional similarity between the standard and this variable stimulus will just match each of the two previously equated one-dimensional similarities. In addition to the unidimensional scales themselves, special rules are needed for specifying exactly what combinations of differences on these separate scales will produce an over-all similarity equivalent to that produced by a given difference along one scale alone.  METRIC SIGNIFICANCE OF ISOSIMILARITY CONTOURS  With respect to the physically defined space, a knowledge of these rules is equivalent to a knowledge of the shape of the locus of all stimuli that have any prescribed degree of similarity to any prescribed standard stimulus. Such a locus or "level curve" will be referred to here as an isosimiliarity contour. Considerations of the continuity and symmetry of similarity indicate that each isosimilarity contour will generally consist of a continuous, centrally symmetric closed curve that changes only gradually with changes in the location of the standard or in the level of similarity. The four heavy closed curves in Fig. 1 illustrate some of the possible shapes for such an isosimilarity contour about a standard stimulus, S. The horizontal and vertical axes in each panel can be regarded, again, as two physical dimensions (such as those of frequency and amplitude, for tones).  The question of the shape actually assumed by such isosimilarity contours is of fundamental importance for any attempt to construct a spatial representation for multidimensional stimuli analogous to the psychological scale for unidimensional stimuli. In particular, the question of this shape turns out to be equivalent to the question of just what metric is appropriate for the psychological space. Thus a basic assumption of all presently available methods for multidimensional scaling (namely, the assumption that the psychological space is always Euclidean) can be shown to be equivalent to the very special assumption that all isosimilarity contours are elliptical in form (as shown in Panel A of Fig. 1). In this case the psychophysical function would be that locally affine transformation that deforms all ellipses corresponding to a given similarity level into circles of the same size (see Birkhoff and MacLane, 1953, p. 290; Klein, 1939, p. 72; Shepard, 1960; Taylor, 1947, p. 216). The finding of a contour like that exhibited in Panel D, however, would raise the question of whether a metric representation is possible at all. The violation of the condition of convexity, there, can be shown to entail a violation of the fundamental inequality for triangles in any metric space; namely, the inequality that requires that the direct distance between two points always be at least as short as the distance between them via any third point (Busemann, 1955, p. 100; Rund, 1959, p. 14).  Of those metrics that do satisfy the Minkowski conditions, the Euclidean is particularly tractable, largely because the expressions for distance and angle in that form alone are invariant under rotations of the coordinate system. However certain of the other Minkowski metrics possess convenient properties, too. The metric associated with the diamond-shaped contour shown in Panel C arises from an engagingly elementary rule for combining differences along the two dimensions. In particular, the Pythagorean formula for distance in two-dimensional Euclidean space [viz., d = (a2 -}- b2)1] is simply replaced by the formula d = I a I + I b I . This is often called the "city-block metric" after that familiar situation in which the total distance that must be traversed between two locations is precisely the absolute distance that must be traversed along the north-south direction plus the absolute distance that must be traversed along the east-west direction.  EVIDENCE FROM EARLIER STUDIES  Curiously, previously assembled psychological evidence, instead of narrowing toward one of these possible metrics, has seemed rather to diverge toward two quite distinct alternatives. Owing to its convenient properties and familiarity, perhaps, the Euclidean alternative has attracted the most serious consideration. And, indeed, certain heuristic arguments suggest that this metric might hold at least locally, i.e., within any region not appreciably affected by possible large-scale curvatures of the space (Shepard, 1958b, p. 252; 1960, p. 38). The most substantial support for this view, however, comes from two kinds of empirical investigations with color stimuli. First, extensive analyses by Brown and MacAdam (1949), MacAdam (1942, 1955), Silberstein and MacAdam (1945), and others indicate that isosimilarity contours in the domain of equiluminous colors are very nearly elliptical. Indeed the assumption of a Euclidean metric might never have been questioned if it were not for the apparently anomalous results reported by Attneave (1950). The stimuli investigated by Attneave were not colors, however, but simple geometrical figures (triangles and parallelograms) varying along the dimensions of size, and either brightness or shape (tilt). For these stimuli, he concluded that measures both of judged similarity and of frequency of confusion conformed more to the city-block metric (of Panel C) than to the Euclidean metric (of Panel A). Although Attneave's data may not be entirely conclusive on this point, to dismiss them for this reason would be risky, particularly since theoretical arguments for the rule of combination underlying this city-block metric have been adduced by Landahl (Householder and Landahl, 1945, p. 76; Landahl, 1945), by Restle (1959), and, in a somewhat different connection, by MacColl (1951a, b). Moreover, some more recent but unpublished results by B. Julesz (personal communication) again indicate a breakdown of the Euclidean metric with simple geometrical figures (in this case circles and squares with or without certain pieces or corners "clipped off").  INFLUENCE OF TYPE OF STIMULI AND STATE OF ATTENTION  The present research took, as its point of departure, a conjecture stated by Torgerson (1958, pp. 254, 292) that the form of the metric may depend upon the extent to which the different dimensions of the stimuli are perceptually distinct. Certainly the geometrical figures studied by Attneave (and by Julesz) appear to differ, in this respect, from the more homogeneous stimuli investigated in the color domain. On the one hand, the Euclidean metric seems to serve rather well for stimuli (like colors) that tend to be reacted to as homogeneous, unanalyzable wholes. Moreover, the apparent absence of strongly preferred reference directions in the color space could then be understood as a manifestation of the uniquely invariant character of Euclidean geometry under rotation of the coordinate system. On the other hand, as Torgerson suggests, something like the city-block metric may be required for stimuli (like Attneave's geometrical figures) that tend to be analyzed into their values along certain preferred reference axes that are not subjectively amenable to arbitrary rotation.  What seems to be needed, then, is a more thorough investigation into the relations of similarity among a set of stimuli that differ along a small number of perceptually distinct and salient dimensions. In all of the present experiments, therefore, each stimulus (a line drawing of a kind of schematic one-spoked wheel) consists simply of a circle (or rim) together with a single radial line (or spoke). The two obvious dimensions of variation, namely, the size of the circular rim and the inclination of the  C)0 SHEPARD  radial spoke, seemed to achieve the desired degree of salience and independence. The initial intention was to determine whether the psychological similarities between such highly analyzable stimuli conform more to the Euclidean metric (as appears to be the case with the essentially unanalyzable color stimuli), or more to the city-block metric (as suggested by Landahl, Attneave, and Restle). However, the results quickly showed that human responses to stimuli of this kind cannot be adequately accounted for by either of these or, indeed, any static metric. In sum they indicate, rather, that the isosimilarity contours (together with their associated metric) undergo a drastic alteration whenever the subject's attention shifts from one aspect of the stimuli to another.  EXPERIMENT I  The dashed lines in Fig. 1 reveal that crucial information about the psychological metric can be gained by measuring the similarities between a standard stimulus, S, and stimuli in various rectilinear comparison series passing near S in the physically defined space. If the isosimilarity contour is "strongly" convex (as in A or B), then along any straight line there will always be a stimulus, P, that is psychologically nearest to S while all other stimuli on that line will fall outside the contour passing through P and, so, will be less similar to S. In the unique metric of the city-block type, however, there will be cases in which all stimuli on the line between two separated points, Q and R (in Panel C), will be exactly equal in similarity to S. Finally, if the contour is concave (as in D), a still more startling anomaly will emerge. Two separated points, Q and R, could then fall inside the contour and therefore be more similar to S, while P, a third point on the straight segment between Q and R, could fall outside the contour and therefore be less similar to S.  In the present experiments, the physically defined space of the stimuli is simply a two-dimensional plane with Cartesian coordinates corresponding to the two physical variables of size and inclination. For this first experiment, a number of series of stimuli are constructed in such a way that the stimuli in each series are equally spaced along a straight line passing through this space in some direction near the fixed standard stimulus, S. For each such series, subjects are asked to pick out the one stimulus in the series that seems most similar to the fixed standard. The distributions of the choices are then examined to see whether all of these are unimodal (as would be expected for the Euclidean metric), or whether some are rectangular (as required for the city-block metric), or even bimodal (as must be the case if the isosimilarity contours are concave as shown in Panel D). For each of the 24 series, 15 pairs of stimulus figures were arranged in a column down a long sheet of heavy paper. The left-hand figure in each pair was always the same standard stimulus, S. The right-hand figures on any one sheet, however, were the 15 comparison stimuli from one of the tangential series. These latter were always arranged sequentially, from the top to the bottom of the sheet. This enabled the subjects to use a "bracketing" procedure and, thus, to narrow down to their final choices progressively. The sheets were 82 inches wide by 27 inches long, and the distance between the centers of adjacent circles was always 4.29 cm (both vertically, between adjacent pairs, and horizontally, between the standard and the comparison stimulus within each pair). Each of the 24 series of stimulus figures was originally drawn, in india ink, on one of 24 sheets of tracing cloth. The sheets seen by the subjects, however, were printed from these originals by a black-on-white offset process.  An idea of the appearance of these sheets may be gained from the abbreviated versions of two of these presented in Fig. 3. A separate, complete set of the 24 printed sheets was put into a different random order for each of 60 subjects. These subjects were all instructed to go through the 24 sheets in the order given and, for each sheet, "to draw a line around the pair of figures that look most nearly alike" or have the "closest over-all resemblance." (The word "similarity" was not itself used in the instructions; a pilot experiment indicated that some of these subjects tend to interpret this word according to its special, technical meaning in geometry.)  Match instructions. In order to clarify certain aspects of the results obtained under the "similarity" instructions, each of the 40 remaining subjects was also given a randomly arranged set of the 24 sheets, but with different instructions. For half of these subjects the instructions were to circle, on each sheet, "that pair for which the variable figure on the right most closely matches the fixed figure on its left with respect to the angle of the radius only." In exactly the same way, the other half of these subjects were instructed to make an analogous match "with respect to the size of the circle only." The 40 subjects were divided at random between the "match angle" and "match size" conditions. This alone seems to rule out the possibility that the psychological metric is Euclidean. For, as can be seen in Fig. IA, the perimeter of an ellipse has only two regions of sharper curvature alternating with two regions of relatively flatter curvature. Regardless of the eccentricity of the elliptical contours, therefore, the distributions of choices should pass through only two cycles as the tangential line proceeds through one full revolution about a fixed circle.  Moreover, the fact that the distributions in Fig. 4 take on essentially the same shape near each of the sigmoid curves, regardless of whether the curve corresponds to a match in size or a match in angle, indicates that equal displacements along the two physical dimensions in Fig. 2 were very nearly equivalent in psychological magnitude as well. (This, of course, is what the original resealing of the physical axes was intended to achieve.) But, in this case, all ellipses originally elongated in one of these two dimensions should be reduced to circles and, hence, the choices for all 24 tangential series should be identically distributed about the same modal point, H. Quite in contrast to this expectation, however, the modes are centered on stimulus H only for the four "single dimension" series (6, 12, 18, and 24.) This evidently implies that the mean isosimilarity contour must have four regions of sharpest curvature. Instead of conforming to an ellipse, then, this contour presumably resembles a roughly "fourcornered" figure, perhaps like one of those presented in the remaining panels (B, C, and D) of Fig. 1.  Further information about this contour is provided by the distributions of choices along the four tangential series (3, 9, 15, and 21) that run at 45through the physical representation (as shown for Series 3 in Fig. 2). These are the series for which differences along the two dimensions are most nearly balanced and for which the subjects are therefore most equally disposed toward matching size or matching inclination. Owing to this matching tendency, then, these four series all yielded bimodal distributions. Such bimodality of course implies that the relatively flatter segments of the "four-cornered" contour must actually be concave (as illustrated in Fig. ID).  The fact that the average distribution of choices changes in such a clear and systematic manner from one tangential series to another supports the notion that the choices were primarily controlled by the intrinsic properties of the stimuli rather than by their positions on the printed sheets. This notion also receives some confirmation from an auxiliary experiment in which the different pairs of stimuli in each series were separately brought behind one fixed viewing window (by turning a wheel). Presumably, since only one pair was visible at a time, subjects were unlikely to have any clear conception of the position of a particular pair within the series. EXPERIMENT II  Apparently, when subjects are asked to judge which of two highly analyzable stimuli is more similar to a third, they experience little difficulty, provided that they are permitted to confine their comparisons to just one of the underlying dimensions (e.g., size or inclination). An entirely different situation arises, however, when these same subjects are enjoined to take account of all aspects of the stimuli and thus to arrive at a choice based on over-all similarity. For, after having performed an immediate and involuntary analysis of the stimuli into their elementary components, the subjects are left with the perplexing task of recombining seemingly incomparable quantities. With regard to three given stimuli P, Q, and S, for example, it may immediately be clear that P is more similar to S with respect to size while Q is more similar to S with respect to inclination. But whether P or Q is judged to have the greater over-all similarity to S will obviously depend upon the relative weight (or attention) apportioned to each of these two dimensions. Possibly, then, the difficulty in judging over-all similarity may in large part be ascribable to the absence of any fixed or built-in rule for comparing units on the two disparate psychological scales. This second experiment was therefore designed to provide a more direct demonstration of the relative inability of subjects to make such cross-dimensional comparisons. In it, subjects were instructed to adjust a difference in inclination to be equal, in subjective magnitude, to another difference that was either given along the same dimension, of inclination, or along the psychologically orthogonal dimension of size. EXPERIMENT III  The results reported thus far appear to establish at least two things. First, they seem to show that, in responding to highly analyzable stimuli of the kind studied here, subjects can adopt different strategies or states of attention. Second, they clearly indicate that, when the responses of different subjects are combined without regard for their state of attention, the resulting data are incompatible with the assumption of a Euclidean metric. However, all of the data upon which these conclusions are based came from judgment tasks that apparently were not always unambiguously defined for all subjects. This is attested by the fact that the instruction to designate which of several stimuli appears most similar to a given standard stimulus frequently elicits the answering query: "Most similar with respect to what?" And, indeed, it was only with an explicit specification of the dimension along which the comparisons were to be made (as under the "match instructions" of Experiment I) that the individual choices of the subjects became highly predictable.  74  SHEPARD  In this last experiment, then, the task was radically changed from one requiring subjective judgments about pairs of stimuli, to one requiring merely that a different arbitrarily specified identifying response be learned for each stimulus individually. DISCUSSION  THE DISTINCTION BETWEEN UNITARY AND ANALYZABLE STIMULI  The present findings, when added to those cited in the introduction, appear to justify the maintenance of a distinction between two types of stimuli: those that are reacted to as homogeneous, unitary wholes, and those that tend to be analyzed into perceptually distinct components or properties. (Of course, instead of a simple dichotomy, here, there may actually exist a graded series.) In any case, the extreme types can readily be recognized on the basis of the way subjects describe the difference between two stimuli of the same type. Thus, when such subjects are confronted with two homogeneous patches of color, they are apt to say something like "these are different shades of red" or "the one resembles ripe tomato while the other approaches persimmon." But, when the two stimuli are highly analyzable figures of the type used in the present experiments, subjects immediately note that "the figures differ in two respects" or that "this one is smaller than that one and also tipped more to the right."  Sometimes subjects do of cour*e speak of one color as being "lighter," "deeper," "warmer," "brighter," "pinker," "more intense," etc., than another; but a different such dimension of comparison is likely to be invoked for each pair of colors. This is in contrast to the case of the geometrical figures for which differences are invariably analyzed into components along the same two dimensions (viz., size and inclination, here). In terms of the spatial representation of the stimuli, this suggests that the psychological reference axes have a preferred or fixed orientation for the analyzable stimuli, but are subject to essentially arbitrary rotation in color space. The lack of rotational invariance for the analyzable stimuli was clearly revealed by the results of Experiment I (Fig. 4), where the distribution of choices along a rectilinear series of stimuli depended so critically upon the orientation of that series. Despite some technical difficulties, the analogue of Experiment I could also be carried out with colors varying, say, in brightness and saturation. Intuitively, the distribution of choices would be expected to be much less dependent upon direction in that domain and, surely, the occurrence of bimodal distributions seems quite unlikely.  IMPLICATIONS FOR LEARNING THEORY  This distinction between "analyzable" and "unanalyzable" stimuli receives further support and clarification from some recent investigations into the relation between identification learning (in which a different response is assigned to each stimulus) and  ATTENTION AND. THE METRIC STRUCTURE OF THE STIMULUS SPACE 81  classification learning (in which each response is assigned to several different stimuli). With color stimuli, Shepard and Chang (1963) found that the numbers of errors made by subjects who were learning different classifications of the same set of eight stimuli could be largely predicted from the pair-wise confusions between these stimuli as recorded during identification learning. With highly analyzable stimuli, however, Shepard, Hovland, and Jenkins (1961, p. 27) found that the same kind of prediction failed in a rather dramatic way. The conclusion was therefore drawn that, in addition to the postulated process of pairwise confusion (which was taken as the basis for the prediction), a quite different process of selective attention intervened to invalidate the predictions in the case of the more analyzable stimuli. Certainly, subjects who are attending only to the inclination of a stimulus, say, would not be expected to confuse two stimuli simply because they are of the same size.  One way of conceptualizing the distinction advocated here is in terms of uniformity of internal response. With highly analyzable stimuli, the evidence indicates that subjects actually "take in" or "sample" quite different aspects or properties upon different presentation of the same physical stimulus (Bower and Trabasso, 1963; Reynolds, 1961; Shepard, 1963b; Shepard et al., 1961, pp. 30-32; Underwood, 1963). With unitary stimuli such as homogeneous colors, however, essentially the same internal representation seems to be evoked on each occasion, and so the nature of the information that "gets in" appears to be relatively uninfluenced by the subject's attitude or state of attention. As a consequence, the psychological characterization of stimuli solely in terms of their pairwise similarities (or confusabilities) is probably adequate only for essentially unanalyzable stimuli (like colors). A complete characterization of a set of stimuli of the more analyzable variety would require, in addition, a specification of just what subsets of these stimuli are perceived as having some salient property in common.  In this respect, then, analyzable stimuli are psychologically more complex; i.e., they require more elaborate characterizations and they lead to more variable responses in learning and judgment tasks. (Wilson, in an unpublished paper (1959), has also made essentially this point.)In at least one respect, however, they may actually be more readily handled by means of simple stochastic models. In particular, the most compelling confirmations of the recent "all or nothing" models for learning have seemed to depend upon the choice of highly analyzable stimuli (e.g., see Bower and Trabasso, 1963). Certainly the degree of analyzability of the stimuli appears to be a factor that should be considered in connection with the current controversy between those who favor "incremental" and those who advocate "one-trial" theories of learning.  IMPLICATIONS FOR MULTIDIMENSIONAL SCALING  The aim of multidimensional scaling can be regarded as one of achieving a quantitative representation of a set of stimuli that is as concise as possible while adequately representing their pairwise similarities (Shepard, 1962). As a complete psychological  82 SHEPARD  characterization, therefore, the spatial solution produced by multidimensional scaling can be only as adequate as the original set of pairwise similarities from which the solution is derived. Thus, while such a representation may be reasonably satisfactory for relatively unanalyzable stimuli, such as colors (as already noted), speech sounds (Wilson, 1963), facial expressions (Abelson and Sermat, 1962), or even Morse Code signals at high tone speed (Shepard, 1963a), it may be quite incomplete for those stimuli that permit a greater play of selective attention. This is indicated by two properties of the average data obtained with this latter type of stimuli: that they violate the triangle inequality, and that they fail to represent the instantaneous tendencies of any one subject.  Consider, for illustration, three such stimuli as "table," "fable," and "chair." "Table" and "fable" are similar because they sound alike. "Table" and "chair" are also closely associated, but for an entirely different reason. On the other hand, a resemblance between "fable" and "chair" is hard to find. Here, then, is a rather strong suggestion of a violation of the triangle inequality; for one stimuluf ("table") must be represented as close to both of two other stimuli that, however, are widely separated from each other. Moreover, such a representation, even if it were possible, would fail to reflect the fact that the pattern of resemblances among these three words will differ for different subjects, depending upon whether they are thinking of the meaning of the words or merely their sounds. Reversible visual figures, like the Necker cube, offer another obvious example of this kind of phenomenon.  One possibility, in such cases, is that a separate multidimensional representation might be achievable for each distinguishable state of attention. Thus the psychological characterization of one set of words, say, would require at least two spatial structures: one appropriate for subjects who are attending to meaning, another for subjects who are attending to sound. Hopefully, the triangle inequality could be satisfied within each domain taken separately. In order for this to be true, however, further levels would presumably have to be included to provide for a proper characterization of ambiguity. The auditory stimulus "horse" (or "hoarse"), for example, has two distinguishable patterns of associations even within the purely semantic domain. But depending upon the subject's state, again, only one of these patterns would correspond to the subject's tendencies at a given time.  The present findings support the view that, if data on interstimulus similarities are taken from subjects who are all in the same state of attention, then such data will indeed satisfy the triangle inequality. This, of course, is the principal requirement for the applicability of a metric (or spatial) representation. However, it by no means guarantees that such a metric will be Euclidean. In fact, although the evidence points to an at least locally Euclidean metric for essentially unanalyzable stimuli, it also suggests that a rather different metric, of the Minkowski variety, may be required for highly analyzable stimuli, even within any one state of attention. A recently developed iterative process for achieving multidimensional scaling solutions on a  ATTENTION AND THE METRIC STRUCTURE OF THE STIMULUS SPACE 83  digital computer is of interest in this connection since it appears to be the first method capable of actually accomodating such non-Euclidean metrics (Shepard, 1962, p. 244). Indeed, J. Kruskal (who will report some important improvements in this method in a forthcoming paper) has already perfected a newer computer program in which the second power and square root that enter into the Euclidean formula for distance are immediately replaceable by the rth power and rth root. "City-block" solutions, for example, have been obtained by setting r = 1.  IMPLICATIONS FOR ESTIMATES OF THE NUMBER OF DISTINGUISHABLE STIMULI  A statement of what form the Minkowski metric assumes for a particular set of stimuli entails a number of psychologically significant statements about those stimuli. As just one illustration, consider the problem of determining the maximum number of discriminably different stimuli in some given domain. This problem is sometimes of practical interest in connection with attempts to construct optimum codes, as for the resistor color-code (e.g., see Halsey and Chapanis, 1954, p. 452). One kind of procedure that is apt to suggest itself, here, is to partition the underlying space of the stimuli into square (or cubical) compartments in such a way that the stimuli at the centers of adjacent squares (or cubes, in three dimensions) are correctly discriminated some specified fraction of the time. Using procedures of this general kind, for example, Stevens and Davis (1938, pp. 152-153) estimated that there are about 340,000 distinguishable sinusoidal tones, and Nickerson and Newhall (1943, p. 421) concluded that the number of discriminably colors approaches 7,500,000 under optimum viewing conditions.  However, if the underlying psychological space is locally Euclidean (as apparently is approximately the case in the color domain at least), the cubical type of arrangement is not the optimum one. Instead, a closer approach to the densest possible arrangement of discriminably different stimuli would be realized by means of a hexagonal packing, in two dimensions, or a rhombic-dodecahedral packing, in' three dimensions (Balinkin, 1941; Coxeter, 1961, pp. 405-410; Hilbert and Cohn-Vossen, 1952, pp. 32-56; Wyszecki, 1954). Thus, to the extent that the underlying metrics are assumed to be Euclidean, the estimate obtained by Stevens and Davis (in two dimensions) could be multiplied by 2/1-,/! = 1.15, and that obtained by Nickerson and Newhall (in three dimensions) could be multiplied by V2 = 1.41. But, if these metrics were of some other variety, a packing might be found that is even more dense (lassenhaus, 1961). For example, if the similarities between pure tones should turn out to conform to the "city-block" metric (with the usual horizontal and vertical axes), the original estimate by Stevens and Davis could actually be doubled.  POSSIBLE INFLUENCE OF CONTEXT ON THE STATE OF ATTENTION  The present experiments indicate that the psychological metric underlying a set of stimuli is strongly dependent upon the subject's state of attention. Apparently it is the  84 SHEPARD  intrinsic volatility of the attentional process, then, that renders the precise determination of the form of this metric so difficult. Such a conclusion is consonant, also, with B. Julesz' unpublished results (cited in the introduction). This marked dependence of "similarity" choices upon the state of attention raises a question as to the adequacy of those theories of choice behavior that postulate a static set of underlying choice tendencies or probabilities. Certainly a process as unstable as that of attention is itself likely to be influenced by the context of other stimuli presented in the experiment. This, however, is in apparent violation of the assumption of "independence of irrelevant alternatives," which is fundamental to a number of theories of choice--notably that of Luce (1959, 1961). Nevertheless, a violation arising from a contextual effect of just this kind has recently been demonstrated by Morrison and Slovic (1962).  Such a contextual effect may account, also, for an apparent disparity between the present results and those reported by Eriksen and Hake (1955). On the basis of errors of identification of stimuli varying along only one dimension at a time (e.g., size or brightness, alone), they estimated the number of "absolutely discriminable" stimuli along each of these continua. They also used the same method to obtain analogous estimates for series in which the stimuli varied along two dimensions at once (e.g., size and brightness, together), but in such a way that the values along the two component dimensions were always perfectly correlated. (Thus even their "multidimensional" series were in a sense linear or one-dimensional.) If their estimates of numbers of discriminable stimuli are taken as measures of psychological distance, then their results appear to favor the Euclidean metric. Thus they find the estimated number of absolutely discriminable positions along the various series to be as follows: 7.19 for size alone, 5.06 for brightness alone, and 7.89 for size and brightness combined; and, again, 7.19 for size alone, 8.45 for hue alone, and 11.90 for size and hue combined. The results for both "multidimensional" series, here, are closer to the figures predicted by applying the Euclidean rule of combination than to the figures predicted by applying the city-block rule of combination. That is, 7.86 is closer to [(7.19)2 + (5.06)2]lie = 8.79 than to 7.19 + 5.06 = 12.25, and 11.90 is closer to [(7.19)2 + (8.45)2]1,2 = 11.09 than to 7.19 + 8.45 = 15.64.  Of course these comparisons are based upon certain assumptions that may not be entirely satisfied in the experiment by Eriksen and Hake. For example, the formulas for distance are appropriate only when the two component dimensions are psychologically orthogonal. Since size and brightness are both dimensions of "magnitude," they may not meet this condition (cf., Hanes, 1951). Moreover, the dimension of hue is psychologically more nearly circular than linear (Shepard, 1962b, p. 236). Thus it is possible to doubt either the results of these comparisons (which favor a Euclidean metric) or the results of the present experiments (which point, rather, to a metric intermediate between the Euclidean and city-block varieties). On the other hand, the apparent departure of the results of Eriksen and Hake from those reported here (and also from those reported by Attneave, 1950) may be attributable to the influence of  ATTENTION AND THE METRIC STRUCTURE OF THE STIMULUS SPACE 85  contextual stimuli on attention. Since the contextual stimuli in Eriksen and Hake's one-dimensional series obviously varied along only the one dimension, subjects would be likely to confine their attention to only that one dimension in order to achieve maximum discriminability. In the so-called multidimensional series, however, subjects might very well divide their attention in some fluctuating manner between the two component dimensions. A direct comparison between a case of fixed attention and a case of divided or fluctuating attention would not necessarily be relevant to a determination of the form of the metric under any one state. The definitive determination of the form of such an "instantaneous" metric will presumably require more refined methods for controlling or measuring attention.  REFERENCES  ABELSON, R.
