Formal Requirements of Markov State Models for Paired Associate Learning 

Konstantinos V. Katsikopoulos and Donald L. Fisher University of Massachusetts, Amherst 

Discrete state learning models that make Markov assumptions are a powerful tool for the analysis and optimization of performance in paired associate tasks. We seek here to derive bounds on the complexity needed by such models in order to account for the critical effects of lag and retention intervals on paired associate learning. More specifically, after establishing that two different Markov chains are needed (one for describing the effects of trials where a paired associate is presented and one for describing the effects of trials where the paired associate is not presented), we determine the minimum number of states required in a Markov model with two chains. It is shown formally that, under certain psychologically plausible assumptions, more than three states are required. A model with two chains and four states is presented and it is shown empirically that it can account for the lag and retention effects in paired associate learning. � 2001 Academic Press Key Words: Markov models; minimum complexity; lag effects. 

INTRODUCTION 

The description of paired associate learning and memory with discrete state models that make Markov assumptions (Markov state models) has a long history (Wickens, 1982). Even though the theoretical power of Markov state models has sometimes been considered limited (Greeno and Bjork, 1973), it is acknowledged that they can provide a useful mathematical framework for the analysis and optimization of performance in paired associate tasks. First, the application of both standard (Greeno, 1967) and more recent statistical techniques (Batchelder, Chosak-Reiter, Shankle, 6 Dick, 1997) enables one to make detailed comparisons of the efficiency of the latent cognitive processes of very different subject populations. For example, Fisher (1995) used the chi-square statistic within the framework of the one-element model (Bower, 1961) and was able to conclude that aging affects negatively the efficiency of the operation of encoding processes in paired associate tasks.

Second, one can apply optimization techniques (such as dynamic programming) within the framework of such a model and derive strategies for allocating the presentations of paired associates so as to optimize the value of a learning objective, say, to maximize the expected number of paired associates learned with a fixed number of presentations (Fisher, Wisher, 6 Ranney, 1996). Karush and Dear (1966) have derived such a strategy for the one-element model which was tested with some success by Lorton (1972) and Katsikopoulos and Fisher (1998). Atkinson (1972) also obtained satisfactory empirical results for a strategy based on a model by Atkinson and Crothers (1964). 

The task of selecting a Markov state model for statistical and optimization purposes is difficult due to the large number of such models that are already available or could be constructed anew. Here, we provide information which can facilitate this selection process by deriving bounds on the required model complexity for the interesting class of paired associate tasks that involve manipulation of the spacing of presentation and test trials. Assuming that a method that determines the complexity of a Markov state model as a function of the number of states and parameters can be developed, it is useful to identify the minimum number of states needed and a specific model that can account for these spacing effects. Here we show that, under certain psychologically plausible assumptions, two chains and four states are needed and that a model with two chains and four states exists which can account for these spacing effects. 

LAG AND RETENTION EFFECTS 

It has been found (Young, 1971; Glenberg, 1976; Balota, Duchek, 6 Paulin, 1989; Katsikopoulos, Fisher, 6 Duffy, 2000) that accuracy on a retention test after two presentations of a paired associate is affected (i) by the number of trials that intervene between the two presentations of the paired associate (i.e., the lag) and (ii) by the number of trials that intervene between the second presentation of the paired associate and the retention test (i.e., the retention interval ). Furthermore, there is an interaction between the lag and the retention interval. If we label the graph of accuracy as a function of lag as the lag curve, it is the case (Crowder, 1976) that for short retention intervals the lag curve is monotonically decreasing, for moderate retention intervals the lag curve is concave, and for long retention intervals the lag curve is monotonically increasing. These effects of lag and retention intervals have been observed for both younger and older adults. An illustration of the lag and retention effects is given in Fig. 1. 

A number of Markov state models (Atkinson 6 Crothers, 1964; Young, 1971; Sperber, Greenfield, 6 House, 1973; Katsikopoulos et al., 2000) have been developed for the description of lag and retention effects. These models assume a different number of states in which paired associates can reside (three states in the Atkinson and Crothers model, four states in the Sperber et al. and Katsikopoulos et al. models, and five states in the Young model). Since lag and retention effects are a function of trials where paired associates are not presented, all these models assume (at least) two Markov chains (one to simulate the effects of presentation

CONCLUSIONS 

Determining the minimum number of chains and states needed by a Markov state model for the description of lag and retention effects is interesting and useful. Such models can provide the mathematical framework for advancing the measurement of latent cognitive processes (through the use of statistical techniques) or for programming human learning (through the use of dynamic optimization techniques). Obviously, working with less complex models can simplify theoretical derivations and ease the computational burden involved in applications. And knowing the minimum number of chains and states required is necessary for deriving bounds on the model complexity needed to account for the lag and retention effects. 

In this article, we have presented a formal argument for concluding that, under certain psychologically plausible assumptions, the minimum number of states is four. A specific model that assumed two chains and four states (and five parameters) has actually been described in Eqs. (6) and (7). 

There exist at least two open problems in this line of research. First, it would be useful to determine if, under the assumptions used to derive Theorem 2, models with fewer than five parameters can describe the lag and retention effects. Second, it would be useful to determine if, relaxing the assumptions used to derive Theorem 2, models with three states and�or less than five parameters can describe the lag and retention effects.

ERRATUM 

Volume 45, Number 2 (2001), in the article ‘‘Formal Requirements of Markov State Models for Paired Associate Learning,’’ by Konstantinos V. Katsikopoulos and Donald L. Fisher, pages 324–333 (doi:10.1006/jmps.2000.1318): In a number of points throughout the article the authors refer to the lag curve of paired associate learning when the retention interval is of moderate size as concave, while more precisely this curve is concave and increasing up to a point and decreasing after that point. This does not affect the substance of the article, and the arguments underlying the proofs, etc., do not need to be revised. However, the authors thought that it was necessary to state things precisely because otherwise the phenomenon is misrepresented and some statements are not formally correct.
