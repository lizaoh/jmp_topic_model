In the current digital era, large datasets with many variables are increasingly accessible, leading to more complex statistical models with numerous parameters to explain intricate relationships ( Azmak et al., 2015; Gomez-Cravioto et al., 2022 ). For example, to study temporal social interaction behavior in social networks,  Perry and Wolfe (2013)  considered a relational event model to analyze 21,635 email messages among 156 employees using 230 predictor variables, and  van Kesteren and Oberski (2019a)  employed a high-dimensional mediation model with 1000 potential mediators to study childhood trauma and cortisol stress. Similarly,  Van Erp et al. (2019a)  used a model with 125 different predictors to explain crime rates across 319 communities. Furthermore, to study the 190 dependencies between 20 PTSD symptoms ( Armour et al., 2017; Williams & Mulder, 2020 ), graphical models are used to identify the most important relations using psychological network theory ( Borsboom, 2017 ). Because many of the key parameters in such studies may be zero, there is a high risk of inflated type I errors where possible spurious effects are incorrectly deemed to be important. Statistical regularization methods are ideal for addressing this by providing parsimonious solutions where negligible effects are shrunk to zero and improving predictive performance.

To apply statistical regularization to higher dimensional problems, different steps need to be considered. First, researchers can use ‘classical’ regularization approaches (e.g.,  Hastie et al., 2015 ) or Bayesian regularization approaches ( van Erp et al., 2019b ). Classical regularization techniques extends the traditional maximum likelihood solution by adding a penalty function on the total magnitude of the freely estimated parameters. The penalty causes truly large effects to be freely estimated at the cost of shrinking negligible effects to zero. Bayesian regularization uses prior distributions, a natural ingredient of a Bayesian analysis. The prior reflects the magnitude of the effects before observing the data. A prior that is concentrated at zero with relatively thick tails results in posteriors for small effects to be shrunken towards zero and posteriors for large effects to remain largely unaltered. As Bayesian algorithms explore the entire parameter shape to quantify the uncertainty, while penalized likelihood mainly aim to find a single optimal solution, Bayesian regularization algorithms tend to provide better quantifications of parameter uncertainty. Although bootstrapping is a common technique to get uncertainty quantifications in penalized likelihood methods, the resulting standard errors can still be unrealistically small; as small as zero for the ordinary lasso ( Park & Casella, 2008a ).

Second, a crucial choice is the selection of penalty functions (in penalized regression) or shrinkage priors (in Bayesian regularization). Many different types of penalty functions have been proposed such as the

These common penalty functions have a Bayesian counterpart: Bayesian ridge regression can be done using Gaussian prior ( Hsiang, 1975 ), the Bayesian lasso uses a Laplace prior ( Park & Casella, 2008a ), and the Bayesian elastic net combines a Gaussian prior with a Laplace prior ( Li & Lin, 2010a ), respectively. As Bayesian regularization relies on MCMC algorithms, it is also possible to use nonconcave priors, such as horseshoe priors ( Carvalho et al., 2009 ), which are known to yield highly sparse solutions. For penalized regression, nonconcave penalty functions can also be used but the optimization problem is not convex, making the computation more challenging ( Hastie et al., 2015  Ch.3). Moreover, Bayesian methods provide a posterior distribution for the penalty parameter, offering a more realistic uncertainty estimate than non-Bayesian methods, which rely on cross-validation or information criteria to determine a single penalty value.

A third key consideration is how to determine which effects are truly zero, which is particularly important in Bayesian regularization algorithms because continuous priors (e.g., Gaussian, Laplace) yield continuous posteriors having zero probability for parameter values of zero. To resolve this, various ‘hard thresholding’ techniques have been proposed (e.g.,  Carvalho et al., 2010; Hahn & Carvalho, 2015; Ishwaran & Rao, 2005 ). In this paper, we shall use the posterior modes to obtain a parsimonious solution as they are easy to obtain and easy to interpret. Alternatively, it is also possible to obtain a (Bayesian) probability of whether a parameter is exactly zero by using priors that have a point mass at zero, such as the spike-and-slab prior ( George & McCulloch, 1993; Ishwaran & Rao, 2005 ) or the general class of ‘model selection priors’ ( Hahn & Carvalho, 2015 ). These priors will not be explored in this paper but we shall come back to other priors in the Discussion.

Due to the flexibility of Bayesian regularization techniques (in terms of prior/penalty choice) to yield parsimonious (and thus interpretable) solutions for higher dimensional statistical problems while maintaining accurate predictive performance and good uncertainty quantifications, the literature on this topic has increased tremendously in the last decades. Many of these techniques have now been implemented in software packages such as ‘bayesreg’ ( Makalic & Schmidt, 2016 ), ‘horseshoe’ ( van der Pas et al., 2019 ), ‘LaplacesDemon’ ( Hall, 2008 ), ‘ShrinkBayes’ ( van de Wiel et al., 2014 ), ‘BayesianGLasso’ ( Trainor & Wang, 2022 ), or brms ( Bürkner, 2017 ) to name a few. These packages provide fully Bayesian regularization algorithms that are tailored to the exact likelihood of the employed model. However, Bayesian regularization algorithms are also highly useful outside the modeling scenarios supported by those packages, such as the broad class of structural equation modeling ( Liang & Jacobucci, 2019 ). Moreover, while Bayesian methods offer advantages in uncertainty quantification, exploring the full parameter space of the regularized posterior can be computationally demanding, particularly for complex models with many latent variables ( Karimova et al., 2023 ), limiting their practical use.

The goal of the current paper is to address these shortcomings by presenting a generally applicable approximate Bayesian regularization (ABR) technique. As input, only a vector of the (unregularized) estimates (e.g., MLEs or posterior means based on noninformative priors) is required together with its error (or posterior) covariance matrix. Thereby, the method can be used for any type of model and any type of parameter vector for which the unregularized estimates and the corresponding error covariance matrix are available, e.g., as output from existing software or from tables in published literature. Because the error covariance matrix of the input parameters must be positive definite, the method can only be used for ‘ 
                         
                            n 
                            > 
                            p 
                         
                      ’ settings, i.e., when the number of observations is larger than the number of unknown parameters. Note that this limitation generally does not apply to existing regularization algorithms, which sometimes can even be applied to extremely high dimensional problems with ‘ 
                         
                            p 
                            ≫ 
                            n 
                         
                      ’. Despite this limitation, in applied statistical practice, especially in the social and behavioral sciences, the number of observations generally exceeds the number of parameters, and therefore the proposed ABR procedure can often be applied.

The ABR methodology works as follows. First, the errors of the unregularized vector of estimates are approximated with a multivariate Gaussian distribution. Next, this Gaussian distribution is combined with a specific shrinkage prior using Bayes’ theorem. Statistical inference is based on the (approximated) full posterior. Note that even though the errors of the key parameters are approximated with a Gaussian distribution, the final posterior generally does not follow a Gaussian distribution. For instance, a posterior could be multimodal when using a horseshoe prior. The methodology is readily available in the new R package ‘ shrinkem ’, which currently supports Gaussian priors (for ridge type penalties), Laplace priors (for lasso type penalties), and horseshoe priors.

To illustrate the flexibility of the methodology, which is its unique characteristic, the paper presents a large variety of empirical applications of the method to various modeling scenarios including linear regression models, relational event models, Gaussian graphical models, factor analytic models, and mediation models. Simulated data examples are also considered. These applications aim to assess the predictive performance of the methodology in comparison to alternative methods, including fully Bayesian regularization methods that are tailored to specific likelihoods, while at the same time assess the parsimony (or interpretability) of the solutions. Predictive performance of the methodology is assessed via cross-validation. Moreover, as the Gaussian approximation on which the ABR method relies will be less accurate when the sample size is small, we will particularly investigate its performance for smaller samples.

The paper is organized as follows. Section  2  gives a very brief overview of existing estimation methods which serve as a stepping stone for the ABR method that is presented in Section  3 . Section  3  also presents a simple illustration of the induced shrinkage behavior of the ABR method when using different priors. Section  4  introduces the R package  shrinkem . Section  5  presents several applications of the methodology for various modeling designs where the results are compared with existing (tailored) regularization algorithms. The paper ends with a discussion in Section  6 .

Despite the ubiquity of the MLE in statistics, MLEs (similarly as OLS estimates) may not be preferred in statistical problems when the model contains many parameters out of which many may be zero and the sample size is relatively small. In this case, MLEs may result in overfitting (as all parameters are freely estimated while the available information is limited) and poor out-of-sample predictions. Moreover nonsparse solutions using MLEs complicate the interpretation of the results as it becomes difficult which nonzero estimated effects (out of many) truly matter to predict one or more outcome variables of interest. Penalized regression methods have been developed to resolve these limitations.

Penalization methods help achieve a more parsimonious model by shrinking negligible effects to (approximately) zero, while leaving important effects largely unchanged. This eliminates noisy effects, making the model easier to interpret, especially with complex models and small samples. Thus, ABR can be applied by following these steps:

Step 1. 
                               Extract an unregularized vector of parameter estimates and error covariance matrix from standard statistical output, such as classical MLEs or unregularized Bayesian estimates. 
                            
                            
                               Step 2. 
                               Choose a shrinkage prior such as the Gaussian (ridge) prior, the Laplace (lasso) prior, or the horseshoe prior, among many options that are available from the Bayesian regularization literature. 
                            
                            
                               Step 3. 
                               Combine the prior from Step 2 with the approximated likelihood using the unregularized estimates from Step 1 via Bayes’ theorem, to obtain the ABR posterior. The posterior parameter space is explored using MCMC algorithms. Using the approximated posterior, a parsimonious vector of estimates can be obtained, e.g., by taking the posterior modes. Statistical uncertainty of the parameters is quantified using the draws from the full posterior.

Extract an unregularized vector of parameter estimates and error covariance matrix from standard statistical output, such as classical MLEs or unregularized Bayesian estimates.

Choose a shrinkage prior such as the Gaussian (ridge) prior, the Laplace (lasso) prior, or the horseshoe prior, among many options that are available from the Bayesian regularization literature.

Combine the prior from Step 2 with the approximated likelihood using the unregularized estimates from Step 1 via Bayes’ theorem, to obtain the ABR posterior. The posterior parameter space is explored using MCMC algorithms. Using the approximated posterior, a parsimonious vector of estimates can be obtained, e.g., by taking the posterior modes. Statistical uncertainty of the parameters is quantified using the draws from the full posterior.

Regarding the choice of the shrinkage prior (or penalty function) in Step 2, many different choices can be considered. In the applications discussed later, we mainly focus on the ridge (Gaussian) prior, the lasso (Laplace) prior, and the horseshoe prior. Furthermore, to assess the predictive performance of the regularized posteriors obtained using different priors, cross-validation can be used by splitting the data in a training and a test set. We will illustrate this in Section  5 .

Note that for the Bayesian lasso prior it is generally recommended to scale the prior to the error variance of the model to avoid multimodal posteriors ( Park & Casella, 2008b ). As this is not possible using the ABR method, multimodal posteriors (with a mode at zero and a mode at a nonzero value) could be obtained implying that there is posterior uncertainty of whether effect is zero or not given the available data.

As the ABR method relies on a large sample approximation of the likelihood, its accuracy will depend on the sample size. To get insights about the practical implications of this aspect, various numerical scenarios will be presented using different models and different sample sizes (Section  5 ). It is important to keep in mind though that common non-Bayesian or semi-Bayesian methods, such as classical confidence intervals, significance tests, or the Bayesian information criterion (BIC), also rely on (large sample) Gaussian approximations while they are also useful for statistical inference in smaller sample problems.

Here we illustrate the induced shrinkage behavior of the ABR method in a simple 1-dimensional problem. As shrinkage priors, we consider a Gaussian (ridge) prior, a Laplace (lasso) prior, and a nonconcave horseshoe prior. These three priors were chosen as they are well-known in the literature and because they show clear differences regarding their shrinkage behavior.

Relational event models are useful to study the drivers of social interaction behavior among actors in temporal social networks. Due to the complexity of social interaction behavior, relational event models can easily consist of a very large number of possible predictor variables as possible drivers of the social interaction in the network. Unregularized estimates are generally not parsimonious complicating the interpretation of the results and limiting our understanding of social interaction behavior in a network.

Here we consider a relational event sequence of voice loops taken from NASA’s famous but disastrous Apollo 13 mission. The data consist of 5402 voice messages among 19 actors (three astronauts and sixteen members at mission control).  Karimova et al. (2023)  considered full Bayesian regularized relational event models for these data consisting of 103 possible predictor variables of drivers of the communication behavior. This relational event model can be seen as a special type of multinomial regression model. Gaussian (ridge) priors, Laplace (lasso) priors, and horseshoe priors were considered to obtain parsimonious solutions. Here we compare their results with the results when using the ABR method on the unregularized estimates using the same priors. The last 500 relational events were left out when fitting (training) the models and to evaluate the out-of-sample predictive performance of the models.

We now illustrate the flexibility of the ABR method to regularize structural equation models (SEMs). Specifically, we replicate the analysis of  Liang and Jacobucci (2019)  who used a restricted factor analysis model to detect measurement bias in 19 psychological tests administered to 301 7th and 8th grade students in two schools ( Holzinger & Swineford, 1939 ). The tests aim to measure four correlated aspects of mental ability: spatial, verbal, speed, and memory. Uniform measurement bias with respect to age was assessed by regressing age on the 19 observed indicators. By regularizing the path coefficients from age to the indicators, we aim to detect substantial effects indicating measurement bias. The data set was obtained from the  psychTools  package and all code to reproduce the analysis is available at  https://github.com/sara-vanerp/ApproxBR . We compare the ABR method with a ridge and horseshoe prior to a classical regularized algorithm with a ridge penalty available in  regsem  ( Jacobucci, 2023 ). For the classical ridge implementation, we use similar default settings as in  Liang and Jacobucci (2019) .

The results for the classical regularized ridge algorithm are not shown because the optimal penalty parameter as chosen via cross-validation based on the BIC criterion in  regsem  was zero, such that the results are the same as the unregularized solution. All methods indicate the presence of measurement bias for certain indicators. For small effects, results are very similar for both the regularized and unregularized solutions, although the confidence interval for the unregularized solution is slightly wider compared to the regularized credible intervals and the posterior mode for the horseshoe prior is virtually zero for small effects. We applied the methodology to explore relevant mediators using data from  Houtepen et al. (2016) . The goal of the study was to identify which locations in the genome mediate the relation between childhood trauma and stress reactivity at a later age. An advantage of the proposed Bayesian regularization method for a mediation analysis is its ability to straightforwardly regularize functions of parameters, such as the product of two effects to quantify the indirect effects while treating the direct effects as a nuisance which are integrated out and thus are not regularized.

We take the same initial preprocessing steps as in  van Kesteren and Oberski (2019b)  who select the top 1000 potential mediators based on their absolute product of correlations with the independent and dependent variable. In addition to centering the independent variable and potential mediators, we scale them to ensure a similar influence of the shrinkage priors on all indirect effects. To avoid extreme differences in the scales of the dependent and independent variables, we also scale the dependent variable by a factor 100.

We compare the ABR algorithm with the unregularized solution using uninformative priors in  blavaan  ( Merkle et al., 2021 ). To obtain the estimates and error covariance matrix for the indirect effects, we first run the mediation model in  blavaan  using uninformative priors and subsequently multiply the relevant posterior draws of the two relevant effects to obtain posterior draws for the indirect effects. This way, the ABR method can regularize the indirect effects directly instead of regularizing the direct effects separately, as is the case in  blavaan . Statistical regularization is becoming an increasingly important technique when working with statistical models with a large number of parameters out of which many may be zero. These regularization techniques aim to obtain parsimonious solutions by shrinking small, negligible effects to zero while leaving large, important parameters largely unaltered. Although many regularization techniques are available in the literature, these techniques are generally tailored to the exact likelihood of the employed model. This paper proposed an approximate Bayesian regularization (ABR) method that does not require the exact likelihood but instead uses a Gaussian approximation of the likelihood of the key parameters of interest. This approximated likelihood was combined with a Bayesian shrinkage prior to obtain a regularized (approximate) posterior. Moreover, sparse solutions can be obtained by taking the modes of the approximated posterior. Therefore, the proposed ABR method will give researchers a simple and flexible alternative for regularization applications.

Out-of-sample predictive performance was assessed on synthetic (simulated) data and on various empirical data applications. These analyses showed that the approximation method generally performs comparable to full (tailored) Bayesian regularization methods. Only when the sample size is extremely small (just barely larger than the required size of ‘ 
                         
                            n 
                            > 
                            p 
                         
                      ’), the approximation performed somewhat worse than the full Bayesian counterparts in specific situations. Additionally, the sparseness of the regularized solutions was assessed by checking whether the posterior is (approximately) zero. The ABR method generally resulted in highly sparse solutions, especially when using the horseshoe prior. Thus, the approximation method is often able to provide simpler solutions while yielding comparable predictive performance.

To facilitate general applicability, the ABR method is readily available using the R package  shrinkem . Currently, a Gaussian (ridge), a Laplace (lasso), and a horseshoe prior are implemented. By default,  shrinkem  uses a horseshoe prior as the applications showed that this prior yields highly sparse solutions and competitive predictive performance. We plan to further extend the methodology and software using other (more advanced) shrinkage priors, such as the Bayesian elastic net ( Bornn et al., 2010; Li & Lin, 2010b ), the spike-and-slab prior ( George & McCulloch, 1993; Ishwaran & Rao, 2005 ), the adaptive lasso ( Zou, 2006b ), and spike-and-slab lasso ( Ročková & George, 2018 ). These priors are known to outperform the classical lasso which is known to be inferior under specific situations (e.g.  Knight & Fu, 2000 ).

When applying the regularization methods such as the one proposed in paper, we recommend users to assess the out-of-sample predictive performance using different shrinkage priors to determine which method is preferred for the problem at hand, in a similar manner as was done in this paper. The reason is that the performance of regularized solutions depends on (i) the true configuration of the parameters (which is unknown in practice), (ii) the accuracy of the Gaussian approximation (which depends on the sample size), (iii) the suitability of the employed shrinkage prior, and (iv) the actual statistical model that is considered. Moreover, a user may also have a preferred level of parsimony of the final solution which could be taken into account when choosing a certain shrinkage prior.

In the long run, following Bayesian asymptotics ( Lehmann & Casella, 2006; Van der Vaart, 2000 ), the posterior that is obtained using the ABR method will converge to the (approximated) likelihood, which, in turn, becomes more concentrated around the true parameter values. Thus, the influence of the shrinkage prior will diminish as the sample size grows. Although we explored the behavior of ABR posteriors in various small sample cases, its finite sample properties under specific situations (e.g.,  Knight & Fu, 2000; Pötscher & Leeb, 2009 ) is worth exploring further.

Moreover, other approximations of the likelihood could also be considered. For example for regression models with relatively small samples, (unnormalized) multivariate Student t approximations may be more accurate than unnormalized Gaussian approximations due to its the thicker tails. As the Student t distribution can be written as a scaled mixture of Gaussian distributions, it will still be computationally fast to implement this in a MCMC sampler. Moreover, depending on the nature of the parameters, one could also consider transformations of the key parameters before applying the ABR method. For instance, if the goal would be to regularize a large set of correlation coefficients, one could consider to apply the ABR method to Fisher transformed correlations rather than to the actual correlations.

Another direction would be to apply the method to other regularization problems such as when comparing Gaussian graphical models between different groups or different measurements ( Van Borkulo et al., 2023; Williams et al., 2020 ). One could first obtain an approximate Gaussian estimate of all edge differences, and subsequently apply the ABR method to this vector to obtain a parsimonious solution regarding the network differences. Moreover, in the case the sample requirement is not fulfilled (i.e., when ‘ 
                         
                            p 
                            < 
                            n 
                         
                      ’ does not hold), a two-step approach could potentially be considered (e.g.,  Touw et al., 2024 ). First, an initial ridge (say) step can be applied to obtain a very mildly regulated estimate and an approximate Gaussian error (e.g., using bootstrapping). Next, ABR is applied to this estimate in combination with a heavier shrinkage prior, such as the horseshoe. We leave these topics for future research.
