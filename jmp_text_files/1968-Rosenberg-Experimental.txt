JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY: S, 281-299 (1968)  An Experimental Test of a Stochastic Model of Intrapersonal Conflict in a Two-Person Situation'  SEYMOUR ROSENBERG  Rutgers University, New Brunswick, New Jersey 08903  This paper describes a stochastic model for a type of intrapersonal conflict that arises in two-person interactions when an individual's perceived goal-approach response is incompatible with a compliant response to another person's influence attempt. The conflict model is an extension of a class of learning models for the response continuum. Results from a two-person laboratory experiment show that with one interactive reinforcement schedule (termed "convergent") influence communication between close friends results in a lower frequency of correct outcomes for such pairs than does influence communication between strangers; the reverse occurs with a second interactive schedule, (termed "divergent"). These and other findings are interpreted in terms of certain parameters of the conflict model that are assumed to be related to the existing social relationship. Also examined are the functions relating a subject's change in response from Trial n to n - 1 to (i) the distance to his goal on Trial n, and (ii) his partner's influence attempt on Trial n. With one exception, the observed functions are in accord with the model.  In the typical two-person situation to which mathematical learning theory has been applied, the social aspect of the situation arises from the fact that the reinforcement schedule of each individual depends at least in part upon the behavior of the other (Burke, 1959, 1960; Hall, 1962; Rosenberg, 1962; Suppes and Atkinson, 1960). The two individuals are not permitted to talk to or see one another, apparently because of the problems of codifying such acts in learning theory terms and of applying contemporary models to such complex phenomena. One strategy for enlarging the class of mathematically tractable social interactions is to gradually add fundamental features of social interaction to the "minimal social situation" and at the same time time to modify and extend existing models-admittedly a slow process.  The present paper is concerned with the effects of adding a limited communication link between the two persons in an interactive learning situation. This communication link is available to both persons on each trial after each is apprised of which response  1 Thanks are due to P. S. The communication is directed at influencing the behavior of the other on the subsequent trial.  From the standpoint of the individual when he receives an influence attempt, intrapersonal conflict arises when a compliant response on the next trial is incompatible with the effects of the reinforced response (or, in different terminology, the response he perceives as necessary for a correct outcome to himself). EXPERIMENTAL SITUATION  Before describing the conflict model, it is helpful to summarize the basic features of the experimental task and the observable variables that enter into the model.  The two subjects were seated in separate booths so that they could not see each other. Each subject was provided with response and display equipment similar to that used in several previous studies (Rosenberg, 1962, 1963a, 1963b).  The experimental task consisted of a sequence of discrete trials. Each trial began with a signal to both subjects to select any number between 0 and 100 by positioning a knob-pointer along a numbered scale. After both subjects responded on a trial, an oscilloscope in each subject's booth displayed the outcome for that trial as a small illuminated dot. A correct outcome was defined for the subject as the dot touching anywhere on a thin black line painted within a square area on the tube-face. The location of this line, referred to as the "goal line," was fixed for a subject throughout an experimental session. A subject did not see the location of the other subject's goal line.  Two sets of goal lines are shown in Fig. 1 along with a "composite graph" for each set. The composite graph, which is not available to either subject, is essentially a geometric representation of the interactive reinforcement schedule or two-person system created by the locations of the two goal lines. The two sets of goal lines shown in Fig. 1 were used in the present experiment. One set is dubbed a "convergent system" and the other a "divergent system" for reasons which will be described later. A complete discussion of the variety of "systems" that can be constructed by varying the location of the two goal lines is available elsewhere (Rosenberg, 1952). Miller's conflict theory and, more particularly, Anderson's quantitative formulation of this theory are related to the type of conflict model presented here (Anderson, 1962; Miller, 1959). Anderson postulated that response speed is an additive function of two components-the values of the approach and avoidance gradients at given distances from the goal. Anderson did not deal explicitly with probabilistic processes governing the momentary behavior of individual subjects. Gradients that are the average oindividual subjects' gradients were used in order to simplify his presentation of the quantitative model. Vacillation can be subsumed in the conflict models described above by any of the hypothetical random variables associated with a subject. Actually, the conflict trials in the experimental situation used here resemble the conflict situation in which there are two spatially separate goals, A and B. An approach and avoidance gradient is associated with each goal. The rationale for postulating two gradients for each of two desirable goals is that the unwillingness to give up A induces a tendency to avoid B and vice versa. This situation is often represented by two "net approach gradients," one for A and one for B. The net approach gradient associated with a given goal is defined as the resultant of the approach and avoidance gradients associated with that goal (Godbeer, 1940; Miller, 1944). The gradient values on each net approach gradient correspond roughly to weighted values of d and c.  Other Applications. The conflict model proposed here is not limited to intrapersonal conflict in social interaction. An additive-type model may be coordinated with a variety of one-person learning situations. It should be possible, for example, to apply an additive model to situations containing two or more sources of feedback, some sources continuous and some "coarse-grained." Subjects were recruited by asking members of the junior and senior classes of a high school to volunteer for "an interesting psychological experiment in learning and communication." Students who volunteered were asked to pick one of their best friends of the same sex and class and then to sign up as a pair. They were told that some friends would participate in the experiment as pairs and others would not. They were also told that each person would receive $1.00 for the hour spent in the experiment.  The data from 40 subject-pairs were used in the analyses described in this paper. There were 38 boys and 42 girls between 16 and 18 years of age. Data from three additional pairs that were run were discarded for reasons which will be described in the Procedure section below.  Experimental Groups. Subject-pairs were assigned to one of four experimental conditions arranged in a 2 x 2 factorial design. There were 10 usable pairs in each condition. One factor in the 2 x 2 design was the use of either the convergent or divergent systems shown in Fig. 1. The second factor was the assembly of pairs who were either: (i) mutually chosen friend and knew each other's identity during the experiment; (ii) or randomly assembled pairs with the restriction that they be of the same sex and academic class and not know each other's identity until the end of the experimental session. These two types of pairs will be referred to as "Friends" and "Strangers," respectively.  The main purpose for using two types of subject-pairs was to generate some pairs (Strangers) whose members were likely to ignore their partner's influence attempts or give them less "weight" than the reinforced response and to generate other pairs (Friends) who would do the reverse. According to the model, differences between subject-pairs in the expected values of gi,and lci,n are sufficient to make the convergent and divergent systems either asymptotically stable or unstable. Suppose, for example, that influence attempts are ignored; the conflict model then reduces to the learning model summarized by Eqs. 3. Accordingly, convergent systems are predicted to be asymptotically stable at the intersection whereas divergent systems are nota prediction that has been verified in previous studies where a communication link was not available to the subjects (Rosenberg, 1960, 1962). Thus, if Strangers tend to ignore the influence attempts of their partners, then the presence of a communication link for these pairs will be negligible; that is, even with a communication link, responses in the convergent system will stabilize at the intersection and responses in the divergent system will not stabilize at the intersection. On the other hand, it can be shown that if Friends give more weight to their partner's request than to the reinforced response, the convergent system with a communication link may not be asymptotically stable for them whereas responses in the divergent system may stabilize at the intersection. (See derivations in the Appendix.)  Obviously, we cannot derive, a priori, the various parameter values for Friends and Strangers nor do we necessarily expect that all Strangers will be relatively unsusceptible to influence whereas Friends will be. However, the verification of these differences in stability between Friends and Strangers would provide support for the formulation that has been proposed.  Procedure.
