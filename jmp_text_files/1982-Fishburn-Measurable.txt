FISHBURN  Bell Laboratories  A new theory of preferences under risk is presented that does not use the transitivity and independence axioms of the von  Neumann-Morgenstern linear utility theory. Utilities in the new theory are unique up to a similarity transformation (ratio  scale measurement). They key to this generalization of the traditional linear theory lies in its representation of binary  preferences by a bivariate rather than univariate real valued function. Linear theory obtains a linear function u on a set P  of probability measures for which u(p) > u(q) if and only if p is preferred to q. The new theory obtains a skew-symmetric  bilinear function 0 on P X P for which 0(p, q) > 0 if and only if p is preferred to q. Continuity, dominance, and symmetry  axioms are shown to be necessary and sufficient for the new representation.  1. INTRODUCTION  This paper axiomatizes a far-reaching generalization of the well-known linear utility-theory of von Neumann and Morgenstern  (1944) for a preference relation > on a set P of probability measures. The generalization represents > by the positive part of  a skew-symmetric bilinear form 0 on P X P rather than by the ordering of a linear function u on P. The new theory uses neither  the transitivity nor the independence axioms of the linear theory, but nevertheless renders utility measurable in a  welldefined sense.  The representational form of the new theory, referred to as the SSB theory because of the skew-symmetric bilinear form, is  introduced in the next section following a background discussion of linear utility theory and a critique of its axioms. The  three axioms of the SSB theory, and its main representation-uniqueness theorem are presented in the third section. The fourth  section begins the sufficiency proof of the theorem by establishing implications of two of its three axioms (continuity and  dominance) that are of considerable interest in their own right. Implications of the third axiom, a symmetry condition, are  developed in the fifth section. The sixth section then weaves together the established lemmas to verify the existence of a  skewsymmetric bilinear form 0 for which > equals {(p, q): 0(p, q) > 0}. A brief conclusion completes the paper.  Throughout, > is an asymmetric binary relation on a convex set P of probability measures defined on a Boolean algebra of  subsets of a nonempty set X of outcomes. LINEAR UTILITY AND THE SSB REPRESENTATION  Typical axioms for linear utility (Marschak, 1950; Friedman & Savage, 1952; Herstein & Milnor, 1953; Jensen, 1967; Fishburn,  1970a) posit conditions of order, independence, and continuity that are necessary and sufficient for the existence of an  order-preserving linear function u on P:  p > q iff u (p) > u (q),  u(Ap + (1 - A)q) = Au(p) + (1 - A) u(q),  with u unique up to a positive affine transformation. That is, u' on P satisfies the representation in place of u if and only  if there are real numbers a > 0 and b such that u(p) = au(p) + b for all p E P. One set of axioms for linear utility  (Fishburn, 1970a; Jensen, 1967) is  Order: > is asymmetric; > and - are transitive.  Independence: If p > q and 0 < A < 1 then Ap + (1 - A)r > Aq + (1 - A)r.  Continuity: if p > q > r then ap + (1 - a)r > q and q >fip + (1 -#)r for some a,# in (0, 1).  Other sets will be mentioned in the next section.  Linear utility theory has been widely criticized. Early experiments by Preston and Baratta (1948), Edwards (1954), and others  suggested that individuals tend to subjectively inflate small probabilities and discount large probabilities. This vitiates  the linearity property, and gives rise to a pseudo-linear form in which the given probabilities are transformed by a  subjective weighting function (Edwards, 1955; Karmarkar, 1978). The distortion-of-probability effect has been reemphasized in  Kahneman and Tversky's prospect theory (1979).  A different attack on linearity, or on the independence axiom, was initiated by Allais (1953). His most famous example showed  that a comparison between composite gambles such as .gyp + (1 - ~)r and .l.q + (1 - A)r may occasion a reversal of one's  preference between p and q owing to holistic considerations that transcend what is immediately suggested by the algebraic  forms of the composites. Other examples of Allais's reversal phenomenon, i.e., p > q and Aq + (1 - A)r > Jp + (1 - A)r, are  discussed by Morrison (1967), MacCrimmon (1968), Slovic and  NONTRANSITIVE MEASURABLE UTILITY 33  Tversky (1974), and Kahneman and Tversky (1979). Recent generalizations of linear utility that weaken independence but  preserve other aspects (order, continuity, no distortion of probability) have been offered by Chew and MacCrimmon (1979) and  Machina (1982). 1 hope to discuss these further in a subsequent paper.  Failures of transitivity in various contexts are discussed by several people, including May (1954), Luce (1956), Griswold and  Luce (1962), Michalos (1966), Tversky (1969), and Fishburn (1970b). An example of nontransitive indifference occurs when an  individual is indifferent between $40 and an even-chance gamble for $0 or $100, indifferent between the gamble and $39, but  prefers $40 to $39. This might be attributed to limited discriminatory ability or to indifference bands. A different type of  intransitivity, namely, cyclic preferences (PI > p2, p2 > p3,..., p.-1 > pn, pn > p,), was observed by Tversky (1969) in a  carefully structured situation. Although evidence against transitivity in monetary contexts is less than overwhelming (Davis,  1958; Griswold & Luce, 1962), other contexts with multiattribute outcomes appear to be good candidates for intransitivities  (May, 1954; Michalos, 1966; Fishburn, 1970b). As mentioned above, the SSB theory accommodates certain types of  intransitivities, including preference cycles, as well as failures of independence.  A typical challenge to continuity (Thrall, 1954; Chipman, 1960) says that if p > q > r with p vastly preferred to q and r but  q barely preferred to r, then there might be no l > 0 where q is preferred to /3p + (1 -/3)r. Axiomatizations that drop  continuity but retain the other axioms (Hausner, 1954; Chipman, 1960; Fishburn, 1971a) give rise to vector-valued utilities  whose lexicographic order preserves preference. Continuity is used in the SSB theory.  Criticisms of linear utility tend to fall into two main categories. The first category argues against the algebraic structure  of the numerical representation but retains a notion of precise preference discriminability. Most of the theoretical work  cited above lies in this vein. The second category criticizes precise discriminability, arguing that preference judgments tend  to be vague at times and subject to thresholds of discriminability. The linear form may or may not be at issue in the second  category. An example (Fishburn, 1972) shows that asymmetry, weak independence [if p > q and r > s, then Ap + (1 - ~)r > ~q +  (1 - A )s ], and a continuity axiom yield a linear u on P that has u(p) > u(q) whenever p > q. This allows - and > to be  nontransitive but requires > to be acyclic.  The distinction between the two categories is illustrated by the number of A in [0, 1 J for which q - Ap + (I -,.)r when p > q  > r. Theories that allow q - Ap + (1 -;,)r for no A. can be either "imprecise" (Fishburn, 1971b, p. 575) or "precise" (Hausner,  1954; Fishburn, 1971a, 1980). The SSB representation is based on a skew-symmetric bilinear function 0 on P X  P, which means that 0 is a real valued function that satisfies  O(p, q) = -O(q, p)  and  0(_~p + (I - A)q, r) = AO(p, r) + (I - A) O(q, r)  for all p, q, r E P and all A E [0, 1 ]. The SSB  representation has O(p,q) > 0 iff p > q, so > is the subset of P X P on which is positive. By the definition of  indifference, p - q iff O(p, q) = 0; so - is the subset of P X P where 0 vanishes.  We shall say that a real valued function f on Q that satisfies a specified set of conditions is unique up to a similarity  transformation if the set of all real valued functions on Q that satisfy those conditions is {af: a > 0}. In the SSB  representation, 0 is unique up to a similarity transformation, so that it is "measurable" in the sense of being a rigidly  specified function.  Linear utility is a special case of SSB utility. In particular, the SSB form gives rise to the linear representation iff  O(p, q) + O(q, r) + O(r, p) = 0  for all p, q, r E P. When this is true, we can define u for the linear model so as to satisfy O(p, q) = u(p) - u(q). More  generally, and despite cautions against such an interpretation, p, q) ran be thought of as a measure of intensity of the  difference in preference between p and q. An operationalized measure of intensity in a nonrisky mode could of course result in  a different function on outcome pairs. CONCLUSION  Numerous extensions and generalizations of the von Neumann-Morgenstern axioms for preferences on a convex set P of probability  measures have appeared in the past 30 years. Most of these preserve the spirit of traditional ordering and independence axioms  along with the notion of utility as a linear function on P. However, mounting evidence that reasonable people sometimes  violate those axioms has led to recent axiomatizations (Chew & MacCrimmon, 1979; Kahneman & Tversky, 1979; Machina, 1982) that  weaken or drop the independence axiom and the notion of linear utility.  The purpose of this paper has been to offer an axiomatization of preferences on P that uses neither ordering nor independence  axioms but nevertheless renders utility as a precisely measurable concept. This is made possible by representing preferences  by a real valued function on P X P that has 0(p, q) > 0 if and only if p is preferred to q. The particular 0 considered is  skew symmetric, with p, q) + q, p) = 0, and bilinear.  Three axioms-continuity, dominance, and symmetry-were shown to be necessary and sufficient for the SSB representation. Given  the continuity axiom, the proofs reveal that the dominance axiom is largely responsible for conditional linearity in 0, while  the symmetry axiom is the prime mover behind skew symmetry for 0. Although the axioms are straightforward and seem fairly  reasonable, they are not immune to criticism or exceptions. Little appears to be known about the viability of the symmetry  axiom.  Because the SSB theory constitutes such a significant generalization of linear utility theory, it can accommodate many  phenomena that challenge the acceptability of the linear theory. Nevertheless, it does not account for effects of imprecise  discriminability, and is not directly designed to encompass other psychological possibilities such as distortion of  probabilities and utility-of-gambling effects.  The opportunity for further research on the SSB theme seems rather good. Potential topics embrance empirical tests of the  axioms, theoretical extensions to integrable forms and the realm of decision under uncertainty, specialized representations  with multiattribute consequences, relationships to stochastic dominance, and a host of areas in finance and economics that  have relied on linear theory in their analyses.  66  PETER C.
