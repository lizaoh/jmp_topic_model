In psychological research one often encounters objects by variables data. Typically, these data can be arranged into a matrix in which the rows refer to the objects and in which the columns refer to the variables. A wealth of information is included in such a data matrix. This includes not only information on the association between object  
                         i 
                       and variable  
                         j 
                       (i.e., the value contained in row  
                         i 
                       and column  
                         j 
                      ), but also on the relation between pairs of objects and between pairs of variables. A concrete example is a patient by symptom data matrix in which the entries indicate the presence/absence of each symptom in each patient. In this case, presence/absence may be represented by a binary variable with 1 indicating presence and 0 indicating absence. Information included in such a data set comprises information on which symptoms are associated to a given patient, information on which patients display similar symptom patterns, as well as information on which symptoms typically co-occur within the set of patients.

A challenge is then to simultaneously capture these types of information as included in a data set at hand. For this purpose, two-mode clustering methods that imply a discrete reduction of both the set of objects and the set of variables are especially suitable (for a comprehensive overview of this family, see  Van Mechelen, Bock, & De Boeck, 2004 ). An important subclass within this family are hierarchical classes models. A distinctive characteristic of these models (as opposed to other two-mode clustering methods) is that they allow for a representation of if-then relations. In many applications these relations are of substantive interest. For example, in case one wishes to capture the internal structure of disease processes, relevant information may be of the following type: if a patient suffers from symptom

                      , then he/she will also suffer from symptom

Originally, the HICLAS model was developed as a decomposition model for binary (e.g., presence/absence) objects by variables data, that is, binary two-way two-mode data ( Carroll & Arabie, 1980 ). Recently, however, new members of the HICLAS family have been developed for data that take values from the set of positive integers ( Van Mechelen, Lombardi, & Ceulemans, 2007 ) and for data that take values from the set of positive reals ( Schepers & Van Mechelen, submitted for publication ). These two types of data may be encountered, for example, in case the association between patients and symptoms is not simply characterized by presence/absence but rather by symptom severity.

An important question concerning HICLAS models is to what extent the decompositions are unique, as nonuniqueness may imply the existence of different substantive interpretations for a single data set. In this regard, one may note that HICLAS models are also closely related (see Section  2 ) to multi-mode component analysis (MMCA) models, including the well-known principal component analysis (PCA) model as one specific case. From this relatedness, one might in general expect an infinite number of HICLAS decompositions to yield the same reproduced data values (given the rotational freedom in many MMCA models). This, however, is not true in that many HICLAS decompositions are unique upon a permutation of the order of the components (see Section  2  for permutational freedom). The major remaining challenge on the level of uniqueness then pertains to identifying conditions under which uniqueness upon permutational freedom does hold indeed.

Up until now, the challenge as mentioned above has only been dealt with for binary HICLAS models. In particular,  Van Mechelen, De Boeck, and Rosenberg (1995)  derived a theorem (see  3.1.1 ) that gives a sufficient condition for uniqueness of binary two-mode HICLAS decompositions. Subsequently,  Ceulemans and Van Mechelen (2003)  have generalized the theorem of  Van Mechelen et al. (1995)  to binary Tucker N -HICLAS models (see  3.1.2 ). Both of these proofs rely heavily on a result by  Luce (1952)  and  Rutherford (1963)  involving the existence of Boolean matrix inverses.

In this paper we will derive novel and more general conditions to deal with the uniqueness issue in the more general case of integer- and real-valued HICLAS models. As the result obtained by Luce and Rutherford only pertains to matrices over a Boolean algebra, the results of  Van Mechelen et al. (1995)  and  Ceulemans and Van Mechelen (2003)  cannot be readily generalized to the integer- and real-valued cases, as such cases do not involve a purely binary decomposition (see Section  2.2 ). To overcome this problem, we will need to take a more general approach.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: In Section  2 , we will first briefly recapitulate two important members of the subclass of binary HICLAS models ( 2.1 ) and next explain the recently developed integer- and real-valued models ( 2.2 ). In Section  3 , uniqueness conditions for the binary models will be recapitulated ( 3.1 ) and two novel, more general uniqueness conditions are derived that also hold for integer- and real-valued HICLAS models ( 3.2 ). In Section  4 , we show that an intermediate result from Section  3  leads to an alternative and insightful proof of the result of Luce and Rutherford (which plays a key role in the proofs of  Van Mechelen et al., 1995  and  Ceulemans & Van Mechelen, 2003 ) on the existence of Boolean matrix inverses.

All HICLAS models imply an approximate decomposition of the data in terms of a number of component matrices (one for each mode in the data, e.g., the set of objects) and possibly a linking array that connects components corresponding to different modes. An important characteristic of these models is that structural relations (e.g., which objects show similar patterns; if-then relations) are represented by the individual component matrices. As decomposition models, HICLAS models are closely related to multi-mode component analysis (MMCA) models (see e.g.  Carroll & Chang, 1970; Harshman, 1970; Kiers, 1991; Kiers & Van Mechelen, 2001; Kroonenberg & De Leeuw, 1980; Tucker, 1966 ). The difference is that in HICLAS models (1) the component matrices are binary, meaning that the reduction for each mode implies a (discrete) clustering instead of a representation of each element (e.g., object) in a low-dimensional space, and (2) the mathematical structure being used is the semiring (

In this paper, we derived two novel uniqueness theorems for the family of hierarchical classes models. These theorems give sufficient conditions for uniqueness of real-valued hierarchical classes decompositions and are more general than those obtained earlier by  Van Mechelen et al. (1995)  and  Ceulemans and Van Mechelen (2003) . As such, the results presented in this paper apply to decompositions for binary and integer-valued hierarchical classes models as well. In addition, from a mathematical point of view, an interesting secondary outcome of our work is a shorter and insightful proof of a result on Boolean matrix invertibility.

Studying the uniqueness of real-valued hierarchical classes models proved to be challenging because of the fact that the associated decompositions do not involve binary components only. As the earlier results that apply to binary HICLAS models heavily relied on invertibility of Boolean matrices, a different, more general, approach had to be taken for the real-valued case. In this regard, the notions of left and right Boolean uniqueness have been introduced. In order to allow for an easy check on the uniqueness of any given real-valued hierarchical classes solution, we have shown how left and right Boolean uniqueness can be verified for positive real-valued matrices.
