A psychometric function is meant to represent for example a subject’s ability to perform a task as a function of the difficulty of the task or the clarity of the presented stimulus ( Kingdom & Prins, 2016 ). E.g. a single trial may ask a participant to identify the geometric shape presented in an image, when the image is shown for a certain number of milliseconds (the intensity of the stimulus). Thus, the stimulus intensity may be thought of as inverse to the task’s difficulty. Usually a number of trials are performed over a range of intensities in what may be said to constitute a single experiment. The purpose of the study is then often centred on some aspect of such experiments, for example comparing performance between groups (e.g. patients and controls), or within person by altering some experimental configuration. As there is a natural upper limit for the time a participant can engage in an experiment, a fair amount of attention has focused on making the sampling in the experiment efficient so as to increase the number of configurations, which is usually the focus of the study. In the following we will focus on a single experiment and the methods to efficiently sample stimulus intensities and we will investigate the consequences to statistical inference, in particular estimation and bias.

The need for flexible sampling leads to the concept of adaptive designs, which is by no means a concept unique to cognitive experiments but also to most types of clinical trials. That the design is adaptive means that the design may change dynamically during the trial, usually based on the observed design and possibly outcomes up to a certain time. For example, a  biased coin design  ( Efron, 1971 ) tries to make the groups in a randomised trial equally large by adapting the randomisation probability depending on the current allocation of experimental units — thus using the design but not the outcome at a given step. This is contrary to a classic, fixed design (also see  Dawid & Didelez, 2010 ) where the trial structure is determined before the trial begins — for example by setting the probability to be allocated to either group to  
                         
                            1 
                            / 
                            2 
                         
                       and thereby running the risk of very unbalanced groups when the number of randomised subjects is small. A classic use of outcome adaptive designs occurs in  group sequential designs  (e.g.  Jennison & Turnbull, 1999 ) where one will allow for a trial to stop at an interim stage if the obtained data show strong evidence against the null hypothesis (stopping for efficacy) or if it seems likely that the trial will have a inconclusive outcome (stopping for futility). It is in the medical statistical literature generally accepted that designs using the outcome for example in an interim analysis must account for this in the analysis phase, one problem being the increased type I error rate due to multiple testing, another being the bias of the estimates (confidence intervals must also be produced using special methods). For example, in a drug trial with an interim analysis stopping for efficacy because the observed effect of the drug is very large, the naïve estimate of the drug effect will be biased upwards.

In psychometrics and psychophysics, the adaptation uses both the outcome and the design. Simply put, the problem is that if the stimulus intensities are chosen too low or too high, the observed accuracies will be almost all zeros or ones leading to poor estimates of the psychometric function. Thus, we would like to sample where there is more information about the psychometric function. As these accuracies are specific to a participant and there is no way to know the participant’s level of accuracy before the experiment, the design needs to adapt based on the previous intensities along with the previous performance. Various designs have been proposed as reviewed for example in  Leek (2001), Treutwein (1995)  and  Kingdom and Prins (2016) , the simplest of which adapt the stimulus intensity at a given time from the previous intensity based on the performance a few trials back. A design that may be viewed as analogous to the group sequential trial as described above is  multistage  adaptive testing (MST,  Yamamoto et al., 2018 ), that has been employed in comprehensive international student assessments. In the MST design, adaptation is based on a group of tasks and a participant is subjected to a new group of tasks based on their performance in previous groups. This is in contrast to item-level adaptation which is the form of adaptation primarily considered in the present paper (see  Appendix C  for some comparisons to MST). In educational testing, item-level adaptation is employed for example in computerised adaptive testing ( van der Linden & Glas, 2000 ).

Estimates from adaptive designs will usually inherit the asymptotic properties of those from the fixed sample design (e.g.  Melfi & Page, 2000 ). However, this does not account for the behaviours in small samples and while there is acknowledgement for the potential need to account for these using specific estimation procedures in the medical trial literature this seems less common in applied psychometrics (cf. for instance section 3.4.2 and 5.4.2 of the textbook ( Kingdom & Prins, 2016 )). As also highlighted by  Bretz et al. (2009) , testing and the control of type I error rates are much better understood than estimation in adaptive designs.

We perform these investigations both in designs employing adaptive and non-adaptive allocation as well as under within-subject independence and dependence through the inclusion of random effects.                             θ

As a direct solution to the maximum likelihood equations is intractable, we consider maximum likelihood estimation using an expectation maximisation algorithm ( Dempster et al., 1977 ). Our purpose is not to suggest an implementation of an EM algorithm, but rather to use the resulting estimates as a basis for discussion of the influence of adaptive designs on small-sample bias. However, we will briefly recall the derivation of the EM algorithm to show that the adaptation has an effect on the choice of measure that is used to bound the likelihood.

Recall that the basis of the EM algorithm is to construct a lower bound on the likelihood (the E-step), maximise this bound as a function of the parameters (the M-step), and then iterate over this procedure. The bound is made tight around the current parameter estimate by choosing the bound to be the conditional expectation of the random effect given the data evaluated at the current parameter estimates.

The issue of bias in the slope following from the up-down design as observed in the simulation study has been observed in the psychophysics literature.  Kaernbach (2001)  describes the slope bias as a consequence of the sequential dependence between the observations under an adaptive design, and further demonstrates that the bias persists also under other adaptive designs including stochastic approximation schemes. The bias could also be explained from our interpretation of the bias formula in  (16) : Accuracy probabilities towards the upper edge of the psychometric function are at risk of being overestimated, as trials where an early overestimate occurs might not be corrected as the adaptive design will migrate towards lower stimulus intensities. Similarly, an adaptive design will generally lead to low accuracies being underestimated. Thus, the psychometric function is wrung out of shape by the upper and lower edges leading to the upwards bias of the slope observed in  Fig. 6 . This would also serve to explain why the intercept estimate, lying close to the middle of the psychometric function in our simulation setup, is less affected. An extension of this argument also explains why the  
                         
                            E 
                            D 
                            50 
                         
                       is not affected as it is by definition in the middle of the psychometric function. Of course, we also note the heuristic nature of these interpretations, in that the slope bias refers to the parametric scenario while the bias formula  (16)  describes the non-parametric scenario. We also stress that this bias is a small-sample problem and that the properties of the estimators are preserved asymptotically as argued in Section  3 . This is reflected also in the simulations by the diminishing bias for increased  
                         N 
                       and  
                         T 
                      . Finally, as noted in Section  3.4  above, the results in the non-parametric case hold for a wide class of adaptive designs. In particular for the case with no random effects, estimators are unbiased under the fixed design but biased under the adaptive. However, the magnitude of the bias depends on many factors, for example the placement of intensities (as shown by the difference between the two study types in the simulations), and will likely be different when considering other types of adaptive designs than the up-down design.

Another question raised in  Kaernbach (2001)  is whether the slope bias is caused by, what is termed, the data-dependency in an adaptive design or whether it is caused by the uneven placement of intensities, concluding that it is the former explanation that is the correct one. Our theoretical results predicts the same: Suppose that a Study A is performed employing adaptive selection of stimulus intensities. It is succeeded by Study B which employs the same placement of stimulus intensities that were arrived at by Study A. Suppose that both studies perform a non-parametric analysis of the psychometric function. In both bias formulae derived in  (16) and (44) , the bias depends on the covariance between the total number of placements at a given intensity and the estimator of the accuracy at the same intensity. In Study A there will be a covariance between the two, but the covariance will be zero in Study B – in principle the intensities in Study B could have been assigned before the experiment even started as long as the  marginal  distribution of intensities conformed to that found in Study A. As such, our results make more rigorous those of  Kaernbach . We note that in the simulations, the marginal distribution of intensities does not coincide between the fixed and up-down design. Rather, the simulations may be viewed as comparing two investigators, one who employs an adaptive design and one who does not. Since the psychometric function is unknown before the experiment, the latter investigator neither would nor could emulate the marginal intensity distribution in the adaptive design. We note that the marginal mean of the number of trials at a given intensity occur in the denominators of bias formulae  (16) and (44) .

The denominator of the bias formulae  (16) and (44)  along with the presented simulations also serve to show that the bias attenuates fairly quickly as the number of participants and trials increases. However, this should be interpreted cautiously, as one could also imagine realistic scenarios which might interfere with this attenuation. For instance, the presence of learning effects and fatigue would lead to a rise in accuracies in the beginning of the trial which then decreases towards the trial’s end. Such effects would render the true accuracy probability a moving target with which the adaptive design might struggle to keep up and thus hindering the convergence observed in  Figs. 6 and 7  ( Fründ et al., 2011 ). Similarly, in multistage adaptive testing a concern is item position effects, where the difficulty of an item is affected by its position in the group of items ( Yamamoto et al., 2018 ). A more precise characterisation of the convergence under such effects could be investigated through further simulation.

As we have argued above, the likelihood function is unchanged under most realistic adaptive designs, showing that the asymptotic properties of the estimators are also unchanged (under the more tacit assumption that the necessary prerequisites for the asymptotics are also unchanged). However, bias may be introduced in small samples. An important question is to what extent it is possible to correct or reduce this bias. This question has not to our knowledge been addressed much in the psychophysics literature, an exemption being ( Chaudhuri & Merfeld, 2013 ) where bias reduction is sought using standard small-sample bias reduction techniques for GLM models (i.e. not specifically developed for bias induced by adaptation). Bias correction of estimators after adaptive designs has comparatively been studied more often in the context of clinical trials as recently reviewed in  Robertson et al. (2022, 2023) . For example, the problem of small-sample bias from adaptation is known in the theory of group sequential designs. Here it is possible to find sufficient statistics in most (exponential family) models. This is normally the “usual” sufficient statistic, e.g. the sum, along with the stopping time, but due to the non-random stoppage the statistic is not complete, and thus the usual invocation of the Lehmann–Scheffé theorem does not produce a best unbiased estimator ( Milanzi et al., 2015 ). In group sequential trials, several adjusted estimators have been proposed to remedy the bias.  Whitehead (1986)  proposed a bias corrected estimator, while other authors (e.g.  Fan et al., 2004; Milanzi et al., 2015 ) consider estimators based on the conditional likelihood given the stopping time.  Fan et al. (2004)  argue that it is more reasonable to focus on the conditional bias given the stopping time, as the bias is usually large and positive (for positive treatment effect) for early stopping, while it is slightly negatively biased for later stopping leading to a, by comparison, small overall bias. They show that the maximum conditional likelihood estimator is equivalent to a conditional bias adjusted estimator. It is less clear, however, how this approach should be applied in an adaptive design, whether one should condition on the intensity at a concurrent time or the design in its entirety. This technique does not easily extend to adaptive designs as the adaptation begins after the first trial thus supplying no information on the psychometric function independent of the data look.  Bowden and Trippa (2017)  propose an inverse probability weighted estimator for an adaptive design to which they also apply Rao-Blackwellisation.

Rather than attempt to adjust the bias analytically following data collection, a different approach to counter bias from an adaptive design is in the design phase, a line of attack that has received comparatively more attention. For example,  Kaernbach  proposes a design to ameliorate the slope bias, and numerous others have been suggested (e.g.  Kontsevich & Tyler, 1999; Prins, 2013 ).

A choice between the fixed and adaptive design may also be viewed as an instance of a bias–variance tradeoff as suggested by the simulations. In  Fig. 6 , the squared relative bias of the intercept parameter when

In other words, the small-sample bias incurred by the adaptive design is in this case offset by the reduced standard error of the estimate resulting in an overall lower mean square error.  Robertson et al. (2022)  argue that a similar consideration applies to attempts to remove or reduce bias, as the modified estimator may suffer in turn from an increased standard error.

In most psychophysical trials, the number of participants is rather low, but it is often possible to collect a fairly high number of trials, as a single trial does not demand much time and effort from a participant. In some psychometric studies, the task to be solved in a single trial may be more involved, for instance if a participant must read a snippet of text and choose a response from a list of alternatives. In these cases, the amount of effort and time spent on a single task will curtail the number of trials to be low. Further, we have not considered the additional complication connected to applying data-driven stopping, which would make the number of trials  
                         T 
                       a random variable. This type of stopping is for instance commonly used in computerised adaptive testing. We have focused on item-level adaptation, which in the field of educational testing would encompasses computerised adaptive testing, and we have noted many similarities to multistage adaptive testing, where adaptation is performed based on groups of items, cf.  Appendix C .

The objective when choosing to apply an adaptive design is to make the sampling more efficient. As we have shown, however, the adaptive allocation will exert itself also in the opposite direction by requiring more samples to be taken to balance out the adverse small-sample properties implied by the adaptation. This begs the need for careful consideration and performance of simulations studies prior to choosing the design.
