Face recognition is one of the most important social skills in human social cognition. Adult humans are face experts capable of recognizing and remembering different individuals over a lifetime. Our brains have developed specific neural regions, notably the fusiform face area (FFA), dedicated to the perception and recognition of faces ( Kanwisher & Yovel, 2006 ;  Loffler et al., 2005 ). Moreover, areas like the superior temporal sulcus are involved in diverse functions, including audio-visual integration and interpreting emotional expressions ( Robins et al., 2009 ). The occipital area, particularly the occipital face area (OFA;  Pitcher et al., 2011 ), is believed to detect faces within a crowd, while the FFA is implicated in more complex cognitive strategies related to face processing.

Since the pioneering work initiated by V. Bruce and colleagues in the 1980s (e.g.,  Bruce & Young, 1998 ,  2012 ), numerous scientists have embarked on process-model-oriented and occasionally quantitative approaches toward understanding face perception and cognition ( Wenger & Townsend, 2001 ). Our current work upholds and contributes to this established tradition.

The ability to perceive and recognize faces depends on the analysis of specific facial characteristics, including the nose, eyes, lips, and skin texture. The features of a face can be described by two types of relational properties: first-order, which refers to the canonical positions of facial features (e.g., eyes are above nose and nose above mouth), and second-order, which includes the symmetry and spatial variations in these first-order relations ( Diamond & Carey, 1986 ;  Fifić & Townsend, 2010 ;  Yang et al., 2018 ). The combination of these properties results in a wide range of facial variability, with some features and their arrangement appearing typical and others more unique and distinctive.

As we progress through life, our exposure to faces varies based on factors like travel frequency, resulting in encounters with individuals who either conform more closely or deviate from our typical expectations. Throughout the years, several studies have investigated the comparison between processing typical and atypical faces in perception and memory ( Bürhan & Alici, 2023 ;  Catz & Lewis, 2020 ;  Jeffery et al., 2018 ;  Metzger, 2011 ;  Schulz et al., 2012 ).

Atypical faces, as defined by  Tanaka et al. (1998) , exhibit distinctive features that render them more easily recognizable within a crowd ( Tanaka et al., 1998 ;  Valentine, 1991 ;  Valentine & Bruce, 1986 ). The presence of facial atypicality influences various cognitive processes and judgments, including assessments of face symmetry ( Ryali et al., 2020 ), trustworthiness ( Ryali et al., 2020 ;  Sofer et al., 2017 ), attractiveness ( Rhodes & Tremewan, 1996 ;  Ryali et al., 2020 ;  Zheng et al., 2021 ), similarity ( Tanaka et al., 1998 ), and memory recognition ( Bartlett et al., 1984 ;  Busey, 2001 ;  Light et al., 1979 ;  Valentine, 1991 ;  Vokey & Read, 1992 ). Empirical findings suggest that atypical faces often exhibit reduced symmetry, diminished attractiveness, and lower perceived trustworthiness compared to typical faces ( Ryali et al., 2020 ).

Tanaka and colleagues have unearthed a groundbreaking discovery regarding the distinction between atypical and typical faces, offering an explanatory framework based on the concept of  attractor vector fields . In our study, we introduce an alternative approach based on non-Euclidean Riemannian geometry. Given the limited familiarity among mathematical and experimental/cognitive psychologists with this somewhat esoteric subject, which resides within the realm of modern differential topology and geometry, we opt to employ a unique strategy by integrating our novel theory with a tutorial elucidating foundational aspects of  Riemannian Manifolds . Alongside outlining key characteristics of this approach, we recognize the value audiences derive from understanding the historical context of its development, which, notably, emerged relatively recently even within the realms of mathematics and physics.

Delving into the historical underpinnings of face space theory, as originally conceptualized by  Valentine (1991) , faces are represented as points within a multidimensional space within the brain, typically described in terms of Euclidean geometry. Each dimension in this space serves to distinguish one face from another, with the prototypical or norm face situated at the origin of the space. The degree of typicality of a face is then determined by its distance from this norm face.

(2009) , where participants evaluated pairs of faces for similarity. Multidimensional scaling analysis has consistently replicated findings that distinctive faces were situated in the outer regions of face space, while typical faces were closer to the center, aligning with Valentine's proposed framework. It is noteworthy that while the notion of a region of space where observers are more typical for a given population doesn't necessarily imply the use of a prototype in perceptual processes, it underscores the non-trivial complexity of face perception.

As an example of such research, we note that the most commonly employed paradigm to examine distinctiveness in memory involves old/new recognition studies, wherein it has been consistently observed across several studies that atypical faces are more easily learned and better recognized than typical faces ( Bartlett et al., 1984 ;  Busey, 2001 ;  Light et al., 1979 ;  Valentine, 1991 ;  Vokey & Read, 1992 ). A recent study by  Catz et al. (2009)  delved into judgments of individual features, constructed a multidimensional space, and confirmed the expected superiority of Yes/No recognition for atypical over typical faces, evident in both accuracy and response times.

Our primary goal is not to address the multitude of findings associated with atypicality. Instead, we narrow our focus to the phenomenon elucidated in a pair of investigations conducted by J. Tanaka and colleagues. These studies employ innovative methodologies and provide a theoretical framework to explain their findings. Following this, we conduct a comprehensive review of Tanaka and colleagues' approach, key discoveries, and theoretical framework. Subsequently, we present our interpretation grounded in a Riemannian-geometric perspective. Alongside highlighting the advantages of this perspective in our current context, we propose its potential applicability to various facets of face perception and cognition. Our intention is to further elaborate on this viewpoint in the General Discussion section, expanding its scope and implications.

Next, let us expand into the specific findings of Tanaka and colleagues and especially those germane to the present study.  Tanaka et al. (1998)  demonstrated a perceptual asymmetry of atypical faces in likeness judgments. In their study, participants were asked to determine whether a morph face, which was a 50/50 combination of an atypical and typical parent face, more closely resembled the atypical or typical parent face. The results showed that the morph face was more often judged to resemble the atypical parent face, a phenomenon they referred to as the  atypicality bias  in likeness judgments.

As intimated above,  Tanaka et al. (1998)  explained this effect by incorporating the idea of an  attractor field  ( Tanaka et al., 1998 ;  Tanaka & Corneille, 2007 ). The vectors represent the velocities or accelerations of point-like objects (modeled thereof) which, when integrated properly produce a dynamic trajectory in the space (see  Townsend & Busemeyer, 1989 , for an example in approach-avoidance decision making). The attractor “object” is a set of points wherein the dynamically described object, usually asymptotically, moves toward a properly defined stable trajectory. We use the term “properly defined” because the general theory allows the attractor state to possibly even be a chaotic and uncountable set of points (see, e.g.,  Townsend, 1992 ). In  Tanaka et al. (1998) , an attractor appears to consist of a single point.

In their proposed attractor field model, a single representation of a face can be triggered by multiple facial inputs through a "many-to-one mapping" which make up the attractor field. The extent of a face representation's attractor field is determined by the density of neighboring representations within the face space. Atypical faces, located in less populated regions with lower exemplar density, are assumed to possess larger attractor fields compared to typical faces. Consequently, according to their viewpoint, a morph face equidistant from both atypical and typical faces tends to be drawn toward the attractor field of the atypical face representation. This tendency leads to a higher likelihood of being judged as similar to the atypical face. This asymmetry stems from the distinct densities of exemplar face-points in the two respective sections of the associated space. The original study by  Krumhansl (1978)  on spatial density is appropriately referenced in this context.

We argue here that there are several weaknesses of the attractor field model for understanding mental representations of faces. One immediate concern is that the model currently lacks quantitative specification, making it difficult to confirm its assumptions through analysis or to use mathematical or computational modeling to simulate mental representations.

In addition, by their very nature and as referenced above, attractor fields are generally constituted as temporally dynamic, and often related to 'force' or 'momentum' in other arenas of application. These dynamics are typically described by linear or nonlinear differential equations (see, e.g.,  Hale & Koçak, 2012 ;  Townsend & Busemeyer, 1989 ). In fact, the usual starting point in dynamics is often the notion of a potential function, in the simplest case, a  scalar potential function,  for instance a potential function of energy. The primary object is the  gradient of the potential function  which offers the direction of steepest descent. The gradient equations then lead to a set of differential equations for the vector field specified by the gradient. As previously mentioned, this approach was adopted by  Townsend and Busemeyer (1989)  in their early analysis of approach-avoidance behavior. However, how dynamics are revealed in data or may constitute an integral aspect of the typicality in face perception, or if they are indeed testable, remains unclear.

Our approach revolves around a more direct geometric representation of face space, building on the works of researchers such as  Busey (1998 , 2001),  Catz et al. (2009) ,  Catz and Lewis (2020) ,  Townsend and Thomas (1993) , and  Valentine (1991 ,  2001 ). Our current study follows an inherently geometric approach, tracing back to the evolution of geometry's role in psychology throughout the twentieth century, pioneered by scholars like Torgerson, Shepard, Kruskal, Krantz, and Tversky. Yet, as we shall see, we aim to deviate from the conventional geometrical methods predominantly used in the past.

The primary focus of our current theory revolves around the set of limitations posed by the traditional adherence to Euclidean geometry and, in a broader sense, the commonly used  power metrics  prevalent in multidimensional scaling methodologies (e.g.,  Kruskal & Wish, 1978 ;  Shepard, 1964 ). We contend that these methods prove insufficient in addressing the various facets of face perception and, by extension, numerous other forms of perception (e.g.,  Townsend, Burns & Pei., 2012 ;  Townsend & Thomas, 1993 ).

                       The General Discussion section elaborates on specific shortcomings of popular power geometries, particularly highlighting their limited applicability.

Some important earlier works, such as  Lindman and Caelli (1978)  who proposed an algorithm for manifolds with constant curvature and  Tenenbaum et al.'s (2000)  scaling routines for developable manifolds—spaces broader than Euclidean geometry but with Gaussian curvature of 0 ( Abbena et al., 2017 )—paved the way for wider geometric and topological exploration. However, these concepts have seen limited exploration within psychology and cognitive science in recent times.

Our previous works have consistently advocated for broadening psychology's geometric scope, as exemplified in  Townsend and Thomas (1993) ,  Townsend et al. (2001) ,  Townsend, Aisbett, Busemeyer and Assadi (2006) ,  Townsend, Burns and Pei (2012) , and  Townsend and Spencer-Smith (2004) , and proceed with this philosophy herein.

To summarize our stance in the present study and the forthcoming discussion: (1) As remarked above, unlike approach-avoidance decision-making theories ( Hull, 1938 ;  Lewin, 1936 ;  Townsend & Busemeyer, 1989 ), the inherent dynamics of attractor fields do not seem especially appropriate or helpful. (2) In conjunction with (1), we advocate for a geometric approach in face processing, aligning with classical links between geometry and pattern recognition. We believe this approach offers deeper insights and potential testability. (3) We propose embedding psychological face features within a  Riemannian manifold  context. While not always mandatory and not included here, the theory should accommodate functions in function-vector spaces alongside finite vectors as in  Townsend et al. (2001) .

To anticipate our present goals, we view the face space as represented by diverse regions, regions where an individual is highly skilled or 'expert' and regions where their expertise is less. The attending metric will be thought to differ depending on the level of perceptual exposure specific to an individual. Moreover, even within one's "own" region, the typical neighborhood surrounding a real or approximate prototype is presumed to be characterized by a more fine-grained metric compared to the metric applied to more atypical faces situated farther away from the prototype. Before delving deeper, we need to lay some foundational background about our approach.

With explicit attention to face perception,  Townsend et al. (2001)  substantially generalized face-space theory by interpreting the basic concepts in terms of modern  point-set topology, functional analysis , and  infinite dimensional Riemannian spaces . One begins with a formally defined set of things, for instance a set of faces. Note that even here, we would be reluctant to assume finiteness since it is certainly possible in principle to construct an infinite set of faces—after all, this set can be construed as an infinite subset of surfaces (in this context also see  Townsend et al. (2012) , who make a case for explicit consideration in many areas of psychological theory for infinite dimensional concepts). Next, because a set has so little structure, we immediately require the concepts of topology (e.g.,  Munkres, 1975 ) and functional analysis, the latter enabling the examination of function spaces (e.g.,  Bachman & Narici, 2000 ).

Thus,  Townsend et al. (2001)  posited that no finite-dimensional metric space adequately encapsulated what must resemble surfaces in three-dimensional Euclidean spaces (note that even a line drawing of the boundary of a profiled face is a continuous function). As we will demonstrate, when necessary, such as when attentional focus is directed to pinpoint a subset of dimensions (e.g., nose shape, eye color, etc.), it is legitimate to permit  dimensional reduction . It can be also seen that the popular approach utilizing eigen-faces and also  principal component  and even  factor analysis  in general, are fit into our geometric cum function-space approach. Not only is dimensional reduction a current mainstay in machine learning technology but can be appealed to when attentional forces drill down onto specific features or dimensions. In any case, our tutorial and theoretical developments here will center on finite dimensional spaces, with an occasional comment otherwise.

We begin with the very general precursor of a set of abstract points. In scientific discourse, these points typically inhabit an  n -dimensional space characterized by linearly independent basis vectors. However, this conception is much more general—it could, for example, be finite, countable or consist of a set of (possibly but not necessarily) continuous functions. Let us refer to the original set as  X  with the constituent set of points being { x | x ∈ X }.

A natural next step constructs a topology on the set of points. A topology necessitates a distinguished collection of subsets, called  open sets  with a single one typically denoted as o. The collection of all such sets is denoted as the  topology T  = { o α 
                      } where α denotes an index set. These sets possess defining characteristics: finite, countable, or even uncountable unions yield open sets, and finite intersections of open sets remain open. A set of subsets that are open called  B  ={ b } from which all other open sets can be obtained from those operations is called a  basis set  or simply a  basis.  Sets that are obtained from taking the complement of open sets are called  closed.  To enlist some intuition from elementary mathematics, observe that the open intervals, ( a, b ) on the real line form a basis for the usual topology there, [ a, b ] is closed (observe this is the complement of (-ꝏ,  a ) ꓴ ( a , +ꝏ) and [ a, b ) is neither open nor closed. Note that the empty set ϕ as well as  X  are also defined as open. The elementary conception of topologies is sufficient to define  continuity of maps  (i.e., functions) between and within topologies. These concepts, among others, comprise the realm of  point-set topology  (occasionally called  general topology ), as expounded by texts like  Munkres (2018) . When one moves to worry about more recondite subjects, such as classification of 3-D surfaces, one must advance to study  algebraic topology  (e.g.,  Hatcher, 2002 ).

While topologies facilitate the comparison of the similarity between points within a set based on set-containment, they do not offer a means to compare the similarity between specific pairs of points, such as comparing the similarity of point  x  to  y  versus that of  u  to  v , despite all points lying within the same space. To address this limitation, an advancement to the concept of a  uniform space  seems logical, as it enhances the structure provided by a topology and permits comparison of relative disparities of separate pairs of points (as demonstrated by, for example,  Beals & Krantz, 1967 ;  Dzhafarov & Colonius, 2007 ). Subsequently, one may establish conditions for the imposition of a metric.

A rather different tactic is to move up to consider the concept of a  topological manifold , which simply means that open sets of the manifold topology can be continuously mapped into a Euclidean space,ℜ n . This mapping is one-to-one, allowing for an equally effective mapping from ℜ 
                         n 
                       to the manifold of interest. This latter tack is often employed for the sake of intuition in introductory courses on differential geometry. For instance, in a typical beginner's format, one maps the Euclidean plane (ℜ 2  or parts thereof) into a surface lying in ℜ 3 .

One initially possesses a "local metric", which is essentially Euclidean in these very small regions. However, without further constraints, the situation can become quite erratic and unpredictable. For instance, there exist topological manifolds that lack differentiability entirely, rendering many essential concepts inaccessible for scientific theorization. Achieving differentiability and establishing a metric become crucial goals, which we will delve into in more detail below.

As commonly observed, the typical progression begins with establishing a topology and then advances toward more robust structures, culminating in the establishment of a  metric . Nevertheless, there is also the option to define a metric directly on a set of points from the outset, as frequently practiced in multidimensional scaling. While there are numerous additional facets of topologies, delving into them comprehensively would warrant an entire volume in itself.

The compound map then provides a  diffeomorphism  that allows consistency in our Riemannian structure. A diffeomorphism is a map that is one-to-one and onto also possessing one-to-one (non-singular) maps between the attendant vector spaces on the manifolds.

Of particular relevance for our current discussion, the existence of smooth vector fields on  X  strongly suggests the imposition of an inner product, thereby establishing a local geometry. Moreover, this concept introduces us to the realm of  tensor analysis , which stands as one of the primary tools of differential geometry and modern physics (e.g.,  Frankel, 2011 ). Tensor analysis, also known as tensor calculus, can be approached in a very systematic yet somewhat dry manner; therefore, we recommend sources such as  Kay (1988)  or  Spain (2003)  for highly accessible introductions. Basically, tensors are of two types,  contravariant  and  covariant . An ordinary vector serves as a  first-order contravariant vector . In dynamics, a vector could represent velocity, for instance. Its covariant counterpart, which maps a vector to real numbers, is the first-order covariant tensor. A classic example of this is the gradient, which behaves like a regular vector in Euclidean space (and thus in Newtonian mechanics), but not in general. Interestingly, on a general Riemannian manifold, inner products are possible only between a covariant and contravariant vector. Typically, a second-order covariant tensor converts a contravariant vector into a first-order covariant vector, which can then be combined with a contravariant vector to yield the inner product. We mention in passing, one of the almost miraculous features of tensors is that a law that is able to be couched in terms of an equation of tensors, is impervious to  any  smooth monotonic transformation of the coordinates. This property likely held considerable appeal for Albert Einstein in his ambitious endeavor of formulating the theory of general relativity—he believed physical laws should retain their essential interpretation despite changes in scale. In the realm of psychology, a similar hope for theoretical stability persists.

As promised in the introduction, we continue our historical glimpse of non-Euclidean and specifically, Riemannian, geometry to accompany and sew together the tutorial types of explication strewn throughout the above sections.

Mathematical in general and geometric in particular, viewpoints have wielded considerable influence in psychological discourse, dating at least back to Emanuel Kant's ideas in 1781 regarding a Euclidean-spatial mental intuition in humans. Johann Friedrich Herbart played an especially valuable role in his mostly algebraic expression on the unconscious and conscious mind paving the way for the first true experimental psychologist Gustav Fechner (as so designated by the eminent psychological historian E. G. Boring, 1950; and see especially the new history of scientific psychology by  Murray, 2021 ). Hermann von  Helmholtz (1852) , perhaps the quintessential physicist of the 19 th  century, emerges as a trailblazer in the application of non-Euclidean geometry to the study of color vision. His endeavors drew inspiration from the seminal endeavors of Karl Friedrich Gauss, whose seminal achievement, acknowledged in 1828, revolved around the formulation of what is now recognized as the most pervasive concept of curvature. Gauss's seminal contributions preceded Riemann's well-known Habilitation (an advanced academic qualification in Germany) by a year, although Riemann's work remained unpublished until 1868 (also see Footnote 1).

Riemann's earth-shaking invention in fact, impressed even the somewhat imperious Gauss, then resident mathematical genius at Göttingen. It more or less completed the epistemological cataclysm in the study of geometry, incited in his theory of curved surfaces by Gauss himself and advanced further by the non-Euclidean spaces of Lobachevsky and Bolyai. In mathematics, these major innovations finally put to rest the 2000-year-old question of the independence of Euclid's parallel axiom from the others—the parallel axiom segregates Euclidean from alternative geometries. Although bearing transcendent importance for modern empirical science these advances, as well as those of the pioneers in tensor analysis Ricci-Curbastro and his student Levi-Civita (invention finished by the year 1900), these rather recondite (for the times) approaches saw little scientific employment until Einstein's monumental  general theory of relativity , based on Riemannian 4-space, to which he gave birth between 1905 (year which witnessed five earth-shaking papers, including one on special relativity and one helping give birth to the emerging quantum theory) and culminating in 1915-17.

Returning to geometry in psychology, once the engine was up and going, it never ceased and we witness especially psychometrics given birth by the likes of Francis Galton then Raymond Cattell, Charles Spearman, and Louis Thurstone into the twentieth century. Even social psychology and learning with topology and geometry were involved thanks to Kurt Lewin and Clark Hull. Charles Osgood made geometric concepts, though mostly qualitatively, central to his notion of the  semantic differential . Modern color theory is currently promulgated to be inherently Riemannian (see, e.g.,  Lenz et al., 2009 ;  Wyszecki & Stiles, 2000 ).

Advancing back to our present enterprise, there is an issue of a more general nature and one that pertains to any use of geometry in science, and not we suspect, previously entertained in the psychological or cognitive science literature (and probably a number of others). In the broader realm of geometry, a fundamental ambiguity exists. One way to approach this matter is by considering a historical inclination, particularly prevalent until the nineteenth century, that associates a metric solely with its appearance as a geometric form within Euclidean space. This viewpoint started to be challenged during that century.

With regard to the present issues, in our usual milieu, the points of our space are of an uncountable infinity and therefore it's hard to visibly make them look more or less dense (one can approximate such by using shades of grey) so it's probably easiest to just let our required distinction reside in the metric per se.

An alternative approach to the assumption that atypical points in face space possess lesser distances than those in the typical region is to adopt Krumhansl's (1978) perspective, which suggests that exemplars are densely packed, possibly within a Euclidean space. And it can also be pointed out that it would be expected that typical exemplars should be more dense in their part of the space than atypical faces—without adjustment of the metric, this would surely predict the opposite effect from what is found. Supportively, several scaling studies indicate the opposite: atypical faces exhibit greater dispersion in multidimensional space. This finding aligns more closely with the recurring observation that Yes/No recognition memory performs better for atypical faces compared to typical ones. This discrepancy prompts consideration of cognitive mechanisms that may align momentary space with different geometries or metrics, as explored by  Busey (2001)  in a different context. In any case, we do not object in principle to the further investigation of this type of model—certainly at some point, more effort needs to be developed on the perceptual learning aspect of the present phenomenon.

A related suggestion proposed by one of our reviewers involves developing a concept akin to general relativity, where the density of points (i.e., exemplars) could warp space similarly to the presence of celestial bodies, thus influencing atypicality bias effects. While this idea is intriguing, preliminary exploration suggests significant challenges lie ahead. Implementing the apparatus of general relativity theory, including curvature tensors and leveraging the stress-energy-momentum tensor, appears daunting. Furthermore, the non-linear differential equations stemming from Einstein's field equations, crucial for finding geodesic trajectories, typically lack closed-form solutions thereby necessitating often arduous numerical approaches (sometimes calling on super computers). Despite these obstacles, the concept remains captivating, and it is conceivable that future endeavors may uncover viable solutions to these challenges.

Another topic involves the disconnect between  distinctiveness  and  discriminability , where the latter involves phenomena like the  other-race effect,  ultimately may call for the use of different experimental paradigms being associated with different dimensions or features as suggested by, among others ( Busey, 2001 ;  Lucas et al., 2011 ). It seems odd that more studies where the identification of individuals (e.g., as in character recognition-confusion studies,  Townsend, 1971 ;  Townsend & Ashby, 1982 ) have not been carried out, since that would be a natural venue for examination of the discriminability of individual faces in varying sectors of the face space.

Moving to the discussion of geometry per se in a broader outlook as espied in the introduction, our prior theoretical studies establish our position that the appropriate geometric milieu is that of an infinite dimensional Riemannian manifold  (Townsend et al., 2012;  
                      Townsend et al., 2001 ;  Townsend & Thomas, 1993 ). Certainly, perceptual attention can selectively focus on facial subsets, therein allowing reduction to finite subspaces, as elucidated in prior works. However, an accurate representation (as accurate as modeling permits) even in simpler domains, often necessitates function spaces, such as the eye, ear, and so on. Even a single spatial frequency wave of sound constitutes a function. For most attributes, including distinct features like discerning a 1000 Hz tone, a single number does not encapsulate 'the' feature; instead, appropriate detection usually involves a function (see, e.g.,  Papoulis, 1967 ). Moreover, the evident application of curvature in describing various features, such as the curvature of a chin, indicates the requirement for a 2 or 3-dimensional space, particularly within continuous function spaces (e.g.,  Bachman & Narici, 2000 ).

The class of Riemannian manifolds expand the geometric milieu beyond all of the above confinements. For instance, the clearly varying similarity observed in the experiments on atypicality vs. typicality and the other-face phenomena, certainly seem to beg for interpretation via a more general geometric perspective such as that offered by Riemannian manifolds and the ensuant RFMs. One promising line of development that has emerged at an opportune time is the appearance of scaling-dimensional-reduction programs that lie in Riemannian manifolds. We refer to the develop of the UMAP software which has started to impact research although only minimally in cognitive psychology to date (but see  Salibayeva, 2024 ).

Although the Riemannian framework is significant, a limitation of the present study is the absence of new empirical results. Also, neither the Tanaka efforts ( Tanaka et al., 1998 ;  Tanaka & Corneille, 2007 ) nor our present results here feature quantitative structures and tests relating the theoretical structures (e.g., multidimensional spaces into which the data are embedded). Therefore, we suggest several experimental designs that could potentially verify the hypotheses and predictions of the RFM.

One possible experimental design to investigate the psychological distance between typical/atypical and morph faces is to adapt  Tanaka et al. (1998)  Experiment 4 but expand through representations in a Riemannian manifold. In their experiment, a linear continuum of morph faces is generated by varying the contributions of typical and atypical parents, and participants made "match-to-sample" judgments about the morph faces. Multidimensional scaling analysis on similarity ratings could be used to determine the location of each face in the face space. This design modification could then be used to investigate the psychological distance between typical/atypical and morph faces in the RFM

Another fruitful endeavor, we think, would be joint studies and deliberations of cause and effect of atypicality and the so-called  other race effect . There seem to be similarities, but it seems not much is know of any relationships.

A penultimate apprehension with regard to the use of morphs as in Tanaka and colleague's atypicality work is that the logic seems to assume that the morphing procedure acts as in a flat and particularly Euclidean space. But of course, this is simply an unspoken postulate. We also cannot prove this implicit assumption. At this point in time, we can simply claim that the psychological metric must involve a non-linear distortion of the trajectory produced by the morphing procedure.

Finally, although geometry has almost from the beginning been directed toward substantive areas of perception, cognition and action, it has not to any extent, been fused with process modeling into a unified scientific approach. This is both regrettable and open to remediation. As one simple but important example, in their valuable study on typical vs. atypical faces,  Catz et al. (2009)  propose, reasonably, that perception of similarity and distinctiveness of faces rely on both individual features as well as metrics that include all of the viable dimensions associated with a multidimensional characterization of face space. They refer to the latter as being of a holistic nature. Although we agree with their finding, as well as the move towards processing modeling involving geometry, we disagree that their finding is a compelling attribute of holism. For instance,  Townsend and Wenger (2014)  propose a number of candidate processing characteristics that holistic perceptual mechanisms should meet that do not appear to be fulfilled by the  Catz et al. (2009)  approach.
