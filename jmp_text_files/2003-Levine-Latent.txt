Quine (1976)  observed that apparently paradoxical assertions sometimes serve to let us know that the way we customarily reason about a matter is incomplete or incorrect. Quine's observation sheds light on a puzzling mathematical result concerning dimensionality and latent variable models.

Many psychologists take “dimension” to mean “the smallest number of continuous variables needed to fit a large representative data sample.” In particular, a multiple-choice test is considered two dimensional if (1) some smooth two-dimensional latent variable model has been found adequately to fit a large data sample and (2) no adequately fitting one-dimensional model can be found.

The main result in this paper ( Theorem 1 ,  Section 14 ) shows that this way of reasoning about dimensionality leads to an absurd conclusion: 
                         There are no two-dimensional multiple-choice tests.

More generally, this paper proves that every multiple-choice test administration that can be fit by a smooth finite dimensional latent variable model can be equally well fit by a smooth one-dimensional model. (“Smooth” is defined in  Section 8 .)

The conclusion, there are no two-dimensional multiple-choice tests, is problematical because at least two logically distinct skills seem necessary for many tasks. For example, children appear to do well on algebra word problems only if (1) they have sufficient verbal skill to reformulate short paragraphs as algebra problems and (2) they have sufficient quantitative skill to solve simple algebra problems.

The conclusion remains paradoxical even if in fact there are no two-trait human tasks. This is true because a computer with a random number generator can easily be programmed to conform perfectly to a smooth two-dimensional latent variable model. The computer could then produce an unending stream of answer sheets for a simulated algebra word problem test. Most psychologists would agree that the data and the generating mechanism are two-dimensional. However, our mathematical results show that the above criterion for multidimensionality would fail to agree.

In the less technical sections of this paper ( Sections 1–6, 15, 16 ) I will attempt to show that our operational procedures for specifying latent variable dimension are incomplete rather than incorrect. We have left out an analysis of the geometric transformations of latent spaces that take place when we intensively coach vocabulary before an SAT exam or change the surround of a stimulus patch of light to be used in a color naming experiment. The transformations of latent variables induce orderly changes in the expected values of statistics computed from responses.  Sections 6 and 16  sketch a modification of latent trait dimensionality that facilitates including an analysis of transformations.

For definiteness and brevity, only psychometric applications are considered in detail. However, the mathematical results are applicable wherever latent variable models are applicable. Thus the results are applicable to latent variable modeling in personality research, judgement and choice, voter behavior, psychophysics and cognition. See  Section 15  for a brief description of five typical latent variable applications.

Hurewicz and Wallman describe an instructive historical precedent from the history of mathematics. They note ( Hurewicz & Wallman, 1948, p. 4 ) that parsimony led to an intuitive but faulty definition of (topological) dimension that endured until the beginning of the 20th century. 
                         Before the advent of set theory, mathematicians used dimension in … a vague sense. A configuration was said to be  n -dimensional if the least number of real parameters needed to describe its points … was  n .

Hurewicz and Wallman (1948, p. 4)  go on to describe the counterintuitive mathematical findings that “exploded” [sic] the nineteenth century, count-parameters view of dimension. 
                         The dangers and inconsistencies in this approach were brought into clear view by two celebrated discoveries in the last part of 19th century: Cantor's 1:1 correspondence between the points of a line and the points of a plane and Peano's continuous mapping of an interval on the whole of a square.

For mathematics, dimension as a topological invariant displaced dimension as “the least number of continuous real parameters” ( Hurewicz & Wallman, 1948, p. 5 ). Least-number-of-parameters failed as index of topological dimension because, as Cantor and Peano demonstrated, the number could be changed by transformations.

Early in the 20th century, Lesbesgue and Brouwer each described characteristic properties of  n -dimensional spaces that were preserved by invertible continuous transformations. Lesbesgue's approach is concisely and clearly outlined by  Townsend and Thomas (1993 , Appendix). Brouwer (and independently, Menger and also Uryson) noted that  n -dimensional space characteristically can be analyzed into subsets with  n −1 dimensional boundaries.

Hurewicz and Wallman (1948)  build upon Brouwer's, Menger's, and Uryson's contributions to exposit a precise, intuitive theory of dimension. Dimension is defined recursively: A set is  n -dimensional if each of its points has a neighborhood with an  n −1 dimensional boundary.

Hurewicz and Wallman demonstrate that a remarkably diverse array of alternative approaches to dimension are compatible with this recursive approach to dimension. Lesbesgue's insight is included; least-number-of-parameters definitely is not.

Now, a century after Cantor's and Peano's discoveries in a deterministic and numerical realm, parsimony again seems to lead to an intuitive but faulty definition of dimension, this time in a stochastic and qualitative setting. Now we are concerned with finding a useful definition of dimension for probability distributions with finite sample spaces.

This paper shows that any of the commonly used latent variable models with a  d >1 dimensional latent space can be transformed to a  d =1 unidimensional model by removing a  d −1 dimensional subset from the multidimensional model's latent space. The original multidimensional model and the transformed model will make exactly the same statistical predictions about data.

Multidimensionality enters latent variable modeling early and late. Early on, multidimensionality is used to synthesize substantive knowledge and formulate a model for data. Later, tests of model fit are used to find out if a multidimensional or unidimensional model parsimoniously fits data. The paradox occurs because these roles currently conflict.

Early, multidimensionality is used to represent knowledge accumulated from years of informal observation and from controlled studies. One dimension is used to model each of several, qualitatively different aspects of data that have been judged to be important.

Later, a single large representative data set is used to evaluate the fit to data for alternative, typically nested, models. The goal is often to decide whether all of the dimensions and model parameters were really needed. Dimensionality is decided by determining which of the competing models most parsimoniously fits the data.

Algebra word problems provide a transparent example of the conflicting roles. Algebra word problems will be used throughout this paper for illustration.

Each of the two requisite skills for algebra word problems has been observed by teachers to be coachable. Some coaching methods primarily affect the verbal skills needed to decode questions; other coaching methods favor computation. Teaching schemata for encoding algebra word problems generally improves performance on items requiring only easy calculations. Algebra drill sessions, on the other hand, have a greater effect on easily encoded but otherwise hard items.

During model formulation one number is used to represent each student's standing on a verbal encoding-skill continuum. A second number is used to represent computational skill. Coaching regimes that emphasize schemata have a relatively large effect on the first number. Coaching regimes with lots of algebra drill are more likely to raise a student's standing on the computational skill continuum.

Note that these assertions refer to at least three data sets: a pre-coaching data set, an after-verbal-coaching data set and an after-algebra-drill data set. By contrast, model evaluations typically analyze one large representative data sample.

The mathematical results in  Section 14  show that two variables are never needed to fit the empirical distribution of response patterns for a single data set. Although a two or higher dimensional model is needed to make sense of the coaching experiments and teachers's observations, it is proven that the data from any single test administration can always be fit with a unidimensional model. The unidimensional model will always be at least as parsimonious, tractable and statistically efficient as the multidimensional model because it will be a specialization or submodel of the multidimensional model.

In sum, the paradox occurs because the model formulator is attempting to quantify relationships among several data sets. The evaluator is evaluating goodness-of-fit to one empirical probability distribution (the empirical distribution of vectors of right and wrong answers) obtained from one sample.

Simultaneously fitting several data sets initially seems to be a way to reconcile the conflicting goals of model formulators and model evaluators. However, coaching experiments are expensive. And teacher-observations are generally pre-quantitative. In addition to these practical considerations there is a methodological reason for using a single data set when studying multidimensionality.

Collecting new data can inadvertently introduce trivial multidimensionality because the original data collection conditions can never be perfectly replicated. Certainly some students are able to ignore distracting traffic noise better than others. And some algebra word problems require a longer period of focused attention than others. Thus, if one testing session happens to be quieter than another, the two data sets together will appear unduly complicated.

For these reasons, I have sought a way to use a single data set to explore the model-formulator's intuitions about the number and nature of qualitatively different transformations of data that can be demonstrated by such experimental manipulations as coaching. I do not have completely satisfactory results at this time. However, some partial results and (hopefully, useful) observations on an alternative approach to latent variable dimensionality in latent variable models are introduced in  Section 6  and discussed further in  Section 16 . First, a second approach to avoiding the paradox is criticized.

A close examination of the proof of the main mathematical result points to an ad hoc route around the paradox that actually may turn out to be good enough for studying simple cognitive achievements. The unidimensional conditional probability functions that appear in the mathematical proof generally are not  monotonic  functions of a latent variable.

Several of my colleagues have observed that the paradox can be avoided by disallowing non-monotonic latent variable models. If multidimensional latent variable modeling is limited to generalizations of factor analysis, there is no paradox. The remainder of this section argues against excluding non-monotonic models.

Thurstone (1928)  and  Coombs (1964)  made essential use of non-monotonic relationships for latent variable modeling of beliefs, attitudes and choices. Currently  Andrich and Styles (1998) ,  Hoitjink (1990, 1991a, b) ,  Jansen (1984) ,  Luo (1998) ,  Roberts, Laughlin, and Wedell (1999) ,  van Schuur (1993)  and others have been actively building upon their insights. The widespread and growing interest in non-monotonic models is evident from the many citations in  Roberts's (2000)  website dedicated to unfolding models.

Non-monotonic models are needed for studying attitudes and beliefs because people are reluctant to subscribe to attitude statements that are too extreme or too conservative relative to their point of view. Non-monotonicities are also expected in choice data. Much of what people choose to pursue—status, fame, job responsibility, free time, physical strength—in excess is unattractive. Non-monotonicities also appear in personality measurement because attractive personal traits such as caution and attention to detail in excessive amounts are unattractive. Thus self-description items on personality inventories (e.g., “I always drive carefully”) can be non-monotonic indicators of the traits they purport to measure.

Unfolding or ideal point models ( Roberts, 2000 ) for attitudes, choices, and self-description easily accommodate these non-monotonicities. Monotonic latent variable models do not.

Recent data analyses confirm the need for non-monotonic models of non-cognitive latent traits.  Roberts, Laughlin, and Wedell (1999)  consider questionnaires with several moderate attitude items. They show with both actual and simulation data that monotonic models fail to correctly order people with very affirmative or very negative attitudes. Very affirmative people tend to get lower attitude scores than moderately affirmative people because they do not agree with some moderately affirmative attitude statements. Similarly, very negative people tend to get higher attitude scores than moderately negative people.

Recent analyses of personality inventory data also confirm the need for non-monotonic modeling. Although it has been demonstrated (e.g.,  Reise & Waller, 1990 ) that monotonic models can fit the proportion of people affirming each of the items on supposedly unidimensional subscales of personality inventories, recent analyses have raised doubts about the ability of monotonic models to fit  patterns  of item responses involving more than one item.  Zickar (1997) ,  Zickar and Levine (2000)  and  Chernyshenko, Stark, Chan, Drasgow, and Williams (2001)  found that monotonic models often fail to account for the four proportions of people making each of the four possible response combinations to a pair of dichotomously scored items. Non-monotonic models (including unfolding models) generally fit item pairs better. When item triples were studied, non-monotonic models continued to fit well. Monotonic models fit much less well.

In brief, non-monotonic models are uniquely able to model psychological processes that have intrinsic non-monotonicities. Monotonic models have been shown to be biased measures of attitude ( Roberts et al., 1999 ) and inferior predictors of relative frequency of personality inventory item response patterns ( Zickar, 1997 ;  Zickar et al., 2000 ;  Chernyshenko et al., 2001 ).

A response to the paradox was implicit in a series of papers presented to the 1998 Joint Annual Meeting of the Classification Society of North America and the Psychometric Society by Douglas Carroll, George Henly, Michael Levine, Sherman Tsien, Bruce Williams, and Michael Zickar. Abstracts of the 1998 conference papers were distributed by the conference organizers. See also  Levine and Tsien (1997)  and  Carroll, Williams, and Levine (2003) .

Carroll, Henly, Levine, Tsien, Williams, and Zickar considered large samples of simulated and actual item response data. Multidimensional simulation data sets were obtained by simulating commonly used two-dimensional models. Multidimensional actual data sets were obtained by combining item response data from a large sample of examinees completing at least two of four cognitive tests. (The tests were the AR, MK, PC and AR subtests of the Armed Services Vocation Aptitude Battery designed to measure word knowledge, math knowledge, paragraph comprehension, and arithmetic reasoning.)

The mathematical results in this paper (especially,  Section 14 ) led us to expect that each of the multidimensional simulated and actual data sets could be fit by  many  unidimensional models. The alternative unidimensional models were expected to be unidimensional specializations or submodels of a single multidimensional model.

Submodels are formally defined after notation for latent variable models is introduced. Roughly, one model is a submodel of a second model if the two models have the same conditional probability functions but different latent variable distributions. When a person's latent trait vector is  specified , both models make the same behavioral predictions. But the submodel asserts that more sets of latent trait vectors have zero probability of being sampled. In particular, for a unidimensional submodel of a multidimensional model, only people with trait vectors that lie on a curve in the second model's latent space are likely to be sampled.

Our expectations were confirmed. Both simulation and actual data sets were in fact well fit by many unidimensional models. For the simulation studies in which the multidimensional model was known, the unidimensional models were indeed submodels of a single two-dimensional model, namely, the multidimensional simulation model.

The observation that the unidimensional models fitting a large multidimensional data set turn out to be submodels of a single multidimensional model suggests modifying the procedures for determining the dimension of a very large data set. In the modification,  d -dimensionality is to be a property of a set of unidimensional models that fit the data rather than a property of a single best fitting model. We propose that data are  d -dimensional if  d  is the smallest number such that there is at least one smooth  d -dimensional model having all the unidimensional models as submodels.

Relating the dimension of a data set to the unidimensional models that fit the data avoids the paradox and allows some data sets to be multidimensional ( Section 16 ). Admittedly there are mathematical and practical questions that must be answered before the modification can be accepted. Some of these are dealt with in  Sections 15 and 16 .

There will be many different equivalent unidimensional models for any multidimensional model. A computer program that fits data by maximizing the likelihood of a sample in principle could converge to any of them. The multiplicity of submodels complicates their use in many standard psychometric applications such as imposing a unidimensional ordering on college applicants. If the latent space is not unidimensional, then different submodels will give different unidimensional orderings. College admissions programs that ignore multidimensionality and proceed as if  d =1 do not have this problem.

The goal of this section is to review current progress in reformulating dimensionality for latent variable models. Our strategy is to associate each latent variable model with a differentiable manifold. Each fitted unidimensional submodel is studied as a curve in this manifold. Dimensionality is to be inferred from intersections of curves and from distances along curves. Experimental manipulations, such as coaching, are to be studied as continuous transformations of the manifold.

A latent variable model determines a unique distribution on vectors of binary variables. But a  data stream  or infinite sequence of identically distributed random binary vectors determines a latent variable model only up to equivalence. For some  d ′> d  there may be a  d ′-dimensional family of unidimensional models determining exactly the same distribution over data streams as a  d -dimensional family.

In application domains in which it is possible to make repeated observations or to transform the latent variables without introducing new variables, many models can be computed from many samples. However, since attempts at replication and since experimental manipulations often introduce new variables, we have been resampling or filtering data. With filtering, a family of distributions and fitted unidimensional models is derived from a single data stream or (infinite) sequence of independent, identically distributed random binary vectors. To filter data we use some of an individual's data to decide whether the individual is to be excluded or retained in a sample.

Linking dimensionality to the minimum number of continuous variables needed to fit an observed distribution led to a counter-intuitive result. Parsimonious fit does not attempt to evaluate a model's ability to account for covariation of behavioral observations as conditions for data collection are changed.

Parsimonious fit of one distribution fails as a measure of dimensionality because there will always be a unidimensional model that fits a distribution of observations determined by a smooth finite dimensional latent variable model.

Multidimensionality is not needed to account for one distribution. Multidimensionality is only needed to account for orderly changes of observed distributions.

Multidimensionality is best studied by comparing distributions of observations. Many distributions can be obtained from many data sets by changing the individuals being observed. For example, students can be changed by coaching.

Alternatively, many distributions can be obtained from a single data sample by using objective observations (e.g., behavioral responses or demographics) to remove some individuals from a sample. For example, students failing to correctly answer one of the items can be removed a sample. Students not having completed a high school course requiring fast, accurate symbol manipulation can be excluded.

If the criterion for multidimensionality is suitably revised, then well-fitting unidimensional models are no longer paradoxical or even problematical. Well-fitting unidimensional models can be used to study the geometry of a multidimensional latent variable model and the data consistent with the model.
