A Method for Comparing Knowledge Structures Concerning Their Adequacy 

Martin Schrepp 

Hockenheim, Germany 

A knowledge domain is represented in knowledge space theory by a finite set Q of problems. The knowledge of a subject in the domain is described by the subset of problems from Q that the subject is able to solve. The set of all possible knowledge states of subjects is called the knowledge structure on Q. A crucial problem is how a hypothetical knowledge structure, obtained for example by querying an expert, can be evaluated empirically. We describe a method for comparing two knowledge structures concerning their ability to describe a set of empirically observed response patterns. Our comparison method is tested in two simulation studies. The results of these studies show that the method is highly accurate in its evaluation of knowledge structures. � 2001 Academic Press 

1. INTRODUCTION 

In knowledge-space theory (Doignon 6 Falmagne, 1985) a knowledge domain is represented by a finite set Q of problems. The subset K of problems that a subject is capable of solving is called the knowledge state of that subject. The set of all possible knowledge states K is called the knowledge structure on Q. It is assumed that for any knowledge state K # K there is a subject who can solve exactly the problems in K and that for every K �K there is no such subject. 

A knowledge structure, in general, does not contain all possible elements of the power set of Q. Thus, it can be used to infer the answers of subjects to particular problems from the answers they have already given. This can be used to define an adaptive procedure for the assessment of knowledge (Falmagne 6 Doignon, 1988a, 1988b). 

A second application of knowledge space theory is the test of psychological models of problem-solving. In this approach a knowledge structure is derived from a psychological model of the problem-solving processes in the domain. See, for example, Schrepp (1995, 1999), Korossy (1993, 1996), Ptucha (1994), Albert, Schrepp, and Held (1994), and Schrepp, Held, and Albert (1999). This knowledge structure is then compared to a set D of observed response patterns. The extent to which

It is important for both applications of knowledge-space theory that we can compare different knowledge structures concerning their adequacy. 

Knowledge structures used for the knowledge-assessment routine are in general constructed by querying experts (Dowling, 1993; Koppen, 1993; Koppen 6 Doignon, 1990; Du�ntsch 6 Gediga, 1996). Assume that we construct knowledge structures by querying n experts. The result is in general a number of different knowledge structures K1 , ..., Kn . There exist several methods of combining these knowledge structures (see for example Dowling, 1994). But combining the structures of several experts does not automatically ensure that the resulting structure is adequate. If the experts differ strongly in their experience the combined structure can be in fact less adequate than some of the single structures. 

When we derive knowledge structures from psychological models we possibly have n competing models for the problem-solving process in a domain. Each of these models allows us to derive a knowledge structure. Again, the result is n different knowledge structures K1 , ..., Kn. A method for comparing these knowledge structures allows us to compare the underlying process models. 

In some papers (see for example Albert et al., 1994, or Baumunk 6 Dowling, 1997) the mean error (see Kambouri, Koppen, Villano, 6 Falmagne, 1994) is used as a measure for the adequacy of a knowledge structure. In the next section we introduce some basic distance measures for knowledge structures and show that the mean error is inadequate to compare two knowledge structures concerning their ability to describe observed response patterns. The principles of our comparison method are introduced in the third section. The quality of this method is investigated in two simulation studies, which are described in the fourth section. A summary of our results can be found in the final section of the paper. 

2. SOME BASIC CONCEPTS 

A knowledge structure is a purely deterministic description of the knowledge in a domain. If we compare a knowledge structure with observed response patterns we have to consider random errors. Under the influence of such errors the response pattern of a subject can differ from her or his knowledge state. Let : be the probability that a subject who is able to solve a problem performs a careless error in the solution. Conversely, let ; be the probability that a subject who is not able to solve a problem finds the solution by a lucky guess. Even if K is the correct knowledge structure we will observe through the influence of lucky guesses and careless errors some response patterns not contained in K. 

We assume in the following that the probability of a careless error or a lucky guess does not depend on the special problem or knowledge state of the subject. Thus, the values for : and ; are identical for all problems in Q.

3. BASIC IDEAS OF THE COMPARISON METHOD 

The central part of our comparison method is an algorithm which allows us to estimate a frequency distribution FM (S) on ^(Q) from a hypothetical knowledge structure M and a set D of response patterns. If we have to compare two knowledge structures M and N, we need to derive in the first step the two frequency distributions FM and FN. In the second step we calculate the quadratic distance of FM and FN to H. We prefer M over N if the quadratic distance between FM and H is lower than the quadratic distance between FN and H. 

In Section 3.1 we introduce our method for deriving a frequency distribution FM from a knowledge structure M. The method to compare FM with H is described in Section 3.2. The comparison method is illustrated by an example in Section 3.3. In Section 3.4 we describe some basic features and possible problems of this comparison method.

3.4. Some Features and Possible Problems of the Method 

Even if M equals the true knowledge structure K we cannot expect that :min and ;min will be good estimations for : and ;. Thus, FM is in general not a good estimation for F. Since we use app(M, D) only to compare the two knowledge structures M and N and not as an absolute value for the adequacy of a single knowledge structure M, this is not problematic. Assume that M is closer to the true knowledge structure K than N is. Thus, we can expect that FM will be a better approximation of F than FN will be and therefore that app(M, D)<app(N, D), i.e. the structure closer to the correct knowledge structure will be preferred. 

Our comparison method considers both the observed minimal symmetric distances di(D, M) of the response patterns to the knowledge structure M and the number of states in M which are not observed. The di(D, M) values are used to determine the number of careless errors and lucky guesses we have to assume under the hypothesis that M is the correct knowledge structure. The number of not observed states is considered, since such a state K # M with K �D will show a high FM (K) value while H(K )=0. If M contains many such states this will clearly result in a high app(M, D) value. 

Assume that a state K of M occurs extremely seldom in the investigated population. In this case response patterns T �M resulting from careless errors or lucky guesses can occur with a higher frequency in the data set D than in K. Thus, K cannot be distinguished empirically from response patterns resulting from errors. The following example gives some insight into the behavior of our comparison method in such conditions.

4. SIMULATION STUDIES 

Is our algorithm able to discriminate between different knowledge structures? The algorithm had to fulfill at least two requirements if this question should be answered positively. First, the correct knowledge structure K, i.e., the knowledge structure the set D of response patterns results from, should show with a high probability the best app(K, D) value. Second, the more a knowledge structure N differs from the correct knowledge structure K, the higher the difference between the app(N, D) value of this knowledge structure N and the app(K, D) value of the correct knowledge structure K should be. There should also be a high positive correlation between the difference diff(N, K) of a knowledge structure N form the correct knowledge structure K and |app(K, D)&app(N, D)|.

5. DISCUSSION 

We described a method for comparing two knowledge structures concerning their adequacy. The central idea of this method is to derive a distribution FM on the power set of Q from a hypothetical knowledge structure M and a set D of observed response patterns. This distribution is then compared to the observed frequencies of states in D. 

We tested our method in two simulation studies. The first study shows that the method is highly accurate in comparing the true knowledge structure to randomly generated knowledge structures. The true knowledge structure was in all simulations judged to be a more adequate description of D than was the randomly generated knowledge structure. This was observed even if the number of simulated response patterns was relatively low compared to |^(Q)|. 

When the randomly generated knowledge structures were chosen similar to the true knowledge structure the accuracy of the method depended strongly on the number of available response patterns. Our second simulation study shows that in this case a significant number of misclassifications can occur if D does not contain enough response patterns. 

As our second simulation study shows, the number of data patterns which are necessary to avoid misclassifications increases with the size of |Q|. For |Q|=20, even with 5000 response patterns 11 of the 150 comparisons (7.30) are still wrong.

In real applications of the method it will, in general, not be possible to collect data sets of the enormous sizes necessary to reach reliable results for higher numbers of |Q|. As Simulation 2 shows, for example, a few hundred data patterns are sufficient to guarantee a good quality of the results for item sets up to 15 items.
