In his classic paper, Shannon ( Shannon, 1948 ) proved that there are certain limits to the amount of data compression that is possible. He formalised the notion of information, and showed that it is impossible to reversibly compress a piece of data to a length that is less than the amount of information contained. Data compression techniques consequently rely on discarding redundant information that can be reconstructed.

The motivation for the topology defined in this paper comes from the method of data compression known as arithmetic coding ( Witten, Neal, & Cleary, 1987 ), in which intervals in the real line are used to represent messages. Shorter intervals are used to represent messages containing more information; the unit interval represents a message containing no information at all. It has been shown that arithmetic coding is theoretically optimal, in that it will compress data right down to the Shannon limit.

We will generalise this notion to a metric topology over strings. Finite messages will be represented by non-empty compact sets, and, as with arithmetic coding, sets with smaller diameter will be used to represent messages containing more information.

The most-studied models of language lie in what has become known as the Chomsky hierarchy. After developing the mathematics of strings in some depth, we will show that large parts of the Chomsky hierarchy can be modelled in this way, giving rise to a connection between linguistics and fractal geometry.

Whenever two formerly separate areas of study are brought together, there will often be a significant exchange of ideas between them. Something similar might happen when languages are viewed as fractals. We take some time to consider some of the implications of the new point of view.

This paper spans a number of discipline boundaries, drawing something from topology, fractal geometry, formal language theory, and linguistics. Each of these areas has its own notations, knowledge and traditions, so it has been necessary to include a certain amount of explanation that might be considered by some readers to be very basic. Undergraduate level topology is necessary, so reminders are included for those readers whose last topology course was many years ago. Readers are referred to  Sutherland (1975)  for more information on basic topology.

In a generalisation of the method of arithmetic coding, the premetric can be used to represent the  cost  of sending a finite message, with smaller premetrics representing more expensive messages. The distance between two messages is then given by the cost of sending their common prefix.

We deliberately used the term “cost” instead of “length” here because the cost of letters may vary. For example, on the printed page, the cost of a printed letter might be the amount of space it takes on the page. The letter “I” requires less space than the letter “M”, and the combination “AV” takes less space than its constituent letters thanks to kerning. In arithmetic coding, compression happens when the cost of sending a message is proportional to the amount of information in the message.

Loosely speaking, a fractal is a compact subset of a metric space possessing a certain amount of self-similarity. One way to describe such sets ( Barnsley, 1993 ) is as the fixed point of a certain kind of contraction mapping. Because such objects are described in terms of themselves, self-similarity naturally arises. In this paper, we will show how formal grammars can correspond to contraction mappings, and how the corresponding languages are formed by taking the projection of the fixed points of those contraction mappings.

We have developed a considerable amount of theory in this paper, which invites questions of what it is all good for and how it came about.

Fractal geometry is a well-developed field with many tools and techniques available to us. Some of these techniques closely mirror those available to practitioners of formal language theory, while others are quite new or even meaningless outside a geometric context.

By viewing language as a fractal and bringing to bear all the machinery of fractal geometry, the author hopes to shed some light on how language works. Because the link between linguistics and geometry is new, it is worth speculating about the kinds of results one might hope to find. It will clearly require a great deal of further work to confirm or refute these speculations.

Fractal interpolation is a well-studied process for generating fractals that contain certain known points. The process is described in some detail in  Barnsley (1993, chapter 6) . In the case of languages, this corresponds to finding a language that contains certain known strings.

The problem of finding languages containing certain strings is generally extremely hard, although there are examples documented in the literature. Most famously, the Baum-Welch Algorithm ( Baum, Petrie, Soules, & Weiss, 1970 ) enables one to find a hidden Markov model that succinctly describes a given string. The Sequitur algorithm ( Nevill-Manning, 1996 ) takes a string and discovers a context-free grammar that generates the given string.

It has been widely noted that all human languages, although mutually unintelligible, contain very many grammatical similarities. This led to Chomsky’s idea of a Universal Grammar. In his theory, many of the basic features of a language are fixed and innate, and the differences arise between languages as the result of setting various parameters. The process of acquiring a language then consists of learning a lexicon and discerning the values of these parameters of the language’s grammar.

The idea of Universal Grammar arose from the observation that the linguistic input available to a young child ought to be insufficient to be able to learn the language, and yet we routinely witness children successfully speaking like their parents. This is known as the “poverty of the stimulus” argument. In particular, there are no examples of  ungrammatical  sentences available to the child, in spite of which we all grow up recognising that certain sentences are definitely ill-formed.

The argument of the poverty of stimulus seems to arise from the fundamental assumption that the acquisition of language involves  extrapolation  from the known grammatical utterances to the language as a whole. The concept of Universal Grammar says that, in fact, it is a kind of  interpolation  that enables a child to exclude all of the ungrammatical sentences that could otherwise be considered.

When we consider a language as the projection of a fractal, the problem of learning a language could be seen as analogous to fractal interpolation. There is a large body of research concerned with fractal interpolation, and maybe this can be brought to bear on the problem of language acquisition.

There has been considerable debate as to how, and indeed, whether, language could have arisen by natural selection. For a long time, it was considered taboo to even ask such questions. That was changed by Pinker and Bloom’s paper ( Pinker & Bloom, 1990 ) in which they argued forcefully that language must have evolved in a gradual process over a period of time, rather than springing into being suddenly as the result of a number of improbable near-simultaneous mutations.

How, then, could language have arisen? There seems to be little doubt that natural language follows the rules laid down by Chomsky, and yet the algorithms for dealing with such languages do not appear to be able to arise by small evolutionary steps. Parsers, for example, require all of their parts to be complete and in working order in order to produce any meaningful output at all. Remove a line of code from a parsing algorithm and you get gibberish.

This is in marked contrast with the evolution of other complex organs, such as eyes, in which an incomplete eye can still function as an eye, albeit one that produces a blurry image. Even cheap disposable cameras produce serviceable images in spite of having a fixed focus, fixed aperture and poor-quality film. A disposable parser wouldn’t be so lucky.

Fractals (or approximations to fractals) have evolved in nature repeatedly, for example, in the shape of a fern’s leaf, the structure of an animal’s lung, or the paths taken by blood vessels through the human body. These complex structures came about because they confer a selective advantage on their users: better ability for the fern to catch sunlight, better absorbtion of oxygen from the air, and better distribution of nutrients throughout the body. They have arisen by a process of gradual embellishment of simpler structures. It would seem that evolution has a natural gift for producing fractal structures.

It’s entirely possible that similar processes could have been at work during the evolution of the language faculty. In particular, by considering language as a projection of a fractal, one can envision a continuum of complexity ranging from the simplest proto-languages right up to human speech and beyond. This contrasts with the great evolutionary leaps that would be required to get from one level of the Chomsky Hierarchy to the next in traditional linguistics.

We have spent some time looking at the basic properties of compact ultrametric spaces, including a characterisation of all such spaces and their contraction mappings. Given the nature of the contraction mappings, it is highly likely that they will prove useful in the study of formal language theory. It may be the case that the use of contraction mappings enables us to generalise current descriptions of formal grammars, and possibly to provide an alternative description of the inner workings of natural language. In particular, the viewpoint put forward in this paper enables us to bring the well-developed machinery of fractal geometry to bear on the problem of describing syntactic structures. Finally, we have provided some wild (but perhaps useful) speculations about the consequences of this new point of view.

No piece of theory, however elegant, can stand on its own if it has no practical purpose. The hypothesis underlying the present work is that fractal geometry will prove useful in describing syntactic structures. As well as giving an exposition of the mathematics, it is hoped that the work will provide a fresh perspective and stimulate further discussion.
