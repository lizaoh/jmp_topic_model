Many years ago, Bruno de Finetti asked whether certain axioms for a comparative probability ordering relation o on the subsets of an n- element set that are necessary for the existence of an order-preserving probability measure are also sufficient. This was answered in the negative for n�5 by Kraft, Pratt, and Seidenberg [Annals of Mathematical Statistics 30 (1959), 408�419]. The present paper extends their analysis of comparative probabilities that satisfy de Finetti's axioms but lack order-preserving measures when it is assumed also that o is a linear order. We refer to a linear order on the subsets of [1, 2, ..., n] that satisfies de Finetti's axioms as an LQP (linear qualitative probability), and say that it is an NLQP (nonrepresentable LQP) if it has no order-preserving probability measure. The paper characterizes all NLQPs for n=5, shows that every n�5 has an NLQP that violates the simplest extension of de Finetti's additivity axiom yet has an order-preserving measure on the family of subsets of every (n&1)-element set, and proves that as n � � there is no upper bound on the number of o comparisons needed to verify the nonexistence of an order-preserving measure. Special examples for small n illustrate other facets of NLQPs, including the necessity of considering multiplicities of an o comparison in testing whether an LQP has an orderpreserving measure. ] 1996 Academic Press, Inc.

Our ensuing study of nonrepresentable qualitative probabilities assumes that o linearly orders 2[S] , and we will refer to (2[S] , o ) that satisfies A1�A4 with linear ordering as a linear qualitative probability. Because of our linear order assumption, nontrivial comparisons of equal likelihood are excluded. This has the effect of sharpening violations of A5 to a rather fine edge. It also allows a simplified and more elegant mathematical exposition. Moreover, our results under the linear restriction apply directly to the general case. Despite the exclusion of t comparisons, their spirit appears in many of our examples and constructions, where solutions to equations of the (:, x) =0 variety are perturbed to enforce linear orders and other desired effects. Indeed, our main construction paradigm, which is stated as Theorem 6.1, has this character. 

The paper is motivated by the penetrating analysis of finite difference structures and finitary theories of measurement in Scott and Suppes (1958) and Luce et al. (1990, Chapter 21). It is influenced also by the combinatoric and algorithmic analyses of finite additive conjoint measurement in Arbuckle and Larimer (1976) and Sherman (1977). Together with KPS the aims of these works are to understand the structures and axiomatics of finite systems of measurement, to devise criteria for testing for the existence of solutions, and to create algorithms for constructing solutions when they exist. 

The primary aim of the present paper is to better understand the structure of finite qualitative probability as defined by de Finetti (1931) and Savage (1954). It says a bit about testing and construction, but much remains to be done on these aspects, as suggested in Section 5. 

Our formal development of finite linear qualitative probability begins in Section 2 with a formulation based on vectors in [1, 0, &1][n] . The section presents elementary results for linear qualitative probability. It includes a vector axiomatization equivalent to A1�A4 with linear ordering, proves some basic consequences of these axioms, and states two versions of A5 in the [1, 0, &1][n] mode. One implication of the initial axioms is that every linear qualitative probability for n�4 is representable. Subsequently, Section 4 gives a complete account of nonrepresentable linear qualitative probabilities for n=5 that augments the KPS example presented earlier. Section 3 precedes this with a dominance result that helps to reduce failures of A5 to the simplest possible terms. We use the dominance result in Section 4 to show that if a linear qualitative probability violates A5 when n=5, then a violation is realized by the M=4 part of the strong additivity axiom. 

The final three sections of the paper investigate the question of how large M must be taken in A5 to cover all possible cases of nonrepresentable linear qualitative 

probabilities for each n. The results of these sections are fragmentary and suggest several avenues for new research Section 5 discusses the notion of irreducible patterns, which is explained further at the end of Section 2. Sections 6 and 7 focus on methods for constructing examples of linear qualitative probabilities that violate A5 for a specific M but satisfy all parts of A5 for smaller values of M. One consequence of our methods is that as n gets large there is no upper bound on M in A5 that covers all examples of nonrepresentable linear qualitative probabilities. Other important results are summarized at the end of Section 2.
