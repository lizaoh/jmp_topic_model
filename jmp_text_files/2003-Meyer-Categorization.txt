The outcomes of decisions often depend not only on the decision maker's correct assessment of the relevant state of affairs but also on other people's approval of the decisions, i.e., the benefits from a decision will be greater if other people agree with this decision. Situations in which a person benefits from agreeing with another person were named Consensus Games ( Gilat, Meyer, Erev, & Gopher, 1997 ). Many decisions in social settings are consensus games. For instance, a personnel officer in an organization will probably seek to maximize the quality of the hired personnel, but will also try to avoid making hiring decisions that will not be approved by others in the organization.

Previous experimental studies of behavior in consensus games ( Gilat et al., 1997 ) reveal that experience with these situations leads decision makers to ignore less likely events.  Gilat et al. (1997)  conducted two experiments on consensus games. The experiments used an external variance signal detection paradigm (see  Lee, 1963 ;  Kubovy, Rapoport, & Tversky, 1971 ) in which participants are asked to categorize stimuli that are sampled from one of two overlapping normal distributions. In both studies dyads of participants responded in 500 trials. In each trial the participants observed a dot at some point along a vertical axis and were told that the point represents the height of a person. Their task was to decide whether the person was male or female. The prior probability for one gender was 0.6. In the control one-person condition, participants received 10 points for an accurate response and lost 10 points for an incorrect response. In the consensus game condition the participants received 10 points only when they both classified the stimulus correctly. In all other cases (when at least one participant misclassified the person) they lost 10 points. Payoff feedback (10 or −10 points) was provided immediately after each choice, and no other feedback was given. The payoff matrix was not explained, and neither was the fact mentioned that a player's payoff depends on the other player's response. Results showed a tendency to neglect less likely events in the consensus game condition, as reflected by a strong effect of the prior probability of a state on the probability of its detection. For example, the rate of accurate detection of the less likely state (prior probability of 0.4) in the last two blocks (rounds 300–500) of Experiment 1 in  Gilat et al. (1997)  was 0.66 in the control condition and only 0.41 in the consensus game condition. On the other hand, accurate detection of the common state (prior probability of 0.6) was 0.8 in the control condition and as high as 0.89 in the consensus game condition.

Gilat et al. (1997)  provide three indications for the robustness of these results. First, these results are predicted by the descriptive variant of signal detection theory proposed by  Erev, Gopher, Itkin, and Greenshpan (1995,  and see  Erev, 1998) . This model, which replaces classical SDT's expected value assumption with the assumption of reinforcement learning (see  Roth & Erev, 1995 ) among cutoff strategies, can account for the main behavioral regularities observed in previous signal detection research ( Erev, 1998 ). It also provided adequate predictions about players’ decisions in two-person perceptual games ( Gopher, Itkin, Erev, Meyer, & Armony, 2000 ). Simulation results from this model reveal that virtual players, that behave according to the assumptions of this cutoff reinforcement learning (CRL) signal detection model (described in details in the appendix and in  Gilat et al., 1997 ), behave very similar to  Gilat et al.'s (1997)  experimental subjects. That is, the probability of indicating less likely events in consensus games decreases. Another indication of the potential robustness of the observed behavioral pattern comes from the observation that it is at equilibrium, i.e., players have no incentive to change their behavior.

Thus, even if the effect may be the result of an adaptive learning process, a completely rational player will not behave differently.

Finally,  Gilat et al. (1997)  observed the consensus effect under distinct information conditions. A strong effect was documented when players did not know the underlying payoff rule. Payoff information slowed the learning process but did not eliminate the effect. This result was accounted for by the assumption that information affects the players’ initial propensities (one of the parameters in the learning model).

The finding that the availability of accurate and potentially useful information may impair adaptation is not unique. The same has been shown in at least three other contexts. In “generalization traps” information impairs adaptation because individuals behave as if they over-generalize a reasonable rule to a situation in which it is maladaptive. For example, Maddox and his associates ( Maddox & Bohil, 1998 ;  Maddox & Dodd, 2001 ) found that observers behave as if they follow the rule “try to be accurate” even when an increase in accuracy is costly. Similarly,  Erev and Rapoport (1998)  found costly overgeneralization of the rule “use the recent information.” In “dynamic traps” information impairs adaptation to a change (see  Hayes, Brownstein, Haar, & Greenway, 1986 ). Finally, in “timing traps” information appears to lead to myopic behavior (e.g.,  Messick & Brewer, 1983 ;  Messick & McClelland, 1983 ;  Busemeyer & Myung, 1992 ;  Thaler, Tversky, Kahneman, & Schwartz, 1997 ).

The games considered here are two-person binary perceptual games in which the observers cannot communicate but can affect each other's payoff. As in the classic Signal Detection Theory (SDT;  Green & Swets, 1966/1988 ), decision makers are required to decide to which of two mutually exclusive categories (S1 and S2) an event belongs. We will assume that S1 is the more common state and S2 is the less common state (i.e., the prior probabilities satisfy the constraint  P (S1)⩾ P (S2)). In the one-person case four outcomes are possible—accurately or wrongly stating S1 or S2. In one-person SDT the outcome of a decision depends only on its correspondence to the actual state of nature. In the two-person extension of SDT the outcome of a person's choice depends also on another person's choice.

Apparatus : The experiment was conducted on a 486PC, with a Super VGA 14′′ monitor. On the screen were two gray rectangles, each 6.6 cm wide and 13 cm high, on a blue background. Participants sat side-by-side facing the screen at a distance of approximately 50 cm from the screen. Both responded by pressing keys on the keyboard that was positioned in front of them. A partition was placed between the participants, preventing them from seeing the other's display or responses.

In each trial the participants saw for 1 s a white square, 2 mm wide and 2 mm high, at one of 66 possible locations along the right vertical edge of the gray rectangle. The base rate was set so that 60% of the stimuli were drawn from one distribution and 40% from the other distribution. The location of the stimulus was randomly determined for each participant in each trial. That is, the participants saw the stimuli at different locations, although both stimuli were sampled from the same distribution (the higher or the lower), keeping the state of nature the same for both participants. Stimuli were independently sampled for each player.

The results of Experiments 1 and 2 can be explained by the suggestion that the consensus effect is sensitive to the availability of accuracy information. A strong consensus effect was only evident when players received no clear accuracy information. When accuracy information is available it moves subjects toward probability matching. Experiment 3 was designed to provide a test of this explanation and to assess its generality by studying a direct manipulation of the accuracy information. Whereas the difference in the available information was one of the byproducts of other manipulations in the first two experiments, it is the only difference between the two conditions studied here. Wider generality is achieved by studying a conjunctive (rather than an additive) consensus game.

The CRL model (with its original parameters) predicts the large consensus effect in conditions such as the  V 
                      C = V 
                      A  condition in Experiment 1 and the No Feedback condition in Experiment 3. The model does not predict the effect of “accuracy feedback.” Thus, the CRL model with a single set of parameters cannot predict behavior in all signal detection tasks. It seems that in order to account for the current results, the value of at least one of the parameters of the model should be “information dependent”.

The idea of an information-dependent parameter in CRL was already noted in  Gilat et al. (1997) . CRL provides a better description of these data under the assumption that additional information affects the value of an initial propensities parameter. Specifically, in  Gilat et al. (1997) , full information increased conservatism (the observed ln 
                      β  values were closer to 0). This effect was captured with the assumption that the initial propensity to select an unbiased cutoff (ln 
                      β  of about 0) increases when the decision makers receive full information. To examine if this assumption improves the fit of the current results we derived the predictions of two variants of CRL to the conditions examined here: the original model (with the parameters estimated in  Erev et al., 1995 ) and the version that assumes information dependent initial parameter (with the parameters estimated in  Gilat et al., 1997 ). The basic assumption and the estimated parameters are described in the appendix. The results (right-hand curves in  Figs. 1–3 ) show that the version that assumes information specific initial propensities with more information better fits the current data. Yet, the results also show that better models can be found. Possible directions to refine CRL can be the addition of a hill climbing process (see  Busemeyer & Myung, 1992 ) and/or explicit abstraction of the probability matching hypothesis. Hill climbing can be naturally added to this model with the assumption that in addition to the cutoff strategies, the decision makers consider a hill climbing strategy. This strategy conditions the action taken at trial  t +1 on the choice and the outcome of the previous trials. Specifically, it implies a cutoff increase if a previous increase was successful and a cutoff decrease otherwise. The potential value of adding hill climbing as a strategy to a reinforcement learning model is demonstrated in  Erev and Barron (2002) . Probability matching can be added as a strategy (that implies a selection of the action that moves the choice proportion toward the population proportions), or as a “subjective transformation” of the outcomes. Under the “subjective transformation” decision makers behave as if they find outcomes that improve matching more reinforcing.

Gilat et al. (1997)  showed that the incentive to reach consensus can affect binary categorization decisions by lowering the probability of detecting rare events and raising the probability of detecting common events, a phenomenon named the “consensus effect”. The three experiments described above were designed to test the robustness of the consensus effect. Experiment 1 has shown that the effect can occur in additive consensus games, and it increases with the relative magnitude of the incentive to reach consensus. Experiments 2 and 3 aimed to identify the conditions that eliminate the effect and revealed that the effect depends on the available post-decision information. A strong effect emerges only when observers receive no information about the correctness of their response.

These results indicate that consensus effects arise when no accuracy feedback is available, and the effect diminishes the more information is available. The increase in the detection of common events and the decrease in the detection of less likely events that occur in consensus-game-like situations may have practical implications. For instance, in personnel decisions this will mean that a person from a group whose members were usually hired for a position will have an increased probability to be hired whether he or she is suited for the position or not. In contrast, a person from a group whose members are less frequently hired will have a smaller chance to be hired in consensus-game-like situations than when consensus in decisions is not rewarded. Therefore, differences between groups are likely to increase when decisions are made in consensus situations.

The effect exists mainly when accuracy feedback is unavailable. However, this is probably the case in most settings that resemble consensus games. For instance, personnel managers almost never receive information about the quality of applicants who were rejected and often receive only partial information on the applicants that were accepted (see  Einhorn & Hogarth, 1978 ). If at all, the feedback becomes available to the decision maker only after an extended period of time, which may make it inefficient. It is, therefore, likely that a strong consensus effect is the rule, rather than the exception, in consensus-game-like situations.

Our results imply that certain measures could possibly alleviate the problems that arise in consensus-game-like situations. First, measures such as affirmative action in hiring decisions may encourage a disregard of prior base rates in decision making. This may eventually lead to an overall improvement of the quality of the employees, an effect that is not usually associated with affirmative action. Organizations may also seek to provide feedback about the quality of decisions. For instance, surveys may be conducted that assess the success of candidates that were rejected and provide the personnel officers in an organization with information about the approximate correctness of their decisions. This information is likely to lower or eliminate the potentially problematic consequences of consensus effects. However, such methods are time consuming, difficult to implement, and costly, and it is likely that in most consensus-game-like situations no feedback about the accuracy of decisions will be available. The awareness of the possible existence of a problem is perhaps the best way to avoid it.

Our study demonstrates that the differences between binary consensus games and individual categorization decisions can be captured by a model of the environment that specifies the incentive structure, and may not require distinct models of the underlying psychological processes. Based on this observation we used the CRL descriptive variant of signal detection theory to predict behavior in consensus games.

The CRL model with the assumption that information affects the players’ initial propensities can account for the information effect observed here. This explanation is consistent with the explanation provided by  Gilat et al. (1997)  for the slowing of learning with prior information about the payoff rule. The finding that both phenomena can be captured by a manipulation of the same parameter in the CRL model suggests that this parameter may describe an important psychological construct.

Yet, it should be noted that there are other possible explanations for the two information effects. For example, it is possible that players find the outcome “you are unbiased” to be reinforcing independently of the payoffs. In the current setting being “unbiased” implies probability matching. The current experimental and simulation results cannot be used to reject these and similar explanations. Future research is needed to improve our understanding of the psychology of the information effect. One conclusion that can be reached from the fact that the information effect can be captured by the CRL model with modification of the initial propensity parameter is that the effect may not be as robust as it looks in the 500 experimental rounds. Under the CRL model the manipulation of the initial propensities in the current games should not have a long-term effect. For example, after 2000 trials the differences between the curves in the second and third columns in  Figs. 1–3  disappear.

The study aimed to provide a deeper understanding of the effect of the incentive to reach consensus on decision making. It shows that the existence of a consensus effect is robust to the modification of the payoff rule, but is sensitive to the addition of information that allows the assessment of decision accuracy. Accuracy information slows learning and can eliminate the effect.

More generally, our paper demonstrates the importance of reinforcement-based learning in decision making, but it also shows that decision makers do not simply alter responses according to the reinforcements they receive. Rather, prior knowledge and cognitive processes will change the patterns generated by reinforcement learning. These factors have to be incorporated in learning models through changes in the relevant parameters. In this sense, our study shows how reinforcement learning and cognitive processes can be integrated into one explanatory model of categorization decisions.

We also suggest that under certain conditions simple reinforcement-based processes may determine the occurrence of complex social phenomena. This does not mean that the reinforcement processes solely determine these phenomena. However, any explanation of these phenomena will have to show why the simple reinforcement mechanism is not sufficient to account for the effects. We show again that the incentive structure can affect people's behavior even when they are not aware of the specific details of the structure. These results demonstrate that in order to be able to predict decisions, we must consider the social context in which the decisions are made, the incentive structure that exists in this context, and the decision makers’ prior knowledge and expectations.
