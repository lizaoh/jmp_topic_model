NAKATANI  Bell Telephone Laboratories, Incorporated, Murray Hill, New Jersey 07974  The confusion-choice model for multidimensional psychophysics proposes the following abstraction of the stimulus recognition  process. Each stimulus Si and its correct response R; are represented by a common point in a multidimensional subjective space  of minimum dimensionality. Presentation of S; yields a set of noisy proximity measures which indicate how close each response  is to S; . All the responses which are close enough to Si according to the proximity measures are considered acceptable; all  other responses are unacceptable. The observer chooses a response at random from among the acceptable responses in accord with  a choice probability distribution which may not be uniform due to response bias. The model successfully predicts the confusion  matrices for color and tone recognition experiments including response bias effects in the data.  The data from a pure recognition experiment (Luce, 1963) can be summarized in a confusion matrix C where element ci; is the  proportion of times response R; was made when stimulus Si was presented, and is an estimate of the conditional confusion  probability P(R; I Si). An adequate model for a pure recognition experiment should predict the confusion matrix including the  asymmetries which are usually present. Multidimensional scaling models (MDS) have been highly successful in predicting  confusion matrices without pronounced asymmetries (Shepard, 1962), but MDS is a statistical data reduction technique rather  than a model of a psychological process. Luce's choice model (Luce, 1963) has successfully predicted confusion matrices  (Townsend, 1971), but the model is response oriented and says very little about the perceptual processes involved in  recognition. THE CONFUSION-CHOICE MODEL  The confusion-choice model proposed in this paper attempts to model the recognition process with a fairly complicated  perceptual process incorporating concepts from signal detection theory, and also with a reasonably complex response process  based on the choice axiom (Luce, 1959). In addition, the model represents stimuli and responses as points in a  multidimensional subjective space so as to concisely characterize the stimuli in terms of their salient psychological  attributes.  The General Confusion-Choice Model2  The most general formulation of the confusion-choice model is based on the following assumptions:  A. Each stimulus is associated with a point in space.  B. Each response is associated with  (1) (2) (3) (4)  a point in space, a noisy channel, a threshold, and a bias probability.  C. The bias probabilities form a probability distribution over the response set. D. When a stimulus is presented, each  response channel yields an output which is equal to the distance between the stimulus point and the corresponding response  point. However, this output is contaminated by noise.  E. If the noisy channel output is below the response threshold, the response is acceptable; otherwise it is unacceptable.  F. The observer chooses from among the acceptable responses, if any, in accord with the bias distribution, conditioned on  acceptability.  G. If there is no acceptable response, the observer chooses from among all the responses in accord with the unconditioned bias  distribution.  The Confusion-Choice Recognition Model  The general formulation of the model is applicable to a broad range of classification or judgment experiments (e.g., what is  the class of exemplar X?). The following assumptions restrict the confusion-choice model to pure recognition experiments:  1. There is a one-to-one correspondence between the members of the stimulus and response sets.  2. The corresponding stimulus and response points coincide in space.  Assumption 1 assures that there is a unique correct response for each stimulus. Assumption 2 assures that the correct response  has the highest probability of being acceptable when a stimulus is presented. Moreover, the coincidence of the stimulus and  response points eliminates the response point coordinates as free parameters in the model.  The following assumptions are made primarily for mathematical convenience. These are perhaps the most dubious of the  assumptions, but they could easily be replaced by other assumptions without altering the model conceptually as long as they do  not lead to a mathematically intractable model.  3. The space is Euclidean.  This assumption defines a metric in the space and assures that the configuration of points remains invariant under  translations, reflections, and orthogonal rotations.  4. The noise in each channel is an additive normal random variable with zero mean and unit variance, and is independent of the  noise in the other channels.  The additivity and independence assumptions are essential for the model to be tractable. The normality assumption is a  hallowed tradition which has received empirical support from signal detection theory. The choice of a unit variance for all  the channel noise distributions fixes the scale of the space by defining a unit of distance.  The following assumption eliminates the thresholds as free parameters of the model.  5. The response thresholds are a function of the point configuration, but are independent of the bias probabilities.  The way in which the thresholds are defined by the point configuration will be deferred until later to allow for a more  concise definition.  Perhaps only the first two of these assumptions are sacred for a recognition model. Other realizations of the model resulting  from a different set of assumptions may be well worth pursuing. In fact, assumption 5 is currently under examination to see if  some limitations of the model can be overcome. For practical reasons, only the  CONFUSION-CHOICE MODEL 107  version of the confusion-choice model resulting from the above assumptions will be derived in this paper. To minimize F, initial X and B  matrices were chosen. These parameters completely defined C and ultimately F through a series of intermediate matrices as  shown in Fig. 2. The gradient of the F measure was computed by finding its partial derivative with respect to the point  coordinates in X and the bias parameters in B (except bn which was defined in terms of the other bias probabilities by the  equation bn = 1 b;). The X and B matrices were adjusted in accord with the information in the gradient of F and the cycle was  repeated. The estimate-fit-adjust cycles or iterations were repeated until F reached a minimum within some reasonable  tolerance. The X and B matrices taken from the final iteration constituted the estimated parameters of the confusion-choice  model. For each iteration, the threshold matrix T was also estimated numerically since the locations of the individual  thresholds could not be solved analytically.  A new procedure was used to obtain a point configuration for the initial estimate of X and also the dimensionality of the  space. The procedure first averaged 0 with its transpose to get a symmetric confusion matric Z Then a distance matrix U was  computed by considering each ci; for i ~ j as an ordinate of a cumulative normal distribution function with mean 0 and  variance 1, and equating uii to the magnitude of the normal deviate corresponding to ci; . Each uai = 0 by definition. The  Torgerson (1958, Chap. 11) scaling procedure was then used to obtain the initial estimate for X from U. Examination of the  eigenvalues computed in this step revealed the most likely dimensionality of the solution space. This procedure yielded an  initial configuration which was very close to that of the final solution in the space of the correct dimensionality. The  problem of local minima was successfully avoided with this procedure for obtaining the initial estimate of X.  TESTS OF THE MODEL  Three sets of data were used to test the various features of the model. The first set was synthetic data generated to bring  out a critical difference between nonmetric MDS and the confusion-choice model. The second set of data was obtained in a color  recognition experiment by Shepard (1958); scaling of the Munsell color chips  CONFUSION-CHOICE MODEL 115  used in the experiment has become more or less a traditional test for MDS models. The final set of data was obtained by Hodge  and Pollack (1962) in a tone recognition experiment. These data further tested the MDS aspect of the model since the shape of  the stimulus configuration was known a priori from considerations independent of the model. The tone confusion matrix also had  rather pronounced asymmetries which tested the decision process of the model.  Synthetic Data  The test of the model with synthetic data had two aims. The first was to determine whether the points and bias probabilities  used to generate a synthetic confusion matrix could be recovered by the parameter estimation program. The second aim was to  show that the confusion-choice model could uniquely scale a configuration of points which was known to yield an infinite set  of solutions using nonmetric MDS. The test with synthetic data has demonstrated that a viable program exists for the estimation of points and biases, and that  the scaling solutions from nonmetric MDS and the confusion-choice model can differ considerably under certain conditions.  Shepard (1966) has demonstrated that the accuracy with which a configuration can be scaled by nonmetric MDS declines as the  number of points declines. No such dependency between accuracy and number of points is to be expected for the confusion-choice  model. The synthetic data were deliberately chosen to dramatize this difference. The difference is due to the fact that  nonmetric MDS is concerned only with the rank order of distances whereas the confusion-choice model takes both rank order and  the magnitude of the distances into account in the scaling process.  Munsell Color Recognition Data  At least seven scaling procedures have been used to scale a set of nine Munsell color chips in two-dimensional space. Despite  the differences among the procedures, they all yielded similar configurations which resembled the original Munsell color  configuration. Because of this unanimity, there can be little doubt about the general shape of the color configuration. Any  new MDS procedure which failed to obtain a similar configuration for the colors would be seriously discredited.  The confusion matrix used to scale the colors with the confusion-choice model was obtained by Shepard (1958) in a task where  subjects learned to recognize nine Munsell colors of a constant red hue which varied in value (brightness) and chroma  (saturation). The original Munsell coordinates (Munsell Book of Color, 1945) for the nine colors are tabulated in Shepard  (1958). Shepard presented each color approximately 22 times to each of 36 subjects for a total of about 800 observations per  color. The confusion matrix was averaged over all subjects so that the estimated points and biases are applicable only to the  group. LIMITATIONS OF THE MODEL  The constant-ratio rule (Clarke, 1957; Luce, 1959) has been highly successful in predicting the confusion matrix CS for a  stimulus set S, from the confusion matrix C1 for a master stimulus set M, when S is a subset of M, and the experimental  conditions remain the same for S and M. Briefly, the constant-ratio rule predicts CS from Cm by deleting all the rows and  columns of C,except those which also occur in CS, and renormalizing the reduced matrix so that each row sums to one. The  rationale for this procedure is given in Luce (1959). Similar predictions for CS given C with the confusion-choice model  using data from Clarke (1957), Egan (1957), Wagenaar (1968), and Hodge and Pollack (1962), consistently underestimated the  recognition probabilities in CS . One may regard this result as equivalent to overpredicting the recognition probabilities in  CM . From this point of view, it appears that the confusion-choice model is failing to predict the decline in recognition  probabilities with an increase in the size of the stimulus set which is a well-documented finding in word recognition (Miller,  Heise, and Lichten, 1951; Pollack, 1959). This failure is perplexing since a simplified version of the confusion-choice model  successfully predicted the decline in average recognition probability as the stimulus set size increased (Nakatani, 1968).  Apparently, the model can predict the effect of stimulus set size on average recognition probability, but not its effect on  recognition probabilities for a particular stimulus set. The reason for this discrepancy is not known.  The model is also unable to predict the channel capacity effect in the recognition of simple stimuli. This effect refers to  the finding that performance in a stimulus recognition experiment, when measured by the bits of information transmitted over  the human channel, initially increases and then levels off at a stable asymptotic value as the number of stimuli increases  (see Garner, 1962, for a review).  In addition to the theoretical limitations on the model just described, there is also a serious practical limitation on the  applicability of the model. Because of the tremendous amount of computing time and memory space required, the estimation of  parameters for the model becomes excessively expensive when the number of stimuli exceeds ten. An appreciation of the  magnitude of this problem can be gained when one realizes that for n = 10, the E and D matrices each contain 10,240 elements.  The computation time is roughly proportional to n2 x 21, x L, where L is the dimensionality of the space. Efforts are being  made to reduce computing time and memory space, but spectacular progress is unlikely since each time n increases by one, the  computing time and memory requirements more than double.  CONFUSION-CHOICE MODEL 125  CONCLUSION  The hybrid confusion-choice model seems to predict the confusion matrices from pure recognition experiments quite well. The  MDS feature reduces the number of free parameters necessary to characterize the perceptual process in the model. At the same  time, the geometric representation provides insight into the attributes used by observers to distinguish among stimuli in a  recognition task. Although the specific version of the model described here used a Euclidean metric, any metric which does not  lead to an intractable model could have been used without altering the model conceptually.  Signal detection theory contributed the notions of perceptual noise and response criterion or threshold in the perceptual  process of the model. And in the tradition of signal detection theory, the confusion-choice model also maintains a rigorous  and testable distinction between a perceptual process which is only affected by stimulus variables, and a response or decision  process which is influenced only by variables which affect the observer's subjective payoff matrix.  It should be emphasized at this point that the assumptions of normality and equal variance are not crucial to the model. The  assumptions were motivated by tradition, mathematical simplicity, and a desire to minimize the number of free parameters. Some  applications of the model will surely require changes in these assumptions. As mentioned at the outset, the definition of the  thresholds may need careful examination. Perhaps the thresholds should be made to depend on the bias parameters so that the  thresholds would be relaxed for common stimuli but made more stringent for rare stimuli; this would incorporate a perceptual  tuning or filtering effect in the model.  The decision process of the model uses the choice axiom as the underlying response model of how people resolve their confusion  when more than one response (or none) are acceptable. It should be made explicit that the confusion-choice model is a  threshold model since proximity information is not used at all in resolving confusions. This model of the response process is  counterintuitive since it appears that valuable information is being ignored in selecting a response, but the model fits the  data well despite the "nonoptimal" decision process based solely on the choice axiom.  Relationship to Other Models  The distinction between the perceptual and response processes in the confusionchoice model was motivated in part by the work  of Atkinson and Kinchla (1965), and the equation C = ED is taken from their notation. However, both processes of the  confusion-choice model are considerably more complicated than those of Atkinson and Kinchla, and represent more than a simple  extension of their work.  Broadbent (1967) proposed a stimulus recognition model with provisions for response bias, but the model has been revealed by  Nakatani (1970) as making the same  489/11-9  126 NAKATANI  prediction as the sample guessing model for a single confusion matrix. Catlin (1969) has also criticized the model for its  inability to fit data from word recognition experiments.  Townsend (1971) recently proposed an overlap activation model which he acknowledged is a special case of the model described  by Nakatani (1968) although Townsend's model was developed independently. Townsend tested a simplified version of the model  with alphabet confusion data, and achieved fits comparable to those of Luce's choice model.  The model most closely related to, and competitive with, the confusion-choice model is the choice model for stimulus detection  and recognition described most concisely by Luce (1963, Chap. 3). The choice model is a more abstract formulation of the  original metric multidimensional scaling model with response weights which Shepard (1957) proposed for the analysis of  confusion matrices. It appears that no program is currently available to estimate the parameters (the point configuration and  response biases) of the choice model. This makes it difficult to compare the models directly. Nevertheless, it is my feeling  that it will be impossible to decide between the two models on the basis of their fits to confusion matrices. More likely, the  selection will be based on the reasonableness of the intuitions which underlie the models, their heuristic value, and  subsidiary predictions of the models. The choice model is a more general formulation of the constant-ratio rule and hence can  make the same predictions as the rule. The confusion-choice model failed, in preliminary tests, to predict the constant-ratio  rule effect. However, neither model can predict the channel capacity effect in stimulus recognition (Luce, 1963, p. 171).  Further work must be done with both models, not merely to prove one model superior to the other, but to gain further insight  into multidimensional psychophysics.  REFERENCES  ATKINSON, R. C.
