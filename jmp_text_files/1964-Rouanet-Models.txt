JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY: 1, 215-232 (1964)  Stochastic Models for the Response Continuum in a Determinate Situation: Comparisons and Extensions'  HENRY ROUANET  University of Paris, Paris, France  AND  SEYMOUR ROSENBERG  Bell Telephone Laboratories, Inc., Murray Hill, New jersey  Several stochastic models for the response continuum with determinate reinforcement have recently appeared in the literature.  A number of these models have developed more or less independently of each other so that underlying psychological concepts,  experimental tasks, and mathematical style all differ to some extent. The present paper examines and compares the mathematical  properties of a number of these models. It is shown that several models are all variants of a simple and general stochastic  process. Considerations in the experimental testing of these models are discussed.  Several stochastic models have recently been proposed for the response continuum both with determinate reinforcement  (Anderson, 1959, 1961; Anderson and Hovland, 1957; Rosenberg, 1961, 1962; Suppes, 1959, 1960; Suppes and Rouanet, 1963) and  with certain types of "nondeterminate" reinforcement (Rosenberg, 1963a; Suppes and Zinnes, 1961). A number of these models  have developed more or less independently of each other so that it is not surprising that underlying psychological concepts,  experimental tasks, and both mathematical style and notation, all differ to some extent.  We note, for example, that the models were formulated by Suppes as direct extensions of the learning and conditioning concepts  which were basic to the development of the linear and stimulus-sampling models for finite-response tasks. The circular  continuum used in the experimental analysis of these models can be viewed as a natural extension of the classical  Humphreys-board situation. By contrast, the initial linear model by Anderson and Hovland has no obvious  roots in conditioning but was formulated for and applied to opinion and attitude change. Later variations by Anderson were  identified as learning models, applicable to a variety of response continua such as the line-drawing task, and based on linear  operators analogous to those used by Bush and Mosteller (1955). The models proposed by Rosenberg are an outgrowth of an early  series of experiments by Hall and Rosenberg (e.g., Hall, 1957; Rosenberg, 1960) on two-person interactions using Bilodeau's  (1953) knob-turning task and, more recently, an oscilloscope task. The models involve two components which correspond to two  psychological processes, viz., a variable rate of response change by the subject based on the distance between actual and  correct responses and psychophysical or estimation errors in judging this distance and in executing a response on the  continuum.  The purpose of the present paper is to examine and compare the mathematical rather than psychological properties of a number  of these models. It will be shown that the Anderson-Hovland Model, the Anderson Models I and 11, the Rosenberg an - eModel,  and the Suppes One-Element Model are all variants of a simple and general stochastic process. We shall refer to this  particular aggregate of published models as the C models. THE RESPONSE CONTINUUM IN A DETERMINATE SITUATION  All models of interest in this paper apply to an experimental situation in which responses and outcomes occur in ordered pairs  over a sequence of discrete trials. The subject, at a signal which starts the trial, makes a response located on a  one-dimensional continuum. An outcome follows the response and the trial is completed.  Familiar examples of a response continuum are drawing a line blindfolded, positioning a concealed lever, selecting a point on  a numerical interval or on the circumference of a circle, depressing a switch for a time interval, turning a concealed knob,  STOCHASTIC MODELS FOR RESPONSE CONTINUUM 217  autokinetic situation with numerical feedback by an experimenter, and expressing an attitude on a continuous scale. For each  task there must be a rule of correspondence between the physical response continuum and a segment of the real line. To date,  specific experimental tests for the various models have employed the circular-disk task (Suppes and Frankmann, 1961; Suppes  and Rouanet, 1963; Suppes, Rouanet, Levine, and Frankmann, 1963) and the oscilloscope task (Rosenberg, 1962, 1963b).  A number of finite or denumerably infinite response sets may also be ordered on a single dimension. However, except for  certain tasks such as selecting one out of a set of integers, finite response sets usually contain a small number of  alternatives (often just two) which are deliberately presented as unordered and treated as such in a model.  The outcome set usually contains an event which is identified for the subject as "correct," "good," "right," "minimum error,"  "maximum gain," "target," "goal area," etc. A determinate situation is one in which the outcome also contains a display of the  response choice which would have been correct in that trial and the way in which the outcome contains this information is made  explicit to the subject. The location of the correct response is not explicitly known by the experimenter in opinion formation  tasks where the persuasive communication presented to the subjects corresponds to the outcome event.  Although the notion of a determinate situation for a response continuum is a generalization from finite-response tasks (Estes,  1957), a few special remarks are necessary concerning some of the outcome sets which have been used with a response continuum.  The most straightforward display of the correct response is the use of the response continuum itself, e.g., in the  circular-disk task the experimenter points to the correct response on the same circumference to which the subject responded.  More frequently the actual and correct responses are both displayed (or evaluated) for the subject on an arbitrary continuum  which is physically different from the response continuum, e.g., the subject responds to the continuous numerical segment [0,  100] by positioning a knob-pointer and both the actual and correct responses are then displayed as x-displacements on a  display-square. For a number of response continua, e.g., depressing a switch for a time interval, it is necessary to use an  outcome continuum which is different from the response continuum. Finally, an arbitrary outcome set may consist only of a  difference value, with sign, between correct and actual responses i.e., response error. There has been no analogue of this  type of outcome set in unstr ured finite-response tasks.  Response variability, particularly during early trials, is undoubtedly larger in a determinate situation with an arbitrary  outcome continuum than with the response continuum as the outcome continuum. The existence of prominent points on the outcome  continuum such as bounds, midpoints, etc. obviously reduces the problem of learning the correspondence between response and  outcome continua. Any random error in response which remains because the subject does not learn the correspondence perfectly  is subsumed in the stochastic process described by C models.  218 ROUANET AND ROSENBERG  BASIC TERMS  All models discussed in this paper apply to the behavior of an individual subject under specified experimental conditions. A bold-face letter refers to a random variable and the corresponding letter in italics denotes a possible value of the random  variable. The comparisons among models have  also pointed up the important fact that various models share certain predictions so that empirical tests are often relevant to  a large class of models. In any case, detailed analyses of data from response continua are limited to a handful of studies on  two or three tasks. Instead of either reviewing the available evidence or attempting to provide a systematic discussion of  model-testing, we will make a few somewhat tentative remarks about empirical tests.  First, it is possible to derive nontrivial parameter-free predictions which distinguish among certain specific models in 'C3.  A number of these differences were described in the previous section, e.g., homoscedasticity vs. heteroscedasticity, the  strong additive variance property at asymptote, and linearity in the dx - d relationship. Anderson (1961) has also pointed out  that under special reinforcement conditions the asymptotic autocorrelations will distinguish between Anderson-Hovland and  Suppes Models, on the one hand, and Anderson I and II, on the other hand; the asymptotic autocorrelations in the first two  models are zero when yn is a constant for all n. Generally speaking, the models at the bottom of the tree in Fig. 1 provide  stronger parameter-free predictions than those near the top of the tree and, hence, are, a priori, likely to be refuted more  often.  The comparisons among models which require parameters are often complicated by the fact that different numbers of parameters  may be involved for different models and that the parameters themselves may be different. It is desirable, whenever possible,  to find comparison tests in which the models are on an equal footing, e.g., the use of a parameter whose estimate is valid for  all the models being compared. The paper by Suppes, Rouanet, Levine, and Frankmann (1963) furnishes an example of this  approach for certain comparisons involving sequential statistics. Except for the Simple Model where it is assumed that c= 1,  c appears to be one such parameter in V3 models.
