This work and the terminology that we  have adopted are motivated by an application to the assessment of knowledge. Suppose that X is a set of problems or questions  in some field 0 of knowledge. It is then reasonable to represent the state of knowledge of any individual with respect to (P  by some subset K of X. That is, K contains all the questions that are solvable by the individual. The set of all possible  states of knowledge of individuals in a population is thus a particular family x of subsets of X, which we assume to be known.  To determine the state of an individual, an examiner will ask a given question, say x. Depending on whether the answer is  correct or not, another question y E X will be chosen. Ideally, no redundant questions are asked and the procedure terminates  only when the knowledge state KE h of the individual is determined. The protocol of the examination thus takes the form of a  word, say  xyt...  in some assessment language L. In this context, the language L symbolizes a particular procedure for the assessment of the  knowledge of individuals with respect to  246 DEGREEF ET AL.  the set X of questions. The set of states implicitly postulated by an examiner in a practical situation may be revealed by an  analysis of the subject's protocols. The possibility of such an analysis motivated questions [Q1] and [Q2] above.  The choice of a particular assessment language may be dictated by consideration of economy. The approach to knowledge assessment followed here is purely set-theoretical, thus deterministic. A good case can be made,  however, that probabilistic methods are desirable. In this connection, we indicate that a class of stochastic procedures for  knowledge assessment has been investigated by Falmagne and Doignon (1985). In such procedures, a trial begins from a given  likelihood of all the possible states. A question is then proposed, the choice of which is based on that likelihood. It is  assumed that the response observed is governed by the unknown state of the student, which may vary from trial to trial. The  computation of the likelihood on the next trial is then performed, based on some predefined updating rule. The goal of the  assessment procedure is to estimate the probability distribution on the set of all possible knowledge states characterizing a  particular student. No further discussion of this work will be given here.  We also mention in passing a practical application. Along lines closely related to the work discussed in this paper, Pavel  (personnal communication) has developed a procedure for the assessment of the mastery of a widely used computer operating  system. This procedure has been applied successfully in a special case in which the structure of the family of states is  consistent with that of a partial order.  In a recent paper, Doignon and Falmagne (1985) have given an axiomatic analysis of the structure of a family of states of  knowledge.
