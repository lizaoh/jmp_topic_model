JUDY  Department of Radiology, Harvard Medical School and Brigham and Women's Hospital  A standard method of estimating a single ROC curve from rating data for two stimulus alternatives has been extended to ratings  of multiple alternatives. An observer's ratings are assumed to represent ordinal classifications of a unidimensional decision  variable that has a separate distribution for each of M possible stimuli. From these rating data, a maximumlikelihood  procedure simultaneously estimates the rating-category boundary values and the 2(M-1) distribution parameters that specify ROC  curves between all pairs of the M stimulus alternatives. Many stimulus manipulations, particularly those investigated in  psychophysical experiments with visual or auditory stimuli, could justify this M-alternative rating procedure and analysis. An  advantage of this method is that it allows reliable measurement of an observer's performance indices at much higher values  than does the two-alternative method. The assumption of a unidimensional decision variable may be too restrictive for general  decision-making situations, where the decisions among alternatives often involve multiple sources of information. However, the  two-alternative method is commonly used to fit rating-ROC curves for some decision-making tasks, such as diagnosis from  medical images, for which the multiple-alternative procedure actually might be more appropriate. INTRODUCTION  Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) analysis is used in perception and memory experiments as a means of quantifying a  subject's ability to perform a specified decision task. The value of this methodology rests on its ability to distinguish  changes in the level of performance from changes in decision criteria. ROC analysis assumes that the (often multidimensional)  input from each stimulus is combined into a single decision variable which lies along a unidimensional continuum. An ROC curve  can be generated from data produced by the subject's use of different binary-decision criteria to categorize stimuli drawn  from two alternative classes. Rating-ROC experiments ask the subject to assign each stimulus to one of several ordinal  categories representing various levels of confidence that the stimulus belongs to one class rather than the other. Any ROC  analysis of such data requires some degree of confusion between these two classes of stimuli.  In plotting or fitting empirical ROC curves it is often assumed that each class of stimuli can be associated with a normal  distribution in the decision variable (Green & Swets, 1974; Metz & Kronman, 1980). The ROC fitting procedure that has become standard (Dorfman & Alf, 1969) uses a maximum-likelihood procedure to  estimate the two parameters of this linear ROC curve (the mean and standard deviation of one assumed-normal distribution,  scaled in terms of the other) and the ROC operating points, given by the category boundaries of the scaled decision variable.  The method is easily specialized to the case of equalvariance normal distributions (e.g., Swensson & Judy, 1981) by fitting a  linear ROC curve (of unit slope) with a single estimated parameter (d').  The procedure developed in this paper extends this method of analysis to situations that present stimuli from more than two  classes, so that a subject's ratings must distinguish among M > 2 alternatives. Like parametric ROC methods developed for two  alternatives, it assumes that the subject's rating judgments represent ordinal partitions of a continuous unidimensional  variable which has a separate two-parameter distribution for each class of stimuli. In this case, there are M > 2 possible  classes of stimuli. One distribution is chosen as a reference to fix the scale of the assumed decision variable. An iterative  maximum-likelihood procedure simultaneously estimates the two parameters for each of the other M- 1 distributions, together  with the boundary values between successive rating categories. The estimated distributions specify an ROC curve between each  pair of binary stimulus alternatives; i.e., this procedure essentially fits ROC curves for all M(M- 1)/2 possible pairs of  these M classes of stimuli.  The assumption of a unidimensional decision variable for a set of M stimulus alternatives is implicit in many scaling  experiments, whose data analyses apply formal models (e.g., Thurstone, 1927; Torgerson, 1958; Adams & Messick, 1958;  Schonemann & Tucker, 1967; McCullagh, 1980) that are closely related to those of ROC analysis (Ogilvie & Creelman, 1968;  Dorfman & Alf, 1969; Grey & Morgan, 1972) and to the method presented here. In many psychophysical experiments, the subject's  decision variable also might be adequately treated as unidimensional across the experimental manipulations of some parameter  (e.g., stimulus intensity). For stimuli whose discriminability is limited by physical noise, a unidimensional representation  is often suggested by a statistical analysis of the optimal decisionmaking strategy. Later in this report we consider the  problem of analyzing decisions based on noisy, gray-scale visual stimuli.  In the next section we present the likelihood equations for the multiple-alternative procedure, followed by details of the  fitting algorithm. Next we illustrate and discuss some of its applications to sets of rating data, including data obtained  from subjects in experiments using gray-scale visual stimuli. A final section considers the application of the  multiple-alternative method-and two-alternative ROC methods -in more general decision-making situations, such as medical  diagnosis, where the decisions between or among alternatives often depend on multiple sources of information.  430 KIJEWSKI, SWENSSON, AND JUDY  GENERALIZATION OF LIKELIHOOD EQUATIONS  Likelihood equations for fitting ROC curves to ratings of stimuli drawn from two classes have been derived assuming different  distributional forms for the underlying decision variable. Logistic distributions were assumed by Ogilvie and Creelman (1968)  and normal distributions by Dorfman and Alf (1969), whose original equations contained errors later corrected by Grey and  Morgan (1972). Our formulation extends the latter approach to any number of stimulus classes and, like these two-alternative  formulations, is a modification of a more general method previously developed to analyze scaling data (Schoneman & Tucker,  1967). THE FITTING ALGORITHM'  In order to apply this set of likelihood equations, the fitting algorithm must assume a specific parametric form for the  decision-variable distributions. Given an assumed parametric form, the maximum-likelihood estimates of a; and b; specify the M  separate distributions up to an arbitrary choice of location and scale. These estimates, however, are inferred from the  realized overlap in the M cumulative probability distributions at each ordinal rating criterion, which preserve only ordinal  relations among the realized decision variables. These cumulative distributions, and hence the estimates of a; and b;, would  be unaffected by any positive monotonic transformation of the actual underlying decision axis. For this reason, a simple and  convenient parametric form, such as the normal distribution, may adequately represent an observer's differentiations among the  M classes of stimuli. Our fitting algorithm assumes a representation by two-parameter (unequalvariance) normal distributions.  In the case of M=2 (the standard "binormal" model), these distributional assumptions have been shown to yield an extremely  robust descriptive model that provides good fits to a wide variety of empirical rating data-even to ordinal ratings of large  samples from underlying distributions (e.g., gamma or triangular) that are far from the normal in form (Hanley, 1988). This  model is easily specialized to the case of equal-variance normal distributions by setting b; - 1 and using a reduced set of  the likelihood equations to estimate the M - 1 distributional parameters, a; ( = d').  An observer's set of rating data is arranged in a K+ 1 x M matrix whose cells contain the values of r;,j, the number of  responses in each of the K+ 1 categories from each of the M stimulus distributions. These can be obtained from the cumulative response distributions for the M stimulus classes by converting each cumulative  proportion to a standardized normal deviate using the inverse normal transformation. Linear regression of the normal deviates  for successive, adjacent pairs of distributions (using only those categories which contain responses from both distributions)  provides consistent estimates for the a;, b;, and z; parameters. Reversing the dependent and independent variables for a given  pair of distributions yields a second set of regression estimates. The two pairs of initial estimates for each parameter are  averaged and rescaled to the reference distribution. The rescaled linear parameters for the M- 1 pairs of adjacent  distributions provide the initial estimates of a; and b;. The rescaled estimates of the K category boundaries (averaged within  and across pairs of distributions) provide a set of initial values for  Z;  For some sets of rating data, the set of initial estimates obtained by this procedure does not lead to convergenc of the  fitting program. In this event, a second set of initial estimates is obtained by fitting standard two-alternative ROC curves  to adjacent pairs of distributions and rescaling all parameter estimates to the reference distribution (averaging the separate  estimates of each zJ If this second attempt does not succeed, a last resort is to fit the data using an equal-variance version  of the fitting program which sets all the b; to 1, thereby reducing the number of estimated distribution parameters by M - 1.  The estimates are successively refined, using the equations shown above, until the program either converges to an acceptable  solution or appears to be diverging. If at some point the denominator in any element of the matrix X is zero-i.e., two  boundary estimates are identical-then the value of the denominator is set to 10-5 in order to avoid singularities. The  condition defining convergence is that the sum of the absolute values of the corrections is less than a specified criterion,  usually 10-3; it is sometimes necessary to relax the criterion when the number of fitted parameters is large. APPLICATION TO PERCEPTUAL EXPERIMENTS  In this section we first discuss the physical basis for the assumption of a unidimensional decision variable in certain  psychophysical experiments. We then consider a three-alternative experiment using gray-scale visual stimuli, in which the  observer's task was to detect features that could be either brighter or darker than the uniform-background (noise-only)  alternative. The multiple-alternative proce-  436 KIJEWSKI, SWENSSON, AND JUDY  dure has a natural application in such experiments, because the issues under investigation dictate a multiple-alternative  design. However, a multiple-alternative design and analysis may also be useful in other situations, such as simple detection,  which could be studied using standard, two-alternative experiments. In an experiment that manipulates the "strength" of some  parameter (e.g., feature contrast) of the stimulus, a multiple-alternative design can considerably extend the range of  physical values over which an observer's performance can be measured. This point is discussed in the final subsection.  1. Decisions about Noise-Limited Visual Stimuli  The physical stimuli in auditory or visual experiments may include stochastic variations (noise) that make the stimulus  classes imperfectly discriminable. In some cases, particularly for experiments with gray-scale visual images, a decision  variable can be calculated directly from the physical representation of the stimulus. Such a calculation serves as a physical  model for the human observer, in the sense that the decision performance achieved by this image calculation can be compared to  the performance of subjects who are confronted with the same decision task. An "optimal" calculation, which realizes an "ideal  observer", provides both a performance standard (for evaluating the human observer's efficiency) and a model for predicting  how the observer's performance might vary in response to physical stimulus manipulations. The definition of an optimal  calculation, as formulated for decisions between binary alternatives (M=2), may be extended to the case of decisions among M  unidimensional alternatives. A unidimensional decision variable can be regarded as "optimal" for differentiations among the M  possible alternatives if it is simultaneously optimal for making binary decisions between all M(M- 1)/2 possible pairs of  those M alternatives.  A given calculation of the sampled data is optimal for binary decisions between a particular pair of specified alternatives  (M=2) if it is a monotonic function of the joint-likelihood ratio of the data under those two hypothesized alternatives. Given  a proper choice of the binary-decision criterion, it is well known that such a decision variable maximizes the expected payoff  (minimizes expected cost) for any specified combination of prior probabilities between the two stimulus-alternatives and  payoffs (error costs) associated with the possible decision outcomes (Chernoff & Moses, 1959). For the gray-scale images  considered here, a cross-correlator provides an optimum calculation, because it is a linear function of the log of the  joint-likelihood ratio for the realized stimulus, given any specified pair of alternatives. Moreover, across certain types of  stimulus variations, the realized crosscorrelations for one specified pair of stimuli are constant multiples of those values  that would be calculated (optimally) for any other specified pair. In such cases of M possible alternatives, therefore, the  cross-correlator for any particular pair of alternatives yields an optimal decision variable (as defined above), since it  provides a unidimensional realized variable that is optimal for making binary decisions between any other pair of these M  alternatives.  The cross-correlator is a simple calculation that consists of a weighted sum of all  ANALYSIS OF RATING DATA 437  elements of the physical stimulus representation. Gray-scale stimuli can be completely described by a set of numbers, < Y;>,  each associated with a particular picture element (pixel). If the random variations in these numbers are stationary, spatially  uncorrelated, and normally distributed, then a cross-correlation calculation provides an optimal decision variable for any  pair of binary alternatives (Wagner, 1978; Wagner & Brown, 1985). The cross-correlator (for a stimulus whose location is  known), C = E; ( W; Y;), combines a realized stimulus with the expected `difference profile', i.e., W; weights each pixel  value in proportion to its expected difference given the two stimulus alternatives. Under the assumptions above, C is a linear  function of the logarithm of the joint likelihood ratio for each realized stimulus, given the two possible alternatives.  Therefore, the cross-correlator is a prescription for combining the stimulus properties into a unidimensional variable that  realizes an ideal observer for decisions between any pair of known alternatives at the specified image locations. Moreover, as  a weighted sum of equalvariance, normal variables, C has a normal distribution with the same standard deviation for both  stimulus alternatives. The cross-correlator's 'signal-to-noise ratio' (SNR), the difference in these two mean values of C  relative to their common standard deviation, completely specifies the expected ROC curve for the ideal observer.  Optimal cross-correlation calculations for visual stimuli have been compared to observer performance in various  binary-alternative tasks (Barlow, 1978; Burgess & Ghandeharian, 1985; Burgess, Wagner, Jennings, & Barlow, 1981; Jones, 1959;  Judy, Swensson & Kijewski, 1987; Loo, Doi, & Metz, 1984; Myers, Barrett, Borgstrom, Patton, & Seeley, 1985; Sullivan, 1983).  Whether or not a physical cross-correlator captures the ideal observer in a particular task, however, it may still provide a  useful descriptive model for the human observer's decision variable. Even when physical properties of the visual images (e.g.,  spatially correlated or non-stationary noise) make the cross-correlator suboptimal, its calculations of SNR often closely  predict the quantitative effects of physical stimulus manipulations on observer's detection and discrimination performance  (Judy & Swensson, 1985a; 1985b; 1986; 1987; Judy, Swensson, & Szulc, 1981; Myers et al., 1985; Seltzer, Swensson, Judy, &  Nawfel, 1988; Swensson & Judy, 1981.  Among the important stimulus variables for tasks involving detection of features superimposed on noisy gray-scale images are  contrast and polarity-i.e., the magnitude and sign of the expected difference in intensity between a feature and the noisy  background. Given a properly chosen physical scale, changing a feature's contrast or polarity simply multiplies its expected  profile by a constant. If M possible stimulus alternatives are features that may differ only in their contrast or polarity  (including the uniform-background alternative), then a single cross-correlation calculation provides a unidimensional decision  variable for distinguishing among these M alternatives at a specified image location. The weighting function can be the  expected difference profile between one (arbitrarily chosen) pair of the M alternatives, because the difference profile for  any other pair of these alternatives will differ from the chosen weighting function only by a multiplicative constant. The cross-correlation calculation  gives rise to a separate distribution of the variable C for each of the M possible stimuli. If C is an optimal decision  variable for any one pair of alternatives, then it will also be optimal for the decisions between every possible pair of these  M stimulus alternatives.  2. A Three-Alternative Experiment Using Visual Stimuli  Multiple-alternative experiments enable us to investigate a greater variety of decision tasks; the parameters of the resulting  multiple-distribution data can be simultaneously estimated by the method reported here. As an example of the flexibility  afforded by a multiple-alternative design, we describe an experiment that studied observer's performance using a  three-alternative, 'dual-polarity' detection task, in which a visual feature, if present, could be either brighter or darker  than the background (noise-alone) level. Separate companion experiments measured performance in the three two-alternative  tasks defined in terms of pairs of these three alternatives. The validity of assuming unidimensionality for the  three-alternative analysis was tested by comparing its simultaneous estimates of observers' ability to separate each pair of  stimulus alternatives with those obtained from the three separate binary tasks, using the standard two-alternative method.  Visual Stimuli and Procedure. The stimuli were film transparencies of images containing backgrounds of stationary,  uncorrelated, Gaussian noise, quantized into 256 gray levels, with a pixel standard deviation of 40. These gray levels were  linear with film optical density over a range of 2.2 optical density units, so that they produced equal log-luminance  increments when the images were viewed on a standard medical film illuminator. The circular background areas (pedestals), 128  image pixels in diameter (29 mm), varied slightly in their mean gray level (128 10). Each set of images consisted of 144  pedestals, whose centers could contain superimposed disks (14-pixel diameter) of a specified contrast (mean shift in gray  level), either darker or brighter than the pedestal mean. Areas of the image outside these pedestals were darkened.  The dual-polarity detection task presented three alternatives; half of the 144 pedestals consisted of the noise background  alone, while half also contained features. Of the 72 superimposed disks, half were brighter than the background, and half were  darker. The other three tasks, known-polarity detection and polarity discrimination, presented only two alternatives. In the  known-polarity detection tasks, half of the pedestals contained a disk of specified polarity (bright or dark) and half did  not. In the polarity discrimination task all 144 pedestals contained a superimposed disk; of these, 72 were bright and 72 were  dark. For each decision task, four experimental conditions which varied the level of feature contrast were tested. These  conditions used independently generated noise backgrounds.  Within each task and condition, different sets of images balanced any fortuitous  ANALYSIS OF RATING DATA 439  polarity effects that might have resulted from the extremes of particular noise samples. For example, a cluster of high-valued  (bright) pixels at a pedestal's center would enhance the detectability of a positive-polarity feature and depress the  detectability of a negative-polarity feature. To avoid bias due to such effects, we produced for each experimental condition  four sets of stimuli representing the four possible combinations of feature polarity and `noise polarity', i.e., the `sign' of  the random pixel variations, relative to the mean background level. The sets for a given condition used the same noisy  backgrounds (with `noise polarity' reversed in sets 1 and 3) and the same `feature' and 'non-feature' pedestals (with feature  polarity reversed in sets 3 and 4). This balanced across the four sets of images any asymmetries which might have resulted  from the particular noise samples used in a given condition.  The three observers used a six-category scale to rate for each pedestal their confidence either that a feature was present  (detection tasks) or that the feature was bright rather than dark (polarity-discrimination task), assigning the ratings 1 and  6, respectively, to the lowest and highest levels of confidence in that binary decision. In the dual-polarity detection task,  the observers made an additional forced-choice judgment of feature polarity, regardless of their judgment regarding the  presence of the feature. This procedure yielded for the three-alternative task a 12-category rating scale consisting of signed  integers (zero not included) ranging from -6 (dark feature definitely present) to +6 (bright feature definitely present). This  12-category scale, like the six-category scale used in the binary-decision tasks, was assumed to represent an ordinal  partitioning of a continuous unidimensional decision variable.  Analysis of Performance. For the three two-alternative tasks, each observer's ratings of the 144 stimulus pedestals in each  set of images were analyzed using the standard two-alternative ROC method (Dorfman & Alf, 1969). The accuracy (detection or  discrimination) implied by each ROC curve was described by the index dQ (Appendix B), a particular positive transformation of  the area below the fitted ROC curve which can be interpreted as a `perceptual SNR' for the observer's decision variable (Judy  & Swensson, 1987; Sandor & Swensson, 1978; Swets & Pickett, 1982). For the three-alternative (dual-polarity detection) task,  each set of 12-category ratings (16 sets from each observer) was analyzed using the multiplealternative method described  above. The fitted distribution parameters provided separate estimates of do for discrimination between each pair of the three  stimulus alternatives (see Appendix B). These estimates of d,, were averaged across the four sets of images for each observer,  task, and contrast condition.  The efficiency of the human visual system is defined in terms of the performance of an ideal observer; in this situation, it  is the squared ratio of the perceptual SNR, dQ, to the task SNR. Application to Other Unidimensional Experiments  Implicit in the ability to analyze data from multiple-alternative experiments is a means of extending the range over which  human performance can be measured. A limitation of traditional ROC analysis is that performance indices such as d' or dQ  cannot be reliably estimated for values greater than about 3 without using  M31,4-5  442 KIJEWSKI, SWENSSON, AND JUDY  prohibitively large numbers of stimuli. For example, a two-alternative experiment cannot quantify the detectability of visual  features whose contrast is sufficiently high that they are never confused with the 'noise-alone' stimuli in the realized  samples. Experimenters have circumvented this practical limitation by using `bootstrap' procedures involving a series of  binary-alternative experiments. In visual detection, for example, Nachmias and Kocher (1970) investigated the variation of the  performance index estimates with luminance level. To expand the range of luminance values over which performance could be  measured, they combined a relatively lowluminance detection experiment with a series of luminance-discrimination experiments  that used successively higher feature-luminance values. The parameter estimates from these separate experiments were then  transformed onto a single scale, assuming a unidimensional decision variable. An alternative to this procedure would be to  perform a single detection experiment using features which, if present, may be at any of several levels of luminance and  analyze the data using the multiple alternative method. As shown by the simulation experiments described below, a  multiple-alternative design and analysis can yield more precise estimates of the performance parameters for stimulus samples  of equal size.  Simulated multiple-distribution data were used to compare parameter estimates from the direct, multiple-alternative method and  for an indirect, bootstrap method. Ten thousand sets of five frequency distributions were simulated using a Gaussian random  deviate generator (Press, Flannery, Teukolsky, & Vetterling, 1986). Each set of distributions simulated 100 samples of a  realized decision variable for each of five classes of stimuli that differed only in mean value (ju; = 0.0, 1.0, 2.0, 3.0,  4.0; ai= 1.0). The 500 samples for each set were sorted into 16 ordinal categories, using category boundaries that equated the  expected values of the total number of stimuli in each category. The parameters a and b were estimated for each distribution  using the multiple-alternative procedure and, from them, estimates of do were derived (Appendix B). (The results of a  multiple-alternative fit to a sample set of simulated rating data are presented in Table 2.) For the bootstrap procedure,  separate estimates of a and b were obtained from the four pairs of adjacent frequency distributions within each set, using the  standard two-distribution ROC method. Before the bootstrap estimates of d. were calculated, the estimates of a and b from  alternate distribution pairs were switched with corresponding estimates from another set of distributions. This eliminated  correlations in the parameter estimates for successive distribution .pairs, and simulated a series of independent,  binaryalternative experiments. The direct analysis produced more precise estimates than did the bootstrap analysis (Fig. 2).  These results were predicted by a first-order propagation of error analysis, using the estimates of parameter variances and  covariances provided by the estimation procedure.  The multiple-alternative method of analysis has another advantage over the bootstrap method, in that it provides a single,  consistent set of category-boundary estimates. Since these estimates locate the ordinal rating categories on the linear axis  of the perceptual decision variable, they permit performance comparisons on a stimulus-by-stimulus basis. APPLICATION TO MEDICAL DECISION MAKING  ROC analysis has found wide application in medical decision making in a number of specialties (e.g., Kassirer, Moskowitz, Lau,  & Pauker, 1987; Richardson, Schwartz, Weinbaum, & Gabbe, 1985; Slap, Connor, Wigton, & Schwartz, 1986). Medical (and other  general) decisions are typically more complex than those involved in psychophysical experiments, and, consequently, the  application of ROC methods is not always straightforward. The requirement for a unidimensional decision variable poses a  severe constraint on applications of the multiple-alternative method, since the various alternatives may depend on different  characteristics of data derived from several sources (e.g., images, laboratory tests, clinical symptoms). For decisions  between any two alternatives, this problem can be circumvented, at least in principle, by formulating the task in terms of the  joint likelihood ratio for the realized data, given each alternative. One can devise an analogous formulation for the  M-alternative task, which would permit decisions to be based on the relative posterior probabilities of (posterior odds ratios  between) these M alternatives for the realized data. But such a formulation would require calculating a sufficient set of M -  1 joint likelihood ratios (e.g., S; vs Se,, for i = 1, ..., M- 1), and hence it leads to a general representation in M-1  (non-orthogonal) dimensions. This means that no single-valued (unidimensional) decision variable will generally describe the  pairwise confusions among M possible alternatives.  Unlike laboratory experiments, however, most natural diagnostic situations do not involve stimuli that are sampled from some  predetermined set of (two or more) discrete classes. Rather than being inherent in the nature of the stimuli, the underlying  structure that defines the set of possible "truth states" is usually imposed by the specified decision task. There has been a  rapid growth in applications of ROC analysis to diagnostic decisions about medical images (see Metz, 1986b), using tasks that  impose a binary-alternative structure on the image stimuli. In such "forced-binary" tasks, physicians are typically asked to  rate patients' images according to the likelihood or degree of perceived abnormality. These assumedunidimensional ratings are  then used to derive ROC curves that measure how well they can differentiate between two subsets of the cases, dichotomously  classified as either "abnormal" (one or more lesions actually present) or "normal" (no lesions actually present). The standard  two-alternative ROC method is commonly used to fit binormal ROC curves, even when these rating data are obtained from  experiments in which one class of stimuli (e.g., "abnormal" cases) actually  ANALYSIS OF RATING DATA 445  comprises several disparate entities (e.g., lesions of different types or in different anatomic locations). When the image  stimuli are more naturally grouped into M>2 classes, however, applying the present, multiple-alternative method of analysis to  these assumed-unidimensional ratings might actually violate fewer model assumptions than does the two-alternative method.  One such study was reported by Swets et al. (1979), who investigated detection of brain lesions on medical images. Observers  were asked to rate the images for each case on a five-category scale (ranging from definitely abnormal to definitely normal)  and to classify the lesions, if present, into one of 16 anatomic locations and one of 12 diagnostic types (four neoplastic,  eight benign). The rating data were used to fit an ROC curve for each observer's differentiations between normal and abnormal  cases, with the implicit assumption that the various (types and locations of) lesions has been judged on a common  unidimensional scale of abnormality. (Indeed, the task was so defined as to force the observers to do so.) The authors also  attempted to fit ROC curves to subsets of the data, restricting abnormal images to those containing lesions of a particular  type or in a particular location, in an effort to discern differences in detection performance. Although these detailed  analyses did not yield conclusive results, they did suggest that such differences in performance may exist. If so, then the  distributions in the decision variable (likelihood of abnormality) associated with particular types of lesions are  characterized by different parameters. This would violate the assumption of two normal distributions in the decision variable  for the overall ROC analysis (all abnormal cases vs normal cases), since a mixture of several different normal distributions  is not, in general, another normal distribution. A two-distribution ROC analysis may be less appropriate in such situations  than a multiple-alternative analysis, which formally allows for the possibility of several distributions with different  parameters.  A multiple-alternative analysis of data from such a `forced unidimensional' task must be interpreted with care, since the  lesions are being rated on a somewhat artificial basis. Although the distributions associated with various types of ,abnormal'  lesions might overlap considerably, this would not imply that these different types of lesions can be confused with one  another. It would only mean that they were not well separated in the unidimensional decision variable imposed by the forced  binary-classification task, i.e., differentiations based on the likelihood (or degree) of abnormality. Metz (1988) alludes to  this point in discussing a similar issue in chest radiography.  Another caveat for interpreting data from forced-binary tasks is that, in general, the measured performance will be  context-dependent, even for estimates obtained by a multiple-alternative method. The forced-unidimensional variable would  generally lead to poorer separations (e.g., between class S;, i= 1, 2, ..., M- 1, the lesions of a particular type or  location, and class Sm, the `no lesion' alternative) than those measured in two-alternative tasks, defined for specific pairs  of these M alternatives (e.g., S, vs S,u). One approach to modeling such forced-binary tasks assumes that the observer  actually performs M- 1 concurrent tasks (S; vs SM for  446 KIJEWSKI, SWENSSON, AND JUDY  i = 1, 2, ..., M- 1), and then uses the maximum of these M- 1 separate values (appropriately normalized) as the  binary-alternative decision variable. Such formal models of observer performance, although restricted to M-1  assumed-orthogonal variables (different signal types or locations), have been considered for general visual experiments  (Graham, Kramer, & Yager, 1987), and for tasks with medicalimage stimuli (Starr, Metz, Lusted, & Goodenough, 1975; Swensson,  1988; Swensson & Judy, 1981).
