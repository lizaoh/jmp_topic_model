Few methods are as well-suited as pairwise comparisons for studying internal consistency in the evaluation of choice options ( Bezembinder, 1981; Fishburn, 1982, 1992; Regenwetter & Davis-Stober, in press ). For example, when option  j  is preferred to option  k , and option  k  is preferred to option  l , an internally consistent choice is to prefer option  j  over option  l . If option  l  is selected in the last comparison then this indicates an intransitive cycle in the judgmental process that may be either systematic or reflective of the stochastic nature of the choice process. Most probabilistic paired comparison models treat inconsistent choices as caused by independent and random errors in the pairwise judgments ( Böckenholt & Tsai, 2001; Sopher & Gigliotti, 1993 ). The purpose of this paper is to propose a more general framework that can account for systematically inconsistent choice outcomes of a group of judges. As illustrated by an application of this framework to an experiment on intertemporal choice, our approach provides new insights on the underlying mechanisms that may cause judges to be internally inconsistent.

Starting with  Thurstone's (1927)  influential paper, a number of probabilistic models have been proposed over the years for the analysis of paired comparison data ( Bock, 1958; Böckenholt, 2001, 2006; Böckenholt & Dillon, 1997; Böckenholt & Tsai, 2001; David, 1988; De Soete, Carroll, & DeSarbo, 1986; Maydeu-Olivares, 2001; Takane, 1987; Tsai & Böckenholt, 2002, 2006 ). Because paired comparison data are collected typically by asking a group of judges to compare multiple option pairs, this work emphasized the importance of distinguishing between sources of within- and between-judge variability. Although in his original proposal for the analysis of paired comparison data,  Thurstone (1927)  ignored this distinction, it is well accepted now that a separate treatment of these sources is crucial to avoid incorrect substantive conclusions and improper statistical inferences (for a recent review see  Böckenholt & Tsai, 2006, chap. 14 ).  Takane (1987)  developed a general analysis-of-covariance framework for modeling between-judge variability. Subsequent developments and applications by  Maydeu-Olivares (2001)  and  Böckenholt and Tsai (2001)  demonstrated the usefulness of this approach in understanding individual differences. Interestingly, the within-subject component received much less attention in the literature and has remained the same as in  Thurstone's (1927)  original proposal. As a result, pair-specific variability is the only source to explain inconsistent choice behavior for paired comparison data collected from multiple judges. This factor is sufficient when inconsistent choices are unsystematic. However, experimental studies on intransitive choice behavior have demonstrated that intransitivities can be systematic ( Lindman & Lyons, 1978; Montgomery, 1977; Tversky, 1969 ) in which case paired comparison models that allow for pair-specific variability only may provide an insufficient account of the incidence of intransitive choices.

Our proposal for a modeling framework that explains systematically consistent and inconsistent choices considers both within- and between-sources of variability. We adopt  Takane's (1987)  analysis-of-covariance structure to describe how judges differ in the evaluation of the choice options. The within-subject component is based on the random-effects model recently developed by  Tsai and Böckenholt (2006)  for the analysis of single-subject paired comparison data. Their approach does not constrain the latent utility of a choice option to be identical in different pairwise comparisons which in a reported analysis of  Tversky's (1969)  gambling study proved crucial in modeling inconsistent choice patterns. To be more specific, subjects in  Tversky's (1969)  study are shown to judge a lottery differently depending on whether it was paired with an adjacent or a non-adjacent lottery. Because the pictorial presentation format of the probabilities complicates the comparison of adjacent probabilities, the participants paid little attention to the small probability difference but based their decisions on the monetary payoff instead. When differences between the probabilities became more salient, however, this information was utilized in the subject's selection of the more attractive lottery. This shift in the response strategy led to significant negative dependencies in the repeated choices of the judges which were well accounted for by the random effects framework.

By unifying  Tsai and Böckenholt's (2006)  approach with  Takane's (1987)  between-subject models, we obtain a general class of paired comparisons models that can account simultaneously for transitive choice behavior and systematic deviations from this normative standard. The next section discusses the between- and within-subject component of the proposed model class in detail. Subsequently, we discuss issues of estimation and model testing in the context of two simulation studies which show that an incorrect model is likely to be chosen when the within-subject level is misspecified. In a re-analysis of intertemporal choice data reported by  Roelofsma and Read (2000) , we demonstrate that the proposed model class can provide a satisfactory fit of the data although close to 60% of the choice triplets are intransitive. The paper concludes with a discussion of the main results.

In summary, we note that the LI estimators work well for sample sizes of 300 or larger. The specified within-subject dependency structure could be detected with reasonable precision in this case. Ignoring dependency effects led to systematically biased estimates for both mean and covariance structures. Although for smaller sample sizes, estimation and model inference require more care, simulation studies as the ones presented here can be conducted readily using Mplus ( Muthén & Muthén, 1998–2006 ). We recommend therefore to examine the behavior of the test statistics and LI estimators of the model structure in a simulation study especially when the sample size is small.

We conclude that the attribute-shift hypothesis can provide a good account of the judgmental process of the intertemporal choice data.

This paper introduced a two-level paired comparison model which simultaneously accounts for within-judge intransitive choice behavior as well as between-judge individual differences. The proposed approach extends both  Takane's (1987)  analysis-of-covariance model by providing a more general within-judge representation and the  Tsai and Böckenholt (2006)  dependent–response model by allowing for multiple judges evaluating the option pairs. As a result, powerful tests of both the local independence assumption between option pairs and of hypotheses on possible sources of intransitive responses are now available. Our analysis of the intertemporal choice data demonstrated the usefulness of the proposed approach in understanding systematic intransitive behavior despite the fairly small sample size of the study.

Applications of the proposed models as well as tests of a wide range of hypotheses about between- and within-judge effects are facilitated greatly by the LI estimation approach. The LI method is especially attractive when the number of options in the paired comparison task is large. In this case, integration- or simulation-based estimation approaches require much computational effort which coupled with slow convergence behavior limits the usefulness of these approaches in applied work. The results of the simulation studies suggest that the LI estimators work well for the considered within-subject dependency structures. Even for moderate sample sizes, the within-subject dependency structure can be estimated with reasonable power whereas ignoring this within-judge effect can lead to systematically biased mean and covariance structures.
