Economic theory postulates that individuals have a utility function and make choices with respect to this utility function, generally under a budget constraint (i.e., what can be chosen is limited by the individuals’ revenue). This central assumption is called the  utility maximization hypothesis . Are agents’ behaviors in the “real world” consistent with the utility maximization hypothesis ?

One way to answer this question is referred to (in the literature) as the non-parametric method. This method relies on an axiomatic approach that states that it is possible to directly test for utility function maximization behavior by testing certain axioms.

                         To the best of our knowledge, no paper using experimental data finds zero violations of GARP. Even  Andreoni and Miller (2002) , who conclude that agents’ behavior is consistent with GARP, observe 18 subjects (over a total of 176) who violate GARP.

Sippel (1997)  finds, in his first experiment (with 12 subjects), 5 individuals (41.7%) who violate GARP. He also finds, in his second experiment (with 30 subjects), 19 individuals (63.3%) who violate GARP.  Mattei (2000) , over three experimental data sets, obtains the same results: 25%, 59% and 51.25% of individuals (from 12, 100 and 320 subjects, respectively) violate GARP.  Février and Visser (2004)  find that 29% of individuals (35 out of 120) violate GARP.

These violations, however, may also simply highlight the fact that preference relations are not transitive in general. Indeed, from a mathematical standpoint, a utility function is an order of isomorphism. Therefore, over a finite set, representing preferences by a utility function is equivalent to  completeness  and  transitivity  of the preference relation. Hence, requiring an agent to maximize a utility function is equivalent to requiring him to maximize a complete and transitive preference relation.

Our argument in this paper is that there is no theoretical or empirical justification for a preference transitivity requirement. This problem is an old one and has its roots in  Fechner (1860) ,  Poincaré (1902) , and  Armstrong (1939) , who all suggest that an agent’s indifference relation (defined as the symmetric part of the corresponding preference relation) is not, in general, transitive.

Theorists of preferences (mostly mathematicians) have therefore constructed several types of non-transitive preference relations with a transitive asymmetric component and a symmetric component, which are not necessarily transitive. The most famous relation of this type is the so-called semi-order, whose functional representation has been widely studied by  Luce (1956) ,  Scott and Suppes (1958) ,  Roberts (1970) ,  Fishburn (1973, 1970) ,  Bridges (1983) ,  Chateauneuf (1987) , and many others (see  Pirlot & Vincke, 1997  for a review).

The requirement of transitivity for the asymmetric part of a binary relation is not easy to justify, and has been challenged by  Kreweras (1961) ,  Burros (1974) ,  Bell (1982) ,  Fishburn (1984) ,  Anand (1987) ,  Fishburn and Lavalle (1987) ,  Loomes, Starmer, and Sugden (1991) ,  Dombi and Vincze (1994) , and many others. Hence the modeling of preferences by acyclic binary relations, where both symmetric and asymmetric components are not necessarily transitive.

To summarize, for most theorists of preferences (for a review, see  Alekserov, Bouyssou, and Monjardet (2007) ), the correct system of axioms for a preference relation is not  completeness and transitivity  but instead  completeness and acyclicity . The reason for this is that, on the one hand, having an acyclic preference does not prevent an agent from choosing (this agent can still choose the best elements with respect to his preferences) and, on the other hand, the acyclicity axiom is compatible with choice function theory. Indeed, as shown by  Jamison and Lau (1973) , when the choice domain is abstract, a choice is Richter-rational ( Richter, 1971 ) if and only if it is rationalizable by a complete and acyclic preference relation.

The question we ask in this paper is the following:  are agents’ behaviors in the “real world” consistent with the maximization of a real-valued function representing a complete-acyclic preference relation?

We test this RARP axiom using three experimental data sets constructed by  Mattei (2000) . The first and second data sets include the consumption behavior of 20 and 100 students, respectively, in 20 different budget situations. The third data set includes 320 subjects who were recruited through an announcement in a Swiss magazine for consumer affairs. We find that the number of individuals who are RARP-consistent are 100%, 97% and 95% of individuals in, respectively, the first, second and third experimental data sets. Moreover, in the first data set, 100% of the individuals who violate GARP respect RARP, in the second data set this share is as high as 93%, and in the third data set the number of GARP-violating but RARP-consistent individuals reaches 84% of subjects. Thus, although over the three data sets more than 30% of individuals are not utility function maximizers, a significant portion of them (more than 90% on average) maximize a real-valued function representing a complete-acyclic preference.

The main contributions of this paper consist in (i) providing a sufficient condition for rationalization of the data set by (locally non-satiated) complete-acyclic preferences, (ii) testing this condition over three experimental data sets and (iii) showing that most empirical violations of utility function maximizing behavior are not related to a failure of maximization behavior, but are simply generated by a violation of the transitivity axiom.

Second, we need to evaluate the power of the tests. From a statistical viewpoint, the power of a test between two assumptions is the probability of rejecting the null hypothesis while the alternative is true. In our case, the former is that the consumer behavior satisfies the axiom we test, and the latter that it does not. Unfortunately, since non-parametric tests of GARP and RARP are non probabilistic, their power is unknown.

A consensus in the literature, however, exists concerning the test of GARP: it consists of computing an approximate power of the nonparametric test. Among various methods for approximation of power on non-parametric tests (see  Andreoni & Harbaugh, 2006  for a detailed review), the one by  Bronars (1987)  is most extensively used. Following  Becker (1962) , Bronars computes the approximate power of the test by taking the assumption of randomness of individuals’ choices as an alternative hypothesis.

The rationale of the Bronars’ method is that the randomly generated data expresses, in some sense, the behavior of “erratic” individuals who choose randomly. As a consequence, the behavior of individuals in the initial data should be different from the behavior of the “erratic” individuals. In particular, the number of violations of GARP over the randomly generated data should be higher than the number of violations of GARP over the initial data.

Mattei (2000)  presented results concerning the Bronars Method 2 power index for the test of GARP. Since, in this paper, we use the experimental data sets of  Mattei (2000) , we present (in order to be comparable) the results from the Bronars Method 2 power index. We call this index the  absolute Bronars power index . We also use Bronars’ method 2 in order to calculate the power of the RARP test.

Unfortunately, as noted by  Andreoni and Harbaugh (2006, page 6) ,  “An advantage of Bronars’ approach is that it is both natural and simple. A disadvantage is that the alternative hypothesis is perhaps too naive.”

Moreover, the Bronars methods 1, 2 and 3 can provide very different results. For instance, in their 2002 paper in Econometrica, Andreoni and Miller report a Bronars Method 1 Power Index of 0.78 for their tests of GARP. However, according to  Andreoni and Harbaugh (2006) , using the same data set, the Bronars Method 2 power index and the Bronars Method 3 power index are, respectively, 0.63 and 0.48 for the GARP test. This shows the sensitivity of the power test to alternative hypotheses.

It is beyond the scope of this paper to discuss the correct method of calculating the power of non-parametric tests. We simply want to point out that RARP is a very weak axiom, both over the initial data and over randomly generated data, such that the number of violations of RARP will be mechanically weak. The main limitation of our work is the power of the RARP tests. Indeed, using the Bronars’ method 2, we find, on average, a power of 54%. That is, 54% of the individuals in the random consumption data sets violate RARP. However, this figure must be compared to the percentage of RARP-violating individuals over the data sets (3% on average).
