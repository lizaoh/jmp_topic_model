Customarily, the analysis of variance is  used to answer this question, but that technique has the grave, although none too widely recognized, drawback that evidence  for significant interaction may mean .no more than that the wrong numerical scale was used to represent the data.  Transformations of the data are frequently made, but their purpose is usually either to equalize the observed variances or to  render the distributions approximately normal, not to minimize the size of the interaction. For this reason, it is of  considerable interest to find sets of conditions that are stated entirely in ordinal terms and that are sufficient to insure  the existence of an additive representation with no interaction term. The result of Luce and Tukey gives one set of sufficient  conditions when the two components OL and  are sufficiently finely graded, and Scott (1964) has provided necessary and sufficient conditions when each component is a  finite set. Some important necessary conditions, such as cancelation, were given earlier by Adams and Fagot (1959).  When a set of data are found to violate conditions, such as cancellation, that are necessary for an additive representation to  exist, then we know that an inherent additive interaction exists in the sense that the two independent dimensions of the  stimuli do not contribute independently-in the additive sense-to the response. What we do not know, however, is anything about  the structure of the interaction. Perhaps in some cases it can be characterized in a way that is almost as simple as when  there is no interaction. One such possibility is suggested by the following consideration. Suppose that in Eq. 1 we set H =  e", f = ef', and g = e", then that equation becomes  H(A, P) = f (A)g(P),  and Eq. 2 is unchanged except that the primes must be dropped. Of course, by the nature of the exponential transformations,  the ranges of H, f, and g are subsets of the positive reals. Were this restriction not met, i.e., if either f or g or both,  and therefore H, map into both the positive and negative reals, then the representation given by Eqs. 2 and 3 cannot be  reduced to an additive one; an inherent additive interaction exists. Specifically, for P such that g(P) > 0, suppose that H(A,  P) > H(B, P), then for any Q such that g(Q) < 0, it follows from Eq. 3 that H(A, Q) < H(B, Q), which clearly is an interaction  that is obvious at the ordinal level. In spite of the fact that such a representation is inconsistent with additive  independence of the components, it is true, nonetheless, that in an important sense the contributions of the two components Ol  and  are still independent-multiplicatively independent-and it appears that knowing this could be important.  The purpose of this paper is to provide an axiom system that, on the one hand, is similar to-in fact, a generalization of-that  of Luce and Tukey and, on the other hand, leads to the multiplicative representation just discussed.
