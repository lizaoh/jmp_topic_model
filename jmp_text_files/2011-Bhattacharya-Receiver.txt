In the field of binary diagnostic decisions, we consider the signal and noise populations, where values of an indicator variable from these populations overlap, with the property that signals tend to have larger indicator scores than noises. A threshold on the scale of the indicator variable provides the basis for classification. Signals that are correctly classified are true positives, and noises that are correctly classified are true negatives. The true positive proportion (TPP) expresses the number of true positives as a proportion of the total number of signals and the true negative proportion (TNP) expresses the number of true negatives as a proportion of the total number of noises. The false positive proportion is FPP=1−TNP. Pairs of TPP and FPP values are obtained by allowing the threshold indicator score to vary over the whole range of the indicator variable. An ROC curve is a graphical plot of TPP against FPP ( Pepe, 2003; Zhou, McClish, & Obuchowski, 2002 ). For an indicator variable that provides no discrimination between signals and noises, the ROC curve follows the main diagonal of the plot (also known as the no discrimination line). Any discrimination between signals and noises produces an ROC curve between (0, 0) and (1, 1) that curves towards the top left hand corner of the plot.

There is a vast amount of literature on ROC curves; see references in books such as  Krzanowski and Hand (2009) ,  Pepe (2003) ,  Zhou et al. (2002) , and Internet sources for more current work. An ROC curve is symmetric if it is so with respect to the negative diagonal.  Killeen and Taylor (2004)  described ROC curve symmetry on the basis of orientation-reversing involutions. Some properties of symmetric ROCs are discussed in  Iverson and Bamber (1997, unpublished) .  Johnson and Dudgeon (1993)  have shown that in the binormal (equal variances) case when the ROC curve is symmetric, the two KLDs are also equal. However, not much is known beyond this in relation to symmetric ROC and KLDs. A close connection between ROC and KLD is investigated by  Eguchi and Copas (2006)  in the context of likelihood ratios. In addition, there is an enormous literature on usage of ROC curves together with KLDs across disciplines including medical, weather, linguistic, geology, chemistry and machine learning. Two examples of the versatility of application in medical field are:  Zhang, Tong, Marks, Shan, and Cottrell (2008) , who used an ROC area metric and KLD to evaluate eye fixation prediction, and  Volkau et al. (2006) , who studied quantitative analysis of brain symmetry for normal and pathological brain discrimination using KLD and ROC curves. Because of such huge interest on the topic, it is certainly worth investigating how symmetry of ROC curves is related to that of KLDs.

Note that the ROC −1  curve is the reflection of the ROC curve around the main diagonal. Assuming the ROC curve is invertible, our results indicate how the two KLDs are directly related to the slopes of the ROC and ROC −1  curves. This paper shows that symmetry of an ROC curve implies the symmetry of the KLDs with respect to the ordering of the arguments of the KLD. However the converse is  not  true. Given the large literature on ROC curves that involve KLD, this result is important because when a researcher knows the ROC curve to be symmetric, she then has to calculate only one of the two divergence measures,
