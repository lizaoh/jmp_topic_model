Subsequently, we use these  results to evaluate both qualitatively and numerically some of the more important predictions for random walk models derived  by means of Wald's approximation. It is shown that some of these predictions do not hold exactly, while others do. The most  serious shorthcoming of the approximation is its failure to recover the true behaviour of the random walk model over certain  regions of its parameter space which may well have some psychological significance. ? 1990 Academic Press, Inc.  1. INTRODUCTION  It is generally recognized that a careful examination of a mathematical model requires simultaneous tests of several of its  predictions. A prime example of this is models which simultaneously predict measures of speed and accuracy, and the functional  relation between them. An important subset within this class is random walk models (for distinct reviews see Towsend & Ashby,  1983, or Luce, 1986), which are often regarded as the most flexible and promising approach to the simultaneous prediction of  speed and accuracy.  In contrast to its theoretical significance, virtually no exact results are known for this class of models, because the usual  derivations of predictions employ an approximation due to Wald (1947), the essence of which is to neglect the conditional  excess over the barriers, given the barrier of absorption. This lack has, for example, recently been emphasized by Luce (1986,  p. 326 ff. ), who has also given some instructive theoretical and simulation results. We have just seen that qualitatively the approximate analysis correctly predicts the conditional mean first passage times  to be equal. Numerically, the approximation yields E[N] z (aa)2/2 (e.g., Cox & Miller, 1965, p. 58, Eq. (86)). Of course, the absolute difference can be made arbitrarily large, but the relative error declines continuously in as and it  vanishes as as tends to infinity.  On the whole, this finding is typical, provided the steps are small relative to the barriers-otherwise the approximation  becomes severely wrong. Thus, it is perhaps fair to say that the most serious shortcoming of Wald's approximation is its  failure to recover the true behaviour of the random walk model over a region of its parameter space, which can possibly not be  neglected without detriment. Specifically, a bad fit of a random walk model to the data may either mean that the model is  inadequate, or that the model is actually correct but the subject operates in a region of the parameter space that is not  accessible via Wald's approximation.
