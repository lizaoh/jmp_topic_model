Previous research and theoretical developments now provide for the first measurement of a thought. This article describes a cybernetic basis for the development of a thought. The components of the measured thought are revealed to be influenced by the cybernetic process itself. Then two measured components of the thought are estimated from choice probabilities and by direct physical measurements. The use of direct physical measures allows for entirely new methods for investigating thoughts. The ideas of Prime Thought and Prime Mind are introduced.

As a thoughtworthy example, a university student is presented with two Occupations and asked which provides the greater salary. A thought occurs and a choice is made. Then the student is asked to show on a computer screen how big the difference in salary is by changing a dot into a horizontal line length of a suitable size. How does thought emerge from the processes of the brain? What does choice require? What does the line length depend upon? Can a physical line length be used as a measure of thought?

The Occupations presented to the student create two Vorstellungen, in the words of  Herbart (1824) , that are fundamental elements of mind. These elements possess basic properties that may be extracted to provide representations of the mental element's characteristics. In this case, each extraction for Occupations 1 and 2, are salaries characterized by Poisson variables, S 1  and S 2  ( Link, 1992 ;  McGill, 1964 ;  Rashevsky, 1938 ). The process for using these values to create a thought about which is larger is a cybernetic process ( Wiener, 1946 ;  1962 ), a control process, that guides the mind into the thought. This process ( Link, 2020 ;  1992 ;  1975 ) is mathematized as an accumulation of differences between S 1  and S 2 , within small time intervals, until one or the other of two thought thresholds is reached. Then the thought occurs, and a physical response may occur.

One value, Aθ, measures a thought. The exponentiated value, a feeling related to the measure of thought, must be generated by another process, another cybernetic process generating feeling ( Link 1992 ;  2020 ). These two relations, supported by numerous psychophysical experiments, show the influence of the various components in creating a thought and the associated feeling. A Power Law for thought.

The logarithmic and power components of thought were argued for 160 years as the basis for the measurement of mental magnitude. As  Link (2022) , showed the joint occurrence of these two values in the amount of a mental magnitude unites the opposed parties. Both components occur together.  Fechner (1860)  and  Stevens (1961)  are united.

An individual's thought is based on the experience in which it occurs. The averaging of the thoughts of many may reveal a “thought” characteristic of the many. Thus, determining how to measure the thought of one individual is the beginning of determining the “thought” of the many. Averaging the measures of the thoughts of the many may reveal a “Prime Thought” characteristic of the many.

At present, to measure the thought of a single element the element must be compared against other elements. In  Link's (2022)  example of students judging which Occupation has the greater salary, the 16 Occupations were: Accountant, Bartender, Bus Driver, Chemist, Computer programmer, Dentist, Engineer, Farmer, Flight Attendant, Lawyer, Physician, Police Officer, Psychologist, Secretary, Teacher, and Welder. Four jobs, Architect, Judge, Reporter, and Waiter/Waitress served as practice stimuli. During the experiment, each student compared each of 16 Occupations against all other Occupations once and extended a dot on a computer screen into a line length to show the difference between the two salaries.

Within each cell is either the value 1 indicating that the Occupation at the top of that column was selected as having a larger salary than the comparison in the row, or 0 indicating that the Occupation at the Top of the column was not selected. If the cell in row 2, column 3 has a value of 1 then the cell in row 3, column 2 must have a value of 0 because the Occupation at the top of column 2 was not selected at the time of the original choice. Given the size of the choice matrix, there are 120 unique pairs of two Occupations. On the main diagonal, all values are 0 because there is no difference between an Occupation and itself. As seen in the Sums of choices at the bottom of  Table 1 , an Occupation is often, and sometimes rarely, chosen.

The measure of the thought of choosing an Occupation exists in the context of choices for all Occupations. When the marginal values for these choices are transformed into choice probabilities, as shown in  Table 2 
                       they may then be converted into values of Aθ, the parameters directing the choice, also shown in  Table 2 .

Corresponding to these choices are the line lengths, L, measuring the difference in salary between two presented Occupations. The cells in the salary difference matrix have positive difference values for the chosen Occupation and negative values for the Occupation not chosen. For example, if cell column 3, row 2, of the choice matrix contains a 1, then cell column 3 and row 2 of the difference between salaries is positive, L 2,3 . The corresponding value for the not selected Occupation in cell 3,2 is the negative value, of L 3,2  = - L 2,3 . These line lengths for each student are divided by their standard deviation to provide a comparative measure of L* = line length/Std. The main diagonal contains zeros because there is no difference between two identical elements. The L* values for this student are in column 3 of  Table 2 .

According to the theory of choice, only the values of θ and A determine the probability of choosing one Occupation over another. But what is the probability of choosing a particular Occupation? And how are these probabilities related to the line lengths measuring the distance between salaries?

First, the probability of choosing an Occupation can be estimated by dividing the number of choices within a column by the number of possible choices, 16. For example, the probability of choosing Occupation 1 is 10/16 = 0.625 which results in a value of Aθ equal to the logarithm of (0.625/0.375) = 0.511. This is a value for the thought measured as Aln(λ 1 /λ) where λ 1  is the strength of Occupation 1 and λ is the geometric mean of all the λ i  (i = 1,…,16) for this student. Exponentiating this value of Aθ yields a measure of 1.87. As shown in  Table 2  this value is near this student's average salary magnitude of 1.66. The value 1.87 is a measure of the strength of the mental event in the context of the student's evaluations of all Occupations.

How are these measures of thought for each Occupation related to the line lengths that measure the difference in salary averaged across the Occupations? The line lengths were standardized by dividing each by the standard deviation of this student's 120 line lengths. The mean values for each Occupation are shown in the third column of  Table 2 .

Such high correlations offer the possibility of using line lengths to measure choice rather than using many judgments to determine a probability.

The dramatic result in  Fig. 2  is the nearly perfect, 0.996, correlation between measures that are now shown to depend upon the cybernetic process that guides the brain towards a thought in the mind. Note that the intercept is zero because zero is the theoretical average value for both series. The exponentiated values of Aθ now provide a clear picture into what I call the “prime” mind of the 20 students. There is no such thing as an average mind, but there is the understanding of a mind drawn together by the thoughts of a group. This mind is “prime” and so are the values of salary developed from it.

These salary values are shown in  Fig. 3

When measured this memory produces “prime” memory. And more. Because the relation between Aθ and L* is essentially perfect, the values of L* which are measured, can be used to predict the probabilities of choosing between Occupations having a larger salary. This is an important application of these theoretical ideas to be discussed in a companion paper.

In summary, a theory that extended the ideas of Fechner and Thurstone describes comparative judgments as the result of a cybernetic process. The cybernetic process guided the mind to a result that created a thought. Derivations from the mathematics of the theory yielded a component of the probability of a choice, Aθ, that related the ideas of Fechner and Stevens. The value Aθ is a direct measure of thought, perhaps of immediate memory. Exponentiating Aθ produces a numerical value equal to the ratio of strength for the two comparisons raised to the power of the amount of comparative difference needed for a thought to occur. Both a physical value, L*and a mental value Aθ are related in the results from the 20 student participants. Then the value of Aθ is the prime value and creates a new image of thought in a prime mind. Values of Aθ are essentially identical to physically measured line length produced after the thought. How well might these values in the “prime mind” predict the choices of the individuals?

Possible simplification for the use of this method in sample surveys is to replace the drawn line lengths by a scale of difference numbers ranging from 0 to 10. The intervals may be sufficiently precise to use

0, 1, 2, 3, …, 9, 10 as measures of a mental difference. Applications of the theory to other thoughts are obvious.  Pallett et al. (2009)  reported studies on female facial attractiveness. Paired comparisons of faces combined with a measure of the difference between faces could be accomplished much more quickly, and less expensive using the technique described in this article.
