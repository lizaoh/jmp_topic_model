STEIN  Texas Christian University, Fort Worth, Texas 76116  AND  AMNON RAPOPORT  University of North Carolina, Chapel Hill, North Carolina 27514  Considered in this paper is a discrete-time Markov decision process with unobservable states, which occurs in equipment  checking, medical diagnosis, signal detection, and reaction time studies. The process is characterized by (i) a state So that  at some unknown time t, which is determined probabilistically, turns into an absorbing state Sl ; (ii) observations that  provide fallible information about the true state and which are taken sequentially to determine whether or not the change from  So to St has occurred; and (iii) losses for a false alarm and for delays in detecting the change. A response model is  proposed, prescribing a terminal decision as soon as the posterior probability that Sl obtains reaches or exceeds a fixed  probability boundary. Predictions are derived concerning the probability of false alarm and several conditional distributions  of the number of observations taken.  For many multistage decision tasks (Rapoport & Wallsten, 1972) the data generating process is known or assumed to be  stationary; its characteristics do not change in the course of the information gathering process. The decision maker (DM)  gathers and processes information over time, at some implicit or explicit cost, until some criterion is satisfied, and then  selects one of several mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive hypotheses. When costs are explicit and the objective  criterion is expected loss minimization, the DM's task is to acquire and process fallible and incomplete information in an  attempt to determine the true hypothesis, balancing between the cost of information acquisition and the costs associated with  making incorrect terminal decisions (Edwards, 1965; Rapoport & Burkheimer, 1971).  As frequently happens in real world and laboratory situations, the process that generates the sequentially gathered fallible  observations may not be stationary; rather, it may be subjected to an irrevocable change. The DM either may or may not be  aware of the nonstationarity of the process, and in the former case either may or may not possess some information about the  mechanism generating the time of change. To illustrate such a situation, consider the very simple case of two distinct states  of nature, called So and SI , each of which generates fallible observations. For either state the observations fluctuate in  value from one time period to another. Under these conditions, the DM's task is to  process at each stage the fallible data with the aim of determining which of the two states obtains, or, equivalently, to  detect whether or not the change from So to Sl has already occurred. That is, the DM attempts to distinguish between  within-state perturbations of the observations and the shift in the characteristics of the data generating process brought  about by a change of state. As such, the task of the DM can be called that of the detection of change.  To motivate the subsequent formal presentation and suggest applications, consider the following two examples. The first, which  is due to Pollock (1967), concerns equipment checking. Here, So is identified with normal production, and Sl with the failure  of a piece of production equipment resulting in the production of defective items. It is assumed, as is common in production  and machine maintenance problems, that the probability distribution of the time of change from normal to defective is known.  The fallible observations are measurements on one or more critical parameters of the items produced. The loss structure is  composed of the cost of needlessly shutting down normally operating equipment, on the one hand, and the cost of producing  defective items, on the other.  The second example, which will be examined in more detail below, is of psychophysical experiments in which neither the  occurrence nor the identity of a signal presented to a subject is at issue, but rather its time of occurrence. The task of the  subject in this case is to notice and then respond immediately when the signal intensity is changed (Luce & Green, 1972). In  this example, So is identified with low signal intensity (absence of signal), and Sl with higher signal intensity (presence of  signal), and the observations are the times between successive "neural" pulses, where the duration of each pulse is assumed to  be negligible. It is reasonable to assume that the observations thus defined are independent random variables (Luce & Green,  1972, p. 14). The subject's losses in this case are either implicitly or explicitly built into the experimental environment,  and are composed of the obvious false alarm and delay of detection components.  In discussing decision rules for this class of experiments as well as for some other experimental designs, Luce and Green  noted that one of the most obvious are truncated sequential rules. They did not undertake the study of these rules "primarily  because of the great difficulty one has in obtaining explicit expressions for response time densities" (1972, p. 18). The  decision model proposed in the present paper is an attempt to meet this challenge.  PRELIMINARY CONSIDERATIONS  Since the characteristics of the two-state detection of change process have been stated very generally, they must be specified  in order to develop testable models. In addition, assumptions should be made about (i) the representation of the two states of  nature, So and Sl , (ii) the probability distribution of the trial of change, and (iii) DM's response mechanism. These  assumptions are stated and justified below.  112 STEIN AND RAPOPORT  It is assumed that the observations conditional on either state So or Sl are represented by two overlapping probability  density functions, fu(x) and fi(x), respectively. This assumption is commonly made by signal detectability theory and reaction  time studies, but it is by no means basic to our proposed model. It can be replaced by the assumption that observations are  discrete, or, if they are not, that the DM actually discretizes the stimulus continuum into M nonoverlapping sections. The  latter assumption is compatible with several psychophysical theories that have been advanced, proposing M-state sensory models  with M = 2 (Luce, 1963), M = 3 (Krantz, 1969), through the multithreshold neural-quantum theory (Green & Swets, 1966).  It is mathematically most convenient to assume that the (unknown) stage of change, t, is geometrically distributed if time is  discrete (Pollock, 1967; Shiryaev, 1963), or exponentially distributed if time is continuous (Bather, 1967; Luce & Green,  1970). The assumption of a geometric distribution of arrival time (change from So to Sl) has been made by other models for  describing behavior, for example by Bartholomew (1967) for social mobility models, and by Atkinson, Bower, and Crothers (1965)  for models of concept identification, avoidance conditioning, and paired-associate learning. The geometric and exponential  distributions provide a constant probability of change from So to Sl per unit time, given that the change has not yet  occurred, and hence have the advantage of describing the occurrence of change as being conceptually "random." Consequently,  they might be expected to result in a mathematically tractable treatment of the detection of change process.  With regard to modeling the DM's behavior, a decision must be made about his response mechanism. In modeling response  mechanisms in decision theory (e.g., Luce & Suppes, 1965) and signal detectability theory (e.g., Green & Swets, 1966) a choice  must be made between deterministic and probabilistic models. When applied to the detection of change process, the former  models assume that the DM establishes some criterion, such as the number of observations to be taken or a fixed likelihood  ratio, and on each stage makes his decision strictly on the basis of whether or not the criterion is satisfied. The latter  models assume that the DM's decision at each stage is determined probabilistically. The former and more familiar deterministic  models are attractive because of their simplicity and tractability. The latter models, which have been motivated by the  observation of inconsistencies in choice experiments, typically yield weaker predictions. Recognizing that the selection of a  response model in signal detectability and decision making experiments is to some extent arbitrary, the tactic in the present  paper will be to allow the DM full information about the detection of change process, then to generate a class of  deterministic response models. A FIXED PROBABILITY BOUNDARY MODEL  As a descriptive model the optimal policy has serious drawbacks. To have a viable descriptive model, allowance should be made  for individual differences, which are expected in detection of change experiments, and statistics should be computed from data  and compared to theoretical distributions derived from such a model. Of particular importance are:  (i) The probability of an incorrect Stop decision.  (ii) The probability distribution of the total number of observations.  (iii) The probability distribution of the number of observations taken after the change.  Given that subjects adhere to a fixed probability boundary model, suboptimal decision behavior may result from inaccurate  revision of the probability of change, utilities that are nonlinear in the costs of delay and false alarm, or misperception of  the value of a or characteristics of the density functions fo(x) or fi(x). Each of these violations will affect the values of  the optimal decision boundaries. Hence, a natural way to develop a viable descriptive model is to extend the optimal policy by  maintaining the assumption of a constant probability boundary to which {P.) is to be compared at each stage, but allowing this  boundary to be chosen by the DM without restriction. Such an approach yields a fixed probability boundary response model,  hereafter referred to as model FPB, which assumes that the DM employs the following decision rule at each stage n (after n - 1  observations):  Stop, if Pn_1 > y,  Continue, if P"'_1 < y,  where y (0 < y < 1) is a fixed constant. For convenience we shall assume that y does not depend on n. In fact, for sake of  generality, no assumption is made regarding the relationship of y to the decision losses. Consequently, y may or may not be  equal to y*.  The desired theoretical probability distributions and expected values of the random variables mentioned above may be derived,  given the decision rule prescibed by model FPB. Whereas only the infinite horizon case will be considered, the finite horizon  case with a constant probability boundary involves similar mathematical manipulations. However, the case of a stage-dependent  probability boundary is more complex (hence the assumption that y does not depend on n) and no closed-form solutions are  attainable. By defining  certain stochastic kernels, it is possible to develop recursive equations that determine all the probability distributions of  interest (Stein, 1975). However, in order to numerically compute the results of these equations, it is necessary to discretize  the density functions f,(x) and f,(x) as well as the probability state space [0, 1]. Indeed, the DM himself may achieve such a  discretization in processing information, in effect producing a finite-state Markov chain.  To derive the probability distributions of interest, the approach adopted in the present paper is based directly upon the  theory of finite-state Markov chains. This approach has the advantage of simplifying the involved mathematics as well as the  resulting equations. Additionally, this approach is closely related to matrix methods used in deriving learning process  statistics for absorbing Markov chain models (Bernbach, 1966; Millward, 1969; Polson, 1972). This class also includes reaction time designs with random foreperiods and the  method of free response. The subject's task in such experiments is to notice and report when the signal intensity is changed.  Our purpose in the present section is to show that the FPB model and a reaction time model developed by Luce and Green (1970)  for the tails of several density functions provide virtually identical predictions.  The task employed by Luce & Green (1970) consisted of a simple reaction time experiment with three unusual features. First,  the signals were difficult to detect, as in most detection experiments. Second, the time from the beginning of each trial to  the occurrence of the onset of the signal was exponentially distributed. Third, the signal remained on until a response  occurred. Specifically, each subject was seated in a sound-treated cubicle and wore earphones. A background of continuous  noise was maintained throughout the experiment. Each trial commenced with the onset of a warning light and after a random  delay (foreperiod), which was distributed exponentially with a mean waiting time of about 5 sec, a 1000-Hz sinusoid was turned  on at a zero crossing. Both the light and the signal remained on until the subject responded by pressing a key, at which point  both were extinguished and information feedback was provided. When the subject's response preceded the auditory signal, the  light was extinguished, the signal was suppressed, and feedback was provided.  "The feedback was provided by lighting one of five lights. This indicated whether or not the response preceded the signal and,  if not, how quickly it came after the signal. Payoff  134 STEIN AND RAPOPORT  in points was associated with these five categories as follows" (Luce & Green, 1970, p. 5):  Category <0 0-300 msec 30(1-400 msec 400-500 msec >500 msec  Payoff -c 5 3 1 -c  Although the experiment just described differs in some minor ways from our two-state detection of change process, the  similarities are striking. Thus, the exponential distribution is the continuous-time analog of the geometric, the key pressing  has the same effect as a Stop decision, the response time is not truncated, -c corresponds to the false alarm loss F, and the  delay loss is approximately linear in time. Knowledge of the exponential distribution of time of change can also be assumed  because of the unusually large number of trials for each subject. The experiment of Luce & Green (1970) may be described,  therefore, as a continuous-time two-state detection of change process with sensory input rather than numerical observations.  A reaction-time model proposed by Luce & Green (1970) permitted them to derive expressions for three independent  distributions. The first, fR , is the density of the time until a false alarm, given that the subject's response preceded the  signal. The second, f, , is the density of time until the signal is presented, given that no false alarm was made. The third,  f,_,r , is the density of time from the signal presentation to the response, conditional on the signal preceding the response.  The labels false-alarm density, signalwait density, and reaction-time density, respectively, are quite fitting.  A key prediction of the Luce and Green model is that the tail of each of these three distributions is exponential. Another  prediction is that the exponential tails off, and f, , but not f,-s , have a common parameter. It is noted that fx , fs , and  fn_s are directly analogous to the probability distributions poo(-), to&), and po&), respectively, in our detection of change  process. As shown in the immediately preceding section, these three distributions have asymptotically geometric right tails,  and, as predicted by Luce and Green, poo(-) and to&) possess a common parameter. Since the geometric distribution is the  discrete-time analog of the exponential, we note the correspondence between the predictions of both models.  In discussing the forms of the tails, Luce and Green stated that "Despite some possible departures of the data from the  expected curve, the exponential function provides an excellent first-order description of the tail of the distribution of  times observed in this experiment" (1970, p. 6). And in discussing the predicted equality of the fn and fs tail parameters,  they concluded that some departure from perfect equality exists, especially for one of their two subjects, "but that it does  not seem particularly serious" (1970, p. 7). As such, their results confirm the parameter-free predictions of the FPB model.
