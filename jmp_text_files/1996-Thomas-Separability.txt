Ashby, Townsend, and colleagues have developed an important model for the psychophysical identification task, the general recognition theory (also called, decision bound theory), based on multidimensional signal detection theory, that has been extended to categorization, Garner filtering, similarity judgments, and preference successfully. The virtue of this theory (and its extensions) is the ability to assess the nature of the perceptual representations of stimuli (e.g., their dimensional interactions), within a single framework and across paradigms. This paper adds to this ability by extending the theory, and related multidimensional perceptual models, to the problem of same�different judgments. Three tasks are described for the example of twodimensional stimuli. In two of these tasks only one dimension is relevant (component same�different). In the third, both dimensions are deemedrelevant (standardsame�different).Several theoremsconcerning both response accuracy and response time have been proved relating the property of perceptual separability to certain response time invariances that can be observed in the component same�different tasks. This property, generally speaking, is the constancy of perception of one dimension when another irrelevant dimension changes in the stimulus. Also, under the assumption of within-trial perceptual independence, response probabilities, and response times of the two�component tasks can be meaningfully related to those same measures in the standard same�different paradigm, provided motor time variance is minimal. ] 1996 Academic Press 

Understanding how elementary dimensions of a stimulus are combined during perception and recognition has dominated a large area of research in cognitive psychology (cf. Ashby, 1992). An earlyquestionconcernedwhethervariations in one dimension (say frequency of an auditory tone) affected the perceptual processing of another (e.g., amplitude). Various operational tasks were devised that, together, provided evidence of the separability�integrality (and the related concept of feature sampling dependence) of stimulus dimensions (Posner, 1964; Garner, 1974; Garner 6 Morton, 1969; Townsend, Hu, 6 Ashby, 1980; Wandmacher,

It became clear that some unifying theoretical framework was needed to organize and relate the various operational constructs of dimensional interactions. To this end, Ashby and Townsend (1986; see also Kadlec 6 Townsend 1992a, 1992b) formally introduced a theory of perceptual identification, termed the general recognition theory (GRT), which provides rigorous definitions of perceptual ``independences'' and relates them to observable behavioral measures such as response probabilities. A strength of this framework is the ability to interrelate performance across different cognitive tasks positing a common perceptual representation. For example, the GRT has been refined and extended to various experimental paradigms such as categorization (Ashby 6 Gott, 1988; Maddox 6 Ashby, 1993), similarity judgments (Ashby 6 Perrin, 1988), preference judgments (Perrin, 1992), and Garner filtering (Ashby 6 Maddox, 1994, Maddox 6 Ashby, 1996). After presenting the basic characteristics of the theory, I will motivate and subsequently outline in several theorems, the current extension of GRT to the same�different judgment task including a more general treatment of multidimensional models assuming the same perceptual representation. These theorems relate underlying concepts of separability and independence to observable invariances of response time and accuracy which can be used, in conjunction with other tasks, to provide converging evidence of these constructs. 

The General Recognition Theory 

The general recognition theory (GRT), when applied to identification, can be viewed as an extension of signal detection theory (Green 6 Swets, 1966) to the multidimensional situation. Like signal detection theory, the GRT divides the identification task into two stages, one for the perception process and one for the decision process. On any trial, when a stimulus is presented to the observer, the output of the perception process, a perceptual effect, can be represented as a point in a multidimensional space. As previously noted, the value of multidimensional models of perception such as the GRT is the ability to interrelate the theoretical constructs and the observable properties (for some problems, though, see Thomas, 1995a). For example, Ashby and Townsend (1986) related perceptual independence and decisional separability to an observable property of the data, sampling independence (see Theorem 1, p. 160). In the context of Gaussian distributions, Kadlec and Townsend (1992a, 1992b) have added several more theorems relating the observable signal detection quantities of d$ and ; to the constructs of independence and separability. More recently Ashby and Maddox (1991, 

1994; Maddox 6 Ashby, 1996) have incorporated response time predictions in the GRT in their analysis of the classic Garner speeded classification paradigm. To accomplish this, they introduced an RT-distance hypothesis (for evidence supporting this hypothesis, see Ashby 6 Maddox, 1991). Simply stated, this is the assumption that the decision time to identify a stimulus is inversely related to the Euclidean distance from the percept of the stimulus to the nearest decision bound. With this hypothesis and the framework of the GRT, Ashby and Maddox (1991) provide theory-based justification for the use of the Garner filtering paradigm to diagnose perceptual and decisional separability. They also point out problems in interpreting results from redundancy gain tasks. When the experimental context dictates that optimal responding violates decisional separability, as in the redundant dimensions task, result normally attributed to perceptual integrality are predicted even if perceptual separability and independence hold for the stimulus components.[1]

� Motivation for Same Different Judgments 

In the next section I will describe theoretical results of applying the multidimensional framework of the GRT to understand perceptual interactions within the same� different task. It may not be clear why this would be necessary if the identification task provides the insights into perceptual processing of stimulus dimensions. In addition, at first glance, it appears that the same�different task is more perceptually complex, although there is neuropsychological evidence to suggest it may be easier perceptually than identification (Farah, Monheit, 6 Wallace, 1991). However, several reasons motivate its use in the present context. First, the identification task places a burden on the observer to learn unique labels for each stimulus, sometimes making it difficult to separate response learning from perceptual learning and�or requiring an extended amount of practice. The same�different task eliminates the need for learning unique stimulus labels by providing the same response choice options for all trials. Also, in some researches, special circumstances associated withstimulusnamesandtheir relationship(i.e., compatibility) to the stimuli renders the naming function a substantive issue in its own right. For example, in face recognition research, there is evidence to suggest that learning of names for faces is such an important component in facial recognition that the naming process has been given a special module in many face recognition theories (Bruce, 1988; Valentine, Bredart, Lawson, 6 Ward, 1991). Consequently, the same� different task has been the paradigm of choice to study perceptual interactions within this literature (e.g., Sergent, 1984). For a nonfacial example of the influence of labels on perception, see Townsend, Hu, and Evans (1984). 

A second reason, alluded to earlier, is the application of a common theoretical framework to multiple paradigms yielding a convergence of operations. As Ashby and Lee (1993) observed, 

Models of perception are frequently short-lived.... One reason for their transience is that, in many cases, the models were designed specifically to account for the data collected from one particular experimental paradigm. Such models do not generalize well; that is, if some, often very minor, aspect of the experiment is changed then the performance of the model collapses (pp. 369�370). 

Suchis the state of affairsin themodeling ofthe same�different task (for a review see Farell, 1985). Many models exist solely to predict paradoxes that are sometimes observed within this task (e.g., the fast�same effect, Krueger, 1978; Proctor, 1981; but see Ratcliff 6 Hacker, 1981). It would be difficult to envision some of these models to simultaneously account for performance in similarity judgments or 

categorization as there is little structure within them to describe the perceptual representation. 

With the exception of Ennis and his colleagues (Ennis 6 Mullen, 1986; Ennis, Palen, 6 Mullen, 1988; Ennis 6 Ashby, 1993) and Takane and Sergent (1983; Sergent 6 Takane, 1987), to my knowledge, no other theoretical framework for this task is rich enough to predict a priori the similarity relationships and�or psychological dimensional representation characteristics of a stimulus set. Here, I make a distinction between representation-based models of cognitive processing and architecture-based models of cognitive processing. Characteristics of both are only coming together now (Ashby 6 Maddox, 1994; Ashby 6 Schwarz, 1996; Lambert, 1995; Nosofsky 6 Palmeri, 1996). The former class of theories explicitly tries to account for the perceived structure of the stimulus set. From that representation, similarity relations among stimuli can be derived and response probabilities predicted. An example, again, is the multidimensional stochastic theory of similarity successfully applied to accuracy data obtained from the same�different judgments task by Ennis and his colleagues. This modeling approach can be viewed as an instance of the distance-based class of models described below. However, that theory did not make response time predictions (but the class will be extended below to accomplish this). Takane and Sergent (1983) also describe a modeling framework that has been successfully applied to same�different response probabilities, based on an underlying similarity) that also capture qualitative response time results that are quite consistent with the distance-based class. 

The architecturally based models, on the other hand, have been primarily concerned with real-time processing characteristics occurring during cognition as a system. They involve concepts such as serial versus parallel arrangement of subsystems, flow properties of information through a network, diffusion processes accumulating evidence toward a criterion, etc. If consideration of the nature of that information (e.g., structure of the stimulus set in terms of psychological dimensions) is given it is usually very descriptive, often representing similarity as a free parameter (e.g., Ratcliff, 1978) rather than providing an underlying psychological representation from which to generate the similarity relations. Much of the modeling of the same� different task has been within the architectural tradition (Krueger, 1978; Link 6 Heath, 1975; Ratcliff, 1981; Proctor, 1981). 

The approach taken here will be to exploit the recently adopted RT-distance hypothesis within the framework of theGRT, addingsomeimportantarchitecturalconsiderations to the decision process, as a first step in an investigation of the same�different paradigm as concerns the issues of separability and independence. This paper will work toward putting on some rigorous foundation the use of the same�different task for identifying separable dimensions in

the Garner and Ashby and Townsend traditions. It will also serve to outline in quantitative detail the assumptions and constructs necessary to accomplish this. As a result, possible mechanisms responsible for failures to obtain evidence supporting independence or separability, particularly in situations where one would expect separability, will be illuminated. 

One early study which had attempted to validate the use of a same�different task as a means for revealing separability is that of Santee and Egeth (1980).[2] However, they apparently failed to find converging evidence of separability of stimulus dimensions in data from same� different judgments along a relevant dimension (which I will later refer to as component same�different judgments) when separability seemingly held within standard speeded classification. This result leads to the authors suggesting that the same�different task is somehow special. There are aspects of their data which implicate a possible alternative interpretation of their main result, which, after controlling for these aspects, has not been replicated (Thomas, 1996). This study and related issues will be revisited in the discussion section. It is eventually of interest to apply the GRT framework to much of the seemingly paradoxical phenomena associated with the same�different task (Farell, 1985) with an eye toward classifying extent models within the framework; this is the goal of a long research program, beyond the scope of the present paper. 

� Theoretical Development of Same Different Extension 

Below, various propositions are presented that interrelate the above defined constructs of independenceand separability to observable response probabilities and response times that can be obtained from different same�different judgment tasks. Because the task demands are different for same� different comparisons than those for the identification task, specific decision strategies need to be considered. There are two broad classes of decision processes that are investigated here. More are possible and a full taxonomy of multidimensional same�different judgment models is in development, but the two of interest here are chosen because there is evidence for them in the unidimensional signal detection literature (MacMillan 6 Creelman, 1991), and they are the two most process-oriented within the representation-based scheme. 

First, the observer could divide the perceptual space into response regions, as in identification, and then respond ``same'' if the percepts associated with the presented stimuli fall are identified with the same response label. That is, two independent ``mini-identifications'' take place. A model

making this type of assumption for its decision process will be referred to as a subclassification model. This subclassification strategy could be guided by a decision bound-GRT approach to the identification task or involve evaluating pairwise similarities which are then put through a Shepard� Luce choice rule (Shepard, 1957; Luce, 1963) to determine the individual identification responses (as in Ennis 6 Ashby, 1993). Theorems will center on the GRT-based approach for the reason that this model can give trial-bytrial response predictions; for the latter model only aggregate response probabilities are available. 

For the second class, the observer could assess some function of the distance between the percepts associated with the presented stimuli and respond ``same'' if this distance result is less than some criterion and ``different'' if it is greater. Models of this type will be called distance-based models. As stated earlier, a specific version of a distancebased model is found in the work of Ennis (e.g., Ennis 6 Ashby 1993) in which the perceptual distributions were assumed to be multivariate normal and the distance function was a Minkowski metric. There, it was referred to as a multidimensional decision bound model for same�different judgments. Models based on similarity comparisons to a criterion are logically equivalent if similarity is assumed to be a monotonic function of distance between percepts in the psychological space (as in Takane 6 Sergent, 1983). Technically, not everyone may consider the distance-based models to be legitimate extensions of the GRT in the sense that in the GRT the perceptual space is divided into response regions via decision bounds, etc. However, I include this class as an important one which is easily integrated into the framework for understanding perceptual interactions by use of a multidimensional representation. 

A subclassification model may make sense for an experiment with only a very few stimuli, an easily nameable stimulus set (Pachella, 6 Miller, 1976), or a long interstimulus interval. On the other hand, if the stimulus set is large, or simultaneous presentation of the stimuli occurs, it might require less resources on the part of the observer to use a distance-based strategy. These two types of decision processes have received attention in the unidimensional signal detection theory literature, where subclassification models is a class known as decision combination (threshold ) models, as the observation and decision about each stimulus is made independently of the other and the end response is a conjunction of the individual identifications. The distance-based judgment process has been referred to as a differencing strategy (Sorkin, 1962; Green 6 Swets, 1966; Macmillan 6 Creelman, 1991; Noreen, 1981). Empirical justification for these classes has been found in ``fixed'' versus ``roving'' standard signal detection experiments (Macmillan 6 Creelman, 1991); the former leading to subclassification type strategies and the latter being better described by a distance-based process. It should be noted

that these two model classes do not exhaust the possible decision strategies. A third class exists, based on likelihoods which contains the optimal model. The decision rule for the unidimensional (optimal) case has been derived by Noreen (1981) and Dai, Versfeld, and Green (1996). The complexity of the optimal decision rule in more general multidimensional cases makes it very difficult to obtain analytic predictions. The propositions presented below relate different types of same�different judgment tasks under various independence and separability assumptions. The standard definitions of perceptual separability and perceptual independence will be used for both types of decision models as the perceptual representation of the stimuli is modeled the same way in each as in standard GRT. Also, the previous definition of decisional separability will be used for the subclassification model. However, because, in the distance-based models, 

there are no decision criteria in the perceptual space, new formulations of decisional separability and the response time hypothesis need to be introduced. Decisional separability for the distance-based model is clarified now. 

Decision separability in the distance-based model for component A (B) holds if and only if the decision to respond ``same'' on component A (B) is not affected by the perceived level and decision for the other component. That is, the differencing is taken only with respect to the perceptual dimension associated with component A (B). Thus, when responding on the basis of both dimensions, the decision to respond ``same'' (on both dimensions), in the presence of decisional separability, is the conjunction of the events of deciding to respond ``same'' on A and deciding to respond ``same'' on B each of which do not depend oh the perceived level of the other component. Some of the propositions concern response times. Recall that, in the subclassification model, there are two separate identification processes involved. The previous notion of RT-distance is adopted for the individual identification processes. However, the overall time to decide whether the stimuli are the same or not is also a function of the processing architecture applied to the separate identification processes. There are a variety of possibilities; but, for now, only some simple arrangements are considered. For this, the concepts of parallel location, serial location, and one location are needed. When a pair of stimuli are presented on a trial, the observer, using a subclassification strategy, is faced with locating the percept of one stimulus (i.e., identifying it) and also locating the percept of the other stimulus. These ``location processes'' could occur in parallel or serial. In the case of sequential presentation of the stimuli, the observer may have already classified the first stimulus by the time the second stimulus is presented and RT measurement usually begins. This case is termed ``one location.'' The different location strategies allowed and the overall decision time predictions for the subclassification model are diagramed in Fig. 2. When only one component is relevant to the decision and decisional separability holds, there is only one decision criterion in the perceptual space for the individual identification processes and it is orthogonal to the axis of the attended dimension. 

Recall that when both dimensions are deemed relevant to the task of judging sameness, if the observer is instead employing a distance-based strategy, then he or she must decide ``same'' or ``different'' on each component (assuming decisional separability). These two decision processes (one for each component) can occur either in serial or in parallel. In general, the following RT-distance hypothesis is adopted for the distance-based model. Now, the decision time is assumed to be a monotonic decreasing function of the difference between this distance, dij , and the criterion, $, in this evidence or decision space. Under decisional separability, separate decisions for each of the two components occurs. In this case, the overall response time to decide if the stimuli are the same or

Each of these component decision times are governed by the RT-distance hypothesis just described. Incidently, when only one component is relevant for the decision and decisional separability holds, only one decision subsystem contributes to the processing time. Finally, the decision criteria do not change across experimental conditions (Propositions 3 and 4), and the decision criteria for the subclassification models do not change across presentation intervals. The latter part of this assumption may not be absolutely necessary for the qualitative aspects of the theorems to hold. It is made for computational simplicity. 

Independence of observations (assumption 2) has always been made when unidimensional signal detection theorists have studied the same�different task until recently (Dai et al., 1996). All other formal multidimensional models have made this assumption (e.g., Ennis et al., 1988; Takane 6 Sergent, 1983), even if not explicitly stated. To reiterate, this assumption requires that the random vector representing the second stimulus is stochastically independent from the random vector representing the first. It is not equivalent to perceptual independence of dimensions within a stimulus which requires the first component of a random vector representing a stimulus to be stochastically independent of the second component of that same random vector. To relax 

this assumption is very interesting and leads to very complex analytic expressions even in the unidimensional case (Dai et al., 1996). Future work will explore such an extension to the multidimensional case. 

The first part of assumption 5 warrants further consideration. Specifically, it states that the decision criteria utilized in the component same�differentjudgmenttasks, conditions1 and 2, are consistent with those employed in a standard same�different task requiring processing of both dimensions (i.e., condition 3). SUMMARY AND DISCUSSION 

In this paper, I have discussed the application of probabilistic multidimensional models of the same�different task to the problem of assessing separability and independence of processing using response accuracy and response time measures. Table 2 summarizes the major theoretical findings. 

To review, all of the theorems presented assume that the appropriate version of decisional separability for both components holds. In addition, for the response time results, the issue of processing architecture had to be considered combined with an appropriate hypothesis relating processing time to distance. Recall that for the subclassification models, it is assumed that the observer independently classified each presented stimulus individually and then determined whether the classifications were the same or not. The classification processes, then, could occur either in series or in parallel. It could also be the case, in particular for sequentially presented stimuli, that the measured response time included only the classification time of the second presented stimulus. Thus, the overall decision time to judge sameness was the function of the individual classification times: sum for serial processing, maximum for parallel, or then only the one classification time. For the distance based models, in the standard same�different condition when both components need to be processed (e.g., for the ``same'' trials), the component decisions could occur in series or in parallel. Hence, the overall decision time would then be the corresponding function of the component decision times, either the sum of the two or the maximum, respectively. 

As seen in the middle column of Table 2, when perceptual separability is satisfied, then in the component same� different conditions, the probability of a ``same'' response on the relevant component does not depend on the physical level of the irrelevant component. In terms of response time, if perceptual separability holds, then the entire response 

time distribution associated with judgments of sameness on one component is invariant across levels of the other component. Thus, this task, under the hypothesis of decisional separability, forms a good test for perceptual separability. 

In the last column are the results involving only the perceptual independence of the two components. Here, the response probabilities and response time distributions for a given pair of stimuli in the component same�different task could be compared to the corresponding response probability or response time distribution in the standard same�different task to evaluate the presence of perceptual independence. It should be reiterated that, in order for these independence theorems to hold, it must be assumed that the perceptual representations and decision criteria are the same across the different experimental conditions. The empirical implications of this assumption were noted in the discussion prior to the statements of the theorems. In particular, the different stimulus pairing probabilities need to be held constant across tasks or an optimizing observer would be obliged to alter his or her decision criteria accordingly Also, for the response time result to hold exactly, there should be no variability in the base time (e.g., motor time etc.). While this requirement is certainly false, if, through practice or care in collecting responses, the variability of residual processes approaches zero, then this result will be approximately satisfied. 

Some overall impressionsof these analyses can be observed. First, the generality of these theorems is quite nice. The results hold regardless of the major decision strategy the observer employs in making his or her judgments, subclassification, or distance-based (assuming decisional separability). Most the implications are true regardless of architecture as well. The one failure to relate response time performance r across paradigms, in a specific way, occurs when the processing architecture involves serial decisions and yet perceptual independence is satisfied. In the very few cases the same�different task has been used to address the issue of separability, empirical results have not been as unambiguous as those from other paradigms such as speeded classification (Santee 6 Egeth, 1980; Overmyer 6 Simon, 1985). As mentioned in the introduction, Santee and Egeth (1980) were apparently first to explore the validity of the component same�different task as a means of identifying separability by way of a direct comparison to results from speeded classification. Their work was a straightforward application of the filtering test taken from the Garner speeded classification paradigm (Garner, 1974; Ashby 6 Maddox, 1994). Suppose the stimuli are two dimensional with the relevant dimension taking two values and the irrelevant dimension taking three values (to be consistent with Santee and Egeth). The task includes three control conditions in which the irrelevant dimension is held constant and an orthogonal condition in which the irrelevant dimension values are combined factorially with the values of the relevant dimension. The filtering test for separability then compares the average orthogonal response time to the average of the control mean response times. If the former is larger than the latter, interference is said to occur and the dimensions are deemed integral. No specific predictions about response time distributions or of pairs of stimuli are made. With respect to speeded classification, the Garner approach entails a number of unstated assumptions which are outlined in Ashby and Maddox (1994). In Santee and Egeth's study, both a speeded classification task and a component same�different task employing the control and orthogonal condition comparison were conducted using, in one case, stimuli composed of two forms (circle versus square) and three sizes, and in the other, two forms (again circle and square) and three shadings. With form relevant, they found no interference in the speeded classification task but they did find interference in the component same�different task (i.e., same�different judgments on form). A closer inspection of their results indicated that separability in the GRT sense did not hold in either task (particularly in the component same�different task) in that the time to respond to the form dimension differed greatly across the three sizes (and also shading, but to a lesser degree) in the control conditions alone in both tasks, indicating a potential mean-shift integrality of the type discussed by Maddox (1992) which can be shown to produce an orthogonal interference. Second, in the component same�different task, not all pairs of stimuli were equally likely due to an attempt to equate the number of stimulus�response alternatives there to the 

number used in the speeded classification task. This unequal distribution of stimulus pair probabilities could lead to a violation of assumption 5 above and, thus, an optimizing observer would likely not respond using decisional separability. Recently, Thomas (1996) has repeated the basic experiment using the dimensions of circle size and line orientation��dimensions that have a consensus of separability in the literature��correcting for the stimulus pair probability problem. First, there was no difference in the mean response time across the control conditions, as would be expected for separability in the GRT sense. The critical result was that no difference between the control conditions and the orthogonal condition was found. In fact, the orthogonal block was 1 ms faster than one of the control conditions and the largest difference among all conditions was only 9 ms. 

One aspect of the Santee and Egeth data that has been replicated in part is the finding that within the orthogonal condition, response times for ``same'' judgments tended to be slowed when the irrelevant dimension did not match across stimuli and ``different'' judgments were speeded when the irrelevant dimension also mismatched. This effect was somewhat contingent on the interstimulus interval within a trial (which may be an important factor in the choice of decision strategy on the part of the observer). Overmyer and Simon (1985), apparently unaware of Santee and Egeth's study, essentially replicated the latter's orthogonal condition using component same�different judgments with form and color as the stimulus dimensions. They found ``different'' responses to be quicker when the irrelevant dimension also differed. ``Same'' judgments were only marginally affected by congruency of the unattended dimension but tended toward the kind of interference suggested by Santee and Egeth. In a detailed study of two observers, in a lower accuracy, signal detection situation, Thomas (1996) found some slight evidence for similar redundancy gains for component ``different'' judgments for the circle�line stimuli, an effect appearing at the level of the response time distribution for specific stimulus pairs for an individual observer. However, results for component ``same'' judgments were mostly consistent with separability. 

An auxiliary goal of the present work was to underscore the nature of the various assumptions necessary for separability and�or independence to be confirmed within the same�different paradigm. In too many cases when an experimental paradigm is employed to reveal cognitive processes, a thorough understanding of the underlying theory is not available. Consequently, empirical findings can be attributed to causes prematurely (Ashby 6 Townsend, 1986; Ashby 6 Maddox, 1994; McKoon, Ratcliff, 6 Dell, 1986). On the other hand, when a full theoretical treatment of a task is undertaken, the realization that several, if not many, assumptions are needed for its correct application can lead one to become sceptical about ever being able to

apply the task fruitfully. Especially troublesome is the observation that when initial theorems relating unobservable constructs to observable behaviors are developed, it is not clear how to interpret data that are inconsistent with those theorems. For example, in the present context, if the response time distribution for indicating sameness on one dimension is not invariant across levels of the irrelevant dimension, what does one conclude? Was the cause a failure in perceptual separability or decisional separability? Or was the decision process not subclassification or distance-based? Could it be that the observations for the two presented stimuli are not independent? These are questions that are important and whose answers will hopefully be forthcoming as more research into these processes is undertaken. No doubt, some notion of converging evidence will play a large role. In any case, before any research issue can be meaningfully addressed, an outline of the various theoretical constructs that are involved is a must. 

One important assumption used to prove the theorems whose failure may be responsible for some of the ambiguity of findings from component same�different judgments is that of decisional separability. This assumption is almost always (necessarily) made in the various tests for perceptual separability and independence that have been proposed within the identification task (e.g., Ashby 6 Townsend, 1986; Kadlec 6 Townsend, 1992a, 1992b; Maddox 6 Ashby, 1994). In many empirical applications using the identification task, response collection strategies have been used to help ensure that a decisionally separable process is employed. For example, when identifying circle�line stimuli varying along circle size and line orientation, observers are often required to input a value for size and another value for orientation, thus experimentally separating responses to the two dimensions (Ashby 6 Lee, 1991). In speededclassification and component same�different judgments, the instructions to attend to one relevant dimension may be interpreted as a plea for decisional separability. Maddox (1992) has investigated some of the consequences of violations of decisional separability within the task of speeded classification (as well as consequences of perceptual integrality). In component same�different judgments, because there are two stimuli present simultaneously, or nearly simultaneously, even if the dimensions are perceptually separable (in the GRT sense), an observer may be inclined to decisionally integrate them especially if such a strategy improves accuracy. Some speculation as to the consequences of this in the present context may be made. 

When decisions are generated by subclassifications and the stimulus presentation is sequential, the response time may consist of only the identification process for the second stimulus. Hence, the Maddox results can be applied in a straightforward way to this one-location case. For the distance-base model, using the Euclidean metric along both dimensions will produce a redundancy gain for component 

different judgments when both dimensions differ when compared to trials when only one dimension differs. This same situation will lead to an slower response times for component same judgments when the irrelevant dimension differs compared to same judgments when both dimensions match, thus, leading to the convergence of results in the data of the kind reviewed above. It should be noted that the effect of congruency of the irrelevant dimension on response time was greatly reduced when the interstimulus interval (ISI) was longer (Santee 6 Egeth, 1980). It might be that for shorter ISIs a nondecisionally separable distance-based strategy is employed. With longer ISIs, the observer has time to classify the first stimulus, thus making it easier to separately attend to the relevant component. Future work is planned that will investigate in more detail the empirical differences between the decision strategies and the consequences of failures of both perceptual and decisional separability. 

In what appears to be a direct test of the decisional separability within standard same�different judgments, Miller (1978) argued that the time to respond ``different'' when both dimensions differed could not have come from independent decisions on both dimensions. He used the cumulative RT distribution function for the two-dimensional different trials and the distribution functions from each of the single dimension different trials to estimate the (unobservable) joint probability of the single dimension difference detectors detecting a difference by time t, assuming independent decisions. He found this joint probability to be negative or zero, indicating an inconsistency with the independence hypothesis. The equation he used, relating the various probabilities actually arises from an unlimited capacity parallel decision process with independent decisions (Colonius 6 Vorberg, 1994) and can be derived from the distance-based decision process. He acknowledges that his data could be consistent with a serial comparison process (as in the distance-based model) which assumes decisional separability. Thus, the issue of decisional separability, as defined here, and architecture (serial vs parallel) needs to be treated separately within a given model. 

Because of the rich conceptual structure of multidimensional models, including the GRT, many open problems within the same�different paradigm offer exciting opportunities for the application of the framework. Taken together, all of the paradigm extensions may be a tentative answer to Ashby and Lee's criticism of the short lives of perceptual theories.
