JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 9, 389-403 (1972)  A Statistical Method for Investigating the Perceptual Confusions among Geometric Configurations'  LAWRENCE HUBERT  University of Wisconsin  The concept of a binary communications channel suggests a way of investigating statistically some of the encoding habits a  person may utilize in recognizing patterns. As a way of illustrating this procedure in more detail, sixty-four geometric forms  were first constructed by connecting the different vertices of a square with line segments. Since all of these patterns have  an obvious representation as six-tuples of zeros and ones, they may be interpreted formally as potential messages to be  transmitted to a person. A confusion matrix between the configurations was then obtained by tachistoscopically exposing each  of the patterns to a subject and requiring its complete written reproduction for a response. By relaxing the usual assumption  of independent errors in the reproduction of the individual components of a pattern, the confusion matrix was theoretically  reconstructed in various ways and several reasonable sources of statistical dependency among the confusion errors were  identified.  Although a major portion of the nonphysiological research in pattern recognition has been based upon either information theory  (Garner, 1962) or methodologies associated in some way with computer applications (Minsky and Papert, 1969), both of these  approaches generally fail to meet the standards now expected of formal psychological models. In spite of the fact that the  techniques used in information theory may be psychologically justifiable, they still do not provide the same type of detailed  quantitative predictions common to Stimulus Sampling Theory. Conversely, the work done by most of the computer-oriented  theorists is sufficiently precise but usually concerned with obtaining efficient recognition algorithms rather than those that  are psychologically defensible as well. There is one area of information theory, however, that appears to have some value in  suggesting more adequate formalizations. In fact, several of the obvious communalities between the visual perceptual processes  and the formal representation of geometric patterns introduced below have already been exploited (Bower, 1967), although not  to any serious degree.  Stated rather broadly, the intent of the present paper is to develop a general procedure for investigating geometric pattern  recognition by elaborating on a few elementary ideas utilized extensively in coding theory. BACKGROUND  The statistical procedure presented below provides a technique for theoretically reconstructing a confusion matrix between  certain geometric patterns. At the beginning, however, the simplest way of explaining the general approach is by concentrating  on one specific case that was investigated experimentally. After this example is discussed in some detail, several suggestions  will be given for a future generalization. Even though a binary notation for these particular geometric forms is rather elementary, it does suggest a very powerful way  of investigating aspects of the visual encoding and decoding process. EXPERIMENTAL EVALUATION  A complete report of the experiment discussed in this section is available in Hubert (1971). The interested reader who wishes  more detailed information regarding the data and the experimental procedure should consult that source.  Subjects  One-hundred fourth, fifth, and sixth grade students from a predominantly black elementary school participated in the  experiment. The complete confusion matrix between the sixty-four geometric forms was constructed by pooling the responses for  all subjects.  Apparatus  Each geometric configuration was exposed briefly to the subjects by means of a Sawyers slide projector that had been fitted  with an Illex shutter. The shutter was mechanically controlled by a cable release and was operated manually by the  experimenter. In addition to this first projector, a second one was used to provide a light field of exactly the same size as  that produced by a slide containing a configuration. This continuous light field was intended as a device for focusing the  subject's attention as well as a mask for curtailing the subject's after-processing of the pattern. The projection screen,  placed about nine feet away, was of a radiant variety suitable for use in a lighted room. The size of the image itself was  approximately a twenty-eight inch square with the centered geometric configuration requiring an eleven inch subsquare. The  response material used by the subject consisted of sets of four points that formed the vertices of a square; after a slide was  presented, the subject reproduced his perception of it merely by connecting the appropriate vertices.  GEOMETRIC CONFIGURATIONS 397  Procedure  Subjects were tested in pairs and were seated at the two ends of a long desk. The projectors were placed on a small stand  immediately back of and between the subjects approximately twelve feet from the screen. At the beginning of an experimental  session, which lasted about twenty-five minutes, the subjects were given instructions on how to answer and individual help  whenever they had difficulty in responding to the practice exposures. This initial period also served as an orientation to the  speed at which the actual test stimuli were to be shown. After the task was made clear, each of the sixty-four configurations  was presented once, with a short period after every exposure for the subject to respond. The presentation of each slide was at  3/32 maximum illumination with a uniform duration of 11150 second. These settings produced an average error rate of about  sixty per cent. Each presentation of a slide was preceeded by the experimenter saying the word "ready," at which time the  subjects were instructed to attend to the middle of the continuous light field.  Explicit details regarding the sequence of instructions, random selection of practice stimuli, randomization in the order of  presentation, response material, subject characteristics, and so forth are available in Hubert (1971). POSSIBLE GENERALIZATIONS AND EXTENSIONS  There are at least three additional topics that are both relevant to the previous statistical technique and important enough  in this context to deserve explicit mention: first, the seemingly large number of required parameters as the partitions include  GEOMETRIC CONFIGURATIONS 401  larger subsets; secondly, the investigation of encoding and decoding habits that cannot be represented by any simple  statistical dependency among the response variables; and thirdly, an extension of the procedure for use with more complex  geometric patterns.  With regard to the number of parameters, there is not much of a theoretical problem at all. For example, even though the  decomposition {{Y,(s),..., Y4(s)J, {Y5(s), Y,(s)}} requires a maximum of 252 parameters for a complete reconstruction of the  confusion matrix, this number is not excessively large considering the degrees of freedom that still remain after estimation.  It is still possible, however, to reduce this number of parameters substantially by a very natural reparameterization of the  transition matrices for the two subsets {Y,(s),..., Y,(s)J and {Y,(s), Y,(s)). This particular reparameterization merely  requires the one simple assumption that no distinction can be made between the two diagonals in the transition table for the  subset { Y,(s), Y,(s)J or between the four sides in the transition matrix for the subset {Y,(s),..., Y4(s)). By placing this  restriction on the response variables, certain probabilities within these two transition matrices will be equal, for example,  P{Y5(s) = 0, Y6(s) = 1 I Xa(s) = 0, X6(s) = 1}  = I'{Y5(s) = 11 Y6(s) = 0 I X5(s) = 1, X6(s) = 0}.  Besides being theoretically reasonable, it turned out that when such a reduction was made and the 37 parameters needed for the  partition were estimated by maximumlikelihood, the various goodness-of-fit statistics were not altered to any substantial  degree.  One of the most useful functions of the statistical procedure discussed in the previous section may be in providing a baseline  for evaluating the saliency of certain properties of geometric forms. Suppose the complete confusion matrix is first  reconstructed by using the partition {{Y,(s),..., Y4(s)), {Y5(s), Y,(s)}}. Given this reconstructed matrix, it is now possible  to add the expected frequencies within the table in such a way that an expected confusion matrix is obtained between any two  classes of configurations formed by the presence or absence of some particular property. Similarly, the actual confusion  matrix may be collapsed to obtain the observed confusion matrix for the same property. Finally, the most ill-defined difficulty of all is in extending this particular technique to the investigation of confusions  among more general patterns. One natural extension would be to define a fixed set of patterns from a matrix of points and  proceed in the same manner as before. This program could probably be carried out to some degree, but the combinatorial  problems would become insurmountable almost immediately. An alternative technique that seems to be more realistic requires a  preliminary identification of position invariant distinctive features for geometric forms similar to Gibson's characterization  of the capital letters (Gibson, 1969). It is clear, however, that further work has to be done before any adequate appraisal  can be made of the value of this approach. Some formal work has been attempted in this direction already that may be of help  (Grenander, 1969), but its scope is far from what eventually will be necessary.
