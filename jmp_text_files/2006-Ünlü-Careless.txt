In medicine, to date, research on evaluating diagnostic tests has provided an extensive library of probabilistic models with corresponding frequentist and Bayesian inference methodologies. For reviews in this field, covering relevant publications for this paper, see  Enøe et al. (2000) ,  Hui and Zhou (1998) , and  Walter and Irwig (1988) . Sensitivity  and  specificity  of a test are defined as the conditional probabilities that a truly diseased, respectively, not diseased individual is correctly diagnosed as diseased, respectively, not diseased by the test. The binary data obtained by testing each sample unit by any of the tests are subject to misclassification errors in general. These are errors of the type  false negative  (truly diseased, but negative test result) and  false positive  (truly not diseased, but positive test result). Ignoring such errors by simply using classical estimators based on observed proportions results in substantial biases in general (see  Bross, 1954; Staquet et al., 1981; Valenstein, 1990 ). For instance, the estimation of disease prevalence by the sample proportion-positive on a diagnostic test (erroneously treated as (error-free) gold standard) does not give an estimator which is (asymptotically) unbiased in general. In absolute value, the (asymptotic) bias of such an estimator can be substantial.

We focus on the estimation of careless error and lucky guess probabilities in KST. The method discussed in this paper is free of assumptions concerning an ‘intricate’ fixed knowledge structure. The simplest knowledge structure consisting of the empty set and the whole set is assumed (cf. 1); for remarks on this choice, see  Section 1.4 . The method is free of other constraints such as the concept of wellgradedness (see  Doignon & Falmagne, 1999 ) (cf. 2, 3). It is based on a stochastic model of small complexity (cf. 3), and the required data are simply the observed absolute counts of response patterns (cf. 4).

This paper represents an application of established biometric modeling and corresponding classical maximum likelihood methodology to KST. Except for minor conceptual adaptations (e.g., notion of equally informative items), the models in this paper are straightforward applications of the biometric models that have been published in the biostatistical literature on evaluating medical diagnostic tests (see  Section 1.1 ). Nevertheless, the application of these models to KST is novel and important. The applied random effects approach aims at the estimation of response error rates (see  Sections 1.3  and  1.4 ), and is general and flexible enough to allow for further developments in KST (see  Sections 9.2  and  9.3 ). It represents a traditional sort of psychometric modeling, and thus provides a connection of KST to traditional psychometric test theory.

Using the biometric models for KST, we  rely  and  proceed on  the classical maximum likelihood methodology (see  Section 7 ) which has been published in the biostatistical literature on these models (see  Section 1.1 ). In particular, we do not question or prove whether certain regularity conditions required for the asymptotics to hold are satisfied (however, cf.  Section 8.5 ). We simply start from the asymptotics, given the ample usage of these models and the corresponding methodology in biometrics. Interestingly, though here is an extensive literature in key journals such as Applied Statistics, Biometrics, and Journal of the American Statistical Association, systematic theoretical work, in biometrics, on the foundations of these models is—to the author's knowledge—still lacking.

Definitely, work on these models based on Bayesian methodology, for instance, using better Bayesian  p -value formulations should be pursued in future research.

The general definition of a knowledge structure allows for infinite item sets as well. Throughout this work, however, we assume that  Q  is finite.

The set  Q  is supposed to be a set of  dichotomous  items. In this paper, we interpret  Q  as a set of questions/problems that can either be  solved  or  not solved . This stands for the observed responses of a subject ( manifest level ), and has to be distinguished from a subject's true, unobservable knowledge of the solution to an item ( latent level ). In the latter case, we say that the subject is  capable of mastering  or  not capable of mastering  the item. For a set  X , let

However, from the viewpoint on estimating the response error rates for a dichotomous item (the aim of this work), the concept of parallelity is a crucial one.

Suppose you have formulated an item, and you are interested in the error rates that may be associated with it. What we suggest in this paper, is to consider this item in the context of other, equally informative items. In a sense, this can be viewed as replications of the ‘same’ item, which allows for refined information about the item's characteristics (error rates). Or—see  Section 1.4 —, suppose you have alternative formulations of one and the same content (equally informative items), and you are interested in choosing the ‘best’ one (with small error rates). In both cases, one is led to the simplest knowledge structure. Based on the simplest knowledge structure, the BLIM results in the  classical unrestricted 2-classes latent class model . This model has been dealt extensively in the literature on latent class analysis (LCA) and on evaluation of medical diagnostic tests. 
                            12

                            For references on LCA, see, e.g.,  Goodman (1974) ,  Hagenaars and McCutcheon (2002) ,  Lazarsfeld and Henry (1968) , and  McCutcheon (1987) . For references on the classical unrestricted 2-classes latent class model, see also  Dunn (1989) ,  Hui and Walter (1980) ,  McHugh (1956, 1958) ,  Walter (1984) , and  Walter and Irwig (1988) .

In fact, for equally informative, educational test items, local independence may not be justified in general (cf.  Section 3.4 ). Nevertheless, the classical ‘local independence model’ is a special case of a general latent class model with random effects. Thus, if the assumption of local independence is empirically justified, this should be appropriately reflected by the estimates of the model parameters of a latent class model with random effects (see  Section 8.5 ).

As stated in  Section 1.5 , using the biometric random effects models for KST, we proceed on the classical maximum likelihood methodology which has been published in the biostatistical literature on these models. We also look at model selection based on the AIC and SIC.

Maximum likelihood (ML) estimates of the model parameters are obtained using the conjugate gradient search algorithm PRincipal AXIS (PRAXIS) by  Brent (1973) , which is a modification of a direction-set method by  Powell (1964) . For a brief description of Powell's method and Brent's modification, see  Gegenfurtner (1992) .  Gegenfurtner (1992)  provides an implementation of the PRAXIS algorithm in the programming language C. 
                            13 
                         
                         
                            13 
                            The C source files are freely available from  
                                  http://archives.math.utk.edu/software/msdos/numerical.analysis/praxis/.html 
                               . 
                          Recently,  Ünlü and Kickmeier-Rust (2005)  have proposed an extension of Gegenfurtner's C implementation which can be pursued for improved and effective applications of PRAXIS. They suggest the use of a three-round procedure for automatic generation and testing of multiple sets of start values. This extended version of the PRAXIS implementation is used in the simulation study in  Section 8 .

There are many other ways to obtain the ML estimates of the model parameters, for instance, by Newton–Raphson and/or EM type procedures. For example, EM algorithms for the models in this paper are provided by  Qu et al. (1996) , a hybrid EM–Newton–Raphson algorithm is described in  Qu and Hadgu (1998) . We do not consider such alternatives in this paper. This work represents an application of biometric modeling and corresponding ML methodology to KST, and we retain the approach to ML estimation which has been commonly pursued in KST: the PRAXIS algorithm. 
                            14 
                         
                         
                            14 
                            For instance, see  Doignon and Falmagne (1999, pp. 149–150, 153, 198–199) ,  Fries (1997) , and  Wickelmaier (2002) . 
                          In KST, and maybe for many psychologists in general, algorithms like PRAXIS have been a common and satisfactory way for fitting parametric models. For PRAXIS applications in other psychological sciences, see, e.g.,  Erdfelder and Buchner (1998) ,  Hartinger (1999) ,  Heller (1997) , and  McClelland and Chappell (1998) . PRAXIS type algorithms have been satisfactorily applied in fields other than psychology as well—e.g., see  Carcione et al. (2001)  (computational acoustics),  Hwang and Tien (1996)  (physics),  Leroy et al. (2004)  (medical image computing),  Ren et al. (2002)  and  Ren et al. (2002)  (nuclear medicine),  Tubic et al. (2001)  (medical physics), and  Woelk (2000)  (magnetic resonance).

An EM algorithm usually has to be developed afresh for each new model and applies to a likelihood function in a statistical framework. The PRAXIS algorithm is a general, efficient method for the minimization of (smooth) functions of several variables without the use of derivatives. The same routine can be applied to any (smooth) model, and it is freely available as easy-to-use C implementation ( Gegenfurtner, 1992 ; see also  Ünlü & Kickmeier-Rust, 2005 ). Also, though EM is state-of-the-art approach to ML estimation, there is no work known to the author which explicitly compares EM and PRAXIS for the biometric models in this paper. This is out of the scope of this paper and could be investigated in future research.

Why is the simulation study conducted in this way? 
                            
                               1. 
                               This paper represents a first application of the random effects approach to KST. In KST, the BLIM is fundamental, in the sense that most of the KST probabilistic models are special cases of the BLIM. In particular, the assumption of local independence is fundamental in KST. 
                            
                            
                               2. 
                               The simulation study describes a useful situation in practice. Suppose you have empirical data on a set of items that come most likely from a BLIM. Further suppose you detect some items to be most likely parallel. In this case, the part of the empirical data which corresponds to the parallel items may be used for the estimation of the error rates for these items based on the random effects approach. In particular, no new empirical data may need to be collected solely based on these items. Along with the BLIM data we thus obtain quality assessments of the parallel items. 
                            
                            
                               3. 
                               The simulation study does not treat data generated under local dependence. In the biometric literature (see  Section 1.1 ), the random effects approach has been satisfactorily applied to situations in which local independence is violated. Based on such related applications, this approach looks promising for handling local dependence in KST applications. To claim this definitely, however, requires more work including extensive simulation studies. This is out of the scope of this paper which gives a first application of the random effects approach to a traditional local independence based formulation of KST (cf. previous point 1). Nevertheless, systematic applications of these random effects models to general empirical and simulated data in KST are important directions for further research (cf. also  Sections 9.2  and  9.3 ).

The BLDM is quite a general and flexible model, and basically any of the huge number of possibilities for restricting its parameters could be considered, at least theoretically. For instance, traditional latent class ‘measurement’ models such as the Proctor model, the Dayton–Macready intrusion–omission model, and more generally, the Lazarsfeld–Henry latent distance model are special cases of the BLDM (for a description of these models, see, e.g.,  Heinen, 1996 ). In particular, for KST interesting special cases of the BLDM could be obtained by restricting (beside maybe other constraints on the  a - and  b -parameters) the state probabilities based on postulated learning mechanisms describing successive transitions of subjects, over time, from the state  
                            ∅ 
                          to the state  Q  (see  Doignon & Falmagne, 1999 ). Such an endeavour could allow for generalized reformulations of dynamic models (stochastic learning paths systems) in KST, weakening the assumption of local independence to allow for local dependence. An interesting question may also be how—if possible at all—the BLDM could help in the (empirical) derivation of a knowledge structure and/or the precise and reliable estimation of the state probabilities. These issues require more work and seem to be interesting directions for further research.
