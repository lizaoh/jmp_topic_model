The second reason for focusing on the LES is empirical. Radner & Schotter (RS) presented the results of several experiments designed to test the sealed-bid mechanism. For the case investigated in our first experiment, their results show that relatively inexperienced subjects are able to capture a large portion of the potential gains from trade and that their behavior is qualitatively consistent with the LES. 

A TRUTHFUL REVELATION MODEL 

If the major purpose of the experimental investigation of the sealed-bid mechanism is to account for behavioral regularities of highly motivated, relatively inexperienced bargainers, theoretical considerations should not be limited to the LES. First, as noted above, the sealed-bid mechanism has a very large set of equilibria. Consequently, even if the bidding strategies are not linear (the results of RS seem to suggest that, with much experience, other equilibria may appear), they may be accounted for by other equilibrium solutions. Second, informal experimentation under incomplete information conducted by Chatterjee & Ulvila (1982) and Chatterjee & Lilien (1984) casts doubts on the validity of Bayesian equilibrium as a predictor of the outcome of two-person bargaining under incomplete information (Chatterjee, 1985). Third, relatively inexperienced bargainers cannot compute the equilibrium strategies for the bilateral monopoly mechanism described above. Hence, it is likely that they will revert to a simple heuristic to guide their behavior. Truthful revelation of their reservation prices, which allows each bargainer to ignore the contingent decision processes of his opponent, is a heuristic that many bargainers may adopt. In concluding their analysis of twoperson bargaining where each party possesses private information about its preferences, Chatterjee & Ulvila write that “truthful revelation, the maximin strategy, may in fact bea good choice for a satisficing bargainer” (1982, p. 402). 

Following their suggestion, we propose below a truthful revelation model (TRM) to be tested against the LES. The TRM assumes that traders choose their bids to reveal their prices honestly. Three major reasons may be forwarded to motivate this alternative model. First, truthful revelation is Suple. Traders may opt to bid truthfully rather than getting involved in strategic considerations which are time COnsuming (and therefore costly) and whose implications are not at all transparent. Second, truthful revelation conforms to social norms of behavior which encourage honesty. The 

third reason concerns the probability of reaching an agreement. If V and C are each independently and uniformly distributed over the interval [0, 100], as in Experiment 1, the probability of reaching an agreement” under the LES is 3, whereas honest bidding increases this probability to 3. Thus, the LES is only 56.3% effective in achieving feasible agreements. If relatively inexperienced bargainers are interested in maximizing the probability of achieving feasible agreements, rather than maximizing expected profit, they may be inclined to reveal their reservation prices truthfully. DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSIONS 

There is an ongoing debate over whether economic models or behavioral models can better account for the regularities observed in bargaining behavior. The empirical evidence seems to suggest that while economic models account for many of the qualitative aspects of bargaining behavior, systematic deviations from rationality limit the predictive power of these models. In an attempt to elucidate this debate in the context of bilateral monopoly with incomplete information, we compared to each other two models which are based on radically different assumptions.
