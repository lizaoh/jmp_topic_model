JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY: 6, 430-443 (1969)  Value of Information for Decisions'  DIRK WENDT  Psychological Institut, Hamburg, Germany  Information that will reduce the risk of a decision may be costly in time, effort, or money. The maximum amount that should be  invested in the information-its fair cost-depends upon payoffs, the diagnosticity of the data source, and prior probabilities  of the hypotheses. These are the independent variables of this experiment. Subjects estimated the fair costs by means of the  Marschak bidding procedure. The subjects' bids changed in the direction appropriate to each of the three independent  variables, but not enough to be optimal.  INTRODUCTION  The purpose of the present experiment was to examine behavior in a simple information buying task that provided the subject's  direct estimates of the value of a single datum bought.  It is an experiment in the field of information purchase which links together two formerly separate areas of reasearch on  human decision processes: choices among well-specified bets on the one hand; probability estimation and probability revision  on the other. In information purchase tasks, the subject decides between bets in the sense that he chooses between a risky bet  without the information or with little information, and a less risky one with more information. This allows him to revise the  probabilities of the outcomes although he has to pay for it. Research on choices among bets has shown that subjects may have  probability preferences (Edwards, 1953), and that their utility function may deviate somewhat from linearity, though  negligibly little in the region of small monetary gains (e.g. Lindman, 1965; Tversky, 1967). The main finding in research on  human probability estimation and revision is that men are conservative estimators in the sense that they do not extract from  the data as much certainty as the data justify. For examples of such experiments, see Edwards, Lindman, and Phillips, 1965;  Peterson and Beach, 1967; Phillips and Edwards, 1966.  Does human conservatism in probability revision affect information buying behavior? Or are men near-optimal in this as in many  other decision tasks ? After observing the first item of  information, he may buy another, and after that another, and so on until he decides to stop. This is a rather complex,  hard-to-understand situation. Moreover, the datum obtained in such experiments, the subject's stopping point, is uninformative  about the interesting question: how valuable did the subject consider each datum at the time he bought it ? Experiments with  optional stopping have found men buying both too much and too little information, but seldom the optimal amount as prescribed  by the normative model of expectation maximation. In spite of that, they do rather well with respect to their final payoff.  This is due to the fact that the expected value functions in these experiments are rather flat around their maxima. This is  also true in the experiment reported here as we will see in the discussion. METHOD  The subject was shown a spinner wheel consisting of a disc with variable red and blue sectors, and a rotating pointer above  it. The experimenter demonstrated that the pointer, after being spun, would stop randomly on either the red or the blue sector  of the disc, with likelihoods corresponding to the proportions of the disc covered by the two colors. The color on which the  pointer stopped represented the datum zk ; the predominant color on the disc represented the state of nature s; . The subject  was told that during the experiment the spinner would be hidden behind a screen, and operated by the experimenter who would  tell him the color under the pointer when it stopped but not the predominant color of the disc. Given the information zk  (color under the pointer when it stopped), it was the task of the subject to guess the state of nature s; (predominant color  on the disc). The subject was told what proportion of the disc was covered by the predominant color, but not what color it  was. Its proportion represents the diagnosticity of the data source, P(zl I sl). The prior probability P(sl) was the probability that one color, sl , was predominant on the disc. The experimenter showed the  subject a bag which contained 10 X P(sl) red and 10 X P(sk) blue poker chips. Then he randomly picked one chip out of this  bag, but did not show it to the subject. The color of this chip determined the predominant color of the disc. If the subject  was correct in his guess about the predominant color of the disc, he would be paid a certain amount, V(al , sl) = V(a2 , s2);  if he was wrong, he would lose a certain amount, - V(al, s2) = -V(a2 , sl). In all trials, the subject was given the payoff  conditions, prior odds (bag composition, P(sl)/P(s2), and the diagnosticity of the data source (disc composition, P(zl I  s,)/P(z2 I SO = P(Z1 s2)/P(Z1 I s2))The subject was told that he would have to pay for the information from the spinner  since this information would improve his chance of being correct in his guess on the predominant color, and thus improve his  chance of winning. The price of information was subtracted from his gain if he was correct, and added to his loss if he was  wrong.  The subject's task was to bid for the information from the spinner. He could bid any amount he wanted, but was told that it  would not make much sense to bid more than he could win in  438 WENDT  case of a correct guess of the predominant color. Also, he was particularity told that 0 would be the fair bid if he felt that  the information from the spinner was worthless. The experimenter compared his bid for the datum to a price chosen randomly  from a uniform distribution of integers from 0 to V(al , s,). If the subject's was higher than the experimenter's random  price, the subject purchased the information from the pointer at the experimenter's random price. If his bid was lower than  the random price, he did not get the information at all, but had to make his decision on the predominant color of the disc  without it. (This is an example of what has come to be called the Marschak bidding procedure; cf. Becker, DeGroot, and  Marschak, 1964; Lindman, 1965.) Instructions were very explicit that it would be optimal for the subject always to bid what he  considered a fair price for the information, and not to overbid or underbid. If he underbid, he would miss out on a bargain  whenever the experimenter's random price was above his bid but below his estimate of fair cost. If he overbid, he would have  to buy information for more than he thought it was worth whenever the experimenter's random price was above his estimate of  fair cost but below the subject's bid. The subject's bid was considered his estimate of the value of the information, the  dependent variable in this experiment. Before starting the main experiment, all subjects were given a couple of practice  trials with varying diagnosticities P(z, I s,) and prior probabilities P(s,) in Experiment 1, and with varying diagnosticities  P(z, 11 sl) and payoffs V(a, , s,) and V(a,, s2) in Experiment 2.  Each subject made a total of 180 estimates, i.e., played 180 games (3 levels of payoff or prior odds times 10 levels of  diagnosticity times 6 repetitions). He could take as much time for his decisions as he wanted; however, most subjects  accomplished their 180 trials within 90 to 120 min. The bids, gains, and losses were expressed in points, and after all 180  games were over, the total gain or loss in points of the subject was figured out. Then the subject drew an envelope which  contained an exchange rate to convert his outcome from points to money. The exchange rates were predetermined so that every  subject got a final payoff between $2.50 and $3.50.  Subjects. Subjects were male undergraduates of the University of Michigan, 17 in Experiment 1, and 15 in Experiment 2.  RESULTS  The data (subject's bids for information) were processed individually for each subject in a 3 x 10 factorial analysis of  variance, with six replications in each cell. In addition, an estimate of variance based on the deviations of cell means from  theoretical fair costs was tested against the within-cell variance estimate. Tables 2 and 3 give the results (F ratios) of  these analyses of variance, and Figs. 1 and 2 show the mean bids over all subjects. All subjects varied their bids as a  function of both diagnosticity and prior odds in Experiment 1, and both diagnosticity and payoffs in Experiment 2. For most of  the subjects, these variables interacted significantly in influencing the bids. These effects were in the direction required  by the theory for all subjects with the exception of one subject in Experiment 1 who made higher bids for the more biased  prior odds. However, subjects' bids were significantly different from objective fair costs of information, except for two  subjects in Experiment 2, one of whom always bid exactly the fair cost. No analysis of variances was computed for this subject  because he showed no error variance. SUMMARY  Seventeen and 15 undergraduate subjects respectively, participated in two experiments in which their estimates of the value of  information (data) in a decision task were measured by means of the Marschak bidding procedure where subjects bid against a  uniformly distributed random list. In both experiments, the diagnosticity of the data P(DI H) was varied in 10 levels from.55  to 1.0. Additionally, in Experiment 1 prior odds were varied in three levels: .5/.5, .7/.3, .9/.1. In Experiment 2 payoffs  were varied in three levels: win/lose 50, 100, 200. Bids and payoffs were made in points which were changed to money after the  game was over. The results indicate that subjects were sensitive enough to all three types of independent variables involved  (i.e., diagnosticity, prior odds, and payoff), but, except for a few of them, did not match the actual (theoretical) value of  the information. In a discussion of the procedure it was shown that it did not pay very much to be accurate, due to the  flatness of the Eh function.  VALUE OF INFORMATION 443  REFERENCES  BECKER, G. M., DEGROOT, M.
