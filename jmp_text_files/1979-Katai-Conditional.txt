JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 19, 259-294 (1979)  Construction of a Conditional Probability Learning Model and Information Theoretical Evaluation of Its Efficiency  OSAMU KATAI AND SOUSUKE IWAI  Department of Precision Mechanics, Faculty of Engineering, Kyoto University, Kyoto City, 606 Japan  A learning model is introduced based on the simple idea that a learning subject has two hypotheses about the results (the  outputs of the environment) caused by his actions and calculates the a posteriori probabilities of the hypotheses. Introducing  a randomized decision rule of actions related to the probabilities, it is shown that the model is equivalent to Luce's beta  model, while these models are derived from completely different considerations. Introducing an information theoretic measure  and martingale theory, the asymptotic behavior of the model is examined. Using the information theoretic measure, the effect  of memory function on the learning behavior is evaluated. Finally, the model is extended in such a way that the learning  subject has more than two hypotheses or a continuum of hypotheses. The learning behaviors of the extended models are discussed.  1. INTRODUCTION  Many stochastic models of learning processes are devised to study the properties of human learning behavior. In these models,  the most fundamental constituents of learning processes are the learning subjects and the environments, and these two  constituents interact with each other. The effect of a learning subject on the environment is called an action and the effect  of the environment on the learning subject is called a reinforcement. Let the possible reinforcements be xI , x2 ,..., xk and  the actions be fI , f2 ,..., f . The case which is most often examined is a situation composed of two actions, fi and Js,  and two reinforcements, xI (penalty) and x2(non-penalty). The learning subject learns to avoid the penalty xI by choosing one  of actions fI or f2 that has less frequency of the penalty.  In this paper, we propose a stochastic learning model based on the following considerations. The learning subject tries to  decide which action, fi or f2 , is preferable based on past experience, or in other words, which action is less penalized by  the environment. Underlying his decision between fI and f2, we suppose that the learning subject sets up two hypothetical  states A and A of the environment. Under hypothesis A, fi is preferable ( , i.e. less penalyzed) to f2 . On the contrary, f2  is preferable tofi under A. The learning subject seeks which of hypotheses A or A seems to fit the actual situation better  than the other.  To embody the above considerations, A and A are set to be alternative hypotheses which could be postulated by the learning  subject as useful mediators in giving the  259  0022-2496/79/030259-36$02.00/0  Copyright 1979 by Academic Press, Inc. Under each hypothesis, A or A, the  learning subject is supposed to guess the probability of output (reinforcement) x; from the environment by input (action) fi .  In the sequel, these hypothetical probabilities are called subjective probabilities. Namely, it is postulated that the  learning subject sets up subjective probability f'(x; I fi , A) (or P(x; I fi , A)) (for i = 1, 2, and j = 1, 2), which  denotes in the model the subjective conditional probability of output x; conditioned upon input fi and hypothesis A (or A).  These are hypothetical quantities and are not to be confused with usual (objective) probabilities. Throughout the paper, P  will denote objective probabilities and P will be used for subjective probabilities.  The preferability of fi to f2 under hypothesis A is reflected in that the value of f'(xl I fi , A) is less than the value of  f'(xi I f2 , A). The preferability of f2 to fi under hypothesis A is also embodied by a similar way.  The learning subject acts as if he calculates the a posteriori subjective probability of A (or A) conditioned upon past  experience, i.e., the observed input-output pairs (action-reinforcement pairs). The calculated value is taken as a measure of  the degree of fit of A (or A) with the actual stochastic property of the random environment. Finally, an act is selected  randomly with the probabilities of action fi and f2 set equal to the a posteriori subjective probabilities of hypotheses A and  A, respectively.  In section 2, it is shown that learning behavior of the proposed model is equivalent to that of the Beta model of Luce (1959),  under certain assumptions about the above subjective probabilities.  In section 3, we evaluate the "efficiency" of the model's behavior by the use of an information theoretic measure which is  derived from the theory of information introduced by Kullback (1959). Also, using the above measure, we examine the asymptotic  behavior of the learning process of the model. Particularly, the effect of the setting of the subjective conditional  probabilities on the asymptotic bahavior is examined in connection with actual (objective) conditional probabilities of the  environment by using a result by Norman (1970). As a result, a condition on the values of the subjective conditional  probabilities is derived in order to assure optimal behavior. When the learning process is viewed as a Markov chain with  infinitely many states, the chain has interesting properties closely related to Norman's result. Moreover, it has a close  connection with an interesting learning model called linear tactics and is derived by the Soviet scientist Tsetlin (1961). A  kind of random process called a martingale process introduced by Doob (1935) seems to have a desirable property when viewed as  a stochastic learning process. We clarify the condition under which the behavior of the model constitutes a martingale process.  In section 4, using the information theoretic measure, the relationship between the complexity of a random environment and the  memory capacity of a learning subject is examined with respect to its efficiency.  In section 5, we extend the model in such a way that the learning subject has more than two hypotheses or a continuum of  hypotheses. The learning behaviors of the extended models are compared with those of the model with just two hypotheses.  LEARNING MODEL 261  2.
