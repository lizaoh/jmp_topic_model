A Probabilistic Feature Model for Unfolding Tested for Perfect and Imperfect Nestings* 

M. J. J. M. Candel 

Wageningen Agricultural University 

Complex stimuli can be represented as sets of features. Coombs' unfolding theory for preferential choice and for comparative similarity judgement is modeled for a feature representation of stimuli. This is called the Additive Random Feature model for Unfolding (ARFU). A special case is obtained if Restle's dissimilarity model applies, which we call ARFU-R. Its implications were tested for verbal and pictorial stimuli which exhibit a nesting or an imperfect nesting of their features. 

Nearly all of ARFU-R's predictions were satisfied at a 100 level of significance, which establishes the validity of ARFU-R for the stimuli used in a triadic comparisons task. The most salient results were (a) the low level of transitivity of subjects' choices, and (b) the subjects' failure to choose the standard when it was among the alternatives presented to the subject. Because of the nature of the task and the stimuli, these results would appear to apply also to two ``real-life'' tasks: Diagnosis of a mental illness syndrome and construction of an identikit picture of an offender. ] 1997 Academic Press Key Words : unfolding analysis; choice models; feature models; nestings; triadic comparisons.

Unfolding theory applies to comparative similarity data as well as to preferential choice data. In the case of similarity data, the ideal is a reference object explicitly presented to the subject, and the subject is asked to rank a set of objects with respect to their similarity to that reference. In the case of preferential choice data, the ideal is in the mind of the subject, and the subject will rank order the alternatives according to their resemblance to that ideal. In either case, the choice mechanism can be described by means of a dissimilarity function. There exist specific unfolding models, which make different assumptions regarding the mathematical structure that represents the alternatives and the dissimilarity function. 

Most models of unfolding are geometric. Objects and ideals are represented as points in a dimensional space, and the dissimilarity between object and ideal is commonly represented by the Euclidian distance. ``Unfolding'' derives its name from the fact that, in the case of a unidimensional scale of the objects, the preference rank order is a folding of that scale at the location of the subject's ideal, so that the scale is recovered by unfolding the preference rank order. If the model is correct for the data, all individually different preference rank orders will unfold into a single common scale of the objects (Coombs, 1964). This scale is called the Joint or J-scale, as both choice objects and subjects are located on it. 

Since people do not always make the same choices in seemingly similar situations, many probabilistic extensions of the folding principle have been formulated. As demonstrated by, for instance, Gati and Tversky (1982), Tversky (1977), and Tversky and Gati (1978, 1982), a representation of stimuli as sets of features may bring up properties of similarity judgments that can not be accommodated by conventional geometric representations. Therefore, we wish to consider a non-geometric unfolding model, in which objects and ideals are represented as sets of features. Formally, a feature is an element of a set. Psychologically, a feature is a property that distinguishes a particular (set of ) choice object(s) from another (set of ) choice object(s). For example, a car may be a choice object with the features ``red color'', ``automatic transmission'', and ``low fuel consumption''. Furthermore, the dissimilarity between object and ideal is defined by Restle's (1959, 1961) distance model: dissimilarity is an increasing function of the number of features that belong exclusively to either the choice object or the ideal. Restle's dissimilarity model is a special case of the contrast model (Tversky, 1977, 1978), which defines similarity as a function of both common and exclusive features. For the task we are considering we will, however, assume that common features play no role in the perception of dissimilarity, and refer to this restricted model as the Restle version of the Additive Random Feature model for Unfolding, or ARFU-R for short. 

The feature sets that represent choice objects and ideals can have various arrangements, called feature structures. We will derive ARFU-R's predictions for two feature structures: a nesting and an imperfect nesting. As will be seen, for each structure ARFU-R yields a different pattern of predictions. 

Usually unfolding models are tested by parameter estimation and model fitting (see for instance, Andrich, 1988; Andrich, 1989; Bechtel, 1968; Greenberg, 1965; Scho�nemann 6 Wang, 1972; Sixtl, 1973; Wang, Scho�nemann 6 Rusk, 1975). An alternative approach consists of statistically testing a model's predictions without estimating its specific parameters (Bossuyt, 1990). These predictions consist of a model's implications for ordinal relations among choice probabilities. Testing for such ordinal relations is more convenient than model fitting, especially if models are mathematically complex, because such testing may lead to a rejection of all models that imply a particular relation, without having to estimate the parameters of each model separately. On the other hand, testing a model's implications often means testing a much more general model, since the implications of a model in general provide only necessary but not sufficient conditions for its validity. However, as a preliminary test of a model's validity, we consider a statistical test of its qualitative implications very useful. 

In the present study, ARFU-R's predictions for both a nesting and an imperfect nesting are tested. This is done for 

both verbal and pictorial stimuli. Since the verbal and the pictorial stimuli are imbedded in a diagnostic task and an offender identification task, respectively, the present study not only serves a theoretical interest, but possibly also has practical significance: It might indicate how people make consequential choices in real life. 

The paper is organized as follows. First, ARFU-R is presented and specified for objects that exhibit the structure of a perfect nesting or an imperfect nesting. In order to derive ARFU-R's predictions, we impose an extra restriction on the structure of the set of objects, which is similar to the existence of a unidimensional J-scale. Next, we derive ARFU-R's implications as regards the degree of transitivity of choices. These implications are given for particular triples of objects, which have different positions on the J-scale relative to the ideal. Also a number of so-called ideal point conditions (Bossuyt, 1990) are examined. A last set of conditions to be examined, concerns the structure of the socalled dominance matrix (Bossuyt, 1990). We close with the presentation and discussion of an experiment, in which ARFU-R's implications are empirically tested. 

THE RESTLE VERSION OF ARFU 

Let O*=[o1 , o2 , ..., o M] be a finite set of choice objects, let S*=[s1 , s2 , ..., s N] be a finite set of standards, ideals or reference objects, and let U*=[u1 , u2 , ..., uP] be the union of these sets. For each oA , oB of O* and sI of S*, P(oA , oB; sI) denotes the probability that object oA is preferred to object oB with respect to standard sI . Depending on the experimental task, sI may be an explicitly presented reference stimulus or an ``ideal'' or ``typical'' stimulus in the mind of the subject. The symbol uA is used when the stimulus may either be an ideal or a choice object.

A J-scale 

A J-scale is an ordinal scale jointly representing both the choice objects and the ideals (Coombs, 1964). This joint order entails, according to Coombs' deterministic model, certain testable properties. For instance, the middle object of each triple of objects is never the least preferred of that triple. Also, when two subjects have their ideals at opposite ends of a subset of alternatives, they should have opposite preference rank orders for these alternatives. In the deterministic unfolding model, the J-scale of a given set of preference orders is thus defined as the common ordering of ideals and alternatives which generates it. 

For probabilistic choice behavior, Coombs (1964) examined particular levels of stochastic transitivity for different kinds of triples of objects, which have different positions on the J-scale relative to the location of the ideal. Various other properties of probabilistic choice behavior also appear worth investigation, as they will be different in different probabilistic models. The J-scale must either be known a priori (as was the case in Coombs' (1964) ``Amsterdam experiment''), or inferred from the data. The J-scale can be inferred from the data by a parametric model of probabilistic unfolding, which estimates the scale values of the objects and ideals. 

The preceding is based on geometric modeling, in which dimensions and dimensionality have a well-defined meaning. The existence of a J-scale means that the space of objects and ideals is unidimensional. For a representation of objects and ideals as sets rather than as points in space, no such definition of what constitutes a J-scale presents itself naturally. We will therefore introduce a definition of what constitutes a J-scale of objects and ideals represented as sets. 

In the ARFU-R model, we state choice probabilities as a function of set-symmetric distances, and the only way to derive a common ordering of ideals and alternatives from 

these choice probabilities appears to be that their set-symmetric distances satisfy some definition of an ordering. TESTABLE PROPERTIES OF AFRU-R 

In the following sections we prove several implications of ARFU-R, which concern ordinal relations among choice probabilities. For given sample estimates of the pertinent choice probabilities, we will subsequently test whether these implications are satisfied by the data. 

Stochastic Transitivity Implications 

Transitivity of pairwise choice means that, if object oA is chosen over object oB and object oB is chosen over object oC , then oA will be chosen over oC . It is a property implied by most models of choice, but sometimes violated by data. For probabilistic models of pairwise choice, various forms of stochastic transitivity have been distinguished (see, e.g., Bossuyt, 1990; Fishburn, 1973). Two of these forms are of interest for ARFU-R: Moderate Stochastic Transitivity

Ideal Point Implications 

There are also a number of implications that concern the choice probabilities when one of the choice alternatives coincides with the ideal, or as they vary as a function of the ideal (Bossuyt, 1990). To the first group belong implications which we call Weak, Strong, and Absolute Ideal Preference. To the second group belong what we call the Unilateral Conditions and the Bilateral Condition. Finally, two implications called Differentiation of Ideals and Symmetry belong to both groups. 

Weak, Strong, and Absolute Ideal Preference. In line with Coombs' (1964) definition of the ideal as the object 

that is preferred over any other object, the ideal might be expected to be chosen over any other object with at least probability one half. This is termed Weak Ideal Preference, or WIP for short. 

A stronger condition is Strong Ideal Preference, or SIP. According to SIP the probability of choosing the ideal over any object oA not only exceeds one half, but also exceeds the probability of choosing any other object over object oA . For this condition the preference of the ideal to the other objects may vary, but for each object, the ideal must have the highest probability of being preferred. 

If the level of ideal preference is maximal, that is, whenever the ideal is among the alternatives the probability of choosing it is one, we have a condition which is called Absolute Ideal Preference, or AIP. The following theorem, which is proven in Appendix A, summarizes ARFU-R's implications. 

Theorem 4. If the structure of U* is an imperfect nesting, ARFU-R implies Strong Ideal Preference, but forbids Absolute Ideal Preference. 

The Bilateral Condition and the Unilateral Conditions. There are two conditions that describe the choice probability as a function of the ideal, when the ideal is different from both choice objects. The Bilateral Condition concerns the choice probability when the ideal is located between a pair of choice objects on the J-scale. The Bilateral Condition is the condition that the probability of choosing oA over oB does not increase if the ideal is displaced from oA towards oB (Bossuyt, 1990). 

The Unilateral Conditions concern the choice probability for a unilateral pair of choice objects. If moving the ideal away from a pair of objects leads to a more extreme choice probability, that is, a choice probability closer to 0 or 1, we have the Unilaterally Increasing Condition. If the probability becomes less extreme, that is, moves towards one half, we have the Unilaterally Decreasing Condition. If the probability remains constant, we have the Unilaterally Constant Condition. To illustrate these conditions, consider someone whose ideal is classical music but who must choose between going to a rock concert or going to a new-wave concert. The Unilaterally Increasing Condition predicts that he will almost always choose the least unattractive of these alternatives, whereas the Unilaterally Decreasing Condition predicts that he will be almost indifferent, that is, he will choose the least unattractive alternative with probability about 0.5. The next theorem is proven in the appendix. 

Theorem 5. If the structure of U* is an imperfect nesting, ARFU-R implies the Bilateral Condition and the Unilaterally Constant Condition.

The Dominance Matrix 

The dominance matrix is a matrix with a row and column corresponding to each choice object and to a single ideal, and with cells containing the probability that the row object is chosen over the column object in the presence of that ideal. The diagonal cells of the matrix contain the choice probabilities for identical objects, which are set equal to one half. The final set of conditions to be discussed concerns the ordinal patterns of the entries in this matrix when its rows and columns are ordered according to the J-scale. Figure 1 contains an example of a dominance matrix for the objects oP , oQ , oR , oS , oT , and oU , and the ideal sI , which constitute the J-scale oP | oQ | oR | sI | oS | oT | oU. Several kinds of ordinal patterns can be distinguished (cf. Bossuyt, 1990), two of which are relevant for ARFU-R: Partial Monotonicity and Bilateral Monotonicity. 

Partial Monotonicity, or PM for short, is defined as follows (see also Fig. 1). In each row of the dominance matrix that is above the row corresponding to the ideal, sI , the choice probabilities do not increase from the left towards the diagonal, and do not decrease from column sI to the right. In each row at or below row sI , the probabilities do not increase from the left towards column sI , and do not decrease from the diagonal to the right. Furthermore, in each row, the choice probabilities between the ideal column and the diagonal do not exceed one half and are not less than the probability in the ideal column. So, in each row the smallest probability is located in column sI . 

Bilateral Monotonicity, abbreviated as BM (Bossuyt, 1990), is a special case of PM. Bilateral Monotonicity is defined as follows: In each row the choice probabilities do not increase from the left to the column corresponding with the ideal and do not decrease from this column to the right.

Summary of Properties of ARFU-R 

To summarize, ARFU-R's properties for nestings and imperfect nestings are: 

1. Moderate Stochastic Transitivity is implied, whereas Strong Stochastic Transitivity is not implied.

2. Weak and Strong Ideal Preference are implied, whereas Absolute Ideal Preference should be violated.

3. The Bilateral Condition and the Unilaterally Constant Condition are implied.

4. Symmetry and Differentiation of Ideals are implied.

There are two differences between perfect nestings and imperfect nestings: 

(a) For unilateral triples, ARFU-R implies Strong Stochastic Transitivity in case of a nesting, but only Moderate Stochastic Transitivity in case of an imperfect nesting.

(b) Bilateral Monotonicity is implied for a nesting, but is not implied for an imperfect nesting. For an imperfect nesting only Partial Monotonicity is implied.

METHOD 

Subjects 

Subjects were recruited by placing notes in several departments of the University of Nijmegen. Each note invited subjects to take part in a choice experiment. Thirtyeight male and forty-two female subjects volunteered, mainly psychology students between the ages of 19 and 37, and were randomly assigned to the experimental conditions. The subjects were paid 86 for their participation and were treated in accordance with the ``Ethical Principles of Psychologists'' (American Psychological Association, 1981). 

Task 

A subject was instructed to make triadic comparisons: Out of a pair of choice alternatives one alternative had to be chosen that most resembled a standard alternative. Since each of the standard alternatives was also presented as a choice alternative, we could test the ideal point conditions WIP, SIP, and AIP. Moreover, two standards were chosen such that Differentiation of Ideals, Symmetry, the Bilateral Condition, and the Unilateral Conditions could also be tested. 

Each subject made one choice for each combination of choice pair and standard, or triad. Because the conditions to be tested involve choice probabilities, choice frequencies were obtained by aggregating choices over subjects. One should be careful in generalizing the results of the present study to individual preference judgments. First, although each subject had the same ideal, data obtained for a single subject may differ from data obtained by aggregating over different subjects. Second, triadic similarity judgments may turn out to be of a different nature than preference judgments. What is important is that, for the task studied, we consider ARFU-R to be an adequate model. 

Verbal stimuli. Verbal stimuli in this study consisted of two sets of eight clinical profiles. Each clinical profile consisted of a list of symptoms and was headed by a label. This label either said ``diagnosis'' or ``clinical profile of the patient,'' depending on whether the list of symptoms acted as a choice alternative or as a standard. One set of clinical profiles was obtained by successively adding symptoms. For example, stimulus 2 was stimulus 1 in addition to the symptoms ``inflated self-esteem,'' ``no organic cause,'' and ``outbursts of anger.'' Stimulus 3 was stimulus 2 in addition to the symptom ``excessively cheerful.'' These stimuli, then, constituted a nesting. A second set consisted of the same clinical profiles, to each of which were added some unique symptoms, that is, symptoms not shared by any of the other clinical profiles. For example, stimulus 2 was stimulus 1 in addition to the symptoms ``difficulty falling asleep,'' and ``no organic cause,'' which also belong to stimuli 3 to 8, and also 

the unique symptom ``high irritability.'' Since this is a nesting augmented with unique features for each set, it is an imperfect nesting. A complete enumeration of symptoms, clinical profiles and possible diagnostic labels, is given in Appendix B. 

To construct both sets of clinical profiles, DSM-III-R (American Psychiatric Association, 1987) was used. Each clinical profile can be labeled by a diagnosis that fits the list of symptoms (e.g., the label ``hypomania'' for the symptoms ``inflated self-esteem,'' ``no organic cause,'' ``outbursts of anger,'' and ``excessively cheerful''). We strove to make clinical profiles characteristic of a particular diagnosis. As the stimuli had to satisfy a particular feature structure, this goal may not have been achieved for all stimuli. Nevertheless, the symptoms of each clinical profile can occur together and are compatible with a particular diagnostic label. 

The choice alternatives were the clinical profiles headed by the label ``diagnosis,'' and, when ordered from few to many symptoms, are denoted by CP1 to CP8. The standard alternatives had the same symptoms as CP4 and CP5, but were headed by the label ``clinical profile of the patient.'' The standards are referred to as CPA and CPB.[2] 

Pictorial stimuli. Two stimulus sets, each consisting of eight drawings of faces comprised the pictorial stimuli. One set was obtained by successively adding features to an empty face. For example, face 2 was the same as face 1, but had more hair. Face 3 was face 2, to which a moustache had been added. Face 4 was face 3, to which a pair of glasses had been added. These stimuli were supposed to satisfy a nesting. The stimuli of the second group were constructed to exhibit an imperfect nesting of the sets of facial features; each stimulus additionally had some features not shared by any of the other stimuli. For both sets of faces, two stimuli (number 4 (F4) and number 5 (F5) of the presumed J-scale) were used as standards. The standard alternatives are also referred to as F4 and F5. 

Design 

One half of the subjects started with the clinical profiles, the other half started with the faces. For both the clinical profiles and the faces one group of subjects first received the stimuli exhibiting a nesting, whereas the other group first received the stimuli exhibiting an imperfect nesting. For each combination of stimulus type and feature structure, there were two series, one series for each standard and each series consisting of ([8] 2[)=28 distinct triads. The order of the] standards was similarly counterbalanced.

In all there were 32 different series of 28 triads, namely eight random series, each of which could also be presented (a) with the triads in reverse order, (b) with a left-right reversal of the stimulus positions, and (c) with both an order reversal of the triads and a left-right reversal of the stimulus positions. Procedure 

In total 80 subjects participated in the experiment. Only one person at a time was subjected to the experimental procedures. Instructions and stimuli were presented on a computer screen placed in front of the subject. To read a new page with instructions, to start a new task, or to express their choices the subjects pressed one of three buttons on a control panel. This panel was connected to a PC (Olivetti M28), which recorded the responses. 

The subjects were instructed to choose the alternative that was most similar to the standard and to make a choice, even when in doubt. The instructions for the faces introduced this task as an offender identification task. A situation was depicted in which the standard could be seen as the face of an offender and a pair of faces could be seen as two identikit pictures. Choosing the face that resembled the standard most could then be interpreted as deciding whether a revised version of an identikit picture resembled the offender more than the original version. For the clinical profiles the subject was asked to choose out of two diagnoses the one that resembled the clinical profile of the patient most. 

Each subject responded to eight series of 28 triads: Four series with faces and four series with clinical profiles (one series for each standard and feature structure). The series were presented in pairs that differed only in the standard. A pair of series was preceded by instructions and a random series of ten (for the faces) or four (for the diagnoses) trial triads. Each series was followed by a one-minute break. 

A triad consisted of a presentation of the standard alternative, followed by a pair of choice alternatives. For the clinical profiles the subjects could inspect both the standard and the choice pair as long as they wished. For the faces a triad consisted of a 2.0-second presentation of a single face, the standard, followed by a pair of faces. The duration of the latter presentation, varying from 2.0 seconds to 3.5 seconds for nested stimuli and from 2.5 seconds to 4.0 seconds for imperfectly nested stimuli, depended on the number of facial features. After the choice pair had disappeared, the subject responded and a new triad was presented. For each subject, the entire experiment took about 75 minutes.

RESULTS 

Because the predictions concern choice probabilities and subjects made only a single choice for each triad, we aggregated the choices over all subjects, yielding 80 choices for each triad. For each combination of stimulus type, feature structure, and standard, a set with choice frequencies for each pair of choice objects resulted, which served as data for the statistical analyses. Let us first consider the results for the clinical profiles.

Stochastic transitivity conditions. The stochastic transitivity conditions can be tested by finding the ranking of alternatives that maximizes the likelihood ratio for a particular pattern of restrictions associated with this ranking (see Bossuyt, 1990, for details). For each standard and feature structure the maximum likelihood moderate stochastic ranking was obtained. DISCUSSION 

The present study provides empirical evidence for the applicability of ARFU-R to triadic comparisons among stimuli that exhibit a perfect or an imperfect nesting. Comparing ARFU-R's predictions with the results for stimuli that exhibit an imperfect nesting, we can conclude that: 

1. The implications concerning stochastic transitivity are confirmed: MST cannot be rejected, whereas SST must be rejected. Additionally, for unilateral triples SST must be rejected.

2. As predicted, AIP must be rejected, whereas SIP cannot be rejected. Thus the probability of choosing the standard is not necessarily unity. Apparently, there is some difficulty in recognizing the standard among the choice alternatives.

3. The Bilateral Condition is perfectly satisfied.

4. The Unilaterally Constant Condition cannot be rejected for the diagnoses, but must be rejected for the faces. For diagnoses, changing one standard for another does not affect the probability of choosing one alternative from a unilateral pair of alternatives. This is not the case for faces.

5. As predicted, Differentiation of Ideals and Symmetry cannot be rejected.

6. As expected, Partial Monotonicity is satisfied. Somewhat surprisingly, also Bilateral Monotonicity cannot be rejected.

Evaluating ARFU-R's predictions for a nesting in terms of the empirical results, we can conclude that: 

1. In line with the predictions, MST is satisfied, whereas SST must be rejected. For unilateral triples SST cannot be rejected.

2. Also, regarding the ideal point conditions, ARFU-R fits the data, so that SIP cannot be rejected, whereas AIP must be rejected.

3. The Bilateral Condition cannot be rejected.

4. For the diagnoses the Unilaterally Constant Condition cannot be rejected, whereas for the faces the Unilaterally Constant Condition must be rejected in favor of the Unilaterally Increasing Condition.

5. As predicted, Differentiation of Ideals and Symmetry cannot be rejected.

6. As predicted, Bilateral and Partial Monotonicity cannot be rejected.

This study seems to indicate that ARFU-R adequately describes stochastic properties of triadic comparisons for stimuli constructed according to a nesting or an imperfect nesting. In addition, the necessity of distinguishing between a nesting and an imperfect nesting emerges. Focusing on unilateral triples, SST cannot be rejected for a nesting, but must be rejected for an imperfect nesting. Further, though Bilateral Monotonicity is not violated significantly for an imperfect nesting, calculating the likelihood ratio of PM versus BM for the four data sets for an imperfect nesting shows that the data are about 56 times more likely under PM than under BM. For a nesting, where according to ARFU-R BM should hold, the data are only 1.14 times more likely under PM than under BM. This indicates that for an imperfect nesting PM gives a significantly better account of the data than BM does. 

Although nearly all predictions of ARFU-R are satisfied, it remains to be evaluated whether the empirical results strongly favor ARFU-R over other unfolding models. Bossuyt (1990, Chapter 1) gives an overview of different unfolding models, which all are geometric. All existing geometric models can be shown to imply a structure of the dominance matrix which is called Bilateral-Characteristic Monotonicity, or BCM for short (Bossuyt, 1990, Chapter 2). Bilateral-Characteristic Monotonicity, which is neither a special case of, nor is more general than Partial Monotonicity, could not be rejected for any of the data sets (see Table I and Table II). However, computing the likelihood ratio for PM versus BCM for all stimulus sets that are constructed according to an imperfect nesting, shows that the data are about 28 times more likely under PM than under BCM. If we compare this value to the likelihood ratio value of 0.40 obtained for a nesting, where according to ARFU-R both PM and BCM should hold, this indicates that, though PM is not more general than BCM, for an imperfect nesting the data fit PM significantly better than BCM. 

Furthermore, each geometric model that implies Bilateral Monotonicity also implies Strong Stochastic Transitivity (see Bossuyt, 1990, pp. 45, 46, and 51). As we have seen, for both a nesting and an imperfect nesting BM was satisfied, whereas, in line with ARFU-R's predictions, SST was violated and MST was satisfied. There is one model that, like ARFU-R, implies the Unilaterally Constant Condition: the Logistic Unfolding Model of Jansen (1981). This model is defined by P(oA , oB; sI)=H[d(oB , sI)&d(oA , sI)], where H denotes the cumulative logistic distribution function, and d denotes the Euclidean distance. One can easily verify

M. J. J. M. CANDEL 

that this model implies SST. As we have seen, this condition was systematically violated by the data. 

The Unilaterally Constant Condition had to be rejected for two of the four data sets. For faces exhibiting a nesting the Unilaterally Constant Condition had to be rejected in favor of the Unilaterally Increasing Condition, and for faces exhibiting an imperfect nesting the Unilaterally Constant Condition had to be rejected in favor of the Unilaterally Decreasing Condition. Although ARFU-R does not provide an adequate description of the data for the faces as far as the unilateral conditions are concerned, other unfolding models will also be inappropriate as they do not allow for the Unilaterally Increasing Condition and the Unilaterally Decreasing Condition at the same time (see Bossuyt, 1990, pp. 48�49). 

As a practical implication of the present study, we can point to the poor quality of subjects' choices in two more or less realistic tasks. One task probably relates to the construction of an identikit picture of an offender. In such a task a witness has to decide whether the original identikit picture or a revised version most resembles his mental image of the offender. The other task relates to the activity of diagnosing a patient. For both tasks the probability of choosing the standard alternative (face of the offender or actual disease of the patient) is significantly lower than unity. For example, if the clinical profile that can be labeled as ``mania'' is the standard as well as one of the choice alternatives and the clinical profile that can be labeled as ``bipolar disorder'' is the other choice alternative, then ``mania'' is chosen over ``bipolar disorder'' in less than 890 of the choices. One may argue that this is mainly due to a memory effect; as the standard has disappeared when the subject makes a choice, not choosing the ideal may be caused by incomplete memory of the standard. However, note that there were only two different standards for each combination of feature structure and stimulus type, all triads were presented in series that had the same standard, each series was preceded by a sequence of trial triads containing the same standard, and for the more difficult verbal stimuli, subjects could inspect the standard as long as they wished. If even for these ideal circumstances memory affects choices, this probably will be true also for choices in real life. 

The low level of transitivity characterizing subjects' choices is also remarkable. For example, if the clinical profile that can be labeled as ``mania'' is the standard, the diagnosis ``mood disorder'' is chosen over the diagnosis ``bipolar disorder, with psychotic features'' in 700 of the choices, the diagnosis ``bipolar disorder, with psychotic features'' is chosen over the diagnosis ``bipolar disorder, with mood-congruent psychotic features'' in 960 of the choices, but the diagnosis ``mood disorder'' is chosen over the diagnosis ``bipolar disorder, with mood-congruent psychotic features'' in only 700 of the choices. This constitutes a striking violation of SST. 

Clearly, to establish the validity of ARFU-R as a theory of choice more experimentation is necessary. This would involve feature structures other than nestings and imperfect nestings and tasks other than triadic similarity judgments. Such research will enhance our insight into how choices are made and how choices depend on properties of both the task and the choice objects.
