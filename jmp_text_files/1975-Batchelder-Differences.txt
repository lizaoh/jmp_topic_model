BATCHELDER  School of Social Sciences, University of California, Irvine, California 92664  This paper provides an analysis of the ability of the all-or-none and incremental learning frameworks augmented with  individual difference assumptions to account for several standard all-or-none learning demonstrations. The paper allows the  one element model (OEM) and the linear operator model (LOM) to have a completely arbitrary individual difference distribution.  Among the results are: 1. the LOM can not account for either the Rock replacement studies or Bower's constancy of the error  given error curve. 2. The OEM can account for Rock's and Bower's results only if there are no individual differences. 3. No  independent responding scheme can jointly predict Rock's and Bower's results. 4. A beta distribution on the learning rate  allows the LOM to achieve a close fit to at most one of Rock's and Bower's results. 5. An RT'"> experiment can not rule out an  independent responding scheme for any n, n >_ 1.  The major learning theories of the first half of the century provided many candidates for "atoms" of learning such as the  bond, the reflex, the habit, the operant, the expectancy, etc. (cf. Hilgard and Bower, 1966). Each of these proposed building  blocks of more complex learning had an associationistic basis, and with the exception of trivial situations, was thought to  develop gradually by increments over trials in the appropriate learning setting. However a number of researchers provided  evidence that simple associations were learned in an all-or-none manner, e.g., Rock (1957), Estes (1960), and Bower (1961,  1962). In each case the all-or-none evidence was argued by other theorists to be consistent with an incremental formulation  with individual differences in the learning rate, e.g., Williams (1961), Underwood, Rehula, and Keppel (1962), Postman (1963).  The counter argument that the all-or-none interpretation was simpler for these data was not that well accepted in light of the  great theoretical precommitment to incremental learning as well as the known aptitude and performance differences of the very  subjects in such experiments. A number of investigators reported data from simple human learning that tended to support the OEM over  the LOM (cf. The historical gap in statistical comparison of the incremental and all-or-none formulations in the context of individual  differences is the subject of this paper. Basically a comparative analysis of the LOM and OEM each augmented with an arbitrary  individual difference distribution is provided. The object of the analysis is to render certain formulations impossible rather  than merely implausible. The assumption is made that each organism's error-success protocol is generated by first randomly  sampling a learning rate from the individual difference distribution and then generating the error-success sequence from the  appropriate model with the sampled rate and the guessing rate fixed over all subjects by the experimental boundary conditions.  In addition to providing a resolution to an important issue in the earlier days of statistical learning theory, the paper is  relevant to the logic of proceeding with the formulation of more complex learning models from basic all-or-none building  blocks. Such has been the strategy sine 1965 as attested by numerous all-or-none motivated small state Markov models for  memory, concept identification, generalization, discrimination, avoidance, etc. (see Greeno and Bjork, 1973, for an extensive  review of these developments).  The paper proceeds by first presenting some preliminary definitions and facts about the augmented models. Then the augmented  LOM and OEM are analyzed in the context of Rock's (1957) and Bower's (1961, 1962) findings. Next the question of joint  individual difference distributions on the learning rate and the guessing rate is considered. Then the notion of an  "independent responding scheme" is defined with the property that the LOM with a joint distribution on its parameters is a  special case. A powerful theorem is established which shows that no independent responding scheme can jointly account for  Rock's and Bower's findings. Then the possibility of statistical approximation to these findings is discussed with a beta  distribution on the learning rate. Finally the Estes (1960) RTT findings are discussed. The LOM and OEM models were designed in part to reflect the simplest exemplars of incremental learning and all-or-none  learning, respectively. Also all-or-none theories for discrimination learning with non-resampling of hypotheses such as suggested by  Harlow's (1959) error factor theory would not have the feature that errors are recurrent events. Nevertheless, from a  mathematical standpoint, it can be argued that the LOM and OEM are the simplest nontrivial exemplars of their respective  theoretical families of models. ROCK REPLACEMENT STUDIES  Rock (1957) ran several two-group simple paired-associate learning experiments employing the study-test methodology. In the  experimental group, items that were missed on a test trial were replaced with fresh (unstudied) items on the next study trial.  The control group did not utilize the replacement strategy. Rock's conclusions were that the trials to criterion for both  groups were nearly identical which supported his notion of all-or-none learning which implied that a missed item had no  residual learning from prior study trials.
