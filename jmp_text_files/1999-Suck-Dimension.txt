The set of all knowledge spaces on a given set of items or questions is investigated with respect to order theoretic and metrical properties. It is argued that a generalization of properties of the subspaces of a finite dimensional Euclidean vector space yields the material for defining a metric which satisfies certain requirements. The fact that the lattice of knowledge spaces is not modular is the reason for most of the difficulties. It turns out that a Hausdorff metric based on the Hamming distance satisfies the postulated conditions. ] 1999 Academic Press 

1. A DIMENSION-RELATED METRIC FOR KNOWLEDGE SPACES 

Knowledge spaces can be thought of as a discrete analogue of the theory of psychological tests. They are useful for assessing the ``ability'' of a subject within a domain of knowledge, but also for diagnostic purposes in various fields. Doignon and Falmagne (1998) is a comprehensive presentation of its basic concepts, expounds the theory, and deals with various applications. 

Given a finite set Q; it consists of a series of questions, tasks, or items which the subject is confronted with. This set contains all the information for the analysis. The body of knowledge captured by Q carries a structure. In the simplest case��the one which seems to dominate classical psychological test theory��the items of Q can be weakly ordered according to difficulty. In the present theory of knowledge spaces a large variety of structures and dependencies within the items can be taken into consideration. To be more precise the following definition clarifies the scope of structures under scrutiny.

Condition (K2) of this definition is the only serious restriction. In Falmagne, Koppen, Villano, Doignon, and Johannesen (1990), and in many other papers on this area, the feasibility of this restriction and the unfeasibility (in general) of further requirements are discussed. Some authors purport arguments for occasionally dropping this condition, e.g., Du�ntsch and Gediga (1996); in particular, in connection with a skill structure Du�ntsch and Gediga (1995) argue in favor of a generalization. In the present paper, only knowledge spaces with the properties of Definition 1 will be regarded. Kn is the main object of investigation. 

One problem of applying knowledge space theory is to construct the particular K # Kn . Various procedures have been suggested: see Koppen and Doignon (1990), Dowling (1993a), and Koppen (1993). 

In many empirical applications one has to deal with several knowledge spaces. These can result from differing opinions or from errors experts show in querying procedures. Or they may reflect different samples of subjects. Recently Schrepp and Held (1995) published a simulation study in which they investigated how sensible the process of constructing a knowledge space reacts to such perturbations. Cosyn and Thie�ry (in press) is an interesting follow-up to this paper. 

In these investigations how to decide whether two knowledge spaces on the same set differ only in negligible details or whether they are fundamentally incompatible still remains problematic. Furthermore, if the differences are judged to be minor, what does the correct space look like? 

For questions of this kind the set Kn is the right domain of discourse. More specifically a metric is called for by which closeness of elements of Kn can be measured. 

Instead of an ad hoc definition of an index which seems plausible, a few basic reflections can be helpful. If Q were a psychological test, developed in a usual procedure of classical test construction, one would distinguish several factors. Some subsets of the items will be called ``subscales.'' The factors��in the sense of factor analysis��are independent, even orthogonal vectors of a vector space. The state of knowledge of a testee will be expressed by his or her factor values. The possible patterns of these values are what most resembles the states in knowledge space theory. If, for some reason or other, only a subset of factors is regarded one is essentially dealing with a subspace of the vector space spanned by all factors. Different subsets result in different subspaces. How would one combine the faculties expressed by two such subsets? By regarding the smallest subspace which contains both of them. How close are the original subspaces to one another? A possible measure is the dimension of the union minus the dimension of the intersection. (Note: the union is not a subspace; taking the linear hull of the union yields the required space; however, in terms of factors simply taking their union generates the subspace.) A measure of distance can be defined by taking the sum of the number of factors in each space minus the number common to both of them. 2. NOTATION AND PRELIMINARY RESULTS 

For the whole paper the particular questions or items which the knowledge spaces are made of do not matter. What matters is their number. Therefore the set Q is represented by [1, 2, ..., n]. The set of all knowledge spaces on Q is denoted by Kn . Denoting the power set of a set X by 2[X] we have Kn �2[2][Q] . 2.3. Hull Operators 

A hull operator on X is a mapping h: 2[X] � 2[X] such that for all A # 2[X] 

(1) A�h(A).

(2) A�B O h(A)�h(B), i.e., h is monotonic with �.

(3) h b h(A)�h(A).

These conditions immediately imply idempotence of h, i.e., h b h=h. 

Examples of hull operators are the convex hull, affine hull, and linear hull in the usual Euclidean space; but there are also very uninteresting ones such as h(A)=X for all A # 2[X] . In the next section an extension of a set of subsets of Q to a knowledge space, called its completion, will be of special interest (see Definition 3). Other systems of subsets of X satisfying certain requirements might be important. If A is a set of subsets of X, i.e., A�2[X] not fulfilling the respective requirements and there exists A$ with A�A$�2[X] satisfying the desired conditions one often constructs the smallest such A$. This operation occurs in topology, measure theory, etc; the completion of a set to a knowledge space is another example of this kind. This hull operator is most important for Section 3 and Section 4. 

It is worth mentioning that (a generalization of) a hull operator is sometimes called a ``closure relation.'' Closely related to these closure structures is abstract convexity, see van de Vel (1993). Hawrylycz and Reiner (1993) investigate the set of closure relations on a given partial order. This connection points to possible generalizations which might be useful when knowledge space theory seems inadequate, e.g., when the conditions of Definition 1 are too general or too restrictive.

5. DISCUSSION AND OPEN PROBLEMS 

The point made in the Introduction was to create a metric which generalizes properties holding in the lattice of subspaces of a finite dimensional vector space. The non-modularity of the lattice Kn is the main obstacle to carrying through this idea smoothly. However, the Hausdorff metric based on the Hamming distance saved an essential part of this project. Its origin in convex geometry points to possibly interesting generalizations. 

Systems of subsets of a given set closed under intersection have been investigated by many authors. When they contain the base set Q then such a system is called

a Moore family. The set of all Moore families on a set Q is closely related to Kn . Bernard Monjardet pointed out to me that results stronger than semimodularity are known for this set, e.g., Demetrovics, Libkin, and Muchnik (1992). Apparently, the most far-reaching result was recently obtained by Nathalie Caspard, drawing on the concept of dependence relations, cf. Monjardet and Caspard (1997). 

Many problems and open questions remain. A list of some of them follows: 

v Characterize all metrics on Kn satisfying (C1), (C2), and (C3). 

v Can the method of proof of Eq. (10) of Theorem 4 be used for more general subsets of 2[Q] such as in Eq. (9)? Such an estimation is of interest because some of the algorithms used in procedures to construct the knowledge space rely on the basis concept, e.g., Dowling (1993a). 

v Does (18) hold with a weaker condition on h and f\ ? A solution of this question can be of interest with respect to the previous problem because it may be possible to estimate d(B1 , B2) drawing on a stronger form of (18). 

v It is not obvious whether the Hausdorff metric is a good tool to handle careless errors (e.g., of an expert in a querying procedure).
