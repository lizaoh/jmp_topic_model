JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY: 5, 163-181 (1968)  Choice Behavior in a Markovian Decision Task'  AMNON RAPOPORT2  University of North Carolina, Chapel Hill, North Carolina 27514  Decision policies are suggested for a special class of multistage decision problems: Markovian decision processes with reward. The policies include a dynamic programming policy maximizing average expected reward, an expected value policy maximizing expected immediate reward, a minimax policy, and a minimax regret policy.  The decision policies were tested in a computer-controlled Markovian decision task. The dynamic programming policy accounted for the final decisions of many subjects relative to their estimates of the transition probabilities among the states of the Markovian decision process. A simpler model based on the estimates of the transition probabilities only did almost as well.  A distinction is made in decision theory among static, sequential, and multistage decision problems under uncertainty. A static decision problem is specified by S, a finite nonempty set of possible states of some system, A, a finite nonempty set of possible actions (decisions) available to the decision maker (DM), and r, the reward associated with each pair (s, a), an element of S X A. In static decision problems DM executes an action, receives a reward, and never makes another decision based on whatever he may have learned.  A sequential decision problem consists of static decision problems repeated periodically and independently. The DM makes a series of decisions, one on each stage. As more information is accumulated about the relative frequencies of the different states of the system obtained in the problem DM's decisions may improve. Learning in sequential decision problems is possible.  A multistage decision problem (a dynamic programming problem) is defined by S, A, p, r, where S, A are defined as above, p associates with each pair (s, a) a probability distribution p(- I s, a) on S, and r is the reward associated with the transition from one state to another. The system then moves to a new state s' (to be presented on the next stage) selected according to p(- I s, a), and DM receives the reward r(s, a, s') associated with this transition. DM's objective is to maximize his total expected reward over the infinite future. In multistage decision problems in contrast to sequential decision problems the probability distribution on S is not the same on each stage but is determined by DM's decisions on the preceding stages. To maximize total expected reward DM has to consider the effect of his decisions on the future behavior of the system.  Decision criteria have been offered to resolve the decision problem under uncertainty. Among others they include the minimax principle, and the minimax regret criterion. Psychological decision models typically consist of some version of an expectation model such as the EV model, and the SEU model, (Luce and Suppes, 1965). Other models, in particular the ones suggested for sequential decision problems, are based on modifications of stimulus-sampling theory and on Bayesian decision theory. A recent review of decision models and experimental work in static and sequential decision problems is given in Luce and Suppes (1965).  Multistage decision problems have received considerably less attention by psychologists than static and sequential decision problems. The reason for this is not because of lack of interest in such problems. Multistage decision problems are probably encountered more often in operational situations than static and sequential decision problems. Thus, military and business decisions are usually made dynamically, information available for later decisions is contingent on the consequences of earlier decisions, and implications of any decision reach far into the future. The reason that multistage decision problems have been relatively neglected by psychologists has to do with their complexity, with the difficulties in studying them experimentally, and in constructing models for describing human behavior in such problems.  The difficulties in constructing models for multistage decision problems are not insurmountable. Bellman (1957, 1961) has shown that dynamic programming may be used for constructing models prescribing optimal control of multistage decision processes. Rapoport (1966a, 1966b, 1967), Ray (1963), and Thomas (1962) have applied dynamic programming techniques to multistage decision tasks performed by human subjects. Discrepancies between observed responses and optimal decisions were noted which suggested modifications of the models.  There are several limitations of the above-mentioned studies of multistage decision tasks. The first has to do with the specificity and artificiality of the tasks. In all of these studies DM was instructed to minimize a quadratic cost function in a highly complex task. The second has to do with the fact that the dynamic programming formulations of these tasks are unsuitable as general models for human decisions in broad classes of problems. Another limitation is that no serious effort has been made to compare other decision criteria with the optimization models derived by dynamic programming. Probably the most important dynamic programming formulation which retains  CHOICE BEHAVIOR IN A MARKOVIAN DECISION TASK 165  computational feasibility and is sufficiently general to serve as a model for a wide class of interesting decision tasks is the decision model based on Markov processes (Howard, 1966). In this formulation, which has found applications in a variety of situations, the decision task is modeled as a finite-state Markov process that earns rewards as it makes transitions from state to state. It is the purpose of this study to apply the dynamic programming formalism together with Bayesian procedures to experimental Markovian decision tasks. Another purpose is to test a model prescribing an optimal decision rule against alternative decision criteria which have been suggested in the psychological literature. It is a decision policy derived from a normative model which prescribes optimization in a Markovian decision problem. The DPP is based on the assumption that both P and R are known. In many real life multistage decision problems this is a strong assumption to make. In particular, while R is typically known, P is not. Other Policies for Markovian Decision Tasks  The dynamic programming policy, DPP, is derived assuming that rr, the vector of limiting state probabilities is known or can be computed. This is also the case when Yn replaces P. The DPP is derived from a model assuming that DM behaves as if he can calculate the effect of his present decision on the future behavior of the process. To put it differently, DPP is derived from a model assuming that DM can project the effect of his present decision into the future, and that his horizon is infinite. This is not exact, as the Markov process will converge rather rapidly when N is small. Thus an infinite horizon should be replaced by a horizon with a finite duration, where the length of the duration is a function of P (or Yn).  Our knowledge of human information processing ability as well as direct experimental evidence (Rapoport, 196601) suggests that the above assumption might not be very realistic. An alternative model is then suggested which makes no assumption about the future behavior of the process. Specifically, another model is suggested assuming that DM does not consider the effect of his decision on the future behavior of the process, but selects his policy so as to maximize the expected immediate reward, qzk. One may recognize this model as a version of the EV model, or as a version of the SEV model when P is replaced by Yn . We shall refer to the policy derived from this model as the expected immediate value policy (EVP).  In many economic systems further cost or profit is discounted. A dollar gained or spent in the future is usually given less weight than a dollar gained or spent at the present time. Discounting causes no problem to the analysis of Markov processes with reward. The discount factor fl, 0 < g G 1, can be defined as the value at the beginning of a transition interval of a unit sum received at the end of the interval, and an iteration cycle for getting the optimal policy, given P, is provided (Howard, 1960). Typically, the discount factor is interpreted as the reciprocal of 1 plus the interest rate that is applicable to the time interval for a transition.  170 RAPOPORT  When discounting is considered EVP may be derived from DPP. The relation between DPP and EVP may be seen better when discounting is not present and 0 is interpreted in terms of the uncertainty concerning the duration of the process. Let /3 be defined as the probability that the process will continue to yield rewards after the next transition. Then 1 - g is the probability that the process will stop at its present stage. The EVP prescribes optimal behavior assuming that the process is going to terminate on the next stage with a probability equal to l . The DPP prescribes optimal behavior assuming that the process will continue earning rewards indefinitely. It should be noted that EVP does not necessarily differ from DPP. The difference between them is a function of P (or 1'.) and R. With certain P (or Y,,) and R, EVP may equal DPP for any value of g.  Two other very simple decision policies that have obtained considerable attention, may be suggested for the Markovian decision process. They are included here mainly because of their simplicity and familiarity. The first is the "local, pure" minimax decision policy, MP. The second is the minimax regret decision policy, MRP. Under MP an index is assigned to every alternative in every state i. This index for alternative k; is the minimum of the rewards r l , r 2 ,..., riN . The MP prescribes choosing for each state that alternative whose associated index is maximum, i.e., that alternative which maximizes the minimum reward.  Under MRP a new table of "regret" indexes, R*, is defined in terms of R. An element of R* r ~* is defined as the amount that has to be added to r2; to equal the maximum reward in the jth column of R. The MRP prescribes choosing for each state that alternative which minimizes the maximum regret index for each alternative.  Both MP and MRP, in contrast to DPP and EVP, do not take account of P or 1'.. These policies are determined as a function of R alone. Therefore, one would not expect MP and MRP to have a strong predictive power, unless P is unknown and the process has just started, or unless DM does not believe that the transition probabilities, are stationary.  METHOD  Subjects. The subjects, referred to as DM's, were 18 male undergraduates of the University of North Carolina. The DM's were tested individually. The experimental session lasted approximately 2 hours.  Task. The task was computer-controlled. The DM was seated in front of a Friden Flexowriter connected to an LGP-30 computer, and instructions were presented in printed form. The DM was told that he would be asked to make many decisions, one at a time, and that he would be paid according to the goodness of his performance. This reward represents the income from the trip after all necessary expenses (gas, oil, amortization, etc.) have been deducted. We noted above that it might be unreasonable to expect DM to follow MP or MRP at the end of the task. The DM would follow one of these policies only if he ignored whatever information he obtained throughout the experiment about the transition probabilities. One may expect that MP or MRP would be followed more frequently than other policies at the beginning of the task, where no information about P is known, and then abandoned as information about P is gradually acquired. To test this hypothesis the first policy used by each DM was examined. The first policy is defined as the first decision made by DM when first starting from B, M, and Q. None of the 18 first policies correspond to either MP or MRP. An inspection of later policies, based on the second decision made in each of the starting cities, the third decision, etc., yielded similar results. In short, it seems that MP and MRP were rarely used by DM's in either the beginning or the end of the experiment.  A more detailed analysis of the first decision made by DM's shows no evidence for rejecting the null hypothesis of a random selection of alternatives in each state. The decisions made by DM's while first starting from each of the three cities were summed over DM's in a 3 X 3 contingency table. The same was done separately for the second and third decisions. The null hypothesis was accepted in all three cases (X2 = 10.0, 4.67, 6.33, for the first, second, and third decision respectively, with p > .1). It was argued before (Rapoport, 1966a, 1967) that decision models prescribing optimal decisions could describe DM's decisions in complex multistage decision tasks if constraints imposed on DM's performance were explicity considered. The approach followed in these previous studies was to detect discrepancies between actual behavior and optimal behavior, assuming complete knowledge of the structure of the environment and unlimited ability to remember and process information. The discrepancies suggested constraints which were interpreted psychologically. The modification of the normative models by including these constraints yielded better agreement between predicted and observed decisions. The constraints included tendencies to overcontrol or undercontrol a multistage decision system, hypotheses concerning sequences of stochastic variables, and DM's ability to evaluate the effect of his present decision on the future behavior of the system.  The same approach has been followed in the present study where the main constraint is the replacement of the transition probabilities by their estimates. By replacing P by Yu, the final decisions of most DM's were accounted for. They behaved optimally or almost optimally in the second half of the final block of trials, relative to their estimates of the transition probabilities. With respect to the four policies considered in this study, DPP* was highly superior to either EVP*, MP, or MRP.  The DPP* as well as DPP may imply that DM behaves as if he can project the effect of his present decision indefinitely into the future. It was mentioned above that this implication is misleading, as the transition probabilities corresponding to a given policy will typically converge very rapidly to their limiting state probability distribution. This is particularly the case when the estimates of the transition probabilities are considered, as they are less extreme than the corresponding transition  180 RAPOPORT  probabilities.
