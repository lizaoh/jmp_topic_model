The Normal Distribution Derived from Qualitative Conditions 

Reinhard Suck 

University of Osnabru�ck 

The normal distribution is characterized in a measurement theoretic framework. The qualitative conditions guarantee that representations can be regarded as random variables. Additional axioms, also qualitative in the measurement sense, yield the normal. One characterization draws on a limit theorem. The main result derives the normal distribution from conjoint measurement axioms. This approach consists of formulating properties of a linear model as a component structure with error as one component. The normal distribution of errors is shown to be a consequence of the measurement theoretic assumptions. The possible impact of these results on statistical models is discussed. � 2001 Academic Press

There, to the contrary, the normal can even be derived from the requirement of error minimization. For instance, in one of the earliest occurences of the normal in history, namely that by Gau?, normality is a consequence of applying a criterion of minimizing measurement errors. 

Instances of (1) are naturally encountered when (X, Y ) enjoy a bivariate normal distribution, in which case the usual regression techniques are invoked, or in an analysis of variance where X takes on a few values and Y is the ``dependent'' variable. In the latter case a normality assumption concerning R or Y is usually added to the model. 

From the viewpoint of representional measurement theory there is a lot more involved in (1): If X and Y are numerical variables, which must be the case for (1) to make sense, then the variables X and R form an additive representation of something, for example, of a treatment and an abstract error component. In this paper I develop a set of qualitative assumptions that are sufficient for the treatment and the error to be scaled such that Eq. (1) is valid. The qualitative error model which will be formulated in Section 3.2 uses a conjoint measurement structure (A, E, � ), formulates qualitative assumptions, and ends up with a representation resembling a linear model. Somewhat surprisingly, the conditions which are needed to make R a random variable are so strong that the representation (1) almost implies the form of the distribution of R. More precisely, when a qualitative condition which takes care of the existence of the variance of R is met, then ``automatically'' the distribution is normal. 

To obtain this theorem the measurement theoretical framework to probabilize structures, developed in Suck (1989, 1998), must be reviewed. This will be done in Section 2. Another way to qualitatively characterize the normal within this general framework, this time drawing on a limit theorem, is presented in Section 3.1. The approach outlined above is the subject of Section 3.2. The variance condition will be commented on in Section 3.3. In Section 4 we take up the problem of error minimization and discuss the role of the main theorem, i.e., of Section 3.2, in this connection. The final section contains remarks on other possible ways to characterize the normal, on other approaches in probabilistic measurement, and on a few open problems.

This condition describes an asymptotic property and is clearly not testable. The only thing one can do with real data is to calculate a few instances and guess whether or not it seems plausible.

5. PROBABILISTIC MEASUREMENT AND ERROR THEORY 

Error theory has been a neglected area of representational measurement theory even though its importance is generally acknowledged��for instance, Luce and Narens (1994) devote several of their 15 open problems to this subject. 

However, probabilistic measurement, which should encompass error theory, has attracted a lot of research activity over the years. Falmagne (1976, 1980), Suppes (1973), and Suppes and Zanotti (1992) are but a few examples. Recent work on random utility theory such as Niedere�e and Heyer (1997) Regenwetter and Marley (in press) is connected with this question. The approach of Falmagne of probabilizing axioms of measurement comes closest to a statistical treatment in Iverson and Falmagne (1985). Suck (1997) takes up this theme in the context of random utility theory. The present paper belongs to probabilistic measurement in so far as it characterizes a distribution by qualitative conditions. It is, however, not concerned with axiom testing. Rather, it proposes a qualitative model of error and tries to push the theory as far as possible toward a viable theory of measurement error. Even in the best case, i.e., all remaining gaps can be closed and the scaling procedure can be formalized such that it yields sensible results, this theory will not test the axioms of an extensive structure, say, let alone the axioms of a probability difference structure. However, I cherish some hope that an error theory based on the notions of this paper may provide a rationale for somewhat improved use of statistical techniques such as the linear model. It is not the linear model which is in need of modification, it is the way in which it is applied in the social sciences. Specifically, it is mainly the quality of the data where there is ample room for improvement. Data obtained from operationalizations may or may not possess the quality needed for sophisticated statistical treatments. The example of Section 3.3 should make that point clear. But where are the criteria to draw the line between a reasonable application and a ``not-so-reasonable one''?

In closing I make a few remarks on generalizations and other approaches to characterizing the normal distribution. 

Improving on Theorem 2 is probably not very difficult if one draws on more refined limit theorems. One way may be of interest in the context of qualitative axiomatizations, namely, exploiting the potential of group representations and harmonic analysis on the automorphism group of a measurement structure. Suck (2000) provides such a framework, using the topology on this group, resulting in a generalization of the famous Alper�Narens Theorem. 

Finally, the point is made that the outline of error minimization described in Section 4.1 is applicable in a wide variety of situations. In particular, it appears to be a promising perspective in discrete cases such as those mentioned at the end of Section 4. The decision on H may be lead by convention, whereas the choice or construction of a metric may require some combinatorial efforts. But metrics on discrete structures are often the object of intrinsic and extrinsic; interest see e.g. Monjardet (1981) for partial orders and Suck (1999) for knowledge spaces.
