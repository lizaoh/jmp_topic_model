JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 14, 187-210 (1976)  Normative Location Theory: Placement in Continuous Space'  LAWRENCE HUBERT AND PATRICIA BUSK  Department of Educational Psychology, The University of Wisconsin at Madison, Madison, Wisconsin 53706  This paper selectively reviews an area of operations research, refered to as normative location theory, that deals with the  placement of objects in continuous space. The computational procedures discussed in this literature require initially that a  certain set objects be located a priori; the placement of the objects in a second set is then determined in such a way that  the total "interaction" among the fixed and variable objects is minimized. A number of strategies appropriate for different  distance functions are surveyed and a numerical example is given illustrating one particular iterative algorithm. Although the  current versions of these location methods depend upon more than the nonmetric information contained in the proximity measures  defined for all pairs of objects, they can be generalized, and consequently, several possible connections to nonmetric  multidimensional scaling are pointed out.  INTRODUCTION  The current interest in nonmetric multidimensional scaling (MDS) started with the basic work of Shepard in 1962 (Shepard,  1962a, 1962b). Several other major contributions followed including Kruskal's (1964a, 1964b) mathematical clarification of  Shepard's ideas and Guttman's (1968) distinctive conceptual and algorithmic orientation. It is assumed that the reader has  some familiarity with the basic motivation and mechanics of MDS, and instead, this paper will attempt to point out and  selectively survey a related area in operations research that has focused on several closely related problems. Apparently,  this latter literature dealing with what will be called normative location theory (NLT), and, in particular, normative  location in continuous space, has been developed parallel to and independently of the work on MDS and conversely. Recently,  however, researchers working in the NLT area have seen the relevance of the MDS literature for their own work (for instance,  see Lea, 1973), even though NLT is not generally recognized in turn by psychologists. In addition, a brief numerical example  is given in a later section that shows how one particular type of NLT procedure can be used with psychological data. More  significantly, this example also illustrates some of the difficulties that can be encountered in applying these strategies.  1 The research of the first author was supported in part by a Postdoctoral Research Training Fellowship from the Social  Science Research Council. The distinctive feature that identifies MDS is a reliance on only the rank order of the object  pairs obtained through a given set of proximity values; the distinctive feature of NLT, on the other hand, is the requirement  of an initial fixed set of objects in the same space in which other objects are to be placed. These differences become  somewhat blurred, however, when the actual mechanics of MDS and NLT are studied more closely. For example, in the general  two-phase algorithms for MDS discussed in detail by Lingoes and Roskam (1973), the major task of what is called phase I is to  place objects optimally in space with respect to a given set of "modified proximity values." The second phase merely defines  another set of "modified proximity values" and phase I is repeated. In short, the purpose of NLT is confined to a phase I task  of optimal object location but now in the presence of a second fixed set of objects in the same space. Interestingly, the  concept of using a fixed group of objects prior to the location of an additional set has also been suggested in the MDS  literature as a way of dealing with the placement of a large number of objects (for instance, see Lingoes and Roskam, 1973).  Many differences obviously exist between MDS and NLT such as the choice of optimality criterion and the actual optimization  procedure. Nevertheless, in many instances these discrepancies can be considered somewhat minor and result more from the  personal preferences of the developers rather than from any inherent constraints imposed by the context of the problem under  study.  With this initial motivation, the remainder of the paper will concentrate solely on NLT and the approaches developed by  researchers with this particular orientation. Hopefully, the major use of this survey will be in suggesting several ways of  developing or extending MDS strategies in the future, and thereby increasing both their effectiveness and general  applicability. The discussion below deals with the location of objects in a continuous space, and consequently, related areas  concerned with various discrete analogs are not reviewed. For several of these generalizations, consult Scott (1971), Francis  and White (1974), and Eilon, Watson-Gandy, and Christofides (1971). The amount of literature in operations research related to  the theory of location is enormous and many appropriate references are not included below. Since only part of the available  work is highlighted, the reader is referred to the bibliographies of Lea (1973) and Francis and Goldstein (1974) for many more  pertinent references. An interesting connection to MDS can now be made by observing that the original objective function in (1) has the general  unnormalized form of a goodness-of-fit (or "stress") statistic used by Guttman (1968). In Guttman's coefficient of  monotonicity t, the values assigned by s(-, -) can be obtained by what is referred to as a rank image principle during each  iteration of an MDS algorithm; the normalization is characterized through a denominator for tt defined by the square root of  the product of the sum of squares of the values assigned by s(-, -) and by d(-, -). This restriction is made both for historical  reasons as well as for simplicity of exposition; in the more general and current literature these restrictions are not made,  and usually, even in the older literature the suggested procedures can be trivially extended for p greater than 2. Random Location Problems. One of the most interesting extensions of the single variable object problem considers the positions  of the "fixed points" as being determined by given probability distributions (Katz & Cooper, 1974a,b; Seppala, 1975; Cooper,  1974). In other words, the variable point must be placed so as to minimize the sum of the expected values of the weighted  distances from x to al,..., a,n. It turns out that the objective function under weak regularity conditions on the density  functions is strictly convex, and furthermore, a natural generalization of the Euclidean iteration  196 HUBERT AND BUSK  algorithm is monotone (i.e., the objective function never increases as a function of the iteration number), globally  convergent to the unique minimum from any starting point, and finally, has a linear order of convergence. In Katz and Cooper  (1974a,b) the normal, exponential, and symmetric exponential bivariate densities are studied in detail, and in particular, it  is shown that a closed form iterative procedure is possible in the bivariate normal case when the coordinates are independent.  In addition, Katz and Cooper give an upper bound on the objective function in terms of the first two moments of the objective  function and the solution of the deterministic problem in which the "fixed points" are located at mean values for the  coordinates. Finally, Katz and Cooper suggest a procedure to accelerate convergence of the iterative algorithm, and discuss  under what circumstances the variable point will be in the convex hull defined by the mean coordinates for the "fixed points,"  e.g., in the independent bivariate normal case. Related work on a slightly different model using fixed regions instead of  fixed points is given by Love (1972), Bennett and Mirakhor (1974), Wesolowsky and Love (1971a), and Papineau, Francis, and  Bartholdi (1975).  THE LOCATION OF MULTIPLE OBJECTS  As to be expected, the location of multiple objects with the objective function of (3) is generally more involved than the  single object case. One particular instance, however, presents no added difficulty and may be disposed of immediately: If s(oi  , o;) = 0 whenever of and o; are variable, then (3) reduces to the solution of n separate single object problems. Obviously,  since this possibility is a very special case, we assume from now on that every variable object has a positive proximity to at  least one other variable object. This prevents the location of any variable object merely by a single variable object routine.  Also, for technical reasons (see Francis & Cabot, 1972), it is assumed that all variable objects are chained, i.e., if object  of is variable than there is an ordered sequence of distinct variable objects and some fixed object such that each adjacent  pair of objects has a strictly positive proximity value. Without this assumption, unchained objects can be placed arbitrarily  at a common point in the plane.  The most obvious and simplistic way to approach (3) is to allocate the objects one at a time, ignoring the proximities between  the variable objects, and repeating the process until no change takes place, i.e., until a local minimum is obtained (see  Vergin & Rogers, 1967; McHose, 1961). Although this procedure could obtain nonoptimal local minima (see Vergin & Rogers,  1967), the objective function in (3) is convex for the common distance functions that we consider, e.g., for the rectangular,  Euclidean, and squared Euclidean cases as well as for the Minkowski and power function models when q > 1. Consequently, the  "one at a time" procedure can be considered "close" to a coordinate search technique that terminates at local optima that are  also global optima. Nevertheless, just as in a general convex programming problem, it is possible  NORMATIVE LOCATION THEORY 197  to develop computationally more efficient search strategies than either a coordinate search or a "one object at a time"  strategy; these techniques are discussed in the subsections below.
