On Setting Response Criteria for Calibrated Subjective Probability Estimates 

Hongbin Gu and Thomas S. Wallsten 

University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill 

Framing the issue of subjective probability calibration in signal-detectiontheory terms, this paper first proves a theorem regarding the placement of well-calibrated response criteria and then develops an algorithm guaranteed to find such criteria, should they exist. Application of this algorithm to tasks varying in difficulty and number of response categories shows that perfect calibration is easiest to attain under median difficulty levels (d$r1.4) and is practically or theoretically impossible to attain when the task is either very hard (d $r0.5) or very easy (d$r10). Implications for calibration research, including the hard�easy effect, are discussed. � 2001 Academic Press 

INTRODUCTION 

Since the 1980s a large body of literature on subjective probability judgment has been devoted to the issue of calibration (Budescu 6 Wallsten, 1987; Juslin, Olsson, 6 Winman, 1998; Keren, 1991; Lichtenstein, Fischhoff, 6 Philips, 1982), which is of both theoretical and applied significance. Accurate calibration requires that forecasts made with probability P occur with relative frequency P. 

With a few exceptions of accurate calibration, such as weather forecasters (e.g., Murphy 6 Brown, 1984), studies show that, in general, people tend to underestimate the uncertainty of their predictions. Theoretical explanations of this ``overconfidence'' phenomenon include the suggestions that people are biased to retrieve supporting rather than contradicting evidence from memory (Koriat, Lichtenstein, 6 Fischhoff, 1980), that they unduly simplify the task due to limited cognitive capacity (Sniezek, Paese, 6 Switzer, 1990), that they set inappropriate response criteria (Budescu, Wallsten, 6 Au, 1997; Ferrel 6 McGoey, 1980), and that the overconfidence is an artifact of non-representative sampling of test items (Gigerenzer, Hoffrage, 6 Kleinbo�lting, 1991). 

Another well-documented phenomenon is the covariation between task difficulty and over�under confidence. Typically, people tend to be overconfident when the task is hard and underconfident when it is easy. In binary tasks, the crossover from

In this paper, instead of attempting yet another psychological explanation, we explore sufficient normative conditions for calibration. The work proceeds by first adopting a signal-detection-theory framework for defining calibration. It then introduces a normative search algorithm for finding well-calibrated response criteria, and shows that the algorithm is sufficient to find the family of solutions if it exists. Applying the algorithm to tasks varying in difficulty and number of response categories, we show that perfect calibration is easier to achieve in some cases than in others and that under some conditions it is impossible. We conclude with a discussion of the implications of our results for theories of calibration, the relationship between our and other investigators' approaches to the issue of calibration, and ways to apply our algorithm more broadly than we do here. Our work formalizes and generalizes the approach first introduced by Budescu et al. (1997). 

2. BACKGROUND AND NOTATION 

Models by Ferrell and McGoey (1980) and Wallsten and Gonza�lez-Vallejo (1994) extend signal detection theory (SDT) as a general framework for judgment about event outcomes or the truth of statements. A typical signal-detection scenario includes classifying each of a set of stimuli as one of two events, typically called signal and noise. According to SDT, each stimulus is sampled from one of two populations defined in terms of overlapping distributions along a decision variable. This theoretical framework captures the uncertainties in a broad range of binary judgment tasks, including, for example, judging ``true'' from ``false'' general knowledge statements, discriminating tumor from a non-tumor in a medical context, or discriminating ``guilty'' from ``non-guilty'' (see Fig. 1). We will generally use the term ``signal'' for target events or true statements, and ``noise'' for nontarget events or false statements. 

According to SDT, for binary responses respondents set a response criterion along a decision variable and classify the stimulus as a ``signal'' if its value on the variable exceeds the criterion or as ``noise'' otherwise. The resulting pattern of response conditional frequencies is sufficient to estimate the two parameters of sensitivity and criterion location under the assumption of equal-variance normal distributions. The sensitivity is measured by the distance between the means of the signal and noise distributions, usually normalized by the standard deviation of the noise distribution. The location of the criterion shows the tendency, or bias, to call a stimulus a ``signal'' or ``noise.'' 

The paradigm is generalized from binary judgments to ranking or rating scales when respondents are further required to give a probability or confidence estimate. In some cases, they give a binary choice first and then rate their confidence with a number from .50 to 1. In others, they evaluate directly, from 0 to 1, that the current stimulus is a signal. 4. EMPIRICAL ISSUES 

The theorem and lemma indicate the constraints under which a set of criteria can be well calibrated given (1) monotonic strictly increasing likelihood ratios, L(X), and (2) a set of N+1 labeled response categories such that R0<R1< } } } < RN+1<RN . 

What remains is to develop algorithms that can systematically search along the decision variable in a method dictated by these constraints to find well-calibrated criteria, should a set exist, or to determine that no such set exists. Note that when a set of well-calibrated criteria exists, there could be an infinite number of sets, which will differ from each other to a larger or smaller degree. 

To see this last point, return to the example of locating C7 between G[&1] (.60) and G[&1] (.70). The setting C7, of course, depends on the value of C6 for the category R6=.60 to be calibrated. For each C6 (G[&1] (.50)<C6<G[&1] (.60)), either there will be a unique C7 satisfying the constraints or there will be none. Thus, for C6=G[&1] (.50)+$, there may exist a C7. Assume there is such a value, and call it C$, then for C6=G[&1] (.5) +2$ there may exist a new C7>C$. Thus there may be a range of values for C6 , which in turn determines a range of values for C7 , etc. Should this process be successful for all Cj , j=1, ..., N, well-calibrated criteria exist. If the distance between the lowest and highest setting of each criterion, C1, C2 , ..., CN , in the acceptable set is great, then the sets may differ from each other to a large degree.

5. ALGORITHM AND RESULTS 

The algorithm we illustrate exhaustively searches all of the possible solutions within the acceptance region defined by Lemma 1. Subject to the precision employed, the algorithm is sufficient to find optimal solutions for calibration if they exist. To implement the assumption of a continuous, monotonically strictly increasing likelihood ratio, we define the noise and signal distributions in the conventional way as normal, equal variance with different means. Specifically, we used the two distributions, identical except for shifted locations, N(&0.5, _[2] ) and N(0.5, _[2] ) for noise and signal, respectively. Prior probabilities of noise and signal are held equal in all that follows. 

To explore the issues just raised, we fixed the population means and controlled task difficulty (d$) by manipulating the variance. The origin was located at the intersection of two populations and the distance between the means was taken as one unit for scaling other measurements. While we believe this procedure is more convenient for comparing criteria locations across conditions, people may find it different from the common practice in SDT, which sets the origin at the mean of noise population and measures d$ in the unit of the standard deviation of the noise distributions. To avoid confusion, we represent results in both ways when necessary. 

With or without .50 included as a category, all of our discussion below assumes symmetric responses about .50. For example, .20, .40, .50, .60, and .80 is a symmetric 5-category set that includes .50; and .10 and .90 is a symmetric 2-category set that does not include .50. Psychologically, asymmetric criteria represent a bias toward one type of event over the other. The topic is beyond the scope of the paper, and we utilize the simplicity gained by assuming symmetry. Under our assumption, it is sufficient to find the criteria on only one side of the origin and to locate the other side as their negative counterparts.

5.2. Other Numbers of Response Categories that Include a Midcategory of .50 

Three response categories might be the simplest among settings including .50. If the two end categories are symmetric there will always exist a unique pair of criteria that yield perfect calibration, because the relative probability function illustrated by Theorem 1 is continuous and monotonically increasing from 0 to 1. In practice, however, the calibration may be hard to achieve. For example, when the task is very difficult the normative criteria for extreme responses, say, 2 0 or 98 0, might be 10 or 20 standard deviation units away from the intersection. Moreover, no solution yielding global calibration is possible if the two end categories are asymmetric, because the .50 category cannot be calibrated in that case. 

Turning, now, to 5 and 21 response categories, we applied the same algorithm to the same levels of difficulties as those illustrated in Table 1. The responses used in the 5-category setting were 0, .30, .50, .70, and 1.00. For 21 categories, the responses were 0, 0.05, ..., 0.95, 1.00. Table 2 lists the range of solutions for the first criterion to the right of the intersection, under the three numbers of response categories 5, 11, and 21. Those for 11categories are repeated from Table 1 for convenience. The broadest range in each case is printed in bold-face type. 

The same pattern of results found for 11 response categories holds for 5 and 21. The range of solutions is narrow for both the easy and difficult tasks and wider in the middle. The widest range of solutions happens at the difficulty level of about d$=1.6 for 21 response categories, d$=1.5 for 11 categories, and d$=0.9 for 5 categories. Thus, as the number of response categories decreases, optimal conditions for perfect calibration occur at increasingly difficult tasks. 

With 5 and 21 response categories, we also replicate our findings with 11 categories regarding the existence of solutions. Although limited to a small range, solutions are possible for easy tasks given a sufficiently small step size, $. But for very difficult tasks no solution exists. The upper limit of difficulty level also varies with the number of response categories. No solution is found for tasks more difficult than d$ of .60 for 5 response categories, d$ of .50 for 11 categories, and d$ of .61 for 21 categories. We do not understand what causes the variation among different number of categories and why it is not monotonic with number of categories.

6. DISCUSSION 

6.1. The Present Results 

In an experiment including confidence ratings, responses are usually prompted in categories. For example, an experimenter may ask the subject ``to report confidence as 50 0, 60 0, 700, 80 0, 90 0, or 1000.'' Studies also show that subjects in 

561 

SETTING RESPONSE CRITERIA FOR CALIBRATION 

general chose to respond in such categories even if no explicit instructions ask them to do so. 

Our algorithm shows that in typical SDT situations, the criteria solutions for calibration depend on both the task difficulty and the number of response categories. We did not explore the effect of different response labels because the sets we used are almost universally applied. In some of the combinations studied, calibration is simply impossible. For example, a subject cannot be well calibrated if he�she only uses extreme categories, like 10 0 and 90 0, for a difficult task. 

Even when solutions exist, their acceptable range is wider in some cases than in others. The results from our simulation show that it is easiest to be calibrated at medium difficulty levels. We explored first a typical situation including 11 response categories from 0 to 1 with intervals of .10. The width of the solution region is relatively wide at medium difficulty levels, with d$ from roughly 1 to 2, and is widest with d$ in the neighborhood of 1.4. This pattern is replicated for 5 and 21 response categories. 

We found no solution for very easy and very difficult tasks. Further explorations showed this to be true for different reasons. When we used a higher precision, that is, employed a smaller step in the search, we always found solutions for the easy tasks, although, practically, it may not be possible for respondents to do so. But for very difficult ones we found no solutions regardless of the precision used, because the proportion of signals per interval simply did not reach the level required by the high confidence ratings. 

The spread of acceptable criteria sets gives us another plausible explanation for the hard�easy effect. The criteria tend to cluster near the center for easy tasks and to spread out for difficult ones. With the example of 11 response categories, the distance between the first right criterion C6 and the last C10 is 0.1 for an easy task (d$=5), as compared to 10 for a difficult one (d$=0.5). If an average respondent naturally assumes criteria to be evenly spread over a reasonable range, she will appear overconfident for difficult tasks and underconfident for easy ones. 

We are not arguing that task characteristics are the only or the most important factors for poor calibration. Our investigation, rather, illustrates the constraints they impose on calibration, which must be considered when forming and evaluating psychological explanations.

6.3. Generalizing the Algorithm 

All of our results assumed two a priori equally likely equal-variance normal distributions for the signal and noise populations. Theoretically, for any set of response categories and pair of underlying signal and noise distributions, we can solve for optimal criteria by working analytically with the cumulative distribution functions. Solutions might be possible, for example, for some special member of the exponential family. But, in general, the task is daunting when the number of response categories is greater than two. Moreover, in many cases, such as for normal distributions, the analytic form of the CDF does not exit at all. Then a computational algorithm becomes necessary. 

Our algorithm can be applied to all situations for which the assumption of monotonic likelihood ratio holds. This assumption is very general and is valid for quite a number of typical SDT scenarios. A parallel development for the nonmonotonic situation would be more complicated.
