“Religion, politics and formula scoring are areas where two informed people often hold opposing ideas with great assurance” ( Lord, 1975 , p. 7).

Subjects’ decisions in multiple-choice tests constitute an interesting case of decision making under uncertainty. When a multiple-choice test is graded with a scoring rule that penalizes for wrong answers, a single item can be viewed as a lottery. The exam taker can always omit an item and get a sure reward (typically zero points) or take the risk of answering and get either a positive reward with certain probability or a penalty with the complementary probability.

Although attitudes toward risk in decision making scenarios have been extensively analyzed, (e.g.,  Weber, 2010 ), very little attention has been paid to subjects behavior in multiple-choice testing. The available evidence suggests that risk attitudes are different across domains.

                      See  Dohmen et al. (2009)  and  Weber, Blais, and Betz (2002)  for an analysis of risk attitudes in different contexts: financial, health/safety, recreational, ethical, social, sports, leisure and career decisions. 
                    Thus, the analysis within the context of multiple-choice tests is interesting if only because it entails different considerations from other domains. The analogy between multiple-choice tests and lotteries generates a large number of interesting questions. For instance, the probability of obtaining a correct answer clearly depends, among other factors, on the knowledge of subjects. Therefore, the analysis of subjects’ behavior in multiple-choice tests provides a scenario for studying the relationship between risk attitudes and knowledge.

We combine Decision Theory (DT) and Item Response Theory (IRT) into a model of subjects’ behavior in multiple-choice tests where risk attitudes play an important role. Our model constitutes a theory of rational behavior of multiple-choice test takers. A rational test taker in our model is expected to answer items to maximize the expected utility derived from the score obtained. This calculation is difficult to perform for various reasons. First, it requires estimates of the probabilities of answering items correctly. Second, the analytic solution to the problem is not straightforward and can only be attempted numerically. Of course, subjects taking multiple-choice tests are in no way assumed to literally perform such calculations in real situations. The model description of subjects’ behavior is meant to be an explanatory tool, not a description of their cognitive decision process. Our model of rational behavior should be understood as a theory and not a rule of behavior (e.g.,  McKenzie, 2003 ).

On the other hand, the model can be considered a benchmark against which other models of behavior can be compared and tested. An interesting feature of the model is that it can easily accommodate deviations from the perfectly-rational benchmark scenario. We exploit this flexibility of our model and explore cases where individuals are not perfectly rational.

A multiple-choice test is a collection of items and in our framework it is viewed as a set of compound lotteries, i.e., lotteries with payoffs which are themselves lotteries. Optimal behavior requires choosing the best compound lottery in this set, a computation that is very unlikely to be explicitly carried out by a test taker. Previous research, (e.g.,  Bereby-Meyer, Meyer, & Flascher, 2002 ), has considered item-by-item optimality, that is, subjects choose whether to answer an item (a single lottery) at a time, independently of the decisions in other items. Item-by-item calculations can be view as an adaptive mechanism or local goal approximation to an in-class computationally intractable global goal (e.g.,  Charter & Oaksford, 2000 ).

By providing a framework for rational choice analysis within multiple-choice tests, our model can be used to study the optimality of correction for guessing scoring rules. There is a long-standing debate in educational measurement literature as to whether the optimal scoring procedure is to penalize for incorrect answers or not (e.g.,  Bar-Hillel, Budescu, & Attali, 2005; Diamond & Evans, 1973; Frary, 1988 ). We contribute to this debate by providing an answer that considers subjects not as passive decision makers who follow a behavioral rule (as in previous work on educational measurement) but who react to the scoring procedure according to their risk attitudes. We also check that the results hold when individuals are not fully rational.

Multiple-choice tests are widely used as an evaluation tool.

Building on IRT, we propose a theory of students’ behavior in multiple-choice tests that might explain why they may find it optimal to answer some items and leave others blank.

Evidence on this was provided by  Bliss (1980)  who administered a MCT under formula scoring (penalty) where some subjects left questions unanswered. When time was up, he allowed subjects to circle the correct answer in those questions left unanswered, this time with no penalty for wrong answers. He found that subjects who omitted under formula scoring would have chosen more correct answers than under chance expectations.  Highham (2007)  ran two experiments using questions from actual SAT tests and with different penalty for errors (no penalty, penalty = 0.25 and penalty = 4); after the SAT was completed subjects were required to complete questions that were originally left unanswered using a pen with red ink (forced report condition). Comparing the free report condition to the forced report condition,  Highham (2007)  found that the decrease in the number of omissions increased both the raw and the corrected score and to a greater extent in the group facing the high penalty. These results are consistent with previous work on the accuracy of withheld answers ( Bliss, 1980; Cross & Frary, 1977; Ebel, 1968; Harden, Brown, Biran, Dallas Ross, & Wakeford, 1976; Muijtjens, van Mameren, & Hoogenboom, 1999; Sanderson, 1973; Sax & Collet, 1968; Sherriffs & Boomer, 1954; Slakter, 1968a,b ). This evidence is not consistent with expected score maximizers.

A possible explanation of this behavior can be advanced using Decision Theory under uncertainty (DT), (e.g.,  Arrow, 1971; Friedman & Savage, 1948; Pratt, 1964; von Neumann & Morgenstern, 1944 ). Unless a subject is completely sure about which of the alternatives is the correct answer, the decision whether to answer an item or leave it blank involves some uncertainty. Subjects face the dilemma of choosing between omitting and getting no reward or answering and bearing the risk of being penalized. Within the DT framework, subjects’ attitudes toward risk can be classified into three categories: risk aversion, risk neutrality and risk seeking. A test taker who omits an item with positive expected reward is called risk averse. Those who answer all items with positive expected reward are labeled risk neutral. A risk seeking individual would answer items with negative expected reward. Risk aversion is a type of behavior that is observed in many real situations. A leading example of risk aversion is the fact that people choose to buy insurance to hedge against several risks such as a car accident, house fire or burglary. However, within educational measurement literature, most papers assume that the test taker is an expected score maximizer and therefore risk neutral (e.g.,  Budescu & Bar-Hillel, 1993 ). If subjects’ goal were to maximize the expected score, they would answer whenever the expected value of answering turned out to be higher than or equal to the payoff for omitting. However, the previously mentioned evidence indicates that examinees do not behave as expected score maximizers. Moreover, although the experiments were not aimed at eliciting the students’ attitudes toward risk the results can be interpreted as  prima facie  evidence of risk aversion.

Other possible explanations for this evidence exist: the influence of directions given before the exam (e.g.,  Budescu & Bar-Hillel, 1993 ), framing of the scoring rule (e.g.,  Bereby-Meyer et al., 2002 ) and personality (e.g.,  Avila & Torrubia, 2004 ). However, in this paper we will develop an explanation based on the idea that under formula scoring rational examinees may try to avoid the risk of being penalized by omitting some items in which they have partial knowledge.

When students omit items with positive expected score, it is reasonable to argue that correction for guessing may discriminate against them and, therefore, to recommend not to use formula scoring (penalty) but number right scoring (no penalty). Formula scoring may reduce the measurement error due to guessing only at the expense of introducing some bias in the scores of those who omit. However, number-right scoring induces students to answer all questions and the bias disappears. Moreover, the measurement error can be reduced by administering tests with a large number of questions, although in practice, time constrains may preclude the use of a large test. Increasing the number of distractors would also be a means to reduce guessing but sometimes the question does not admit a large number of alternatives.

The debate on whether to use formula scoring or number right scoring is not settled. Early studies by  Edgington (1965)  and  Little (1966)  recommended number right scoring while  Cureton (1966)  and  Davis (1967)  argued in favor of formula scoring.  Sabers and Feldt (1968) , on the other hand, found that validity and reliability did not exhibit significant differences for correction or non-correction. Face validity however would favor correction for guessing.

In this paper we develop a theoretical framework to analyze the optimal correction for guessing, which incorporates a model of students’ behavior combining IRT and DT. The model can be solved by simulation and yields some support to the use of formula scoring. The reason can be found in the following trade-off: under no penalty (or a low penalty) for wrong answers, students have an incentive to guess, thus increasing measurement error, but a high penalty does not discourage guessing uniformly: students with high risk aversion are more easily discouraged, so penalties introduce a bias in favor of risk takers. Therefore, if risk aversion is correlated with gender, knowledge or social group, a high penalty would discriminate against these groups of students, (e.g.,  Prieto & Delgado, 1999 ). Although the theoretical framework developed in this paper allows us to make some claims about the desirability of correcting for guessing, the actual endorsement of these claims should await for field experimentation.

We begin the analysis using the binary knowledge framework of  Guttman et al. (1950) . Under this setting, a student knows the correct answer or cannot rule out any distractor as incorrect. Unrealistic as it is, this model is helpful to understand the more realistic framework of partial knowledge, i.e., when a student can rule out some of the distractors as incorrect, but cannot select the correct answer among the remaining ones. The binary knowledge assumption allows us to illustrate the trade-off between bias and measurement error and how the choice of the penalty level affects that trade-off. In this framework we introduce the basic concepts borrowed from DT and are able to state and prove three propositions regarding the optimal scoring in MCT. When we relax the assumption of binary knowledge and allow for partial knowledge the model becomes more complex and an analytical solution cannot be found. We then proceed to obtain a numerical solution by simulation.

This paper formalizes the idea that under formula scoring students’ behavior in terms of the number of omissions depends on the penalty: the higher the penalty the larger the number of omissions. Rational but cautious students may leave questions blank even though the expected score is positive. This theoretical result is consistent with the experimental evidence ( Bliss, 1980; Cross & Frary, 1977; Ebel, 1968; Harden et al., 1976; Highham, 2007; Muijtjens et al., 1999; Sanderson, 1973; Sax & Collet, 1968; Sherriffs & Boomer, 1954; Slakter, 1968a,b ). To introduce this idea in a formal model we use the theory of decision under uncertainty (e.g.,  Arrow, 1971; Pratt, 1964; von Neumann & Morgenstern, 1944 ) which explains why individuals may behave cautiously and, for example, be willing to buy insurance. According to this theory, subjects may not behave in order to maximize expected payoffs; rather, individuals may be willing to pay a risk premium to avoid bearing too much risk.

Combining IRT and the theory of decision under uncertainty, we have developed a model of students’ optimal behavior. First, we introduce this idea in a binary knowledge model where examinees either know the answer or take a pure guess. In a binary knowledge model, the examiner can set the exam to get a perfect correlation between knowledge and scores. We then analyze a partial knowledge model where subjects can sometimes rule out some of the distractors but are not sure which of the remaining choices is the correct one. In the partial knowledge model the examiner cannot set the exam to guarantee that the correlation will be one; nevertheless, the exam can be set to maximize the correlation between scores and knowledge levels. Our simulations indicate that when subjects are assumed to act rationally and asses the probability of a correct answer accurately, the optimal value of penalty is relatively high and the optimal mean difficulty coincides with the mean level of knowledge. This last result is consistent with  Felt (1993)  and  Lord and Novick (1974)  who have dealt with the question of optimal item difficulty and estimated it to maximize test score variance. These results are robust to a number of changes of the benchmark model (different normalizations of the score, positive or negative correlation between the student’s knowledge and cautiousness and to a pass–fail break point) and also to different assumptions on the discrimination parameter, the variance of the error term and the mean and variance of the distribution of knowledge and difficulties.

We have extended the analysis to two settings where examinees are not perfectly rational or have limitations on processing capacity: (i) when subjects cannot asses their level of knowledge accurately and (ii) when examinees use local optimization rules as an approximation to a global goal. However, these deviations from the perfectly rational setting do not alter the main results of our analysis.

Our model provides a framework for further research on the optimality of different features of multiple-choice tests. In particular, the model could be easily adapted to incorporate the extended IRT model of  San Martín, del Pino, and De Boeck (2006)  where the guessing parameter (or probability of correct guess) does not only depend on the item, but also on the ability of individuals. The model could easily be extended to account for different deviations from the assumption of expected utility maximization, for instance, considering that the utility of rewards is smaller than the disutility of penalties ( Bereby-Meyer et al., 2002; Kahneman & Tversky, 1979 ) or considering generalizations of expected utility theory ( Mura, 2009 ).

Our results raise some interesting questions for further research. First, field experiments should be performed to verify whether or not our findings have any empirical relevance.

Second, IRT ignores the fact that, when a penalty scoring rule is used, subjects optimally decide the number of omissions. Our contribution to this line of research is the point that risk aversion determines the number of omissions and that the corresponding parameter should also be estimated. IRT treats omissions in different ways: (i) missing by design, (ii) not-reached items, (iii) imputing partial credit and (iv) imputing omissions as incorrect. None of these procedures is appropriate when omission is due to risk aversion. Conceivably, a mixed DT–IRT model could be used to estimate risk and knowledge parameters jointly. In other words, decision theory should be incorporated to IRT model to yield a correct result.

Third, the idea of test-takers as decision makers faced with uncertainty has been considered recently in the literature on choice response time modelling (see  Bogacz, Brown, Moehlis, Holmes, & Cohen, 2006 ).
