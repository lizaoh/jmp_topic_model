Order Preservation of PEST Determined Certainty Equivalents: A Test Based on Lotteries with a Fixed Preference Strength 

Robert Sneddon 

California Institute of Technology 

A major assumption of many utility theories is that preferences over lotteries are monotonic in consequences. Under additional reasonable conditions, this means that the choice certainty equivalent (CE) of a lottery should be substitutable for the lottery, but some empirical evidence has suggested otherwise. So several reasonable conditions need to be studied. They are monotonicity of consequences, order preservation of CEs, and bias in estimates of CEs. This paper addresses the second assumption. To do this an algorithm is devised that generates pairs of lotteries with exactly the same preference probability of choice. This permits one to average over both subjects and lotteries. With a sample size of 102, there was no evidence that order preservation of CEs is violated. © 2002 Elsevier Science (USA) 

INTRODUCTION 

Utility theory attempts to describe individual decisions under risk or uncertainty, where ‘‘risk’’ is used when the events that control consequences occur with well specified probabilities and ‘‘uncertainty’’ is used when such probabilities either do not exist or are not estimatible. See, e.g., Quiggin (1982). Uncertain alternatives are often called lotteries. A lottery is defined to be a finite set of possible events , and to the occurence of each such event is assigned a valued entity—often money—that is called a consequence. [In some theories, e.g., Luce (2000), one has compound lotteries in which the consequences are themselves lotteries. Such compound lotteries play no role in this paper.]

The question arose for the following reason. Cho and Luce (1995) pointed out that the question of consequence monotonicity of lotteries under certain assumptions can be reduced to a simple formula involving certainty equivalents. Cho and Luce (1995) checked the formula empirically using an up–down sequential method called PEST which had been adapted from psychophysics in earlier papers. They found the formula to be rejected, which they interpreted as meaning either consequence monotonicity is incorrect or that CE s are not order preserving. Later Sneddon pointed out that there really is a third possibility, namely, that the PEST estimates of CE s are biased. So three studies were undertaken. Cho and Fisher (1999) studied consequence monotonicity using repeated direct choices, and they seemed to sustain it. Sneddon (2000) showed by using simulations that, indeed, the PEST procedure as implemented in that study induces small biases, and this may well have been the source of the problem. The remaining question addressed in this paper is whether PEST determined CE s can be shown to be order preserving. We will conclude that there is no evidence against this hypothesis. 

One major difficulty in doing this arises from the empirical fact that when the subjective value of two lotteries is very close, subjects’ responses are not consistent, and when the values are sufficiently different that the responses are consistent, the fact that order is preserved is trivial. So, we must confront the fact that in the domain of interest, the behavior is probabilistic, not algebraic. We first take up the issues involved in replacing Eq. Experimental Test of CE Substitutability 

The problem to be faced in testing the substitutability hypothesis is how to collect sufficient data to get good estimates of both P(f, g) and P[ CE (f), CE (g)]. To do so by just repeatedly presenting f and g and also repeatedly offering choices of the estimated values of CE(f) , and CE(g) is almost bound to fail for reasons irrelevant to the hypothesis. The former repetition runs into the problem that, even with quite a few pairs of lotteries intermixed, the subjects may recall their responses. The second repetition would necessarily entail unique estimates of the CE s, not random variables, and the choice between numbers will not be statistical. And yet we know that these CE s are only samples of what are really random variables and so were we to repeatedly estimate them we would find that which seems larger would vary. 

The idea of the present experiment is to bypass these two difficulties as follows. Suppose that we begin with pairs of initial lotteries (f0i, g0i), where i=1, ..., S= the number of initial stimulus pairs. And suppose we can devise a sequential procedure of adjusting the lotteries in a pair separately for each subject j where j=1, ..., N= the total number of subjects, so that the procedure converges to a pair of lotteries (fij, gij) for which P(fij, gij)=l, where l is a fixed numerical

The 84% Algorithm 

The details of this algorithm are somewhat involved; however, it is conceptually simple. The underlying principle of this algorithm is that subjects will choose between a pair of lotteries which have had their consequence values altered according to a subject’s history of choices. (This algorithm is an adaptive procedure.) Specifically, the value of the first consequence will be raised in one lottery and lowered in the other, or vice versa. Furthermore, the amount of this change will be increased or decreased according to a subject’s history of choices. Thus, a subject’s choices will eventually lead to a convergence point, i.e., a point where the consequences are being changed by a very small amount. At this point, the adaptive procedure is finished. 

An experimenter can choose a specific convergence point by deciding how much of a subject’s choice history is examined by the algorithm, as well as the algorithm’s responses to this choice history. That convergence point was hypothesized to be the point where a subject was as likely to make a gamble increase in value as to make it decrease (Rendleman, Rose, and Teller, 1970) . At this convergence point, one can estimate the probability of choosing one lottery over the other. This is called the choice probability. The details of estimating the choice probability are given the Appendix, Part (v). A value of l=0.84 was chosen for this experiment because it is above pure chance ([1] 2[)][, but not too close to 1.] 

These are the essentials of the algorithm’s design. The details below and in the Appendix, Part (v), are largely specific techniques used to ensure stable convergence of this algorithm. These details are given a mathematical justification in the Appendix, but their final justification was done by computer simulations.

Computer Simulations of the 84% Algorithm 

The validity of these assumptions was tested by conducting computer simulations of the 84% algorithm. A subject’s choices were simulated by Gaussian random variables. Specifically, the value of a lottery was simulated by a Gaussian distribution with a mean at the weighted expected value of the gamble. The weighted expected value is: CE(x, p; y)=w(p) x+w(1−p) y . Here, w(p) is a generic weighting function and should not be confused with the weighting function used in weighted utility theories. The weights took on four forms: over-weighting, w(p)=p[.5] ; underweighting, w(p)=p[1.5] ; Tversky and Kahneman’s inverse-S function, w(p)=p[.5] /(p[.5] +(1−p)[.5] )[1/.5] (see Tversky and Kahneman, 1992; or simply the probability, w(p)=p . At any step in the algorithm, the lottery with the highest value was chosen. The simulated subject’s standard deviation was set to $10.

THE EXPERIMENT 

Since computer simulations demonstrated the efficacy of the 84% algorithm, the 84% algorithm was used to test the substitutability of CEs. 

Procedure 

Participants. Eighteen students at the University of California, Irvine, participated. Each participant received $21 dollars for 3 one-hour sessions. 

Stimuli to be adjusted. The stimuli were presented on a computer screen. In the first experimental condition, designed to arrive at stimuli so that P(f, g)=0.84 , the stimuli were two pie charts, representing a pair of lotteries, with one on each side of the monitor screen. Each chart was divided in two, an upper part colored blue and a lower one colored red. Each division contained a dollar amount which could be won. The upper division always contained the larger value of the two possible consequences. The probability of winning that dollar amount was proportional to the size of the division.

As an additional test, participant’s choices were tested for skew. Sneddon (2001) demonstrated that a skewed psychometric function can cause biased PEST estimations of certainty equivalents. A basic test of skew is to fit a participant’s PEST choices to a logistic function (Sneddon, 2001). This test was applied to participants’ data. If the measure of skew of a participant’s PEST choices was significant at p < .01, the participant’s certainty equivalent was considered biased. This condition of using only unbiased CEs was applied both separately and in combination with the ‘‘No Catch Violation’’ condition to the data. Applying the average computer simulation value, p=0.835 , to these conditions yielded t(77)=−0.40, p=.69 for the ‘‘No Skew’’ condition and t(59)=−0.09, p=.93 when ‘‘No Skew’’ was combined with the ‘‘No Catch Violation’’ condition. Again, none of these t -scores are significant. 

The hypothesis that participants’ data were not significantly different from binomially distributed data was also tested. This hypothesis assumed that the participants’ data distribution had N groups of six trials, each with a probability of order preservation, p=0.835. Thus, this test examined a group of participants, each of which had completed all six lottery pairs. Each lottery pair had to show a preference, i.e., one certainty equivalent had to be greater than the other. Some participants did not fit these criteria. One participant did not complete all six lottery pairs of the experiment. Two participants had a lottery pair where each lottery in the pair had the same certainty equivalent. These participants could not be included in the binomial analysis. However, for all other participants, the q[2] value was q[2] (7, N=15)=12.10, p=.10 . In the case of the ‘‘No Skew’’ condition, participants who had at least one skewed gamble were omitted. Under this condition, the data yielded q[2] (7, N=6)=7.96, p=.24 . A more likely fit was found when the criterion of a participant not violating any catch trials was used. In this case, the q[2] value was q[2] (7, N=12)=7.19, p=.41. Therefore the hypothesis P(f, g)= P[ CE (f), CE (g)] could not be rejected. 

GENERAL DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION 

These experimental results suggest that certainty equivalents are order-preserving. There does appear to be some nonsignificant, downward bias. However, when certainty equivalents are elicited from attentive participants who possess unskewed 

222 

ROBERT SNEDDON 

psychometric functions, the CE s appear to be completely congruent with the orderpreserving hypothesis. Furthermore, these data suggest that the interpretation of PEST-computed certainty equivalents as (quasi) random variables, instead of median scores, is an accurate one. To the extent that this interpretation is correct, the statistical definition of substitutability (Eq. (4)) appears to hold. At an indirect level, the new stochastic assumption, preference bounding, also appears to hold. This assumption needs further testing. 

The results of this experiment also illustrate the importance of precise measurements of preference strength, as less precise methods, such as frequency of choice, can lead to apparent preference reversals. Thus, methods such as ‘‘the 84% algorithm’’ can be used for precise preference order experiments.

Part (v) 

This algorithm was hypothesized to converge to the balance point , as has been postulated for other adaptive procedures, e.g., Rendleman et al. (1970). The balance point is the point where the probability of a lottery being incremented is equal to its probability of being decremented. This probability is computed to be 0.841 . Since a probability of 84% is the same as that of d[−] =1 for equal variance Gaussians (from signal detection theory), the algorithm was dubbed ‘‘the 84% algorithm.’’ 

The derivation of P((xN, p; y), (wN, q; z))=0.841 is as follows. (Here, N is the final step of an 84% algorithm sequence.) Let s be the probability that a participant chooses the lottery (x, p; y) . Then the probability that the CE of this lottery is decreased (by decreasing x) is s[4] . All other groups of four choices will increase the CE of (x, p; y) . These possibilities are just the binomial distribution of s and 1−s for four choices, excluding s[4] . The balance point hypothesis states that the average convergence point is the point where the probability of a lottery increasing is equal to its probability of increasing.
