INTRODUCTION  The great expectations raised with the advent of conjoint measurement some twenty years ago for its providing social  scientists with a powerful tool of fundamental measurement seem to have gradually ebbed away. Nowadays, it can still be  considered important in theoretical work as an intermediate step in proving representation theorems. Tversky's (1977)  axiomatization of feature theory, the foundations of multidimensional scaling of similarity data (Beals, Krantz, & Tversky  (1968); Tversky & Krantz (1970)), and Croon's (1984) approach to modeling choices between multi-attribute stimuli are all  examples of this technique. In dimensional analysis, the role played by conjoint structures is genuinely significant for  obvious reasons; see, e.g., Krantz, Luce, Suppes, and Tversky (1971, Chap. 10), Luce (1978), and Luce and Narens (1983,  Section 7).  Original experimental work, however, using the conjoint measurement model explicitly is rarely presented. Notable exceptions  are the risk theory of Coombs and Lehner (1981, 1984), the discussion of loudness summation by Levelt, Riemersma, and Bunt  (1972), Falmagne, Iverson, & Marcovici (1979), Gigerenzer and Strube (1983), or Smolenaars' (1984) attempt to measure  complexity of sentence processing. The major obstacle to successful empirical applications of conjoint measurement probably stems from the difficulty of  verifying the axioms in the absence of an error theory. Such problems are inherent in any application of axiomatic algebraic  measurement theory. But another important point may be that often the attribute to be measured is not composed of two (or  more) components or, if it does consist of two (or more) components, they are not independent, which becomes evident only  after the experiment has been completed. Prior to any application of conjoint measurement, however, one has to decide on the  components. Even in theoretical work this is a drawback. The aforementioned approach to similarity of Beals, Krantz, and  Tversky (1968) fails to provide a foundation for multidimensional scaling for that very reason: the dimensions are primitives  in their theorems, and therefore a derivation or extraction of dimensions or components is ruled out.  In this paper we investigate the notion of conjointness without a priori knowledge of the components. To get components is not  difficult, but to find empirical or theoretical meaningful ones which are useful or efficacious "as a device for working a  manageable structure into the flux of experience," in the words of Quine (1953), is much more of a task. Much of the  literature on psychological methods is devoted to this problem under various names, e.g. factors, dimensions, clusters.  In the context of the above remarks let us emphasize that good components are often independent ones. This leaves us with the  question of how to define independence. But since we are aiming at the usual conjoint measurement structures, where a useful  definition of independence is already at our disposal, our goal is to present conditions which result in independent conjoint  structures.  Of course this restriction is a matter of taste; there are important cases of nonindependent components. But some kind of  constraint seems unavoidable, since mere conjointness is too broad a concept to be theoretically useful.  Apart from Section 3, where a very general decomposition of a relational system as a Cartesian product is described, we always  assume the existence of a weak order on a set A. Section 6 deals with the automorphisms of the conjoint structures that arise through application of the  representation theorems established in Sections 4 and 5. Our main result characterizes conjoint structures with only  factorizable automorphisms.  A natural generalization to more than two components is dealt with in Section 7. The main condition of Section 4 is shown to  be closely related to a lattice theoretic description of the set of weak orders induced by independent components. Each of the theorems presented in this paper contains a condition dealing with the existence and uniqueness of certain  elements. These are solvability conditions which can, in most cases, be considerably weakened. For example, the uniqueness  requirements can be dropped by (carefully) factoring out equivalences. The existential assumption cannot be dropped  completely, but can be relaxed, for example by an embeddability condition which is investigated in Suck (1987).  We chose not to give the weakest possible formulation with respect to solvability so as to ease the presentation of our  theorems. Moreover, the role of solvability axioms is not yet completely understood, as pointed out by Luce and Narens (1983);  and this remark applies to the present work too.  2. APPLICATION TO THE THEORY OF PSYCHOLOGICAL TESTS  In this final section the theory expounded so far will be applied to the problem of how to account for the apparatus,  instrument, or psychological test with which measurements are made. Traditionally the "tests and measurements" literature with  which we are concerned has dealt with this question only tangentially.  As an aside we mention that a major breakthrough in the development of quantum mechanics constituted the interpretation of the  observed data as a result of an interaction between the physical object and the measurement apparatus.  The chief point of the following development is to extend the formal structure of measurement theory in such a way that the  psychological test or whatever instrument is applied is explicitly included in its formulation. Conjoint measurement provides  a good vehicle for describing the behavior of objects and instruments simultaneously, and perhaps even their interaction.  To make this point a little more explicit we gradually introduce the formalism needed. Let X, be a set of objects; somehow we  have information about an order relation on X, , but information which goes beyond an ordinal scale is very hard to obtain. First, the assumptions involving the index set I with the intended but not necessary interpretation of repeated observations  of the objects a E A are less restrictive than they might seem. Any scheme assuming measures to constitute a random variable  to account for error is stronger. It can be shown that a continuous distribution of the measures for fixed a E A gives rise to  a symmetric biorder ({a} x I, T, P, Ka).  Second as already pointed out, the connection between the Rasch model and conjoint measurement is well established. Our  treatment differs from this theory in many respects. For example, the requirement for the existence of sufficient statistics  does not occur in our approach. The connection with the concept of specific objectivity especially as formulated in Roskam and  Jansen (1984) seems quite close; however we do not investigate the matter further.  Third, Theorem 8.1 can be reformulated in the following way: Given the test T, there is a construct X independent (in the  conjoint measurement sense) of T, such that T and X jointly can be used to measure A. What kind of thing this artificial  component X is, or whether it can be interpreted in terms of the experiments performed to constitute the biorder is a question  we cannot answer. But while it certainly would shed light on the issues under scrutiny here, it is by no means necessary to  describe X materially. Furthermore, since this is an empirical question depending on the content of the investigation, the  formal approach of our treatment can at best provide hints or principles for an interpretation.
