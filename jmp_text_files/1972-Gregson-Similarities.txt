JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 9, 368-388 (1972)  Quadratic Similarities and a Collapse-Points Condition  R. A. M. GREGSON  University of Canterbury, New Zealand  Quadratic relative similarities involving three reference stimuli, one comparison stimulus, and one judgment on the latter  against all three reference stimuli simultaneously, are defined and described for visual stimuli with three dimensions of  variation. The special case where all comparison stimuli lying outside the range of magnitudes of the reference stimuli yield  the same response is considered. Individual results on each of 25 subjects under three different cases are analyzed by  numerical methods, and the goodness of fit of a model together with estimates of each of its scalar parameters representing  selective attention biases and nonlinearity are given. Implications for scaling theory are noted.  1. INTRODUCTION  Similarity as a central concept in theoretical psychology has a long history, but mathematical models which attempt to  quantify degrees of similarity as a function of the composition of the stimuli compared are relatively few and recent. A  number of examples have been developed by Swedish psychologists, whereas in the U.S.A. investigators have often tried to  circumvent measurement-theory problems by defining a similarity to be a monotone transformation of a distance, or more  generally a proximity. The latter approach leads into nonmetric multidimensional scaling, the former into models of cognitive  processes. The former approach, with no prior assumptions about proximities, is most relevant here.  Similarity judgments are either made on pairs of stimuli, or are more properly relative similarities with pairwise comparisons  made between three or more stimuli at once, as in the method of triads. In real life, the number of reference stimuli or  concepts involved in assessing a degree of relative similarity is fluid and imprecise; in laboratory studies it is controlled  but no general theory exists to say what can be expected to occur when the number of reference stimuli is increased. New  perceptual strategies can and do emerge as the task of comparison becomes more complex, their form cannot readily be predicted  from behavior on paired or even triadic comparisons. If the number of reference stimuli, and the dimensionality of those  stimuli, is increased sufficiently, perception may break down and large parts of the stimulus input become effectively  irrelevant. It is thus possible to move between simple tasks reminiscent of classical psychophysics and complex judgments,  demanding as reasonable models some  Copyright Qc 1972 by Academic Press, Inc. It is a matter of choice where the theoretician starts within the series  of levels of complexity which can be constructed, and here we have started deliberately at a fairly complex level. Triadic and  paired comparisons can then be treated as degenerate or limiting cases.  Quadratic similarities are judgments of relative similarity between one compared stimulus, the referent, and three fixed  stimuli, the referees. One judgment is made, with two degrees of freedom, to the simultaneous presentation of the four  stimuli. Such judgments are a special subordinate case of the complete method of quads, and may be thought of as the next  terms in a logical progression which runs: absolute stimulus judgment, similarity in pair comparisons, relative similarity in  triad comparisons between one variable and two fixed reference stimuli, and then quadratic similarities (hereafter abbreviated  to QS). Relatively little is known about human judgment processes at this level of complexity, but some empirical findings for  three- and four-dimensional stimuli, and a theoretical model have been reported (Gregson, 1969, 1970, 1972a; Gregson and  Bulow, 1970).  A series of experiments has shown that it is possible to impose some meaningful structure on responses to the QS task, and it  has been possible to characterize, by the parameters in a model, features of relative similarity judgments which vary with the  complexity, dimensionality, and interrelatedness of the stimuli used. The pace of the task, and selectivity of attention while  doing it, are also demonstrably factors which affect judgments. The model presented here is substantially the same as that  used in the earlier studies with some slight extension. The method of recording responses is the same, but the stimuli are a  new series devised deliberately to create a situation in which the model might cease to fit behavior adequately.  Our concern here is with a special case which parallels a counterintuitive prediction in the method of triads, with relative  similarity judgments, first pointed out by Luce (1961). The case is critical for any model postulated, and has theoretical  implications outside the QS task, because it involves the possibility that some similarity responses may be expressed  necessarily and sufficiently in terms of stimulus composition, whereas others may be independent of the precise composition of  one of the pair of stimuli involved but not of the other. If this can be shown, it raises subtle problems for scaling theory.  The notation and measures of relative similarity previously used are first summarized, and the method used for recording  responses is described. There are two aspects of QS as treated here; a model which predicts what numerical value a  dimensionless similarity will be, given a specification of the stimuli compared, and a geometrical convention for recording  and interpreting relative similarity responses. The two are not logically inseparable, alternative models and recording  conventions could obviously be used, and comparative assessments have been made of other models (Waern, 1970; Gregson, 1972b).  However, if either alone is inappropriate the whole structure should fail; their conjoint assertion constitutes the model  being examined.  370 GREGSON  2. Then if (1) and (2) hold, and the referent stimulus compositions satisfy  (3) or (4) and are outside the range of the referees, the relative similarities (but not the similarities) between the  referents and each of the referees in turn are constant and solely a function of the composition of the referee set. These constant values are  representable by one point inside the QS triangle for condition (3), and another single point for condition (4). These two  points are named "collapse points" as the judgment process of QS, if correctly defined by (1), (2) and the assumed mapping of  responses into the equilateral triangle diagram, collapses under these extreme conditions (3) and (4) into two points, or  rather due to intrinsic response variability into two tight clusters.  Alternatively, if the rx values are not all equal, the collapse-points condition may or may not occur. This situation arises  because the substitution of rx - m{X;} for m{X;} in (3) or (4) can alter the inequalities on A, B, C, D. In other words, the  perceived inequalities are changed by the biases until they are inconsistent with the physical inequalities represented by (3)  and (4). If both conditions (3) and (4) are presented in the same series of referents, and the rx are constant over the whole  series, then there would be a tendency for rx values which destroy one collapse point to destroy the other, given that the  constraints on (1) hold.  In short, the rx values affect conclusions based on (3) and (4) as applied to (1) and (2). It is not asserted that the rx are  fixed and independent of the stimulus interrelations in the cases examined below; either the rx remain all near unity and the  model holds as (1) and (2) under (3) and (4) as well as it holds under other intermediate conditions, or the subject  circumvents the collapse-points conditions by adjusting his rx so that m{D} is always within the range of the measures on the  referees, or the whole process is no longer describable by the model. There is no constraint on subjects to conform to any one  of these three predictions so far as the task instructions are concerned. The model can thus capture a representation of two  possible rational perceptual strategies in its configuration of parameter values, both of which are psychologically meaningful.  The parallel "collapse points" case noted by Luce (1961) is where, in a unidimensional triad comparison, the referent stimulus  outside the range of the two referees is judged to have a fixed relative similarity to the two referees which is soleley a  function of their difference in magnitude, and not of the magnitude of the referent. In fact, this prediction is not quite  born out according to Luce, who suggested a modification to his prediction formulas (Luce, Bush and Galanter, 1963, Vol. 1, p.  256) involving a second-order correction.  The empirical extension here is thus from triads to QS, and from univariate stimuli to multidimensional stimuli capable of  being represented by measures on partitioned sets, under the strict restriction that judgments of relative similarity are  being elicited and that the stimuli are pairwise discriminable. The theoretical definition of similarity  QUADRATIC SIMILARITIES 373  here is closer to that of subjective probability in the Suppes (1969) axiomatization of S-R theory than to the proximity  definitions employed in nonmetric scaling approaches, and is not the same as that which Luce used to derive his  collapse-points prediction.  3. EXPERIMENT  Previous results enable some specific stimulus configurations to be nominated as comparison conditions for the collapse-points  situation, and in the experiment described here three referee-referent cases were used, each with 15 referents in sequence.  All stimuli were representable as vectors of three elements or subset measures.  Case 1: Nonintersecting Referees  The referees are (m{Al), 0, 0), (0, m{B2}, 0) and (0, 0, m{C3}). Each referent is (m{DI}, m{DZ}, m{Da}). Previous results  suggest that d is close to 1 for this task, which is degenerately simple in that it confounds dimensions and referees.  Case 2: Nesting Referees  Here all referee subsets are nonnull, and for all j, m{A;) < m{B;) < m{C;).  Case 3: Intersecting Nonnesting Referees  This is the general case where all the referee subsets are nonnull and no constraints on their relative measures are imposed.  The nomenclature of the cases here is in set-theoretic terms for consistency with the earlier formal definitions (Gregson,  1970); as given here they could be redefined in vector terms. The collapse-points situations are most readily examined in  Cases 1 and 2; stimulus series were accordingly constructed that entailed, in Case 2, condition (3) for 3 members of the  referent set, condition (4) for 6 members, and normalcy for the remaining 6; in Case 1, (4) for six D [(3) is impossible in  Case 1], and in Case 3 (4) for one D in the set of 15 referents.  The general prediction made is that the model (1) and (2) will fit comparably well under the collapse-points condition as  under other conditions examined here and previously, and that, consequently, quadratic relative similarities are solely  expressible as functions of the composition of the referee stimuli outside specifiable boundary conditions given by (3) and  (4). This is a parallel prediction to that made by Luce, in the unidimensional method of triads, but from a different starting  point so far as the axioms defining similarities are concerned. METHOD  Subjects  Twenty-five first-year undergraduates, 15 female and 10 male, naive both to the method and theory of QS judgments, served  unpaid as part of course requirements.  Procedure  Subjects were seated in front of a projection screen 1 m x 1 m on which were projected three referee stimuli in the positions  corresponding to the apices of an upright equilateral triangle; these were retained for a block of 15 trials. On each 30-sec  trial the fourth referent, D, was projected onto the empty middle of the A, B, C referee configuration to the same scale so  that each circular stimulus pattern was the same diameter. Projection was from two Kodak carousel random-access projectors,  the referent sequences being selected by predetermined randomization. On each trial subjects put a numbered cross on their  individual QS grid chart record sheets, as in Fig. 1, whose orientations correspond to that of the screen presentation of the  referees. A new grid sheet was used for each 5 trials. The conditions Case 1-3 were done in a random order for each subgroup  of subjects, who came in twos, threes, and fours as convenient. A near balance for order of the cases was achieved over the 25  subjects, as order and warm-up effects in QS can be marked (Gregson, 1969).  Stimuli  The stimuli were circle diagrams, black lines on a white field, divided into three 120segments by three radial partitions,  one of which was vertical. Filling in segments clockwise from the partition lines, the first 120contained a red segment, the  second blue, and the third yellow. The angles of the color segments in degrees are the measures m{X;} and m{D;} used in the  analysis. DISCUSSION  It is not asserted that the Cases 1 and 3 are complete controls on the judgment processes in Case 2; QS is a complex task,  near the limit of information-handling capacity for some subjects, and analyzed by a model of sufficient complexity to be  sensitive in its parameter values to a variety of types of change in judgment behavior which are of theoretical interest.  A large proportion of the response behavior is sufficiently regular to be mapped into the model, and so far no competing  theory has been advanced to do better. The nonintersecting referee condition, Case 1, is employed as a simple case, which  confounds stimulus dimensions with referee identity, and imposes a minimal information load on subjects performing QS. LT here  is nearly 1, as found earlier (Gregson, 1969) for different visual stimuli with three dimensions. The individual differences  in iwii, {r,}, and F are marked and confirm the marked interindividual differences noted by other workers, such that any one  similarity model has a poor chance of fitting the behavior of a large group of randomly selected subjects. Some of the  variability in F is due to subjects not using information as instructed (subjects 7 and 12) which matches earlier results on  the effect of experimenter-induced selective attention on QS (Gregson, 1972).  Case I shows FA > jr, > rc , which implies a slight contraction of the relative similarity spacing of the referees.  The observed and adjusted entropies in Case 1 are nearer to theory than in the other two cases, and the tabulated residuals  are smallest. These residuals are the summed squares of errors on all 45,4 values in a block, they are not the A residuals,  though the latter behave analogously. The mean and SD of A values for each case, both at the start of parameter estimation  (all of w, r, d at unity) and at minimum are included in Table 5. Each is a mean of 25 sums each of 45 disparities as defined  in (5). There is no evidence that the model fits worse in Cases I and 2 than in Case 3. An interesting feature of the data is  the intercorrelation of residuals over the three cases; the productmoment correlations (with 23 df) for the 3 sets of 25  individual A values under the cases are r12 -= -0.03, r23 = -}-0.22, and ris = +0.51. There is some consistency between the  two cases with fewest instances of (3) and (4) but none between Case 2 and the rest. Some subjects fit the model better under  the collapse-points condition, others not so.  Case 2, the most complex collapse-points situation, shows two distinctive features, the highest mean exponent, 4.74 (14  subjects gave di < da < 42 , 7 subjects gave A, < A,, < ds , the difference in mean exponent between cases being significant),  and extreme imbalance in the {w;} for practically all subjects. This latter effect, however, follows from the algebra of the  estimation problem to some extent; the partial deri-  386 GREGSON  vatives of the parameters in (5) are small for the configuration of stimuli, referees and referents, used. Consequently the  absolute values of the {w;} should not be given interpretation; they are bounded by the constraints that their product is  unity and that no one shall be greater than 100.0, to conserve search time in the parameter space. It is the imbalance that is  notable, because it suggests that the redundancy may allow subjects to dispense with some stimulus information. Eob$ is least,  indicating that responses move outwards from the triangle centroid, and the residuals both before and after parameter  estimation greatest. The differences between residuals after w, A, r estimation in Cases 2 and 3 are not, however,  significant; 11 subjects had greater variables in Case 3 than in Case 2, one had equal residuals, and the remaining 13 less.  The 71 is greatest, however, and these parameter shifts, taken with inspection of the 75 plots of the regressions of model  upon data, suggest that QS judgments do cluster about the collapse points, as is appropriate.  But the loci of the collapse points themselves move away from the unweighted model prediction loci; this is equivalent to the  similarity scale readjusting its spread as the total variability in the stimuli increases, so that the referees which lies  within the total range of values in the collapse-points cases are closed up by some process like an adaptation of the  subject's capacity to resolve stimulus differences as a function of the total range of stimulus variability present. As we are  dealing with relative judgments by definition, this is not surprising. In Case 2, the values of rX are such as to inflate m{A}  and reduce m{C}, and the high exponent exaggerates large similarities in the composition of (2) from (1). The value of 3.71  for d here is the highest so far noted in a series of QS experiments; values of about 1.6-2.4 for 4-dimensional stimuli in  intersecting nonnesting cases have been found.  Case 3 shows almost complete balance of the rx, an exponent of the same size as in previous experiments, and larger residuals  than Case 1. The Eobs value shows that responses tend to move towards the apices of the QS triangle grid more than in Case 1,  but not so much as in Case 2, which does not support the idea that this difference is due simply to the referee composition or  to the intercorrelation of referent dimensions. Comparison of the and values for the three cases suggests that the biases are  more effective in Case 3 than in Case 2, yet the mean values do not reveal this.  The 3-dimensional collapse-point condition, in summary, produces a larger A, but no other evidence of any systematic departure  from the model predictions apart from the slight nonsignificant increase in the residuals. The collapse-point condition is  confounded, deliberately as a simplifying approach in this first study, with the intercorrelation of the referent dimensions,  but comparing Case 1 with a similar data set in Gregson's (1969) study, where the dimensions were not highly intercorrelated  but gave comparable results, suggests that the high A of Case 2 is in large part a consequence of the collapse points effect. CONCLUSION  The QS model (1) and (2) is consistent with data under the collapse-points conditions (3) and (4) sufficiently to suggest that  relative similarity judgments can behave counterintuitively, in a way first predicted by Luce for a simpler situation. Results  of this nature indicate that considerable caution is needed in treating relative similarity and similarity judgments as  necessarily compatible with metric axioms, or as predictable necessarily and sufficiently from a knowledge of the composition  of the stimuli presented. The magnitude and number of implicit reference standards brought into play by subjects will  attenuate the validity of generalizations from simple methods such as paired comparisons and associated scalings in  multidimensional space. QS are much more complex than the judgment processes of traditional psychophysics, but their behavior  is no longer an intractable subject of study.
