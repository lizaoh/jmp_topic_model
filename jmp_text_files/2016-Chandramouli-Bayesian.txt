Shiffrin and Chandramouli (in press)  proposed an extension of Bayesian induction in which priors are assigned to possible data distributions for the present experiment. The idea in short is that one (unknown) data distribution represents reality and a sample from that ‘true’ data distribution produces the observed data. Then Bayes Theorem is used to revise the prior distributions and produce posterior distributions for the possible data distributions. This general idea is not novel and is represented in a different form in a Journal of Mathematical Psychology article by  Karabatsos (2006) .

Where do model classes and model instances fit into the simple scheme? The model instances in all the classes under consideration each predict a data distribution. These predicted data distributions are only a small subset of the possible data distributions, but the priors and posteriors for each can be derived from the priors and posteriors for all data distributions by simple summation. One first defines a metric by which to compare distributions—we have termed this criterion  
                         G 
                       (it could for example be some form of Kullback–Leibler). Using  
                         G 
                       we can establish which data distributions are best matched by the predicted data distribution for each model instance. Ignoring the possibility of ties, which would not occur often enough to matter, ‘best’ match implies that all data distributions are partitioned into disjoint subsets; each subset contains distributions that are a best match to a given model instance. The priors (or posteriors) for a given instance are then a simple sum of the priors (or posteriors) for its best matching subset. Then the priors (or posteriors) for a model class are simply a sum of the priors (or posteriors) of the model instances in that class.

Thus what we are doing is using the observed data to carry out inference, but not inferring the probability that some model instance is ‘true’, but rather inferring the probability that some model instance is the  best approximation  to the truth. Of course ‘best’ depends on the choice of  
                         G 
                      , which depends in turn on one’s goals of inference. When matching distributions one could for example choose to weight the center of distributions more than the extremes, or any other metric that satisfies one’s inference goals.

Why do we represent the ‘true’ state of the world that produces the data as a distribution? There are uncounted numbers of variables that affect the results that are unknown, un-measurable, or ignored, but would alter the results in what would be the most precise replication possible. Thus if we flipped a fair coin 100 times we might expect a binomial distribution for n with

The uncountable number of unknown variables include the forces imported to the coin when it is released, the air currents and air pressure, the nature of the surface on which the coin lands, and the state of the gravitational field that depends on the current position of Jupiter with respect to our position on Earth. Such unknown variables are present in all studies including our toy example (success probability of a drug).

It should be emphasized that the possible distributions are not equally probable. Before we carry out the study or see the results we have a good deal of knowledge about the results that could be expected. We always have a good deal of such knowledge even for a study never done before. In our toy example we would not expect a distribution for n successes in 10 trials that would assign high probabilities to prime numbers (1,2,3,5,7) and low probabilities to other  
                         n 
                      ’s (0,4,6,8,9,10). Such prior knowledge is a critical part of inference. The example just given is extreme, but most scientists are familiar with the importance of priors through interaction with their new graduate students: A student reports ‘strange’ results from a new study, results claimed to be accurate because they have been checked for errors in the program and analysis. The scientist nonetheless ‘knows’ the results are almost certainly in error. Subsequent investigation almost always reveals this to be the case. Even though the study may be new, the scientist’s prior knowledge allows generally accurate assessment of the likelihood of error.

Thus the prior probabilities we assign to the possible data distributions are based on our prior knowledge before taking the current results into account; these are listed to the left of each table row. These sum to 1.0 down the column. The way one assigns such probabilities will not only vary with prior knowledge, but with the goals of inference. This point will be elaborated in the discussion of reproducibility. Just as in traditional BMS the assignments of priors is of course subjective, and almost always based on vague rather than precise information. Thus the assignments of priors are guesses designed to capture qualitative knowledge from past experience. The fact that priors are in these senses imprecise does not change the fact that they are an important part of inference.

We use models to try to gain approximate understanding of the enormously complex factors and processes operating in the real world that produce the experimental data. Models are always wrong and generally crude oversimplifications but nonetheless useful. They typically reduce the enormous dimensionality of the problem space to something human minds can grasp. We often group models into classes of model instances that are similar. In our toy example a particular model instance might be a binomial distribution for number of successes in 10 trials based on a success rate of 0.4. It is worth noting that scientists who carry out model assessment know their models are approximations and act on that knowledge in appropriate ways. It is also worth emphasizing that BMS* solves the long-standing problem of placing inference more on data rather than on (wrong) models, and incorporates data priors into the inference process in a coherent way.

How can model classes be compared in light of the observed data? A model class is a subset of the model instances in the right hand column. Let us assume that the classes do not overlap—i.e. they do not share instances. Then each class has a posterior probability that is the sum of its instance posterior probabilities. The ratio of these class posteriors for two classes produces the equivalent of the traditional way to carry out Bayesian Model Selection, what we have termed BMS*: I.e. the ratio is the Bayes Factor times the Odds Ratio for the two classes, albeit the inference in BMS* is not that the class contains the true distribution, but rather that the class contains the instance predicting the closest approximation to the true distribution. Partly in order to favor smaller model classes (i.e. impose the Bayesian Occam’s Razor) it is traditional in BMS to compare classes with the Bayes Factor, alone, ignoring the Odds Ratio. The same can be done in BMS*. In fact, in general, traditional Bayesian Model Selection, BMS, is simply a restricted case of BMS*: If we restrict the data distributions to only those predicted by the model instances, then everything remains the same as we have discussed, except that we are assuming that one of the model instances predicts the ‘true’ data distribution.

What we have just said about model selection holds as long as the model classes being compared do not share instances, something we believe ought to be the case when comparing models. This was the subject of another article in this issue ( Shiffrin et al., 2016 ). The problem of shared instances is most easily seen when one model class is wholly embedded in another (as when linear models are embedded in quadratic models). In this case, the sum of posteriors for the larger class has to be larger than the sum for the smaller class, and there is no penalty for class complexity. In Shiffrin et al. we recommended that shared instances in the smaller class be subtracted from the larger class before model comparison is carried out. In almost all cases in practice this will leave the traditional results unchanged, because the number of instances in the larger class is so much larger—subtracting the shared instances produces a negligible change. Thus the familiar methods for comparing fully parameterized models to restricted models (ones in which certain parameters have specified values) are unaffected by subtraction of shared instances from the larger class. There are many aspects of data we could wish to see reproduced in a replication study: e.g. conclusions, qualitative patterns, or particular statistics defined on the data outcome (a simple one dimensional statistic would be the mean outcome of some aspect of the data). Let S represent a quantitative statistic defined on the data outcome. One straightforward way to assess reproducibility compares the value of S in the replication to the predicted distribution of S. The predicted distribution of S is trivial to obtain from the vector of outcome probabilities along the bottom of the posterior (or prior) table: Certain outcomes have the same value of the statistic S (same in the sense of lying in the same discrete interval). The outcomes

The shape of the predicted distribution for S depends critically on the assignment of prior probabilities to data distributions. One can assign them in a way that assumes the replication must be an exact copy of the original, what has been termed ‘posterior predictive’ in the literature (see   Gelman, Meng, & Stern, 1996 ) or in a way that assumes that the replication has additional variance (one version of which is termed ‘prior predictive’—see  Box, 1980 ).  Shiffrin and Chandramouli (in press)  discussed this issue in terms of within-setting replication variance, and between-setting replication variance, and discussed how the goals of inference might alter how much between-setting replication variance is incorporated in the priors.

BMS* has extremely high dimensionality: The number of different experimental outcomes is almost always enormous, and the number of distributions of those outcomes is almost unimaginably larger yet. Thus the theory represented here must be implemented in way that would be computationally feasible. One approach would parameterize the data distributions in a way that allows quite general distributions but still allows induction to be carried out in real time. A good example is the class of ‘stick-breaking’ prior distributions used by  Karabatsos (2006) .

Although the new system (we term it BMS*) places more emphasis on data than models, it is nonetheless the case that the two go hand in hand—we use models to simplify and understand the immense complexities of the world, and we use those models, whether implicit or explicit, to form the priors that are the basis of induction.
