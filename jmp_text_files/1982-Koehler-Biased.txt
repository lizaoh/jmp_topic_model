INTRODUCTION    hod of paired comparisons has been frequently used in comparative studies where it is difficult to quantify differences among  the items under consideration. This method considers independent comparisons of pairs of items. For each pair considered, the  two items are merely ordered with respect to strength, preference, or superiority. This technique has been applied to consumer  preference tests, psychological studies, as well as athletic contests. In this paper paired comparison models are used to  describe the results of competition between professional basketball teams. In this case an item is a team and two teams are  ordered according to a winner and loser by playing a game. There are no ties allowed in basketball.  deas about paired comparisons appear in the writings of Fechner (1860, 1966). These ideas were further developed and  popularized by Thurstone (1927a, 1927b). Thurstone presented a concept of a subjective continuum on which pairs of items could  be ordered, but on which actual distances between items could not be measured. Bradley (1953) showed that the  Bradley-Terry model could be obtained by replacing the normal density function used in the Thurstone case V model with the  logistic density function. Luce (1959) posed a choice axiom which leads to a choice model of essentially the same form.  Consequently, the model has been referred to as the Bradley-Terry-Luce model, and it is referred to in this paper as the BTL  model. Fienberg (1980, Chap. 8) applied the BTL model to professional baseball data. In this paper, extensions of the BTL  model are used to analyze home and away records for professional basketball teams. Models considered by Davidson and Beaver  (1977) to account for order effects are used to assess the potential advantages teams have when playing on their home courts.  Davidson and Beaver consider one model where each pair of objects has a different multiplicative order effect and a second  model where the same multiplicative order effect is used for all pairs of objects. Some additional cases are considered in  this article. Separate multiplicative effects are used to account for order effects within and among different groups of  objects. These models are used to assess differences in the home court advantage within and among basketball teams in  different divisions. It is shown that maximum likelihood estimates for any of these models can be obtained from a simple  iterative scaling procedure.  h the basketball example is the only one considered, these extensions of the BTL model can be applied to many other paired  comparison problems. For example, these models could be used to assess the effect of behavior modification drugs in a study of  animal behavior where the animals are placed in cages in pairs and observed to determined which animal displays dominant  behavior. The same animal is used in more than one pairing and for some pairings one of the animals is given one of several  drugs designed to modify behavior. These models may also be useful for assessing the effects of various attributes on  individual choice behavior. In situations where individuals are forced to choose between two alternatives the Davidson and  Beaver model with a single multiplicative parameter may be adequate to account for the effect of the order of the presentation  of the alternatives. However, the extensions of the BTL model considered in this article could be used to allow the order  effect to vary for different groups of objects or to differ with respect to attributes that categorize the individuals such as  sex, level of maturity, or type of training. Many other applications of the BTL model have been considered. Bradley (1976)  discusses major developments in paired comparison methodology. Haberman (1974, 1979) describes a modified Newton-Raphson algorithm to find maximum  likelihood estimates for the model parameters and estimates of the variances and covariances. This algorithm is more  complicated to implement than the iterative scaling algorithm. While it often converges in fewer steps it requires more  calculations to be done on each step. General guidelines for the relative economy of the two algorithms are not available,  although Haberman (1974) indicates that the iterative scaling procedure will be more efficient when the model contains a  sufficiently large number of parameters.  terative weighted least squares estimation procedure for paired comparison models has been described by Imrey, Johnson, and  Koch (1976). This procedure finds the parameter estimates which minimize Neyman's (1949) modified X2 statistic. These are  generally not maximum likelihood estimates, although they have the same large sample properties and are close to the maximum  ikelihood estimates for large samples. This procedure also produces estimates for variances and covariances. Since it is not  an iterative procedure it is generally cheaper to use, and it is recommended for large samples. However, the data for the  basketball example are too sparse for the accurate use of this procedure, and maximum likelihood estimates are computed using  the iterative scaling method.
