Model  Identifiability  pertains to the theoretical possibility to determine (i.e., ‘identify’) in a unique manner the ‘true’ hidden parameters of a model, based on a dataset. This property is vital for any model—a perquisite for using it as an efficient research tool. For example, identifiability is a necessary condition for precise model-based statistical inferences. The other side of the coin is that non-identifiability entails several hazardous consequences such as, undermining a model’s use qua measurement and inference tool and subjecting the model to inflated model-comparison penalties. Thus, non-identifiable models may underlie non-valid conclusions, leading research astray. Accordingly, the issue of model-identifiability has attracted substantial interest across diverse scientific domains such as statistics (e.g.,  Casella & Berger, 2002 ;  Lehmann & Casella, 1998 ), economics (e.g.,  Fisher, 1966 ;  Reiersøl, 1950 ) and psychology (e.g.  Brainerd, Howe, & Desrochers, 1982 ;  Brainerd, Howe, & Kingma, 1982 ). In the current paper, I examined the identifiability properties of the influential Two High Threshold Model (henceforth, 2HTM;  Bayen, Murnane, & Erdfelder, 1996 ;  Bröder & Meiser, 2007 ;  Bröder & Schütz, 2009 ;  Erdfelder & Buchner, 1998 ;  Meiser, 2005 ;  Meiser & Bröder, 2002 ;  Province & Rouder, 2012 ;  Snodgrass & Corwin, 1988 ) in the popular confidence-rating recognition paradigm.

Recognition memory is our ability to discriminate between ‘old’ objects that have been encountered in a given context and ‘new’ objects that have not. In laboratory settings, participants first study a list of items and are later tested on a different list, which consists of both studied (target) and unstudied (lure) items. Participants decide whether each test probe is ‘old’ or ‘new’. Often, as in the paradigm focal to the current study, the old–new judgment is graded along a confidence scale, ranging from high confidence in ‘old’ to high confidence in ‘new’.

Notably, both target and lure-detection are  high threshold  processes, meaning that lures are never detected as old (i.e., never pass the detect-old threshold) and targets are never detected as new. In case of non-detection the participant enters a state of uncertainty, i.e., a ‘guessing’ state.

Considering first the guessing state, it does not discriminate between targets and lures. Consequently, the probability of selecting a particular (confidence) response from this state is independent of the type of the test probe, target or lure—an instance of the  conditional independence  assumption ( Province & Rouder, 2012 ). Additionally, guessed items can be rated with any of the old or new confidence judgments, with probabilities that are given by free, response-mapping, parameters.

Unlike the guessing state, the various 2HTM variants make different assumptions with respect to the confidence mappings from detect states. Traditionally, 2HTM has adopted the  certainty  assumption, according to which detect states always produce a correct response at the highest confidence level ( Fig. 1 (A)). This variant predicts linear ROC functions ( Egan, 1958 ;  Swets, 1986 ;  Swets, Tanner, & Birdsall, 1964 , Ch. 1). More recently, however, the certainty assumption has been critiqued as untenable and it has been argued instead (see  Fig. 1 (B)) that in the detect-states participants may distribute their responses across high and low confidence levels (e.g.,  Broadbent, 1966 ;  Erdfelder & Buchner, 1998 ;  Malmberg, 2002 ;  Province & Rouder, 2012 ;  Swagman, Province, & Rouder, 2015 ). Under this relaxed assumption, 2HTM can produce curved ROC functions (see  Malmberg, 2002  for a detailed explanation)—meeting the challenge of accounting for typical empirical confidence-rating ROCs (e.g.,  Wixted, 2007 ). Notably, the model displayed in  Fig. 1 (B) (henceforth, DC) adheres to the  Detect–Correct  assumption according to which, detected items always yield a correct response: detected targets and lures are always assigned to one of the old and new confidence ratings, respectively.

Recently,  Chen, Starns, and Rotello (2015)  considered a more flexible ‘ Full Response Mapping ’ 2HTM variant (henceforth FRM;  Fig. 1 (C)), which has also been presented elsewhere (e.g.,  Province & Rouder, 2012 ,  Fig. 1 (D);  Swagman et al., 2015 ,  Fig. 1 (D)). In FRM, the detect–correct assumption is forsaken; any confidence rating can be given from the detect states, including erroneous ratings (e.g., new ratings for detected targets). Presumably, such erroneous ratings reflect response-errors and/or demand characteristics e.g., the common instructions to use the entire confidence scale. In sum, before FRM was introduced, 2HTM models were not allowed to make erroneous responses for detected items. Such responses were possible only in  low threshold  models (e.g.,  Krantz, 1969 ;  Luce, 1963 ). For example, in a low threshold model a lure may be mistakenly detected as a target and consequently, be given an erroneous old response.

Here, I examined the identifiability of the various 2HTM variants. I begin with a definition of identifiability and I highlight the perils imposed by its absence. I prove that: (1) 2HTM with the certainty assumption is identifiable, (2) DC is identifiable if there are multiple target-strength conditions but non-identifiable if there is a single target condition, and (3) FRM is not identifiable. Such proofs comprise a valuable method for establishing model-identifiability or its absence, as they bring to the foreground mathematical principles that can aid researchers in developing a deeper and broader understanding for the structure and operation of models. Indeed, a close examination of the non-identifiability proof for FRM, revealed  why  this model is non-identifiable. This understanding inspired a novel extended paradigm, super-recognition, which renders the FRM identifiable. Next, revisiting a model comparison study of  Chen et al. (2015) , I illustrate how non-identifiable models can distort scientific conclusions. This investigation highlights the need to identify models and/or to devise appropriate solutions for the problem of non-identifiability. Such solutions are discussed in the final part of the paper.

In other words, different combinations of parameters do not yield the exact same model-predictions. More generally, I will say that a specific model-outcome  identifies  the model-parameters if and only if a unique combination of parameters predicts that outcome. Note that it is possible that certain outcomes identify the model’s parameters, whereas others do not. In practice, model parameters are typically estimated via model-fitting procedures, which aim at finding parameters that minimize the “distance” between the model-outcome and an empirical dataset (e.g., deviance). I will say that an  empirical  dataset identifies the model-parameters, if the minimal distance is obtained for a unique parameter-set. If a model is non-identifiable, different parameter-combinations may yield the minimal distance and hence, the ‘true’ operative parameters cannot be determined.

How does one establish identifiability? Identifiability proofs, i.e., rigorous mathematical demonstrations that there is a one to one mapping between the  parameter  and the  outcome  spaces of a model, constitute the best method. This is the method I followed in the current study.

One general ‘recipe’ to prove that a model is  non -identifiable is based on the “rank test”: showing that the Jacobian matrix of the function that maps model parameters to model-outcomes is, of deficient (i.e., not full) column rank over an open environment in the parameter space ( Bamber & van Santen, 1985 ). This implies one easily-tested  necessary  condition for identifiability: that the number of free data points in the focal dataset (i.e., the dimensionality of the outcome space), exceeds the number of free model-parameters (e.g.   Bishop, Fienberg, & Holland, 1975 ;  Greeno & Steiner, 1964 ). Indeed, if there are more parameters than free data points then the rank of the Jacobian is necessarily deficient (having more columns than rows). Importantly, the rank-test does not provide a  sufficient  condition for identifiability: A model can be non-identifiable even when the number of free data points exceeds or equals the number of free parameters (e.g.,  Batchelder & Riefer, 1990 ;  Brainerd, Howe, & Desrochers, 1982 ) and more generally, even if the Jacobian is of full column-rank over the entire parameter space.

Often, devising a (global) identifiability proof is too difficult a task. Is such cases, the modeler can still practically use a method that is based on repeating the model-fitting procedure to a given dataset several times, each time with a different ‘starting point’ (it is recommended that the starting points are spread across different regions of the parameter-space to extend its coverage). Suppose, however, that the global-fit minimum is achieved for a unique set of parameters. This finding enhances one’s confidence in the model’s identifiability. Several caveats are noteworthy: first, this repetitive-fitting method does not address the question of  global  identifiability, but the more limited question of whether a specific dataset identifies the model (often, this is all the modeler cares about). Furthermore, the repeated-fitting method cannot provide a conclusive answer to this question, because typically, fitting algorithms cannot guarantee foolproof convergence to a global, rather than some local, minima. While, the probability to ‘miss the global minima’ decreases as a function of the number of starting points, it always remains positive (however small). Thus, unless one can ensure that the fitting algorithm necessarily detected all the global minima, it remains possible that a global minimum has not been found (i.e., the global-fit minima is not a true global minima; see Footnote 3), or that only one, out of multiple global minima, has been found. Finally, even when a model is  globally  identifiable, some empirical datasets may fail to identify the model, because there may be multiple model-outcomes that minimize the global distance to the dataset (in this case, changing the distance-metric may identify the parameters).

The above notwithstanding, the repeated fitting method is often the best one can practically do. In such cases and when global identifiability is of main interest, it is advised to use a ‘ doubly-repetitive ’ fitting method, where multiple model-outcomes are each fitted multiple times (with different starting points). If each of the outcomes seems to identify the model, then one’s confidence in global identifiability increases. Once more, caution is advised because, on top of the caveats mentioned above, infinite outcome spaces can only be sampled (but not exhausted). Therefore, it always remains possible that outcomes that do not identify the parameters were not sampled.

Model non-identifiability has some hazardous implications and it is therefore critical that this problem is detected before a model is put to use. First, non-identifiability undermines a vital function of the model qua measurement tool. For example, if the lure-detection probability is non-identifiable in FRM, one cannot quantify the efficiency of the lure-detection process in individual people, based on this model. Consequently, one cannot conduct statistical inferences e.g., comparing amnesiacs and healthy participants, with respect to their lure-detection abilities.

I now examine the identifiability of the various 2HTM variants. To anticipate my conclusions, I find that: (1) the certainty-2HTM is identifiable, (2) DC is identifiable if there are multiple target-strength conditions but non-identifiable if there is a single target condition and, (3) FRM is non-identifiable. In the following sections, I present supporting identifiability and non-identifiability proofs. Because the proofs are somewhat technical, they are preceded by intuitive preambles that have two purposes: (1) to highlight the operative principles for readers who skip the proofs and (2) to facilitate proof-reading for ‘non-skippers’. The proofs highlight important mathematical principles (e.g., from algebra and convexity), which can aid modelers in better understanding the structure and operation of discrete-state models. Indeed, the non-identifiability proof for FRM, suggested the design of an extended, identifying  super-recognition  paradigm, for this model.

I note that the following identifiability proofs will establish  almost global  identifiability, meaning that almost (but not) all model-outcomes identify the parameters. Still, there are exceptional, ‘degenerate’ outcomes that fail to identify the model. Thus, identifiability is proved under general ‘non-degenerate’ assumptions. These assumptions are hardly limiting in the sense that they were met with probability 1, when model parameters were drawn randomly (see  Appendix ). It is thus unlikely, to encounter one of those degenerate outcomes when fitting empirical data.

Briefly, by using different numbers of study-repetitions and encoding conditions,  Chen et al. (2015)  manipulated target-strength across four levels. The finding that FRM may provide better fits than the DC is informative for proponents of discrete state models, who may wish to use FRM in future studies. Can FRM’s non-identifiability problem be solved, so that it can better serve researchers?

One solution is seeking alternative paradigms, or variants of the current paradigm with extended outcome spaces, wherein FRM would be identifiable. DC provides an illuminating demonstration of this approach: when there is a single target condition the model is non-identifiable. However, a task-extensions with multiple target-strengths, renders the model identifiable. I now introduce a novel design, the (confidence-rating)  super-recognition paradigm , which identifies FRM. The presentation aims to highlight one important advantage of identifiability proofs over repeated-fitting, as a method for diagnosing identifiability: proofs go beyond informing about non-identifiability by clarifying  why  this is the case. By implication, they suggest model-identifying task-extensions, which address the non-identifiability causes. What identifying insights can be drawn from FRM’s non-identifiability proof?

Recent studies of recognition have made a case for relaxing the certainty assumption for detected probes in high threshold models (e.g.,  Broadbent, 1966 ;  Bröder, Kellen, Schütz, & Rohrmeier, 2013 ;  Erdfelder & Buchner, 1998 ;  Malmberg, 2002 ;  Province & Rouder, 2012 ;  Swagman et al., 2015 ). Whereas adopting the more relaxed DC assumption seems both psychologically plausible and beneficial, particularly with respect to accounting for curved ROCs ( Malmberg, 2002 ), taking a further step and using full response mappings in standard confidence-rating recognition paradigms is problematic. Indeed, 2HTM with the certainty assumption is identifiable and so is DC, when there are multiple target conditions. FRM, however, is perilously not. Consequently, (a) FRM’s recovered parameters cannot be interpreted, undermining its uses qua measurement and inference tool, and (b) in model comparisons, one should be cautious in penalizing the model based on naive parameter-counts. The novel super-recognition paradigm forms one remedy for FRM’s non-identifiability. By extending the standard-recognition paradigm to include ‘super-strong’ probes, it identifies FRM, under the assumption of certain super-probes detection.

One concern that might be raised is that the assumption that super-probes are  always  detected is too strong and is hence, nothing more than an ‘idealization’. I argue, however, that like any other model, FRM would have to be  empirically validated  and compared with other models for the task, before it can be accepted as an account for super-recognition. The question of whether the certain-detection assumption is reasonable is thus, an empirical one and should be addressed in future studies. On a broader level, I note that all psychological models idealize to some extent. Consider the very notion of  high-threshold  detection in 2HTM. Advocatus diaboli could argue that it is always possible that a participant was distracted during the study phase and hence, failed to encode a subset of the items. If so, these ‘formal targets’ (from the experimenter’s perspective) are, nevertheless, ‘psychological lures’ and as such, could be detected as new. Consequently, a high-threshold lure-detection process, too, is  unrealistic . Still, the question of whether 2HTM is a ‘good’ or ‘useful’ model is ultimately empirical: if distraction occurs to a very low extent, 2HTM might still provide a better account for data (as compared to low threshold models), taking into account parsimony. Similarly, the certain super-probe detectability assumption may prove useful, if it is ‘almost true’.

Next, I discuss approaches for solving non-identifiability problems generally, and for FRM, particularly. As demonstrated by the super-recognition paradigm, extending the task or seeking alternative identifying paradigm, is one vital strategy (DC with multiple vs. single target-conditions provides another demonstration. see also  Brainerd, Howe, and Kingma (1982)  for an example from the domain of recall).

Another approach to dealing with non identifiability, which has been instrumental in the study of source memory ( Batchelder & Riefer, 1990; Bayen et al., 1996 ) is to replace the model with identifiable sub-models that are obtained by imposing psychologically plausible parameter-restrictions (see also  Buchner & Erdfelder, 2005 ). Recently, this approach was successfully applied with respect to DC, in a paradigm with a single target condition ( Bröder et al., 2013 ).

Considering FRM, however, any restrictions on its confidence-mapping parameters would undermine its very essence, as it will abolish the unconstrained freedom in distributing confidence responses from each of the psychological states. Another possibility is to replace FRM with the identifiable ED, whose outcome space is identical to that of FRM’s, as a model for standard recognition. Alas, while ED is a mathematically valid model, its assumptions are psychologically problematic. Indeed, in  standard -recognition, the assumption that lures are always detected implies that the memory system achieves perfect target–lure discriminability, as reflected in the absence of overlap between internal states for targets and lures. In this case, the interpretation of the guessing state is vague, because whenever an item is in that state, it can be inferred that it must be a target. Notably, for super recognition, this problem does not arise for FRM, because when a target enters the guessing state, it could still be confused with a standard (but not super) lure. Furthermore, ED’s assumptions that the weakest and strongest targets in standard recognition are never and always detected, respectively, are subject to the concern that these strength-categories are relative to experimental settings. Assume that the weakest targets in context A are stronger than the strongest targets in a different context B. The assumption that the former targets are never detected (when the model is applied in context A), whereas the latter targets are always detected (in context B) conflicts with an absolute interpretation of target detection. Once more, there is no such problem for FRM qua model for super-recognition, because it does not assume that the weakest targets are never detected.

Finally, Bayesian parameter estimation methods ( Gelman, Carlin, Stern, & Rubin, 1995 ) may bypass some of the problems imposed by non-identifiability ( Chechile & Meyer, 1976 ). In such methods, one defines a prior probability distribution over the model-parameter space and derives parameter-estimators based on the posterior distribution (given data). Posterior- mean  estimation, for example, provides a unique-estimator even if the focal model is non-identifiable. Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) estimation, however, demands more caution. First, MAP encounters the identifiability problem when a non-informative uniform prior is postulated, because for such a prior the posterior density is proportional to the likelihood function, which has multiple maxima when the model is non-identifiable. A non-uniform prior, on the other hand, might provide a unique MAP-estimator (for a non-identifiable model) if it yields a single-peaked posterior density. Nevertheless, informative priors can also ‘create a problem’ by generating multiple posterior maxima when the likelihood is single-peaked and the model is identifiable. More generally, using Bayesian methods without explicitly exploring identifiability properties could prove to be a ‘double-edged sword’: if an identifiability problem was successfully sidestepped, then the modeler may remain ignorant with respect to the problem, entailing the risk that the model might later be used in other contexts, wherein non-identifiability can distort theoretical interpretations.

Model-comparison methods that penalize models based on the flexibility of their functional form, rather than parameter-counts, bypass the inflated-penalty problem for non-identifiable models, which affected the study of  Chen et al. (2015) . Such methods include, among others, the normalized maximum likelihood (e.g.,  Klauer & Kellen, 2015 ;  Myung, Navarro, & Pitt, 2006 ), the Fisher information approximation ( Rissanen, 1996 ), the parametric bootstrap cross-fitting method ( Jang, Wixted, & Huber, 2011; Wagenmakers, Ratcliff, Gomez, & Iverson, 2004 ) and–when contender-models form a sub-model hierarchy–the bootstrap generalized likelihood-ratio test ( Moran & Goshen-Gottstein, 2015 ). Note, however, that even when an adequate penalty is applied, model non-identifiability may pose problems for fitting algorithms, hindering convergence to a global minimum.

The current research emphasized the necessity to identify models, not only in recognition, but in any domain of research. If model-based conclusions are to be valid, the perils posed my non-identifiability must be addressed. Modelers are advised to pay special attention to identifiability-issues when developing model-extensions and/or introducing model-relaxations. These situations are risk-prone because, when a model is identifiable, it is tempting to believe that so are its extended and/or relaxed variants. Alas, this is not necessarily true, as the example of FRM demonstrates. Importantly, the current research highlighted the importance of identifiability (and non-identifiability) proofs for a deeper understanding of models and their operation. Furthermore, non-identifiability proofs provide essential guidance towards solving identifiability problems. By revealing the reasons for identifiability-failures, proofs educate the modeler with respect to identifying solutions.
