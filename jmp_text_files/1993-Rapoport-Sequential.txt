We consider a class of sacia] dilemmas in which groups of size » are asked to share a common resource pool whose exact size, x, is not known. Rather, x is sampled randomly from a probability distribution which is common knowledge. Each group member requests a share of the common pool; requests are made sequentially with complete information about the preceding requests. Individual requests are granted if and only if the total group request is equal to or smaller than x. Experimental data show significant and consistent effects due to the player's position in the sequence and the amount of uncertainty about x, The results are used to test competitively two models—a subgame perfect equilibrium solution grounded in the logic of game theory and a modified equal share model based on the notion of focal points. On the average, the modified equal share model outperforms the equilibrium model in predicting the players’ requests. Additional tests of the two models are proposed. © 1993 Academic Press, Inc. 

1. INTRODUCTION 

Resource dilemmas arise when the combined requests of a number of individuals from a common pool of shared resources exceed the supply and their rate of consumption is sufficiently high to threaten the future functioning of the pool. As noted by Edney (1980), each member of the group faces the dilemma whether to reduce his/her request from the resource pool, thereby decreasing his/her gain and sacrificing his/her desire and freedom of comsumption, or to continue depleting the common pool at the same or higher rate, thereby threatening its existence. depletion of groundwater, and deforestation in the Amazon basin are all examples of resource dilemmas in which initial natural resources are exhausted because of overconsumption. 

The structure of the resource dilemma was investigated by Hardin in his classical essay “The Tragedy of the Commons” (1968). Hardin examined the hypothetical situation in which a number of herdsmen sharing a common pasturage must decide how many sheep to graze on the commons. When an individual herdsman considers whether to increase his herd by one more sheep and letting it graze on the commons, he realizes that the gain from this action accrues to him while the loss from reducing the supply per sheep is distributed among all the herdsmen. Therefore, each herdsman has an incentive to increase the size of his herd, butif all do so the ultimate outcome is the destruction of the commons. 

Hardin’s analysis and its controversial conclusions have had much impact on the theoretical analysis of population contro! (Baden, 1977), pollution contro) (Dawes, Delay, & Chaplin, 1974), provision of public goods, and management of natural resources. The analysis also stimulated experimental research on a class of social dilemmas known as resource dilemmas (Allison & Messick, 1990; Brechner, 1977; Jorgenson & Papiack, 1981; Kramer & Brewer, 1984, 1986; Messick, Wilke, Brewer, Kramer, Zemke, & Lui, 1983; Samuelson & Messick, 1986a, 1986b; Rutte, Wilke & Messick, 1987; Samuelson, Messick, Rutte & Wilke, 1984). 

Underlying most of this research is the assumption—implicit in Hardin’s parable—that the size of the resource is commonly known. Suleiman and Rapoport (1988) and subsequently Rapoport, Budescu, Suleiman, and Weg (1992) and Budescu, Rapoport, and Suleiman (1990) have questioned the plausibility and generality of this assumption. They have pointed out that many social and economic problems exhibiting the structure of the resource dilemma do not satisfy this assumption because no mechanisms exist to determine the size of the resource with precision. Thus, the carrying capacity of the commons in Hardin’s celebrated parable may not be determined with precision, the reserves of oil cannot be determined to the last barrel, and the number of whales cannot be determined with certainty. Rather, resource dilemmas are frequently characterized by environmental uncertainty regarding the size of the resource as well as strategic uncertainty regarding the requests of the other group members (Rapoport & Suleiman, 1992). A simple, single-stage resource dilemma game was devised by Rapoport ef ai. (in press) to study the joint effects of environmental and strategic uncertainty without adding complexity and sacrificing mathematical tractability. In this resource dilemma, groups of n persons participate in a sequence of independent, single-trial noncooperative games in which the actual size of the resource pool in each trial is determined by sampling independently from some probability distribution. Whereas the actual size of the resource on each trial is unknown, the parameters of the resource distribution are common knowledge. Players make their requests from the resource pool simultaneously and anonymously. If the total group request exceeds the resource, players receive no payment for the game. If not, each player is granted his/her request.

With the exception of two studies by Rutte ef a/. Rapoport efa/. (1992) and Budescu efa/. (1990), also implemented the same protocol of play, There have been no attempts to examine an alternative sequential protocol of play, in which requests are made in order such that each group member is fully informed of the total requests of all the players preceding him/her in the sequence. The investigation of the sequential protocol is important for at least two major reasons (Erev & Rapoport, 1990). First, in many dilemmas concerning natural resources decisions are made sequentially rather than simultaneously. This is certainly the case in the devastation of pastural lands, depletion of groundwater, and deforestation. Second, many resource dilemmas provide an incentive for some of the players to bind themselves to particular courses of action thereby inducing the remaining players in the sequence to take actions that they do not particularly like. For example, in the resource dilemma game of Rapoport ef ail. (1992) played under the sequential protocol, the first player in the sequence has an incentive to make a relatively large request, thereby inducing—although by no means forcing—the remaining players to lower their requests or face the risk of realizing no profit at all. This is the “first mover” advantage that also appears in two-person sequential bargaining with time discounting and in two-person ultimatum games (see Roth, 1988). Similar but weaker incentives may present themselves to other players in the sequence, depending on their position in the sequence and the requests already made by previous players. Of course, like in two-person bargaining games in extensive form, players who appear early in the sequence may hestitate to fully exploit their advantage because of the risk of antagonizing the remaining players in the sequence each of whom may unilaterally block all the players from getting their requests. In an attempt to simplify the task of finding solutionsto the resource dilemma players may focus on one or more focal points and then apply some simple heuristic like equity or equality. 

When the resource distribution is uniform, two focal points suggest themselves. The first is the upper limit of the distribution—the maximum value of the resource

This is the maximum amount that player j may obtain when it is his/her turn to state his/her request; one may think of it as an “aspiration level.” The second focal point—which may be thought of as a “security level”——is the lower limit of the resource distribution. We propose a heuristic model, termed the modified equal share model. The starting point of this model is an equal share of the player’s aspiration level. However, this value is adjusted downwards as a function of the player’s position in the sequence, subject to the constraint that one’s equal share of the security level be guaranteed. A strict equal share of the aspiration level of the jth player is (B—k,_,}/(n—j+1). This value is, simply, the resource’s upper limit, adjusted for the previous requests, divided by the number of players who are yet to make demands. 3. METHOD 

Subjects 

Forty-five undergraduate students at the University of Haifa participated in the study. They volunteered to take part in the experiment by listing their names on a bulletin board. In addition to receiving credit in an introductory psychology class for their participation, the subjects were paid contingent on their performance and the performance of their other group members. 

Design 

The study employed a three-way, within-subject factorial design. The three factors were level of environmental uncertainty (three levels), sequential position in the group (five levels), and the presence/absence of a post-decisional questionnaire (two levels). The actual resource pool on each trial was chosen randomly from one of three different uniform distributions (conditions) with an equal mean of 500 but different limits. The parameters of the uniform resource distributions were a= f= 500, a= 250 and 8 = 750, and «=0 and # = 1000 for Conditions |, 2, and 3, respectively. Note that Condition | provides no variability, Condition 3 provides maximum variability (when » = 500), and Condition 2 falls in between. 

Subjects were run in groups of five. At the beginning of each trial each group member was assigned a position in the sequence (first, second, ..., fifth). The positions determined the order in which the subjects entered their requests. Each subject participated in all the 15 combinations of resource uncertainty and position. These {5 combinations were presented in a different random order for each group. The 15 games were then repeated a second time in a different random order. Each game in the second block (trials 16-30) was followed by a post-decisional questionnaire (see below). We adopted this procedure in order to decrease the contamination of the individual requests by the questionnaire. To eliminate sequential effects, the subjects received no feedback about the size of the resource (except in Condition |) and the outcome of the trial. 

Procedure 

The experiment was conducted by a PDP 11/73 computer. Upon arrival to the laboratory, the subjects were seated in private and soundproof cubicles and were handed written instructions. The instructions (in Hebrew) explained the nature of the task, the parameters of the three distributions, the payoff scheme, and the operation of the computer terminal. The subjects were informed that they would participate in 30 games, and that the limits of the resource distribution and their position in the sequence would vary from one trial to another. 

At the beginning of each trial subjects were provided with information about the trial number, their position in the sequence, and the values of « and f. After players 1 through j;—1 entered their requests privately and anonymously, player j was

To motivate the subjects to maximize gain, they were instructed at the beginning of the experiment that at the conclusion of the experiment six trials would be chosen randomly and the points earned on these trials would be converted into money to determine their payment. At the end of the experiment this payoff scheme was actually implemented (the conversion rate was 20 points to | shekel). On the average, the experiment lasted two hours.

5. DISCUSSION 

Our major goals in the present study were to investigate sequential requests in single-stage resource dilemmas with and without certainty about the exact value of the resource, and to test competitively two models—one incorporating the gametheoretical notion of equilibrium and the other the psychological notion of focal points. We were especially interested in the questions whether players appearing early in the sequence exploit their advantage and whether the magnitude of uncertainty about the size of the resource affects the frequency with which the group members are granted their requests. 

Our previous studies using the simultaneous protocol (Budescu et al., 1990; Rapoport eral., 1992} establishedthe finding that, on the average, when the mean of the resource distribution is held fixed players increase their requests from the common pool as the uncertainty about its exact value increases. The same finding was also obtained under the sequential protocol in the present study. A second robust finding, which holds under both protocols of play, is an overestimation of the random resource as the uncertainty about its size increases. In the present study all the players overestimated the size of the resource as they moved from Condition 2 to 3 regardless of their position in the sequence. We speculated before that the overestimation of the size of the resource may be attributed to misperception of the random mechanism generating the resource due to an illusionary correlation between the mean and variance of the probability distribution. Clearly, this speculation is not sufficient to account for the increase in the mean individual requests over conditions because the rate of increase in the mean individual requests far exceeds the rate of increase in the mean individual estimates. Other speculations forwarded in our previous papers suggesting the existence of different subgroups of subjects with different expectations about the behavior of others cannot be tested adequately due to the lack of independent procedures for assessing subjects’ beliefs about the risk attitudes of others. 

Perhaps the most important finding of the present study—one that could not be established when the requests are made simultaneously—is that players make use of the advantage given to them by their position in the sequence. These results generalize previous findings about the “first mover” advantage in two-person ultimatum games and two-person sequential bargaining games with alternating offers and infinite horizon (Rapoport, Weg, & Felsenthal, 1990). In the present study, players assigned by the experimental design to earlier positions in the sequence requested a larger share of the resource pool than players placed in later positions. This finding holds whether the size of the resource is known with certainty or not. Although the position effect was statistically significant, its size was moderate. In general, players placed in favorable positions did not make excessive requests that might deny later players their “fair” share. 

Two alternative models have been proposed to account for the individual requests: a subgame perfect equilibrium model grounded in the logic of game theory and a modified equal share model incorporating the notion of focal points.

As shown by Figs.1 and 2, in principle the two models are distinguishable. However, the three ranges that we chose for Conditions 1, 2, and 3 do not allow a distinction between the models in terms of their qualitative predictions. The position, range, and uncertainty effects, which do not depend on any parameter, were supported by the findings. 

When the size of the resource distribution was known with precision, the equal share model outperformed the equilibrium model. Whereas the latter model predicts that the first player in the sequence will request practically the entire resource, very few subjects who were assigned the first position in the sequence in Condition | exploited their advantage to the maximum. Because reputation effects were prohibited by the experimental design it is difficult to attribute this finding to the iterative nature of the task. The results (Tables 3 and 4) clearly favor the modified equal share model in the case of environmental uncertainty (f #«), even when an additional parameter (c) is fitted for each subject. Note that under the assumption that all players share a common power utility function with c=0.25 the equilibrium solution coincides with the modified equal share prediction. However, the empirical utility functions do not satisfy these constraints. Most subjects (41 of the total 45) have c values greater 0.25 (most between 0.6 and 0.8), and there is a considerable amount of variability between subjects. To summarize, our results support the simpler heuristic modified equal share model in all the cases examined. 

We do not interpret these results as sufficient for a definitive rejection of the equilibrium model. At least three points justify this position: 

(1) Following standard experimental procedures, we assessed the subject’s utilities in an individual choice task and then applied them to predict results in an interactive group situation. As pointed out most recently by Loewenstein, Thompson, and Bazerman (1989), in interactive situations of the type explored in the present study people may care about their outcomes as well as the outcomes of others. Thus, it makes sense to lock for alternative methods of establishing utility functions in such contexts. 

(2) In deriving the equilibrium requests (Eq. (II), it was assumed that all group members have identical utility functions u(x)=x‘* with equal power indices c. As noted in Footnote 2, this assumption is justified on the grounds that if not informed about other players’ utilities, each individual might assume that they resemble his own. Clearly, this is a strong assumption since not all subjects will necessarily assume that their group is homogeneous with respect to its members’ utilities. 

(3) Finally, although our results favor the modified equal share model, based on its relative accuracy in predicting the individuals’ requests, they do not allow a complete comparison of the various qualitative predictions of the two models. Of special interest are their opposing predictions regarding the relationship between the requests of different players in the sequence, and the range of the resource uncertainty f—«. The equilibrium model predicts that as f—« increases, the
