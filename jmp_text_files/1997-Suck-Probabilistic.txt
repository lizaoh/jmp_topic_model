Random utility theory has been generalized by various authors to a large variety of relations. A fairly general description of this theory is given and its combinatorial, geometric, and probabilistic ingredients are outlined. Subsequently, the technique is applied to the class of biclassification relations. Furthermore, a modification is presented which is applicable in cases in which the standard theory breaks down. The principles of applying the modified version to the problem of interrater reliability are outlined. The paper closes with considering statistical questions both in the standard and in the modified version. ] 1997 Academic Press 

1. INTRODUCTION 

The following problem of categorization will be addressed with the random utility technique: Given a set A of objects and a set B of properties which each a # A may or may not exhibit, subjects are asked to judge which properties of B an element of A has. Ideally, the classification of one subject should be consistent in the sense that if he or she ascribes property b and b$ to a and b to a$ then a$ should also have the property b$. This is what Ducamp 6 Falmagne (1969) call a biclassification (cf. Definition 2). 

Experience shows that neither will one subject be entirely consistent in the sense of a biclassification nor will different subjects agree on the classification. If such an experiment is run with several subjects, a matrix p=( p(a, b)) will be observed where p(a, b) is the relative frequency of subjects which ascribe property b to object a. 

Scaling and measurement theorists keep coming up with methods to account for what they call fallible data. Probabilistic versions of deterministic models have been formulated. One idea is to represent the objects in question not by fixed numbers but by random variables and to think of the (unobserved) values sampled from these random variables as constitutive of the observable behavior. In a large number of observations some features of the objects and parameters of their representing random variables should be detectable.

Formerly mostly parametric models were regarded, i.e., the distributions of the Ui were determined from a parametric family, e.g., the normal distribution in the Thurstone model. However, the pioneering work of Block 6 Marschak (1960) initiated the search for representations by random variables in a much broader context. This development can be retraced in Luce 6 Suppes (1965), Suppes, Krantz, Luce 6 Tversky (1989), and Marley (1990). 

Recently, the framework of these representations has been considerably extended in still another direction: relations other than dominance judgementsï¿½ï¿½which are usually provided by paired comparison experimentsï¿½ï¿½ should possibly be treated by a similar technique. Heyer 6 Niedereï¿½e (1992) formulated the general theory in a model theoretic framework; Suck (1996; 1997) and Regenwetter (1996) developed random representations in the context of semiorders, interval orders, partial orders, equivalence relations, and betweenness relations. 

In the present paper the random utility technique is described in a general setting emphasizing its geometric content. The aim of this method is to represent stimuli (objects, items, etc.) by random variables and to explain data which arise in several Bernoulli experiments, in a way comparable to (1), but with arbitrary relations, not just linear orderings. 

In particular the geometric gist of the technique is stressed. The general approach is applied to the problem of classification data described at the beginning of this section.

6. CONCLUDING REMARKS 

The preceding sections contribute in part to an error theory of measurement, although the roots of random utilities are formulated as scaling techniques. At least 3 of the 15 problems highlighted by Luce 6 Narens (1994) deal with the error problem and the approach taken here is discussed by them, but only in connection with linear orderings. 

It is easy to find an even more general framework for the above-described version of random utility theory: One can regard m-ary relations Rï¿½A1 _ } } } _Am instead of binary ones. The theory generalizes without much ado to this case. There are, however, no characterizations in the sense of Theorem 1 published in the literature. 

Many problems are left open. I shall summarize a few of them. To begin with an easier one, the translation of axioms defining a class of relations into their respective inner product form involving the characteristic functions can be formulated more algorithmic. More of a problem seems to be the question of under which conditions the axioms translate into facet-defining inequalities. In all cases considered so far (linear orders, semiorders, interval orders, equivalence relations, biclassifications) this turned out to be the case, but always with rather specific arguments. 

Finding more facets of the polytopes of often-used relations continues to be of interest both for theoretical and for practical purposes. However, even the case P[n] BC[,][ k][appears to] be rather intriguing. An indication of this stems from the known difficulty of a similar polytope, namely the cut polytope investigated by Deza 6 Deza (1994). 

The considerations of Section 5 leave many questions unanswered. Likelihood ratio considerations look promising in principle, but more detailed investigations of the ML-estimators under the conditions of random utility theory are called for. The overview of Iverson (1991) deals with a different approach to probabilizing structures, but the statistical issues might well be similar. 

It is tempting to investigate how the Iverson 6 Falmagne (1985) approach to testing weak stochastic transitivity works in the case of random utility theory. The Falmagne technique of introducing probabilities in measurement structures consists of mimicking the measurement axioms with probabilities (cf. Falmagne, 1979; 1980).
