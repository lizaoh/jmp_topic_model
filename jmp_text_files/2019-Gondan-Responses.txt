The identification of processing architectures is one of the major topics in cognitive psychology ( Donders, 1868; Sternberg, 1969 ). Systems factorial technology (SFT,  Tonwnsend, 1992; Townsend & Nozawa, 1995 ) distinguishes cognitive architectures on the basis of the response time distributions in factorial experiments ( Eidels et al., 2011; Townsend & Ashby, 1983 ). SFT thereby generalizes  Donders ’ ( 1868 ) subtraction method and additive  Sternberg ’s ( 1969 ) factor method to distinguish serial and parallel processing, with first-terminating and exhaustive stopping rules ( Snodgrass & Townsend, 1980 ). A major strength of SFT is that predictions for different processing architectures can be tested in a non-parametric fashion, that is, without assuming specific distributions for the processing times involved.

The basic experimental framework to employ SFT is a task in which the participant is required to report the presence or absence of a pre-defined target, typically consisting of two components. Double factorial manipulations on the stimulus components are used, and their effect on detection latency is investigated. In the simplest case, a 2  
                         × 
                       2 factorial design is used, that is, the stimuli are defined by two experimental factors, each with two levels. One factor is the saliency of the target, which can be either high or low. The second factor comprises the processing channels; for example, the stimuli can be auditory and visual (e.g.,  Altieri, 2017 ) or they are presented at different locations ( Fific et al., 2008; Townsend & Altieri, 2012 ). In many applications, the task is to respond if any of the two targets is present (OR processing)—or, alternatively, to respond only if both targets are present (AND processing), and otherwise to withhold the response. The OR task is associated with a first-terminating stopping rule; the response can be given as soon as either component has been processed (also known as redundant signals task, e.g.,  Miller, 1982 ). The AND task is associated with exhaustive processing, because an informed response can only be produced when both targets have been processed. Performance with high and low saliency targets in either channel can then be compared by means of survivor or distribution interaction contrasts and mean interactions  ( Sternberg, 1969; Townsend & Nozawa, 1995 ), and conclusions can be drawn on processing architecture and stopping rules.

Some experimental setups within the framework of SFT include single component stimuli and use an OR task (i.e., redundant signals experiments). I do not consider this setup here because of its limited scope to OR tasks, and because it has already been treated elsewhere (e.g.,  Miller, 1982 ). Nevertheless, if single component stimuli are employed, assessment of processing capacity allows for a fine-grained analysis of possible interactions between the channels in OR tasks (e.g.,  Eidels et al., 2011  Figure 4).

Although the assessment of processing capacity has been generalized to redundant signals experiments with incorrect responses  ( Townsend & Altieri, 2012 ), it is fair to state that  Townsend & Nozawa ’s ( 1995 ) seminal theory was derived for easy response time tasks that allow perfect accuracy ( Fific et al., 2008 ). No straightforward solution has been proposed to incorporate incorrect responses into the calculation of the interaction contrasts involved; therefore, studies using SFT interaction contrasts typically exclude the incorrect responses from the analysis ( Houpt & Townsend, 2010  Footnote 3).

In this theoretical note, I consider situations in which performance is not at ceiling and derive predictions for the distribution interaction contrast in experiments in which errors are observed. The paper is organized as follows: After some notation, the problem is illustrated using an extreme example. I then investigate two mechanisms that may lead to incorrect responses in an SFT experiment, and I describe the approaches that are typically used to recover the uncontaminated set of processing time distributions. I will then describe the limitations of the proposed approach and propose an alternative study design for SFT experiments.

I consider two models,  Yellott ’s ( 1971 , Chapter 9) deadline model and  Ollman ’s ( 1966 ) fast guess model. The names of both models are unfortunate because their validity is not limited to slow or fast erroneous responses. In fact, the two models share similarities with the canonical architectures for two choice decisions (e.g.,  Smith & Ratcliff, 2004  Figure 2); namely, accumulator models ( Pike, 1973; Vickers, 1970; Townsend & Ashby, 1983 ) and random walk models ( Link & Heath, 1975; Ratcliff, 1978; Townsend & Ashby, 1983 ). The deadline model ( Yellott, 1971 ) assumes two independent racers. One racer represents an informed response, which is triggered as soon as a pre-set threshold is reached. The other racer is a random deadline; if the deadline is reached prior to the informed response, a guess is made. Because the informed response and the deadline process are independent, censoring by the deadline is said to be “uninformative” and the censored task completion times can be recovered without bias from the contaminated data using the Kaplan-Meier estimator ( Kaplan & Meier, 1958 ). Thus, under the deadline model, standard survival analysis methods can be used to perform an SFT analysis (e.g.,  Kalbfleisch & Prentice, 2011 ). If the deadline produces lucky guesses, a kill-the-twin correction may be applied ( Eriksen, 1988 ).

In the fast guess model ( Ollman (1966) , a better name is “two-state model”), mutually exclusive states exist for informed responses and guesses. In survival analysis terms, this is known as a competing risks situation, and the subdistributions for informed responses and guesses can be estimated using the Aalen–Johansen method ( Borgan, 2005 ) in case of censoring. I will describe the two models in more detail, as well as their practical use for testing SFT predictions. In experimental psychology, response time is primarily used as a performance measure that reflects the speed of mental processing or the buildup of evidence ( Luce, 1986 ). In theory, response time is the time to find the correct solution (in this article, I used the term task completion time). Performance is “good” if the response is fast and correct, and it is “bad” if the response is slow (Case A) and/or it is incorrect (Case B). Interestingly, in mainstream research, these two cases are handled differently: whereas correct, but slow responses (i.e., Case A) are essential part of the data, incorrect responses (Case B) are typically excluded from the analysis. In other words, current response time research relies on conditional performance measures, with performance being good if the correct responses are fast—regardless of the incorrect responses. Thus, there is a mismatch between the theory (good = fast  and  correct) and the measurement (good = fast,  given  correct).

In the present theoretical note, I demonstrated that this mismatch also affects the test of SFT predictions. Without stating it explicitly,  Townsend and Nozawa (1995)  developed the theory for error-free scenarios, and it is not trivial to generalize these scenarios to more demanding tasks that are associated with non-perfect accuracy. If incorrect responses and guesses are external processes (i.e., outside the SFT box that generates the observable response times

Under this restrictive assumption, the Kaplan–Meier or Aalen–Johansen method then allows for the analysis of SFT predictions in experiments with, in principle, any amount of errors.

Matters are more complicated if one allows noise within the system. Using computer simulation,  Fific et al. (2008, Appx. A)  concluded that interaction contrasts for double-factorial experiments are robust for error rates up to 20%. It would, of course, be more satisfactory if there was a formal proof of their robustness. For the classical double factorial setup with salience manipulation, I have shown that such a general proof is not possible (see the counterexample above). If one allows for errors, stochastic dominance is not guaranteed anymore, which in turn affects the derivation of all the theorems in  Townsend and Nozawa ’s ( 1995 ) seminal work.

Their article was widely unnoticed, but it turns out that by manipulation of the onset asynchrony, the problematic consequences of incorrect responses in SFT experiments can be avoided (see Ineq. 8). Note, however, that at this stage, the generalization only holds for the parallel architectures. For the serial and coactive models, matters are more complicated, for two reasons: In a serial exhaustive model, processing of the first component might be finished before the delayed onset of the second component. As a consequence, selective influence might not be established in the onset delay paradigm (see  Gondan & Blurton, 2013  for a discussion). Moreover, it is not obvious how to deal with conflicting information. In the parallel architectures, channel crosstalk is typically excluded by assumption (e.g.,  Miller, 2016 ; for a different opinion, see  Mordkoff & Yantis, 1991 ). For the serial and coactive architectures, additional assumptions would be needed that integrate a positive response tendency in one channel and a negative response tendency in the other channel (e.g.,  Schwarz, 2006 ).
