JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 19, 221-258 (1979)  Alleys in Visual Space  TAROW INDOW  Keio University, Mita Minato-ku, Tokyo, Japan  Geometry of frameless visual space is dealt with. First, parallel and equidistant alleys, horopters in the horizontal plane of  eyes' level are discussed within the framework of the Luneburg's model that the frameless visual space is a Riemannian space  of constant curvature. That basic postulate and the specific mapping functions assumed by Luneburg between the euclidean map  of visual space and the physical space are kept separate, and efforts are directed to make the model applicable to more  natural conditions of our visual space. A possibility is pointed out to remove the constraint "frameless" in the sense that  perceptual geometrical properties are primarily determined by the convergence of optic axes. So far, only alleys in the  horizontal plane extending from us toward infinity have been studied, but more often we perceive parallel lines, horizontal or  vertical, in front of us like shelves of a bookcase. Hence, equations are derived for horopter plane appearing fronto-parallel  in the three-dimensional visual space and alleys running horizontally or vertically on the horopter plane. It is shown that  parallel and equidistant alleys are not the same in the horopter plane as in the horizontal plane, if the visual space is not  euclidean. A method to evaluate the discrepancy between the two alleys without using any mapping functions is stated with some  numerical examples.  1. PROBLEMS AND NOTATION  There is no a priori reason for that the space we visually perceive has to be euclidean in its geometrical structure. Inspired  by a set of empirical observations available at that time, Luneburg(1947, 1948, 1950) proposed an idea that the visual space  be describable in terms of hyperbolic geometry. For almost 40 years since then, the model was modified in part by Blank (1953,  1957, 1958a, b, 1959; Hardy, Rand, Rittler, Blank and Boeder, 1953) and tested in several ways by a number of investigators  (e.g., Shipley, 1957 and many others referred to later). However, except for a few attempts (e.g., Hoffman, 1966; Schelling,  1956), neither fundamental modification nor extension of the basic idea has been proposed, and no discovery of new phenomena  to be included in the model has been reported. The model has three distinguishing characteristics. Firstly, it is tight in its  logical structure and each assumption involved is accompanied by rationales. Secondly, phenomena to be dealt with are global  in character compared with other local phenomena like brightness or color contrast. Thirdly, the model has potentialities to  1 The study was partially supported by a grant from Japanese Bureau of Education in 1974. Primarily, the model is concerned with geometry of frameless visual space as a whole under binocular  observation. By visual space such a space is meant that we are perceiving in front of us and that contains a set of perceived  objects such as chairs, walls, etc. By geometry it is implied in this context that only distances between or relative location  of perceived points and perceptual form and size of perceived objects are dealt with. In other words, brightness, color, and  texture of perceived surface will be matters of concern only to the extent that these affect the geometrical structure of  visual pattern. By the term frameless, it is meant that stimuli are presented in a homogeneous Ganzfeld without any visible  frame of reference such as ceiling or walls, and the perceptual geometrical properties of pattern are primarily determined by  the convergence of optic axes only in the absence of monocular and experiential factors. As will be discussed later, it is  doubtful whether the condition of being frameless is essential in order to make geometry of visual space tractable.  Nonetheless, discussions in the present article will be also limited to frameless visual space and the abbreviation VS will be  used throughout. The subject is allowed to scan the physical space with both his eyes but his head is fixed.  Features of VS to be captured in the model are as follows:  (VS. 1) It extends in three major directions: left or right, above or below, and near or far in the direction extending from  us.  (VS. 2) It is bounded in all directions. In other words, our line of sight always ends up with some percept if "darkness or  blackness" in perception is counted as a percept, too. We cannot perceive infinity or nil and hence there is no gap in the  boundary of VS in front of us.  (VS. 3) Of objects in VS, we can perceive geometrical properties such as curves, straight lines, planes, intersections,  angles, betweenness, and parallelness, etc.  (VS. 4) Of two perceptual distances, we can tell which is larger or two are equal. Usually it will also be possible to tell  something about the subjective ratio between two perceptual distances.  Features of visual space with framework are much richer, but the properties enumerated above will be sufficient for  discussions to follow. An attempt to extend the model to visual space with richer features will be described elsewhere. In  Sections 2 and 3, the essential features of the Luneburg model will be restated to the extent that is necessary for some  extentions in the subsequent section. Ultimately, his model is reduced to the following two postulates, each of which was  derived from a set of rationales.  I. VS is a Riemannian space of constant Gaussian total curvature K and it is most likely that K < 0.  II. A set of specific mapping functions are assumed between the physical space where stimulus points are presented and the  euclidean map onto which VS is projected.  Logically both postulates are independent and what Blank tried to modify was concerned with a part of the second postulate  only. In the present article, the mapping  VISUAL SPACE 223  functions assumed by Luneburg will be used only when discussion about physical configuration of stimulus points is made, and  the main focus will be placed upon the first postulate that is directly concerned with the intrinsic structure of VS. In the  first half of the article, a most basic observational fact on which K < 0 is based, i.e., alleys in the horizontal plane, will  be discussed with special emphasis upon the role of K, which is independent of the second postulate. Two lines of stimulus  points extending away from the subject at the level of eyes and arranged so that these are reported to appear parallel, do no  appear laterally equidistant at every point. Conversely, rows of stimulus points which appear laterally equidistant do not  appear parallel. In other words, the parallel alley (P) and the (equi)distance alley (D) are perceptually not the same and the  latter always lies outside the former in the horizontal plane of eye level (Blumenfeld, 1913). An interesting question is  whether the same discrepancy between the two alleys is to be expected even when no difference of depth is involved among pairs  of stimulus points; namely under the condition that rows of points appear in a fronto-parallel plane and parallel or  quidistant in either vertical or horizontal direction. That problem will be discussed in the second half of the article. EUCLIDEAN MAP; EM  As it is the policy of the present article to keep separate the euclidean map EM and the physical space of stimulus points X  and to define all equations in EM, not in X, hence the relation between VS and EM is described in detail. In the original  article by Luneburg (1947), EM and X were interlocked and most equations were directly derived in terms of physical space,  which makes it difficult to take a more flexible approach. In his third paper, Luneburg (1950) gave equations in EM but  without derivations. Once all equations are derived in EM, then, if necessary, we can modify the mapping functions in whatever  way we like. However, postulate I stated in the previous section is fundamental and it is also touched upon in this section.  The most basic assumption in the Luneburg's model is that VS is homogeneous in the sense of being of constant curvature. On  the one hand, mathematics would have been untractable without this postulate. Yet, the following rationales were provided by  Luneburg and Blank for the assumption.  1. We have a definite sensation of distance of length Sit between two points Pi and Pf in VS3 and moreover we can judge  whether Si; > Ska or Ska > Sii . If this distanceordering relation is supplemented by two technical conditions, convexity and  finite compactness of VS3, then a distance function Si3 between Pi and P1 is uniquely defined except the unit and VS3 becomes  a metric space.  2. If VS3 as a metric space is regarded to be locally euclidean, then VS3 is a Riemannian space R3.  3. We can perceive plane surfaces, VS2, in any given orientation and localization in VS3 and the perceptual geodesics  connecting any two points in a VS2 does not anywhere depart from that plane VS2. This property leads to the conclusion that  VS3 is Desarguesian.  4. If a particular figure is given at a definite place in VS3, another figure can be constructed at any other localization and  orientation in VS3 that is perceptually congruent with the given figure. This property was called "free mobility" in VS3. By  the Desargue sian property and free-mobility, we are lead to postulate I that K is a constant in VS-3. By perceptual  congruence, such a situation is referred to in which the subject adjusts  VISUAL SPACE 227  the size and form of the figure at hand so as to match the standard figure at a distance in experiments on size-constancy.  5. But, some of the above mentioned conditions are testable  independently of others. An experiment of Foley (1964a, b), designed to test the Desarguesian property of VS3, it seems to the  author, gave excellent quantitative agreements with the prediction. A qualitative but conclusive experimental evidence for K <  0 was given by Blank (1961) for VS2. On the other hand, another experiment of Foley (1972) provided quantitative results  against the free-mobility in VS3. Furthermore, when the values of K and a that were estimated by the so-called 3and 4-point  experiments are used, the theoretical alley curves usually do not yield satisfactory fits to the data (Indow, Inoue, and  Matsushima, 1962a, b; Zajaczkowska, 1956a, b).  Of VS, the author is not very optimistic about the possibility to obtain quantitatively interlocking results across different  experimental conditions. Perceptual process in VS and hence the geometry of VS are much more dynamic in nature and we cannot  conceive of VS as an empty container into which different sets of perceptual objects are put in without affecting its  geometry. Geometrical properties of VS may change according to the pattern and size of {Qt} under discussion. The change may  be limited to values of the parameters involved or may include the geometry itself. However, VS under a given condition must  have a geometry. We cannot claim that it must be one of the mathematically established geometries. Nevertheless, it is  interesting to pursue the question, to what extent VS under a given condition can be approximated by an already known  geometry. Insofar as the alley experiment is concerned, hyperbolic geometry in a broader sense is the best candidate. It will  not be necessary to assume that VS as a whole is of a constant curvature. As mentioned in Section 2, perceptual space is anisotropic to  some extent, and in order to take into account the anisotropy, size constancy, as well as role of framework, either the  mapping functions (1.3) have to be modified or the assumption of constant curvature has to be limited to a portion of VS  within a glimpse. According to direction of glimpse, K may change its value and/or mapping functions may change their forms.  By keeping postulates I and II in Section 1 separate, we can take a more flexible approach and perceptual space under more  natural conditions will become object of study.
