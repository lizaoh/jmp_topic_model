Many social interactions can be formulated as rent-seeking contests in which agents compete by expending resources (e.g., money, effort, time) to achieve power, gain fame, secure governmental subsidies, or be granted monopoly rights. The theory of rent seeking was initiated by the pioneering work of  Tullock (1967, 1980) ,  Krueger (1974) , and  Posner (1975) , who focused on rent-dissipation in single-stage contests (see  Nitzan, 1994 , for a review). An important and recent extension of this theory has been to  multi-stage contests  in which two or more stages are interdependent such that expenditures of resources in earlier stages affect the expenditures or outcomes of later stages. Examples include political races, sport competitions, and R&D contests. Political candidates aspiring to the US presidency, membership in the US House of Representatives and Senate, or state governorships typically first expend resources to secure their party nomination and only then, if successful, expend additional resources in the between-party competition.

Theory : Theoretical studies of two-stage contests have been conducted by  Amegashie (1999, 2000) ,  Baik and Kim (1997) ,  Baik and Lee (2000) ,  Baik and Shogren (1995) ,  Clark and Riis (1996) ,  Gradstein (1998) ,  Gradstein and Konrad (1999) ,  Katz and Tokatlidu (1996) , and  Stein and Rapoport (in press) . These studies differ from one another in the institutional arrangements that determine the type of interdependency between the stages of the contest as well as the number of groups, group size, and symmetry (or asymmetry) of the contestants in terms of their initial resources, prize valuation, or both. All of these models assume that the contestants incur expenditures of resources during both stages, and that the winners of each stage—be they single players or groups—are chosen probabilistically by the logit-form contest success function ( Tullock, 1980 ). In contrast to the present study, these models (with the exception of Stein & Rapoport) assume that the resources are not constrained; expenditures of resources on stage 1 affect the outcome of stage 2 but not the resources available to the contestants on stage 2. Although this assumption considerably simplifies the analysis, it is often made to gain tractability rather than reflect reality.  Stein and Rapoport (in press)  have questioned the generality of this assumption by noting that contestants often operate under budget constraints. Firms in two-stage R&D competitions are subject to budget constraints as are candidates in multi-stage political competitions. An agent may win the first stage of a two-stage contest by expending a relatively high proportion of her resources only to find herself with depleted resources not sufficient to win the second stage. Conversely, she may lose the stage 1 competition by not expending enough resources at this stage. Examples from political contests where candidates have been known to miscalculate their expenditures abound.

The budget constraint may be binding depending on the budget to prize ratio. When it is binding, the imposition of budget constraint shifts the focus of the investigation from determining the  extent  of rent-dissipation to the  optimal allocation  of the constrained resources between the two stages of the contest. If it is not binding, then the emphasis shifts to determining the percentage of budget that is not expended. Using the method of laboratory experimentation, the present study is designed to construct and experimentally test the Stein–Rapoport equilibrium model of two-stage contests in which the constraint may or may not be binding. Budget constraints have also been introduced and their consequences discussed in auction theory (e.g.,  Benoıˆt & Krishna, 2001; Brusco & Lopomo, 2002; Che & Gale, 1998, 2000 ).

Experiments : Experimental analyses of rent-seeking behavior are relatively new. A dozen or so experiments have been conducted in the last 15 years to test the predictions of different models of rent-seeking behavior ( Anderson & Stafford, 2003; Davis & Reilly, 1998; Millner & Pratt, 1989, 1991; Önçüler & Croson, 2004; Potters, de Vries, & van Linden, 1998 ,  Schmitt, Swope, Shupp, & Cadigan (2004) ;  Schmitt, Shupp, & Walker, 2003 ;  Shogren & Baik, 1991; Vogt, Weimann, & Yang, 2002 ). A comparison and critical examination of these experimental studies is beyond the scope of the present paper in part because the models they set up to test are very different from one another. For example,  Millner and Pratt (1991)  report the results of an experiment designed to test theoretical predictions of  Hillman and Katz (1984)  that more risk-averse agents dissipate more of their rent.  Shogren and Baik (1991)  test a rent-seeking model with an exit option.  Anderson and Stafford (2003)  test a rent-seeking model proposed by  Gradstein (1995)  in which they vary the entry fee and cost heterogeneity of their subjects.  Vogt et al. (2002)  test a model due to  Leininger and Yang (1994)  in which expenditures are made sequentially, rather than simultaneously, until bidding is stopped by some random mechanism. These experiments collectively testify to the richness of the rent-seeking literature (see  Nitzan, 1994 , for a review). Almost all of them are concerned with single-stage contests, and most of them do not impose a budget constraint on the subjects.

Despite the considerable differences among them in experimental design and hypotheses, most of these experiments share a major finding: Observed rent-seeking behavior significantly exceeds the equilibrium predictions.  Millner and Pratt (1989)  were the first to report that their subjects expended more resources than predicted by the Nash equilibrium under a proportional contest success function (the one used in the present study), as did later  Millner and Pratt (1991) ,  Davis and Reilly (1998) ,  Potters, de Vries, and van Linden (1998) ,  Anderson and Stafford (2003) ,  Önçüler and Croson (2004) , and  Schmitt et al. (2004) . In contrast,  Shogren and Baik (1991)  and  Vogt et al. (2002)  reported support for equilibrium play. However, the former study allowed players to exit the contest (not participate), and the latter study requested the subjects to alternate their bids until the bidding process was stopped randomly. Moreover, the contest success function used in the latter study, rather than being proportional, was highly discriminative. A major purpose of the present study is to determine whether excessive expenditures are also observed in two-stage contests with budget-constrained subjects.

The rest of the paper is organized as follows.  Section 3  presents the contest model and the subgame perfect equilibrium solution.  Section 4  presents an experiment designed to test the implications of the solution. Two conditions are studied experimentally. In agreement with the majority of previous experiments that were briefly reviewed in this section, our results show that the equilibrium solution does not account for the major behavioral regularities. In particular, subjects’ expenditures during stage 1 substantially and significantly exceeded equilibrium predictions for several prize values regardless of whether or not the budget was binding.  Section 5  proposes a descriptive model that  (1)  incorporates a non-pecuniary utility for winning either stage of the contest, irrespective of the prize value, and  (2)  allows for a systematic misperception of probabilities consistent with the weighting function of  Tversky and Kahneman (1992)  and subsequent experimental evidence supporting it (e.g.,  Wu & Gonzalez, 1996; Prelec, 1998 ). Whereas the equilibrium solution for the descriptive model better accounts for the mean stage 1 expenditures, it can neither account for  (1)  the significant difference in expenditures (for each prize value) between players who won the competition on stage 1 and those who did not; nor can it account for  (2)  learning. Therefore, as an alternative approach, in  Section 6  we turn to the fEWA model of  Camerer, Ho, and Chong (2002)  to account for the dynamics of play across iterations of the stage game.  Section 7  concludes.

Subjects : Ninety-six undergraduate students participated in four separate experimental sessions each including 24 subjects. The subjects were recruited by advertisements and class announcements promising monetary reward contingent on performance in a group decision-making experiment. Both male and female students responded in nearly equal proportions. Each session lasted about two hours. The mean payoff per subject was $18.12 in the private knowledge treatment (Condition NI) and $18.08 in the public knowledge treatment (Condition FI). In addition, all the subjects received a $5.00 show-up bonus for their participation.

Procedure : The four sessions were conducted at the Economic Science Laboratory (ESL) of the University of Arizona that includes 40 networked computer workstations in separate cubicles. At the beginning of each session, subjects individually and sequentially drew poker chips from a bag (numbered 1–24) to randomly determine their seat assignment. Subjects were then seated in their designated cubicles, received written instructions (see Appendix A), and proceeded to read them at their own pace. When all the subjects completed reading the instructions, the supervisor privately entertained questions from individual subjects. Very few questions were actually asked.

It is well known in the individual decision making literature that people often make erroneous decisions in the face of ambiguity by relying on judgment heuristics. A commonly known heuristic is that of “anchoring and adjustment.” This heuristic is often invoked when a person focuses on a particular piece of information (which can be completely irrelevant) as a baseline for her decision and then attempts to adjust her choice in the direction of what she believes to be the correct answer. Empirical findings indicate that although people adjust their choices in the proper direction of the normative solution, the adjustment is reliably insufficient “anchored” by the initial focal point ( Tversky & Kahneman, 1974 ). Our purpose in this section is to explore whether an adaptive learning model, that does not invoke the common knowledge assumption underlying the equilibrium solution, can account for the expenditure pattern of our subjects. The “Experience-Weighted Attraction” (EWA) learning model proposed by  Camerer and Ho (1999)  has been shown to be successful in accounting for the behavior of subjects in varied experimental settings ( Camerer, Hsia, & Ho, 2002 ,  Camerer et al., 2002 ). However, a common criticism of the model is that it has too many free parameters, and could, therefore, potentially over fit the data. This criticism is indirectly addressed by out-of-sample validation (see  Rapoport & Amaldoss, 2004 ). It is addressed more directly in the single-parameter functional EWA (fEWA) model where the model parameters are a function of experience and structure of the game ( Camerer et al., 2002 ). It is not our intent here to compare the fEWA with other learning models. (e.g.,  Roth & Erev, 1995; Fudenberg & Levine, 1998 ); this comparison is beyond the scope of the present paper. Rather, we want to ascertain whether the fEWA model can track the mean stage 1 expenditures of our subjects. We have chosen to focus on stage 1 of the contest so that we can investigate whether the over-expenditure on stage 1 is a consequence of a simple decision error or some utility for winning and misperception of probability, as discussed earlier. Neither of these descriptive models fully accounts for all the major patterns discerned in the data. The fEWA model accounts for the non-significant difference between the stage 1 mean expenditures in Conditions FI and NI. The goodness-of-fit measures associated with its performance are of the same order of magnitude as reported in previous tests of the model. However, the fEWA model systematically and significantly under-predicts the mean stage 1 expenditures in both information conditions. The equilibrium solution for the descriptive model accounts remarkably well for the mean stage 1 expenditures and their decline over time ( Fig. 3 ). However, it under-predicts the stage 2 mean expenditures, as subjects retain fractions of their budgets even when equilibrium play calls for expending the entire budget over the two stages of the contest. Neither model can account for the considerable and consistent individual differences in expenditure. Simultaneously accounting for aggregate results and individual differences remains a challenge for adaptive models of learning in both extensive and normal form games.

This experiment has been designed to study how people allocate their budget (effort, resources) in a two-stage tournament. The instructions for this experiment are quite simple. If you follow them carefully and make good decisions, you may earn a considerable amount of money. Therefore, it is important that you try to do your best. A research foundation has contributed the funds to support this research.

Main features of the game : You are one of four players who are asked to compete in a two-stage tournament game with payoffs contingent on performance. Each tournament has the same structure. At the beginning of each tournament, the players are randomly numbered as Players 1, 2, 3 and 4. Players 1 and 2 will compete against each other in the Group A semi-final match while Players 3 and 4 will compete with each other in the Group B semi-final match. Each group winner (finalist) will compete in the final match. The winner of the final match will be awarded a prize. The prize value will be publicly announced prior to each tournament but may vary from one game to another.

The tournament consists of two stages

Stage  1— “ The semi-finals ”: During the first stage of the tournament, each player is given an initial budget of 1000 “francs” (a fictitious currency that will be converted into US dollars at the end of the experiment). Each of you will be asked privately and independently to decide how much of your budget you wish to spend. You can spend any amount between 0 and 1000 francs. The more you spend, the greater your chances of winning the first stage. Once you and the other member of your group have decided how much to spend, the computer will determine the winner of your group. Simultaneously, the two players assigned to the other group will be competing against each other in a similar fashion. The rule used by the computer for choosing a winner in each of the two semi-finals will be explained below.

Stage  2— “ The finals ”: After the computer determines the two finalists (one winner from each group), they will compete against each other in a final match. The other two players—one from each group—who did not win during the semi-finals will no longer participate in this game. Just like in the semi-finals, each player in the finals will be asked to decide how much he or she wishes to spend to compete against the other player. Neither player can spend more than the amount remaining from the initial budget. Once both finalists have made their decisions in the second and last stage of the contest, the computer will determine the Champion.

The Champion will receive a predetermined prize. The other three players will receive no prize at all. Regardless of whether or not a player receives the prize, each of the four players will be allowed to keep whatever portion remains of his or her initial budget of 1000 francs that was not spent during the tournament.

Determining the winner : Once the computer has calculated the chances of winning based on the amounts spent, it will assign “lottery ticket” numbers to each of the players in proportion to the total expenditures. The tickets are numbered from 1 to 1000. For example, if Player 1 has a 0.333 chance of winning and Player 2 has a 0.667 chance of winning (the chances of both players must always sum to 1.000), then Player 1 will be assigned ticket numbers 1–333 (333 tickets) and Player 2 will be assigned ticket numbers 334–1000 (667 tickets).

After each player has been assigned her tickets, the computer will randomly generate a number between 1 and 1000. The winner of the contest will be the player holding the lottery ticket number corresponding to the randomly drawn number. Thus, the greater the “chances of winning” are for a player, the more lottery tickets are been assigned and thus, the more likely she is to be holding the winning ticket.

Note that under this rule it is possible for a player to win the contest as long as he or she holds at least 1 ticket. This rule for determining a winner is the same for both stages of the tournament.
