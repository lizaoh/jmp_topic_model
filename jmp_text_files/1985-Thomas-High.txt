JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 29, 231-242 (1985  A Theory of High Mathematical Aptitude  HOBEN THOMAS  Pennsylvania State University  Talent searches identifying mathematically able junior high school youth have shown, on a number of summary statistics,  remarkably consistent sex differences on mathematical aptitude test scores. To date no theory has been presented which can  account for the data. A model is proposed which assumes there is a sex-linked gene which facilitates high mathematical test  score performance. The model accounts for the ordered data relations. (01985 Academic Press, Inc.  Among the most widely known and replicated findings in the individual differences literature are sex differences on  mathematical achievement or aptitude tests (e.g., Maccoby & Jacklin, 1974). Many investigators have reported sample test score  means which typically favor boys. However the most widely known data, popularized by two Science articles (Benbow & Stanley,  1980, 1983), are sex differences in mathematical aptitude reported from the continuing talent searches initiated at Johns  Hopkins University in 1971. While the thrust of these searches has broadened in recent years, the focus has been on  identifying mathematically superior junior high school youth. Performance on the mathematics portion of the Scholastic  Aptitude Test (SAT-M), viewed as a high level aptitude test, has been the major variable of interest. A variety of sample  statistics, including averages, show striking differences favoring boys. While these differences have been known for almost a  decade (Keating, 1976), Benbow and Stanley (1980, 1983) have drawn attention to the consistency of the differences in searches  involving about 65,000 largely seventh and eighth grade youth.  To date no theory has appeared which can explain the data. This paper will provide a theory which accounts for all the test  differences which have been reported. In brief, the theory will propose that a recessive X-linked gene facilitates superior  mathematical performance. The central idea is similar to an earlier proposal which satisfactorily accounted for sex  differences in field dependentindependent data (Thomas, 1982); however in the present case, the model structure must be more  complicated because of the talent search sampling procedure. From a theoretical perspective this model is more interesting  because the empirical facts constrain the model in interesting ways. In 1980, with a broadening of the search, either a correspondingly high verbal or general  intellectual test score was acceptable for eligibility (Benbow & Stanley, 1983). Model development will proceed assuming the  selection test was a mathematical achievement test. No important SAT-M sex differences appear to have emerged as a result of  this change. But to reflect this sampling change the model would need to be altered in ways that will be noted later.  The central empirical facts for which any adequate theory must account in a coherent way have been summarized by Benbow &  Stanley (1980, 1983). They will be referred to subsequently as (i) to (iii) and are as follows: (i) The SAT-M mean for boys  has always exceeded the mean for girls. This difference has never been less than 30 points in any search; a 35 point  difference is perhaps more representative. This finding contrasts sharply with the same subjects' scores on the verbal portion  of the SAT where no consistent mean sex differences (in either magnitude or direction) have been reported. (ii) The SAT-M  variance, in every search, has been larger for boys than for girls; the girls' variance has been typically about 70 % of the  boys' variance. Again, there have been no consistent differences in the verbal score variances for the same subjects. (iii)  For a fixed SAT-M score, s, the ratio of the proportion of boys with scores at or exceeding s, relative to the corresponding  proportion of girls, has always exceeded one; as s increases, the ratio increases. For instance, if s = 420, the ratio is  about 1.5: 1; if s = 700, the ratio averages about 13: 1. GENERAL DISCUSSION  This development has shown that derivations from a falsifiable model with one sex difference parameter are in correspondence  with all the ordered data relations (i)-(iii ). Furthermore, the constraints imposed on the model by empirical data,  particularly that q must be small, appear reasonable and necessary for the model to be plausible.  Furthermore, the numerical analysis suggests that the model is satisfied for plausible ranges of the parameter values. The  model also makes testable predictions. For example, it is expected that mathematical achievement test scores  240  HOBEN THOMAS  (untruncated) should be a 2-component mixture distribution. It is unfortunate that the conceptual complexity of the model is  greatly increased with the addition of the truncation parameter t; yet it is difficult to imagine any simpler model accounting  for the data as well.  Parameter estimation and model testing would require a large sample of achievement test and SAT-M data pairs. Unfortunately,  such data may not be readily available.' In any event, some model simplification, particularly in the truncated case, would be  necessary in order to make estimation and testing feasible.  No model of reality may be regarded as correct. A clear point of model vulnerability concerns the assumed bivariate normality  of N. No psychological test scores are normal (nor a mixture of normal distributions) at least not exactly, since test scores  are positive. Furthermore, it is in the tails of the normal distribution where the discrepancy between reality and theory is  likely to be greatest, and it is precisely the tail areas that provide fascinations for the talent search data. Note however,  that the model predictions concerning the ordering of the means and variances in the untruncated case are not dependent on  normality assumptions. Most remaining developments, particularly those for the truncated model, are dependent on the normality  assumption.  Since the model does not apply to boys and girls selected by verbal or other nonmathematical tests, if the number so selected  by such tests were not negligible, the model would need to be altered. For instance, (1) would require three components: the  third component would represent the proportion selected by the verbal test. Such a model, with truncation, would be difficult  to parameterize. There is clearly a simple alternative: report data separately for those selected by different tests. Benbow  and Stanley (1983) make no comment regarding the proportions selected with different types of tests.
