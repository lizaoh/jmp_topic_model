The model for ordinal factor analysis represents individuals as vectors and represents their tankings on tests as projections onto suitable directed axes. This paper constructs such a vectorial model (analytic affine and spherical geometries) from a synthetic geometry whose undefined concepts are abstract orders on an arbitrary set. The first part of the construction uses Only the equivalence relations obtained from the set of orderings. A series of seven axioms permits construction of linear and dual (spherical) subspaces satisfying the ordinary dimensional rules. The one-dimensional linear objects constructed in this way are called fines. The second half of the construction is based on Axiom 8, which asserts that all the orderings are consistent in the betweenness relation induced on any given line. This permits the introduction of order topology on each line. Four topological axioms are introduced, including connectedness of lines, which implies constructibility of certain intersections. A theory of affine congruence can then be developed, leading to the introduction of real-valued coordinates. © 1993 Academic Press, Inc.

The ordering of individuals on a particular test is simply the ordering of the orthogonal projections of their vectors $(b) on a line in the direction determined by the vector a(a). 

It seems natural to ask what patterns of empirical observations are consistent with this model. In the present paper, we focus on orderings of individuals for each of a number of tests. Putting aside statistical modeling, we assume perfectly reliable orderings, which can be completely described in terms of a limited number of factors (in particular, there can be no “unique” factors; see Mulaik, 1972, for a discussion), and we ask what properties must be satisfied by these orderings in order that they be representable as orthogonal projections in different directions within a fixed, finite-dimensional space. This question was considered by Bennett (1956; see also Bennett & Hays, 1960; Coombs, 1964, Chap. 12), who discovered an important necessary condition for a model in at most n dimensions. We discuss Bennett’s condition in our concluding section. 

From a different standpoint, our question concerns the foundations of affine and spherical geometry. In the classical axiomatic treatments of these foundations, the undefined concepts are points, lines, planes, etc., and an incidence relation for points with lines, lines with planes, points with planes, etc. A typical axiom states that for any two distinct points, there is exactly one line incident with both. (See Suppes, Krantz, Luce, & Tversky, 1989, Chap. 12 and 13.) Our previous work, motivated by the need for a measurement-theoretic analysis of multidimensional scaling, axiomatized various metric geometries, using an ordering of pairs of points as the main undefined concept (Beals & Krantz, 1967; Beals, Krantz, & Tversky, 1968; Tversky & Krantz, 1970). In this paper, motivated by the possible application to factor analysis, we reconstruct these geometric foundations on the basis of quite different undefined concepts: points (individuals) and orderings of the points (results of tests). That such a reconstruction is possible seems less surprising if it is recognized that, under the scalar product model, the equivalence classes of individuals under any given test lie in n-1-dimensional hyperplanes. Thus, given the individuals and their orderings for different tests, one obtains points and hyperplanes; and the intersections of hyperplanes—equivalence classes for several simultaneous tests—produce the standard affine geometric objects with dimensionalities inter-

mediate between points and hyperplanes. There are, however, several interesting aspects to this construction. 

First, one discovers what sorts of assumptions about the test orderings are needed in order that the subsets constructed from intersections of equivalence classes satisfy the usual geometric incidence axioms. This part of the theory, developed in our first three sections, gives a qualitative axiomatization of the concept of linear dependence. It is quite elementary, very pretty, and possibly instructive for students of linear algebra, so we have taken pains to make it mathematically accessible. 

Second, the goal of applying the construction as a theory of measurement of traits or abilities leads us to place certain restrictions that are not usually imposed in studies of the foundations of geometry. 

The picture that guides us has both idealized and realistic aspects. We represent individual trait or ability profiles as points of the n-dimensional affine space, and tests as directions within the n-dimensional vector space, ie., as elements of the n-1-dimensional sphere. The individuals range over a bounded convex region of affine space, while the test directions form an open cone. In particular, we assume that there are potentially as many individuals to test as we like, filling out a convex region; but we do not assume an entire affine space. Similarly, tests can be varied continuously, but within a limited cone of directions. The geometry of a bounded region is’ potentially more difficult to develop than that of a full affine space, since expected intersections need not exist-—-for example, two coplanar lines may be neither parallel nor intersecting. The task turned out to be simpler than we expected, largely because there is an alternative criterion for parallelism, far more convenient than nonintersection. Two subsets of individuals are parallel if they are distinct equivalence classes for the same family of tests. The constructions make very heavy use of such parallel sets. 

Finally, each test is only a direction, not a magnitude; we make no assumption that it is meaningful to compare the position of individual 6 on test a with that of individual 6’ on a different test a’, nor even to compare scores of the same individual on two different tests. Thus, one can imagine making tests as long as required to achieve a desired approximation to perfect ordinal reliability; there wil] be no comparisons among the scores for different tests, only among scores of individuals with respect to each test. For example, in a piano competition, one can rank the pianists with respect to performance of Tchaikovsky and again with respect to Bach; there is no requirement to compare a performance of Tchaikovsky’s music with one of Bach’s. In the measurement literature, this situation is sometimes called conditional connectedness of an order relation; that is, comparisons can be made only for (a, b) pairs in which the a terms are identical. In the present context, it seems simpler to consider each a as a separate ordering of the b's.

7. TESTING AXIOMS AND CONSTRUCTING SCALES 

The present axiom system, in common with much foundational work, assumes error-free orderings and a potential continuum of entities (individuals and tests). While these are not strictly attainable, one may hope that the actual situation is sufficiently well approximated by such idealizations. We avoid what we consider to be less attainable concepts: in particular, we do not assume that results on different tests can be compared, and our axioms do not force the set of individuals to be unbounded in the numerical representation. 

Under these circumstances, some axioms are difficult or impossible to test in isolation, because they make use of the idealized underlying continuum; yet this difficulty is not necessarily distressing, since, modulo the idealization, the axioms seem intuitively to be true in many of the intended interpretations. Therefore, serious testing is not envisaged. These are the “technical” axioms. Other axioms are directly testable in isolation, in the envisaged empirical interpretations. A third class consists of axioms that are hard to test directly (because they involve determinations of exact equality with respect to one or several of the weak orders), but that have testable consequences in conjunction with some of the other axioms. In the present paper we have relied heavily on axioms of this third type, accepting the difficulties of testing in order to make use of mathematically simple concepts. 

A pervasive problem relates to the status of equivalence assertions, of the form ab =ac, or more generally, bJc, where J < A. Such assertions may be much harder to verify than inequalities, e.g., b>, c. (See Suppes et a/., 1989, Chap. 16.) A substantial improvement, from the standpoint of testability, would be a formulation that used only inequality observations. Bennett’s Axiom, discussed below, could be viewed as a contribution toward such a formulation. 

We present a summary of the axiom system, followed by a brief discussion of all 12 axioms in light of the preceding distinctions. The last part of the section briefly discusses problems of scale construction.

Discussion of the Axiom System 

Weak Ordering. Axiom 1 comprises two subconditions, transitivity and comparability. Transitivity is often taken as the prototype of a directly testable axiom, though in practice, there can be severe problems due to “random error,” and others due to “systematic error.” In addition, the equality part of the transitivity condition involves the difficulties with equivalence assertions mentioned above. At any rate, establishing that the elements a of A correspond to procedures that (apart from observational errors) generate transitive binary relations is a key requirement for application of the present theory. The comparability subcondition is usually a technical axiom. That is, for some ordering procedures, it is clearly wrong, and such binary relations therefore cannot be modeled as projection on a direction in a vector space; while for other binary relations, it is obvious that the ordering procedure can be applied in principle to any pair of elements of B. 

Separation of Points. Axioms 2 and 3 can both be made to be true, by treating the points in B as equivalence classes of individuals, by treating the points in A as equivalence classes of orderings, and by choosing only one of two opposite orderings for inclusion in A. 

Well-Defined Span. Axiom 4 asserts that if 6’ ties with 5 for every test in set J, but these two individuals are distinguished by test a, then for any other individual c, there is some corresponding individual c’ who ties with c for every test in J but is distinguished from c by test a. Direct tests of this encounter two problems. One is the verification of equivalences or ties, discussed above. The second problem is, where should one look for such a c’? If one doesn’t look in the right place, then failure to find such an individual is not much good as evidence that the axiom is false. This second problem is eased considerably when Axiom 4 is tested in conjunction with Axioms 5 and 6, so that we have bases available for subspaces. Thus, suppose that dim(A}=WN and that J consists of an independent set of cardinality N—1. Then every individual c’ distinct from c but tied with c for all the tests in

The counterexample given in Section 1 shows clearly that it cannot be purely technical, ie., it is closely related to the notion that each test is “unidimensional.” As formulated, it seems to be testable mainly via its various consequences (propositions in Sections | and 2).

To show that the dimension exceeds n, it suffices to exhibit tests @,,..., 4,4, such that some pair of individuals is tied for a, but not for a), some other pair is tied for a, and a, but not for a;, etc. Thus, modulo the difficulty in verifying ties, Axiom 6 is surely refurable for each possible dimension n.

It is convenient to use Bennett’s axiom as a criterion for determining the dimensionality of a maximal level. From the present axioms, it is not hard to show that n points in a maximal level are in general position if and only if every possible partition of them can be achieved using some weak order in A. Thus, if a set of n points is chosen in a maximal level, and it is discovered that some partition is not achievable by any available test, then the points cannot be in general position. Either the dimension of the maximal level is less than n— 1, .e., N <n, or the choice has been unlucky enough so that the points happen to fall in a lower-dimensional level. 

Note that Bennett’s axiom does not rely on verification of any equivalences. It would be valuable to find a system in which Axioms 4-6 and 8 could all be replaced by conditions involving only inequalities. This probably requires discovery of other results involving only inequalities, and recasting our whole axiom system. We have proved Bennett’s axiom only by making essential use of the coordinate representation. 

Solvability. The essential idea of Axiom 7 is seen in the case of two individuals: if they tie on any tests, then many different tests can be found on which they tie (N — 1 independent tests, in fact). The analysis of this axiom in Section 2 suggested that it should be viewed as a technical axiom.

Scale Construction 

The essential step is the construction of coordinates on an affine line, embodied in Theorem 5.01. Once a line has been determined, coordinatization can be carried out using pure inequality observations, as discussed by Krantz ef al. (1971, pp. 155-157; see also Suppes et al., 1989, pp. 349-352). However, as we discussed above in connection with testing of Axiom 8, the determination of collinearity depends in a crucial way on equivalences, and it is unclear how the scale construction will fare when only approximations to collinearity are available. A similar problem arises in foundations of metric geometry (see Suppes ef al, 1989, pp. 165-168). Clearly, further work on constructive procedures is needed. 

Note added in proof. Wille (1991) axiomatized the representation of an equivalence relation on the full Cartesian product A x B by a bilinear form. In her representation, 8 maps onto an entire vector space (over an arbitrary field), 4 maps onto the dual space, and equivalence is represented by equality of the bilinear function a(a) - 8(b). The methods are algebraic, making use of the existence of dual bases.
