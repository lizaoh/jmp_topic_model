INTRODUCTION  It is generally accepted that the actions or decisions of an individual in an organization or social unit are governed by his  or her preferences, aside from nonnegotiable constraints and external controls. The preferences are among the alternative  actions which the individual can take. Sociological and psychological observations strongly suggest, however, that a person's  behavior is also influenced by the autonomy which the individual can exercise in making decisions.  This paper develops an axiomatic approach to the concept of individual autonomy in a decision-making environment, and derives  a numerical measure of "degree" of autonomy analogous to the utility function of economic/game theory. The discussion will be  limited to an individual in an organization, though much of what we develop may be relevant to other social or economic units  also.  If some autonomy in individual behavior is allowed, the organization has to specify a set of possible actions for the  organizational member, rather than a specific action, for each contingency. Under these circumstances, two different  preference relations will govern the individual's choice from among the allowable set:  (i) the individual's basic preference among alternative actions;  (ii) another preference relation over sets of actions which describes the individual's feeling of autonomy.  The first preference relation is of a standard kind and needs no discussion. Some of the implications of this, in connection with the individual's  choice and his/her performance as a member of the organization, will also be studied. For the latter, another  sociopsychological concept is important-the loyalty of the individual to the organization. Thus we also introduce the notion  of loyalty in an axiomatic manner and develop relations exhibiting the effect of personal preference, individual autonomy, and  loyalty, on organizational performance. (Like all generalizations, the validity of this statement  depends on interpretation and context, which will be clarified later.) NOTATION AND UNDERLYING NOTIONS  The organizational member is assumed to take an action from among a finite set yj (j = 1 to n), depending on the input he  receives. The input is also assumed to be from a finite collection x; (i = 1 to m). The role of the member in the  organization, or his operating procedure (this is the term we shall use from now on), is then specified by the rule which he  uses to determine the output from the input, i.e., a function F: X -Y, where X = {x, ,..., xm }, Y = { y, ,..., y,, }. This  can be considered to be a formalization of the notion of the "role" of a member in the sociological literature (e.g., Porter  et al., 1975). In the extreme case, the organization specifies F for each member. We are considering more flexible situations  here, and we wish to generalize the allowable operating procedures. Thus we consider the member's operating procedure (OP) to  be specified by a probability distribution over the class of all rules F, which class is finite since X and Y are. The member  is assumed to choose different rules according to this distribution. This, of course, includes the fixed rule case by taking  trivial probabilities. An alternative and more convenient way of viewing this is that the member's OP is specified by a  stochastic matrix P (i.e., row sums are unity), whose elements pt, define the probability that the member takes action yj when  he receives input x,. Thus the organization specifies a stochastic matrix P for a member rather than a rule F. We note that in  this section we are not considering any real autonomy, and are only trying to describe the basic framework. Subsequent  Many situations are covered by the above notion of a stochastic OP. The following  INDIVIDUAL AUTONOMY IN ORGANIZATIONS 335  couple of examples should provide an idea of the range of possible interpretations. Consider the organizational member to be a  supervisor whose task is to assign jobs to two subordinates A and B, and assume there are just two types of jobs, x, and x2.  Suppose job x, takes a long time to do and/or the relative frequency of x, type jobs is high. If the supervisor uses a  deterministic OP and always assigns x, to one member, say A, the result may be an overload of A and omissions in job  execution. This is because A can have only a finite job queue awaiting him (Sen, 1984). Feasible operation thus may require  random assignment, and the situation is similar to the need for mixed strategies for feasibility under constraints, in game  theory or linear programming.  In another situation, a member may have to produce a machine part which meets certain specifications. Errors will be made,  however, and a certain percentage of the parts produced may be "rejects." Assuming the organization allows this, the member  would be operating stochastically-producing the part with probability 1 - e and producing "rejects" with probability e, where  e is his frequency of errors.  Clearly the notion of a stochastic OP is an idealization. However, it is a convenient generalization which includes the  traditional view of an OP and is useful in many situations as the examples above show.  The essential trait that differentiates a person from a mere machine component in an organization is that he has preferences  among his actions. Thus we assume the existence of a preference relation over the basic assignment rules or deterministic OP's  F. Assuming consistency of preferences, it is easy to extend these preferences to stochastic OP's, under mild assumptions  (e.g., an OP which requires a distasteful task to be executed only rarely will be preferred over one that does the opposite). PERSONAL AUTONOMY, AmOMATICALLY FORMULATED  Sociological observations suggest (see, e.g., Porter et al., 1975, p. 303), that the productivity of an organization member is  improved if he is given a greater degree of autonomy. Intuitively the notion of autonomy may seem quite clear, but its  mathematical formulation is less obvious. What follows is such a formulation.  To begin with, an organization member will presumably feel that he has no autonomy at all if the organization prescribes for  him the output yj, he must produce in response to every input x; he can possibly receive. He may in fact feel the same way if,  more generally, the organization prescribes the (stochastic) OP P which he is to follow. If this view is accepted, it follows  further that he will regard himself more autonomous, if the latitude he is given in the choice of his own OP is greater. This  makes it necessary to define what is meant by "latitude."  Surely if this "latitude" exists, the member will not be required to operate under a single OP. Rather, he will be assigned a  set 7r of OP's and be left free to choose the P in 7t under which he wishes to operate. His perception of the latitude of  choice that he has been given under this arrangement is likely to depend on the utilities he attaches to the various P in 7r.  Thus one may surmise that he will be unimpressed by the degree of his autonomy if, for example, he is indifferent among all P  in 7r. Such observations suggest that a member will have preferences among various sets 7r of OP's that may be assigned to  him, and these preferences will be related to the range of preferences he attaches to the individual OP's available to him in  each of those sets. CONCLUSIONS  This paper has developed a mathematical basis for certain behavioral characteristics of individuals in an  organization-specifically for the notions of autonomy and loyalty. An attempt has been made to clarify the interplay between  the concepts of personal satisfaction, individual autonomy, loyalty to the organization, and organizational performance.  Clearly this is just a first effort in this direction, and certain simplifying assumptions have been made. Further work will  have to address the task of building on this model for more realistic situations or of developing alternative models for this  purpose. The approach has been intentionally axiomatic to facilitate this and make comparisons easier.  The results of this paper also affect the issues of organizational design and control. Much has been written on the  qualitative aspects of these (Porter et al., 1975; Simon, 1976), but most attempts at a quantitative approach seem to have  proceeded on the basis that organizations can be designed and controlled analogous to a system of machines (see, e.g., Carzo &  Yanouzis, 1967). Taking into account the behavioral characteristics studied in this paper, this analogy breaks down.  A system consisting of human beings, unlike one made up of machines, will perform poorly if it is specified as completely as  the latter usually are, because complete specification implies complete absence of autonomy. Even the notion of an optimal  system design loses much of its conventional meaning. The designer, in an effort to encourage good performance among the  organization members, must leave their mode of operation partly unspecified. This looseness of specification will inevitably  be imparted to the system as a whole, and will leave its performance indefinite. The performance will, mathematically  speaking, be set-valued rather than point-valued. Its optimization, in the normal sense of the term, will accordingly be a  meaningless proposition. Planning and control with set-valued uncertainty may be the right treatment (Sen, 1982). It will be  of interest to study the implications of results of this paper in this direction.
