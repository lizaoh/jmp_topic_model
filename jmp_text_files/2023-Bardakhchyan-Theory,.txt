The history of expected utility theory (EUT) started with Bernoulli’s work resolving the St. Petersburg paradox ( Stearns, 2000 ). Several axiomatic schemes for EUT are known ( Luce & Raiffa, 1989; Savage, 1972 ). Currently, EUT has applications in a wide range of fields, including economics ( Hey, 1997 ), psychology ( Baron, 2000 ), evolutionary game theory ( Hofbauer, Sigmund, et al., 1998 ), and general artificial intelligence ( Everitt, Leike, & Hutter, 2015 ).

There are also other situations where EUT does not apply. EUT cannot be used directly when the lottery outcome remains uncertain even after the lottery choice has been made. A good example of this situation is the decision problem known as Savage’s omelet ( Savage, 1972 ). To our knowledge, this problem was never studied from the perspective of EUT’s inapplicability. The problem is not in questioning a specific axiom of EUT. Rather, EUT does not work for deeper, conceptual reasons.

We confine ourselves to these two problems because they amount to important (but different) limitations of EUT, which are resolved the regret theory. Other violations of EUT including cases with unknown probabilities (e.g. Ellsberg paradox), and known examples of transitivity violations such as framing effects and preference reversal phenomena are not addressed.

As we show below, both Allais’ paradox and Savage’s omelet can be resolved by the regret theory (RT), which is one of the alternatives of EUT. The main difference of RT compared to EUT is that RT does not operate with a value functional for a single lottery. Instead it counter-factually compares two lotteries. RT has an intuitive emotional appeal, and it is also related to cognitive aspects of decision making ( Bourgeois-Gironde, 2010 ). RT was first proposed by Savage in minimax form ( Savage, 1972 ) [see  Acker (1997)  for an update of this approach], and later brought to its current form in  Bell (1982), Loomes and Sugden (1982) ; see  Bleichrodt and Wakker (2015), Machina (1992)  for a review.  Loomes and Sugden (1982)  extended the regret to independent lotteries and noted its potential in explaining Allais’ paradox.

This work has three purposes. First, we want to show how Allais’ paradox is solved by a transitive and super-additive RT. People mentioned regret in the context of Allais’ paradox [see e.g.  Baron (2000), Bourgeois-Gironde (2010), Loomes and Sugden (1982) ], but so far no systematic and complete solution of this paradox was provided. Our solution is rather complete, because it also predicts conditions under which the paradox does not hold. Both transitivity and super-additivity have transparent meaning for regret theories in general. We do clarify their applicability range. This is especially important for transitivity, because generally regret theories do not lead to transitive predictions ( Starmer, 2000 ). While in context of Allais’ paradox lotteries are compared in pairs, in more general frameworks, with more than two lotteries involved, the violation of transitivity can lead to predictive indecisiveness ( Bar-Hillel & Margalit, 1988 ). So transitivity condition provides applicability of regret theory to general decision problems. Transitivity is one of cornerstones of classic rationality, and it may intrinsically be present even in some of those situations, which look intransitive at an aggregated level ( Regenwetter, Dana, & Davis-Stober, 2011 ). 
                         2 
                      
                      
                         2 
                         This of course does not deny that important truly intransitive situations do exist ( Bar-Hillel & Margalit, 1988 ).

Second, we prove that the transitive and super-additive regret theory is consistent with the stochastic dominance criterion ( Luce & Raiffa, 1989 ). Stochastic dominance is a useful tool, but it does not apply to comparing any pair of lotteries. The previous literature in this direction is mostly negative showing that regret-based approaches violate first order stochastic dominance ( Levy, 2017; Quiggin, 1990 ).

                       Third, we demonstrate—using as an example Savage’s omlet problem—that RT can recommend choosing between lotteries with not resolved outcomes, a task which cannot be consistently addressed by EUT.

In decision making theory the functional form is frequently derived from axiomatic foundation. In contrast, here we first introduce the functional considered, then derive its properties. Section  3  is devoted to Allais’ paradox and its relations to other concepts. Stochastic dominance abidance is considered in Section  4 . Section  5  analyzes Savage’s omelet problem, identifies an aspect that prevents the applicability of the expected utility theory, and solves this problem via the regret. We summarize in the last section.

Allais’ paradox is a major and intellectually rich challenge to expected utility theory (EUT). The paradox generated a lot of activity in reconsidering EUT foundations and proposing alternatives to them ( Allais & Hagen, 2013; Baron, 2000; Kahneman & Tversky, 2013; Machina, 1992; Schoemaker, 1982; Tversky & Kahneman, 1992 ). Methods proposed for its solution include:

(i)  Prospect theory solves the paradox at the cost of violating transitivity ( Kahneman & Tversky, 2013 ).

(ii)  Cumulative prospect theory and rank-dependent expected utility ( Quiggin, 2012; Tversky & Kahneman, 1992 ) hold transitivity, but violate stochastic dominance.

(iii)  Chew–McCrimmon weighted mean approach does respect transitivity and stochastic dominance, but solves Allais’ paradox at the cost of reevaluating the lottery probabilities ( Hong, 1983; MacCrimmon & Larsson, 1979 ); see Appendix for  B  for a detailed discussion. Note that the weighted mean theory were generalized towards lottery-dependent utility ( Becker & Sarin, 1987 ).

(iv)   Baron (2000), Bourgeois-Gironde (2010), Loomes and Sugden (1982)  mentioned regret in the context of Allais’s paradox, but did not provide systematic solution of the paradox via the regret theory.  Bleichrodt and Wakker (2015)  studied the applicability of regret to a group of paradoxes including Allais’ paradox, preference reversals, common ratio effect. The consideration of these effects in a single framework led  Bleichrodt and Wakker (2015)  to abandon transitivity, as needed e.g. for describing preference reversal effects. It was later shown that also stochastic dominance is violated ( Loomes, Starmer, & Sugden, 1992 ). In  Bleichrodt and Wakker (2015)  regret consists of two separate terms: regret functional and utility. This aspect differs from later regret theories; see  Fishburn (1981), Loomes and Sugden (1982)  for attempts to make regret theories axiomatic.

We aim to show that the regret theory can resolve Allais’s paradox by respecting transitivity and stochastic dominance and that it also provides the validity limits of the paradox, i.e. it shows when the prediction of EUT is recovered. We also show that the solution of Allais’ paradox proposes an important constraint  (35)  on the regret function that does differ from the standardly assumed feature of convexity. Finally, we study conditions under which Allais’ paradox disappears and make pertinent connections with experimental results.

This paper studied regret functionals over the utility differences of two probabilistic lotteries; see Section  2 . There are various types of lotteries, from independent to fully dependent that refer to the same state of nature. The regret functional compares the lotteries counter-factually taking notice of their probabilities. For particular cases, the regret reverts to the expected utility. More generally, it does not satisfy the independence (from irrelevant alternatives) axiom of the expected utility, also known as the sure thing principle. This not satisfying is by itself non-trivial and is explored in  Appendix A . In contrast to the expected utility, the regret is also generally not invariant with respect to multiplying the utility by a positive number. It is also due to these two differences compared to the expected utility that the regret is efficient for explaining and resolving Allais’s paradox; see Section  3 . The resolution demands a non-trivial features of the regret functional,  viz.  its super-additivity, which does make an intuitive sense. We show that the regret functional can be chosen such that the regret-ordering holds transitivity. In particular, Allais’s paradox can be resolved via a transitive regret, and this resolution provides a consistent account of changes in monetary outcomes.

We devoted a special attention to relations between (the first-order) stochastic dominance and the regret-preference; see Section  4 . The former ordering is normatively appealing, but it is incomplete, since not every two lotteries can be compared with each other. We show that for independent lotteries the stochastic dominance implies the regret-preference. For dependent lotteries the relations between the two are more complex. Here we proposed a sufficient condition for the implication stochastic dominance  
                         → 
                       regret-preference, which, interestingly is also based on the super-additivity of the regret; see  Proposition 5 .

Finally, we show in Section  5  how the considered regret theory can be useful in those situations, where actions of the decision maker do not resolve the uncertain situation. The expected utility theory does not apply to such a situation in the sense that there is an important information about the lotteries that it simply discards. In the regret, this information is employed, since the regret compares the unresolved uncertainty with the resolved uncertainty.

Our results show that though the concept of regret was initially deduced from certain emotional features of decision makers, it does have many features one intuitively expects from rationality. Hence we envisage its further applications in e.g. reinforcement learning.

We close by mentioning the drawbacks and limitations of our approach. First, we did not address issues related to transitivity violations, though we indicated how the regret theory can model those violations. Indeed, it is reasonable to expect that any representative transitivity-violating regret should hold the super-additivity feature  (35) , but more specific constraints are currently unclear. Transitivity violation is a convoluted issue in decision-making; see  Bar-Hillel and Margalit (1988), Regenwetter et al. (2011)  for reviews. Indeed, it is frequently met in the group-based (social or voting-based) choices, but even there it is frequently vindicated on the individual choice level, e.g. in the Condorcet paradox the individual choices are transitive ( Bar-Hillel & Margalit, 1988 ).  Regenwetter et al. (2011)  argued that a large amount of data on transitivity violation can in fact be explained by assuming that people choose in different states but adhering to transitivity in each given state. There are however also true examples of intransitive decision-making that in one way or another reflect cyclicity that is already present in nature ( Bar-Hillel & Margalit, 1988 ). In this context, we do expect interesting applications of the regret theory for modeling intransitivity.

Second, we may need to generalize the regret to more than pair-wise comparisons. an interesting task that may be eventually related to intransitivity modeling.

Third, our approach is based on utility differences. This is convenient, also for comparing with stochastic dominance, but it also discards pertinent effects. For example, in our approach the regret does not allow multiplicative transformation of the utility function, i.e. multiplication with a positive constant may change the regret-based preferences (utility shifts by a constant factor are allowed).

Fourth, we employed precise probabilities that are trusted, because they accurately represent the true likelihood of events occurring (veridical probabilities). We plan to extend the approach to the case of trusted, but imprecise (upper and lower) probabilities.

Fifth, we need a more detailed regret theory for dependent lotteries. Although we touched on this important subject (Savage’s omelet and sufficient conditions for stochastic dominance), there is still much to be learned about it.
