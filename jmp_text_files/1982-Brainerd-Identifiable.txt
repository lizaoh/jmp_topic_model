HOWE  University of Victoria  AND  JOHANNES KINGMA  Twente University of Technology  An eleven-parameter model for two-stage learning is developed. The model's principal advantage over extant two-stage models is  that its parameter space is completely identifiable, thereby eliminating the tedious procedure of locating acceptable  identifying restrictions. Identifiability is achieved by defining the model over a slightly modified outcome space. Following  the identiflability proof, the necessary statistical machinery for parameter estimation, goodness-of-fit analyses, and  hypothesis testing is presented. These latter developments are illustrated with data from an adult cued recall experiment and  a free recall experiment with elementary school children.  Many human and animal learning paradigms have the interesting feature that subjects are unable to make a correct response  before the first learning trial has occurred. By and large, these paradigms are production tasks in the sense that making a  correct response involves more than a forced choice between well-defined alternatives. Illustrative animal paradigms are  avoidance conditioning, escape conditioning, and Pavlovian conditioning. Illustrative human paradigms are cued recall, serial  learning, free recall, certain types of verbal transfer (e.g., AB -AC and AB -AB,), and certain types of concept learning in  children (e.g., learning conservation concepts).  It has been known for some years that acquisition data from these tasks are normally in close agreement with the predictions  of two-stage Markov processes (for  Preparation of this paper and the experiments reported herein were supported by Grant A0668 from the Natural Science and  Engineering Research Council. These models assume that learning consists of an initial state U in which only errors are  possible, an intermediate state P in which errors occur with some average probability 0 < q < 1, and a final absorbing state  in which only successes are possible. In animals, such models have given a good account of data from rat avoidance  conditioning (Theios, 1963), rat escape conditioning (Theios, 1965), and Pavlovian conditioning (Theios & Brelsford, 1966). In  human adults, analogous findings have been reported for free recall (Kintsch & Morris, 1965), cued recall (Humphreys & Greeno,  1970), AB -AC and AB -AB, transfer (Greeno, James, & DaPolito, 1971), eyelid conditioning (Bower & Theios, 1964), and  discrimination reversal (Bower & Theios, 1964). Perhaps the most vigorous recent applications have been in the developmental  literature, where two-stage analysis has been used with children's cued recall (Bisanz, Vesonder, & Voss, 1978), children's  free recall (Brainerd, 1983), infants' learning of spatial concepts (Heth & Cornell, 1983), and children's learning of logical  concepts (Brainerd, 1982). Current developmental interest in two-stage analysis stems from the fact that it permits  investigators to gain leverage on such perennial theoretical questions as characterizing children's rule usage (Brainerd,  1979), measuring stages of development (Heth & Cornell, 1983), isolating age-invariant properties of learning (Brainerd,  1983), and eliminating stages-of-learning artifacts (Brainerd & Howe, 1982).  The mathematical machinery for two-stage models has undergone gradual development since their initial appearance in the  learning literature (Theios, 1961). As things now stand, most of the technical obstacles that one encounters in applying any model to data have been resolved for  (1). The sampling distributions of several learning statistics have been derived, a maximum likelihood procedure exists for  estimating parameters, and a technology has been developed for effecting withincondition and between-conditions comparisons of  parameters values (for a review, see Brainerd, Howe, & Desrochers, 1982). However, a key problem remains, namely, parameter  identifiability. It has been proved that the number of parameters that can be independently estimated for the outcome spaces  over which (1) is defined is fewer than the 10 parameters it contains. Greeno (1968; Greeno, Millward, & Merryman, 1971) first  obtained this result for a restricted version of (1) in which g = h. He proved that this nine-parameter model had only seven  identifiable parameters. Brainerd et al. (1982) subsequently showed that the unrestricted 10 parameter model has only eight  identifiable parameters. Worse, it turns out that a is the only parameter of (1) that is also a member of the identifiable  set. The other parameters of Eq. (1) are all complex functions of identifiable parameters.  The fact that only a is identifiable in the outcome space of Eq. (1) creates many practical and intepretative problems for  investigators. The chief practical difficulty is that an identifying restriction must be found before the values of the other  nine parameters can be estimated. Several such restrictions can be evaluated statistically using likelihood-ratio tests  developed by Greeno (1968). Generally speaking, a testable identifying restriction is some constraint on the freedom of the  parameters of Eq. (1) to vary that (a) implies one or more constraints on the freedom of the identifiable parameters to vary  and (b) delivers a unique expression for each theoretical parameter in terms of the identifiable parameters. Examples of  testable restrictions include r=e= c=0, a'b'=ab, b=d, and c=0, and r=e= 1-g and c=0. Despite the availability of such testable  restrictions, there is no guarantee that one can be found that will be acceptable for all the conditions of a given  experiment. In fact, if an experiment contains a large number of conditions with treatments whose effects on the parameters of  Eq. (1) are powerful, it will be very difficult to locate a restriction that is acceptable for all conditions. But unless one  can be found, the treatment effects cannot be measured with the parameters of Eq. (1).  When some restriction is acceptable for all conditions, new complications arise from the fact that parameter estimates will  always be relative to the identifying restriction. That is, instead of being able to say, "The effect of the treatment on  parameter x was this," one can only say, "The effect of the treatment on parameter x was this under the identifying  restriction." This leads to two interpretative dilemmas,  one that crops up within given experiments and one that occurs in comparing the results of different studies. The  within-experiment dilemma refers to studies where there is more than one acceptable restriction. Because the number of  testable restrictions is large, this is the normal situation for any experiment with a small number of conditions. Because  different restrictions will give different estimates of the parameters of Eq. (1), the overall pattern of findings may be  quite different for different restrictions. For example, a treatment may be found to have large effects on the parameters r,  e, and c when the identifying restriction is a'b' = ab and b = d = c, but it cannot have any effect on these same parameters  when the identifying restriction is r = e = c = 0. The between-experiment dilemma occurs when there are two (or more)  experiments concerned with the same treatment that make use of different identifying restrictions. If different patterns of  parameter behavior are observed in the experiments, this may simply be attributable to differences in the methods of parameter  estimation rather than to actual discrepancies in findings. In the cued recall literature, for example, Humphreys and Greeno  (1970) found that word difficulty manipulations have larger effects on State U parameters than on State P parameters, whereas  Brainerd et al. (1981) found that such manipulations have comparable effects on States U and P parameters. But it is unclear  whether this discrepancy is due to the fact that different manipulations were used or to the fact that Humphreys and Greeno's  restriction was a'b' = ab, b = d, and c = 0 while that of Brainerd et al. was r = e = c = 0 or both.  Although the process of finding and testing identifying restrictions creates many other problems, these few examples suffice  to show that two-stage learning analysis would be greatly facilitated if a completely identifiable model were available. We  develop such a model, along with the necessary procedures for applying it to data, in this paper. In the first section, we  present the model and provide an identifiability proof. Except for the addition of an eleventh parameter that measures  forgetting, its parameters are the same as those of Eq. (1). The reason that this model is identifiable is that it is defined  over a slightly different outcome space than (1). In the second section, procedures for parameter estimation and hypothesis  testing are given. In the third section, goodness-of-fit tests are developed. An interesting feature of the third section is  that a completely identifiable model of one-stage learning is presented. We also show that the parameter space of the  one-stage model is a subspace of the parameter space of the two-stage model, which means that the goodness-of-fit test for  one-stage versus two-stage learning has an exact Xz distribution. In the last section, we illustrate the procedures developed  earlier with the data of an adult cued-recall experiment and a child free-recall experiment.  MODEL AND IDENTIFIABILITY PROOF  A key point to bear in mind about identifiability is that it is not a property that is intrinsic to models per se. Because the  usual procedures for investigating identifiability are mathematical rather than empirical, it is natural to regard iden-  tifiability as depending solely on the structure of a model. In fact, however, it depends on both model structure and the  outcome space over which a model is defined. It is quite possible for a model that is nonidentifiable or partially  identifiable in certain outcome spaces to be identifiable in others. Essentially, that is what we show in this section. More  particularly, we show that a very minor adjustment in the outcome space of Eq. (1) produces a fully identifiable two-stage  model.  Theoretical Model  The standard outcome space for Eq. It is critical that the algorithm be coded in such a way that the program will search when equality and inequality constraints  are imposed on the parameters of Eq. (2), as well as when the parameters are completely free to vary. The reasons are related  to hypothesis testing. Mathematically, these tests involve repeated minimizations of (30)  under various equality and inequality constraints. If a given program will not search under such constraints, it is  essentially useless in research applications of (2). Necessity tests are concerned with whether or not simpler models give a  statistically acceptable account of the data. As two-stage models assume that learning involves two discrete transitions, it  is logical to compare them to models which assume that learning involves one discrete transition. In those instances where  learning appears to involve more than one stage (i.e., when necessity tests are passed), sufficiency tests are instituted to  determine whether or not learning may consist of more than two stages. These tests involve comparing the observed and  predicted distributions of several fine-grain statistics of error data. Traditionally, a two-stage model has not been said to  be sufficient unless it can give a good account of three classes of such statistics: (a) statistics concerned with State U  (e.g., errors before first success); (b) statistics concerned with State P (e.g., errors after first success); and (c)  statistics concerned with both States U and P (e.g., total errors).  In this section, we develop such procedures for (2). First, we present a necessity test based on a one-stage model for the  revised outcome space. Unlike previous necessity tests for two-stage learning, this test involves a one-stage model that is  completely identifiable and a likelihood ratio statistic whose exact XZ distribution is known. Next, we present the sampling  distributions of some learning statistics from each of the categories mentioned above. Finally, we exhibit some illustrative  results from the tutorial experiments. For each observed-predicted comparison, the  null hypothesis of exact correspondence can be evaluated in either or both of two ways. First, as the precise degrees of  freedom for X2 tests of goodness of fit are not known for these statistics, we can simply compute parameter-free tests such as  the Kolmogorov-Smirnoff. HYPOTHESIS TESTING  In applications of two-stage analysis, the details of parameter estimation and goodness of fit are technical matters that are  not of central concern in the interpretation of data. Instead, principal interest attaches to within-condition and  betweenconditions comparisons. (Of course, such tests are superfluous if there is not clear evidence of goodness of fit.) The  general aim of between-conditions tests is to localize treatment effects within particular parameters of the model (e.g., cf.  Greeno et al., 1971; Humphreys & Greeno, 1970), whereas the purpose of within-condition tests is to determine whether the  first or the second learning stage is more difficult to complete (e.g., cf. Brainerd et al., 1980, 1981). The fact that the  method of maximum likelihood is used to estimate parameters greatly simplifies matters because likelihood ratio tests whose XZ  distributions are known can be devised for both types of comparisons. We outline the pertinent tests to conclude this paper. Within-Condition Tests  Although the bulk of the hypothesis testing in two-stage analysis is between conditions, certain within-condition tests are  often of interest. These latter tests are of two general sorts, namely, exact numerical hypotheses and inexact numerical  hypotheses. The former refer to any prediction which specifies that one of the parameters of Eq. (2) shall take on a  particular value. The most obvious instances of exact hypotheses are predictions that a parameter value shall be zero, one, or  chance. For example, if the subjects in a cued recall experiment are memorizing a very difficult list of words (e.g., CVCs),  it would be natural to expect that no items would skip the intermediate state (i.e., b' and b are both zero). Similarly, in a  two-choice discrimination reversal task, it would be natural to expect that the intermediate state performance parameters 1 -  e, g, h, and 1 - r would all equal the nominal guessing probability of i.  Inexact numerical hypotheses, on the other hand, refer to any prediction which specifies that a particular algebraic  relationship (simple or complex) will obtain between two or more parameters. The most elementary predictions of this sort are  hypotheses to the effect that one parameter is or is not larger than another. For example, certain theories of associative  learning anticipate that the first stage of learning is normally more difficult than the second stage (see Brainerd et al.,  1980), which leads to inexact hypotheses such as a' < b', a < b, a < c, and a < d. Certain theories of free recall make  different assumptions about the relative effectiveness of study trials that follow test trial successes and test trial errors,  respectively (see Halff, 1977). Some theories assume that study trials following successes are more effective (which implies c  < d), others assume that study trials following errors are more effective (which implies c < d), and still others assume that  the effects of study trials are independent of test trial performance (which implies c = d).  If the data of a particular condition have passed goodness-of-fit tests, then the value of LZ (Eq. (30)) will already be known  for that condition. Illustrative Within-Condition and Between-Conditions Results  Earlier (Table 1) we reported the estimates of the parameters of Eq. (2) for the two experiments. Recall that the manipulation  in the first experiment was word difficulty (familiar versus unfamiliar), whereas the manipulation in the second experiment  was age (second graders versus sixth graders). To conclude this paper, we report illustrative tests of numerical hypotheses.  We also note points of similarity between these findings and earlier two-stage analyses of recall data. The between condition  data are considered first.  To begin with, because each experiment consisted of only two conditions, the experimentwise test (Eq. (53)) and the  conditionwise tests (Eq. (54)) are identical for these experiments. For each experiment, the null hypothesis, that the values  of the parameters of Eq. (2) were the same for the two conditions, was rejected; X'(11)=  130.52 for the adult experiment and X2(11)=342.38 for the child experiment. The next question concerns the locus of the  treatment effect in each experiment. As all parameterwise test have a X2(1) distribution, the critical value is always 3.84.  In the adult experiment, word familiarity had a significant effect on six parameters, namely, a', a, b', b, f, and r. As can  be seen from Table 1, the direction of the differences between these parameters favored familiar words in each case. In the  child experiment, age had a significant effect on five parameters, namely, a', a, b', b, and d. The direction of parameter  differences always favored older children.  There are two important similarities between these results and other data on recall memorizing in which the nonidentifiable  two-stage model has been used. First, note that the values of the learning parameter c, the probability of a P to A transition  following correct recall in State P, appear to be insensitive to the treatments, despite the fact that the treatments had  large effects on other learning parameters. This result is consistent with earlier two-stage results on adult cued recall  (e.g., Humpheys & Greeno, 1970), adult free recall (e.g., Halff, 1977), child cued recall (e.g., Brainerd & Howe, 1982), and  child free recall (Brainerd, 1983). Second, note that the values of the four performance parameters (e, g, h, and r) were also  insensitive to the treatment. Of the eight possible parameterwise tests for the performance parameters, only one, the r test  in the adult experiment, exceeded the critical value. This second result is also consistent with previous two-stage  experiments in that constant values of performance parameters have been observed in adult cued recall (Brainerd et al., 1980,  1981), child cued recall (Brainerd, 1983), and child free recall (e.g., Brainerd, 1983). About the only treatment that is  known to have a substantial effect on the performance parameters is pictures versus words in cued recall, with pictures  suppressing performance parameters when the subjects are children (Brainerd & Howe, 1982).  With respect to within-condition differences, it is obvious that a large number of comparisons is possible for each condition.  However, only some of then are sensible, either from a theoretical point of view or on the basis of previous two-stage  experiments. We report results for a few exact and inexact hypotheses of the latter sort. Concerning exact hypotheses, a  familiar finding in previous two-stage experiments on adult cued and free recall is that c = 0 (e.g., Humphreys & Greeno,  1970; Halff, 1977). Hence, we tested the null hypotesis c = 0 for both conditions of the adult experiment. It could not be  rejected in either case. In contrast, it has sometimes been found in child experiments that children learn on study trials  that follow successes (e.g., Brainerd & Howe, 1980), which suggests that this null hypothesis might fail in Experiment 2. When  we tested c = 0 with the two sets of child data, it was rejected in each case.  Turning to inexact hypotheses, previous adult research has suggested that the first stage of learning is normally more  difficult than the second (e.g., Brainerd et al., 1080, 1981), and there are some data indicating that the reverse may be true  with children (Brainerd, 1983). Thus, we are interested in possible inequality relationships between a' and a, on the one  hand, and b', b, and d, on the other. For the adult experiment, we tested the null hypotheses a' < b', a' < b, a' < d, a < b',  a < b, and  a < d with the data of both conditions. Of the 12 tests, only two failed to exceed the critical value of 3.84 (a < b' and a <  b in the unfamiliar condition). It seems, therefore, that it was easier for subjects to enter A than it was for them to escape  U. For the child experiment, we tested the opposite of the aforementioned hypotheses with the data of both age levels. Of the  12 tests, only two failed to exceed the critical value (a' > d in younger children and a' > d in older children).
