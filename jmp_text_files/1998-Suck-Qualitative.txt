A Qualitative Characterization of the Exponential Distribution 

Reinhard Suck 

Universita�Osnabru�ck 

An axiomization of a random variable representation is developed. The existence of the probability measure with respect to which the representations are measurable is derived from qualitative conditions in the sense of measurement theory. The structure is based on a combination of a difference with an extensive measurement structure. Furthermore, a special condition, qualitative as the others, is shown to yield the result that the real valued representations of the structure are random variables with an exponential distribution. The key property is a condition relating the difference order and the concatenation in such a way that shifting two intervals by concatenating their endpoints with the same element does not affect their order relation. � 1998 Academic Press 

1. INTRODUCTION 

Random variable representations derived from qualitative axioms are a major goal of probabilistic measurement. Most of the previous approaches assume the existence of a probability measure and impose conditions on it. The older literature specifies a family of distributions and provides techniques to estimate the exact distribution from the family. This procedure is characteristic of Thurstonean scaling and many related methods. 

A new line of research has been initiated by Falmagne (1976). He also assumes the probabilities given, but they are restrained by conditions closely related to the axioms of measurement structures. Occasionally these are tested. 

The most recent development in this area is the work of Suppes and Zanotti (1992). It differs from all previous work as the existence of a probability measure is derived from qualitative axioms, and the distribution of the random variables is a result of a representation theorem (cf. also Suppes, Krantz, Luce, 6 Tversky, 1989, pp. 366�379). 

The Suppes�Zanotti axiomatization is rather complicated; it relies on a characterization of qualitative raw moments, an extensive structure, and some conditions guaranteeing the compatibility between these structures. Further restraints yield

4. CONCLUDING REMARKS 

There are a few points which should be emphasized in this final section to avoid misunderstandings, to point to open problems and further research. 

(1) In theories of qualitative probability the approach is similar to Section 2. I do not take a _-algebra as a primitive. The sets from which the development of the probability measure starts are the intervals of �. The _-algebra is constructed afterwards. In that sense the function . is a distribution function��axiomatized qualitatively. 

(2) The functions !a , defined in Theorem 5, are random variables corresponding to the elements of A. They are, however, rather different from representing random variables constructed by Suppes and Zanotti (1992). The most striking difference is that the !a are not independent. It would be an interesting problem to use the approach of Sections 2 and 3 to construct independent variables. To develop a method for this case would require some fundamentally new ideas. The simple fact that independent exponentially distributed !a , !b , and !a b b cannot satisfy !a b b=!a+!b is indicative of a deeper problem. 

(3) Can Theorem 5 be used with data? Can its assumptions be tested? This is a difficult question, and I am not too optimistic about that. However, there are a few points in favor of a tentative ``Yes.'' For one thing, the exponential is empirically important; and if subprocesses for which the exponential is a candidate cannot be directly observed, a qualitative evaluation of the probability of their duration can be useful. The next step would be to find scale values, perhaps by a constructive method used in a representation theorem for extensive structures. Then, the validity of the construction can be examined with a goodness of fit test. If it rejects the exponential some of the assumptions are violated, but one does not know which one. This is different from other approaches, e.g., Iverson 6 Falmagne (1985) where the focus is on testing each axiom individually. 

(4) Naturally, the question arises whether some of the conditions on (A, �, b) such as, e.g., associativity, follow from Eq. (9) and the other conditions. I conjecture independence, but could neither find an example proving independence nor, conversely, a derivation of the associative law from the other axioms. Perhaps, one way to deal with this problem is to use a real representation of a positive concatenation structure (PCS) by an operation v in R[+] and interpret the deviation of the representation from the exponential distribution as a non-additivity coefficient of b. For representations of a PCS compare Narens (1985, Theorem 7.1) or Luce, Krantz, Suppes, and Tversky (1990, Chap. 19.3). 

(5) Some of the technical assumptions of the theorems such as Dedekind completeness can perhaps be relaxed if embeddability techniques are applicable (cf. Narens, 1985, Theorems 13.1 and 13.2; or Cohen, 1988). 

(6) Several aspects of the 15 problems discussed in Luce and Narens (1994) deal with error theory. The project of finding random variable representations is

The chief point of the present paper is to develop a framework different from the usual approaches to testing, such as Iverson and Falmagne (1985), Suppes and Zanotti (1992). Seen from this viewpoint, it is just a step for which the famous remark about the dog walking on two legs is applicable: the point is not that the feat was done well, but that it was done at all.
