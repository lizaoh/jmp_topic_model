The study of context effects in decision-making — how choices can be influenced by a supposedly innocuous change to the set of possible alternatives — is a powerful lens for revealing insight into human choice behaviour. Beginning with observed violations of Luce’s axiom of Independence of Irrelevant Alternatives, the existence of context effects have led to the development of new choice theories, and in turn, choice experiments to test them ( Debreu, 1960; Luce, 1959; Rieskamp et al., 2006 ). One noteworthy example is the  asymmetric dominance  effect, in which a choice set containing two alternatives  
                         x 
                       and  
                         y 
                       is expanded to include a third alternative  
                         z 
                       which is dominated on all attributes by only  
                         x 
                      . A large number of laboratory studies in humans and other species has found that this “decoy” alternative tends to influence decision-makers to choose  
                         x 
                       more often, suggesting a violation of the  regularity  axiom which underlies the canonical theory of rational choice ( Castillo, 2020; Huber et al., 1982, 2014; Król & Król, 2019; Parrish et al., 2015 ). 
                         1 
                      
                      
                         1 
                         As described by  Huber et al. (2014) , “the asymmetric dominance effect also violates ‘scalability’, in which the probability of an alternative being chosen can be represented as a positive function of its utility value, and ‘menu independence’, in which knowledge of a preference relation between two objects can be used to predict a person’s choice over different menus or choice sets).... Finally, as ( Tversky, 1972 ) notes, menu independence follows from the assumption that a decision maker has a complete preference order across all options, and that — given an offered set — he or she selects the option highest on the order (i.e., ‘value maximization’)”.

The asymmetric dominance effect and other examples of context-dependent choice behaviour have led to a general recognition across multiple disciplines that preferences over alternatives are not simply known and immutable constructs endowed to a decision-maker, but instead must be constructed at the time of choice in a given environment ( Li et al., 2018; Lichtenstein & Slovic, 2006; Lieder & Griffiths, 2019; Weber & Johnson, 2006 ). For the asymmetric dominance effect, a number of proposed explanations describe how the attributes of a choice alternative are perceived relative to the attributes of other alternatives in the choice set. 
                         2 
                      
                      
                         2 
                         This focus on perception is perhaps largely due to the predominant use of symbolic numeral stimuli in these experiments ( Frederick et al., 2014 ), though these effects have been observed in other perceptual domains including visual stimuli ( Crosetto & Gaudeul, 2016; Farmer et al., 2016; Trueblood et al., 2013 ). 
                       One early hypothesis held that such decoy effects are related to the numerical range of attribute stimuli ( Huber et al., 1982 ). This proposal has recently been formalized by models of  range normalization , in which an attribute dimension is weighted by the range of stimuli on that dimension ( Bushong et al., 2020; Soltani et al., 2012 ). 
                         3 
                      
                      
                         3 
                         These theories are also based on reports that the activity of single neurons adapts to the range of their stimuli (e.g.  Padoa-Schioppa, 2009 ). 
                       As the range of an attribute grows, the decision-maker under-weights that attribute. By placing an asymmetric decoy  
                         z 
                       into a choice set, this increases the observed range on the attribute for which  
                         y 
                       is best (Attribute 2 in  Fig. 1 ). This attribute thus gets less weight and the target alternative  
                         x 
                       appears more valuable.

Alternatively, context effects like the asymmetric dominance effect might arise from  diminishing sensitivity  to objective stimuli — the tendency to treat a difference between small values as if it were greater than an equal-sized difference between large values. For example,  Kahneman and Tversky (1984)  find that subjects are often willing to drive twenty minutes to save $5 on a $15 calculator, but not to save $5 on a $125 jacket. Such phenomena can be considered choice analogs of Weber’s (1834) Law of Perception applied to the domain of numbers — increasing the intensities of two stimuli diminishes the perceptability of their difference. 
                         4 
                      
                      
                         4 
                         For example, a one-gram difference in the weights of two rocks is more easily detected if the rocks weigh 1 g and 2 g than if they weigh 101 g and 102 g. 
                       Diminishing sensitivity to symbolic number stimuli (as well as numerosity more generally) has been previously demonstrated in behaviour and is observed in the activity of cortical neurons thought to be responsible for an analog numerical perception system ( Moyer & Landauer, 1967; Nieder & Dehaene, 2009 ).

Recently, it has been shown that a value function which exhibits diminishing sensitivity can arise from the principle of efficient coding of sensory/reward quantities ( Frydman & Jin, 2021; Steverson et al., 2019 ). When a decision-maker faces a constraint in their ability to represent objective quantities (perhaps rooted in their neurobiology), it is optimal to compress valuations to scale with the frequency and/or importance of reward stimuli in the environment. This compression can be implemented by a simple neural computation in which the neural activity for each stimuli is  divisively normalized  by the sum of other stimuli in a reference set ( Louie et al., 2015, 2011, 2014 ). While efficient, this compression still has normative consequences. Behavioural models incorporating divisive normalization have previously been used to explain context effects in choice, including choice set dependence and violations of IIA ( Li et al., 2018; Louie et al., 2013; Marley et al., 2008; Webb et al., 2020 ). Essentially these models allow the variance of a random utility formulation to depend on the composition of the choice set, an issue particularly critical to analysing consumer choice data in both the lab and field ( Louviere et al., 2002; Salisbury & Feinberg, 2010 ). Recent theoretical work has extended the divisive normalization model to multi-attribute choice phenomena, including the dominance and compromise effects ( Dumbalska et al., 2020; Landry & Webb, 2021 ). Experiments reported by  Dumbalska et al. (2020)  and  Somerville (2022)  find multi-attribute choice behaviour consistent with divisive normalization.

The contribution of this paper lies along two dimensions. First, we implement a “double decoy” experiment designed to separate the two competing accounts of the asymmetric dominance effect arising from attribute normalization. 
                         5 
                      
                      
                         5 
                         The experimental study by  Dumbalska et al. (2020)  robustly samples the attribute space and finds that a model which implements divisive normalization provides a better fit of choice behaviour than does range normalization. Here, we test a clear contradictory prediction of these two modelling approaches in addition to reporting model fit. We discuss some additional modelling differences in the Conclusion. 
                       The experiment is quite simple; we place an additional decoy alternative within the range of existing alternatives ( Fig. 1 ) in a within-subject design. 
                         6 
                      
                      
                         6 
                         Recent examples demonstrating an asymmetric dominance effect using a within-subject experimental design include ( Crosetto & Gaudeul, 2016; Li et al., 2018; Noguchi & Stewart, 2014 ). 
                       Since the attribute range remains constant, a range normalization model would predict a null effect on the relative choice probabilities between the target and the competitor. This is the null hypothesis we set out to test. A divisive normalization model, on the other hand, predicts a double decoy effect (almost surely). This is our alternative hypothesis. In our sample, we observe a significant decrease in the relative proportion of targets chosen (on average). We also observe considerably more variation in individual behaviour than expected under the null hypothesis of range normalization.

Addressing the variation in the double decoy effect across subjects is our second contribution. It is well-recognized that subject/consumer-level heterogeneity is an important issue in discrete choice analysis ( Fiebig et al., 2010; Salisbury & Feinberg, 2010 ). While much of the existing literature on the dominance effect has studied the presence of a robust average effect across subjects, there is also compelling evidence of substantial variation across subjects in both the presence of a dominance effect ( Rooderkerk et al., 2011; Sharpe et al., 2008 ), as well as its correlation with other context-dependent choice behaviour like the compromise and similarity effect ( Liew et al., 2016; Noguchi & Stewart, 2018; Trueblood et al., 2015 ). This considerable heterogeneity in decoy effects requires both a theoretical account as well as empirical methods for addressing it. Taking guidance from both the neuroscience literature on perception as well as the discrete choice econometrics literature on estimating heterogeneity, we provide an empirical extension of the pairwise normalization model for multi-attribute choice of  Landry and Webb (2021) . This model allows heterogeneity in its parameterization, in particular the baseline by which a participant might normalize alternatives, allowing the model to capture the variation in behaviour that we observe in the double decoy experiment.

Finally, a compelling choice model should not only provide a source of variation in individual-level behaviour, it should also be adaptable to choice sets that do not contain decoys. After all, most choice sets typically encountered by human decision-makers are not constructed by behavioural researchers or prescient marketers, or at least, this has not typically been the norm for most of human history. In addition to addressing the seemingly esoteric edge-cases that are fruitful ground for testing choice theories, a choice model should also be useful in standard empirical settings. Therefore we also compare the range and divisive normalization models to hierarchical versions of the Logit model in a standard discrete choice experimental design previously used by  Fiebig et al. (2010) . Using Bayesian econometric methods, we find that the pairwise normalization model outperforms both the range normalization model and the Logit model in both of our datasets. These results suggest that a choice model which incorporates diminishing sensitivity via divisive normalization provides both a theoretical account of multi-attribute choice as well as a useful empirical model for researchers in applied settings.

In the first 30 trials, the participants were asked to choose one credit card from a three-alternative choice set. These choice sets were designed to accurately estimate the parameters from the preference model in Section  4.2  for each individual, using an adaptive algorithm described in  Daviet (2019b) . This algorithm constructs choice sets to maximize the expected Shannon information obtained from observing a choice. Essentially, it attempts to design “informative” choice sets where at least 2 alternatives are likely to be chosen and the choice of any of these alternatives would decrease the uncertainty around estimated parameters. 
                         9 
                      
                      
                         9 
                         For example, asking between 2 credit cards with the same price but different reward percentages is not expected to be very informative, as subjects would likely select the better reward program and the result would not allow us to estimate their price sensitivity parameter. 
                       The algorithm is adaptive as the model is re-estimated between each question, in order to increase the accuracy of the choice prediction for the next choice set. Simulation results indicate that parameter recovery is more effective in trinary choice set than in binary choice sets.

In-sample model fits to the Double Decoy experiment data display a decisive advantage for the PN model. This advantage for PN is also apparent from an average out-of-sample marginal likelihood. First, in order to reduce the importance of the prior, we train the model on the first 30 trials. Since these trials were optimized for parameter estimation, they should provide relevant information on the probable parameter range for each model. We use the posterior distributions resulting from this training as our new prior distributions. We then compute the marginal log-likelihood on the single and double decoy data (the probability of observing these choices given the previous data according to each model), and average over trials. The single and double decoy data are treated as out-of-sample since they are predicted from models trained on the first 30 trials only. The average probability that each model puts on a held-out observation is 54.4% for PN and 52.0% for RN ( Fig. 10 ).

In a Double Decoy experiment, we observe both a decrease in the proportion of target choices after the introduction of a second decoy, as well as substantial variation (across subjects) in the direction of the effect. This double decoy effect is particularly pronounced in subjects which exhibited an initial response to a single decoy. This pattern cannot be captured by models which only rely on the range of attributes to capture decoy effects. A parameterization of the pairwise normalization model, which implements the principle of diminishing sensitivity to perceptual stimuli, can better account for these patterns in human behaviour. Therefore in the context of this experiment, these two models represent a null and alternative hypothesis of a statistical test: our dataset rejects the range normalization account of dominance effects (the null hypothesis) when compared to a model of divisive normalization (the alternative hypothesis). Our results are confirmed in a standard discrete choice dataset where the pairwise normalization model has a substantially better in-sample fit and out-of-sample prediction (approaching a 5% improved likelihood  per observation ). While the results reported here only reject range normalization in favour of an alternative, they do suggest that a choice model which incorporates diminishing sensitivity via divisive normalization provides both a theoretical account of multi-attribute choice as well as a useful empirical model for researchers in applied settings.

To understand the limitations of range normalization, it is important to distinguish between range sensitivity as a property of human judgement, and the formal implications of range normalization as an algorithm in choice behaviour. It is well established that human perception is sensitive to the range of stimuli, however range is but one summary statistic of a distribution. Solely focusing on the range would force a perceptual or reward system to ignore relevant information about the frequency of stimuli, an additional important aspect of human judgment ( Parducci, 1995 ). Modern theories of efficient coding come to similar conclusions, and predict that decision maker’s will be sensitive to the range of stimuli  in addition  to other statistics of the stimuli distribution ( Frydman & Jin, 2021; Heng et al., 2020; Wei & Stocker, 2015, 2017 ). Neuro-computational models, including the divisive normalization model studied here, also predict sensitivity to the range. The difference between these models and range normalization is that they do not predict that humans are sensitive  only  to the range.

Other recent results also confirm the importance of incorporating diminishing sensitivity into discrete choice models.  Li et al. (2018)  propose a theory of decoy effects derived from gain control on a population of neurons that code for features of the experimental stimuli and apply it to a wide variety of perceptual biases including decoy effects.  Daviet (2018)  and  Webb et al. (2020)  incorporate Divisive Normalization into the value function through an asymmetric weighting, and  Dumbalska et al. (2020)  augment this approach by considering an additional non-linear transformation for the attributes of stimuli. Though unexamined here, we conjecture that these models should also predict a form of a double decoy effect.  Landry and Webb (2021)  and  Noguchi and Stewart (2018)  also describe a number of other multi-attribute choice effects on which these models can be compared, including a number of positive predictions of pairwise divisive normalization. Finally, we note that  Bavard et al. (2021)  find evidence for range normalization in settings where attribute information has to be learned by experience over repeated trials (i.e. in the context of reinforcement learning). This suggests that the normalization algorithm which underlies learning may differ from the one that determines choices. Further experiments to distinguish these theories, both in terms of choice behaviour as well as computational evidence, remain to be conducted.
