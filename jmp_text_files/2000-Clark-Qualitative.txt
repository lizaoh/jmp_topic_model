The Measurement of Qualitative Probability 

Stephen A. Clark 

University of Kentucky 

This essay presents necessary and sufficient conditions for representing a binary relation on a space of bounded random variables with a unique quantitative expectations operator. This result is used to provide a new characterization of qualitative probability. We also show that two distinct probability measures can induce the same qualitative ordering of events, even though they always produce different qualitative expectations relations. � 2000 Academic Press 

Key Words: qualitative probability relation; probability measure; qualitative expectations relation; quantitative expectations operator. 

1. INTRODUCTION 

The problem of measuring qualitative probability originated with a treatise by J. M. Keynes (1921) founding the logical school of probability. The major premise of this school is that probability measurements are assigned by logical necessity. Although its proponents have studied a variety of complex measurement schemes for qualitative probability (e.g., see Fine (1973)), we intend to take a relatively simple and straightforward approach. Suppose someone (weakly) orders a collection of events by intuitively judging their likelihood of occurrence. We want to know when this relation can be represented by a probability measure in the sense that equally ranked events are assigned the same probability and that higher ranked events are assigned a greater probability. The ordering of events is called a qualitative probability relation provided that there exists a unique probability measure representing it. In this sense, the assignment of numerical probability would be logically compelled by the underlying order of events. 

An axiomatic solution to this problem also holds the promise of securing personal probability theory upon a logical foundation that clearly identifies its subjectivistic origins. Only DeFinetti (1937) has ever proposed a plausible alternative approach based upon his coherency axiom. Interpreting the probability of an event as the price of a bet that pays 81 upon the event's occurrence and 80 otherwise, DeFinetti's coherency axiom asserts that someone cannot devise a system of monetary bets on the family of events without exposure to the possibility of a financial loss. Although this axiom precisely characterizes a finitely additive probability measure, it is often

criticized for its adherence to a financial, and not purely psychological, interpretation. This difficulty actually stems from the presumption that probabilities assigned to events are measured a priori, and the coherency axiom merely addresses whether or not these assigned values are ex post facto consistent with the requisite properties of probability measure. 

The logical school attempts to probe more deeply into the source of probability measurements, identifying them as a numerical representation of a subjective ordering of events. Of course, this approach does not exclude the possibility of someone assigning probabilities according to a relative frequency model. More broadly, we must clarify the rules of proper assignment in situations where relative frequencies cannot necessarily be analyzed. For example, how does someone assign probability to an event of unique historical content? 

The literature on qualitative probability can be categorized into existence theories, i.e., theories that yield the existence of a probability measure representing a qualitative relation, and uniqueness theories, i.e., theories that yield the existence and uniqueness of such a probability measure. A number of these theories offer sufficient, but not necessary, conditions (e.g., see Krantz et al. (1971) or Narens (1985) for surveys), but we focus here on theories which offer both necessary and sufficient conditions. In the context of a finite sample space, Scott (1964) originally solved the existence problem, and Cohen (1991) solved the associated uniqueness problem. Nevertheless, their methods do not generalize straightforwardly to infinite sample spaces. On the other hand, solutions to the existence problem for an infinite sample space have been proposed by Chateauneuf (1985) and Lehrer (1991), but these theories are obscured by their unintuitive continuity conditions. The associated uniqueness problem has been solved by Suppes and Zanotti (1976), who successfully isolate the continuity condition into a weak Archimedian axiom. Although these theories are mathematically complete, there is good reason to continue developing them. An ideal system of qualitative axioms would have a subjectivistic interpretation as compelling as the relative frequency interpretation of probability mandated by the empirical school. For example, one might attempt to devise qualitative axioms that mirror the Kolmogorov axioms for probability measure. Needless to say, the current theories of qualitative probability fall short of this lofty standard. 

In this essay, we work exclusively with finitely additive probability measures on an algebra of events. One reason for this approach is to follow Bayesian tradition. Yet the foremost reason is mathematical expedience. We will apply a duality theory for topological vector spaces to the problem of measuring qualitative probability. Suppose the space of bounded random variables is endowed with its supremum norm. Then its topological dual is readily identified as the space of all finitely additive, finite signed measures endowed with the total variation norm (Yosida 6 Hewitt, 1952). On the other hand, the space of all countably additive, finite signed measures on a _-algebra of events is not the dual of any well-known Banach space (e.g., Dunford 6 Schwartz, 1971, pp. 374�379). Yet we can analyze a countably additive probability measure as a special case of a finitely additive probability measure provided that the algebra of events happens to be a _-algebra. Indeed, many Bayesians argue that a probability measure should be defined on the algebra of all subsets of the sample space (e.g., DeFinetti, 1937). We only need to enlist a

qualitative axiom proposed by Fine (1973, p. 27) that is necessary and sufficient for the countable additivity of a finitely additive probability measure to resolve this difficulty. 

Following the technique of Suppes and Zanotti (1976), we embed the qualitative probability relation on an algebra of events into a qualitative expectations relation on an associated space of bounded random variables. In brief terms, an ordering of bounded random variables is called a qualitative expectations relation provided that it is represented by a unique quantitative expectations operator. This concept gives formal meaning to the popular idea of ``psychological expectations.'' Furthermore, it is always represented by the quantitative expectations operator induced by the probability measure representing the underlying qualitative probability relation. 

We now describe the axiomatic system that characterizes a qualitative expectations relation E in rough terms. If the relationship xEy holds, then we say that the random variable x is ``at least as expected'' as the random variable y. Here are the necessary and sufficient conditions on E. First, E must be nontrivial; i.e., some bounded random variable must be more expected than the random variable y=0. Second, E must be positive; i.e., a nonnegative bounded random variable must be at least as expected as the random variable y=0. Third, E must be total on the space of bounded random variables. Fourth, E must be a linear relation in an algebraic sense explained below. Finally, E must have a topologically closed graph, using the supremum norm topology on the space of bounded random variables. These conditions correspond to well-known properties of a quantitative expectations operator. 

The above result leads to a characterization of a qualitative probability relation Q that complements the work of Suppes and Zanotti (1976). If the relationship AQB holds, then we say that the event A is ``at least as probable'' as the event B. Our characterization involves the construction of a topologically closed, linear relation Q� on the space of bounded random variables, which is interpreted as the qualitative expectations relation induced by Q. Here are the necessary and sufficient conditions on Q. First, the universal event must be more probable than the empty event. Second, every event must be at least as probable as the empty event. Third, Q� must extend Q; i.e., Q must be a subrelation of Q�. Finally, Q� must be total on the space of bounded random variables. An interesting feature of this approach is that the last axiom is a uniqueness condition, which is isolated from the existence conditions embodied in the first three axioms. 

Nevertheless, a simple example shows that a probability measure can fail to satisfy the uniqueness condition. Indeed, it is possible that two distinct probability measures can produce the same qualitative ordering of events. The qualitative distinction between them manifests itself only when we compare their induced quantitative expectations operators. In this sense, a qualitative expectations relation is more fundamental as an information carrier than a qualitative probability relation. Therefore, the axiomatization of qualitative expectations seems to provide a more satisfactory foundation to the logical school.
