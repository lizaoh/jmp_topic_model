Gigerenzer and Goldstein (1996)  analyzed three heuristics for making paired comparisons: tallying, Take The Best, and Minimalist. All three heuristics are  naïve , meaning that they ignore possible dependencies between cues. It is known that the extent to which people use these heuristics depends largely on  environmental structure , e.g., on the cost of obtaining cue values or on the characteristics of the cues ( Bröder, 2000 ;  Bröder & Schiffer, 2003 ;  Newell & Shanks, 2003 ;  Newell, Weston, & Shanks, 2003 ;  Rieskamp & Hoffrage, 1999 ).

Furthermore, the normative success of the heuristics has been demonstrated by computer simulations and analytical results ( Dawes & Corrigan, 1974 ;  Gigerenzer & Goldstein, 1996 ;  Martignon & Hoffrage, 2002 ). But a full understanding of the general conditions under which the heuristics perform well or optimally has not yet been achieved. We perform a formal analysis of the relative accuracy and optimality of the heuristics. We also argue for the applicability of our results in practice.

Minimalist  is another lexicographic heuristic; the difference from Take The Best is that cues are inspected in random order. Another difference between the two is that there exists a linear rule that makes identical predictions with Take The Best ( Martignon & Hoffrage, 2002 ), but not with Minimalist.

In the remainder, we use the following terminology: Let  A  and  B  be two objects randomly sampled to be compared. The  predictive accuracy  of a rule  R = R [ c ( A ),

While at a first glance the heuristics might appear simplistic, simulation studies have shown that they compare remarkably well to statistical benchmarks. Three decades ago,  Dawes and Corrigan (1974)  convincingly argued that tallying can have greater predictive accuracy than linear regression.  Einhorn and Hogarth (1975)  provided statistical reasons for this, like the absence of sampling error in the estimation of weights. Gigerenzer and his colleagues replicated this finding in 20 real-world datasets ( Gigerenzer, Czerlinski, & Martignon, 2002 ;  Gigerenzer & Goldstein, 1996 ). Furthermore, they found that on predicting new data, Take The Best was, on average, more accurate than regression and the difference in accuracy between fitting and prediction was smaller, on average, for Minimalist than for regression. Take The Best also did not fall too far behind a Bayesian network that took informative cue dependencies into account ( Martignon & Hoffrage, 2002 ).

Thus, naive decision makers have two good options—to add cues or to order cues (by validity or randomly). There has been some analytical work on determining the conditions under which ordering is more accurate than adding and the converse:  Martignon and Hoffrage (2002)  showed that Take The Best tends to be more accurate than tallying when the number of cues is, in an information-theoretic sense, small relative to the number of objects. They also showed that tallying tends to be more accurate than Take The Best when the number of cues is large relative to the number of objects.

More recently,  Hogarth and Karelaia (2005a)  undertook an exhaustive analysis for tasks with three–five cues. For tasks where the criterion value is a linear function of cue values plus an error term, they identified relationships between cue weights for which tallying is more accurate than Take The Best and vice versa.  Hogarth and Karelaia (2005b)  also analyzed the relative accuracy of Take The Best, tallying, and regression for continuous cues. There are also results on the accuracy of general lexicographic heuristics for comparing more than two objects ( Baucells, Carrasco, & Hogarth, 2005 ).

We present new analytical results. In what follows, we say that cues are  conditionally independent  if they are independent given the values of the objects on the criterion; otherwise we call the cues ( conditionally )  dependent . The results are stronger for conditionally independent cues: We first show that there exists an optimal linear rule that is equivalent to  naïve Bayes  (the Bayesian rule that computes  Pr 
                      [ A > B | c ( A ), 
                      c ( B )] under the assumption that the cues are conditionally independent;  Martignon & Hoffrage, 2002 ). We use the result to characterize the conditions under which tallying and Take The Best are optimal; the conditions do not involve cues-to-objects ratios or cue weights, but only cue validities. We also show that the conditional independence assumption is necessary for the result to hold. We finally show that tallying and Take The Best are more accurate than Minimalist.

It is harder to apply our approach to dependent cues. Nevertheless, for two cues, we show that tallying and Minimalist are equally accurate and we state a necessary and sufficient condition for Take The Best to be more accurate than tallying and Minimalist. This condition allows to construct an example where tallying and Minimalist are more accurate than Take The Best. It also helps to understand why Take The Best tends to be more accurate than tallying when the number of cues and objects is small. We also summarize other work on the relative accuracy of the heuristics.

The conditional independence assumption is a simplification, but there are reasons for it. Practitioners in computer science often assume that cues are conditionally independent: Applying Bayes’ rule for dependent cues is computationally intractable ( Cooper, 1990 ), and naïve Bayes does not fall too far behind Bayesian networks that take dependencies into account ( Domingos & Pazzani, 1997 ;  Friedman, Geiger, & Goldszmidt, 1997 ).

How useful are the two corollaries in practice? There is an environmental and a psychological question. The environmental question is whether the odds condition is satisfied in the real world. We checked the 20 datasets used by  Gigerenzer et al. (2002)  and found that the odds condition holds in three of them. The psychological question is if people can estimate cue validities. This is theoretically plausible because people log frequencies ( Hasher & Zacks, 1984 ). To answer the question experimentally,  Bröder (2000, Experiment 1)  provided instances of cue and criterion values on specific objects and asked participants to provide ratings of cue validity. He found that, given sufficient feedback, the ratings were almost identical with the validities.

People use Take The Best more often when the odds condition is satisfied than when it is not: cues with validities .70, .67, .65 do not satisfy the odds condition while cues with validities .87, .65, .58 do, and  Martignon and Krauss (2003)  found that the proportion of participants that seemed to use Take The Best jumped from 20% to 75% across these cases.  Bröder (2000, Experiment 2)  also found an increase from 20% to 35% in the proportion of participants who appeared to use Take The Best between cues with validities .90, .82, .74, .66 and .96, .82, .68, .54.

In these studies, there was no cost for obtaining cue values. We want to emphasize here that decision makers might choose not to use the most accurate rule even though they are able to apply the corollaries: people switch heuristics to trade accuracy with cost ( Payne, Bettman, & Johnson, 1993 ;  Rieskamp & Hoffrage, 1999 ).

Finally, we point out that the corollaries also hold when more than two objects are compared simultaneously. This is clear for the results involving linear rules. If Take The Best is generalized ( Hogarth & Karelaia, 2005a ) so that an object is eliminated if the cue being inspected equals zero on it and there exists an object with a unitary cue value,  Corollary 1  holds again. This is so because Theorem 3 of  Martignon and Hoffrage (2002)  holds for this generalization of Take The Best ( Katsikopoulos & Fasolo, 2006 ).

For this result, we build on work in the social choice literature, combining it with the following analogy: A cue used in a paired comparison can be viewed as a  juror  voting for one of two options where only one option is correct; a nondiscriminating cue maps to a juror who abstains. Finally, it is natural to ask how the extent to which cues are dependent affects the relative accuracy of the heuristics.  Rieskamp and Dieckmann (2004)  ran a simulation that provides an answer. They recorded the average accuracy of tallying, Take The Best, and naïve Bayes across 2000 artificial environments each with  n =6 and  m =50. In half of the environments, the average correlation between cues was low, .01, and in the other half it was high, .51. In the first case, naïve Bayes was most accurate (.87), while tallying and Take The Best were almost equally accurate (.84 versus .83). When cues were more dependent, all heuristics lost in accuracy but now Take The Best was most accurate (.71), with naïve Bayes following closely (.69) and tallying being the least accurate (.66).

We analyzed the relative accuracy of three heuristics for paired comparisons based on binary cues, which are all naïve in that they ignore possible dependencies between cues but take different approaches: linear (tallying) and lexicographic (Take The Best, Minimalist). When cues are independent given the values of the objects on the criterion—an assumption often made by practitioners—there are simple conditions that characterize when tallying and Take The Best are optimal. Also, tallying and Take The Best are more accurate than Minimalist. We also discussed the relation of the heuristics to naïve Bayes, a Bayesian rule that ignores cue dependencies. When cues are dependent and the number of cues and objects is psychologically plausible, Take The Best tends to be more accurate than tallying, but it is also possible that tallying, and Minimalist, are more accurate than Take The Best.
