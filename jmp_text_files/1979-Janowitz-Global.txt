INTRODUCTION  Since the publication of the now classic paper by Johnson (1967), cluster analysis has  played an increasingly important role in psychological research. However it is interesting  to note that the input data in a typical clustering problem tends to have only ordinal  significance. This was pointed out by Johnson (1967, p. 242) where he states, ften in  psychology we have confidence in our data only up to rank-order; the absolute numbers  obtained from the experiments may lie along virtually any scale.Bromley (1966)  explains how ordinally significant data arises in psychology and gives some interesting  applications. Miller ( 1969) uses ordinal properties to cluster various classes of nouns,  while Stringer (1967) uses a rank-order clustering technique due originally to Con-  stantinescu (1967) in an investigation of facial expressions. A more recent paper by  Peay (1975) uses ordinal similarity measures to discuss some problems related to ethnic  intermarriage. The graph theoretic methods advocated by Hubert (1973, 1974, 1976, 1977)  and Matula (1977) are all based upon a rank-ordering of the input data. One should also  notice that methods such as these play a central role in the work of Jardine and Sibson  (1968, 1971) and Sibson (1970, 1972). Th us cluster techniques based upon ordinal  input data seem to provide a useful tool for the analysis of psychological data.  The aforementioned cluster methods may conveniently be thought of in the following  context: The input data is a finite set P of objects to be classified together with a finite  set of attributes that the objects possess in varying degrees. CONCLUSION  At the beginning of the paper we proposed an investigation of those cluster methods  that are suitable for use with ordinal input data. The arguments in Section 3 show that?88 M. F. JANOWITZ  monotone equivariant methods that preserve weak order equivalence are the correct ones  to use. In view of the discussion at the beginning of Section 3, such methods are essentially  graph theoretic in nature. A careful proof of this fact will appear in Janowitz, 1979. This  then gives a mathematical justification for the use of cluster methods such as those  advocated by Hubert and Matula. Additional cluster methods satisfying the above  criteria and lying between single and complete-linkage clustering are introduced in  Janowitz, 1979.
