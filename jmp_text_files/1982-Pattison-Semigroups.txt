JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 25, 87-118 (1982)  The Analysis of Semigroups of Multirelational Systems  PHILIPPA E. PATTISON  University of Melbourne  A finite family of binary relations on a finite set, termed here a relational system, generates a finite semigroup under the  operation of relational composition. The relationship between simplifications of the semigroup of a relational system in the  form of homomorphic images, and simplifications of the relational system itself is examined. First of all, the list of  relational conditions establishing a relationship between a homomorphic image of the semigroup of a relational system and a  simplified, or derived, version of that relational system, is reviewed and extended. Then a definition of empirical  relationship is introduced (the Correspondence Definition) and it is shown how, in conjunction with a factorization procedure  for finite semigroups (P. E. Pattison & W. K. Bartlett, Journal of Mathematical Psychology, 1982, in press), it leads to a  systematic and efficient analysis for a relational system. Applications of the procedure to an empirical blockmodel and to a  class of simple relational systems are presented.  1. INTRODUCTION  The finite semigroup generated by a collection of binary matrices under Boolean multiplication has been proposed as a  representation for relational structure in multiple, social networks (Boorman & White, 1976; White, Boorman, & Breiger, 1976).  The binary matrices may represent relations in a blockmodel (Arabie, Boorman, & Levitt, 1978; Boorman & White, 1976; White et  al., 1976) or networks in a multirelational system (Lorrain, 1975; Lorrain & White, 1971); they may also be replaced by fuzzy  relations in a fuzzy blockmodel (Pattison, 1980). In the first two cases, the semigroup operation of Boolean multiplication  corresponds to ordinary composition of binary relations (for example, Lorrain & White, 1971); in the third, max-min  composition of fuzzy relations (Kaufman, 1975) has the necessary properties.  A number of empirical analyses using the representation have been undertaken (for example, Boorman & White, 1976; Breiger,  1979; Breiger & Pattison, 1978; McConaghy, 1981; Pappi, Perner, & Kappelhoff, 1979), generating, in the process, a collection  of heuristic principles for interpreting features of the representative  The author is indebted to Warren K. These principles have not, however, been subject to general agreement and have been shown to lead to interpretative  inconsistencies (Bonacich, 1980; McConaghy, 1981; Pattison, 1981). Pattison (1981) has argued that, to date, most applications  of the semigroup representation have used essentially two kinds of semigroup features for substantive interpretation. (A third  kind, namely, the partial order associated with the semigroup (Boorman & White, 1976), has been given less interpretative  weight.) These two kinds of features are, on the one hand, the equations of the semigroup (White, 1973; Bonacich, 1980;  McConaghy, 1981), and, on the other, the homomorphic images which it admits (Boorman & White, 1976; Breiger & Pattison, 1978).  The use of one or the other set of descriptive features leads to quite different constructions for the relational structure  common to two multirelational systems (Pattison, 1981); it is consequently, important to clarify in some detail, the analysis  of a multirelational system which each kind of descriptive approach permits.  In this paper, the analysis of the semigroup of a multirelational system in terms of its homomorphic images is examined. A  homomorphic image of a finite semigroup is a simpler (smaller) semigroup whose structure "approximates" that of the original  semigroup (for example, Boyd, 1969). It has been argued that a homomorphic image of the semigroup of a relational system  coresponds to a simplification, or partial representation, of that system (Lorrain & White, 1971; Boorman & White, 1976). It  contains simpler patterns of relational equations which tend to hold in the original relational system.  In association with this simplification of the representation, one may ask whether there exist any corresponding  simplifications of the relational system itself. That is, given a simplification at the semigroup level of representation, can  a parallel approximation be identified at the relational level? (The question is illustrated in Fig. 1; terms are defined  formally in the next section.) For, if correspondences between the two levels of representation can be found, then the  relational domains in which the equations of a homomorphic image of the semigroup are true, and not true, may be exactly  specified. The homomorphic images, together with relational qualifications upon the truth of their equations, would then  provide a clear base for substantive interpretation (Pattison, 1981).  The general mathematical problem of constructing the collection of "simplifications" of a relational system corresponding to  an image of its semigroup is a difficult one. Equally challenging is the converse task of determining conditions under which a  given simplification of the relational system induces a homomorphism of the semigroup of the system. These two questions are  examined below in two stages. In the first, known conditions under which simplifying a relational system leads to a  homomorphism of its semigroup are reviewed, and some new ones are given. In the second, an empirical solution to the problem  of identifying simplifications of the system consistent with a given homomorphic image of its semigroup is developed and  illustrated. Finally, it is shown how the latter technique may be used in conjunction with the factorization procedure for  finite algebras (Pattison & Bartlett, 1982) to produce a systematic and efficient analysis of the multirelational system. Generalisations of Structural Equivalence  The listed relational conditions under which homomorphisms of a relational system induce homomorphisms of its semigroup  account for known conditions under which  (RiRi)17r = (Ril7r)(Ril7r),  a condition which is itself sufficient, but not necessary, for the induction of a homomorphism from SX onto S,,, with Y=X/7r. In fact, the indegree and outdegree conditions and the central representatives condition may be seen as alternative  generalisations of the notion of structural  MULTIRELATIONAL SYSTEMS 95  equivalence. Structural equivalence requires that each element in any class, or block, is connected to exactly the same set of  elements, for each type of relation. Johnson's outdegree condition stipulates that if for a relation R an element of a block A  has an outdegree of at least one to a block B, then so does each element of block A for the relation. For the indegree  condition, the indegree of members of block B is at issue. The central representatives condition, on the other hand, requires  the existence of an identified central representative in each block, which is connected to every other element to which any of  its block members are related.  In other words, the indegree and outdegree conditions rely more upon the consistency of connections throughout any class of  the partition n of X into blocks, by requiring that every element has at least one relationship of the specified type. The  central representatives condition, however, involves a central block element who maintains all of the relationships of members  of its block. The distinction between these two generalisations of structural equivalence is drawn particularly sharply in the  context of structural change. Suppose that a relational system 5PX on a set X is expanded by adding new members to X and/or  new ties between members. One can interpret the above conditions as defining some of the ways in which the addition can be  made so that the structure of the new system is an extension of that of the old (Boyd, 1969). More precisely, they specify  rules for building . `r into a relational system, Mz, whose semigroup SZ = S(MZ) contains SX = S(MX) as a homomorphic image.  Such rules partially describe how elements and ties can be augmented to ,',without destroying its prevailing structural  distinctions, although with the possible creation of new ones. In the indegree and outdegree cases, one may add elements so  that their relationships are consistent with those of some existing element (in the sense described by Johnson's conditions),  whereas in the central representatives case, one adds the new elements in ways which are strictly constrained by the ties of  the existing elements (the central representatives). The Extent of the Relational Conditions  The foregoing list of relational conditions, under which semigroup images are induced, constitutes a useful set of  correspondences between semigroup images and derived relational systems of the kind envisaged earlier. However, the general  problem of characterizing derived relational systems for which such a correspondence exists remains largely unsolved. Taken together with a concern for  investigating the semigroup representation in relation to empirical data, these observations indicate an alternative  investigative strategy. In particular, they suggest an empirical approach to establishing links between the semigroup and  relational levels, not only to assist the analysis of actual data, but also to provide further input to the pursuit of the  kind reviewed and developed above.  4. The Form of Analysis  The factorization technique for finite algebras (Pattison & Bartlett, 1982), and, in particular for finite semigroups,  identifies a collection of maximally independent homomorphic images of the algebra sufficient to describe its structure. The  problem of describing features of the algebra may, by use of the technique, be exchanged for that of describing features of  its identified homomorphic images, or factors. In conjunction with the application of the Correspondence Definition,  therefore, it may be used to provide an analysis of a multirelational system (for example, a blockmodel) of the kind  illustrated in Fig. 3. The broken arrow marks an interpretative path by which distinctive relational features of a relational  system may be described. While the path to that description is heavily dependent upon the powerul but often complex machinery  of the semigroup representation (denoted by the solid arrows of Fig. 3), its results may be reported in purely relational  terms.' The analysis suggested by Fig. 3 thus produces a comprehensive but nonredundant account of the structural content of  the system in terms of the original relational data. By contrast with many analytic strategies, the proposed analysis attempts  to determine ways in which the data may be aggregated so as to emphasize its significant structural features. Like  blockmodelling, therefore, it endorses "the philosophy that aggregation is to be inferred at the end of the analysis, not  imposed at the beginning" (Arable et al., 1978, p. 22).  In combination with the factorization technique, the Correspondence Definition allows one to search for the "concrete"  structural analogues of the abstract algebraic factors, or components, of the structure, and so to identify the irreducible  forms of relational interlock at the relational level. There is a one-to-one correspondence between the factors of S and some images of T and the theory has provided for  direct determination of the latter by graph-theoretical operations on T.  6. DISCUSSION: DESCRIBING RELATIONAL STRUCTURE  The semigroup generated by a system of binary relations has been argued to be a faithful representative of its relational  structure (Boorman & White, 1976; Pattison, 1980; but see also Boyd, 1980). Further, a useful analysis of that structure has  been argued to take the form of a transformation of the full representative semigroup into a number of simpler components, or  images, each of which induces one or more reductions of the full relational system. In that way, the essential features of the  total structure, in the form of factors of the full semigroup, are captured; as well, one can specify the relational domain in  which the equations conveyed by each factor apply.  This particular approach to the translation of relational structure into features which may be given substantive  interpretation is not unique, however. Firstly, there are many other "distinctive feature" descriptions of relational systems,  not involving semigroup construction, which may be advanced. (The approach of Seidman & Foster, 1979, for example, is one of  the more general graph-theoretical versions.) Secondly, a notion implicit in the paper by Bonacich (1980) is that the features  of the semigroup, in terms of which substantive structural implications may be sought, are some nonredundant set of its  equations, rather than some collection of its homomorphic images. As both Bonacich (1980) and Pattison (1981) have observed,  these two strategies for describing semigroup features (in terms of equations, on the one hand, and images, on the other) lead  to quite distinct accounts of structure in a single population, and to significantly contrasting methods for representing the  structural features shared by two comparable populations. The description of structure in terms of semigroup images (and,  especially, factors) and their relational counterparts  114 PHILIPPA E. PATTISON  has been elaborated in the present work so that adequate substantive comparisons between the two alternative approaches may be  made.  In the light of the need for such comparisons, some aspects of the analytic scheme of Fig. 3 are of interest. Firstly, as the  analysis of Breiger's (1976) blockmodel for members of a biomedical research network illustrates, the proposed analysis yields  a description of relational structure in terms of features which possess concrete relational referents in the generating  relational system. Thus some analytic power is given to the overall project of evaluating the initial semigroup  representation. Given a set of empirical data, the salience of relational features identified by its analysis may be examined  directly for substantive import (as, for example, was done and with promising results, in the case of the scientists'  blockmodel).  Secondly, the capacity to relate semigroup images to derived relational systems also means that the proposed analysis may be  used to refine a blockmodel analysis. As indicated by Arabie et al. (1978), some arbitrariness enters the process of  blockmodel construction, through the choice of clustering method, the number of clusters found and the cutoff density used. A  useful adjunct to the blockmodelling process might therefore be a procedure in which one produced possibly too many clusters  at the blockmodel stage but which then allowed different homomorphisms of the blockmodel semigroup to suggest subsequent  aggregations of the data. In this way, a more faithful representation of the original data would be obtained, a representation  even more sympathetic to the notion that aggregation is best inferred at the end of the analysis rather than imposed at the  beginning (Arabie et al., 1978). Thus by combining the proposed procedure with blockmodel analysis, some steps can be taken to  overcome analytic inconsistencies associated with the use of the latter technique alone. Such a strategy takes seriously what  Boorman and White (1976) termed the "quest... for a dual description of social structure" (p. 1385).  For example, consider the scientists' blockmodel consisting of four blocks. The coarser blockmodel with two blocks consisting  of blocks a and b, on the one hand, and blocks c and d, on the other, corresponds to the derived relational system induced by  the partition (ab)(cd) on the full block set. It has a semigroup of five elements isomorphic to the image SIC, of the factor  S/QZ ; hence given that the full semigroup on the four-blockmodel is of order 13, it may be proposed that the semigroup of the  two-blockmodel represents 5/13 or 38% of the structural content of the full semigroup. By using the coarser blockmodel, which  corresponds to the twoblock description of the scientists' contact network, one derives a structural representation of the  system which is consistent with that of the more refined one, but which lacks some of its structural detail. Similarly, the  two best possible three- block models, corresponding to the partitions (ab)(c)(d) and (a)(b)(cd), account for 8/13 and 9/13  (or 62 and 70%) of the structural content of S, respectively. One may in this sense evaluate the loss of structural detail  associated with any particular merging of blocks. Further, by choosing a division into blocks which is sufficiently fine, one  is guaranteed sufficient structural detail without the risk of dealing with blocks which are too finely split, since the  proposed analysis recovers their structural similarity. Indeed, the same technique may be conceived as producing overlapping  clusters,  MULTIRELATIONAL SYSTEMS 115  whose usefulness in a social psychological context has been demonstrated by Shepard and Arabie (1979; see also Arabie, 1977).  A comparion of the results of the analytic strategy proposed here with those of the Shepard and Arabie ADCLUS algorithm would,  in fact, provide an interesting bearing on both techniques. (Input to ADCLUS might consist of a matrix of similarities among  blocks constructed from the three-way block-by-block-by-relations array constituting the blockmodel; for an example using  multiple social networks, see Arabie, 1977.)  A third observation of interest is that the analytic power with which the task of evaluating the semigroup representation is  now invested, is in the spirit of that originally proposed by Lorrain and White (1971). They sought "models of aggregation of  relations and of individuals, by mapping C,-or any representation of a social network as a category-onto a smaller, simpler,  reduced category," a process which involved "two simultaneous mappings: one of objects and the other of morphisms" (pp.  60-61). They argued further that:  A reduction of a category constitutes a "point of view", a "cross-section", a "projection' of this category, leaving out  certain aspects of its structure and retaining others. Many such reductions, such points of view on a category are possible,  more or less refined and overlapping to varying degrees. Virtually nothing can be done with a category such as C, outside of  the context of its reductions and their interrelations; only in this context can its sociological fruitfulness be assessed.  (p. 61)  The technique described in the present paper offers a systematic, comprehensive and maximally nonredundant approach to the  derivation of "reductions" of a relational system. Its adoption will indeed hopefully permit the assessment of the  "sociological fruitfulness" of the semigroup representation.  One aspect of this assessment is permitted in the form of a preliminary evaluation of the usefulness of identifying relational  systems with the same semigroup. Such systems with the same relational structure need not be isomorphic, but the relational  features giving rise to their identical semigroup components, or factors, may be compared, component by component, and their  substantive degree of "identity" assessed. The analysis of the scientists' blockmodel demonstrated how the features and  components identified by the technique often refer to simple blockmodels on small numbers of aggregated sets of blocks. Thus  handbooks such as those of Lorrain (1973) and Schwartz (1973) have applications to much larger blockmodels than those with  only two or three blocks. Lists of blockmodels with the same semigroup even on just two blocks may, by virtue of the methods  developed, be used to provide partial assessment of the proposed identity of relational structure, more generally. That is,  the results of detailed analyses and assessments of small cases are guaranteed at least partial generalisability to larger and  more complex ones, making the further consideration of small cases a particularly useful approach.  Finally, the form of the description of role interlock induced by the representation and the proposed analysis is of some  theoretical interest. "Simple" features of relational interdependence are identified and expressed as "simple" collections of  equations among compound relations; the features are qualified by a statement of the  116 PHILIPPA E. PATTISON  specific relational domain to which they pertain. Thus, in terms of this analytic scheme, relational interlock is not seen as  being constant across an entire network but something whose precise form varies as a function of network positions. A  particular feature of relational interdependence may be shown empirically to be universal to a particular network but it is  not assumed to be so (cf. balance theory and its generalisations; for example, Cartwright & Harary, 1956, 1970). Some features  have relevance for only some aspects of some parts of a network (for example, in the scientists' blockmodel, the  centre-periphery pattern and its relational reference in the contrast between blocks a and b and block c, with block d not  necessarily contributing to the contrast because of its central involvement in another unstudied scientific network). Hence it  makes sense that such features refer only to the network domains in which they obtain.  The analysis also identifies features of role interlock shared by certain network positions as well as those upon which they  differ. (One may consider again, for instance, the scientists' blockmodel: blocks a and b are similar in forming part of the  centre of the centre-periphery structure, but they differ in status, as the first interlock feature makes clear.) In fact,  features of role interlock are often described in terms of network domains contrasting one network position with all of the  others (for example, the status feature of the scientists' blockmodel, associated with the partition of blocks (a)(bcd)). Such  features identify the position of a given block as contributing to a unique type of interlock in the network concerned.  In fact, an implicit description of individual positions whithin a network follows from an analysis of the kind proposed.  Linking features of relational interlock to contrasts within specific relational domains leads to an account of how particular  positions fit into the total network structure. The application of the methodology to the empirical data set illustrates the  process and, in so doing, demonstrates its potential value. The rationale for constructing a representation of the relational  structure of a network includes a concern for describing the social context in which individual behaviour takes place; the  proposed descriptive scheme permits the required contextual accounts to be given in some detail.
