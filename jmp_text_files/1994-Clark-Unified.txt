Toward a Unified Theory of Weakly Rational Choice 

STEPHEN A. CLARK 

University of Kentucky 

A theory of weakly rational choice is developed by replacing the directly revealed strict preference relation in the traditional theory with a primitive binary relation interpreted as an indirectly revealed strict preference relation. We characterize weakly rational choice, weakly quasitransitive-rational choice, weakly transitive-rational choice, and weakly additive-rationa! choice in terms of appropriate revealed preference axioms. Our approach unifies a number of recently proposed models of weakly rational choice. © 1994 Academic Press, Inc. 

1. INTRODUCTION 

The economic theory of rational choice is traditionally based on an optimality principle, which asserts that a decision-maker’s acceptance set (or rejection set) consists of all optimal actions (or non-optimal actions, resp.) in a particular constraint set. The purpose of this essay is to examine the more recent theories of weakly rational choice which modify this optimality principle by requiring the acceptance set to be a mere subset of all optimal actions in the constraint set. The main reasons cited for this modification have an empirical tone: (i) If the decisionmaker is forced to choose just one optimal action when several are available, then the empirical choice function is forced to be weakly rational! (e.g., Varian, 1982); (ii) if the decision-maker faces the same constraint set more than once in the forced choice scenario, then the empirical choice correspondence might not cover all the optimal actions from a particular constraint set and, hence, is weakly rational (e.g., Matsuyama, 1985]. This approach, for example, is a plausible alternative to a stochastic theory of rational choice in an observational study, where repeated experiments are not allowed, or in a laboratory study, when the number of trials is not necessarily large. A secondary reason for studying this topic is to clarify the logical underpinnings of the more traditional theory. 

A positive theory of choice behavior must be empirically meaningful. Note that a constant utility function always weakly rationalizes a choice correspondence in the Joose sense we have described it, and another condition must be imposed to guarantee that the theory leads to refutable predictions. The traditional model of rational choice avoids this difficulty through the interplay of the revealed weak preference relation V and the directly revealed strict preference relation V*. By

The previous argument breaks down in a general theory of weakly rational choice, because directly revealed strict preference does not necessarily indicate greater utility. We resolve this dilemma by replacing V* with some indirectly revealed strict preference relation W to serve the same logical role. Most of this theory can be developed without making explicit assumptions about the structure of W, treating it as a primitive binary relation on the choice space. Some of the candidates for W that are explored in this essay include (i) the directly revealed strict preference relation V*; (ii) Varian’s (1982) modified revealed strict preference relation V‘'; and Bandyopadhyay’s (1990) modified revealed strict preference relation ¥V** for handling intransitive indifference. This paper is organized in the following manner. In the next section, we present a formal model of weakly rational choice. A generalization of the Weak Axiom of Revealed Preference (e.g., Arrow, 1959) is introduced that characterizes weakly rational choice. This theory reduces to strictly rational choice theory (Clark, 1988} when we take W as V*. In the third section, we study the problem of recovering an ordinal utility function from a weakly rational choice correspondence. The key condition is a generalization of the Houthakker Axiom of Revealed Preference (e.g., Suzumura, 1977). In the fourth section, we weaken utility theory by allowing intransitive indifference. A generalization of the Strong Axiom of Revealed Preference (e.g., Arrow, 1959) is introduced that characterizes weakly quasitransitive rational choice. In the fifth and final section, we study additive utility theory. A suitable transformation of the well-known cancellation axiom (e.g., Scott, 1964) leads to a new behavioral axiom, the Generalized Conjoint Axiom of Revealed Preference, which characterizes weak additive rationality. The method is analogous to our treatment of ordinal utility theory.
