INTRODUCTION  Response Scale Transformations and the Form of Behavioral Laws  In psychological research, response measure transformation has been dictated either by statistical considerations or by  measurement-theoretical considerations. Statistical considerations have played the major role; measurement-theoretical  considerations have entered only occasionally.  Standard statistical texts have discussed variance homogeneity and error distribution normality as desirable properties which  facilitate statistical analysis, and have provided tools for "bending the data nearer the Procrustean bed of the assumptions  underlying conventional analyses" (Tukey, 1957). Such statistical discussion and techniques have directed attention to the  effect of the measurement scale on the outcome of statistical tests. Standard sources on experimental design (Lindquist, 1953;  Winer, 1962) have considered the dependence of statistical interaction on the response scale. Statistical lore has also  entered in the form of specific warnings. For example, there is general awareness that interactions can be produced by  "ceiling effects" or "floor effects," and that these interactions do not necessarily indicate meaningful processes. Functional Measurement Theory  Functional measurement theory (Anderson, 1962a, 1962b) and its application to substantive psychological problems such as  information integration for verbal stimuli (Anderson, 1968) and psychophysical judgment (Anderson, 1970) is described  elsewhere. This section gives just enough of the important ideas to clarify the role of our results in the general approach.  From the viewpoint of functional measurement, behavior theory discovers the laws relating psychological values of stimuli to  psychological values of responses. This involves the joint solution of three basic problems.  The three problems are to find a stimulus scale relating the physical stimulus event to the psychological stimulus value, to  find a response scale relating the underlying psychological response value to the observed numerical response, and to find the  psychological law which is the functional relationship between the underlying stimulus value(s) and the underlying  psychological response.  The givens of these problems are the inputs to the subject, stimuli and temporal intervals measured categorically or on  physical scales, and the responses outputs, which are usually values of a continuous measure. Functional measurement relies  upon the characteristically greater efficiency of continuous response measures, relative to qualitative choice responses. It  also routinely uses factorial designs with analyses of variance, and where necessary, a monotone resealing procedure applied  to the response measure. It is to the conjunctive use of factorial designs and a monotone resealing of the response that the  present results are most relevant.  The functional measurement approach can be exemplified using an averaging model for the bisection task discussed by Anderson  (1970). In this task the subject is given two reference stimuli which define an interval on some dimension. He is to adjust a  variable stimulus to divide the given interval into two sections.  Any bisection judgment can be viewed as an averaging of the two reference interval endpoints. This being the case, an  averaging model is a natural condidate for the psychological law for bisection judgment.  g80/8/3-8  42,0 SOGARTZ AND WACKWITZ  The use of a factorial design will not only bring to bear a powerful test of the model but also provide solutions to both  scaling problems if the model is correct. Let the rows and columns of the design correspond to the physical reference stimuli  S; and T; . To each Si and T; there corresponds a subjective stimulus value s; and t; . Thus, to each physical interval (Si ,  T;) there corresponds a sensory interval (si , t;). The averaging model asserts that the psychological response value for the  interval (si , t;) will be  rii = wsi + (1 - w)t;,  where w and (I - w) are subjective weights. This model assumes that w is constant for all reference intervals and that si and  t; are independent of context.  The averaging model is assumed to apply to the values rig on the psychological scale, but not necessarily to the observed Rij  which are on the physical scale. However, under the reasonable assumption that the subjective response ria is some continuous  strictly monotone transformation of the Rij , it should be possible to transform the observed Rij to an appropriate  psychological response scale.  The search for the transformation is directed by a correspondence between terms in the psychological model and terms in the  experimental design model. Indeed, this factorial design is chosen precisely because it translates the additivity in the  proposed psychological law into a predicted additivity of the row and column factors after appropriate resealing of the  response measure. Equation (1) says the psychological response values are an additive combination of the two stimulus values.  Therefore, transformation of the Rij to an appropriate psychological scale should remove any row X column interactions in the  transformed data. The search for an appropriate scale transformation becomes a search for a transformation which will remove  the row X column interaction. If such a transformation is found, the averaging law is supported and interval scaling of the  response measure and the stimuli is achieved. Interval scaling of the response occurs since any transformation which removes  the above-mentioned interaction is a linear transformation of any other transformation which does so. Interval stimulus  scaling also occurs since in the factorial design table of transformed data, the marginal mean for row i is wsi + (1 - w)t,  and the marginal mean for column j is ws + (1 - w)t; , where s is the mean of the si and t is the mean of the t; .  This bisection example reflects an uncommon and remarkable interlacing of theory, experimental design, and statistical  treatment of the data. The experimental design is selected because of its relation to the theoretical formulation. Statistical  features of the data are predicted by consideration of the relation of the theory to the experimental design. A transformation  of the response measure is sought not exclusively to satisfy considerations arising out of statistical theory or to facilitate  statistical analysis, but rather also to satisfy psychological considerations and to facilitate theoretical analysis. There are two natural candidates for a stopping rule. One is stop when the F statistic for the source being  reduced is nonsignificant. The other is stop when the proportion of systematic variance accounted for by the source being  reduced is below some selected value, e.g., .05. Both rules can be argued for. The argument for the significance test is that,  strictly speaking, a theory is inferentially falsified by a significant statistic for a source which that theory predicts will  be zero in principle, and therefore nonsignificant in practice. The counter argument favoring the proportionof-variance rule  is that a sufficiently powerful design will detect as statistically significant a source which accounts for a very small  proportion of variance. Therefore, a stopping rule should be used which, though it does not necessarily yield an analysis of  variance in which the sources to be reduced are nonsignificant, it does yield an analysis in which the sources to be reduced  are negligible in the same sense that a Pearson product-moment correlation coefficient of .1, significant or not, is  ordinarily negligible. We, on our part, are inclined to use the proportion-of-variance rule.  In the event that a proportion-of-variance stopping rule is adopted, the following result will be useful. It seems too easy to expunge effects such  as interactions. A wave of the wand and evidence against additivity disappears without even a poof. It smacks of fudging, of  throwing away data. Such uneasiness is not without justification. A powerful tool for scaling out interactions or other  sources of variance can be abused. Still, we believe that often this attitude is based on a general lack of familiarity with  the use of scale transformations as a theoretical tool. As this method becomes one of the standard tools of psychological  theorizing, much of the uneasiness should disappear. During this period of accommodation, a skeptical attitude will probably  be helpful in avoiding abuse of the method.  The concept of an output function should be useful in dispelling the not uncommon, usually implicit, assumption that truth is  found on the original scale, and, therefore, scale transformation entails truth distortion. Many psychologists accept a  perspective in which information processing and decision making is a central activity, related to inputs to the behaving  animal and reflected by behavioral output. For example, in Hull-Spence theory, excitatory potential is functionally related to  input variables while speed, amplitude, and probability are related to excitatory potential by different output functions. In  psycholinguistics, the competence-performance distinction separates a central computing process from a more peripheral output  process. In such theoretical approaches, the hypotheses concerning central processes are of greater importance; details of the  output process can be filled in as the picture becomes clear. Initial assumptions concerning the output process can be minimal.  The response transformation serves as an inverse output function which transforms the response measure to a scale more nearly  revealing the processes of central interest. Viewed thus, response scale transformation can be seen as a correction of  distortion produced by the output process rather than as an introduction of distortion. Functional measurement places such  scaling into a theoretical context, providing a rationale for  442 BOGARTZ AND WACKWITZ  the scaling and a test of its validity. It is in the absence of such a context, where response scale decisions are most  arbitrary that they will be most difficult and require great care.  Monotone and Nonmonotone Transformations  The present method does not constrain the ai to force a monotone transformation. In this regard it is inferior to Kruskal's  method (Kruskal, 1965) which does force monotonicity. It is superior to Kruskal's method in that it provides an explicit  transformation function and specifies the degrees of freedom lost in transforming, at least approximately. This allows a  statistical test of the residual effects that is not available with Kruskal's method. We expect that when a monotone  transformation is appropriate, our method will yield a polynomial which will be monotonic at least in the region of observed  values of the independent variable. The obtained transformation should of course be checked in this regard.  A single-valued output function implies monotonicity. If the transformation is nonmonotonic, the output function assigns more  than one overt response value to a single underlying psychological response value. This could result if the subject fluctuated  between two or more different monotonic output functions. Nonmonotonic transformations cannot be rejected out of hand,  therefore. A more serious problem will exist if the output function assigns the same overt response value to more than one  psychological response value, as in the case where the overt response is a U-shaped function of the underlying response. In  this case the appropriate transformation would be a many-value function, and the present method would presumably break down.  Eliminating Sources Which Should be Left for Tests  Special attention should be given to the example in which selected components of interactions were reduced to provide a test  of the averaging model vs. the multiplicative model for the multisection task. If the bilinear components had been scaled out,  the experiment would have been disabled as a test between the two models. The general consideration is the following. For  models in which scaling out a given source implies that another source or other sources will also be scaled out, a robust test  of the model will be available if only the given source is scaled out, since it will then be possible to employ an F test for  the other sources whose removal is implied.
