FISHBURN  Advanced Research Department, Research Analysis Corporation, McLean, Virginia  This paper presents a set of axioms for additive conjoint measurement in utility theory when the set of consequences, C, is a  subset of the Cartesian product of two sets. Axioms for preference, patterned after the von Neumann-Morgenstern axioms, are  first presented. These imply the existence of a utility function on C and gambles formed from the elements in C that is unique  up to a positive linear transformation, but do not imply the additive form of conjoint measurement. It is then shown that the  additive form results when one of the basic axioms is strengthened, so that the utility of each consequence equals the sum of  the utilities of its two factors. Uniqueness properties of the factor utilities are also discussed.  INTRODUCTION  In most discussions of independence or additivity in utility theory, including those in economics as well as psychology and  the management sciences, it is assumed that the basic set of consequences (or pure prospects, alternatives, etc.) is a product  set. When one is concerned with two basic factors or the two-dimensional case, as we shall be in this paper, the product set  takes the form X X Y. Each element (x, y) in X X Y is a consequence or an ordered pair of attributes of a consequence,  depending on the interpretation given for the situation at hand. The situation where X X Y is taken to be the relevant set of  consequences has either been discussed directly or results as a special case of the discussions of Adams and Fagot (1959),  Debreu (1960), Fishburn (1965a), Houthakker (1960), Krantz (1964), Scott (1964), Luce and Tukey (1964), and Tversky (1964,  1967). These papers are in general concerned with "simultaneous conjoint measurement," for which additivity (independence) in  utility theory is seen as a special area of application.  The purpose of this paper is to investigate the notion of additive conjoint measurement or additivity in the context of  expected utility theory when the set of relevant consequences, say C, is a subset of X X Y. A special case of the treatment  given here, which has been discussed. by Fishburn (1965a), arises when C is, in fact, the whole product set C = X x Y, which  was mentioned above. Tversky's papers (1964, 1967) include the possibility that C is a subset of X x Y, but his investigation,  like the  104  CONJOINT MEASUREMENT: INCOMPLETE PRODUCT SETS 105  others mentioned above, does not use probabilities or gambles. The reason for looking at cases where C is a proper subset of X  X Y arises from the fact that there are many situations in which one can formulate X and Y so that every consequence is an  element in X x Y" but not every element in X X Y is a consequence. Because some of the (x, y) in X X Y, but not in C, may seem  like so much nonsense to the individual whose utilities are of concern if one tries to interpret them as consequences, it may  be better to exclude them from consideration than to pretend that they share the same relevance as elements of X x Y which may  occur as the result of one or more of the strategies or courses of action that are available to the individual. One of the  most common instances of this sort arises from interpreting elements of X as actions taken by the individual in implementing a  strategy with elements of Y being possible outcomes of these actions. In many cases a given outcome in Y may be judged to be  capable of occurring in connection with some x in X but incapable of occurring with other x in X. For additional discussion of  this point the reader can consult Fishburn (1964), p. 33.  By taking C C X X Y as the set of relevant consequences we exclude any (x, y) in X r' Y but not in C from the individual's  preference-indifference relation <. Hence, if C is a proper subset of X X Y, we cannot admit the axiom that < is a weak  ordering of X X Y, which is assumed in the papers cited in the opening paragraph, Tversky (1967) excepted. In addition, by  taking C C X X Y instead of C = X X Y we destroy much of the easily-handled geometry of the latter case, and shall have to  take account of this. Without loss in generality, it may be supposed, however, that every x in X appears in at least one  element in C and that every Y in Y appears in at least one element in C.
