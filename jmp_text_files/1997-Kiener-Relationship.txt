On the Relationship between Two Types of Effects Caused by Color Adaptation: Changes in Color Appearance and Color Discriminability 

Stefan Kiener 

Universita�t Regensburg, Germany 

Varying the state of color adaptation not only causes changes in the appearance of color stimuli but also leads to changes in their discriminability. The effects of adaptation on color appearance can be determined experimentally by the method of cross-context matching. If cross-context matches are perfect with respect to all attributes by which color stimuli can be discriminated, it should be possible to use theories for cross-context matching in order to predict color discrimination across contexts. To characterize the empirical situation in which predictions of this type can be made, some conditions for cross-context matching and color discrimination are formulated in an axiomatic way and an elementary representation is derived from them. In order to test predictability of color discriminant across contexts empirically, an experiment was conducted with consists of two parts. In a first step, eight stimuli were matched across contexts. To the adjustments of each of the eight subjects a projective mapping f was fitted. For a second experiment pairs of stimuli were chosen that, according to f, should correspond to each other. Subjects repeatedly had to match each of these stimuli within the original context taken from the first experiment. Discrimination ellipsoids were calculated, which can be considered as approximations of isodiscriminability contours in color space. For most subjects, corresponding discrimination ellipsoids can be predicted by f with a sufficient degree of precision. ] 1997 Academic Press 

1. INTRODUCTION 

Varying the state of color adaption[1] produces two striking effects. First, there is a change in the appearance of color stimuli. E.g., a patch of light the color coordinates of which are x=y=0.33 and L=20 cd�m[2] will look white to a dark-adapted observer, if it is presented against a dark background, but it will be perceived as reddish (bluish), if the observer is exposed to a bluish (reddish) adaptation field. The second of the two major effects of color adaptation is that thresholds for color discrimination are changed, a finding obtained in several empirical studies (e.g., Brown, 1952b; Krauskopf, Williams 6 Heeley, 1982; Krauskopf 6 Gegenfurtner, 1992). 

In order to study the effects of adaptation on color appearance the experimental method of cross-context matching is frequently used. In this method, each of the subject's eyes is adapted to a different background field. Within one of the background fields, the subject is presented with a test stimulus and within the other background field she or he has to adjust the color of a matching field so that it has the same hue, saturation, and brightness as the test stimulus. Since this procedure is only one of several possible realizations of cross-context matching[2] and since the following theoretical considerations are not restricted to a particular experimental situation, the term ``background field'' will be avoided in the following in the favor of the more unspecific ``context''. 

The influence of adaptation on the discriminability of color stimuli can be determined by means of various experimental procedures suitable for threshold measurement. One might, for example, use signal detection techniques, apply adaptive procedures to find certain points on a psychometric function, or take reaction times for the detection of differences as an indicator of discriminability. However, one of the most popular research methods in this field is the determination of so-called discrimination ellipsoids, a technique described in more detail in Section 5. This technique has the advantage of saving much time and effort in comparison to traditional psychophysical procedures. In the following sections, two objectives are pursued. The main purpose of Sections 2�4 is to develop a general formal framework in which various assumptions implicit in many current ideas about color adaptation and color discrimination are stated as empirical axioms, in the style of modern measurement theory. In a first step, cross-context matching and color discrimination will be treated separately in Sections 2 and 3. This part of the theory serves mainly to summarize some widely accepted basic results about color vision and to formulate some essential technical properties of the empirical relations under consideration. As far as cross-context matching is concerned, the development relies heavily on the work of Krantz (1975) on Gra?mann-structures and, with respect to certain aspects, on Krantz's (1968) treatment of cross-context matching. 

In Section 4, a simple relationship between the empirical relations corresponding to cross-context matching and color discrimination is established. That relationship, if satisfied empirically, permits the prediction of color discrimination across contexts. It constitutes the central aspect of the whole theory. Another aim of Section 4 is to formulate explicitly some widespread assumptions about the relation of color discrimination to the algebraic structure of the color space. From the axioms introduced in Sections 2�4, a fairly general representation is derived and it is discussed to what extent the basic assumptions are justified by available empirical results. The subsequent sections are concerned with an empirical study designed to answer the question whether color discrimination can in fact be predicted across differing contexts.

4. RELATING COLOR DISCRIMINATION, CROSS-CONTEXT MATCHING, AND THE STRUCTURE OF COLOR SPACE 

So far, nothing has been said about the relationship between # and -. In order to generate hypotheses about this relationship it is helpful to speculate on the principles that underlie the subject's behavior in cross-context matching and color discrimination. Suppose that each color stimulus gives rise to a particular color sensation that depends on the stimulus itself as well as the context in which it is presented. Suppose further that there is a complete specification of color sensations in terms of a finite number of attributes such as hue, brightness, saturation, and maybe others. 

There is no need to assume that the attributes in question can be refered to by terms used in everyday language. Theoretically, it might prove more fruitful to represent color appearance in a more abstract way; the attributes in question could be given, for instance, by the outputs of a number of hypothetical neural channels. A rather general approach is presented by Suppes, Luce, Krantz, 6 Tversky (1989, Chapter 15, p. 258), who suggest that color appearance might be represented by a complete independent set of continuous open codes; for the present purpose it is not necessary to specify how many of these codes are needed and how they are related to particular aspects of color appearance. 

Given there is a complete specification of color appearance in terms of certain attributes it is quite clear how to account for color discrimination: Two stimuli that are presented within the same context can be discriminated if the corresponding sensations are different with respect to at least one of the attributes in question. In the case of crosscontext matching, however, there is more than one way to think about the contribution of the hypothetical attributes. The most straightforward idea is that two stimuli are judged as cross-context matches if and only if the corresponding sensations are identical with respect to all of the attributes by which color appearance is characterized. On the other hand, it could be the case that, in general, such perfect cross-context matches do not exist. In that situation, the subject's judgements might depend only on a few of the attributes under consideration, while the others are ignored. Only if the relation # expresses perfect identity of color appearance, i.e. identity with respect to all attributes that underlie color discrimination, it can be expected that there is a simple relationship between # and -. In that case, the following axiom can be stated: 

Axiom 8. For all stimuli a, b, a1 , b1 , a2 , b2 and contexts C, C1 , C2 , two conditions hold:

5. THE EMPIRICAL DETERMINATION OF ISODISCRIMINABILITY CONTOURS 

For the empirical part of the present study it will be necessary to find an experimental procedure for the estimation of difference thresholds in color space. As will be argued in Section 7, it is desirable for an efficient test of the theory to determine complete isodiscriminability contours instead of single pairs of just noticeably different stimuli. Usually, contours of equal discriminability are seldom determined directly by measuring thresholds in color space, a procedure that is very time consuming. However, there are empirical studies using such procedures and they indicate that just noticeable differences in color space can in fact be described 

113

by ellipsoids. E.g., Krauskopf 6 Gegenfurtner (1992) and Wandell (1985) use adaptive procedures in order to find just noticeable differences in a couple of directions from the coordinate vector of a given standard. If ellipses are fitted to these differences, the predictions are very close. 

In the light of these results, it may seem appropriate to apply the technique used by Brown 6 MacAdam (1949), Brown (1957), and Wyszecki 6 Fielder (1971) in order to obtain estimates of isodiscriminability contours. The subject repreatedly has to adjust the color of a small test field to make it correspond to that of a fixed standard stimulus. According to a study by Brown (1952a) the color coordinates of the subject's adjustments can be regarded as normally distributed, where the mean of the distribution is given by the coordinates of the standard stimulus. The matrix of variances and covariances can be estimated from the data and the resulting matrix determines a quadratic form, which, in turn, defines an elliptical confidence region around the coordinate vector of the standard stimulus. 

To the author's knowledge there is no empirical study concerning the relationship between discrimination ellipsoids determined this way and ``real'' isodiscriminability contours. In the sequel it is taken for granted that, for a given standard a and any context C, the sets E(a, b, C ), b # [b$ | a N[+] C[b][$][]][, are concentric ellipsoids the principal] axes of which have the same relative size and orientation. A natural assumption is that the variability of color matches in a particular direction from ,(a) is proportional to the relative extension of E(a, b, C) with respect to that direction. In the absence of empirical evidence supporting this view, it is adopted for the experiments reported in Sections 8 and 9 just for the sake of convenience. At the end of Section 7 some reasons are given why the assumption of a direct correspondence between isodiscriminability contours and discrimination ellipsoids is not too problematic for the purpose of the present study. 

6. EMPIRICAL EVIDENCE 

In Section 4 it was shown that Axioms 1�10 are sufficient to establish an elementary theory which can account for color appearance and color discrimination under varying states of adaptation. After a closer inspection of these axioms it will turn out that only a few of them need to be tested empirically. Axiom 1 and Axioms 4�6 can be regarded as minimal requirements for the experimental techniques by which the relations # and - are determined empirically. These axioms are introduced in order to guarantee that # and - reflect the ``natural'' understanding of ``having equal appearance'' and ``discriminability''. 

Whether Axiom 7 is satisfied empirically, depends mainly on the experimental technique used to determine -. If this 

technique aims at estimating the probabilities pC (a, b), a, b # A, C # C, it is reasonable to consider only a limited range of them, since it requires more and more effort to obtain reliable estimators as the probabilities get higher (assume here that pC (a, b) never reaches 1). Thus, as one cannot hope to cover the whole set of color appearances using the experimental procedure in question, it is convenient to introduce an upper limit pmax for pC (a, b) and treat all triplets (a, b, C ) for which that probability exceeds pmax as members of the same equivalence class with respect to -. 

Partial evidence for the validity of Axiom 10 is provided by Wandell (1985). He found that under suitable experimental conditions the isodiscriminability contours for standards with slightly differing coordinate vectors almost coincide, if the difference vector of the standards is added to one of them. To justify Axiom 10 completely, it would be necessary to show that this holds in every region of ,(A) and under various contexts. Moreover, a certain neighborhood U of the vector 0 would have to be specified such that the isodiscriminability contours for all standards a1 , a2 , for which v=,(a1)&,(a2) is a member of U, can be assumed as differing only by v. However, it is practically impossible to show that Axiom 10 is too strong with respect to these aspects, unless there are clear hints, in which region of color space violations are to be found. As long as there are no such hints, Axiom 10 might be a reasonable working hypothesis. 

The validity of Axiom 2, essentially a mere re-statement of Gra?mann's laws, is accepted by most color researchers. This is reflected in the common use of the traditional vector space representation, which is based on Gra?mann's laws, for displaying experimental results and theoretical predictions. Thus, it must be assumed that there is at least one context for which these laws hold. However, Gra?mann's laws are formulated without reference to contexts and they would surely not deserve their status as empirical laws if they were valid only for a particular experimental situation. The present formulation explicitly claims the universality of Gra?mann's laws, supposing that these laws hold regardless of the context within which the stimuli are presented, provided that this context remains unchanged. 

A substantial empirical constraint is imposed by Axiom 3 which claims that the equivalence classes of metameric color stimuli are invariant over contexts. If this were not true for the experimental situations traditionally used in cross-context matching studies, it would not make sense to display the results of such experiments in form of partial mappings in color space, as it is usually done. However, there is some evidence that metamerism persists over a broad range of viewing conditions and breaks down only for very high and very low intensities, or in case of extreme differences in intensity with respect to the stimulus used for adaptation (Wyszecki 6 Stiles, 1982; Jameson 6 Hurvich, 1972).

One of the most interesting aspects of this conception is that, in case of its empirical validity, one could use theories of color adaptation in order to predict color discrimination across contexts. On the other hand, known facts about color discrimination could be used to discriminate adequate theories of color adaptation from inadequate ones, since, for example, these facts might impose constraints on the mappings used for the prediction of cross-context matches. Thus, the remaining sections of this paper are concerned with the problem of testing Axiom 8 empirically. 

7. FORMULATION OF THE PROBLEM 

The most direct approach to an empirical test of Axiom 8 would be to proceed like this: First, the subject produces cross-context matches under contexts C and D for two stimuli a, b presented within context C; then, the probability pC (a, b) is compared to the corresponding probability for the equivalents of a and b under D; if Axiom 8 holds, these probabilities must be identical. But there is a major problem with this approach. Since pC (a, b) must not be too close to 1, the corresponding confidence regions for withincontext matches will overlap considerably. If Axiom 8 holds, this must also be the case for the confidence regions around ,(a$) and ,(b$) under context C, where a$ and b$ are the ``true'' matches of a and b. Thus, in many cases the subject's adjustments for a$ and b$ will not even be significantly different from each other and, consequently, cannot be regarded as a reliable basis for the determination of pD (a$, b$). 

Certainly, the problem of experimental error in crosscontext matching can never be avoided altogether. But in order to minimize this error, one can use the fact that certain assumptions about isodiscriminability contours and cross-context matches do not seem very critical. One of these assumptions is that isodiscriminability contours are elliptical (see Section 6) and that they are closely related to discrimination ellipsoids. Another one is that cross-context matches can be described by projective mappings operating in color space, as will be argued below. 

The reason why these observations can be used when designing an empirical test of Axiom 8 lies in the fact that this axiom guarantees preservation of isodiscriminability contours under the mappings fCD , C, D # C. By Axiom 8, (a, C )#(a$, D) and (b, C )#(b$, D) together imply

First and foremost, the estimates for the discrimination thresholds under consideration are much more reliable, since, again, information about many different stimuli is integrated. 

A second advantage lies in the fact that the specific nature of the isodiscriminability contours sets constraints on the general form of the function fCD . 10. DISCUSSION 

For most subjects, observed and predicted discrimination ellipsoids are rather close to each other. Unfortunately, no adequate statistical test for this similarity is available, because part of the deviation could be caused by errors in the determination of the function fCD ; these, in turn, may have led to a wrong choice of standard stimuli for the second experiment. This error term could only be accounted for, if, for a certain neighborhood of the standard's coordinate vector, the variation of discriminability were known. But the general pattern is quite regular and it seems hard to find any convincing evidence for the rejection of the hypothesis that discrimination ellipsoids can be predicted across contexts via fCD . 

If seen in this light, the empirical results support the view that cross-context matching ``preserves'' isodiscriminability contours��at least for contexts varying within a limited range comparable to the one spanned by this study. Of course, this is true only if there is a close relationship between isodiscriminability contours and discrimination ellipsoids. The conditions introduced in Section 5 may seem plausible but have not been tested yet. On the other hand, if cross-context predictability of discrimination ellipsoids is accepted on grounds of the present data, the most straightforward explanation for this regularity is the direct correspondence between isodiscriminability contours and discrimination ellipsoids, as described in Section 5. 

Cross-context predictability of isodiscriminability contours is a direct consequence of Axiom 8. Thus, the present data also support this axiom's empirical validity. As there is no evidence to date in favor of the objections cited above, Axiom 8 may be accepted as a working hypothesis. Therefore, it may be tentatively assumed that in color discrimination tasks subjects do not use more or other attributes of 

color appearance than in cross-context matching. In other words, if a particular stimulus can be matched across contexts, this match is perfect with respect to all attributes of color appearance by which different stimuli can be discriminated. 

Provided that the attributes in question can be conceived of as a complete independent set of continuous open color codes in the sense of Suppes et al. (1989, Chapter 15, p. 258), their number must be three (see Suppes et al., 1989). However, it can nevertheless be the case that not all aspects of color appearance are captured by three attributes, a view held by Mausfeld 6 Niedere�e (1993). There is some empirical evidence indicating that cross-context matches are incomplete in this sense (see Mausfeld 6 Niedere�e, 1993). But judging from the results of the current study, possible additional attributes do not contribute to color discrimination. 

Thus, it may be possible to find three independent color codes and a mapping relating A_C to these codes that can account for both types of adaptation effects, changes in the appearance of color stimuli and changes in their discriminability��provided, of course, that there is an adequate theory for color discrimination under a fixed context. One elementary example for a theory of this kind is the restriction of the function h introduced in Section 4 to the context under consideration. The axioms introduced in Sections 2�4 leave the exact nature of h unspecified, however. To render h useful as a means for the prediction of color discrimination, some additional restrictions must be introduced.
