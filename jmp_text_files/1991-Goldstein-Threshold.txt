GOLDSTEIN  The Universith of Chicago  Subjects who must decide whether an alternative is acceptable, whether a stimulus belongs to a specified category, or whether  a stimulus is recognized as previously displayed, have often been modeled as attempting to determine whether a  multidimensional stimulus exceeds a threshold. This paper examines and axiomatizes some general models for these discrete  judgment tasks. The threshold models considered, viewed as models of preference, generalize the conjunctive and disjunctive  rules, among other noncompensatory and compensatory decision rules. Viewed as models of categorization, the threshold models  generalize independent cue models and the notion of linear separability of categories. For each threshold model considered,  necessary and sufficient conditions are presented. The axiomatic analyses direct attention to the features of the models that  can be subjected to empirical test. It turns out that the most general form considered is too general to be falsifiable. In  each restricted case, the key feature of the model turns out to be a variant of a "context-independence" condition that is  highly fasifiable. ~0 1991 Academic Press, Inc.  Consider the person who must decide whether a particular alternative is acceptable, whether the person in the next seat on the  bus is an old acquaintance, or whether some division of goods among claimants is reasonable. The person must decide,  respectively, whether the alternative is sufficiently attractive, the traveling companion sufficiently familiar, or the  division of goods sufficiently fair to warrant the corresponding response. These examples illustrate the sort of tasks that  concern this paper: deciding whether a multidimensional stimulus exceeds a threshold. As the examples indicate, models for  this kind of discrete judgment are relevant to studies of preference (Einhorn & Hogarth, 1981; Pitz & Sachs, 1984),  recognition memory (Atkinson & Juola, 1973, 1974; Gillund & Shiffrin, 1984; Mandler, 1980), and concepts and categorization  (Medin & Smith, 1984; Oden, 1987; Smith & Medin, 1981). Since models in these diverse substantive areas share a common formal  structure, it is important to understand that formal structure at an abstract level that is divorced from the particular  substantive application. This paper attempts to explicate, in measurement theoretic terms, some general threshold models for  dichotomous and trichotomous judgments.  The models that concern us here can be described as follows. DISCUSSION  The threshold models discussed in this paper, for dichotomous and trichotomous judgment and for binary choice, have been  applied to a wide range of psychological phenomena. Most notable among these are applications to studies of preference,  categorization, and recognition. Analyzing the models axiomatically provides insight into the nature of the models'  similarities and differences, and provides guidance for conducting and interpreting experimental tests of the models. To  summarize, the unrestricted threshold model was seen to be too general to be useful. It is virtually certain to be satisfied,  whether or not the subject's responses are guided by subjective impressions of the component dimensions. Some sort of  restriction is required to render the models falsifiable. In this paper, this was accomplished by requiring the function F to  be one-to-one, weakly increasing, or strictly increasing in each variable separately. Each of these restrictions imposes an  implicit standard of what it means to process the component dimensions "independently," and yields a highly falsifiable  decomposable model. The key property of each decomposable model was found to be a variant of context-independence, which  spells out the implicit standard of "independent processing." Any experimental test of the decomposable threshold models must  target these context-independence properties.  As an example, the context-independence conditions can be used to help interpret some results on category learning. Two  categories G and B of n-dimensional stimuli are said to be linearly separable if there exist real-valued functions ui defined  on S;, for i = 1, ..., n, and real numbers K and a;, i = 1, ..., n, such that for all x E S,  n  x e G implies Y_ a; u; (x;) + K > 0, and r-i  n  x E B implies Y_ a; u; (x;) + K < 0. r-i  If people's categorization behavior can be described by an independent cue model (see Expression (2) above), then the  categories into which people sort exemplars are linearly separable. For this reason, researchers in concept learning have been  interested to see whether subjects can learn to distinguish linearly separable categories more easily than categories that do  not have this property. Medin and Schwanenflugel (1981) found that categories violating linear separability were learned as  easily as categories that satisfied linear separability, while Wattenmaker,  DECOMPOSABLE THRESHOLD MODELS 75  Dewey, Murphy, and Medin (1986) found that ease of learning interacted with the contextual knowledge that subjects could bring  to bear on the task. For our purposes, the interesting thing to note is that some of the experiments required subjects to  learn categories that violated not only linear separability, but all strictly monotonically decomposable threshold models. For  example, in Experiment 1 of Medin and Schwanenflugel (1981), some subjects were told that stimuli x=(1,0,0,0) and y=(0, l, 1,  1) were members of category A and that stimuli z = (1, 0, 0, 1) and w = (0, 1, 1, 0) were members of category B. Because these  classifications violate Axiom 2 of Theorem 2 (with respect to the fourth stimulus dimension), the categories violated the  entire class of strictly monotonically decomposable threshold models, not just linear separability. Therefore, the finding  that subjects had no more difficulty in learning these categories than in learning linearly separable categories casts doubt  not only on independent cue models of categorization, but on all strictly monotonically decomposable threshold models.  Finally, the form of the context-independence conditions raises an interesting issue about whether one can separate the  hypothesis that subjects process stimulus dimensions "independently" from the theoretical burden of specifying what the  psychological dimensions are. Because the context-independence axioms make reference to specific values on the component  dimensions, any test of these axioms must presuppose a particular set of dimensions for the stimuli. These axioms may be  satisfied for one dimensionalization and violated for another. Therefore, any experimental test of the models jointly tests  the adequacy of the dimensionalization and the empirical validity of the axioms.  Because the decomposable threshold models require the use of a "privileged" set of dimensions, it may be reasonable to  consider them as "psychological" dimensions. Psychological dimensions, in this sense, do not necessarily correspond to  perceptual dimensions. Indeed, the subject's perception of the stimuli may be integral; he or she may not even be able to  direct conscious attention to specified dimensions. Rather, these psychological dimensions refer to stimulus components whose  manipulation results in a systematic and orderly shift in the subject's overall assessment of the stimulus as a whole. Thus,  these are informational, rather perceptual, dimensions. In essence, the context-independence conditions themselves provide a  standard of what is or is not a "psychological" dimension in the sense intended here. Whether this definition proves to be a  useful one, and the relation between the resultant informational dimensions and, say, perceptual dimensions is a matter for  further research.
