Among the 305 articles which refer to Miller’s article from 1982 (Aug. 2008, ISI web of knowledge), only a small number refer to the kill-the-twin procedure (KTT) suggested by  Eriksen (1988) . Closer inspection of these articles reveals that only 10 of them actually used it. Two potential reasons come to mind: First, the KTT is derived from specific assumptions, namely, the simple fast guess model ( Ollman, 1966 ) with two exclusive states, a guess state and a response state. In the derivation of Inequality  (7) , we show that the KTT can be derived from a more general model in which the guess and response processes operate in parallel, the assumptions can, therefore, be relaxed to some extent. Second, there is little need for a correction procedure if only a few anticipatory responses are observed or if the violation of Inequality  (3)  is large, for example, with auditory-visual redundant signals. In contrast, in unimodal tasks (for example, redundant targets within the visual modality,  Feintuch & Cohen, 2002 ), coactivation effects are generally small or absent. In such a situation, the kill-the-twin procedure might help distinguishing parallel from coactive processing (e.g.  Krummenacher, Müller, & Heller, 2001 ).
