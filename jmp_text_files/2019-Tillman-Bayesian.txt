The concept of information processing is the cornerstone of the cognitive revolution within psychology. The increased focus on how humans process information has led to a much greater understanding of the higher-level functions of the human brain, as well as its capabilities and limitations. In an attempt to understand how human minds process information, researchers have proposed a wide variety of hypothetical cognitive systems across a range of sub-fields within psychology, such as language comprehension ( Coltheart, Rastle, Perry, Langdon, & Ziegler, 2001; Marslen-Wilson, 1975; McClelland & Elman, 1986; Seidenberg & McClelland, 1989 ), decision-making ( Brown & Heathcote, 2008; Ratcliff, 1978; Usher & McClelland, 2001 ), and visual attention ( Eriksen & James, 1986; LaBerge & Brown, 1989; Posner, Snyder, & Davidson, 1980; Treisman & Gelade, 1980 ), just to name a few.

There are two important properties of information processing systems relevant to processing stimuli in an environment and deciding between alternative ways to respond to the stimuli. These properties are the systems’ architecture and stopping rule. Architectures and stopping rules differ in how stimuli, signals and targets are processed. Stimuli are comprised of multiple signals which can vary in strength. A target is defined by whether a particular signal is present or absent. Two possible architectures of cognitive systems are serial and parallel. Serial architectures process each signal one-at-a-time while parallel architectures process multiple signals at the same time. To respond to the target, a system must also have a decision stopping rule. A system could stop processing and respond after all signals are processed, which we call an exhaustive stopping rule. Or the system could stop processing as soon as sufficient information has been collected to make a correct response, which we call a self-terminating stopping rule. A specific kind of self-terminating stropping rule is first-terminating, where a system will response as soon as a single signal has been processed.

Townsend and Nozawa (1995)  developed a methodology that allows researchers to identify the architectures and stopping rules of participants performing simple response time experiments: Systems Factorial Technology (SFT; see  Harding et al. (2016)  and  Houpt, Blaha, McIntire, Havig, and Townsend (2014)  for introductions). The core idea of SFT is to use signatures in interaction contrasts of response time (RT) distributions to determine whether a participant used a serial or parallel architecture with a self-terminating or exhaustive stopping rule on all trials. In the current paper, we explore the idea that participants may not be using the same architecture or stopping rule on each trial, which opens the door to many different signatures in the SFT analysis. Next, we will overview the SFT methodology before moving on to our exploration of mixtures.

Since the seminal paper of  Townsend and Nozawa (1995) , SFT has become a popular framework for distinguishing between serial and parallel processing architectures and self-terminating and exhaustive stopping rules ( Eidels, Houpt, Altieri, Pei, & Townsend, 2011; Eidels, Townsend, Hughes, & Perry, 2015; Fox & Houpt, 2016; Johnson, Blaha, Houpt, & Townsend, 2010; Townsend & Fifić, 2004 ). SFT is applied to a double factorial design. The double factorial design involves presenting a participant with one, two, or zero target stimuli on a single trial, where the participants respond based on target detection and some rule. Importantly, each target can be of high salience (i.e., easy to detect) or low salience (i.e., more difficult to detect), where the salience manipulation speeds up or slows down the RTs. The double target trials include four different types of trials: both targets have high salience (“HH”), the first target has high salience and the second has low salience (“HL”), the first target has low salience and the second has high salience (“LH”), and both targets have low salience (“LL”).

In double factorial designs, participants respond with one of two possible “rules” (defined by the experimenter). Participants must respond when  either  of the stimuli are a target (i.e., an “OR” rule), which requires the participants to respond after they have observed any target (i.e., “first-terminating” processing). Or participants must respond when  both  of the stimuli are targets (i.e., an “AND” rule), which requires the participants to respond only after they have observed both targets (i.e., “exhaustive” processing).

SFT uses the “interactions” between these four types of double target trials – known as “interaction contrasts” – to make inferences about the underlying processing architecture used by each person. Specifically, SFT relies on two interaction contrasts: the means of the response time distributions (i.e., the mean interaction contrast, MIC; see  Sternberg, 1969; Townsend & Nozawa, 1995 ) and the survivor functions of the response time distributions (i.e., the survivor interaction contrast, SIC; see  Townsend & Nozawa, 1995 ). For example, all serial models discussed so far (i.e., both “first-terminating” and “exhaustive” processes) predict an “additive” interaction between the mean response times, meaning that the MIC should be approximately equal to 0. In contrast, the parallel models predict non-additive interactions, with parallel exhaustive predicting a negative MIC, and parallel first-terminating predicting a positive MIC. The SIC can go a step further and distinguish between all types of processing architectures and stopping rules, with parallel exhaustive predicting a negative deflection in the SIC function over time, parallel first-terminating predicting a positive deflection, serial first-terminating predicting no deflection, and serial first-terminating predicting an initially negative deflection, followed by a positive deflection.

Crucially, the standard MIC and SIC assessments make a very strong assumption about processing of these multiple pieces of information: that the observer (i.e., a participant) uses the same specific combination of architecture and stopping rule on every trial of the task. Overall, this extremely useful assumption leads to the strong predictions for the MIC and SIC function for each type of processing. In addition, this assumption may be accurate in many circumstances where SFT is applied, such as simple tasks where each trial is near identical to the previous one. However, the assumption may not necessarily hold in all situations, and some research has suggested that people may not use the same stopping rule on all trials throughout a task ( Bushmakin, Eidels, & Heathcote, 2017 ). Additionally, research into these potential mixtures has been limited, with little understanding of how these mixtures may affect the SIC functions, and no method to measure the amount of between-trial mixture in the data.

Our article aims to make three key contributions that relate to the mixtures of processes within the SFT framework. Firstly, we explore the impact of different mixtures of architectures and stopping rules across trials on the predicted SIC functions, and what general patterns may be expected if mixtures exist. Secondly, we propose a method for measuring the potential mixtures within empirical data, so that researchers can potentially make more formal assessments about the presence of mixtures. Lastly, we discuss how our method can be extended to a hierarchical model, which provides a potential method for population assessment of SIC functions, as well as a method to assess mixtures for research questions that may be interested in this phenomenon.

As can be seen, the mixture of architecture appears to have a gradual, and systematic, effect on the SIC functions. For example, a 0.5 mixture of architecture under a first-terminating stopping rule falls exactly half way between the pure architecture functions. However, from a practical perspective, this may make the detection of mixtures difficult. Taking the above example, the 0.5 SIC function appears to resemble a pure parallel first-terminating architecture at an absolute level (with different generating parameters), showing a clear positive deflection. In fact, even the 0.2 (i.e., 80% of trials are serial) appears to resemble the parallel architecture, also containing a positive deflection. Therefore, in the non-parametric assessments of SFT, mixtures may be incorrectly detected as pure processes, leading to incorrect inferences.

Fig. 2  displays the effect of different trials having different stopping rules for both parallel (left) and serial (right) architectures. As with the architecture mixtures, the SIC functions show gradual and systematic shifts based on changes in rule mixture, with the intermediate probabilities resembling intermediate functions of the pure forms. However, this again appears to create a mimicry problem for classic SIC analysis within the SFT framework. One key example concerns the 0.5 mixture of the stopping rules for the parallel architectures. Interestingly, this perfect mixture creates a predicted SIC function with no deflections, which is exactly what is predicted by a serial architecture with a first-terminating stopping rule (right panel, purple line).

Mimicry between parallel and serial models has been documented for nearly 50 years ( Townsend, 1971, 1972, 1976; Townsend & Wenger, 2004 ). Some examples include, the fact that parallel models with capacity limitations can produce a mean RT slope that increases linearly with set size, an empirical finding often associated with serial models. Moreover, serial models with efficient processing can produce the flat RT set-size function that is often associated with parallel models. Beyond mean RT, for every parallel model’s RT distribution, there is always a serial model RT distribution that is equivalent, but the converse is not true ( Townsend, 1976 ). These mimicry issues were addressed by  Townsend and Nozawa (1995)  with the development of the SFT methodology. We have demonstrated here that even within the SFT framework, when there are mixtures of processing architecture and stopping rules, there is a potential identification problem within standard SIC assessments. The mixtures of certain architectures and/or stopping rules can resemble the SIC functions of pure, mixture-free processes, even when the pure process makes up little, or none, of the true mixture of processes.

As discussed above, the presence of mixtures appears to create a problem for standard SIC analyses, due the mixture functions mimicking pure functions. Therefore, if mixtures are present, then misleading inferences can be made with standard SIC assessment methods, and there is also no way of knowing whether misleading inferences are being made. Below, we propose a method that aims to measure the amount of mixtures present within a data set, and potentially detect any large mixtures that could result in misleading inferences. Specifically, our method aims to assess the response time distributions of each type of trial (i.e., HH, HL, LH, LL), and use the additional information contained within each of these distributions to attempt to estimate the mixture probabilities.

A pictorial explanation of our method can be seen in left panel of  Fig. 3 . As with our simulations above, our method begins with the predicted finishing time distributions for each channel (see  Houpt et al. (2017)  for a similar simulation design with the pure processes). To overcome this limitation, we use the recently proposed probability density approximation (PDA) method ( Turner & Sederberg, 2014 ). PDA involves simulating synthetic data via model simulation.

This method is less in line with the non-parametric philosophy of SFT, but also does not require any assumptions about the capacity of the system.

The “model-free” method uses the RTs of the single target trials (i.e., only one channel presented) as a proxy to the finishing times of each channel, meaning that only the mixture probabilities need to be estimated. We believe the “model-free” method falls more within the spirit of the non-parametric SFT methodology, and therefore, we chose to implement this method for the remainder of our article.

Both methods assume context invariance, where the experimental manipulation only affects the processing channels of interest. This assumption prevents using our methods in experimental situations where the target stimuli are accompanied by distractor stimuli. With violations of context invariance, such as the presence of distractors, the SFT methodology no longer provides unique diagnostic information about an individuals processing capacity ( Little, Eidels, Fific, & Wang, 2015 ). Context invariance is essential for our current mixture model approach because the method relies on the RTs of single target trials. Mixing single target trials that include distractors will not provide a reasonable estimate of the double target RT distributions. The model-free method also makes an implicit assumption of unlimited capacity, which we discuss and test in more detail later.

Here, we show how our method can be extended to the hierarchical case, allowing the group-level mixture probabilities to be estimated. Hierarchical models have become a popular method of applying computational cognitive models ( Shiffrin, Lee, Kim, and Wagenmakers (2008) ; see  Evans, Rae, Bushmakin, Rubin, and Brown (2017) ,  Tillman, Benders, Brown, and van Ravenzwaaij (2017) , and  Evans, Brown, Mewhort, and Heathcote (2018)  for examples), and hierarchical methods have recently been proposed for SFT using both the MIC ( Houpt & Fifić, 2017 ) and SIC ( Cox & Criss, 2017; Houpt, MacEachern, Peruggia, Townsend, & Van Zandt, 2016 ).

For the remainder of this paper we use the hierarchical extension to regularize the estimated parameters of each participant and only assess the estimation of the parameters of each individual. For estimation purposes, we place fairly uninformed priors on the  
                            μ 
                          and

                          parameters, being a truncated normal with mean 0.5, standard deviation 0.5, lower bound 0, and upper bound 1, and a gamma distribution with shape and scale of 1, respectively. All fits were performed using Differential Evolution Markov chain Monte Carlo (DE-MCMC;  Ter Braak (2006), Turner, Sederberg, Brown, and Steyvers (2013) ), with 12 parallel chains that each ran for 800 iterations of burn-in, with 200 posterior samples draw to obtain the posterior means.

Note that our method can also be used for the population assessment of pure-process SIC functions, in either a model-free or model-based manner. In order to perform these comparisons, the mixture parameter for the architecture/stopping rule is fixed to be 0/1, which allows a likelihood to be obtained for each of the 4 potential processes. After this, the likelihood of the 4 potential processes is compared to see which is most likely (this could also be implemented with corrections for model complexity, see  Myung (2000) ,  Roberts and Pashler (2000) , and  Evans, Howard, Heathcote, and Brown (2017)  for discussions, and  Annis, Evans, Miller, and Palmeri (2019)  and  Evans and Annis (2019)  for recent methods). However, we leave a more detailed assessment of these potential methods for future research.

Overall, the previous 4 simulations show good recovery, with only minimal discrepancies between the generated and recovered values. Therefore, when only considering mixtures of architecture or stopping rule, researchers can use our method to make sensible inferences about the mixture probabilities. However, in many situations, researchers want to make inferences about both architecture and stopping rule.

To assess the combination of both architecture and stopping rule mixtures we performed recoveries that generated and estimated both architecture and stopping rule mixture probabilities. The two mixture probabilities were generated from group distributions  
                            μ 
                          values of 0, 0.5, or 1. Factorially combining these generating values for each mixture probability created 9 different simulations, and the recoveries can be seen in the left column of  Fig. 5 . In general, these simulations show poor recovery for both the architecture and stopping rule mixture probabilities, with the recovered values rarely falling on or around the green line. The failed recovery suggests that the mimicry of different mixtures of architecture and stopping rule discussed earlier prevents both mixture probabilities from being determined simultaneously using RTs alone.

In an attempt to remedy this issue, we further constrained the estimation process by combining the information contained within response times with the additional information contained within task accuracy. The use of both response time and accuracy is common within model-based assessments of decision-making ( Brown & Heathcote, 2008; Ratcliff, 1978 ), as well as a recent model-based investigation of “rule-breaking” (e.g., an OR rule being used in an AND task;  Bushmakin et al. (2017) ). Within our framework of estimating mixture probabilities, the concept of rule-breaking can be used to provide a strong constraint of stopping rule.

If participants do respond according to the appropriate task stopping rule, then the error responses in the single target trials provide a strong constraint of stopping rule mixture probabilities. In particular, we can calculate the number of errors that are made given the number of trials the participant has used the incorrect stopping rule — rule breaking errors. For example, for an OR task, the participant must make a target present response when any target is detected. However, if they are using an AND stopping rule for 20% of the trials, then they will incorrectly respond target absent on at least 20% of the single target trials. Another example is for the AND task, where a target present response should not be made for single target trials. However, if the participant uses an OR stopping rule for 20% of the trials, then they will incorrectly respond target present on at least 20% of the single target trials. In both examples, the participant will have a minimum error rate of 20%.

For example, consider a single target is displayed in an OR task and the participant is using an AND stopping rule. Noise in the system may lead to the detection of the target in the channel for which there is a signal and detection of a target in the channel for which there is no signal.

When rule breaking is the only reason for errors being made in single target trials, forcing the mixture model to account for the number of errors in these trials – compared to the expected number based on the rule probability – should provide strong constraint on the parameter estimation. However, in many cases there will be other reasons that participants may make errors. For example, participants may not focus on the task properly on some trials, or make anticipatory responses, resulting in guessing behaviour in those trials with error rates at chance performance. Participants may also miss that a stimulus is present on some portion of trials, or incorrectly detect a stimulus that is actually absent, which could result in errors, depending on the trial type and whether the task requires OR or AND responses. In these cases, any errors made in the single target trials could be due to either rule breaking or another process, meaning that rule breaking errors become indistinguishable from other types of errors. However, further constraining the mixture model using the errors made in another condition where rule breaking would not result in errors – such as double target or non-target trials – would provide a clear distinction between errors due to rule breaking and errors due to other processes in single target trials, allowing rule breaking to again provide strong constraint on the parameter estimation.

For the next simulation, we assumed that errors in single target trials could be caused by either rule breaking, or a failure to pay attention to the task that resulted in guessing. We believe that this is a reasonable assumption in some cases, such as easy tasks where stimuli are difficult to incorrectly perceive even in the low salience condition, and that this assumption provides an adequate example for how accounting for errors can constrain parameter estimation. However, we do not believe that this is the only way that non-rule breaking errors occur, and that as tasks become more complex participants may become likely to make errors due to other reasons. For double target and non-target trials, the number of errors are purely determined by how often participants fail to pay attention to the task, as using the incorrect rule would still result in a correct response.

The previous section showed that with the incorporation of errors, our method is able to simultaneously recover mixtures of both architecture and stopping rule, despite the large amount of mimicry present in their predictions for the response time distributions. However, as mentioned earlier, when using the single target trials as a “model-free” proxy to the finishing times of each channel, our method makes the implicit assumption of unlimited capacity, where the processing of channels in single target trials are assumed to be identical to their processing in double target trials. As shown in previous work, cognitive systems can have limited (i.e., worse processing in double target trials), unlimited, or super capacity (i.e., better processing in double target trials), meaning that this assumption may not hold in some circumstances ( Townsend & Eidels, 2011 ).

The recoveries can be seen in the first column of  Fig. 6 . The recovery of the AND mixture probability remains reasonable, with the recovered AND probabilities mostly falling close to the green line. Therefore, it appears that violations of unlimited capacity still allow for reasonable estimation of stopping rule mixtures, but make the estimation of architecture mixtures impossible, with serial always being advantaged over parallel.

In an attempt to remedy the issues resulting from unlimited capacity violations, we added the estimation of a “capacity parameter” to our method, which was used to transform the single target trials before using them to as a proxy to the channel processing times. Specifically, we defined this capacity parameter as a real positive number, and multiplied the single target trial RTs by 1/(capacity parameter), meaning that an estimated value of 1 would indicate unlimited capacity, an estimated value below 1 would indicate limited capacity, and an estimated value above 1 would indicate super capacity. The recoveries can be seen in the middle column of  Fig. 6 . The recovery of the AND mixture probability is now extremely good again, with all points lying perfectly along the green line. The serial mixture probabilities are also greatly improved, with the recovered values generally falling in the area of the green line. However, there were still some recovery issues, especially in the case of a pure serial OR process (blue points), and a perfect mixture of both architecture and stopping rule (deep purple points). When looking at the recovery of the capacity parameter, both of these simulations seem to show the poorest recovery, with most recovered values falling closely along the green line, though the blue and deep purple points showing larger deviations. Therefore, when combining our method with the estimation of a capacity parameter, the ability to estimate both mixture probabilities is fairly robust even with violations of unlimited capacity, when the capacity parameter is estimated correctly. However, when the capacity parameter is estimated poorly, the recovery of the serial mixture probability is unsurprisingly poorer. We explored how mixtures of architectures or stopping rules across trials led to different predictions of SIC functions in double factorial designs. A key function of SFT is to use the response times (RT) recorded from a double factorial experiment to construct interaction contrasts that identify a participant’s processing architecture and stopping rule. The SIC function uses the entire RT distribution to distinguish parallel and serial processing architecture and first-terminating and exhaustive stopping rules. The key assumptions are that if the participant uses the same architecture and stopping rules on each trial then there will be unique signatures for each property.

In our simulation, we assumed participants could use a mixture of different architectures or different stopping rules across trials. What was interesting is that there was mimicry between different SIC functions. For example, a parallel processing system that uses a first-terminating rule on 50% of trials predicts an identical SIC function to a serial processing system that uses a first-terminating rule on 100% of trials. A crucial next step is to determine if participants really do mix architecture or stopping rules across trials, yet there is some evidence for participants mixing stopping rules ( Bushmakin et al., 2017 ).

Due to the mimicry, we developed a method for estimating mixture probabilities in double factorial designs. The method used a hierarchical Bayesian model of mixture probabilities for mixtures of architectures and mixtures of stopping rules. Our method was able to recover mixtures of architecture or stopping rule separately, and with the addition of error information we could recover mixtures of architecture and stopping rule simultaneously. Interestingly, this method not only allows for the identification of mixtures, but could also serve as an inference method for the SIC functions. With the new hierarchical Bayesian method, mixtures can now be assessed within SFT experiments, and assessments do not need to make the dichotomous choice between pure architectures and pure stopping rules.

Our method is limited because it uses the RTs from the single target presented trials. This means the RTs on the double target trials are produced from a mixture of single target trials, but this will only be plausible if we assume unlimited capacity. We attempted to remedy this issue with the inclusion of a capacity parameter, which we used to transform the response times for the single target trials before creating the mixture distributions from them. This capacity parameter allowed the mixture of stopping rule to be recovered regardless of capacity, and greatly improved the recovery of the mixture of architecture. Another potential solution is to use a “model-based” approach, where the single channels are assumed to be drawn from a specific distribution, which are used to general the mixture distributions. Although this would alleviate the need for capacity assumptions and/transformations, we believe that this approach is not in line with the non-parametric nature of SFT, and therefore, may not be preferred by researchers.

Another limitation was the missed recoveries in mixtures of architecture. Although the deviations were relatively small in most situations, the overall pattern of the groups was in line with the true mixture probability. One reason for these mis-estimations may be the small number of single target trials used in our simulations to construct the density kernel (i.e.,  
                         ∼ 
                      800) compared to much larger numbers normally used in PDA applications (i.e.,  
                         ∼ 
                      10,000,  Holmes (2015) ). Therefore, a larger number of single-target trials, or potentially a bootstrapping procedure, may need be used to create great stability of the density kernel in future developments. Alternatively, the model-based approach avoids this problem as the individual channels are created through simulation based on a specific model, which may make it a more promising alternative.

In future developments of the mixture model, we also hope to estimate the group level parameters of the hierarchical Bayesian model. This will allow for simultaneous estimation of individual and group level processing architecture and stopping rules, the group-level inferences on the SIC functions, and potentially resolve some of the recovery issues seen when looking at individual subjects.

Overall, our exploration of mixtures of architectures and decisional stopping rules provided some novel insights. Our simulations revealed a pattern of interaction contrasts across different mixtures, some of which mimic each other. In response to the mimicry, we developed a hierarchical Bayesian inference method that can identify mixture probabilities in architecture and stopping rules. The method may be useful for researchers investigating the architecture and stopping rules of cognitive systems, in conjunction with standard, well validated SFT methodologies. ‘
