Macmillan and Kaplan (1985), stating that A; underestimates A,,, considered A’ (their A(g)) as an alternative estimator. They expressed concern that A’ may be suspect because of an absence of reports of empirical ROCs shaped like isopleths of A’. These isopleths appear concave upward on a binormal graph (Macmillan & Kaplan, 1985, Fig. 11, p. 195). According to Aaronson and Watts (1987), however, such empirical ROCs may not have been observed because, as “is generally the case in cognitive psychology, particularly studies of psycholinguistics and verbal memory” (p. 441, italics theirs), it may be impossible to measure more than one point on a given ROC curve. They also suggested that such ROCs may not have been reported, even when multiple ROC points could be obtained, because investigators who knew beforehand that the assumptions of SDT analysis were violated (as the authors believed is generally the case in cognitive psychology) would not. publish binormal graphs. 

Recent users of A’ have tended to accept Pastore and Scheirer’s (1974) interpretation regarding the assumptions it requires and its meaning. For example, Stevens, Cain, and DeMarque (1990) used A’ because it “is a nonparametric statistic and thus circumvents assumptions concerning the form of the memory operating characteristic curve”(p.294). Cromer, Tarnowski, Stein, Harton, and Thornton (1990) chose A’ because it is “a nonparametric sensitivity index that is analogous to d’, but which does not require any assumptions concerning underlying signal and noise distributions” (p. 296). Recent users have considered A’ to be a more theoretically based measure of sensitivity than percentage correct. In the words of Boyce, Pollatsek, and Rayner (1989), A’ is “the best estimate of what the percentage correct would have been if a forced-choice procedure had been employed instead of a presenceabsence procedure” (p. 561). 

Measure A’ has been applied well beyond the field of normal human memory mentioned by Snodgrass and Corwin (1988). The 30 articles were distributed among 29 journal titles and covered applications of A’ to learning and memory (nine articles), perception (12 articles), and attention and vigilance (six articles). In addition, there was an article on cardiovascular reactivity and another on artifact detection, and one article discussed methodology. The applications involved normal and abnormal subjects, including both humans and animals.

Asis true for other such measures, however, a change in the value of A’ may occur for reasons other than a change in discriminability (Link & Heath, 1975). The same caution applies to measure A”.

For point (0, 0), it could be argued that Norman’s (1964) region / 1s the area below the positive diagonal and region A, is the entire area above the positive diagonal, so that regions S and A, both have zero area. Pastore and Scheirer’s (1974) use of the notation A, to mean both trapezoidal area A, and area A’ sometimes leaves the reader guessing as to what measure actually was used in a study. For example, Podd (1990), citing Pastore and Scheirer, used A, in a study that involved four rating levels, resulting in three ROC points. When computing hit proportion, false alarm proportion, and d’, however, Podd combined rating levels to produce a single ROC point. The article did not state whether rating levels similarly were combined when computing A,. If they were, Podd’s measure of discrimination accuracy was A’; if not, it was Ay. The review of the origins of A’ showed that, contrary to statements in the literature (Cromer ef ai/., 1990; Pastore & Scheirer, 1974; Stevens et a/., 1990), A’ does require significant assumptions about the form of the underlying ROC curve. It is true that measure A’ does not need the assumptions required by SDT measures d’ and A,,i¢., that there be underlying distributions that are transformable _ simultaneously to Gaussian form with equal variance. Measure A’, however, does assume that the underlying ROC curve is proper, that is, that the ROC curve is monotonically nondecreasing with nonincreasing slope. This assumption is not always met. For example, when Craig (1977) examined results from a number of vigilance 

experiments, he found that a substantial proportion (30% ) of individual ROC curves failed to satisfy this assumption. Contrary to statements in the literature (Grier, 1971, Nelson, 1984; Pastore & Scheirer, 1974; Richardson, 1972), A’ is not the algebraic average of the upper and lower bounds on area under all possible underlying ROC curves through a given ROC point. Neither does A’ have the interpretation stated by Boyce et a/. (1989) of being the best estimate of the area under the underlying ROC curve. These interpretations remain untrue when the ROC curves are restricted to being proper. A comparison of (3) to (1) and (2) shows that A’ can be interpreted correctly to be the weighted average of the upper and lower bounds on area under all possible proper underlying ROC curves, with the upper bound on the area assigned a greater weight. The weighting, however, varies with the position of the ROC point. If the user wants a measure of discrimination accuracy that is the straightforward, unweighted algebraic average of the upper and lower bounds on the area under all possible proper underlying ROC curves, then that measure is A”, 

Nelson (1984), believing that A’ (his A, for a single ROC point) is “computed in terms of the mean of the maximum and minimum possible areas associated with the single ROC point” (p. 122, italics his), expressed concern that the choice of the algebraic mean for a given application may be “arbitrary and without any scaling foundation” (p. 122). The present paper shows that 4’ is not the average Nelson believed it to be and that, with the additional assumption that the ROC curves are proper, measure A” is actually that average. Nelson’s concern, however, still applies to A”. 

Pollack and Norman (1964) did not consider sample variability in the development and application of A’. A shortcoming of both A’ and A” is that their sample statistics presently are not known, so confidence intervals are not available for, and statistical tests cannot be performed on, individual values of A’ or A”. Ross-Farhang, Gadient, and Wasserman (1990) stated that sample values of A’ are distributed binomially, citing Pollack and Hsieh (1969). In actuality, however, Pollack and Hsieh studied trapezoidal area (their A,), not A’, and they showed experimentally that the sample statistics of trapezoidal area were only approximately binomial. Measures A’ and A” are defined only for a single ROC point, require restrictive assumptions about the existence and nature of an underlying ROC curve, involve averaging that may be considered arbitrary, and have unknown sample statistics. If the user wants to avoid these properties of A’ and A”, there are other useful measures of discrimination accuracy that are generalizable to multiple ROC points, that do not require assumptions about underlying ROC curves or distributions, that have straightforward and meaningful probabilistic interpretations and for which sample statistics are known (Bamber, 1975; Nelson, 1984, 1986).
