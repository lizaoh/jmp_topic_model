DOIGNON  University Libre de Bruxelles  We consider a class of systems, the states of which can be represented by particular subsets of features in a basic set. A  procedure for assessing the state of a system in which the presence of a particular feature is tested on each trial is  described. The feature is chosen so that the outcome of the test is as informative as possible (in a specific sense). This  outcome results in a modification of a class of plausible states. The sequence of plausibilities is a finite Markov chain.  Results of practical significance are obtained and cover cases in which the response data are noisy, and the state of the  system may even change randomly from trial to trial. An application of primary interest to the authors is the assessment of  knowledge. In this case, the systems are human subjects, the features are questions or problems, and the state of an  individual is that particular subset of questions that the individual is capable of solving. The conditions investigated in  the paper, even though primarily dictated by this application, are nevertheless of general scope. C: 1988 Academic Press, Inc. The analogies among these situations are not superficial, and it makes sense to construct not only a common descriptive  framework but also a common theory, and even highly similar procedures to solve the problem proposed, namely, that of  efficiently assessing the state of a system. Thus, this system is a physical device in Example [1], a person in Examples [2]  and [4], and a visual display in Example [3]. (In this last case, the state of the visual display is thus a pattern.) The  literature on some of these topics is quite vast, and we shall not attempt to summarize it here (see, e.g., Shortliffe &  Buchanan, 1975; Shortliffe, 1976, for medical diagnosis; and Duda & Hart, 1973; Fu, 1974, for pattern recognition). Our  primary interest in developing an assessment procedure lies in Example [4]. For us, this paper is only a step toward practical  implementation of computerized knowledge assessment procedures in an educational context. Accordingly, in the rest of this  paper, we shall adopt a terminology consistent with that particular application. The reader should, however, keep the other  examples in mind when checking our axioms or our theorems. A systematic comparison between the procedures developed in this  paper and various others in use in this and other fields will be undertaken in a later paper.  Let us sketch the basic ideas. We assume that a possibly large, but finite, basic set Q of questions is available to describe  the knowledge states. In a simple case, each state is defined by a specified subset of questions, which some individual is  capable of solving. (Later in this paper, a less restrictive definition of a "knowledge state" will be used, involving the  concept of a "random state.") Not all subsets of questions are possible states, however. We suppose that the set of all  possible states forms a particular family rc of subsets of Q, called the knowledge structure. Axioms constraining the  knowledge structure, which will play a critical role, will be given. The task at hand is to uncover as quickly as possible, by  a sequence of well-chosen questions, the knowledge state of an individual. We suppose that the responses to the questions are  coded simply as correct or incorrect. Thus, for example, if an incorrect response to a question is recorded, an inference is  made which may alter the plausibility of the various knowledge states. Taking these altered plausibilities  234 FALMAGNE AND DOIGNON  into account, the next question is then chosen to be as informative as possible (in a sense to be specified).  Ideally, a procedure designed to assess the knowledge state of an individual concerning a body of information should satisfy  the following requirements:  (i) It should be efficient, that is, capable of providing an assessment of the knowledge state of the individual after a  minimum, or at least relatively small, number of questions.  (ii) It should be robust, that is, capable of uncovering the true state, or one resembling it, even in a situation where the  data are noisy, or the structure of the knowledge states under consideration is not fully adequate. For example, if for some  reason, the true state of the subject has not been included in the structure of states envisaged by the assessment procedure,  then the predicted state should have much in common with the true state.  (iii) If the true knowledge state of the subject is not stable, but oscillates stochastically from trial to trial, the  assessment procedure should reveal important features of the process governing these oscillations.  (iv) The procedure should be based on an explicitly defined model of the structure of the set of all knowledge states  considered possible and of the assessment mechanism. This would permit a thorough analysis of the biases and mistakes of the  procedure and would help considerably in the comparison of alternative versions of the procedure, and in the construction of  (v) Finally, an application of the procedure should be feasible, in important practical cases, with a minimum amount of  computer memory (less than one megabyte). In education, for instance, our goal is a procedure that can be implemented on a  small computer, such as those likely to be found, at the present time, in public schools in the U.S. While it is certainly  true that powerful computers will become increasingly available, the complexity of the knowledge structures that we will want  to code, to analyse, and to search will also grow. Economizing the computer memory will thus remain a reasonable concern, even  in the long term.  The class of procedures that we are about to describe was designed with these requirements in mind.' It is cast in the  framework of the theory of finite Markov chains, as presented in standard texts (such as Kemeny & Snell, 1965; Feller, 1970).  We assume in the sequel that the reader is familiar with the standard concepts and results of this theory. Except when  indicated otherwise, our terminology is that used by Kemeny and Snell.
