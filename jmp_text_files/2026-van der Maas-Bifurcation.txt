The network approach proposes that mental disorders emerge not from a single latent cause but from direct interactions among symptoms ( Cramer et al., 2010 ). The connectivity hypothesis specifically proposes that mental disorders may emerge when symptoms are strongly interconnected, causing the system to settle into stable, self-sustaining pathological states ( Borsboom, 2017 ).

This influential idea has been examined in several empirical studies, some supporting ( Guloksuz et al., 2016; Pe et al., 2015; Schweren et al., 2018; van Borkulo et al., 2015 ) and others challenging ( de Vos et al., 2017 ) the hypothesis.

                    We assume that networks exhibit reasonably high connectivity and predominantly positive (balanced) interactions ( Gleeson et al., 2012 ). Under these conditions, the mean-field approximation is justified. The mean-field approximation is useful because it reduces a high-dimensional network to a tractable low-dimensional system that still captures its essential dynamics. Although we employ the mean-field approximation throughout, it is worth noting that this approximation can fail for sparse or unbalanced networks. These networks behave in more erratic ways, and our conclusions do not extend to those settings ( Binder & Young, 1986 ).

                            1 
                            / 
                            1 
                         
                       Ising model, connectivity acts as a splitting variable in the cusp catastrophe: as connectivity increases, the range of external field values that lie within the bistable region widens. The effect of connectivity is analogous to that of the inverse temperature. For both Ising models, increasing connectivity enlarges the bifurcation set and thereby increases the extremity of the possible states as well as the magnitude of hysteresis effects. However, in the

                         τ

                            For somewhat lower external field values (red line), the healthy state (dominated by zero-valued nodes) is initially the only stable state. We hope our results will benefit the study of connectivity as a risk factor in clinical research. The analytical derivation of the bifurcation set may also be of interest for other non-psychological applications of the Ising model.
