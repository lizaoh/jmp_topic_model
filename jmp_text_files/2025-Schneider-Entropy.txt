Understanding human decision making can be difficult because researchers often only have access to the result of the decision and limited knowledge of the causal internal mechanisms. People are able to describe how certain their decisions are, and probing this introspection can yield valuable insights ( Fleming, 2024 ). One problem in analyzing uncertainty is that, unlike the results of a decision that can be described by psychometric functions derived from signal detection theory, decision uncertainty has not been described with a quantitative functional form. Analysis of uncertainty judgments has instead often resorted to simply smoothing the data, measuring the maximum or fitting the data to an arbitrary function ( Caziot & Mamassian, 2021 ;  Gallagher et al., 2019 ,  2021 ;  Maldonado Moscoso et al., 2020 ), or provides measures of overall sensitivity comparing uncertainty to performance ( Maniscalco & Lau, 2012 ), which does not allow insight into subjective perceptual effects where there is no objectively correct response. Here we use information theory ( Shannon, 1948 ) to derive a functional form for the uncertainty reports.

Efforts to quantify uncertainty have focused on the accuracy of confidence reports in predicting behavioral decision responses ( Fleming & Lau, 2014 ;  Maniscalco & Lau, 2012 ). These measures are tied to particular performance levels ( Guggenmos, 2021 ) and have difficulty dealing with bias ( Fleming & Lau, 2014 ).  Dayan (2023)  improves upon these measures using the mutual information between accuracy and uncertainty. However, performance-based measurements are theoretically troublesome when measuring subjective perception criteria, where accuracy and the “correct” response are nebulous.  Guggenmos (2022)  introduces a generative approach and presents a “confidence link function” that describes the confidence reports as a rescaling the choice probability. Though the general framework is valuable, this particular procedure in choosing the linking function may work in special cases, but it is not derived from theory and is not generalizable to different types of decisions such as equality judgments. Here I present a generative model derived from first principles, a function that describes the intrinsic uncertainty in a decision—the entropy—that the confidence reports presumably measure. The model can be applied to any decision function, and the bias parameters are explicitly included in the entropy model. Its meaningful model parameters can be compared to those from the psychometric function and can aid in detecting biases in the decision responses.

As a demonstration and essential application of this technique, we apply it to otherwise intractable data that were acquired to investigate whether paying attention to an object change the way it looks or merely alters the decision mechanism.

One anomalous but influential study concluded that an exogenous attentional cue seemed to alter the luminance contrast appearance of a subsequently presented stimulus, causing it to appear higher in contrast than uncued targets ( Carrasco et al., 2004 ). Other studies, employing a variety of different stimuli and experiments, consistently have shown that, because the effects of attention depend on the type of decision made, and because attention does not compromise the ability to make veridical judgements about appearance, attention does not alter appearance but instead influences the decision mechanisms used to report appearance ( Itthipuripat et al., 2019 ;  Kerzel et al., 2010 ;  Linares et al., 2019 ;  Schneider, 2006 ;  Schneider & Komlos, 2008 ;  Schneider & Malik, 2021 ;  Zhou et al., 2018 ). We have proposed that attention increases the salience of a stimulus without altering its appearance ( Beck & Schneider, 2017 ); however, the exact mechanism by which attention operates upon the decision mechanism remains unclear. In this study we show that attention does not prevent veridical perception of contrast appearance but can alter the decision process, such that different types of decisions, even based on indistinguishable uncertainty distributions, can yield different reports.

Using the entropy model of uncertainty allowed a precise quantitative comparison between the participants’ internal assessments and their decisions and reports. Given the nature and form of the participants’ uncertainty reports, these conclusions would not be possible without a quantitative model. The entropy model can be applied to any psychometric function sampled at multiple stimulus intensities, since the uncertainty reported is not absolute but relative to the context—observers employ a subjective criterion to report whether they are “certain” or not, and that criterion may vary. For example, an observer might choose a centering criterion that yields roughly an equal number of “certain” and “uncertain” responses throughout a particular experiment.

External factors such as the attentional cues and bias instructions were seen to decouple uncertainty and the decision. Attentional cues (Exps. 2a and b) increased participants’ uncertainty (significantly in Exp. 2b, but the trend in Exp. 2a did not reach significance), especially in comparison to the post-cued bias instructions (Exp. 1) that significantly shifted the uncertainty distribution in the opposite direction. The different decision mechanisms of the attention-cued comparative (Exp. 2a) and equality (Exp. 2b) judgments produced significantly different reports even though their uncertainty distributions were not significantly different. The disconnect between awareness and accuracy are not unprecedented—the most famous example of this is blindsight, in which patients report being completely uncertain but are nonetheless able to make accurate decisions ( Weiskrantz, 1996 ).  Landry et al. (2021)  concluded that attention does not directly affect subjective perception but rather indirectly affects confidence by increasing perceptual evidence and lowering decision criteria.

Several previous studies have investigated the effects of attentional cues on single targets in discrimination or detection tasks. For endogenous attentional cues, there is disagreement whether the cues increased ( Kurtz et al., 2017 ), decreased ( Recht et al., 2021 ) or had no effect on confidence ( Landry et al., 2021 ;  Wilimzig et al., 2008 ). These studies all agreed, however, that the exogenous cues, such as those used in the present study, had no effect on confidence ratings for single stimuli. The present study differs from these studies in that we were measuring the effects of attentional cues on the comparison between two stimuli, one of which was cued and the other not.

The example experiments demonstrated that the uncertainty involved in comparing identical sets of target stimuli can vary considerably given the nature of the task. Confidence is based on the totality of the evidence available in a decision, which changes based on the stimuli, the type of decision made and the attentional cues or instructions. Attention can affect the decision mechanism and the participants confidence in their decision differently, depending on the task. This suggests that attention is operating upon the decision, or the influence or salience of the stimuli ( Beck & Schneider, 2017 ), rather than altering the content or appearance of the stimuli before the decision is made.

One interesting aspect of the results is that a participant's internal uncertainty assessment could be quantified as the sum the entropy of two external observable parameters, the entropy of the stimuli and the entropy of the observer's response probabilities. The fact that the stimulus contribution to the total entropy did not significantly vary among the different cue conditions is consistent with the cues and attention not altering the internal representations of the stimuli themselves, but only the decision mechanism.

We derived a simple model of uncertainty, the total entropy of the psychometric function and the stimuli, to quantitatively model observers’ confidence in their judgments during perceptual tasks. We collected experimental data using two different types of decision and showed that the observers’ uncertainty consistently reflected the total entropy. This model expands the power and usefulness of uncertainty judgments in probing the mechanisms of human perception, permitting a precise measurement of factors, such as response and decision biases, that may cause differences between people's internal assessments and the outcomes of their decisions.

Participants . Fourteen undergraduates were paid to participate in the study. All participants had normal or corrected-to-normal visual acuity, gave their written, informed consent under a protocol approved by the York University Human Participants Review Committee, and were naïve to the purpose of the experiment.

Three participants were excluded from the analysis: one gave the same response to every trial (S5), one responded randomly in one experiment (S10), and one very rarely indicated uncertainty (S14). We show the data from all participants, including these three excluded, in Appendix 3 (online supplementary material).

Power analysis . Given the large effect sizes (Cohen's d > 1) observed in previous similar studies (e.g.  Schneider & Komlos, 2008 ), the suggested sample size is approximately 10 for 80 % power with a two-tailed  t -test significance level of 0.05. Therefore, our experiments with 11 participants were adequately powered to detect the expected effects.

Apparatus . The stimuli were generated on a Macintosh Pro computer (Apple, Inc., Cupertino, CA) using the Matlab (The Math Works, Inc., Natick, MA) programming language and displayed using Psychophysics Toolbox 3 functions ( Brainard, 1997 ;  Kleiner et al., 2007 ;  Pelli, 1997 ) on a ViewSonic P225fb monitor (ViewSonic Corp., Walnut, CA) with a refresh rate of 119.6 Hz. The stimuli durations reported below were constrained by the refresh rate and were rounded up to an integral multiple of the 8.4 ms frame rate. The output channels of the video card were combined with a video attenuator device (Video Switcher, Xiangrui Li, Los Angeles, CA) to enable 12-bit precision in the gray-scale luminance values ( Li et al., 2003 ). The gamma function and luminance of the monitor were measured using an LS-100 photometer (Konica Minolta Photo Imaging USA, Mahwah, NJ).

Visual stimuli . A diagram of the stimulus sequence is shown in  Fig. 1 . Two Gabor stimuli targets, 4 cpd sine gratings, oriented vertically, zero phase and Gaussian envelopes with a standard deviation of 1°, were simultaneously presented for 40 ms on a uniform gray field. The centers of the targets were located 4° eccentricity to the right or left of the fixation point. In Experiments 2a and b, 120 ms before the onset of the targets, a 0.3° diameter black dot was presented for 67 ms at the location 1.5° directly above the center of one of the two targets. In Experiment 1, the onset of this cue stimulus was 500 ms after the target onset (460 ms after the targets disappeared), but the other parameters were the same. Procedure . Participants were seated alone in a dark room and viewed the display unrestrained from 50 cm. The cue stimulus appeared randomly on the left or right for each trial. Each of the 61 contrast levels of the uncued target were repeated 25 times, for a total of 1525 trials, randomly distributed throughout the session.

The task of the participants on each trial was to determine, In Experiments 1 and 2a (comparative judgment), which target had the higher contrast, and in Experiments 2b (equality judgment), whether the two targets were equal in contrast. In Experiment 1, a decision bias was explicitly introduced by instructing the participants to choose the post-cued stimulus if they were uncertain which of the two stimuli had the higher contrast. For the comparative judgment, participants pressed the left arrow key on the keyboard to indicate that the target on the left had the higher contrast or the right arrow key to indicate that the target on the right had higher contrast. For the equality judgment, participants pressed the S key to indicate that the two targets had equal (“same”) contrast or the D key to indicate that the two targets had unequal (“different”) contrast.

In all three experiments, in addition to the relative contrast judgment, participants also indicated the certainty of each of their responses. While still holding down the first response key, participants pressed the up-arrow key if they were certain about their response or the down-arrow key if they were uncertain. Participants had unlimited time to respond. The cue stimulus for the next trial appeared 0.5–1 s after the response was made.

Each experimental sessions lasted approximately one hour, during which the participants were automatically allowed to rest and break fixation after every 50 trials, resuming fixation and the experiment when ready. Each participant began each experiment with a practice run with the experimenter in the room, demonstrating understanding of the instructions of that experiment and the ability to perform the task.
