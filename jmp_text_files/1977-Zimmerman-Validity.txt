WILLIAMS  University of Miami, Coral Gables, Florida 33124  In the theory of test validity it is assumed that error scores on two distinct tests, a predictor and a criterion, are  uncorrelated. The expected-value concept of true score in the classical test-theory model, as formulated by Lord and Novick,  Guttman, and others, implies mathematically, without further assumptions, that true scores and error scores are uncorrelated.  This concept does not imply, however, that error scores on two arbitrary tests are uncorrelated, and an additional axiom of  "experimental independence" is needed in order to obtain familiar results in the theory of test validity. The formulas derived  in the present paper do not depend on this assumption and can be applied to all test scores. These more general formulas  reveal some unexpected and anomalous properties of test validty and have implications for the interpretation of validity  coefficients in practice. Under some conditions there is no attenuation produced by error of measurement, and the correlation  between observed scores sometimes can exceed the correlation between true scores, so that the usual correction for attenuation  may be inappropriate and misleading. Observed scores on two tests can be positively correlated even when true scores are  negatively correlated, and the validity coefficient can exceed the index of reliability. In some cases of practical interest,  the validity coefficient will decrease with increase in test length. These anomalies sometimes occur even when the correlation  between error scores is quite small, and their magnitude is inversely related to test reliability. The elimination of  correlated errors in practice will not enhance a test's predictive value, but will restore the properties of the validity  coefficient that are familiar in the classical theory. Unfortunately, however, the correlation between error scores on distinct tests X and Y, that is, p(Ex, E,,), is quite  different from the other correlations listed above. The construction of true scores and error scores from the expected-value  concept does not guarantee that this correlation is always zero. In order to rule out correlated errors of measurement, it is  necessary to introduce a somewhat arbitrary axiom of "experimental independence" or "linear experimental independence," which  is independent of the other axioms in the model. Several writers, from time to time, have expressed doubts about this axiom.  Rozeboom (1966) remarked that "responsible test theorists have repeatedly pointed out that it is madness to expect the  measurement-error components of test items administered on the same testing occasion to be untainted by correlation."  Previous studies, as the above quotation suggests, have focused attention mainly on correlated error scores on items or  subtests which affect estimates of test reliability by internal-consistency methods, such as coefficient alpha (see, for  example, Guttman, 1953; Rozeboom, 1966). Relatively little attention has been paid to test validity in predictor-criterion  situations, where the influence of correlated errors is also quite serious, because the well-known validity formulas that are  widely used in practice can be derived only if the correlation p(Ex, E,.) vanishes.  It has been generally supposed that a systematic treatment of correlated errors in test theory would be difficult and  complicated. The present paper will show, however, that a relatively simple approach to the problem is possible and that the  classical theory can be generalized in a very natural way, in which the correlation p(EX, E,) appears in the main formulas for  test validity. In the first part of this paper we present a model based on the theory of conditional expectation, which turns  out to be equivalent to the models of Novick (1966) and Lord and Novick (1968), except for those results which depend on the  notion of experimental independence. Next, we derive general formulas for test validity that hold in all cases, whatever the  correlation between error scores on two tests. These formulas are surprisingly simple, although their implications are  unexpected and somewhat paradoxical.  OBSERVED SCORES, TRUE SCORES, AND TEST RELIABILITY  The expected-value concept of true score in test theory is based on the notion of a "propensity distribution" of an  individual's observed scores over independent, repeated measurements (Lord and Novick, 1968). An individual's true score is  identified with the expectation of the observed score with respect to that distribution, and the error score is the difference  between the observed score and its expectation. This construction is carried out for each individual in a population, and the  random variables X, T, and E that are familiar in the classical theory range over the entire population. That is, the usual  observed scores, true scores, and error scores represent variation over all individuals, as well as variation that could arise  over repeated measurements on each individual. CORRELATED ERRORS IN TESTING PRACTICE  The implications of the results derived in this paper depend on the extent to which test scores in practice may be influenced  by correlated errors of measurement. The very difficulty of estimating this correlation is perhaps good reason for exploring  the more general point of view mentioned above. It is likely, as several writers have pointed out, that correlated errors do  arise frequently in testing practice (see, for example, Rozeboom, 1966). The examples given below show that the general  formulas derived in this paper sometimes lead to substantially different results even when the correlation between error  scores is small. The more general theory has the advantage of making available formulas which apply in all cases, whatever the  degree of correlation between error scores. If there is ever reason to believe that the assumption of experimental  independence is satisfied, the usual formulas can be recovered.  146 ZIMMERMAN AND WILLIAMS  First, the results in this paper correct what may have been a misconception in previous work. Correlated errors of measurement  clearly do not generally decrease or attenuate test reliability and validity. Since reliability is the ratio of one test's  true variance to its observed variance, it is not affected by the correlation between error scores on distinct tests.  Furthermore, we have seen that positively correlated errors spuriously inflate validity and lead to overcorrections when the  Spearman correction for attenuation is used. Only negative correlations between error scores will decrease the correlation  between observed scores (see Eq. (15)). Accordingly, the elimination of correlated errors in practice will not in general  enhance a test's predictive value. Rather, it will ensure that validity coefficients have the properties that are familiar in  the classical model and that the usual formulas will not lead to misinterpretations.  It should be noted, however, that estimates of test reliability using internal consistency methods such as coefficient alpha  are influenced by correlated error scores on items or subtests (see, for example, Guttman, 1953; Rozeboom, 1966). Similarly,  parallelforms methods cannot estimate reliability accurately if, because of correlated errors, the two forms are not in fact  parallel. Also, formulas for the reliability of difference scores depend on an assumption of uncorrelated errors (Williams and  Zimmerman, to appear).  Correlation between error scores on distinct tests can arise whenever the tests are administered on the same occasion (test  batteries, profiles, and so on) and perhaps in other circumstances. Variables such as illness, noise, and cheating can produce  covariance between scores on tests administered together (see Novick, 1966, p. 13 for a description of one case).
