Positive answers are  given in the cases of ordinal and nominal measurement, together with some indications of the sizes of the errors involved.  Problems of extending the theory to more complex types of measurement are discussed, some open problems and conjectures are  formulated, and a relation between the `approximate representation' and `stochastic choice model' approaches to measurement  with fallible data is established.  1. INTRODUCTION  This paper proposes precisations of the intuitive ideas of an empirical relational system's approximately satisfying laws of a  theory of measurement, and of a numerical measure over its domain giving an approximate representation of the empirical system  in a corresponding numerical system, and then investigates the question: is approximate satisfaction of the laws of a theory  of measurement necessary and/or sufficient for the existence of an approximate numerical representations of the appropriate  kind, and, if so, how may such representations be constructed ? Though the problem of approximate representation is general,  we shall concentrate on just the simplest cases: nominal and ordinal scales. More exactly, we will consider approximate weak  and simple orderings, and approximate equivalence relations and the numerical representations corresponding to them. REMARKS, OPEN PROBLEMS, AND CONJECTURES  5.1. Possible Applications  We have not attempted to compare the approximate scaling procedures described in Theorems 2, 3, and 4 with those employed in  practice in empirical systems which do not exactly satisfy various ordinal axioms, and we shall confine ourselves there to a  few unsystematic comments. First, detailed comparison only seems appropriate with one of two quite different kinds of `ordinal  scales': namely with those which are based directly  REPRESENTING APPROXIMATE ORDERINGS 203  on simple binary relational judgements, such as is Mohs' scale hardness (and possibly individual or team ranking in various  types of competition), in contrast to "causal indices' such as are exemplified in the Beaufort Wind Scale and the Richter  Scale of earth quake intensity. Focussing just on the Mohs' scale, it seems plausible that these hardness indices do in fact  yield approximate weak ordering representations for the empirical system of scratch comparison, but it is less plausible that  the Mobs' scaling procedure, which involves comparisons with standards, is at all similar to the ranking or bunched rank  ordering procedures considered in section 3. In basing the ordinal scaling procedure on comparisons with standards, the Mobs'  scale is in fact closer akin to the method of approximate equivalence relation representation described in Theorem 4, which is  easily seen to be a `comparison with standards' procedure. Clearly, more work is needed on the general topic of relations  between what are essentially statistical scaling procedures, exemplified by percentile rankings, and ones which involve the  use of standards.  A further comment is in order on a special problem which arises with statistical scales like rankings. The practical  applicability of these procedures clearly requires the estimation of, for example, the proportion of individuals in a  population to which a scaled individual has some relation, and therefore the detailed analysis of these scaling methods cannot  neglect the analysis of the kinds of estimation employed. Though problems of estimation may be raised even with respect to  exact systems of measurement, they seem to assume a more central role in connection with approximation measurement.  5.2. Connections with Probabilistic Ordinal Theories  It is natural in considering approximate ordinal systems to allow the possibility that two objects in a domain may be compared  several times, with differing results on different occasions. This seems to be the case in competitive rankings, where, for  example, two opponents may meet several times with each one winning on some of them. One way to deal with this sort of  situation is to work formally with the proportions, IIx, y, of times an individual x stands in some relation, R, to y, out of  all occasions on which they are compared. This is essentially the probabilistic ordering approach which has been employed in  the study of binary choices (cf. Luce and Suppes (1963)). Problems of Other Kinds of Approximate Representation  (I) Other Purely Universally Axiomatized Systems  We are presently studying the possibility of applying approximate representational analysis to systems of measurement other  than those discussed in sections 1-4, but have encountered serious difficulties which will be commented on in this and the  final sub-  REPRESENTING APPROXIMATE ORDERINGS 205  section. It will appear that perhaps the greatest difficulties arise with types of measurement like additive, interval, or  conjoint, which are normally axiomatized with axioms of other than purely universal form (in first-order logic), but something  may be said about systems which are universally axiomatizable, of which Luce's semiorder theory is perhaps the most important  example. Here there is no difficulty in defining an approximate semiorder and an approximate semiorder representation, or in  proving that approximate semiorder representability entails the approximate satisfaction of the usual semiorder axioms. The  problem lies in proving the converse: that approximate satisfaction of the semiorder axioms entails approximate semiorder  representability. Our methods for weak order representation do not appear to generalize, nor, for reasons which will become  apparent in the final subsection, can Luce's own method for constructing a weak ordering generated be a semiorder be modified  to generate an approximate weak order from an approximate semiorder. Thus it is an open problem whether in fact approximate  satisfaction of the semiorder axioms entails approximate semiorder representability, and a resolution of this question may be  important in evaluating the `adequacy' of the semiorder theory (if the axioms were not sufficient to entail approximate  representability, this would suggest that they were not `robust' and relatively insensitive to `slight' lack of fit with data). (11) Systems Axiomatized with Non-Universal Axioms  We believe that it is not a serious complication that in moving to systems like additive and interval measurement usually  axiomatized with non-universal axioms, we also move to ones whose standard models are infinite, for which degrees of truth can  no longer be defined as proportions. Indeed, our work on the general logic of approximate truth deals with truth measures  defined in terms of arbitrary countable additive measures over the  206 ADAMS AND CARLSTROM  domains of models, and this work suggests that no essential difficulties should arise with approximate representation theory  because of the need to consider infinite models. The more serious problem arises from the fact that, though there is no  difficulty in defining precisely, say, the idea of an approximate interval representation, it is trivially false that  approximate satisfaction of standard axioms for interval measurement entails the existence of an approximate interval  representation, and there is a very real difficulty in formulating analogues to the axioms of ordinal measurement, the  approximate satisfaction of which will guarantee approximate representability. As the following suggests, the root of the  difficulty lies in the fact that while the logic of approximate truth is almost the same as the logic of exact truth in  application to purely universal axioms, the two logics differ very radically in application to non-universal axioms such as  occur in standard interval and additive measurement theories.  Consider the example of interval measurement axiomatized in terms of a quaternary relation xyRzw over a domain D, interpreted  to mean that the difference between x and y is at least as great as that between z and w. Typical axioms (see Krantz et al.,  Chapter 4) include a few of purely universal form, such as interval transitivity,  xyRzw & zwRuv :D xyRuv,  plus a universal-existential Solutions Axiom,  (3w)(xyRzw & zwRxy),  plus a non-elementary Archimedean Axiom. The key fact of the logic of exact truth is that the universal axioms plus the  Solutions Axiom entail all of the other purely universal laws of interval measurement, while the universal axioms alone do not  (this is proved in Adams and Carlstrom (1977)). On the other hand, this is trivially false in the logic of approximate truth,  and in fact we can say that the universal axioms plus Solutions Axiom do not measure-entail any universal law not  measure-entailed by the universal axioms alone. To see this, suppose that some universal law 0 is not measure-entailed by the  universal axioms, hence it is not approximately true in some model <D, R), in which the universal axioms are approximately  true. Then <D, R> can be extended by the addition of a single new element, c, to a model <D V {c}, R'> in which the universal  axioms are still approximately true, 0 still is not approximately true, but in which the Solutions Axiom is exactly true. To  do this it is only necessary to make R' agree with R in D, and to make xyRzc true for all x, y, and z in D v {c} (the key fact  here is that while universal laws have approximately the same degrees of truth in systems and their approximate subsystems,  this is not the case for non-universal laws). In effect, what this shows is that approximate satisfaction of  universal-existential axioms which are common to additive, interval, and conjoint measurement theories, can have almost  nothing to do with the existence of approximate representations, and we are at loss to know that they should be replaced by. The example is Luce's semiorder theory, which can be  formulated (see Luce (1956)) in terms of a binary `strict preference relation,' xPy over a domain D, satisfying three  universal axioms. The standard semiorder representation is, however, constructed in terms of a weak ordering relation, S,  generated by P, defined such that  ar -(vz) ((zpx D zpy) & (ypz D xpz)).  What cannot be established is the approximate equivalent of the theorem that if P exactly satisfies the semiorder axioms then  S is an exact weak ordering such that xPy D xSy, and in fact it is easily shown that the semiorder axioms can be arbitrarily  highly true while xSy is universally false.
