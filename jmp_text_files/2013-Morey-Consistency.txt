In a number of articles, Francis has tackled the issue of replication, with a dual focus on “to-good-to-be-true” sets of experiments–that is, sets of experiments with apparently too few non-significant results–and on educating other researchers about the statistics of replication. Although I will argue against the use of the statistical consistency test of   Ioannidis and Trikalinos (2007)  used by Francis in these articles, I agree that attention to these issues is important. Both education and action is needed to deal with the issue of publication bias in psychological research, and Francis’ work as a whole provides needed education about these issues. My critical comments about the consistency test, and the claims made on its behalf, should be understood in this context.

There are several problems with Francis’ approach. First, publication bias is a property of the research publication process, which is known to be biased. Whether individual products of this biased process are biased is the wrong question. Second, the null hypothesis does not express a reasonable model of lack of publication bias, and in fact is surely false even if there were no publication bias; finally, as a significance test, the consistency test does not properly assess the evidence at all.

Consider a researcher who has developed a test of whether successive Bernoulli trials are independent or have negative dependencies such as those that occur in human-randomized sequences ( Falk & Konold, 1997 ). It is known that human-randomized sequences tend to contain too many alternations (01 and 10), and such sequences “look” more random. One possible test to assess the independence (null) hypothesis is to count the number of alternations in a sequence of  
                         N 
                       trials and if this number is too high, reject the null hypothesis. Suppose we wish to evaluate the sequence 011101000. We note that the sequence has 4 of a possible 8 alternations: exactly the number we would expect under the null hypothesis. Does this sequence exhibit bias?

One might be tempted to say that the sequence does not exhibit bias, but suppose further that we knew that it was a human generated the sequence. The answer now is surely “yes” — or, more precisely, the question is wrong. If we know the sequence was human-generated, the fact that the particular sequence chosen happens to have the expected number of alternations under the independence assumption is irrelevant, because bias is not a property of the sample but is rather a property of the sampling process. To say that a sample is biased is simply shorthand for saying that the sampling process that led to it is biased. The situation with the consistency test is exactly analogous. I do not know anyone who disagrees that publication bias exists, and is pervasive. As Francis points out, publication bias has many sources across the publication process: authors, who may be overly concentrated on supporting a particular narrative that they ignore their non-significant results; reviewers, who have their own narratives, and desire a clean story from an article; editors, who may want to cut difficult-to-interpret null effects in the interest of space; and plausibly many more.

Thus, if we ask the question of whether there was bias in the publication process of a particular set of studies, the answer is “yes”. Because the research and publication process is biased,

As   Simonsohn (2012)  points out, a test of publication bias is therefore like    Cohen’s (1994)  tongue-in-cheek test for whether the Earth is round. In the presence of sure publication bias, we want to know whether the bias is large and what the implications of the bias are. The consistency test of   Ioannidis and Trikalinos (2007)  does not provide such information. Further, as   Francis (2013)  points out, an estimate of the size of the bias is probably impossible; a significant result from the consistency test thus cannot be used as a proxy for a claim that the publication bias is large.

We know the answer to our research question before we even propose a statistical test for it. But supposing we did require a statistical test, we might ask whether the consistency test on offer is a good one.

Consider the assumptions underlying the consistency test: in particular, consider the binomial assumption that requires a sample of fixed size  
                         N 
                       be taken. Active researchers who carefully consider the model of research implied by the null hypothesis will find it odd. In my own experience, the number of experiments in a set is almost never fixed. Experiments are typically planned one by one, the outcome of a previous experiment determining whether another experiment is run. A non-significant result may convince a researcher that they are wasting their time; a significant or borderline result may encourage a researcher to improve the research protocol and run another experiment. These sequential decisions are complex and idiosyncratic, but not necessarily problematic.

Consider the following scenario: a researcher performs an experiment. If the results of the experiment are significant, she runs another experiment. The details of each experiment are meticulously planned before each experiment to avoid problematic  
                         p 
                        value snooping. The researcher continues to run small variations on the initial experiment until she obtains a non-significant result. Because the researcher misunderstands replication ( Francis, 2012b,c ), she assumes that the effect is not robust enough for study, and gives up. She immediately publishes all her results, including the final non-significant experiment.

Although this scenario is simplistic, it captures the sequential nature of the actual research process. Under this scenario, the consistency test’s null hypothesis is strictly false, and should be rejected. The expected number of non-significant results is always exactly 1, which may be greater than or less than the number expected by the consistency test assumptions — yet there is no publication bias. The core problem is that the consistency test incorrectly equates violations of the  binomial  assumption with publication bias.

Could the consistency test be changed to accommodate the sequential nature of scientific testing? Probably not, for several reasons. First, under any sequential model, it is critical that the entire sequence be selected at once, with no studies that were not part of the sequence included. Of course, knowing which studies to include would require knowing the mind of the researcher who performed them, which is generally impossible (perhaps even to the researcher herself). Consider a researcher who performs 5 studies, one of which has a non-significant effect. Suppose her behavior roughly conforms to the sequential model above, and she attempts to publish all experiments in a single article. A reviewer, unhappy with a small detail in one of the studies, requests an additional study to be done before resubmission, which turns out significant. The final version of the paper has the experiments in an order different from that in which they were performed, for clarity of narrative. We do not know which experiments form a sequence, and we may never know. The second reason to doubt that a sequential consistency test is possible is that sequential decisions are idiosyncratic. One researcher may decide to stop after a single non-significant experiment. Perhaps a researcher seeking to debunk the first researcher will stop after a single significant effect. It seems unlikely that we could design a good test for publication bias under such diverse scenarios, especially without knowledge of the process that led to the experiments.

                         N 
                       would be the smart thing to do, in fact, because larger sets yield more power. If, however, the true model is sequential and there is no publication bias, then selectively choosing large experiment sets leads us unwittingly to almost certain error.

To be clear, my argument is not that small deviations from the null hypothesis render the test trivial. A common argument against hypothesis testing is that because the null hypothesis is surely false–because of inconsequential deviations from distributional assumptions, or tiny deviations from the null mean, or the like–that hypothesis testing is useless ( Cohen, 1994; Gelman & Shalizi, 2013 ). I have argued elsewhere that models are not really false, but rather can fail to be useful because their central assumptions do not relate to reality in a meaningful way ( Morey, Romeijn, & Rouder, 2013 ). It is not simply the side assumptions in the consistency test that are violated; rather, the central assumption that studies can be thought of as coming from a fixed-size group is violated. This assumption does not resemble actual scientific practice. Common violations of this assumption, such as sequential experimentation, are neither publication bias nor are they problematic. Inferring publication bias from a rejection of the null hypothesis is thus unwarranted.

One of the interesting features of popular statistical practice is the effortless, but highly problematic, way in which statistics without connection to formal epistemic notions like evidence ( Neyman, 1957 ) are called on to support such claims. In the consistency test, for instance, we move from the finding that  
                         p 
                         < 
                         . 
                         1 
                      –a number that is purely conventional–to the assertion that there is sufficient evidence to warrant claiming publication bias (e.g.,    Francis, 2012a , “Evidence that publication bias contaminated studies relating social class and unethical behavior”).

Francis takes a dual attitude toward hypothesis testing: when using the consistency test to critique other researchers, the hypothesis test is a tool for assessing whether a given claim has sufficient evidence against it. When discussing the general use of hypothesis tests, however, Francis states that “a hypothesis test is appropriate if the outcome drives a specific course of action” ( Francis, 2013 ). Given Francis’ own use of hypothesis testing as an epistemic tool rather than a decision tool, this recommendation seems incongruous.

Also seemingly incongruous is Francis’ suggestion that researchers move to Bayesian methods ( Francis, 2012e ) citing  Wagenmakers (2007) , who reviews arguments that null hypothesis significance testing cannot be used as an evidential tool. Francis himself affirms that his approach is problematic, stating that “[t]here are admittedly difficulties with hypothesis testing, and Bayesian methods might be better in many respects...but the basic logic is not entirely unreasonable and the approach is widely practiced...” ( Francis, unpublished manuscript ).

As Francis alludes, significance tests are deeply problematic for stating evidence. Both Bayesian and non-Bayesian statisticians have pointed out that the  
                         p 
                       values from a significance test are not formal measures of evidence, in spite of the common practice interpreting them as such ( Berger & Sellke, 1987; Royall, 1997; Wagenmakers, 2007 ); further, they have a bias to overstate the evidence against the null hypothesis ( Berger & Sellke, 1987; Rouder, Speckman, Sun, Morey, & Iverson, 2009; Sellke, Bayarri, & Berger, 2001; Wetzels et al., 2011 ). The fact that significance testing is “widely practiced” is not an argument for it; such a statement is unexpected from a researcher arguing  against  questionable research practices that are themselves widely-practiced.

Francis uses a “not entirely unreasonable” method that yields no principled measure of evidence, one that tends to exaggerate the evidence against the null hypothesis, and claims that it somehow yields sufficient evidence to completely ignore entire sets of studies in the psychological literature. Justifying his call to ignore the studies, Francis states that “[t]he responsibility for providing evidence about the existence of an effect belongs to the authors running the substantive experiments, and a biased set of such experiments does not”. Given that null hypothesis significance tests and the use of  
                         p 
                        values as measures of evidence are deeply problematic, it is Francis who has not met his evidential responsibility.

Three weaknesses are apparent in Francis’ use of the consistency test to detect publication bias. Bias is known to be inherent in the process of research and publishing, making the test unnecessary; the null hypothesis does not correspond to any reasonable model of bias-free publication and thus the logic of the test is flawed; and claims of sufficient evidence for publication bias on the basis of a significance test are unfounded, because  
                         p 
                        values cannot be interpreted as measures of evidence. In light of these issues, I believe that drawing inferences from results of the consistency test to be critically flawed.

The foregoing critiques should not be interpreted to mean that I deny that publication bias is a problem; in fact, I think it is a widespread problem because I have witnessed the way that publication bias is manifest in the scientific process from analysis and interpretation to review and publication. I do not, however, believe that the consistency test should be used to infer publication bias, because it does not–and cannot–deliver what is advertised on its behalf. Because the consistency test is questionable at best, and completely misleading at worst, we should instead focus on changes in scientific culture to reduce publication bias. We will never eliminate this bias, of course. As long as imperfect people are doing research, we will have to content ourselves with an imperfect–but hopefully constantly improving–scientific process.
