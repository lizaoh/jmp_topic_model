RAO  University of Florida  The psychological framework proposed by Thompson and Singh (1967) is extended to obtain a statistical interpretation of the  stimulus-response phenomenon permitting ties when comparing two stimuli. Using this interpretation, the generalized  BradleyTerry model (Rao and Kupper, 1967) for paired comparisons and the Bradley-Terry model (1952b) for triple comparisons  are derived as the appropriate asymptotic models for experiments permitting tied observations.  1. INTRODUCTION AND SUMMARY  The paired and triple comparison experimental arrangements are common in behavioral sciences. In a paired comparison  experiment, a subject is exposed to two stimuli and is asked to report the greater of the two he experiences. In a triple  comparison, on the other hand, the subject is exposed to three stimuli and is asked to report the relative magnitudes of the  resulting experienced sensations.  Several models have been proposed to account for the outcomes in paired and triple comparison experiments. A widely used model  for paired comparison experiments is due to Bradley and Terry (1952a), later generalized by Rao and Kupper (1967). For a  triple comparison experiment, there exists a model due to Bradley and Terry (1952b) and another due to Bradley and Pendergrass  (1960).  Thompson and Singh (1967) using a statistical interpretation of the stimulusresponse phenomenon, and some limit theorems of  probability theory, obtained an asymptotic derivation of the Bradley-Terry paired comparison model, thereby showing how this  model can be justified in paired comparison experiments.  The objectives in this paper are twofold. First of all, it will be shown that the method of Thompson and Singh can be used to  derive the generalized Bradley-Terry paired comparison model for an experiment in which ties are permitted. Secondly, the  method will be used to derive the Bradley-Terry model for a triple comparison experiment. PSYCHOLOGICAL FRAMEWORK  We begin with a brief review of the psychological framework proposed by Thompson and Singh (1967). Sensory perception begins  with the activation of a subject's receptors by a stimulus. A large number of signals are then transmitted from individual  receptors to the brain where they are synthesized into an overall intensity of the stimulus. Intensity of a given stimulus is  thought to depend upon the number of receptors activated and the frequency of neural activity within each receptor. To account  for the fact that a subject reports the more intense stimulus as the lesser stimulus, a random error in the transmission  system is postulated. This random error appears as the relative frequency with which a subject reports stimulus Xt as greater  than stimulus X2 , which approximates P[Xl -> X2],t the probability that Xl is greater than Xz . They assume that a subject  receiving a stimulus X experiences a (random) sensation y, which depends on a large number of transmitted signals, and that  the experienced sensations yI and y2 corresponding to the stimuli Xl and X2 satisfy  PIXi --, X2] = P[yr > yd.  Thompson and Singh (1967) use the above psychological framework with two specific modes of dependency of y on the signals.  They show that if y can be represented as a sum of a large number of signals, then the well-known Thurstone-Mosteller model is  appropriate for describing P[Xl --* X2], whereas if y can be regarded as the maximum of a large number of signals, then the  appropriate model is the Bradley-Terry model.
