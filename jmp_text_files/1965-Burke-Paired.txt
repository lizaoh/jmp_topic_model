ZINNES  Indiana University, Bloomington, Indiana  The theory of choice developed by Luce and the paired comparison theory due to Thurstone are restated so as to permit direct  comparison. It is shown that the theories differ in general, but coincide for certain sets of stimuli. The class of stimuli on  which they coincide is described, and it is proved that they can coincide on no stimuli outside of the class. The problem of  finding a class of stimuli which gives maximal discrepancy between the theories can be posed in a number of ways. For one  statement of the problem, solutions are found. Unfortunately, the nature of the solutions makes it very difficult to design an  experiment for deciding between the theories. The general problem of an experimental decision is discussed briefly, and some  pair comparison data are analyzed. The sets of data conform somewhat more closely to the Thurstone than to the Luce model, but  superiority is by no means definitive.  Psychological theorizing has frequently occurred in the guise of a search for measurement scales. This was indicated by  Bergmann and Spence (1944), but their philosophical point had little direct influence on theoretical work. However, in  researches of Thurstone (1927, 1959) among many others, it is quite clear that measurement scales are psychological theories.  That this is the fact for pair comparison data was made transparent by Luce (1959) in an important monograph.  The revolution initiated by R. D. Luce's monograph (1959) in the measurement field, as it appears to us, was to look at  probabilities instead of scale values. In so doing techniques and laws of scaling immediately gave way to theories of choice;  the pursuit of equal interval scales became the problem of predicting choice in a variety of experimental contexts; and the  question of whether two scaling techniques led to the same scale values was replaced with the question of what statistics  would discriminate between two theories of choice.  The present paper is written in the context of this new look in psychological scaling in the sense that our analysis of two  well known theories of binary choices, the Thurstone and Bradley-Terry-Luce theories (Bradley and Terry, 1952; Luce, 1959;  Thurstone, 1927), is in terms of the actual constraints they impose on choice  1 Research reported here has been supported in part by grants PHS M3939 and M6754(A) from the National Institute for Mental  Health.  z Now at the California State College at Hayward.  53  54 BURKE AND ZINNES  probabilities. DEFINITIONS  Following the notation in Suppes and Zinnes (1963), we let B be a set of stimuli, p a probability function on B x B such that  for a, b in B, pab = 1 - pba . The couple <B, p> is then called a pair comparison system. In the usual pair comparison  experiment pab corresponds to the proportion of occasions on which stimulus a is chosen over b and B to the set of stimuli  used in the experiment.  The purpose of introducing the notation <B, p> is to be able to discuss pair comparison data without reference to a particular  theory of pair comparison choices. It also provides a focal point for comparing different theories. By a (nontrivial) pair  comparison theory we mean a theory that restricts the total set of possible pair comparison systems to a smaller subset of  admissible pair comparison systems, that is, to the systems said to be compatible with the theory. In fact one way of defining  a pair comparison theory implicitly is by enumerating the set of admissible pair comparison systems. Two theories are then  readily compared by indicating the nature of the intersection of the two sets of admissible systems. If the admissible sets of  pair comparison systems for the two theories coincide exactly, then the two theories are said to be equivalent, even though  the conventional formulation of the two theories may be exceedingly different and the scale values assigned by the theories  nonlinearly related.  The following definitions of the Thurstone and Bradley-Terry-Luce theories are stated in terms of pair comparison systems and  depart, therefore, from the usual definitions. MAXIMIZING L AND T DISCREPANCIES  Theorem 1 indicates that no experimental comparison of the L and T theories can be made when n = 3 and all the choice  probabilities are equal to one-half. (Actually, this holds for any value of n.) Furthermore, if the experimental test consists  of predicting pac from pab and pbo then when either pab or Pb, is equal to one-half, an experimental test of the two theories  is nullified. In this case, both L and T theories predict the same value for pac . The same null test also occurs when pab and  pbc are complementary. EXAMPLES AND CRITICAL COMMENT  The analysis carried out above does not do full justice to the problem of comparing L and T theories. Among other things we  have neglected to consider possible differences in the reliability of the estimates of (pa1)L and (p,,,),, that is, in the  variance of the corresponding estimators. Still it is of interest to compare the predictions of the L and T theories based on  pairs of extreme probabilities to see if any consistent trend emerges in favor of one theory or the other. This has been done  for three sets of data in the literature. The results are reported in Table 2.  In each case the pair comparison data reported in Table 2 are only a very small part of a larger pair comparison matrix given  in each of three studies. The pairs of extreme proportions selected from the larger matrix and labelled pab and Pb, in Table 2  were those which according to Table 1 would lead to large values of I D(x, y) 1. The data from Gulliksen and Tukey (1958) are  based on the pair comparison judgments of 200 subjects as to the superiority of handwriting samples. The Guilford data in  Table 2 (Guilford, 1954, p. 157) were obtained from a lifted weight experiment involving a single subject and 200 replications  of each pair of weights, and the Thurstone data (Thurstone, 1959, p. 251) from 266 subjects judging the seriousness of  nineteen different crimes.  It may be noted in Table 2 that for the Gulliksen-Tukey and Guilford data the T predictions are considerably better than the L  predictions. The total chi square for the former predictions are two to three times larger than the latter. The Thurstone  data, however, shows only a slight bias in favor of the T theory.  The general problem of distinguishing experimentally between the two theories is not quite as simple as the foregoing  discussion might imply. To obtain a discrepancy as large as 0.11 requires the introduction of normal deviates as large as 2.8.  A good estimate of the extreme probability associated with so large a deviate will itself require a considerable batch of  data. The difficulty may arise from two sources. The first is a discrepancy between experimental needs and theoretical  preoccupations.
