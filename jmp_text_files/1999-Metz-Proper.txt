``Proper'' Binormal ROC Curves: Theory and Maximum-Likelihood Estimation 

Charles E. Metz and Xiaochuan Pan 

The University of Chicago 

The conventional binormal model, which assumes that a pair of latent normal decision-variable distributions underlies ROC data, has been used successfully for many years to fit smooth ROC curves. However, if the conventional binormal model is used for small data sets or ordinal-category data with poorly allocated category boundaries, a ``hook'' in the fitted ROC may be evident near the upper-right or lower-left corner of the unit square. To overcome this curve-fitting artifact, we developed a ``proper'' binormal model and a new algorithm for maximum-likelihood (ML) estimation of the corresponding ROC curves. Extensive simulation studies have shown the algorithm to be highly reliable. ML estimates of the proper and conventional binormal ROC curves are virtually identical when the conventional binormal ROC shows no ``hook,'' but the proper binormal curves have monotonic slope for all data sets, including those for which the conventional model produces degenerate fits. � 1999 Academic Press 

1. INTRODUCTION 

Receiver operating characteristic (ROC) analysis is recognized widely as the most meaningful approach to quantify the accuracy of diagnostic information and diagnostic decisions (Swets, 1983; International Commission on Radiation Units and Measurements, 1996). In particular, the conventional binormal model (Dorfman 6 Alf, 1969) has been applied fruitfully to evaluate many diagnostic medical procedures (Swets, 1979; Swets 6 Pickett, 1982; Metz, 1986a). Computer programs such as RSCORE (Dorfman, 1982) and ROCFIT (Metz, 1988; Metz, 1989) that are based upon the conventional binormal ROC model provide satisfactory fits to data obtained in a very broad variety of practical situations (Swets, 1986; Hanley, 1988).

The conventional binormal model employs two parameters to specify an ROC curve. In terms of the conventional binormal model's ROC curves when they are plotted as straight lines on ``normal-deviate'' axes, a represents the ``y-intercept'' and b represents the ``slope'' of a particular ROC. In terms of a latent pair of normal distributions, the parameter a can be interpreted as the difference between the conditional means, relative to the standard deviation of the actually positive distribution, whereas the parameter b can be interpreted as the ratio of the standard deviations of the actually negative and actually positive distributions (Metz, Herman, 6 Shen, 1998). When b{1, the ROC curves provided by the conventional binormal model always lie partly below the +45% diagonal ``guessing line'' of the ROC plot and always have non-monotonic slope on ``probability'' (i.e., zero to one) axes, though this non-monotonicity is not evident graphically if b is sufficiently close to one. Therefore, when the conventional binormal model is used to fit an ROC curve to a data set that includes only a small number of cases and�or is collected on a discrete ordinal scale in which the categories are few and poorly distributed (Metz, 1989), it may yield a ``hooked'' ROC curve that has obviously non-monotonic slope and a portion that lies visibly below the ``guessing line'' of the ROC plot. In extreme situations, these properties cause the fitted ROC curves to become ``degenerate,'' with ``zig-zag'' shapes (Metz, 1989). Degenerate fits can be quite common in some situations (Rockette, Obuchowski, 6 Gur, 1990; Dorfman 6 Berbaum, 1995). However, results from both real and computer-simulation studies show no degeneracy when the data sets include a large number of cases and are collected with a large number of appropriately distributed categories. Therefore, we consider hooks and degeneracy in fitted ROC curves to be artifacts of the conventional binormal model's inherent ability to produce fitted ROC curves with non-monotonic slope. In the work reported here, we propose and implement a ``proper'' binormal ROC model that always yields fitted ROC curves with monotonic slope. 

Before proceeding further, we introduce some terms that are employed in applying ROC analysis to evaluate medical diagnostic tests. An actual positive (or actual negative) is a trial (e.g., patient or specimen) in which the signal (e.g., disease) of interest is (or is not) in fact present. A true positive is a trial in which an observer decides in response to an actually positive trial that the signal of interest is present, whereas a false positive is a ``positive'' judgment by an observer in response to an actually negative trial. True positive fraction (or sensitivity) is the ratio of the number of true positives to the number of actual positives, whereas false positive fraction (or 1-specificity) is the ratio of the number of false positives to the number of actual negatives. We give mathematical definitions for these terms in Section 2. 

As discussed above, undesirable hooks and zig-zag degeneracies in the fitted ROC curve are possible because the conventional binormal model does not ensure that the decision variable which is assumed to underlie the ROC curve is a monotonic function of the likelihood that each case is actually positive. One can show that any ROC that arises from use of likelihood ratio (Egan, 1975; Van Trees, 1968) or any monotonic transformation thereof (Egan, 1975; Swets, Tanner, 6 Birdsall, 1961) as the decision variable must have a slope that changes monotonically with false positive fraction (Egan, 1975) and, thus, allows no ``hooked'' or

``degenerate'' ROC fits. Hence, in an attempt to overcome the problem of inappropriate fitted ROC curves, we build upon early work by Birdsall (1966) to develop here a ``proper'' (Egan, 1975) version of the binormal model in which decisions are assumed to be based upon use of a monotonic transformation of likelihood ratio as the decision variable. This new model is still ``binormal'' in the sense that it employs as its decision variable a monotonic transformation of the likelihood ratio that is associated with a quantity which arises from a pair of normal distributions with generally different means and variances. 

A simple visual interpretation of the relationship between conventional and proper binormal ROC curves can be seen as follows. Suppose that a conventional binormal ROC curve is divided into many small line segments. Because the slope, on ``probability'' axes, of any ROC curve at any given point is equal to the likelihood ratio of the underlying decision variable at the critical value of the decision variable which produces that ROC point (Egan, 1975), a corresponding proper binormal ROC curve can be formed by reordering the line segments, if necessary, according to the values of the slopes associated with them. Thus, we see immediately that a proper binormal ROC curve is essentially equal to that of the conventional binormal model when the conventional binormal ROC curve shows no evident ``hook'' or ``degeneracy.'' 

This paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, the theory of the proper binormal model is formulated, and several different parameterizations of this model are considered. Algorithms for reliable maximum-likelihood estimation of the parameters of the proper binormal model are considered in Section 3. Results of extensive simulation studies that validate the theory and algorithms are presented in Section 4. The conclusions that we draw from these results are discussed in Section 5. 

The theory and algorithms associated with this work are described here primarily in terms of discrete ordinal-category data, but they can be applied readily to continuously distributed data by use of the categorization schemes proposed by Metz et al. (1998).

2.3. The ``Proper'' Binormal Model 

We propose here an alternative ROC curve fitting model in an attempt to overcome the problems of hooks and degeneracy in ROC curves that are fit by the conventional binormal model. Because the conventional binormal ROC model provides appropriate fits to data in many situations, we believe that any alternative ROC model should yield ROC curves that are essentially equivalent to those of the conventional binormal model when the fitted conventional binormal ROC does not show an evident ``hook'' or ``degeneracy.'' 

Use of likelihood ratio as a decision variable always produces ROC curves with monotonic slope (Egan, 1975). Therefore, our new curve-fitting model is based upon a ``proper'' (Egan, 1975) version of the binormal model, in which each decision is assumed to be based upon the likelihood ratio (or any monotonic transformation thereof) of a variable that arises from one of two normal density functions, rather than upon the normally distributed variable itself. 3. ALGORITHM 

Here we consider the use of the proper ROC model and the parameters (da , c, v) described in Section 2.6 to fit a particular set of data collected in I discrete categories. Although the (d$e , c, v) parameterization yields somewhat simpler expressions for the arguments of the 8 functions in Eqs. (28) and (29), the expression for the area Aprop in Eq. (31) has the undesirable property of yielding identical values of area for different values of c when d $e is held fixed and either c>0 or c<0. On the other hand, the (da , c, v) parameterization eliminates this behavior. Although the arguments of the 8 functions become somewhat more complicated when they are expressed in terms of da in Eqs. (33) and (34), they remain linear functions of the variable v. Therefore, we use the (da , c, v) parameterization and Eqs. (33) and (34) to evaluate FPF and TPF for calculation of the probabilities pi and qi , which are needed for evaluation of the log-likelihood function LL. A numerical optimization scheme is then employed to find the values of the curve parameters (da , c) and the cut-point parameters (v1 , v2 , ..., vI&1) that maximize the log-likelihood function LL given by Eq. (2). 

3.1. Topographic Features of the Log-Likelihood Function 

Equation (2) indicates that, for a given set of data, the value of the function LL depends upon the values of the parameters (da , c, v1 , v2 , ..., vI&1) that span an (I+1)-dimensional parameter space. The task of maximizing this function consists of finding the particular set of parameter values in this space at which LL reaches its maximum. We have found empirically that, in general, the log-likelihood function expressed by Eq. (2) has distinct topographic features in the two parameter subspaces [da , c] and [v1 , v2 , ..., vI&1]. This difference is not surprising, because the curve parameters (da , c) are distinct from the cut-point parameters (v1 , v2 , ..., vI&1) in the sense that an ROC curve is completely determined by the former and independent of the latter. After several unsuccessful attempts to develop iterative schemes to maximize Eq. (2) directly, we found that a clear understanding of the topographic features of the log-likelihood function in parameter space, especially in the [da , c] parameter subspace, provides important insight concerning the selection of a reliable strategy for finding the maximum of the log-likelihood function. It is virtually impossible to study the topography of the log-likehood function analytically, due to its complicated form as a function of the parameters (da , c, v1 , v2 , ..., vI&1). However, the topography of the log-likelihood function can be investigated numerically. 

To study the topographic features of the LL function in the [da , c] parameter subspace, we evaluate this function at each (da , c) point on a grid by maximizing

it in the [v1 , v2 , ..., vI&1] parameter subspace. This data set was obtained by converting, according to Eq. This behavior of the log-likelihood function occurs because, as noted above, the proper binormal ROC curve is virtually unchanged at different positions on an isopleth of constant area in the [da , c] parameter subspace if da �2 |c| (see Fig. 4). Hence, if the loglikelihood function of a particular data is maximized by a combination of da and c such that da �2 |c|, the data set will be fit almost as well, in a maximumlikelihood sense, by other combinations of da and c that lie on the same isopleth in this region. This issue is discussed in more detail in Section 4.1. 

Similarly, one can study the topographic features of the log-likehood function in the [v1 , v2 , ..., vI&1] cut-point parameter subspace. In our experience, the log-likelihood function LL always has an essentially quadratic form in the neighborhood of the cut-points that maximize the LL in the subspace [v1 , v2 , ..., vI&1], with isopleths that are much more elliptical than those shown in Fig. 8. 3.2. Maximum Likelihood Estimation 

Because the log-likelihood function generally has a quadratic form in the neighborhood of the cut-point parameter values that maximize log-likelihood, we employ a quasi-Newton method (Polak, 1971; Dennis 6 More, 1977) to optimize the log-likelihood function in the subspace [v1 , v2 , ..., vI&1] for fixed values of the curve parameters (da , c). This method requires calculation of the gradient of the log-likelihood function in the subspace [v1 , v2 , ..., vI&1]. Our reparameterization from the parameter y to the parameter v in Eq. (27) causes the arguments of the 8 functions to be linear functions of v in Eqs. (33) and (34) and, thus, simplifies and numerically stabilizes the task of calculating the gradient of the log-likelihood function LL in the subspace [v1 , v2 , ..., vI&1]. 

On the other hand, the likelihood function may have a long, curved ridge in the [da , c] parameter subspace, as shown in Fig. 8. A quadratically convergent method such as the quasi-Newton method is unreliable in such situations, because these methods try to extrapolate along a quadratic surface which simply is not yet there, whereas the conjugacy of the transverse directions is spoiled by the curvature of the ridge. Therefore, we use Powell's method (Brent, 1973; Press, Flannery, Teukolsky, 6 Vetterling, 1989) for maximization of the log-likelihood function in the subspace 

19 

``PROPER'' BINORMAL ROC CURVES 

[da , c]. Powell's method requires only the evaluation of values of the function to be maximized and is particularly robust in searching for the maximum of a function with a ridge. 

Thus far, we have identified only the topographic features of the log-likelihood function in the subspaces [da , c] and [v1 , v2 , ..., vI&1] and corresponding iterative strategies that may be effective in the two parameter subspaces. In order to maximize the log-likelihood function in the full parameter space [da , c, v1 , v2 , ..., vI&1], we employ a composite strategy, as follows. Powell's method is used to find the maximum of the LL function in the subspace [da , c]. At each point (da , c) that is explored by Powell's method, the value of the LL function there is evaluated by using the quasi-Newton method to maximize it with respect to values of the cutpoint parameters [v1 , v2 , ..., vI&1]. Therefore, the maximum of the LL function in the subspace [da , c] that is obtained from Powell's method is the maximum of the log-likelihood function in the full parameter space [da , c, v1 , v2 , ..., vI&1]. 

3.3. Initial Estimates of Parameters 

In principle, the choice of the initial estimates that are provided to an iterative optimization algorithm should not affect the final results. However, in many practical situations, initial estimates play an important role, often affecting the rate of convergence and even the final result. Therefore, it is important to provide an optimization algorithm with consistent, objective, and empirically fruitful initial estimates. 

A method was proposed by Metz et al. (1998) to obtain reliable initial estimates of the cut-point parameters x1 , x2 , ..., xI&1 in the conventional binormal ROC model, given any particular estimates of the values of the curve parameters. Basically, the idea is to consider each cut-point parameter individually and to find the particular value of that parameter which maximizes the LL function when the data are dichotomized by it��a simple univariate optimization task. In the work reported here, we employ that method to calculate initial estimates of the cut-point parameter values for optimization of the log-likelihood function of the proper binormal model in the subspace [v1 , v2 , ..., vI&1], given current values of the curve parameters da and c. 

Powell's method, which searches for the maximum of the maxima of the LL function in the subspace [da , c], also requires initial estimates of the curve parameters da and c. Because FPF and TPF are expressed simply in terms of 8 functions in the conventional binormal ROC model (Eqs. (5) and (6)), rough initial estimates of the curve parameters a and b for that model can be evaluated easily by simple linear regression when the data are plotted on a normal deviate plot (Dorfman 6 Alf, 1969). However, due to the complexity of the expressions for FPF and TPF in Eqs. (33) and (34), it is not immediately evident how to obtain initial estimates of the proper binormal ROC curve parameters da and c from available data. However, for a given data set, one can readily evaluate the trapezoidal area under the data points in the ROC plot (Hanley 6 McNeil, 1982). Using the value for this area, one can identify a particular isopleth of area in the [da , c] subspace, as shown in Fig. 2. The curve parameter values that maximize the log-likelihood function for 

20

this particular data set should, in general, lie in the neighborhood of this isopleth curve. Therefore, we maximize the log-likelihood function in the subspace [v1 , v2 , ..., vI&1] for 20 sets of da and c that are equally spaced on this isopleth, and we choose the (da , c) point that yields the largest value of the log-likelihood function as the initial estimates of da and c. 

3.4. Computer Program 

We have developed a FORTRAN computer program entitled ``PROPROC'' for Unix, MS Windows, and Apple Macintosh operating systems by implementing the theory and algorithms discussed in Sections 2 and 3 for the proper binormal ROC model. PROPROC fits proper binormal ROC curves to either discrete ordinalcategory data or continuously distributed data. 

4. RESULTS 

To validate the proper binormal ROC model and to investigate the behavior and reliability of the PROPROC program, we conducted a series of simulation studies in which both discrete-categorical data and continuously distributed data were generated and then analyzed. 

4.1. Five-Category Data 

The solid curve in Fig. 9 shows a conventional binormal ROC curve with a=1.5 and b=0.8 (i.e., da=1.657 and c=&0.111) that is typical of ROCs encountered in observer-performance studies in diagnostic medical imaging. A proper binormal ROC curve with da=1.657 and c=&0.111 also is plotted as a solid curve in Fig. 9. As discussed above, because this conventional binormal ROC has no evident hook, it is virtually identical to its proper binormal counterpart. Therefore, the two solid curves in Fig. 9 are graphically indistinguishable. However, for a five-category data set (30 actually negative cases, 40 actually positive cases) that was sampled from this population ROC (indicated by diamonds on Fig. 9), the ROC fit by the conventional binormal model (dotted curve) has an obvious hook, whereas the ROC fit by the proper binormal model (dashed curve) has monotonic slope. Figure 9 clearly demonstrates that the proper ROC curve is closer to the true curve than is the conventional curve, especially in the region of high FPF values. 

A conventional binormal ROC curve discussed by Metz (1989) and studied extensively by Dorfman and Berbaum (1995) has population curve parameters a=1.5 and b=0.8 and population operating points (FPF, TPF )=(0.00025, 0.10), (0.000328, 0.25), (0.03040, 0.50), and (0.28114, 0.85). 5. DISCUSSION 

We have proposed a new ``proper'' binormal model for ROC curve fitting that does not allow ``hooked'' or ``degenerate'' curve shapes. Some might argue that the broad variety of ROC curve shapes provided by the conventional binormal model is an advantage, allowing greater flexibility in ROC curve fitting. However, we consider ``hooked'' or ``degenerate'' ROC curve fits to be artifacts, at least in humanobserver studies. In our experience with human confidence-rating data, obviously hooked or degenerate ROC curve fits are obtained with the conventional binormal model only when data sets are small, and�or when the data are collected on a discrete categorical scale with categories that are few and�or poorly distributed. In the first of these situations, the conventional binormal model produces hooked or degenerate fits to accommodate large statistical fluctuations in the data, whereas in 

29 

``PROPER'' BINORMAL ROC CURVES 

the later situation, the conventional binormal model's inappropriate behavior occurs outside the range of the data and can be considered an extrapolation artifact. Empirically, no hook or degeneracy is evident for data sets with large numbers of both negative and positive cases collected on a continuous or well-distributed categorical scale, in which case the data contain little statistical uncertainty and span the ROC graph. Because the proper binormal ROC model prevents the hooks and degeneracy that are possible with the conventional binormal ROC model, the new model yields more accurate and more precise ROC curve estimates than does the conventional binormal model in situations where fits based on the conventional binormal model are vulnerable to this behavior. However, the proper binormal model provides fits that are virtually identical to those of the conventional binormal model when no hooks are evident with the conventional approach. This theoretical prediction is confirmed by the results of our simulation studies. 

Another possible objection to the proper binormal ROC model is that it employs likelihood ratio (or any monotonic transformation thereof) as its decision variable, yielding the highest possible ROC curve for a given pair of underlying normal distributions. A human observer may or may not use likelihood ratio as his or her decision variable; we may never know. However, it is equally unclear whether such observers use the normally distributed latent decision variables assumed by the conventional binormal ROC model. From an empirical perspective, the ROC curve fitting model of choice should be that which allows appropriate curve shapes and is as insensitive as possible to weaknesses in the data sets of practical interest in real ROC studies. According to the results reported in this paper, we consider the proper binormal model to be superior to the conventional binormal model in that sense. 

We have introduced a set of parameters for the proper binormal model that greatly simplifies the model's mathematical expression. In addition, we have proposed a novel approach to the task of maximizing the log-likelihood function of the data by splitting this task into two alternating maximizations in two different parameter subspaces. Different optimizers, Powell's method and the quasi-Newton method, are employed to converge robustly upon the maxima of log-likelihood functions that may have long, narrow, and curved ridges in the [da , c] parameter subspace. We also devised a scheme that provides reliable initial estimates of the curve parameters. Based upon this approach, we developed a computer program, entitled PROPROC, to calculate maximum-likelihood estimates of proper binormal ROC curves from discrete-categorical or continuously distributed data. 

In extensive computer simulation studies, we tested PROPROC on more 10,000 computer-simulated data sets of various sizes with various combinations of population ROC curve parameters without encountering a single failure to converge. Therefore, we conclude that PROPROC is high reliable. Although our proper binormal ROC model yields biased curve parameter estimates in some situations, it reduces both the bias and the standard error of various indices of diagnostic accuracy, such as sensitivity and ROC area, that are practically meaningful in comparisons of diagnostic performance.

The magnitudes of such biases depend upon several factors, such as data-set size and the population values of da and c. In general, we found that the estimates of da and c obtained from data sets with small sizes and�or with population values of da and c in the region da �2 |c| are more strongly biased than are those from data sets with large sizes and with population values of da and c in the region da -2 |c|, probably because the likelihood function in the former situation is likely to have a long curved ridge in parameter space. We have developed versions of the PROPROC program for Unix, MS Windows, and Apple Macintosh operating systems. The exact computation time required for fitting a proper binormal ROC curve depends upon various factors, including the height of the ROC curve, the scale used to collect the data, and the particular outcome of the data set. For a typical ROC curve that is likely to be encountered in practical applications (e.g., ROC area=0.85) and five-category data, execution of the current version of PROPROC requires about 4 s on an SGI Indy-2 workstation, and about 6 s on a 133 MHz Pentium PC or a 120 MHz Power Macintosh 8500.
