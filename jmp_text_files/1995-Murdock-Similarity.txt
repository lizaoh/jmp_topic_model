In TODAM, single items are represented as random vectors after Anderson (1973). That is, they are vectors whose elements are random variables drawn from a Gaussian “feature” distribution with mean zero and variance P/N, where P is the power of the vector (normally P= 1) and N is the number of features. To model similarity we use correlated random vectors where corresponding elements in any two item vectors are random samples froma bivariate normal distribution with the same mean (Le., zero), variance (ie., P/N), and theoretical correlation coefficient p. Similarity is measured by the dot product, and this correlation coefficient determines the similarity. If p = 1.0 the two item vectors are identical, if p = 0.0 the two item vectors are uncorrelated or independent, and intermediate levels are graded by p. Thus, similarlity varies continuously from 0.0 to 1.0 as a function of p and is distributed across all the elements of the item vectors rather than localized in some 

Similarity has long played a central role in cognitive psychology. In the verbal learning and memory area it has been a cornerstone of interference theory at least since McGeoch (1932) and played a major role in retroactiveand proactive interference (Postman, 1961). Generalization gradients show the effect of similarity on some measure of response strength; see e.g., the theoretical analyses of Gibson (1940), Osgood (1949), and Shepard (1987). Similarity is a basic concept in semantic memory models (see, €g., Collins & Loftus, 1975), prototypes (see, eg., Rosch, 1977) and categories (Nosofsky, 1992; Ashby & Perin, 1988}; for a recent connectionist model using “receptive fields” see Kruschke (1992). It is also important in judgment and decision making (see, e.g., Mellers & Biagini, 1994).

In testing predictions of TODAM (and other global matching models too) it is important to consider the role of similarity. Global matching models include SAM (Gillund & Shiffrin, 1984; MINERVA2 (Hintzman, 1988), CHARM (Metcalfe-Eich, 1982), and the matrix model (Humphreys, Bain, & Pike, 1989; Pike, 1984). The models are “global” in that, however the information is stored, it all enters into the retrieval process. Similarity is important because even the unrelated words used in memory experiments are not completely dissimilar (i¢., they do not have a p of zero), and even small values of p will increase the means and variances used in computing d’ measures. Since in TODAM both recall and recognition depend on these means and variances (see, e.g, Murdock, 1992), any TODAM predictions that do not include similarity are approximations at best. 

Similarity in TODAM has been considered in a few special cases (Lewandowsky & Murdock, 1989; Murdock, 1989), but no general principles are available. In this paper I first present such principles for the item-recognition case, then apply them to two experimental paradigms of considerable current interest, the list-strength effect and the list-length effect of Ratcliff, Clark, & Shiffrin (1990).

LIST-STRENGTH EFFECT 

The list-strength effect (LSE) refers to the fact that the strength of an item as measured by d’ is unaffected by the strength of other items in the list. Generally strength is varied by number of presentations (R) so we will consider only this case. The usual design for studying the LSE involves three different types of lists: pure weak (PW), pure strong (PS), and mixed (M). In the pure lists (PW, PS) all items are of one type (weak or strong), whereas in the mixed list both weak and strong items are used, generally the same number of each. The total number of items in each of the three types of lists (PW, PS, M) is also generally the same, so if there are Z items in the PW and L items in the PS then there are L/2 weak and L/2 strong items in the mixed list. 

In studying the LSE a blocked design is usually used, so in each block of three lists there is one list of each type (PW, PS, M), randomly ordered. The critical measure is the ratio of the rallos (ROR), where if d’(-) denotes d’ for the relevant condition then
