Bayesian statistics has become increasingly popular among mathematical psychologists ( Bayarri et al., 2016; Ly et al., 2016a; Rouder et al., 2009; Wagenmakers et al., 2010; Wang & Liu, 2016 ). The benefits of the Bayesian approach are the accordance with the likelihood principle ( Berger & Wolpert, 1988; Birnbaum, 1962 ), conceptual simplicity ( Edwards et al., 1963 ), ease of interpretation and the possibility to obtain inference even for highly complex models due to the advent of modern computational methods ( Kelter, 2020a, 2020b; Kruschke & Liddell, 2018 ; Makowski et al., 2019 ). A particular strength of the Bayesian approach is given by the freedom to use optional stopping, independence of the researcher’s intentions ( Kruschke & Liddell, 2018 ), and the interpretation of censored data, all of which are consequences of the accordance of the Bayesian paradigm with the likelihood principle ( Berger & Wolpert, 1988; Birnbaum, 1962 ). Next to hypothesis testing and parameter estimation, Bayesian model comparison is one of the major applications of the Bayesian approach, and the comparison of statistical models is an essential part of psychological research ( Edwards et al., 1963; Gronau et al., 2019; Kruschke, 2015; Ly et al., 2016a, 2016b; Wagenmakers et al., 2010 ). However, the range of techniques proposed to compare competing statistical models in the Bayesian literature is vast, which makes it difficult to select an appropriate method for model comparison and selection based on (i) the assumptions made about the structure of the model space  
                         M 
                       and (ii) the computational complexity of the procedure. The latter aspect becomes important when the dimensionality of the model and the sample size become large, a situation which is becoming the norm rather than the exception in a variety of psychological research ( Harlow & Oswald, 2016; Kang et al., 2019 ).

Established methods to compare competing Bayesian models in psychological research include the Bayes factor ( Held & Ott, 2018; Jeffreys, 1961; Kass & Raftery, 1995; Robert et al., 2008 ), posterior model probabilities, maximum a posteriori (MAP) model selection ( Held & Sabanés Bové, 2014; Marin & Robert, 2014; Piironen & Vehtari, 2017 ) and Bayesian model averaging (BMA) ( Barbieri & Berger, 2004; Claeskens & Hjort, 2008; Hoeting et al., 1999; Raftery et al., 2003 ).

The second category of approaches consists of cross-validation methods. These include  
                         K 
                      -fold cross-validation ( Geisser & Eddy, 1979 ), which is a biased estimate of precise leave-one-out cross-validation (LOO-CV) ( Burman, 1989 ), which itself often is referred to as the gold standard for Bayesian model comparison ( Piironen & Vehtari, 2017; Vehtari & Ojanen, 2012 ). As precise LOO-CV is computationally very costly, analytical approximations (for some specific models) and numerical approximations (in more generality) have been developed ( Vehtari et al., 2017, 2014, 2015 ). Additionally, approximations to precise LOO-CV have been developed, which reduce the computational effort both when the sample size is large and the model is potentially high-dimensional ( Magnusson et al., 2019, 2020 ).

Another branch of Bayesian model selection approaches is given by various information criteria like the Akaike information criterion (AIC) ( Akaike, 1974 ), the Bayesian information criterion (BIC) ( Schwarz, 1978 ), the deviance information criterion (DIC) ( Spiegelhalter et al., 2002 ) and the widely applicable information criterion (WAIC) ( Watanabe, 2009, 2010 ). For an overview and relationships between these see  Konishi and Kitagawa (2008) ,  Stone (1977) ,  McElreath (2020)  and  Vrieze (2012) , where  Vrieze (2012)  places emphasis in particular on the comparison of the AIC and BIC for model selection in psychological research.

Approaches to Bayesian model comparison which are efficient in settings with a large number of predictors and a large number of candidate models include the reference predictive method which minimises the Kullback–Leibler divergence between a prespecified reference model and a candidate model ( Martini & Spezzaferri, 1984; Piironen & Vehtari, 2017 ), and the projection predictive inference approach of  Goutis and Robert (1998) , which was extended by  Dupuis and Robert (2003)  and  Piironen et al. (2018) .

An often overlooked fact is that the applicability of existing model comparison and selection techniques strongly depends on the assumptions made about the model space  
                         M 
                      , which is the sampling space of competing models without any further restrictions, like the requirement of models being nested or belonging to the same family ( Bernardo & Smith, 1994; Piironen & Vehtari, 2017; Vehtari & Ojanen, 2012 ). This complicates the selection of an appropriate and computationally efficient model comparison method in practice. Additionally, some of the above-mentioned techniques are approximations or biased estimators of each other, which complicates the situation even further. For example, it is well-known that  
                         K 
                      -fold cross-validation is a biased but computationally more efficient estimator of LOO-CV ( Burman, 1989 ). However, if computational efficiency is not needed because the model’s complexity and the sample size are only moderate, introducing bias to reduce the variance may be superfluous. Also, LOO-CV itself is asymptotically equal to the AIC for ordinary regression models ( Stone, 1977 ), but in the massive-data setting with a huge sample size, it may be much more efficient to compute AIC instead of precise LOO-CV. Additionally, AIC and BIC can be derived in the same Bayesian framework just by using different prior probabilities ( Burnham & Anderson, 2004 ), but the optimality of AIC or BIC depends again (for example, in linear regression) on specific assumptions on the model space  
                         M 
                       as shown by  Yang (2005)  (the “true“ model must be in the candidate set). These examples show that the various methods are often intertwined and the selection of an appropriate technique is often unclear in practice. Model comparison is, therefore, an important technique in psychological research – see also  Vrieze (2012) ,  Wasserman (2000)  and  Zucchini (2000)  — and the appropriateness of a specific model selection method depends strongly on the assumptions made about the model space  
                         M 
                       and the method’s computational efficiency.

The plan of the paper is as follows: Section  2  introduces Bayesian model comparison from a decision-theoretic perspective, which strives to maximise an expected utility. It is shown that Bayesian model comparison techniques can be unified under this paradigm by selecting the logarithmic score as the utility function, thereby judging the out-of-sample predictive ability of a statistical model under consideration. Section  3  then discusses different perspectives on the model space  
                         M 
                      , and the possible model selection methods which are available in each model view. It is argued that the  
                         M 
                      -open view is the least restrictive and most realistic for a variety of research. Section  4  then introduces Pareto-smoothed importance sampling leave-one-out cross-validation (PSIS-LOO-CV) as a general method for model comparison in the  
                         M 
                      -open model view, and relates the underlying theory to standard LOO-CV and importance sampling LOO-CV (IS-LOO-CV). Additionally, the theory behind LOO-CV using posterior approximations and subsampling as proposed by  Magnusson et al. (2019)  is detailed, which makes the method applicable even in large-scale models with a large number of model parameters and large sample size. Section  5  then provides a case study in which logistic regression is used as a running example to showcase how to use the approach in practice. It is shown that PSIS-LOO-CV using posterior approximations and subsampling significantly reduces the computational effort needed for Bayesian model comparison, while retaining high accuracy of the ELPD estimates. Section  6  provides a second case study which shows how to select between the psychometric power law and exponential law models which relate reaction times to practice based on a simulated dataset. This demonstrates that it is straightforward to apply the method to non-standard models. Section  7  concludes by discussing the benefits and limitations of the method.

                            M

However, there is no reason to assume in general that the encompassing model using both predictors (and the interaction effect between them) is preferable to the models including only one of both predictors in terms of predictive accuracy. One of the predictors could be pure noise without any effect on the response variable. Clearly, the reference model in such a case would not be the full encompassing model, but the model which includes only the relevant predictor(s).

Comparison of competing statistical models is an essential part of psychological research. From a Bayesian perspective, various approaches to model comparison and selection have been proposed in the literature. However, the model view strongly influences the applicability of these approaches in practice. This paper provided a tutorial on approximate leave-one-out cross-validation methods for Bayesian model comparison and selection in the  
                         M 
                      -open view based on the ELPD.

First, several perspectives on the model space  
                         M 
                       were discussed and the corresponding Bayesian model comparison methods in each view were analysed. Based on this discussion, the  
                         M 
                      -open view seems to be the least restrictive and most realistic model view for a variety of psychological research.

Traditional methods in the  
                         M 
                      -open view are various leave-one-out cross-validation (LOO-CV) techniques and a variety of information criteria. However, these methods become computationally inefficient when sample size  
                         n 
                       or the number of model parameters  
                         p 
                       becomes large. Therefore, this paper showed how to scale LOO-CV using posterior approximations and subsampling for large  
                         n 
                       and  
                         p 
                      . The Bayesian logistic regression model was used as a running example in the first case study to compare three competing models using data from the  Western Collaborative Group Study  on chronic heart disease ( Faraway, 2016; Rosenman et al., 1975 ). Precise LOO-CV and WAIC were compared with IS-LOO-CV and PSIS-LOO-CV, and both importance sampling-based approaches already improve upon LOO-CV and WAIC. However, PSIS-LOO-CV does not scale when sample size  
                         n 
                       and the number of predictors  
                         p 
                       becomes large. Using posterior approximations in combination with probability-proportional-to-size subsampling it was shown how computations can be sped up substantially. In the first case study, even small subsample sizes  
                         m 
                       of approximately 10% sufficed to yield identical conclusions as full sample inference.

The second case study demonstrated how to apply the method to domain-specific models. Therefore, the exponential and power law model were compared, both of which relate response times to practice. The second case study showed that the method allows for model comparison in the large  
                         n 
                       setting even when other approaches fail or encounter problems.

Also, it was shown how to perform marginal posterior predictive checks to safeguard against solely relying on ELPD estimates. These are substantial to prevent choosing the best model among a set of models which are all bad descriptions of the true data generating process.

Both case studies demonstrated that the reduction of computing time for comparing models depends on two quantities: First, the difference between computation time to derive a posterior distribution via precise MCMC and via an approximate method like Laplace or variational inference is crucial. This difference depends on the number of parameters  
                         p 
                       and the geometry of the posterior distribution, but there exist no precise relationships. However, it can be expected that for increasing number of parameters the spared computation time by switching to an approximation method like Laplace or VI will grow, compare  Blei et al. (2017) , as the geometry of the posterior approximation is simpler. 
                         22 
                       
                      
                         22 
                         However for large  
                               p 
                             it is questionable if a multivariate normal mode obtained via numerical optimization is indeed a global maximum. It can very well happen that the maximum is local and not global, undermining the reliability of the Laplace approximation or VI. Still, advanced numerical optimization techniques like simulated annealing can help, see  Robert and Casella (2004) . 
                       Second, the number of LOO posteriors which need to be computed is relevant. If the subsampling approach is used, and  
                         
                            n 
                            ∕ 
                            10 
                         
                       subsamples are used where  
                         n 
                       is the size of the full dataset, the reduction in computing time compared to LOO-CV is 90% under the assumption that each LOO posterior requires approximately equal computing time. If subsampling is combined with posterior approximations, and one assumes that the savings due to switching to the approximation method are 50% per LOO posterior distribution, the reduction in computing time is  
                         
                            ≈ 
                            95 
                            % 
                         
                      .

It should be noticed that the approach also has some limitations: First, posterior approximations may encounter trouble in approximating large-dimensional model posteriors. This is true in particular when predictors are correlated, an assumption which is often true in practical research. For example,  Magnusson et al. (2019)  showed that variational inference behaves badly already for  
                         
                            p 
                            = 
                            100 
                         
                       predictors if these are moderately correlated. However, the method provides convenient feedback in form of the Pareto  
                         
                            
                               k 
                            
                            
                               ˆ 
                            
                         
                       diagnostic values and marginal posterior predictive checks to indicate if the reliability of the provided ELPD estimates needs to be doubted. Second, the assumptions for the convergence of the estimator need to hold. However, these are not very restrictive (for details, see  Magnusson et al., 2019, 2020 ). A more important limitation is that the method is not suitable for comparing a large number of competing models. For such situations, approaches like the projection predictive inference are more suitable, see  Piironen et al. (2018) .

Another limitation of the method is that ultimately, it cannot safely be known how many subsamples  
                         m 
                       are required to achieve the same inferences like the ones based on full sample size  
                         n 
                       and precise MCMC sampling. Although the selection of the subsample size  
                         m 
                       introduces a “researcher degree of freedom”, the case studies showed that already 10% of subsamples can be enough in practice, offering a substantial reduction in computing time even compared to PSIS-LOO-CV.

A third limitation which applies to LOO-CV methods in general is that these are discussed controversely in the literature.  Gronau and Wagenmakers (2019a)  discuss some limitations of LOO-CV methods for Bayesian model selection, and argue that the true data-generating model is not chosen with certainty as the sample size approaches infinity. However,  Chandramouli and Shiffrin (2019) ,  Navarro (2019)  and  Vehtari et al. (2019)  provide different (and more positive) perspectives on LOO-CV and  Vehtari et al. (2019)  argue that the examples given in  Gronau and Wagenmakers (2019a)  are pathologic ones. Details can be found in the papers and the rejoinder of  Gronau and Wagenmakers (2019b) , the interested reader is referred to to form his own opinion.

Together, using subsampling and approximate posterior inference a variety of statistical models frequently used in psychological research can be compared via estimation of the ELPD. At the time of writing, PSIS-LOO-CV using Laplace approximation (or variational inference) and subsampling is available for any  rstan  model ( Stan Development Team, 2020 ) which has a factorizable likelihood ( Magnusson et al., 2020 ), making the method widely applicable for psychological research. Due to the flexibility of the probabilistic programming language Stan ( Carpenter et al., 2017; Kelter, 2020d ) models can be customised easily to researcher’s needs as demonstrated in the second case study.
