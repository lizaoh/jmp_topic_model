Strategic Play and Adaptive Learning in the Sealed-Bid Bargaining Mechanism 

Terry E. Daniel 

University of Alberta 

Darryl A. Seale 

University of Alabama, Huntsville 

and 

Amnon Rapoport 

University of Arizona 

We report the results of two experiments on bilateral bargaining under the sealed-bid double auction mechanism in environments where theory calls for decidedly strategic play. The observed individual sellers' ask functions and buyers' bid functions, each based on 50 rounds of bargaining, are shown to be in good agreement with the Bayesian�Nash piecewise linear equilibrium solution of Chatterjee 6 Samuelson (1983). Although the game is played with random matching of traders on each round, both sellers and buyers change their behavior over time. The buyers in particular learn to bid more aggressively. The information sets of the two players are shown to be a major determinant of this result and of the strikingly disparate profits earned by buyer and seller during the experiment. To address the dynamics of this result, we propose a simple adaptive learning model postulating roundto-round changes in the entire bid�ask function which are proportional to the actual gain, if a deal was struck, or the gain that could have been but was not realized, if no agreement was reached on the previous round. Based on results from the experimental psychology literature, this model captures the major features of the mean bid�ask functions and accounts for most of the trial-to-trial variability in the buyers', but not the sellers', decisions. � 1998 Academic Press

INTRODUCTION 

It is well-known in both theory and practice that in interactive decision making situations with asymmetrical information, strategic misrepresentation of private information is both advantageous and commonly practiced. Beginning with Arrow (1951), there is a vast literature on the futility of designing decision mechanisms which induce participants to truthfully reveal private information and at the same time lead to efficient and otherwise desirable outcomes (see, e.g., Hurwicz, 1974; Gibbard, 1973; Myerson 6 Satterthwaite, 1983: Satterthwaite, 1975). In practice, one only needs to look over the shoulder of someone playing poker or at a trader in a bazaar to observe strategic misrepresentation in the flesh. While such behavior is predicted by non-cooperative game theoretic models (see, e.g., Linhart, Radner, 6 Satterthwaite, 1992), it is clear that most individuals do not utilize the hyperrational reasoning and cognitively demanding computations necessary to find the equilibrium strategies suggested by these models. None-the-less, there is often a surprising correspondence between the strategies actually played in competitive environments and those prescribed by the equilibrium solutions (e.g., Radner 6 Schotter, 1989; Rapoport, Seale, Erev, 6 Sundali, in press; Sundali, Rapoport, 6 Seale, 1995). Equally clear, whether observing behavior in the laboratory or studying it in the field, is the fact that participants often change their behavior over time, and that their strategies evolve as a function of their experience in a particular competitive environment. In this paper, we report on strategic play in bilateral bargaining experiments, formulate a simple model of adaptive learning that captures many of the important elements of the experimental results, and then test it on the individual level. In doing so, we draw on previous work on mechanism design, the psychology of learning, experimental economics (e.g., Kagel 6 Roth, 1995), and the recently introduced reinforcement-based adaptive learning model of Roth 6 Erev (1995). 

The bargaining situation that we examine is that of two persons, a seller and a buyer, negotiating over the price of an indivisible good that may be exchanged between the two. One mechanism that has been proposed for this setting, apart from the commonly used, face-to-face, open-ended bargaining familiar to us all, is the sealed-bid double auction. Each trader submits an offer and, if the buyer's bid is at least as large as the seller's ask, then the item is sold at a price between the two offers. If the offers do not overlap, then no trade takes place and the negotiations are broken off. This mechanism was first modeled by Chatterjee 6 Samuelson (1983). Using the Bayesian�Nash model of noncooperative games with incomplete information proposed by Harsanyi (1967, 1968), they derived equilibrium strategies for the buyer and seller, each based on that trader's private reservation value for the item being traded and on his or her uncertainty about the other trader's reservation value. These strategies specify an exaggeration of one's true reservation value when making the sealed ask or bid, just as the same theory applied to a model of a game of poker specifies that a player should bluff regarding the contents of his or her hand. They are not ex post efficient. 

Thus, in bilateral bargaining both traders know that while their 'optimal' independent behavior is to play strategically, they could both be better off by

bidding truthfully. They also know that each bid they place involves a trade-off between increasing the odds of a successful trade (accomplished by placing a bid closer to their reservation value) and increasing their share of the joint gain should a trade occur (enhanced by placing a more aggressive bid). In a laboratory setting, we will investigate how individual traders resolve this trade-off and compare their responses to that of the theory. In particular, we will consider the factors that appear to determine whether a subject places bids that are close to, or exaggerated from, his reservation value. The information sets of the traders is one such factor. We will vary the amount of information that each player has about the other's reservation value and measure the impact this has on the bidding behavior of both. Then, an adaptive learning model will be used to predict how this behavior evolves from trade to trade. The predictions of the model are surprisingly good and the observed laboratory behavior quite striking. 

The paper is organized as follows. We briefly review the theory of the sealed-bid double auction mechanism. We then describe two experiments designed to examine the strategic behavior of financially motivated subjects using this mechanism. The results of the experiments are discussed, and conclusions are drawn with respect to the observed behavior and the factors contributing to it. A reinforcement-based model of adaptive learning is then introduced. Simulations are conducted using this model to determine whether it can capture the range of bidding behavior observed in the experiments. Finally, the adaptive learning model is tested at the individual level. We conclude with a brief discussion of strategic play and learning in the sealed-bid mechanism and in other competitive environments with information asymmetries. 

The Sealed-Bid Double Auction Mechanism 

Consider a bilateral bargaining situation, where a seller has a single object that he may sell to the buyer if an acceptable price, p, is agreed upon. Assume that B denotes the buyer's reservation value of the item and A denotes the seller's reservation value. If trade occurs with no delay, then the gains from trade for the seller and buyer are p&A and B&p, respectively. If negotiations fail, then neither trader incurs any loss and the gain for each is zero. The case of two-sided incomplete information��the one examined in the present study��is modeled as follows. Each trader's reservation value is a random variable whose value is contained in some interval. The reservation values A and B are randomly drawn from the distributions that are assumed to be common knowledge to the traders. 

Under the sealed-bid mechanism, simultaneously the seller submits an ask a and the buyer submits a bid b. Trade occurs with no delay at the price p=(b+a)�2, if b>a. (A generalization allows the price p to be set at some other value between a and b.) If b<a, then negotiations are broken off. 

The best-known Bayesian�Nash equilibrium solution for the sealed-bid mechanism with two-sided incomplete information was constructed by Chatterjee 6 Samuelson (1983). Leininger et al. (1989) have shown that there are a multitude of other Bayesian�Nash equilibria for this mechanism. However, it has also been proven by Myerson 6 Satterthwaite (1983) that the linear equilibrium solution

Previous Experimental Tests 

This example has formed the basis for a set of experimental tests of the sealed-bid mechanism performed by Radner 6 Schotter (1989). Presenting each pair with several reservation values drawn independently from the corresponding prior distributions, they found substantial qualitative agreement between observed behavior and the predictions of the LES. Individual observed bidding functions were highly linear and in close correspondence (particularly for sellers) with the piecewise-linear LES functions. However, as can be seen in Fig. 1A, it is rather difficult to detect whether a subject is playing an LES strategy or another linear strategy such a truth-telling because of the similarities between the two functions in this case. In a subsequent experiment including 40 trials (their previous experiments consisted of only 15 trials) with skewed, non-uniform prior distributions of value, and with fixed trading partners, they started observing different behavior&step functions that gave the appearance of attempts at coordinating behavior. 

In a subsequent paper, Linhart, Radner, and Schotter (1990) sought to isolate the factor(s) driving this bargaining behavior. Their tentative conclusion was that at least 30 trials and highly skewed priors were necessary to generate substantial deviations from linear behavior. They categorized these deviations as ``bent'' or ``broken,'' but did not explicitly address the issue of the similarity of the observed behavior to the behavior suggested by the LES. 

In a more recent paper, Rapoport and Fuller (1995) speculated that these deviations from linearity are more dependent on the degree of overlap of the supports of the two prior distributions. They succeeded in eliciting such behavior in substantially less than 30 trials, with partially overlapping uniform priors.

TWO NEW EXPERIMENTS 

In the experiments reported below, we construct two bargaining environments where the theory calls for very distinctive strategic play. In Experiment 1, the seller's value distribution remains uniform over the range from 0 to 100, but the buyer's value distribution is given a much wider support. It is uniformly distributed over 0 to 200. This is the case used in Experiment 2 of Rapoport and Fuller, who succeeded in generating ``bent'' and ``broken'' bid functions in this case without either the 30 or more trials or skewed priors deemed necessary by Linhart et al. This figure shows that the LES model prescribes a substantial departure from linearity for the buyer with the function becoming ``broken'' at a reservation value of 50 with linearly increasing bids (slope=0.67), and then again at 150 with a constant bid of 116.7 prescribed for all reservation values above 150. The seller's departure from truth-telling is substantial; as his reservation price changes from 0 to 100, his asks should increase linearly from 50 to 116.7. 

In general, the results of Rapoport and Fuller suggest that the observed departures from linearity (``bent'' or ``broken'' bid functions) are supportive of the LES model and occur in situations, such as those with partially overlapping uniform prior distributions, predicted by the model. They also observe a tendency of a subset of subjects to ignore strategic play and bid either their true reservation values or mild exaggerations of them. As a base case, we will replicate this experiment with three major changes. We will extend the experiment to include 50 trials, implement the bargaining mechanism on a network of computers rather than conduct the experiments manually, and provide each subject a complete history of his or her bids from the beginning of the task. Rapoport and Fuller's design of randomly matching partners on each trial so as to minimize reputation effects over the 50 trials will be retained.

Experiment 2 is a key element of the present study. In it, we follow up on the tentative conclusion reached by Rapoport and Fuller, namely, that the supports of the value distributions of the buyers and the sellers are a critical factor in determining their bidding behavior. In Experiment 2, the buyer's reservation value distribution is again uniformly distributed between 0 and 200, but the seller's uniform distribution is now constrained to the narrow range of 0 to 20. As a result, this situation approaches one-sided uncertainty, often observed in practice, with the buyer having little uncertainty about the reservation values of the seller, and the seller having considerably higher uncertainty about the buyer's reservation value. The two LES functions for the buyer and seller are portrayed in Fig. 1C. As shown in the figure, we expect decidedly strategic play on the part of both buyers and sellers. The seller should never bid below 50. If the buyer adheres to the prescription of the LES, she should bid a constant value of 63.3 for all reservation values in the range from 70 to 200. In this case, it should be easy to separate strategic play from truth-telling behavior. Apart from the obvious non-linear nature of the buyer's bid function, this case introduces a new factor into the testing of the sealed-bid mechanism. It should be almost completely transparent to the buyer when the seller is playing strategically (for example, any observed ask price above 20, which LES calls for on all responses from the seller, is clearly non-truthful bidding). Similarly, over time, the lack of bids above 63.3 on the part of the buyers should be more clearly observable by sellers. As a result, in repeated play, even with randomly matched opponents who change from trial to trial, we expect more adaptive learning over time. In particular, we wish to ascertain whether bidding strategies evolve towards cooperative joint maximizing outcomes or towards more competitive solutions. We will attempt to model this evolution using our adaptive learning model. 

Method 

Subject 

Forty undergraduate and graduate students from the University of Arizona participated in this study. They were recruited through advertisements placed in the campus newspaper. The advertisements promised monetary reward contingent on performance in an experiment on group decision making. Both male and female students responded in nearly equal proportions. Twenty subjects participated in 

140 

DANIEL, SEALE, AND RAPOPORT 

each of the two experiments performed on successive days. Excluding show-up fee, the mean payoff per subject was approximately 815.00. 

Procedure 

Each experiment lasted approximately an hour and a half with the first half hour consisting primarily of instructions and orientation. The laboratory contained twenty networked micro-computers separated by a wide aisle into two groups of ten. At the front of the room was a workstation containing a monitor computer. All the terminals were well separated rendering communication between the subjects and views of other's monitors nearly impossible. At the door, subjects drew a token which determined their role in the experiment (buyer or seller) and their terminal number. Player roles remained fixed during the experiment. Buyers sat on one side of the room and sellers on the other. 

The experiment began with instruction on the use of the computers and with a guided tour through the instructions for the sealed-bid double auction mechanism. The subjects received the instructions both on paper and on their monitors. Briefly, on each of the 50 rounds of bargaining, each of the 10 buyers received on her screen, from the central computer, a reservation value that was chosen randomly from the interval of 0 to 200. No one knew anyone else's value. Similarly, each seller received on his screen a reservation value randomly selected from the integers 0 to 100 in Experiment 1 and between 0 and 20 in Experiment 2. To allow for between-subjects comparisons, each subject within a role received the same 50 reservation values but in a different random order. On each trial, all the subjects were shown a graphical depiction of the two uniform distributions of the reservation values of the buyers and sellers. Players were then prompted to input their asks�bids for the round. The computer required them to confirm their response and warned them if their ask�bid might lead to a loss (i.e., if sellers asked less than their reservation value or buyers more than their reservation value). Prior to entering their ask�bid, the subjects could review their own previous results in the experiment by calling up a separate screen. After all 20 subjects responded, the central computer randomly matched each buyer with a seller, determined whether a deal was struck, and calculated the payoffs for each pair member (B&p and p&A for the buyer and seller, respectively). Each subject then received a report listing his or her decision, that of the anonymous opponent, and if a deal was struck, the calculation of the price p and the profit for the round. This concluded the round. There was no time limit on any of the deliberations. 

After all 50 trials were completed, a brief one-page questionnaire (see Appendix) was administered, and one buyer and one seller were randomly selected to play one randomly chosen bargain with higher stakes based on their responses to the questionnaire. Subjects were then paid in cash, thanked, and dismissed.

At the end of the experiment, after completing 50 rounds of bargaining, both buyers and sellers were clearly aware that their trading partners were not bidding truthfully. In responding to Question 2 (see Appendix), 9 of the 10 buyers in each of the two experiments and all 10 sellers in each of the two experiments reported their belief that their partners were shading their reservation prices. Of the 10 sellers, 4 in Experiment 1 and 5 in Experiment 2 reported that the buyers were asking prices ``much below'' their reservation prices, whereas only a single buyer in each of the experiments believed that the sellers were asking prices ``much above'' their reservation prices. Shading of reservation prices by the sellers in our bilateral bargaining game seems to be considered more acceptable than shading by the buyers. 

Eight of the 10 buyers and 7 of the 10 sellers in each of the two experiments indicated a change in their decisions during the experiment (Question 3), in agreement with our analysis of the decision data. In Experiment 2, 7 of the 10 buyers and 6 of the 10 sellers expressed their belief that the buyer had the advantage. Question 1 was administered in order to implement the strategy method (Selten, Mitzkewitz, 6 Uhlich, 1997) in which traders make simultaneous offers for several different reservation prices. The advantage of this procedure is that it allows studying the trader's strategy rather than a single choice. A REINFORMCEMENT-BASED ADAPTIVE LEARNING MODEL 

To study the dynamics of the observed bidding behavior, we first describe a simple adaptive learning model that does a surprisingly good job of replicating the behavior of our subjects in both of the experiments. In constructing the model, we have adopted several of the general principles outlining the learning model of Roth 6 Erev (1995). Like them, we have searched for a model that is parsimonious and consistent with learning as observed in the vast experimental psychology literature. Specifically, the model outlined below embodies the ``Law of Effect'' (Thorndike, 1898): Decisions leading to good outcomes are more likely to be taken in the future whereas those leading to poor outcomes are suppressed. It also embodies the ``Power Law of Practice'' (Blackburn, 1936): The impact of the consequences of such decisions is stronger during early choices than it is later on. Our model also incorporates two additional principles cited by Roth 6 Erev, namely, that reference points play a role in determining if outcomes are perceived as negative or positive, and that negative reinforcements have a larger impact on decisions than do positive ones. Because the strategy sets in our experiments are infinite, as opposed to the finite and small sets in the studies of Roth 6 Erev, our implementation of these characteristics of adaptive learning behavior differs markedly from theirs.

The next step is to test the behavior of this model at the individual level. 

Tests of the Adaptive Learning Model at the Individual Level 

The adaptive learning model was tested next on the data of our two experiments. For each of the ten buyers and ten sellers in each of the two experiments, the parameters of the learning model were selected so as to best replicate the behavior of the chosen subject. For each subject in turn, we simulated the 50&round experiment by matching that subject, represented by the adaptive learning model with the fitted parameters, against the actual bids�offers made by the real opponent in the experiment. A quasi-Newton search method was used to find the set of parameter values which minimized the sum of squared errors between actual bids in the experiment and those given by the model in the first 30 trials of the simulation. As is generally the case with such non-linear methods (Hamilton, 1992), it remains possible that other values of the parameters give as good or better a fit. The graph of the error function has several local optima relative to the parameters of both the buyer and seller models; although we conducted the search from several different starting points, it is conceivable that for some subjects another set of parameters might be equally representative.

DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSIONS 

The major purpose of the present study has been to assess and then account for the behavioral regularities that are elicited under the sealed-bid mechanism in bilateral bargaining games in which decidedly strategic play on both parties is called for. Like all other previous experimental studies, the present study has been driven by the theoretical results of Chatterjee 6 Samuelson (1983). We departed from the previous experimental studies by shifting the focus from assessing the descriptive power of the LES constructed by Chatterjee 6 Samuelson (Radner 6 Schotter, 1989; Linhart et al., 1990), or competitively testing the LES against a model postulating truth-telling (Rapoport 6 Fuller, 1995), to an explanation of the observed behavior in terms of adaptation. This change in focus, which is typical of recent attempts to construct behavioral theories of interactive behavior, is motivated by the observation that in games repeated over time, behavior is likely to change as players acquire more information about the structure of the game and, more importantly, about the behavior of the other players. Moreover, players may change their behavior not only in response to previous experience but also in an attempt to establish reputation and thereby affect the behavior of the other players in subsequent interactions. 

The results of the two experiments reported above leave little doubt that most participants in the sealed-bid bargaining game behave strategically. Earlier experiments using the same mechanism suggested this result, but were in varying degrees inconclusive because the predictions of the LES and truth-telling models were similar over large ranges of the reservation values or because they used fixed pairs, thereby allowing strong dependencies between successive iterations of the game. In Experiment 1, there was a partial overlap of the prior distributions of the buyer and the seller leading to LES functions with a significant flat portion for the 

164

buyer. In that experiment, all 10 buyers and all but 3 of sellers deviated considerably from truth-telling behavior. Many of them, particularly among the buyers, bid in close approximation to the LES, particularly after gaining some experience with the structure of the game and behavior of the other players (Fig. 8). In Experiment 2, the buyers were presented with an environment in which there were significant advantages to strategic play over three-quarters of the range of the reservation values. All the 10 buyers took advantage of this opportunity. In the final 25 rounds of bargaining, 96 percent of the time that a reservation value was between 70 and 200, the buyers bid below 63.3 (the ``plateau'' value). 

Although both buyers and sellers behaved strategically, a major finding of both experiments is that, relative to the potential payoffs achievable if both parties had adhered to the LES, the seller's performance was distinctly worse than the buyer's. We account for this finding in terms of the disparity in information between the buyer and seller coupled with a certain reluctance of most people to openly bid large departures from true reservation values. The adaptive learning model then provides an explanation of how this effect is amplified from trial to trial. 

In Experiment 2, under the LES a seller's minimum ask price for any reservation value is 50, but this is 2.5 times larger than any possible reservation value that he could have. But, if a buyer bids 63.3, her largest bid under LES, the seller does not immediately recognize this as a massive exaggeration of the buyer's reservation price (though it may well be only one-third of her true value). In other words, traders in bargaining situations are prone to exaggerate strategically but may be somewhat reluctant to do so in an obvious manner. This tendency, if true, plays against someone whose value distribution is fairly well known by the person on the other side of the table. Often there remains an aggressive strategic response for that party (the seller in Experiment 2), but it requires open and substantial misrepresentation of one's private information. There appears to be a strong force in the North American society (Kahneman, Knetsch, 6 Thaler, 1986), which may not be shared by people in other societies, against openly strategic actions that maximize one's share of the gains from trade. Most people seem to recognize the private advantages of strategic misrepresentation but are more effective in carrying it out behind a heavy veil of asymmetrical information. 

In the single-stage bilateral bargaining game played under the sealed-bid mechanism, each trader faces a basic strategy trade-off. ``By making a more aggressive price offer, a player earns a greater profit in the event of agreement but, at the same time, increases the risk of disagreement'' (Chatterjee 6 Samuelson, 1983, p. 837). If the bargaining game is repeated in time, this trade-off keeps changing until it stabilizes. In this paper we have proposed a simple adaptive learning model, which postulates that these changes in the trade-off point are proportional to the trader's gain, if agreement is reached on the previous trial, or to the trader's ``regret'' for failing to reach agreement on the previous trial because of his�her greediness. Our results show that subjects are more sensitive to negative outcomes (missed trades) than to positive ones. This provides a plausible explanation as to why the sellers (especially in Experiment 2) bid more conservatively from trial-totrial. With the buyers bidding aggressively, due to the assumptions that both parties appear to make initially about the likelihood of the sellers confining their asks to

the supports of the distribution of their reservation values, sellers face increasing odds that they will make no trade if they bid near their LES values. These tendencies snowball from trial-to-trial even though, as previously documented, most of our subjects are closer to being strategic bidders rather than truth-tellers. 

Tests of the model on the individual level suggest that the adaptive behavior of the buyers is simpler than might have been expected. Comparing predicted and observed bids trial by trial, the model accounted for (with a single exception) 740�960 of the variability in the individual bids. Although we regard these results as impressive, the appropriate way to assess the performance of the model��as with any learning model��is not through hypothesis testing but through a competitive test against alternative models (Bush, 1963).
