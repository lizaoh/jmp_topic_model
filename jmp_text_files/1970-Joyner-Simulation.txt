JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 7, 478-514 (1970)  Computer Simulation of Individual Concept Learning in the Three-Person Common Target Game'  ROBERT C. JOYNER  York University, Toronto, Ontario  Following five laboratory studies a computer model was constructed that simulated the behavior of the individual player in the  three-person common target game. The concept selection model, COMTARG, contained three model-relevant individual difference  parameters as well as general deterministic mechanisms. Computer subjects were constructed according to a specified method of  sampling the values of the three parameters. Program runs for the computer subjects were made previous to observing humans  playing the game under two previously unobserved treatment conditions in a sixth experiment. A number of tests demonstrate  that COMTARG compares favourably with the human grouped data and reveal some of COMTARG's limitations.  This research represents an explicit attempt to extend Simon and Newell's general theory of individual problem solving to  minimum social situations; that is, to situations where an essential component of the individual's (experimental) environment  is the behavior of other persons with whom he is trying to coordinate his behavior. The general theory asserts that human  thinking is to be understood as a set of elementary psychological processes, organized hierarchically and executed serially  through the search and evaluation of the symbolic contents of a changing memory structure. The research reported combines  laboratory experiments using the three-person common target game with the construction and testing of a computer model of  individual behavior in the game.  The experimental task, the common target game, was developed by Leavitt (1960). In the three-person version of the game the  experimenter presents three subjects, who cannot communicate directly with one another, with a series of target numbers. EXPERIMENTAL PROCEDURE  In the experiments being reported the necessary restrictions on communication in the game were effected through having  subjects in separate laboratory rooms play over an intercom where they could not talk to one another and could communicate  with the experimenter only when called. This permitted the utilization of an experimental procedure that is crucial to the  study of individual concept acquisition. That is, on each trial the experimenter reported back to each subject the number that  subject contributed plus two numbers for the other subjects that were in fact numbers selected by the experimenter in advance  of play. When this procedure was followed, the subject was asked on each target number (TNi) to respond not only with his  number (Rii) but also with the two numbers he expected from the other subjects (i.e., ERi2 and ER,,) to sum to TNi . This  ordered set of three digits could be compared with those uniquely required by the particular experimental condition. This  permitted the observation of the acquisition of various experimenter-prescribed concepts by individual subjects playing three  at a time. The laboratory task is unlike those used in many laboratory experiments in two ways. The input (next target number) and  feedback information is presented briefly on each trial; also, it is presented by voice alone and is not perceptually present  while the subject is processing information. The time between the presentation of target numbers-about 1 min-is long enough to  permit a lot of thinking. These dimensions of the task are considered to permit the subject's behavior to be under the control  of his own memory structure to a greater degree than in most psychological experiments. These factors led to the selection of  the common target game as a task in which to construct and test an information processing model of human behavior that  contains strong postulates about the control that cognitive processes exercise on behavior.  The game requires behavior that is different from that observed in most studies of individual concept identification. It  demands both the selection and the utilization of a concept (like CI) that produces various objects (numeric responses) that  are satisfactory instances of (or logically equivalent to) the concept, given other multivariate objects (target numbers) as  stimuli or inputs. The common target game is considered to be an example of a task requiring concept generation rather than  concept identification. A similar distinction was made by Simon and Kotovsky (1963) in their report on an information  processing model of a sequential pattern generator.  COMMON TARGET GAME 481  COMTARG-AN INFORMATION PROCESSING MODEL  General Structure  The computer model, COMTARG, was written in IPLV after the fifth pilot study. It contains over 5000IPLV statements. A  deliberate attempt was made to design COMTARG to conform to the general characteristics of hypothetico-deductive psychological  models. The presentation of COMTARG will center around a description of its psychological mechanisms.  COMTARG contains statements about the postulated human information processing and also specifies the relevant aspects of the  experimental task. The basic structure of the model is simple and isomorphic with the laboratory conditions of Experiments  4-6. On a given trial the Executive Routine generates the target number from the stored list of TNs for the experimental  condition being simulated. Then the simulated player evaluates his current strategy and produces his responses: R , ERn2,  ERn3. Next, the two dummy players respond as required by the experimental condition being simulated. The experimenter-program  stores the responses and when all three players have responded produces the feedback-TN" , Z R , Rn1 , R12, R,,3. The  program next prints out the following information about the simulated subject: Rnl , ERn2, ER,,,, the symbolic names of the  three concepts that produced these responses, the contents of the subject's memory at the moment he produced his responses and  a sequential trace of the names of the major program subroutines that describe the subject's sequence of information  processing on any trial. The next trial is then initiated by the experimenter-program. This continues until all target numbers  have been presented.  Each player is represented by a description list of attribute-value pairs (e.g., current concept for self-concept's symbolic  name). Any value may be a single name or may name a list of symbolic information-e.g., a list of concepts that already have  been evaluated by the player. Aside from the information necessary for program housekeeping, this description list always  represents the player's current memory structure and the values of certain postulated behavioral variables. All symbolic  information presumed to be processed by the simulated subject is selected from, added to, deleted from or returned to his  description list. Since COMTARG simulates the entire process of behavior in the game, the player's description list initially  represents the postulated state of his memory after he has heard the experimental instructions and just before he hears the  first target number. This description list or representation of memory structure is updated continually as the simulated  player processes symbolic information through subroutines that change values, add new attribute-value pairs, change the  length, order, or content of information on lists and the like. Since the model simulates individual differences, the required  values of three individual difference parameters are loaded on the first (simulated learner) player's description list prior  to making any computer run.  482 JOYNER  COMTARG contains a number of macroscopic routines that effect the postulated information processing. These include the various  search (e.g., "find the next different symbol on a specified memory structure") and evaluation (e.g., "was ER.-1,2 = Rn_1.2  ?") mechanisms. The presumed control of the subject's sequential utilization of these processes is represented in the  program's Executive Routine and in a major Subject metaroutine.  Conceptual Structure  CONITARG is a concept selection rather than concept induction model. It presumes that behavior in the common target game can  be understood as an example of the human acquisition and alternation of simple, game-relevant, numeric, conjunctive concepts  that are selected from some set already stored in memory.  COMTARG premises that concepts have the following characteristics:  (i) a symbolic name;  (ii) a description;  (iii) a generation program that given certain objects (e.g., target numbers) as inputs can produce instances of the concept  (as responses).  Thus, when the name of a concept is retrieved from memory the above associated structure of symbolic information and  information processing is presumed to become accessible. One further postulate about conceptual structure is central to  COMTARG.  Models of concept identification typically presume that simple concepts like red, large, and the like are retrieved  independently from some source or store. Models (e.g., Bower and Trabasso, 1963; Levine, 1966) of this sort usually make no  additional statements about the structure of memory. This is not their objective. Rather it is that of evolving abstract  statements that will map response probability on to stimulus variability under certain conditions. In contrast, COMTARG  postulates that concepts are stored in a memory structure. We further postulate that any concept is stored in memory with its  related and defining concepts-i.e., that any concept is part of a concept structure. For example, the related concepts of  "divide by two," "remainder of division by two" and "add remainder to quotient of division by two" are presumed to form a  concept substructure.  We can expect variability in conceptual structures for most concepts like truth and goodness. Simple numeric and arithmetic  concepts seem to be different. These are universal within a number system and are highly overlearned by the adult members of a  culture. Therefore, it was considered feasible in the common target game to investigate concept acquisition while postulating  certain simple, numeric, conceptual structures to be stored in memory by all subjects. Concepts are represented in memory as  description lists-ordered associations of symbolic information. Each concept has a list of the related concepts that  constitute its conceptual structure  COMMON TARGET GAME 483  as the value of one of its attributes. Its related concepts are presumed to be readily accessible once a subject has retrieved  the name of a concept from storage. The character of the concept structures M21, M31, M41 and of M22, M32, and M42 was derived from a relevant finding in  Altemeyer's (1965) research. Altemeyer gave individuals seated in a classroom a description of the three-person common target  game that was adapted from our experimental instructions. He then asked some subjects (players) to write down a description of  how they would respond to the first target number. He asked others (coaches) to write down how they would instruct three  players to respond to the first target number. The subjects' responses were the only known additional data from which to  estimate the game-relevant concepts that players may have in memory when the first target number is presented. Relevant is  Altemeyer's finding that when subjects described a TENs or TWOs concept, they presented a set of rules that would generate the  full set of responses associated in Table 2 with concepts M21, M31, M41 or with M22 and M42. In contrast, in describing THREEs  concepts many subjects simply suggested, "divide by three."  Thus, given the name of any concept and a (target) number as its inputs, an interpreter was programmed that would construct an  instance generating program that produced any appropriate numeric response shown in Table 2 as its instance. A corollary of  this is that given a numeric response to a given target number, a search of accessible concepts can be made to determine  whether any produces a response for this target number that is identical to some numeric response under consideration. For  example, either concepts M22 or M33 will produce the response 3 on target number 7. Inspection of Table 2 shows numerous  examples where the same instance is produced by different numeric concepts. This seems comparable to a large, red circle being  identifiable as an instance of numerous concepts. Multiple instances are usually needed to permit reliable concept  classification by subjects in studies of either concept generation or concept identification. The Information Processing Model  The data structures and routines that produce the subjects' responses will now be described in detail in the following  sequence:  1. a description of COMTARG's general structure;  2. a separate description of the information content of each of COMTARG's three major partitions and of the fourth section,  the microprocessing mechanisms;  3. an outline of the behavioral laws of the hypothetico-deductive model that account for the selective search and evaluation  of information within the general structure and the hierarchical organization of information processing in sequential  macroprocessing units;  4. a discussion of the individual difference parameters and the method specified for sampling their values in loading the  simulated subjects' description lists for each computer run.  The general structure of the model contains four partitions, namely, Fast Access Memory, Slow Access Memory, Microprocessing  Mechanisms and Immediate Environment.  Symbolic information is presumed to be processed only in immediate memory-the intersection of the Information Processing  Mechanisms (IPM) and Fast Access Memory (FAM). Since the mechanisms in IPM are micromechanisms like "is M31 = M21 ?" the  reference here is to very small time intervals. COMTARG assumes: that symbolic information can only be selected for processing  from FAM, that this information is returned to FAM when processed, that a copy of some of the processed information exits to  the Immediate Environment (E) as observable responses and that symbolic  486  JOYNER  information from E and from SAM is selected into FAM for potential processing. No information is transmitted from FAM to SAM;  that is, no new symbolic information is presumed stored in or acquired by slow access memory during concept acquisition.  Finally, COMTARG postulates an irretrievable loss of some bits of information during processing.  A description of each partition of the model's structure will specify the microprocessing mechanisms and the premised  information content of E, SAM, and FAM.  Information Processing Mechanisms (IPM)  The information processing mechanisms are characteristic of list processing models. They are: read, write, copy, or erase a  symbol; test for identity between two symbols; find the next symbol on a list; add, subtract, multiply, and divide numeric  symbols; test for relative magnitude or equality between numeric symbols. When COMTARG's general behavioral laws are  described, they will specify the organization of these micromechanisms into organized information processing sequences.  Environment (E)  The immediate environment contains the following information that is updated on each trial and is available to simulated and  human subjects only during the 8- to 10-sec period of experimenter feedback on each trial: TN, , Y Ri; , Ril , Rig , Ri3 ,  TNi,l. The model further specifies a two-way categorization of this symbolic information into:  1. information symbolic of specific persons-Rig , Rig , Ri3;  2. other information-TNi , Y, Ri;-that is considered to be symbolically either more apersonal or aggregatively social.  Slow Access Memory (SAM)  The model postulates that Slow Access Memory always contains an ordered list of five concepts: M22 (TWOs), M23 (THREES), M49  (FOURs), M51 (FIVEs) and one TENs concept-M21, or M31, or M41. While the concept of respond with the "quotient of division by  n" was considered to have priority over its related concepts, no such priority was thought obvious for the three TENs concepts  which were assigned with equal probability to SAM and to FAM.  SAM, therefore, is represented as an ordered list of invariant length. That is, we assume that all subjects have potential  access to the essential concept structures associated with the attributes of division by two, three, four, or five and  grouping by ten. SAM has no other informational content and no additions or deletions are made during processing. That is, we  assume that subjects do not learn any new, game-relevant, simple arithmetic concept during the experiment. This is a  simplifying assumption of the model; namely, that we are dealing with highly overlearned,  COMMON TARGET GAME 487  universal, simple numeric concepts and that no new insights into arithmetic or our number system will be induced by subjects  during the experiment. Further, the list is small-due to the postulate of concept structures-but sufficient to generate most  of the subjects' numeric responses that were observed during experimentation. Access to this list is governed by the general  behavioral laws; accessing it presumes that the subject is searching for a new concept.  Processing of SAM occurs in only one way. The top concept in SAM will always be the last one that was copied and fetched to  FAM. SAM is always searched through demoting the top concept to the end of the list and fetching the next different concept in  to FAM and also placing it on the top of a list FAMLI in FAM. This method of processing a memory list for new concepts means  that the initial ordering of concepts in SAM will have strong effects on concept learning in this model since list position  determines concept accessibility. Further, individual differences in concept ordering in SAM can effect, in part, individual  differences in concept acquisition. These issues are dicussed further in the section on individual differences as this list is  one of the three relevant parameters.  Fast Access Memory (FAM)  The most complex memory structure is FAM. The model makes simplifying assumptions about the storage of specific information in  FAM. COMTARG postulates a two-trial perfect memory for: responses, their sum, expected responses and target numbers. This  specific information is presumed to be forgotten completely after two trials and is continually updated. A "Recent Outcomes"  binary memory switch is set plus whenever the feedback responses to the subject (R;, , R,2 , Rz3) hit TN; (i.e. Y R;; = TNz)  on two or more of the last three previous trials (n - 1, n - 2, n - 3). If the Recent Outcomes switch is set plus and the four  still previous trials (n - 4, n - 5, n - 6, n - 7) were hits a Strategy switch is set plus, indicating that the subject  considered the group to have a working strategy. Once this switch has been set plus, it remains plus, postulating the fact  that the subject will continue to remember that he believes the team to have had at least one working strategy. Thus,  excepting these recollections of earlier outcomes, the model postulates a two-trial memory for specific symbolic information.  FAM contains a concept list referred to as FAMLI (Fast Access Memory List). It is initially loaded with from none to three of  the same concepts that are loaded in SAM. The number of concepts in FAMLI was varied across simulated subjects. The concepts  were selected without replacement from a sampling distribution with p values of .03, .50, .40 and .07 for zero, one, two and  three concepts, respectively. This range of concepts that were loaded initially was estimated from postexperiment discussions  with subjects. The specific concepts selected for each position in FAMLI were also generated by a sampling distribution  estimated in part from Altemeyer's (1965) data that produced the following ordered likelihood of their selection-  488  JOYNER  M23 > M21 > M22 > M51 _>_ M49. i.e., THREEs > TENs > TWOs > FIVEs FOURs. The details of this will be outlined in the section  on individual differences since this is the second individual difference memory parameter.  Any concepts loaded in FAMLI are placed in the same order at the top of SAM. The remaining concepts in SAM are selected  according to a sampling distribution that generates the same ordered probability of concept selection as in FAMLI. These  procedures will result in individual differences in the initial loading of subjects' memory structures. They will also effect  a differential probability of: (1) concept selection on first and subsequent trials; (ii) the acquisition of various  strategies; and (iii) errors prior to the last error.  Other than natural numbers the only symbolic information loaded into the subject's initial memory structure is the set of  concepts in FAMLI and SAM. Only FAMLI is made accessible to the simulated subject in making his first response, which he  produces without having any symbolic information from the other players. SAM is not made potentially accessible until after  the experimenter-feedback on the first trial. Specific information such as Riis, of course, only acquired in memory during  this first feedback.  FAMLI is updated from the top with the name of a new concept whenever one is selected for evaluation. In addition to the  updating mechanisms, three additional characteristics of the memory model can be discussed.  A simple chunking mechanism is incorporated in that, in updating, FAMLI is scanned and whenever all members of a set of  related concepts are perceived on FAMLI, all but the most recent are deleted and all are placed on a chunked list that is  searched whenever FAMLI is searched or updated. This mechanism reduces the amount of immediately accessible information the  simulated subject is presumed to hold in FAMLI. Basically, the process postulates that as concept learning progresses  sucessfully, the human carries less (relevant) information in fast access memory. During the early stages of concept  acquisition this mechanism postulates that there is usually a rapid buildup in the amount of information retained-with its  resulting increased cognitive strain-and that chunking reduces this cognitive strain.  A second characteristic has to do with forgetting. The model postulates that no concepts that have been evaluated in immediate  memory are dropped off FAMLI. The response time available during the game (about 25 sec) and the time between response and  feedback (about 20 sec) are considered to provide ample time for the fixation, rehearsal, and retrieval of any concepts that  have been evaluated.  Two specific mechanisms are introduced for demoting concepts on lists and selecting new concepts. When FAMLI is searched after  negative evaluation of a strategy the name of the top concept (typically the name of the current concept for self) and the  names of the current concepts for the others are demoted to the bottom of FAMLI. The next (different) concept is selected and  assigned as the current concept for self and as expected of each of the other players. An additional method of searching and  COMMON TARGET GAME 489  evaluating the concepts on FAMLI can be utilized by simulated subjects. Namely, they can test whether they have a concept that  will produce an instance or response equal to some other number of interest-e.g., "Have I a concept that will produce 5 on  target number 15, where 5 = Rn2 ?". Positive evaluation of this sort promotes any concept to the top of FAMLI. These processes  were considered requisite to an information processing approach to understanding the human acquisition of complex conjunctive  concepts where concepts that are once evaluated as incorrect may require subsequent re-evaluation.  COMTARG's BEHAVIORAL LAWS  Given the postulates: that all processing occurs at the intersection of IPM and FAM, that information within FAM is used  selectively for processing, and that symbolic information may be selected from E or SAM for immediate or subsequent processing  in FAM, the model further requires an explicit and detailed statement of the rules that govern the sequential flow and  processing of information. A deliberate strategy somewhat from that adopted in  of model building has been followd that differs constructing many computer models of behavior.  In constructing computer models there is a continual tug between programming sophistication and the objectives of scientific  explicitness and reproducibility. The former course dictates the construction of programs that hand over control of sequential  operations to the data structure of the program. Essentially, the simulated problem solver lays a spoor of where he has been  and, through evolving and collapsing nets, he learns to associate images and responses in patterned activity. Such programs  have relatively large data structures, may improve their performance over trials and yield detailed time paths that have been  compared in various ways with the behavior of individuals or with the average behavior of groups. Although most such models  have a cognitive flavor, they have been constructed almost independent of any behavioral regularities suggested by cognitive  or other theories. They have usually proved to be difficult to test or to alter systematically. Reitman (1965) and Hunt (1968)  recently suggested they may have to be evaluated differently than are other behavioral models.  Implicit in their evaluation is the view that these models have a different character than we find in hypothetico-deductive  (HD) models. Be that as it may, an objective here was to construct a model that contained explicit and general statements of  completely deterministic behavioral laws where as explicit a set of relationships is stated between the model's postulates,  their deductive consequences and antecedent and consequent observables as we find in most scientific models.  The behavioral laws to be outlined for COMTARG draw on organizational and social psychological theory. They were derived from  postulates about organizational search behavior (Cyert and March, 1963; March and Simon, 1958; Simon, 1956) and  490  JOYNER  from social comparison theory (Festinger, 1954). The description of COMTARG presented here will state the deductive  consequences of these postulates and provide linking definitions to antecendent and consequent observables within the model's  system. Thus, the model is considered to possess many of the characteristics of a narrow-domain HD theory of behavior in that  it has the formal structure of HD models and it operationalizes general behavioral concepts within a specified task  environment.  Generally, COMTARG's behavioral laws specify several goals and associated performance criteria that are postulated to guide  the selection and evaluation of symbolic information. The laws introduce mechanisms for switching goals and criteria and state  a simple stop-processing rule associated with any evaluation; they also specify an ordered search through the memory structure  for satisfactory concepts among concepts that have been postulated to vary systematically in their accessibility for  retrieval. The postulated processes may be thought of as a set of STOP-SHIFT TO (see Miller, Galanter, and Pribram, 1960)  rules that postulate the detailed processing of information and the conditions that govern shifting to specified, different,  information stores and to specified, different, processing mechanisms.  The statement of this general learning program that guides model performance is made in a single large routine (Subject  Processes and Responds) that calls on the macroprocesses and hierarchically controls the simulated subject's information  processing on each trial. Because of this construction, the program is susceptible to theoretical revision through changing  connections among processes within this metaroutine. Given the general postulates, then, this suggests that a computer model  can be devised that may be as subject to moderate theoretical revision as are many mathematical models. COMMON TARGET GAME 495  Individual Differences  Computer models have frequently been tested against detailed verbal statements or other responses made by individual subjects.  Yet model-deduced values of individual difference parameters have rarely been estimated for specific human subjects and  simulation runs made prior to observing these subjects' responses. There is no reason why these predictive tests cannot be  made other than the difficult practical one of obtaining such estimates, or of securing these estimates without altering  unspecifiable parameters that affect the subject's predicted behavior. It was judged that these limitations applied to the  current research where it was not feasible to obtain estimates of I or of the entire ordered contents of FAMLI and SAM for  individual subjects immediately before the first trial.  In psychological experiments differences in behavior between subjects are observed under a given treatment condition. Computer  theories also have faced certain difficulties in estimating average behavior or group data. When deterministic computer  theories have been tested against predicted variation under different treatment conditions they have produced one set of  estimated values associated with one set of "average" predictions for each experimental condition.  An understanding of the between-subject differences was of interest and COMTARG was constructed to predict grouped data. The  model attributes most of this variability to individual differences rather than to experimenter-error or conditioninduced  randomness. A modest attempt has been made to construct COMTARG to exhibit individual differences. Much of this work has  already been described. This information is summarized below, and the details of the sampling procedures used in the initial  loading of the individual difference parameters of I, FAMLI, and SAM are described.  The model basically postulates that individual differences are produced through subjects having different symbolic information  available in memory for processing or by subjects applying different processing mechanisms to the same information. Symbolic  information is presumed to be selected for processing from three sources: namely, FAM itself, SAM, and E. Thus, any  differences in the initial loading of the number (0-3), order and content of the concepts in FAMLI and in the order of loading  the five concepts in SAM can be deduced to effect differences in the selection of concepts for processing by the same  deterministic set of search and evaluation mechanisms.  In addition, the evaluation of concepts often requires the selection of some of the symbolic information from E or from copies  of this information in FAM. The model has postulated that an important determinant of this selective process is the extent to  which the subject notices and utilizes information that is specifically symbolic of the other two persons (Ri2 , Ri3 , ERi2 ,  ERi3 , and his current expected concepts for the others). An approach to this issue was described earlier. Each computer  subject was loaded with a value of the parameter I and this value was unchanged during a subject  496 JOYNER  run. A description was given earlier of the effects of different values of I that were built into the deterministic model. The  sampling distribution of I used in selecting all subjects wasp = .35, .40, and .25 for values 1, 2, and 3, respectively. This  arbitrary sampling distribution was chosen from impressions gained during debugging runs of COMTARG. Summarv  COMTARG has been described as an information processing model of behavior in the three-person common target game that has the  following characteristics:  1. It postulates a conceptual structure of simple, game-relevant, numeric concepts that are stored in a memory structure.  2. It postulates an ordered search and evaluation through successively less accessible  498  JOYNER  parts of memory structure for a set of concepts for three players that match those uniquely required under any experimental  condition.  3. The search is directed by the goals of change other versus change self and concepts are tested and evaluated by criteria  that differ as goals change. Goals change as a function of outcomes and the value of an Independence parameter. The first  concept(s) evaluated that satisfies the current criteria on any trial is accepted, search stops, and responses are produced.  The search and evaluation mechanisms are completely deterministic.  4. The model postulates a specific probabilistic distribution of individual differences among subjects on three parameters;  viz, Independence, and in the initial loading of concepts in two memory lists, FAMLI and SAM.  5. The model postulates the selection of concepts from a store of known concepts in memory and the association of specific  concepts with specific players. Several of these are:  1. testing the model's internal consistency through deducing or observing the effects of manipulating its parameter values or  parameter relationships;  2. testing the model's deductive consequences experimentally when the model's parameters are not set from observation of the  data against which it is tested;  3. testing it against other "reasonable" models.  Computer models offer almost unlimited opportunities for applying the first procedure. Since the objective is that of  providing information that leads to the improvement of the model, few such tests are reported since no other variant of the  current model was run that gave as good a fit as the one being considered.  Without computer runs of COMTARG we can deduce that:  1. simulated subjects will not learn if the mechanisms for evaluating related concepts for self and others are deleted;  2. a change in the order of search that postulates subjects seeking new concepts (in SAM) before evaluating previously held  concepts (in FAM) will effect more rapid learning;  3. substitution of a set of rational criteria of concept evaluation for the satisficing criteria will produce an additional  increment in learning;  4. nonselective extensions of memory for game-relevant specific information will also increase rate of learning;  5. fixing the I parameter at a single value for all subjects will either increase or decrease learning in a predictible way  across all conditions; that is, if I = 1 for each subject the rate of acquisition will increase under all conditions excepting  the THREEs condition; the reverse holds if I = 3;  6. postulating a random initial selection and ordering of the concepts stored in FAMLI and SAM will produce simulated subjects  that:  (i) produce responses from equiprobably selected concepts on the first trial;  COMMON TARGET GAME SOS  (ii) exhibit stationarity of errors previous to last error; and  (iii) demonstrate the same rate of strategy acquisition across different experimental conditions.  All three of these latter predictions are contrary to the observed human responses.  In summary, many of the above changes might make the model more artificially intelligent. All would introduce less humanoid  mechanisms and diminish its fit to the human data.  Other sampling distributions of the concepts loaded into FAMLI and SAM either produced less variability among simulated  subjects or made little difference in their behavior. While it is likely true that the sampling procedures can be improved and  simplified, none that were tried out effected any apparent improvement in the model.  Two systematic variations were made in the mechanisms for searching and evaluating FAMLI and SAM:  (i) demote a negatively evaluated concept one place in these lists and promote the next concept;  (ii) forget (or evaluate without replacement) any negatively evaluated concept. Neither of these rules effected a better  simulation. The reasons are apparent. The first process introduces "wheel spinning" or alternation between negatively  evaluated concepts. The second disposes of what may be the required concept(s) after one negative evaluation.  The other two methods of consequence testing-empirical tests of the model's deductive consequences and comparison with other  models-have been used and will be illustrated.  Two procedures responsible for much of the simulated subjects' behavior and the model's fit were:  l. concepts were loaded initially in ordered lists according to estimated probability distributions;  2. each concept was loaded in memory in a structure with its related concepts and these concepts were more accessible than any  other concepts in memory.  From Statement l (above) we can deduce that TENs concepts will be learned more rapidly than will TWOs by simulated subjects  since the probability of loading TENs concepts in FAMLI and SAM was higher than was that of loading TWOs. The human data exhibit no such ordering and demonstrate an interaction between concept attributes (TENs vs TWOs)  and required values (TENs First Digit, TWOS Remainder, etc.). Given the "related" results we can deduce further that the model may exhibit less variability across sets of experimental  conditions than will humans. One alternative model can be constructed after the experimental data have been gathered. We can  then compute the average percentage of subjects that were observed to have responded correctly on each trial across the (five)  experimental conditions. This was done for each of the first 24 trials. These data were shown as the last row (M observed) of  Table 9. The absolute difference between this overall average percentage observed as correct and the percentage of human  subjects observed as correct under each condition was compared with the absolute difference between the estimated (COMTARG)  and observed (human) percentages correct on each of the first 24 trials under all of the experimental conditions. A sign test  shows no difference between this post hoc Average model and COMTARG. The simulation predictions were closer to those observed  in 63 of 119 comparisons (one was tied). The only within-conditions differences in predictability were in the TENS First Digit  condition where the simulation model was more accurate in 17 of 23 comparisons and in the TWOS Remainder where the Average  model was more accurate in 17 of 24 comparisons.  The fact that COMTARG does not predict human performance better than does a model that predicts no difference between  experimental conditions is evidence that COMTARG exhibits less variability across conditions than do humans. Interpretation of  this finding is obscured somewhat by the fact that all experimental conditions were designed to effect rapid strategy  acquisition by the human subjects. Three of the conditions did so; the rate of learning was less rapid in the TWOS Divide  condition and still less in the THREES condition although rate of correct responding was high under this condition. Overall  variability between these experimental conditions was not great. Therefore, this must be regarded as a rather stringent test  of COMTARG.  The Average model can only be constructed after the experimental observations  have been made; it also contains no explanatory mechanisms. It can only predict that any yet unobserved behavior will be the  average of all that is already observed. Therefore, another test of COMTARG versus the Average model could be made by deducing  from COMTARG a deviant set of conditions where very little learning by humans would be predicted and comparing simulated and  human subjects under this condition. The Average model could not "predict" such a deviant condition well. CONITARG might. One  such condition was already observed; namely, a TWOs High Quarter condition of Experiment 4 (Joyner, 1969). Since the model was  not available prior to this experiment, no predictive test can be made. A postdictive test was made. The individual difference parameters in COMTARG and the isomorphism between the human and simulated responses meant that most  parameterc and nonparametric statistical tests could be used in this study. Yet many hypothesis-testing methods were not very  informative except where there were tests made of the empirical deductions of alternative models or evaluations of the effects  of the systematic variation of the model's parameters. Two additional approaches were demonstrated. These were the use of  statistical estimation techniques and tests of the model's consequences. Neither was regarded as having been used  satisfactorily. The former methods were  COMMON TARGET GAME 513  simple and actually added little to the display of the pattern of fit that was discernible by an inspection of the descriptive  statistics. The consequence tests were weak primarily because the experiments were not designed to test the specific  deductions. Both methods are regarded as indicative of those that can be developed in evaluating and improving process models  that lead to detailed numerical predictions of empirical outcomes. Both served the purpose of permitting a rapid evaluation of  the model and suggesting some possibilities for its improvement. Finally, no satisfactory way was discovered of partitioning  the variance into the three sources that can be controlled and manipulated by the structure of the model, viz, the  experimental environment, the general behavioral laws and the individual difference parameters.  The data that have been presented appear to warrant the conclusion that Simon and Newell's general theory can be extended to  problem solving in minimum social situations. One additional postulate was required to link the general theory's premise about  multiple, changing, acceptable level objectives to situations requiring the coordination of social behavior. This postulate  was derived from a statement in social comparison theory that referred to the changing motivational tendencies that are  presumed to occur in the process of resolving differences through social interaction. This premise was selected as consistent  with the process character of the general theory and permitted the construction of a model that made detailed predictions of  the sequence of behavior occurring during concept acquisition under minimum social conditions.
