The operations are bisection, and two inverses  of the bisection operation called right and left displacement, analogous to "displacement" of an interval "up" or "down" a  scale. Following Pfanzagl, "biased" operations are permitted, and linear, reflexive representations are presented for the  biased operations. Results are obtained on the constructability of unbiased scaling operations, and several methods of  constructing standard sequences of equally spaced stimuli using biased operations, are described. A striking property of  biased linear representation models is derived, showing that if bias is present, indefinite iterations of one or the other of  the two displacement operations will converge to asymptote. Preliminary data support this surprising convergence property.  1. The first theoretical issue we shall consider involving bisection and its inverses concerns the possibility of employing the  three operations in combination to yield bias correction procedures, or, more exactly, compound operations that are  theoretically unbiased, and that can be used for scaling in a way that avoids the necessity of estimating the bias parameter a  before particular scale values Y(x) can be determined. The practical motivation for this aspect of the investigation is  obvious. Routine application of Eq. (1) provides a solution for scale values in the absence of bias,  BIASED BISECTION OPERATIONS 37  but not in the presence of bias without prior determination of the value of the bias parameter, which itself is only  implicitly defined by Eq. (1), and the estimation of which involves special problems of its own. In fact, Fagot and Stewart  (1970) were forced to rely on assumptions as to the form of the psychophysical function in order to obtain estimates of a. A  striking feature of the system of bisection together with its inverses is that if all three operations are employed it is  possible to construct corrected (theoretically unbiased) scaling operations, with which values of F(x) can be determined  without prior estimation of a. Furthermore, it can be shown to be theoretically impossible to construct unbiased operations  from just two of the three basic operations. The corrected scaling operations provide a means of constructing equally spaced  sequences of stimuli with biased operations. These matters and related questions concerning constructability will be discussed  in Section 3.  Our second main topic concerns a surprising prediction of the biased scaling model: namely that if bias is present, iterations  of one of the two displacement operations should converge to asymptote, right displacements converging with positive bias and  left displacements converging with negative bias. If displacements are conceived geometrically, it would seem intuitively that  iterated displacements in either direction should cover the entire continuum (i.e., they should satisfy Archimedean  conditions), and therefore the prediction that iterations converge is counterintuitive and in principle should furnish an  important and novel test of the bias model. Theoretical aspects of these convergence properties are discussed in Section 4,  and in Section 5 we cite some preliminary empirical findings concerning subjective brightness judgments, tending to support  the conclusion that iterated displacements converge in the way predicted by the model. Section 2 below gives the essential  formal prerequisites to the investigations to follow.  2. ON THE CONSTRUCTABILITY OF UNBIASED SCALING OPERATIONS  This section is concerned primarily with the possibility of constructing unbiased scaling operations (bisection and its  inverses) from biased bisection operations and their inverses, in which case the unbiased operations can be used in turn for  scaling without prior estimation of bias. Unbiased bisection, left and right displacement  operations are thus defined by the existence of unbiased representations of the corresponding kinds for the system <D, <, o>.  Given an arbitrary RMOA F = <D, <, *~, it is clear that there exists at most one unbiased scaling operation of each kind for  it. If the RMOA is autodistributive and intern, furthermore, there must exist an unbiased bisection operation, o, for it, and  Pfanzagl (1968, p. 84) gives the following algebraic condition defining it:  For all a < b in D and all x, y and z in the interval (a, b),  x0y=z if and only if (a*z)*(z*b)=(a*x)*(y* b). (11)  Our present concern, however, is not with the definability of unbiased scaling operations (indeed, it is a consequence of the  Beth theorem on definability (Beth, 1953) that if one and only one unbiased scaling operation of a given kind exists, then it  must be "algebraically definable" in a form analogous to condition (11)), but rather with the explicit constructability of the  operation in terms of o, ~, and --*. The distinction between definability and explicit constructability from given operations  is much the same as the corresponding one in geometry, where, for example, though it is clear that the trisection operation  (for angles) is definable in terms of basic geometrical concepts, it is shown that angle trisection cannot be constructed by  ruler and compass operations alone. In the present case our "basic geometrical operations" are possibly biased bisection and  its inverses (or subsets of this triad), and the analogue to the angle trisection problem is the interval bisection problem,  and the problem of constructing unbiased displacements. Where random errors are present that may compound with the  iteration of basic scaling operations, it is essential to take them into account in assessing the significance of empirical  findings relating to indefinitely iterated operations. The small normal curve in Fig. 2 is meant to represent the fact that if  random errors are present, operating on the pair (x, y) by a right displacement operation will not lead with certainty to the  pair (y, x ->. y), but rather to a distribution over points (y, z) where YJ(z) is assumed to be normally distributed with mean  F(x --> y).  The foregoing shows that if random errors are present, right displacement can be regarded as a Markov process, whose "states"  are stimulus pairs (x, y), and whose basic transition probabilities are the probabilities that right displacement will lead  from state (x, y) to the new state (y, z). The general analysis of such Markov processes is quite complicated, and cannot be  entered into here (see, e.g., Doob (1953), Chapter V, Section 5), but results concerning finite Markov processes are at least  suggestive as to what might be expected in the present case. A basic theorem on finite Markov processes states that if the  process is regular (any state can be reached from any other state by a finite number of non-zero probability transitions),  then there is an asymptotic probability distribution over the states that is approached arbitrarily closely by iterated  transitions, and which is independent of the initial state (an exact statement is given in Kemeny and Snell (1960, p. 71). EMPIRICAL DEMONSTRATION OF CONVERGENCE  We cite a preliminary experimental result that supports the hypothesis that iterated displacements converge, and that this  convergence is not merely a matter of approaching extremes in the stimulus domain.  Two subjects were run in a pilot study using brightness. The apparatus provided three luminous white circular targets, 13 mm  in diameter, placed horizontally and separated by 90 mm center to center. The right target was continuously variable and under  the control of the subject. The left and middle targets-stimuli x and y, respectively-were fixed by the experimenter, and the  subject's task was to adjust the right target to obtain x -* y. On each trial (for fixed pair x, y) the subject made 5  different adjustments with a randomly determined "starting point" for each. The median of the 5 settings was taken as x -; y.  On trial 1, the subject was presented with x = 0.1 ft-L and y = 0.25 ft-L. On trial 2, y was the left target and x -, y the  middle target. Hence the middle and right targets of trial i were the left and middle targets, respectively, of trial i + 1.  Figure 3 shows, for one subject, a plot of log O(x --*n y) as a function of trials, where O(x -3-n y) is the physical scale  value in ft-L on trial n. Inspection shows a definite horizontal trend from about trial 15 to 20, giving strong empirical  support to the theory predicting convergence to asymptote, in this case for the right displacement operation in the presence  of positive bias. The second subject showed the same effect but with a markedly unstable asymptotic region.  Fagot and Stewart (1970), in a test of the bisection bias model for brightness, found positive bias for each of the four  subjects. This finding suggested that the right displacement operation would converge to asymptote, precisely as demonstrated  for the two subjects in the pilot study described above.
