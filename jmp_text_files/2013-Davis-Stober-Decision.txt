Recent work on  
                         p 
                      -additive utility theory by  Luce (2010a,b)  has led to the characterization of three types of utility representation, in what has been referred to as “the three types of people”.  Luce (2010b)  argued that individual decision makers (DMs) must be classified according to type prior to data analysis, due to potential confounds created by aggregating utility functions across individuals. 
                         1 
                      
                      
                         1 
                         Indeed, the dangers of aggregating across individuals extend to nearly any non-linear functional representation, see  Luce (1995)  for a discussion. 
                       To this end, Luce and colleagues ( Luce, 2010a,b; Ng, Luce, & Marley, 2009 ) derived a criterion, both necessary and sufficient, for determining the appropriate  
                         p 
                      -additive representation for a DM, which we term  Luce’s Proposed Criterion  (LPC). This criterion is defined in terms of a DM’s pairwise preferences and indifferences among a set of binary gambles.

Luce (2010a,b)  demonstrated that these three  
                         p 
                      -additive representation types directly correspond to the risk attitude of the DM. In other words, each of the three  
                         p 
                      -additive representations directly corresponds to a DM having either: risk-averse, risk-neutral, or risk-seeking preferences over a given decision environment. We use the term “decision environment” to refer to the set of probabilistic outcomes under consideration, e.g., for binary gambles, all possible payouts and probabilities of winning. Given the connection between  
                         p 
                      -additive representation type and risk attitude, it is worth noting that substantial prior research has demonstrated that DMs may not have a single risk attitude within a given decision environment.For example,  Holt and Laury (2002)  found that risk attitude could vary with respect to the relative magnitude of a gain or loss. Other examples include the well-known “reflection effect” ( Kahneman & Tversky, 1979 ), i.e., risk-seeking preferences for losses and risk-averse for gains. If a DM’s risk attitude varies within a decision environment then a single  
                         p 
                      -additive representation type may not be descriptive of her choices. We could accommodate such a DM by allowing her  
                         p 
                      -additive representation type to vary as a function of the decision environment. This raises the question: How can we extend the original LPC to formulate such models and how would we test them?

In this article, we develop a method to extend the original LPC to evaluate DMs whose  
                         p 
                      -additive representation type is allowed to vary as a function of the decision environment. In particular, we construct a series of  
                         p 
                      -additive representation models whose type assignments are motivated from existing empirical results on risk attitude. This extension contains the original “three types” as special cases. We apply a Bayesian methodology ( Karabatsos, 2005 ) to our framework, which allows the direct comparison of the various  
                         p 
                      -additive models via a closed-form Bayes factor computation. This methodology allows us to jointly evaluate whether a DM’s preferences are consistent with either single or multiple  
                         p 
                      -additive representation types within a given decision environment, and, importantly, which one(s) best account for a DM’s choices.

This methodology can be cast as a general method to evaluate the risk attitude of a DM. By utilizing the  
                         p 
                      -additive theory, risk attitude is defined in terms of DM preferences and indifferences, as opposed to the shape of an estimated utility function (e.g.,   Pratt (1964) ). Further, our methodology is sensitive to DMs with potentially complex risk attitudes, i.e., risk attitudes that are context dependent. As we demonstrate, our methodology affords excellent statistical power to discriminate among competing models.

We report the results of a within-subjects experiment designed to evaluate the LPC under this extension. We found that no single  
                         p 
                      -additive representation model emerged as the best description of the DMs’ preferences—consistent with other, related analyses on choice variability (e.g.,  Cavagnaro and Davis-Stober (submitted for publication); Loomes, Moffatt, and Sugden (2002) ). We found that participants were best described by consistent type assignments and type assignments that vary as a function of the decision environment in roughly equal numbers. These empirical results suggest substantial heterogeneity among DMs with respect to risk attitude.

This article is organized as follows. First, we briefly summarize the  
                         p 
                      -additive theory and present the LPC. Second, we describe the geometric space of all LPC-consistent pairs of gambles and demonstrate how the LPC can be extended by partitioning this space into regions corresponding to models of risk attitude. Third, we illustrate how the Bayesian methodology integrates with our extension of the LPC. Finally, we present the results of our experiment, followed by a discussion.

Risk attitude is typically operationalized as the shape of a pre-specified (or estimated) utility function where the concavity, respectively convexity, of the function indicates risk-averse, respectively risk-seeking, preferences ( Arrow, 1971; Pratt, 1964 ). Most measures of risk attitude defined in this way depend upon the choice of utility function (e.g.,  Levy and Levy (2002) ) and/or methods of estimation thereof ( Luce, 2000 ). This is problematic, as different choices of utility function may indicate different risk attitudes given the same set of data, although see the work of  Quiggin (1982)  and  Yaari (1987)  for rank-dependent utility approaches that do not require a particular choice of utility function. In contrast to previous approaches, the LPC does not depend upon a specific choice of utility function ( Luce, 2010a,b ). In addition, the LPC contains several other risk attitude criteria as special cases ( Schneider & Lopes, 1986; Tversky & Kahneman, 1992 ). The LPC is not to be confused with correlation aversion, which has been used extensively in the economics literature to evaluate risk attitude (see e.g.,  Lichtendahl, Chao, and Bodily (2012) ).

The  
                            p 
                         -additive theory is axiomatic, in that if the basic assumptions and axioms on preferences (indifferences) hold then the  
                            p 
                         -additive representation follows as a logical consequence.  Luce (2010b)  lays out a comprehensive discussion of the assumptions and measurement axioms underlying the  
                            p 
                         -additive theory and provides a review of the empirical literature.  Luce (2010b)  goes on to describe a possible program of additional experiments to evaluate the  
                            p 
                         -additive framework. The current work can be cast as the beginnings of a methodological and empirical answer to the questions raised in Section 4.2 of  Luce (2010b) . Specifically, we develop a methodology to classify individual DMs according to  
                            δ 
                          type which accommodates both heterogeneity in  
                            δ 
                          assignment across DMs as well as within DMs, in the form of varying  
                            δ 
                          assignment according to gamble environments, e.g., gains as opposed to losses. Our empirical results suggest that  
                            δ 
                          assignment is stable across gamble environments for some, but not all, DMs.

In the current work, we assume that all of the assumptions and measurement axioms underlying the  
                            p 
                         -additive theory hold. Many of these axioms have been examined within the literature and have been found to be well supported empirically, such as transitivity ( Regenwetter, Dana, & Davis-Stober, 2011; Regenwetter & Davis-Stober, 2012 ), monotonicity ( Cho & Fisher, 2000 ) and consequence monotonicity ( Ho, Regenwetter, Niederée, & Heyer, 2005; von Winterfeldt, Chung, Luce, & Cho, 1997 ). Recent empirical work has also evaluated the underlying axiomatic assumptions for a class of  
                            p 
                         -additive representations that model psychophysical processes.  Steingrimsson and Luce (2005a,b, 2006, 2007)  carried out a series of empirical studies evaluating the behavioral axioms for this class of representation under perceived auditory loudness, finding strong support for them. See also the parallel empirical work of  Steingrimsson (2009, 2011) , who evaluated these axioms within the context of perceived light intensity, finding strong support for those axioms as well. Future work should examine the descriptive accuracy of the respective  
                            p 
                         -additive axioms within the environments the present work considers, taking into account that, for some axioms, DMs must be classified according to  
                            δ 
                          type prior to analysis, e.g., duplex decomposition. Our empirical results suggest that great care must be taken when evaluating these axioms over different gamble environments. We refer interested readers to  Luce (2010b)  for a comprehensive discussion.

It is important to note, however, that the Bayesian methodology we adopt to carry out model comparison only requires the weaker assumption of exchangeability (e.g.,  Bernardo (1996) ), not full independence, between trials. In our context, exchangeability states that the likelihood of a DM selecting a gamble from a given LPC pair on the  
                         i 
                      th presentation is the same as the likelihood of a DM selecting that same gamble, from the same LPC pair, on the  
                         j 
                      th presentation of that pair.

While we advocate the ternary choice framework for reasons laid out in  Regenwetter and Davis-Stober (2012) , our approach could easily be adapted to binary forced choice by using a product of binomial likelihoods in  (5) . This framework is generally referred to as a marginal choice probability framework, which has been utilized by other, similar approaches for modeling choice probabilities (e.g.,  Bröder and Schiffer (2003) ,  Davis-Stober (2009, 2012) ,  Davis-Stober and Brown (2011) ,  Glöckner (2009) ,  Iverson and Falmagne (1985) ,  Myung, Karabatsos, and Iverson (2005)  and  Regenwetter, Dana, and Davis-Stober (2011) ). However, see  Birnbaum (2011)  for a critique of this modeling approach and  Regenwetter, Dana, and Davis-Stober (2011)  for a counter-argument.

To the best of our knowledge, no data have been collected specifically to test the LPC in this context, despite recent challenges in the decision theory literature to do so ( Luce, 2010a, 2011 ). From the axiomatic perspective of the LPC, we do not yet know whether DMs are generally consistent with a single  
                         p 
                      -additive representation. These empirical questions have implications for the descriptive accuracy of a host of decision theories. For example, recent field work ( Harrison, Lau, & Rutström, 2007 ) suggests that very few subjects are risk neutral. Should experimental results confirm this from the perspective of the LPC, then a host of well-known utility theories would necessarily be rejected as descriptive models (see  Luce (2010a)  for a discussion). Also, as noted by  Luce (2010a, 2011) , different values of  
                         δ 
                       correspond to different measurement scales, therefore different DMs may not be directly comparable to one another, requiring a within-subjects design ( Luce, 2010a, 2011 ).

We computed the full marginal likelihood for each subject and model combination. We then computed BFs for each model compared against the reference model for each subject. Finally, we computed the BF for the “best-fitting” model against the next-best-fitting model for each subject. This gives us a measure of distinguishability, as multiple models may be improvements over the reference model for a participant. A high BF in this case shows that the best-fitting model is the clear winner among the models considered for that participant’s choice data. Our experiment used 26 gamble pairs, so our full marginal likelihoods were the product of 26 individual marginal likelihoods (each with its own prior value reflective of the model’s preference prediction). This approach allows us to determine which  
                            p 
                         -additive models (if any) are descriptive of the choices made by the participants.

We presented a method of evaluating decision making preferences under  
                         p 
                      -additive utility representations. Our approach extends the original criterion described by  Luce (2010a,b)  by accommodating DMs with risk preferences that vary as a function of the decision environment. We utilized a Bayesian model comparison approach for evaluating choice data under this modeling perspective. We presented the results of a new decision making under risk experiment designed to evaluate DMs under various  
                         p 
                      -additive representations.

Our approach can be cast as a general method for evaluating risk attitude. The  
                         p 
                      -additive theory operationalizes risk attitude as an intrinsic property of DM preference (indifference) and not the shape of any pre-selected utility function. This perspective allows us to construct and subsequently test models of risk attitude that are sensitive to different decision environments (e.g., gains vs. losses), as well as detect heterogeneity across DMs. In this way, our approach to evaluating risk attitude is quite general. Future work could explore applying this method to other decision environments as well as gambles defined over non-monetary outcomes.

Future work could explore  
                         p 
                      -additive representations based upon “mixture model” specifications, similar to distribution-free random utility models (e.g.,  Block and Marschak (1960) ,  Falmagne (1978) ,  Regenwetter (1996)  and  Suck (1992) ). From this perspective, a DM would be allowed to have multiple models of risk attitude, with all choice variability being modeled as “shifts” from one model of risk attitude to another. More formally, a DM’s choice responses would be modeled via a probability distribution over a finite set of decision states, in this case, various  
                         δ 
                       assignments. Such a generalization would have interesting implications for the  
                         p 
                      -additive theory. Individuals would not have a fixed “type” for certain gamble pairs—they would probabilistically switch between a subset of “types”, e.g., a DM may vacillate between being risk averse and risk neutral but would never follow risk-seeking preferences.

Participants were encouraged to indicate indifference when they felt truly indifferent between the two alternatives. A few participants responded in a fashion consistent with complete risk neutrality by consistently indicating indifference between gambles. However, it is possible that choices distributed evenly between two alternatives of a gamble could also be considered as “indifferent.” For example, if for a particular gamble pair a participant responds in favor of the risky alternative six times and safe the other six times, is it the case that they are actually indifferent between the two? If this is the case, indifference may be indicated by equally distributing choices between the two alternatives or by using the indifference key. This presents a difficulty when fitting models of risk attitude to data, as there exist multiple ways of indicating the same preference, whereas indicating a strong preference for the risky alternative is only attainable by consistently choosing the risky alternative.

Our experiment yielded substantial heterogeneity in DMs’ behavior. It is interesting that only one-third of the participants in our experiment were categorized as one of the original “three types of individuals”, i.e., consistent risk attitude across decision environments. These results are yet another argument against a “one-size-fits-all” approach to decision modeling. Also, roughly a third of our participants were not best described by any model of risk attitude. This suggests that other models of risk attitude may be required to fully describe the behavior of all participants. Future work could consider additional substantive models of risk attitude with larger samples of DMs.
