The United States National Institute on Aging describesAlzheimer’s disease as “an irreversible, progressive brain disorder that slowly destroys memory and thinking skills, and eventually the ability to carry out the simplest tasks”. It estimates that about 5.5 million people in the United States are affected, and that it may be as high as the third leading cause of death in the United States, behind heart disease and cancer ( National Institute on Aging, 2016 ). Understanding Alzheimer’s disease presents a basic theoretical and empirical challenge for cognitive scientists studying memory, with the potential for important societal impact.

While much of the scientific focus is in cognitive neuroscience, on the neurobiology and chemistry underlying the disease in the brain, there is an important complementary role for cognitive science in developing behavioral models. Alzheimer’s disease leads to impairment in memory performance that can be observed in basic behavioral memory tasks, such as recognition and recall. Memory models that can describe, explain, and make predictions about the changes in observed behavior caused by impairment, in terms of changes in underlying psychological variables and processes, have the potential to improve diagnosis and inform treatment.

In this article, we pursue an approach to this cognitive modeling challenge pioneered by Bill Batchelder and his collaborators. The approach relies on Multinomial Processing Tree (MPT) models of memory, explicitly designed as measurement models of behavior. MPT models aim to account for observed behavior in terms of constituent psychological parameters and processes that are easy to understand ( Batchelder & Riefer, 1999; Erdfelder et al., 2009 ). They have been widely applied to the study of human memory, including phenomena like forgetting ( Chechile & Meyer, 1976 ), clustering in recall ( Batchelder & Riefer, 1980 ), source monitoring ( Riefer, Hu, & Batchelder, 1994 ), process dissociation ( Erdfelder & Buchner, 1998b ), and hindsight bias ( Erdfelder & Buchner, 1998a ). MPTs have also previously been used to understand changes in memory arising from impairment ( Alexander, Satalich, Shankle, & Batchelder, 2016; Riefer, Knapp, Batchelder, Bamber, & Manifold, 2002 ).

The MPT we use is the one developed by  Alexander et al. (2016) , explicitly for the purpose of modeling multi-trial free recall behavior in clinical settings to understand memory impairment. We apply their model to a large real-world clinical data set, involving the results of thousands of patients completing tens of thousands of multi-trial free recall assessments. We start by showing that the simplest form of the MPT model is inadequate as an account of the data. We then proceed to develop progressively better models by improving the account of individual differences and incorporating theoretical assumptions about the differences in learning and retrieval for words in different positions in the list.

As part of their assessment, patients are routinely given the Mild Cognitive Impairment Screen (MCIS). The MCIS involves a battery of cognitive assessment tasks, including multi-trial free recall with a list of 10 semantically-controlled words ( Shankle et al., 2005 ). There are three immediate free recall tasks, before each of which the words are presented, always in the same order, and patients are asked to recall as many as possible in any order. After other non-recall tasks in the battery, there is an expected delayed free recall task for the same list of words, but the words are not presented again beforehand.

Independent of their performance on these memory tasks, a trained neurologist used the Functional Assessment Staging Test (FAST) to classify the severity of cognitively-related functional deficits for each patient, into one of seven stages ( Reisberg, 1988 ). FAST stage 1 corresponds to normal aging and includes patients with no memory deficits. FAST stage 2 corresponds to pre-clinical Alzheimer’s disease and includes patients who are not affected by memory impairment in their observable real-world activities, but who report a subjective impression of functional deficit. FAST stage 3 corresponds to mild cognitive impairment and includes patients with objective functional deficits that interfere with their most complex real-world tasks. FAST stage 4 corresponds to mild dementia and involves patients who are affected in simpler tasks, such as paying bills, cooking, and traveling. FAST stage 5 corresponds to moderate dementia and involves patients who need help with very simple tasks, such as selecting appropriate clothing. FAST stage 6 corresponds to moderately severe dementia and involves patients who need help with basic tasks, such as bathing. Our data do not include patients diagnosed with FAST stage 7, who are typically unable to complete the MCIS testing battery.

Fig. 1  provides a graphical overview of the data set. Individual patient assessments correspond to columns and are colored according to their FAST stage. Multiple assessments from the same patient are in adjacent columns, and are connected by brackets at the top of the columns. The four free recall tasks are shown from top to bottom. Within the tasks, individual rows show whether or not the patient recalled each word according to its order of presentation on the study list. A subset of about 5% of the data is shown in  Fig. 1 , but illustrates the major empirical regularities in the data set overall. First, from left to right, fewer words are recalled. This means that overall memory performance decreases as memory impairment, measured by FAST staging, worsens. Secondly, more words are progressively recalled over the three immediate free recall tasks, consistent with learning. Thirdly, there is evidence of meaningful individual differences. For example, the first patient listed in FAST stage 6 completed three assessments and performed better in each than the other patients with the same diagnosed level of functional impairment. Finally, there is evidence for serial position effects in all four tasks. For the immediate free recall tasks, there are primacy and recency effects, in which words presented near the beginning or end of the list are better recalled. For the delayed free recall task, there is only a primacy effect.

Fig. 2  provides a more direct visualization of the serial position effects. It shows the average probability each word is recalled across all patients in a FAST stage, as a function of the item position in the list the word was presented. The primacy and recency effects and the learning over the three immediate free recall tasks are clear. It is also clear that memory impairment with increasing FAST stage leads to worse recall performance overall, in all of the tasks, for all of the words.

Together,  Figs. 1 and 2  suggest that it should be possible to predict FAST stage from basic measures of recall performance. Counts of the number of words correctly recalled, for example, provide a strong indication regarding the appropriate FAST stage classification. Accordingly, our goal is to focus on  understanding and explaining  the worsening of memory with impairment, and not to focus on making predictions. In other words, we aim to use cognitive models to tease apart what changes in basic underlying memory processes, like learning and retrieval, lead to the changes in observed performance in multi-trial recall.

Eq.  (1)  makes it clear that the Batchelder model is an MPT model, according to the characterization provided by  Hu and Batchelder (1994) . This is not immediately obvious from the way the model is presented in  Fig. 3 , which resembles a Markov-state model, rather than the non-cyclic tree structures typically associated with MPT models. The state transition representation in  Fig. 3 , however, is just an efficient way to express the various possibilities for the retrieval or non-retrieval of an item on each of a sequence of recall attempts. The enumeration of these possibilities leads to familiar MPT expressions like that in Eq.  (1) , and makes clear a standard (but cumbersomely large, and less interpretable) tree-based representation of the model would be possible.

The Batchelder model is designed to account for the patterns of retrievals of individual items across a series of recall tasks. To apply it to our data, two additional sorts of assumptions need to be made. The first involves individual differences, and the second involves how the parameters of the model apply to the different items in each serial position in the presented list.

The Batchelder model itself does not make any assumptions about individual differences, but it is clear in our setting that two sorts of assumptions need to be made. One concerns how model parameters differ across FAST stages, and the other concerns how model parameters differ between individual patients within a FAST stage.

In all of the models we consider, we assume independent parameterizations of the Batchelder model for different FAST stages. Our main goal is to understand the changes in memory that arise from impairment, and we want to use the data and model in an initial exploratory way to measure these changes. From this perspective, inferring model parameters separately for each FAST stage is a simple and reasonable data-driven approach. The independence across stages also lets us examine a basic theoretical question about memory impairment. This question is whether decline is monotonic for all of the learning and retrieval processes we consider, or whether there are compensatory effects. For example, it is possible that people attempt to compensate for worsening retrieval abilities by focusing on specific learning strategies. If this were the case, some parameters would improve across FAST stages. The alternative hypothesis, which is that everything worsens across FAST stages, could be modeled by introducing order constraints on the parameters. But, initially, we want to test this hypothesis, rather than assume it.

The use of a truncated Gaussian distribution is not a common assumption for modeling individual-level probability parameters hierarchically. A more common formalization, both in statistics and cognitive modeling, uses logit or probit functions, which map a group distribution over the real line to the valid probability range (e.g.,  Klauer, 2010; Lee & Wagenmakers, 2013 ). Our preference for the truncated Gaussian assumption derives from the fact that it gives significant prior density to parameter values near the boundaries of 0 and 1. For several model parameters, we think values near 0 and 1 are plausible. Examples include the testing effect parameter  
                            b 
                          being near zero in impaired patients, and the immediate retrieval from a learned state for the last item on a list being near 1 for non-impaired patients. The standard approach using logit or probit functions typically leads to extreme values having too little probability density.

Another advantage of the truncated Gaussian assumption is that it is easier to set intuitive and informative prior distributions on their  
                            μ 
                          and  
                            σ 
                          parameters, because they lie on the same probability scale as the learning and retrieval parameters themselves. Technically, because of the truncation the  
                            μ 
                          parameters are modes, and the  
                            σ 
                          parameters are not exactly standard deviations. The prior on the mode represents the possibilities for the most likely value of the group value of a parameter. The prior on  
                            σ 
                          quantifies how large we expect the average difference in a parameter value to be across different people in the same FAST stage. As a modeling assumption for the  
                            σ 
                          parameters we use positive-truncated Gaussian distributions that approximately assume it is likely that the difference between individuals for a given parameter is between about 0.05 and 0.25. This is a fairly vague prior, reflecting our lack of detailed understanding of individual differences, and allowing for possibilities that would range from significant homogeneity to significant heterogeneity in the parameterization of the Batchelder model used by different patients. We think it is important, on purely theoretical grounds, that individual differences are incorporated in some way. The idea that every patient in a given FAST stage has the same learning and retrieval probabilities seems unreasonable. In practice, failing to incorporate individual differences leads to very different results for the current model and data. The results based on aggregating response patterns within each FAST stage are shown in the  Appendix . They are qualitatively different, and suggest very different conclusions about memory impairment, than the ones we find based on assuming individual differences.

Besides individual differences, the second assumption we need to make involves how the parameters of the model apply to the different items in our 10-word lists. The Batchelder model itself does not make assumptions about the different learning and retrieval probabilities associated with where an item is presented in the list.  Alexander et al. (2016)  considered the possibility of placing order constraints on some parameters, with mixed success, and never developed a complete account of how each parameter changes with its serial position in the list. Providing such a complete account is the main modeling goal that we pursue. In the following sections, we develop progressively better accounts of this item-position variation.

A standard part of Bayesian model evaluation is model checking using posterior predictive analysis ( Gelman, Carlin, Stern, & Rubin, 2004 ). This evaluates the descriptive adequacy of a model, in terms of its ability to re-describe the data on which its inferences are based. Without some level of descriptive adequacy, there is no basis for relying on the inferences of a model, since the inferences are conditioned on the truth of that model. Often, posterior predictive analysis directly compares the posterior predictive distribution of the model to the observed behavioral data.

For our modeling goals, however, the basic behavioral data are not easy to represent in a meaningful way. What we want to model is the probability with which an individual patient in a FAST stage produces each of the 16 possible recall patterns for each of the 10 words. This probability will be different for different patients in the FAST stage, consistent with the assumptions of individual differences. Thus, the goal is to capture the  distribution  of the probabilities over the 16 recall patterns. The challenge presented by the real-world nature of our data is that many patients complete just a few assessments, and some complete only one assessment. This means that a frequentist estimate of the rate with which the 16 patterns are produced will be a poor characterization of the true underlying rate, and the distribution of these estimates over patients in the same FAST stage will consequently be a very crude estimate.

To address this issue, we take the extra step of applying a saturated model to the data. This model is not designed to capture and test theoretical assumptions, but to provide inferences about the underlying rates of recall patterns and their distributions. It is a flexible statistical model of the data, designed to provide an appropriate summary representation for posterior predictive checking based on as few statistical assumptions as possible. It provides a mechanism to make the inferences required to characterize the properties of the data that substantive theoretical models needs to be able to capture.

The saturated model we use assumes that the individual-patient probabilities for each response pattern are unconstrained, and come from a Dirichlet distribution. We allow flexibility in the Dirichlet distribution to allow for distributions of probabilities over the patterns that have just a few patterns with high probability, or have all patterns with similar probabilities, or anywhere between these extremes. The agreement is not exact: for example, the model tends to expect more variability across patients for the probability an item is never recalled. The figures for the results of the independent-item model for all of the FAST stages are available in the supplementary material. They show that similar levels of descriptive adequacy are found for the other FAST stages, except for FAST stages 5 and 6, for which the model is less descriptively adequate.

Overall, we view the independent-item model as being sufficiently descriptively adequate to make it reasonable to interpret its inferred posterior distributions over model parameters. These inferences are summarized in  Fig. 6 . It is striking that, although the parameters for each item position are independent and free to vary over item position in any way, they generally show clear theoretically interpretable patterns. There are clear and strong serial position curves, with primacy and recency effects, for the retrieval from partial and immediate retrieval parameters ( Healey & Kahana, 2014; Murdock, 1962 ). There is also an expected pattern of change for delayed retrieval, showing a primacy effect but not a recency effect.

Fig. 6  also shows that a number of the learning parameters are compatible with similar theoretical interpretations. One-shot learning and, to a lesser extent, partial learning both show serial position curves, with higher learning probabilities for words near the start and the end of the list. The consolidation and testing effect learning parameters, meanwhile, show less change, and certainly less systematic change, over item position.

Collectively, the inferences about learning and retrieval parameters made by the independent-item model suggest that the change in parameters across item position can be constrained in theoretically interpretable ways, especially in terms of serial position curves and primacy and recency effects. Before we pursue this extension, however, we consider a variant of the independent-item model, largely motivated by the observation of its relatively poor descriptive adequacy of the model for FAST stages 5 and 6.

The models developed so far incorporate individual differences by assuming the parameters for individual patients come from truncated Gaussian group-level distributions. This allows for individuals to be different from one another, but in a constrained way that assumes the differences are well represented by typically small continuous variation in parameters. Another possibility is that individual differences are more qualitative, and are represented by discrete differences in the parameters, consistent with there being two or more distinct subgroups of people ( Lee & Webb, 2005 ).

The independent-item model can be extended to test for sub-groups by incorporating a latent-mixture structure ( Bartlema, Lee, Wetzels, & Vanpaemel, 2014; Lee, 2018b ). As noted earlier, the consideration of possible subgroups was motivated by the lack of descriptive adequacy of the independent-item model for FAST stages 5 and 6. The independent-item model shows much better descriptive accuracy when it is applied to the 85% and 95% majorities in FAST stages 5 and 6, respectively. The relevant posterior predictive analysis for FAST stage 5 is also shown in the  Appendix . Accordingly, we now restrict our modeling to only the majority subgroups of patients in FAST stages 5 and 6. We believe these subgroups represent the patients following the course of memory impairment that we aim to study.

The independent-item model, once targeted at the appropriate subgroups of patients in FAST stages 5 and 6, provides reasonable description of the changes in recall performance across FAST stages in terms of underlying learning and retrieval processes. One important limitation, however, is that it essentially requires the independent application of Batchelder model to every position in the studied list. This makes the model statistically complicated, in the sense that it is able to predict a wide range of possible data ( Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002 ). The complexity also hinders interpretability, in the sense that psychologically meaningful concepts that relate to item positions are not naturally expressed by model parameters.

For example,  Fig. 6  suggests an interesting set of results for one-shot learning. There are clear primacy and recency effects that change in different ways across the FAST stages. In particular, there are large decreases in one-shot learning probabilities for items near the beginning of the list as cognitive impairment begins in FAST stage 3 and then progresses to later stages. The corresponding decreases for items near the end of the list appear to start later, with only FAST stages 5 and 6 showing low learning probabilities. Collectively, this suggests an interaction between primacy and recency effects and impairment in determining one-shot learning ability. There is no natural way, however, in which the parameters of the independent-item model can be used to measure this interaction directly or undertake direct tests of significance. This is because there is no “primacy” or “recency” parameter that corresponds to the concept. Instead, there are parameters for each item. This means it is not clear, for example, how many of the parameters for words early in the list should be included to measure primacy, or if they should all be given the same weight.

To address this issue, we extend the independent-item model to incorporate explicitly theoretical assumptions of serial position for each of the parameters. This hierarchical extension introduces parameters that do correspond to concepts like primacy and recency, and simplifies the model by constraining the way in which the group-level modes of parameters can change over the items ( Lee & Vanpaemel, 2018 ).

Fig. 10  shows the posterior predictive analysis for the serial-position model for patients in FAST stage 3. The analysis suggests that the serial-position model retains a similar level of descriptive adequacy observed for the independent-item model in  Fig. 5 . It produces very similar patterns of agreement, although it perhaps slightly underestimates recall more than the independent model. The results for each FAST stage for the serial-position model are provided in the supplementary information, and show similar levels of descriptive adequacy. Overall, we interpret the posterior predictive checks as showing that the strong additional assumptions of the serial-position model, which make the model statistically much simpler and theoretically much more interpretable, retain an acceptable level of descriptive adequacy.

The inferences made by the serial-position model are summarized in  Fig. 11 , using the same approach as  Fig. 6  to show the results of the independent-item model. These results are consistent with those found using the independent-item model, but the additional theoretical assumptions made by the model mean the results have more structure and certainty. The results of the serial-position model suggest a number of substantive conclusions about how learning and retrieval change across the FAST stages.

The most important general finding relates to the overall pattern of decline in both learning and retrieval processes, and the lack of evidence for any compensatory changes. For essentially all of the parameters, the pattern is one of either worsening or remaining the same across successive FAST stages. One hint of a compensatory increase is in the retrieval of items near the end of the list from the partially-learning state, but this effect seems small. Another hint of compensation involves the items near the end of the list for one-shot learning, but it also small. There is certainly no evidence of patients adopting fundamentally different learning strategies to maintain recall performance as retrieval becomes impaired.

Another important question is whether and how FAST stage 1 and FAST stage 2 are different, since FAST stage 2 does not involve functional differences, but relies on a subjective report of worsening memory.  Fig. 11  suggests these differences may involve partial learning and consolidation, which seems worse for FAST stage 2. There are also clear differences in how soon and how quickly delayed retrieval worsens for items toward the end of the list.

An unexpected result in  Fig. 11  is that the testing effect initially decreases as memory becomes more impaired, but then appears to improve for patients in FAST stages 5 and 6. We believe this inference reflects a weakness in the modeling rather than a surprising finding about human memory. So few items are recalled by patients in FAST stages 5 and 6 that there is little opportunity to observe a testing effect. Given this lack of data, the inferences in  Fig. 11  for the testing effect parameter probably reflect a vague prior that has not been updated, rather than a confident conclusion of a large probability.

As mentioned earlier, a significant advantage of the serial-position model is that it includes interpretable parameters for concepts like primacy and recency. The best theoretical interpretation of the primacy and recency parameters, and their relationship to the memory states in the Batchelder model, is a challenging question. One way to understand the distinction is in terms of the different patterns of interference from the presentation of other items that they involve. Items at the beginning of the list are followed by many others, meaning that any learning process could be potentially disrupted by following items. These disruptions are measured by primacy parameters. Items at the end of the list, in contrast, are not followed by many others. The impact this has on learning and subsequent retrieval is measured by recency parameters. In this sense, the primacy and recency parameters provide a descriptive summary of how impairment differentially affects items in different positions in temporal sequences.

We emphasize that these conclusions are illustrative, and intended to emphasize the way in which the serial-position model uses parameters that correspond to abstract concepts about learning and retrieval processes in episodic memory. We also caution against reaching strong conclusions about significant differences, since our illustrative conclusions are based on observing posterior distributions of parameters. Drawing conclusions about whether the observed differences are significant should not be based on overlap in credible intervals or other unprincipled approaches using posterior distributions for model selection ( Wagenmakers, Lee, Rouder, & Morey, 2020 ). Instead, they should be addressed using Bayesian model selection, based on Bayes factors ( Wagenmakers, Morey, & Lee, 2016 ). This would require reformulating the model with effect-size parameters that explicitly correspond to the difference between the key parameters of interest. Given priors on these effect sizes, and their posterior based on applying the model to the data, it would be possible to use the Savage–Dickey method to estimate Bayes factors that tested whether differences in the parameters were significant. Extending the measurement model we have developed to one that can test for significant changes in learning and retrieval processes is an obvious priority for this line of research.

The approach to model development we used is an exploratory one, following a process of abductive reasoning. We started with data observing changes in memory performance with impairment, and chose an MPT model of multi-trial free recall as the basis for seeking a descriptively adequate and theoretically parsimonious account of the data. After making the theoretical decision to incorporate individual differences, we found that the simplest application of the model with fixed parameters over items was descriptively inadequate. Accordingly, we allowed parameters to vary independently over items and observed better descriptive adequacy for most of the FAST stages. Where we continued to observe the model failing to account for data, we allowed for more flexible individual differences, in the form of subgroups. Under this approach, we identified majority subgroups for FAST stages 5 and 6 that were better described by the independent-item model. Given this descriptive adequacy, we used the parameter inferences from the independent model to create an extended serial-position model, which imposed theoretical constraints on the changes in parameters that previously had been allowed to vary independently.

We argued that the serial-position model provides a useful account of how memory changes over impairment, measured by FAST stages in our clinical data. The model brings together three key elements: the Batchelder MPT model as an account of multi-trial immediate and delayed free recall; serial position curves and logistic decay curves as accounts of primacy and recency effects on learning and retrieval; and an underlying assumption of discrete and continuous individual differences. We want to emphasize, however, that the approach to model development, by incrementally building on results based on the available data, means that our findings need to be regarded as exploratory ( Lee et al., 2019 ). A confirmatory approach to evaluating the serial-position model would be to apply it to related problems and to test whether the key ideas are robust and generalize. One possibility would be to apply the model to other multi-trial free recall tasks, such as those used in the Auditory-Verbal Learning Test (AVLT), which includes 15 words across 7 free recall tasks ( Ivnik et al., 1990 ). Another possibility is to apply the model to the Alzheimer’s Disease Assessment Scale-Cognitive Subscale (ADAS-Cog), which has the same basic structure as the MCIS, of three immediate free recall tasks followed by a delayed free recall task, but which presents the words in different orders for each of the three immediate free recall tasks ( Christensen, Multhaup, Nordstrom, & Voss, 1991 ). A different approach to testing the serial-position model involves the idea of selective influence, in which experimental manipulations are shown to affect only the model parameters they theoretically should affect ( Lee et al., 2019; Voss, Rothermund, & Voss, 2004 ). One way to do this, for example, would be to manipulate the length of time and distractor activities leading up to the delayed free recall task, which should systematically affect only the recency parameters.

Our approach relied heavily on the MPT model developed by  Alexander et al. (2016) . This is merely a good starting point, and could be extended in a number of ways. We assumed that all of the changes arising from memory impairment are reflected in changes in the parameters of the model. It is possible that impairment also affects the memory processes formalized by the model. One possibility is that impairment at some point removes the partially-learned state, and hence removes the processes associated with partial learning, consolidation, retrieval from the partially learned state, and the testing effect. Clear support for this possibility is provided by the results for the serial-position model in  Fig. 11 . The probability of consolidation learning is inferred to be near zero for FAST stages 5 and 6, and there is little evidence of a testing effect. In addition, while identifying the majority and minority subgroups in FAST stages 5 and 6 improved the descriptive adequacy of the Batchelder model considerably, these stages remain less well described than the others. Thus, it may be that a better model of free recall for patients in these stages is a reduced version of the model in  Fig. 3 , with the intermediate stage removed. Future research should consider incorporating model selection for each FAST stage, considering the full model and theoretically plausible reduced forms, and infer only the parameters involved in the preferred model. In addition, given the evidence the current results provide for the hypothesis that impairment involved monotonic decline in all of the learning and retrieval processes assumed by the Batchelder model, it would make sense to impose order constraints on the parameters. These changes would likely resolve the unexpected inferences about the testing effect. More generally, they would also simplify the model, in terms of the range of data patterns it predicts, and could help address the computational sampling convergence issues we noted.

A second worthwhile extension of the model we used involves a richer model of individual differences. The latent-trait MPT framework developed by  Klauer (2010)  allows for individual differences to be correlated between parameters. The basic intuition is that, for example, if a patient has a relatively high one-shot learning probability, they might also be relatively better at retrieval than others in their FAST stage, and have relatively high immediate and delayed retrieval parameter values. The same patient might also be expected to have relatively lower partial learning and consolidation probabilities, since they generally learn using the one-shot mechanism that transfers a word immediately to the learned state. Our hierarchical approach to modeling individual differences cannot capture this structure, because it assumes individual variation is independent between parameters. The latent-trait approach, in contrast, introduces a covariance structure across parameters. Extending the serial-position model in this way might provide a descriptively better account of our data, and so improve inferences about parameters, as well as provide additional information about the psychological relationships between the various learning and retrieval parameters themselves.

Most generally, future research should consider other types of memory models ( Norman et al., 2008 ). The Batchelder model commits to a discrete multi-store theory of memory, and an important alternative theory relies on a more continuous notion of context drift. It would be worthwhile applying some version of the temporal context model ( Howard & Kahana, 2002; Polyn, Norman, & Kahana, 2009 ). This class of model has been used to address the related question of how memory changes with aging ( Healey & Kahana, 2016 ), and it would be interesting to see the inferences of temporal context models for our data involving impairment.

On the methodological front, we think the case study provided by the Batchelder MPT model and the clinical data provides a good worked example of the effectiveness of Bayesian statistical inference in cognitive modeling ( Lee, 2011, 2018b ). Bayesian inference has the advantage of a principled and simple framework for inference and can readily be applied to a wide range of cognitive models for behavioral data. Our modeling made extensive use of hierarchical structures for individual differences, and to express theoretical assumptions about serial position effects, as well as latent-mixture structures to allow for subgroups.

Throughout the model development process, we were able to formalize theoretical ideas as graphical models, and use standard computational methods to approximate the joint posterior distribution over parameters. This joint posterior distribution measures everything that is known and unknown about the combinations of parameters, based on the modeling assumptions and the data. In this way, Bayesian inference provides a natural vehicle for allowing theoretical creativity in model development, as well as completeness in making inferences about what follows from models and data.

While there is ongoing debate comparing frequentist and Bayesian approaches to data analysis in the cognitive sciences ( Cumming, 2013; Lakens, McLatchie, Isager, Scheel, & Dienes, 2018; Wagenmakers, 2007 ), and for some relatively simple cognitive modeling problems ( Birnbaum & Quispe-Torreblanca, 2018; Lee, 2018a ), it is difficult to imagine how the sequence of models we developed, evaluated, and applied could have arisen relying on frequentist methods. In terms of the debate, we think the ability of Bayesian methods to tackle new problems is at least as important as its superiority over frequentist methods for existing problems.

Our goal in this article was to demonstrate how cognitive models of memory could be used to understand the way in which Alzheimer’s disease impairs memory. The key idea is that the cognitive model provides a window for understanding behavioral data that is not offered by standard statistical data analysis. The model we used provides an account of how and why patients perform the way they do in free recall assessment tasks, described in terms of psychologically interpretable processes and parameters. Applying the model to real-world clinical data thus offered insight into how basic properties of memory, like learning rates and retrieval probabilities, change over the course of impairment. We view this as an example of a powerful approach that Bill Batchelder pioneered and championed throughout his career: using cognitive models as psychometric models, picking apart the cognitive properties and processes that make people the same and different, and enabling the creative measurement of psychological phenomena in practical and insightful ways.
