JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY: 4, 140-150 (1967)  Statistics and Moments-Parameter Estimates for a Duoprocess Paired-Associate Learning Model  IRWIN D. NAHINSKY  University of Missouri, Columbia, Missouri  A model postulating all-or-none unlearning of wrong alternatives as well as acquisition of correct alternatives is presented.  Moments estimates based on the total error distribution are developed for c, the learning rate constant, and for a, the  unlearning rate constant. Statistics for testing the model are presented. They are: mean and variance for total errors,  theoretical learning curve, trials until last error, and distribution of responses until the first trial upon which some  alternative occurs for the last time.  Investigators have recently developed extensions of the one-element stimulussampling model for paired-associate learning  (Bower, 1961; Estes, 1959). A natural extension involves a model postulating a one-stage elimination of errors analogous to  the one-stage learning of correct responses of the initial model. Rapaport (1961) has presented a "perfect learner" model  applicable to a number of rote-learning situations, including paired-associate learning. This model serves as a base line for  the proposed duoprocess interpretation since it postulates unerring elimination of incorrect alternatives when they occur and  immediate learning of the correct alternative upon its first occurrence. Millward (1964) and Nahinsky (1964a, 1964b) have  developed models independently which stem from similar sets of axioms allowing a duoprocess interpretation. However, these  models allow for departure from perfect-learner assumptions and require estimates of learning and unlearning rate parameters  in the paired-associate situation. The two models have been developed in somewhat different fashions, but they generate the  same types of hypotheses.  Certain problems confront both models, among the foremost of which is the estimation of parameters. This paper presents the  development of parameter estimates using the estimates of moments derived from certain statistics. This will be introduced by  a consideration of the basic axioms of the model and the derivation of expected values for certain statistics useful for  testing the validity of the model.  140  A DUOPROCESS MODEL 141  A DUOPROCESS MODFL,  The axioms of the model allow for a one-stage learning of correct alternatives and a one-stage elimination of errors, with  corresponding constants, c for learning rate and a for error-elimination rate. This is true, because there should be stationarity of correct response probability during the trials until  some incorrect alternative occurs for the last time. If we accept the axioms of the model, this follows from the fact that no  transition can occur until at least one incorrect response which has been made stops recurring. It follows that a stationarity  analysis (see Suppes and Ginsberg, 1963) of blocks of trials both before and after s should provide parameter-free tests of  the assumptions of the model. Stationarity should hold for the former analysis but not for the latter analysis. Thus there are  differential predictions for comparison with incremental models on the one hand and the uniprocess all-or-none model on the  other. Furthermore, a detailed study of the distribution of responses prior to s would contribute to a rigorous test of the  assumptions of the model.  PARAMETER ESTIMATION  The moments-parameter estimation procedure presented does provide a relatively simple means of making the estimates. In view  of the fact that the estimates are consistent, large sample sizes should provide reasonably good estimates.  The estimation problem is complicated by the fact that all the information latent within the usual response protocol is not  available to the experimenter. He does not know how many upward transitions have been made before the final error in each  instance. This inaccessible data contains information about the parameters. Hence the distribution for any remaining set of  statistics does not allow the extraction of suqicient statistics. Thus any statistic used will not give all the necessary  information about parameters, which leaves every estimation procedure with something to be desired.  REFERENCES  BOWER, G. H.
