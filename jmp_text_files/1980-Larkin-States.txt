WICKENs  University of California, Los Angeles  The structure of a Markov learning model can often be appreciably simplified by analyzing the eigenstructure (eigenvectors and  eigenvalues) of its transition operator, and by focusing on population states representing distributions of individuals rather  than on subject states representing individuals. This view often produces considerably simpler "reduced" models, which are  equivalent to the originals in that they make, identical predictions. We apply these reduced representations to determine the  number of estimable parameters a model supports and to answer questions of model identifiability: when two models are  mathematically equivalent and when they are likely to predict observations in practice distinguishable on the basis, of  limited data.  Models based on Markov chains provide useful ways to express psychological assumptions in forms that make testable  predictions. However, even when the psychological assumptions are straightforward, the models can be computationally complex.,  Anybody who has attempted to derive predictions from a Markov model with very many states is well aware of the difficulties  that can beset one: calculation is tedious, it is hard to tell which parameters are really estimable, the mathematical  formalism becomes psychologically opaque.  A start in addressing these problems has been made by applying matrix methods from linear algebra. In this paper we combine a novel view of Markov models with additional results from linear algebra to carry these  simplifications considerably further. We develop for Markov learning models a particularly simple representation based on two  ideas. First, reflecting the stochastic nature of these models, the representation does not emphasize subject states, which  describe individuals, but rather population states, which describe distributions oindividuals. Second, using selected  population states, we express models in a representation which is uniquely parsimonious, both mathematically and  psychologically. This "reduced" representation lends itself to geometric visualization, and we develop a geometric analog as  an aid to guiding detailed calculations, and to making qualitative judgements about the major features of a model. To make  visualization possible, the geometric analog is stated in terms of examples in two or three dimensions. However, we have also  found the geometric representation useful in thinking about more complicated, problems. It also serves to emphasize those  aspects of the model that remain invariant over different algebraic representations.  In the first, or theoretical, part of this paper, we describe our reduced representation and the procedures for representing  any model in this way; these comments are applicable to any finite-state Markov chain. In the second part of the paper, we  apply these general procedures to a large class of learning models, giving explicit formulae for the reduced representation.  On this basis, we then confront the issues of parameter identification and model equivalence, both the formal mathematical  problem and the more practical question of when two models are likely to make observably different predictions.  DESCRIPTION OF THE REPRESENTATION  We investigate the general class of psychological models in which responses are described as functions of the unobservable  states of a finite and homogeneous Markov chain. Most Markov learning models are of this type. The states in the chain  correspond to the subjects' underlying knowledge, and so will be called knowledge states. They are ordinarily not directly  observable in a subject's behavior. Observable behavior is predicted by means of a mapping from the knowledge states onto a  different state space, consisting of response states. Each response state corresponds to an observable class of behavior;  thus, a subject in a particular response state makes the response associated with that state. The sequence of response states  need not (and usually does not) form a Markov chain. Using Eigenvectors as a Basis  The form of a population state vector and of the matrix representing the transition operator depends on the choice of basis  vectors used to express the population states. Thus, as we shall show below, a clever choice of basis can considerably enhance  184 LARKIN AND WICKENS  transparency and tractability of a model. It is standard to use as a basis the subject knowledge states, in which case the  elements of the state vectors are probabilities and the transition operator is represented by a matrix having as elements the  transition probabilities between pairs of subject knowledge states. The model in the example just presented was expressed in  this form.  Although the subject knowledge-state vectors form a convenient basis, one can use equally well any set of basis vectors that  allow all the knowledge states of the model to be expressed. The Number of Estimable Parameters  One immediate benefit of the reduced representation is that it is easy to tell how many parameters are supported by a model.  This is not obvious in unreduced models, for the excess states allow many slots for parameters which cannot in reality be  estimated. The argument we make is simple: since any model can be put into a reduced form without modifying its predictions,  no model can support more parameters than a corresponding reduced model. A model is characterized by its transition processes,  by the parameters determining the responses associated with each reduced knowledge state, and by its initial state. We count  the parameters associated with each of these aspects.  The transition process is determined by its eigenstructure, that is, its eigenvalues and essential eigenvectors. Since the  eigenvalue of the absorbing state is one, only b - 1 eigenvalues are free, giving that many parameters. Because the model is  determined by specifying the progress of the points representing population states in 9, neither the absolute orientation of  the eigenvectors, nor their relation to the absorbing state is needed to specify a model. Thus all that is required is the set  of (b - 1)(b - 2)/2 angles between the b - 1 eigenvectors lying in 9.  With each knowledge state is associated one parameter indicating the proportion of each kind of response. If, as in most  models, the response of the absorbing state does not vary, then at most b - 1 parameters are required to describe the  production of responses (recall that we are restricting our discussion to the case of two responses).  Finally, the complete reduced model must specify an initial state. Because the reduced model has b subject states, b - 1  parameters are required to specify its initial state.  EIGENSTRUCTURE OF LEARNING MODELS 201  This completes the roll of parameters; in summary, a total of no more than  (b-1)+(b-1)-I-(b-1)(b-2)/2+(b-1)=(b-1)(b+4)/2  parameters can be identified.  Forming this total is somewhat misleading, for parameters cannot ordinarily be exchanged among the three groups. Constraining  some of the parameters in the initial state, for example, does not allow one to add meaningful parameters to the description  of the transition process. The relation between the parameters of the transition and response processes is more complex. When  knowledge states are split into response substates, then the probability of a particular response (from a given state) is just  zero or one, and there are no free response parameters. Instead, information about the response distribution is reflected in  the parameters in the transition matrix that split knowledge states into response substates. If a model is completely split  into response substates, the number of parameters describing the transition process is equal to the sum of the numbers of  parameters describing both the transition and response processes in the reduced form of the model.  It is possible for a model to have some parameters associated with the initial vector beyond those ennumerated above, which  may be estimable, but only weakly so. This happens when the initial state falls outside the space spanned by the essential  eigenvectors of the transition matrix, i.e., in the null-space of the transformation (Greeno & Steiner, 1964; Steiner &  Greeno, 1969; Greeno, 1968). It is possible to estimate parameters associated with the component of the initial state vector  in the null space, but because the first application of the transformation reduces this component to zero, information about  the parameters can be obtained only on the first trial. This makes it difficult to obtain good estimates of these parameters,  for relatively few data can be brought to bear on them.  The limits on identifiable parameters can readily be applied to the examples considered earlier. The hypothesis-testing model  of Eq. (15) can be reduced to two blocks, and so at most three parameters, including one specifying the initial state, can be  estimated. In fact, the transition matrix, as originally stated, has two parameters, and so is fully parameterized. This is  consistent with the well-known fact that the parameters of the hypothesis-testing model in the form of Eq. (15) can be  identified.  With the two-stage model (Eq. (35)) there are three blocks, so there should be seven estimable parameters, two associated with  the initial state, two with response processes, and three with the transition processes (in reduced form). Since the model as  originally stated has 11 parameters, including four characterizing the initial state, this model has a total of four excess  parameters. Two of these are associated with the initial state, and the remaining two with the transition and response  processes. The two extra initial state parameters might be weakly estimated from first-trial data.  Formal Xdentifiability  Closely related to the problem of the number of parameters in a model is the question of when two models make the same  predictions. In the first part of this section we look  202 LARKIN AND WICKENS  at the conditions for models to make identical predictions; in the second, we consider some ways of finding the transformation  that changes one member of a pair of equivalent models to the other. In both discussions we assume that models are in reduced  representation, and this representation makes everything more simple. Differences between models that are not associated with  the reduced form, for example, differences in the number of unreduced states, do not affect the results.  Conditions for Models to be Identical  There are several different ways to view the equivalence of models. Because this problem has been treated in a number of ways  in the past, we consider three of them.  Similarity of Geometry. If the geometry of two models is the same, then their action is the same and they make identical  predictions. This correspondence is easily expressed in terms of the same set of properties used to count the parameters. In  particular, two models which have the following four characteristics in common must be identical: (1) the relationships  between the eigenvectors, that is, the angles between them; (2) movement along the eigenvectors, that is, the eigenvalues; (3)  the initial population state; (4) the mapping from knowledge states to responses. Whenever appropriate parameter assignment  gives two models the same eigenstructure, the same initial state, and the same response mapping, they are the same.  Basis Change. One way to change the algebraic appearance of a model, without changing its geometry, is to change the basis in  which the transition matrix is expressed. This is exactly the transformation we have used (see Eq. (10)). If two models are  related by a basis change, then they are equivalent. This fact has been exploited (although not in these terms) to show the  equivalence of a number of models (Wandell et al., 1974).  Identical Likelihoods. Other work has emphasized the fact that two representations are the same if and only if they give  exactly the same probability for every string of responses (Erickson, 1970; Wandell et al., 1974). In fact, if two models have  equivalent geometry, or are related by a basis change, then all their predictions, including likelihoods, are identical.  However, as already shown in Eq. (28), likelihoods expressed in the original and in the reduced bases are equal.  It is clear from the preceding discussion that if two reduced models have identical geometry (i.e., are related by a basis  change), then they are equivalent in that they make identical predictions, and specifically yield the same likelihood for any  string. The converse is also true (Erickson, 1970) if the two reduced models have the same dimensionality. However, it may  still be that reduced models of different reduced dimensionality could make identical predictions, and we have not pursued the  analysis of this point. Specifically, (b - 1)(b + 4)/2 quantity pairs  (considered when we counted parameters) must be equal. Relationship to Previous Work  In this paper we have taken a different approach to identifiability from other workers in the psychological literature (Greeno  & Steiner, 1964; Greeno, 1968; Halff, 1976; Polson & Huizinga, 1974; Greeno et al., 1971; Wandell et al., 1974). In this work  and in our own, the goal has been to convert a model to some simple canonical form, and to observe that models that reduce to  the same form are equivalent. The choice of both form and method is different, however.  48o/22/3-4  204 LARKIN AND WICKENS  The most fundamental difference is in the nature of the canonical form. Greeno and other workers have chosen to work toward a  representation in which the states are identifiable, in the sense that by observing a sequence of responses the exact sequence  of underlying knowledge states can be determined. While this approach makes the parameter estimation procedures simple, it  leaves the transition matrices comparatively complex. More seriously, there does not seem to be a good way to determine the  most simple observable-state representation that corresponds to a particular model, particularly when the original model is  complicated. In contrast, we have chosen to define our reduced form so as to make the representation of the transition  operator most simple, at the expense of observational simplicity. In particular, we introduced probabilistic response  operators, which have more non-zero and non-unit entries, but which are more compact. An important advantage of our  representation is that there is an unambiguous algorithm for constructing it.  A second difference between the two approaches is the emphasis we have placed on the geometry of the situation. This is more  heuristic than fundamental, however; much of the later work by Greeno and his associates (see particularly Wandell et al.,  1974) can be interpreted in geometric terms. Nevertheless, we have found that the geometrical picture has led us to insights  that would have been more difficult if we had approached only from an algebraic point of view.  Practical Identifiability  Even when two models are mathematically distinct, their predictions of data may be so similar that they are not  distinguishable in any practical way. Although a complete statistical treatment of the conditions for practical  identifiability is fairly complicated, our geometric representation of a Markov model allows these conditions to be addressed  at a qualitative level. In this section we present such an analysis. Our aim is to illustrate, by means of an example, the  utility of the geometric representation in checking whether two models predict sufficiently different results that they are  likely to be practically identifiable. However, these remarks are not intended to substitute for a more detailed analysis,  based on probabilistic considerations or estimation procedures (Polson & Huizinga, 1974), that should ultimately be performed. SUMMARY AND CONCLUSION  We have presented a way of viewing Markov learning models which provides substantial mathematical simplifications. The basic  ideas are that states can represent populations, rather than individuals, and that explicating the mathematical structure of  the transition operator of a model (its eigenstructure) often suggests substantial simplifications.  Our reduced representation also provides some appealing advantages of psychological transparency. Specifically, in the common  case of learning models, the reduced representation separates explicitly the process through which subjects go between  internal knowledge states, from the process by which subjects in a given knowledge state give responses. The first process is  modeled by the reduced transition matrix, the second by the various response parameters associated with each reduced knowledge  state.  As a result of reducing Markov learning models, we have been able to present fairly transparent solutions to several problems.  These include (1) whether two models are distinguishable, (2) the number of parameters a model can support, and (3) whether  two models make predictions which are qualitatively very different and thus are distinguishable in practice.
