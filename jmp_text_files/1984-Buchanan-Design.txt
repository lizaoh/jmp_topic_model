MORRISON  Columbia University  Researchers often test a null hypothesis of no ability in the populattion (the so-called "parity" hypothesis) using a single,  forced-choice question with k alternatives. In this study a result is presented which should help researchers select the  number of alternatives that maximizes the statistical power of the parity hypothesis test. Also the conditions under which it  is always beneficial to add alternatives to the test are derived. Finally, the derived result is used to compare several  popular parity test designs. The results show that the frequently used triangle test is optimal under a very broad range of  plausible conditions. 01984 Academic Press, Inc.  INTRODUCTION  In an early, classic article, Tversky (1964) described a technique for dividing a fixed number of alternatives k into an  optimal number of forced-choice points. For example, a test with eight alternatives yields many different choice point  configurations-1 choice point with 8 alternatives, 2 choices points with 4 alternatives each, 2 choice points with 3 each, and  1 with 2, etc. Tversky found that the use of 3 alternatives at all possible choice points maximizes the test's  discriminability, where discriminability is defined as the number of distinct possible response patterns on the test. He also  showed that discriminability and two other common criteria, power and information, are strictly monotonically related to each  other, so maximizing one maximizes all.  This study is similar in spirit to Tversky's. We study a class of tests where subjects are presented with k alternatives, one  of which is different from the other (usually identical) k - 1. The subject's task is to select the odd alternative. The class  of tests could be called "Pick 1 of k" designs. If the subject has the ability, he selects the odd alternative with  probability 1. If not, he chooses randomly, thereby choosing the odd alternative with probability 1/k. Where Tversky fixed the  number of alternatives, k,  Address reprint requests to B. S. In  the sensory testing literature, this null hypothesis is sometimes called the parity hypothesis.  Parity tests are commonly performed in the food and beverage industries where they are used to maintain batch-to-batch  consistency in product quality. In these applications, a panel of subjects samples products from each of two batches. Usually  one batch is a standard and the other is newly produced. If they can, the new batch is reworked or discarded. Parity tests are also used to evaluate cost-saving product  reformulations. Here, panel subjects compare the existing product formulation to the cost-saving substitute. Again, if the  panel cannot discriminate, then the substitute can be placed on the market without fear that consumers will notice the  difference. Otherwise, the producer risks being found out and losing customers.  In these two applications, there are large costs associated with accepting the null hypothesis when it is not true. In the  first, producers risk certifying substandard products which, when consumed, could damage their brand's reputation. In the  second, they risk losing market share when consumers notice the change in formulations. For both applications, then, producers  wish to maximize the statistical power of the parity test. This point has been discussed in the product testing literature by  Greenhalgh (1966) and Hopkins and Gridgeman (1955).  In product testing, three parity test designs are frequently employed. The first, the so-called "triangle" test, is simply a  Pick 1 of 3 design. The second, the paired comparison, is a Pick 1 of 2 design, which can only be used when the change in  formulations involves a difference in one attribute ("Which is sweeter?") or the presence or absence of an attribute ("Which  has cherry flavoring added?"). The third common parity test design is the "duo-trio" test, which, like the triangle, involves  two identical product samples and a different one. The subject is first given one of the identical pair, then told: "Of the  next two samples you will taste, one is identical to the first, one is different. Identify the different sample."  To find the most powerful parity test design we must consider two factors. The first is complexity. By our assumption, ability  may decline as the number of alternatives in the test increases. This decline reflects fatigue, confusion, and other effects  480/28/4-7  456 BUCHANAN AND MORRISON  that depend upon k. From Fig. 1 it is clear that, other things being unchanged, the power of the parity test decreases as  ability declines. When concerned about complexity, then, our inclination is to minimize the number of alternatives in the test.  The second factor to be considered is the effect of correct chance choices, or correct guesses, as we call them here. Correct  guesses confound efforts to measure ability, or the lack of it, because the total number of correct responses contains an  unknown number of correct guesses. Since the probability of guessing correctly is 1/k, it is easy to see that the effect of  guessing declines with increasing k. Up, does not also decline with increasing k, our intuition suggests, and we will later  show, that the statistical power of the parity test increases with k. To find the most powerful parity test design, then, it  is clearly necessary to determine the number of alternatives that yields the optimal trade-off between the effects of  complexity and guessing.  In this paper, we present a technique for performing this trade-off. First, for large samples, we show that the power curve of  a Pick 1 of k + r test can be derived from that of a Pick 1 of k test simply by scaling the latter by a constant determined by  k and r. For any given value of uk, this result allows us to find the value of uk+r that yields a parity hypothesis test of  the same power for equal sample sizes N and Type I error probabilities a. Given this result, the choice of a most powerful  parity test design depends upon how ability declines as more alternatives are added to the test. A simple model for this  decline is presented and the results it yields are discussed. Finally, we apply these results to the triangle, duo-trio, and  paired comparison tests, and derive the conditions when each is more powerful than the other two. CONCLUSIONS  The basic result of this paper is that the power curve of a parity test with k + r alternatives can be simply derived from  that of a test with k alternatives where k is arbitrary. From this result alone, we have derived the conditions under which it  is always beneficial to add alternatives or, equivalently, where the most powerful parity test is the Pick 1 of oo design.  Combining the basic result with a simple model relating the decline in ability to the addition of alternatives, we have  demonstrated a technique for determining the conditions under which a particular number of alternatives provides the most  powerful parity test. Finally, we have presented a reasonable heuristic for finding most powerful parity tests, and we applied  our basic result to evaluate three designs that are frequently used in product testing.
