Generalization of the Theory of Signal Detectability to n-Event m-Dimensional Forced-Choice Tasks 

Brian K. Scurfield 

Victoria University of Wellington, Wellington, New Zealand 

This paper presents a generalization of the theory of signal detectability to n-event forced-choice tasks where the evidence can be modelled by an m-dimensional vector. The generalization is based on a nonparametric model that encompasses decision rules for maximizing the proportion of correct decisions. The model assumes that observers identify events by partitioning a decision space of dimension n&1 with a template. Translating the template by varying the decisional bias yields a set of receiver operating characteristic (ROC) surfaces. Following B. K. Scurfield [1996, J. Math. Psych. 40, 253�269], event-discriminability is defined by considering the Shannon entropy of the volumes under the ROC surfaces. The resultant discriminability measure is interpreted with respect to the random vectors assumed to be associated with the decision space and shown to equate with the channel capacity of an observer in a multiple-interval forced-choice task. � 1998 Academic Press 

The theory of signal detectability (TSD) was developed in the early 1950s by engineers working on problems of radar and sonar detection (Peterson, Birdsall, 6 Fox, 1954; Van Meter 6 Middleton, 1954). The theory was developed by amalgamating statistical decision theory (Neyman 6 Pearson, 1933; Wald, 1950) with the theory of ideal observers (Lawson 6 Uhlenbeck, 1950). Soon after its inception, a number of psychologists realized that TSD could be used to analyze a broad range of psychophysical problems (e.g., Egan, 1957; Jeffress, 1964; Marill, 1956; and Tanner 6 Swets, 1954; see Swets, 1964, for an edited collection of early applications

of TSD). The theory solved the long-standing problem of distinguishing between discriminability and decisional bias and provided independent measures of each. Many psychological effects that had previously been attributed to changes in discriminability were shown instead to be attributable to changes in decisional bias (Swets, 1973). Despite these successes, a major limitation of TSD is that it is primarily applicable to decision problems involving two events and cannot easily be applied to decision problems involving more than two events. 

Recently, there has been a growth of interest in multidimensional models of categorization and identification and a number of new models have been developed (see Ashby, 1992, for an edited survey). Many of these models are applicable to decision problems with more than two events and appear to be generalizations of TSD. Like TSD, most assume that observers represent the stimuli associated with a set of events as points in a multidimensional space and that each event is represented by a probability distribution defined on this space. Another shared feature is a distinction between the processes that are assumed to be involved in representing stimuli and those that are assumed to be involved in decision making. For example, like TSD, decision-bound models (e.g., Ashby 6 Gott, 1988; Ashby 6 Perrin, 1988; Ashby 6 Townsend, 1986; Chen, 1973; Fukunaga, 1972) assume that observers partition the space used to represent the stimuli into regions and identify events by determining the region in which the current stimulus representation lies. Despite the similarities of the current multidimensional models to TSD, none can be interpreted as a generalization of TSD because none has yet provided a way to distinguish changes in discriminability from changes in decisional bias. 

The theory of signal detectability distinguishes between discriminability and decisional bias by using a statistical technique called receiver operating characteristic (ROC) analysis. For a discrimination task with a ``signal'' event and a ``noise'' event, the conventional ROC curve shows the relationship between the probability of choosing the signal event when it occurs and the probability of choosing the signal event when the noise event occurs as an observer's decisional bias varies. As the form and location of the ROC curve are independent of the observer's decisional bias, the area under the ROC curve can be used as a measure of discriminability (Green 6 Swets, 1966). Although ROC analysis can be applied to a wide range of decision problems, it has not been clear how to generalize the concept of a ROC curve to decision problems involving more than two events. This difficulty is one reason why attempted generalizations of TSD have not been successful. 

Another reason was noted by Luce (1994): ``The generalization of the theory of signal detectability to the absolute identification of several signals...has not been very effective,'' because, ``it was difficult to provide a sensible interpretation of two or more axes in terms of likelihood ratios'' (p. 276). In decision problems involving more than two events, each pair of events has its own likelihood ratio axis. Although these axes can be embedded in a likelihood ratio space, it is not clear when these separate axes can be assumed to be orthogonal and how the space should be partitioned to arrive at a decision (Luce, 1963). Presumably this is why the current literature on multidimensional models contains no representations of likelihood ratio spaces.

GENERALIZATION OF TSD TO n-EVENTS AND m-DIMENSIONS 

This paper is a companion paper to Scurfield (1996). In that paper the problem of generalizing TSD to n-event unidimensional forced-choice[1] tasks was considered. A unidimensional decision-bound model was assumed in which observers represent the stimuli associated with a set of events as points on a unidimensional axis. Within the context of this model, Scurfield solved the problem of generalizing ROC analysis by introducing the concept of an ROC surface and a new measure of discriminability based on the Shannon entropy of the volumes under a set of ROC surfaces. The purpose of this paper is to present a generalization of TSD to n-event m-dimensional forced-choice tasks. 

The outline of the paper is as follows. First, a general multidimensional model of identification and discrimination in forced-choice tasks is presented. It is shown that this model encompasses observers who maximize the proportion of correct decisions and thereby ``provides a sensible interpretation of two or more axes in terms of likelihood ratios.'' It is shown, also, that the general model encompasses the unidimensional model considered by Scurfield (1996). Next, ROC surfaces are discussed, and it is shown how a set of ROC surfaces can be generated from the event-conditional probability distributions postulated by the general model. In a further section, the volumes under the ROC surfaces are interpreted with respect to these probability distributions. Using this interpretation, it is shown that the definition of discriminability considered by Scurfield can be carried over without change. Finally, extensions of TSD to observers who maximize utility functions such as the expected payoff are considered. 

THE GENERAL MODEL 

Definition of Tasks 

In the single-interval n-alternative forced-choice (I1An) task, an observer attempts to discriminate among a set of n events, denoted [e1 , e2 , ..., en]. The task consists of a number of trials, each of which contains three well-defined time intervals: an observation interval, a decision interval, and a payoff interval. During the observation interval one event from the set of n events occurs. During the decision interval the observer decides which event occurred and selects one decision from a set of n decisions, denoted [r1 , r2 , ..., rn]. The decisions are indexed so that, for each i, ri indicates that the observer decided ei occurred during the observation interval. During the payoff interval the observer may receive payoffs or knowledge of results. The fundamental detection task of TSD (see Egan, 1975) is equivalent to the I1 A2 task. To simplify the exposition, this paper focuses on the I1 A3 task.

SUMMARY AND CONCLUSIONS 

This paper presented a generalization of TSD to multiple-event forced-choice tasks where the evidence can be modelled by an m-dimensional evidence vector. By considering m-dimensional vectors, the paper extended the results of Scurfield (1996), where unidimensional evidence variables were considered. 

For the I1A3 task, a general nonparametric model of discrimination was presented. In the model, an observer is assumed to make a decision by mapping the evidence to a two-dimensional space of criteria, called the decision space. After transforming the evidence, the observer partitions the decision space into regions by referring to certain values of the criteria and selects the decision ri if the transformed evidence lies in the region corresponding with ri . The boundaries of the partition were collectively referred to as the decision template. It was shown that the general model encompasses observers who use log-likelihood ratio variables to maximize the proportion of correct decisions. 

By manipulating the criteria, the decision template can be translated in the decision space, thereby changing the observer's decisional bias. The variation of the probabilities of the observer's event-decision matrix with respect to the criteria can be represented by six ROC surfaces. Theorem 1 showed that, given a set of ROC surfaces, the entire set of event-decision matrices can be reconstructed. Theorems 2 and 3 showed that the volumes under the ROC surfaces can be interpreted as the probabilities of obtaining particular mappings between the events and the decisions. Corollary 1 showed that the set of volumes sum to unity. Theorem 4 equated the volumes under the ROC surfaces to the entries in the event-decision matrix of an observer in the I3 A6 task. This theorem is an extension of Green's (1964) theorem equating the area under the ROC curve to the proportion of correct decisions in a two-interval forced-choice task. 

Following Scurfield (1996), a global measure of discriminability was defined by subtracting the Shannon entropy of the volumes from the maximum possible entropy. The resultant measure was shown to be nonparametric and independent from the observer's criteria. Theorem 5 equated the discriminability measure to the channel capacity of an unbiased observer in the I3 A6 task. Local measures of discriminability were defined in a similar way to the global measure by considering the areas of the ROC curves associated with the ROC surfaces. Theorems 6 and 7 showed that if the decision space is the log-likelihood ratio space, then the local measures are distance measures and each is no greater than the global measure. 

Finally, it was shown that the performance of an observer who maximizes the expected payoff cannot be analyzed within the framework described in this paper.
