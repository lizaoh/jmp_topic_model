INTRODUCTION  Learning Theory is the study of systems that implement functions from evidential states to theories. Of chief concern is the  characterization of conditions under which such functions stabilize to accurate theories of a given environment. Within  Learning Theory, the informal notions of "evidence," "theory," "stabilization," "accuracy," and "environment" give way to  precise definitions. Alternative formulations of these concepts yield distinct models within the theory. The vigorous  development of Learning Theory began with a celebrated paper by Gold (1967).  In the course of their fruitful study of learning functions, Blum & Blum (1975) proved a lemma that has been central to much  subsequent work; the proof offered for the lemma has a constructive character. The purpose of this paper is to place the Blum  & Blum lemma in fresh perspective by exhibiting it as a consequence of rather general, topological considerations. Attention  is initially restricted to text identification, one of a variety of learning paradigms introduced by Gold (1967); the Blum &  Blum lemma as originally stated is modified for this context. We also weaken the lemma somewhat by dispensing with effectivity  issues central to the original work.  The discussion proceeds as follows. After reviewing the definitions and contruals proper to text identification (Section 1),  we state a version of the Blum & Blum lemma and prove it in the original manner (Section 2). An application of the lemma is  discussed in Section 3. Section 4 reformulates and proves the Blum & Blum lemma in topological terms. The utility of the  topological perspective is discussed in Section 5.  The second author was partially supported by NSF Grant MCS 80-02937.
