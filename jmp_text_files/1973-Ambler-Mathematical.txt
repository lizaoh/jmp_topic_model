JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 10, 364-386 (1973)  A Mathematical Model of Learning under Schedules of Interresponse Time Reinforcement  STEPHEN AMBLER*  University of Stirling,t Scotland  A model is proposed for free responding under schedules of interresponse time reinforcement. Each response generates a pattern  of stimuli that changes in an orderly way over time. At any time the changing pattern may be sampled, and the state of  conditioning of the sampled pattern determines whether or not a response actually occurs. The prediction of this model for the  asymptotic distribution of interresponse times is derived. The predictions are tested against data from ratio, interval, and  DRL schedules of reinforcement. The fit of the model is sufficiently good to make it worthwhile continuing investigation of  the model.  1. INTRODUCTION  In 1966, a paper by Norman appeared, which showed that the commonest types of reinforcement schedule (ratio, interval, and  DRL, (differential reinforcement of low rates of responding)) could all be described as schedules of interresponse time (IRT)  reinforcement. For interresponse time reinforcement schedules, a function u(t), chosen by the experimenter, gives the  probability that a response, which occurs after an IRT of duration t, is reinforced. AN INFORMAL DESCRIPTION  Assume that any response generates, or is associated with, a pattern of stimuli X that change over time. If t denotes the time  since the last response occurred, the pattern found at time t will be denoted by X(t). The changing pattern X(t) can be  conceptualized in many ways-a stimulus trace, the reading of some internal clock, the position in a chain of collateral  responses-the particular conceptualization is not important. What is required, however, is that changes in X(t) are  consistent, in the sense that, after two responses, which are considered to be the same, the patterns X(t) are the same for  each corresponding t value for each response.  Perhaps the most general way to envisage the situation is to consider that X(t) consists of a set of stimuli. Changes in X(t)  can then be described as the appearance, or disappearance, of stimuli from this set. A stimulus is available as a component of  a pattern for a period of time. At any time t since the last response, X(t) is the characteristic set of all the then  available stimuli. These sets are not all unique for different values of t, e.g. X(t) might be the same as X(t -}- 8t). The predictions for the DRL schedules show a striking peak in the region of the cutoff point. The interesting thing to note is  that if the effect of reinforcement is greater than that for nonreinforcement, then the peak tends to lie to the left of the  cutoff value. A large number of the IRT's then emitted are just less than the value required for reinforcement, and  performance is relatively inefficient. If, however, the effect of nonreinforcement is greater than the effect of  reinforcement, then the peak tends to lie to the right of the cutoff value, most IRT's are reinforced and performance is  relatively efficient. One of the characteristic differences between pigeon and rat performance on DRL schedules is that  pigeons tend to produce an IRT distribution whose peak is to the left of the cutoff point, while rats tend to produce an IRT  distribution whose peak is to the right of the cutoff point. Interpreted through the framework of the present model, this  indicates that rats are relatively more sensitive to the effects of nonreinforcement than are pigeons and suggests, for  example, that superstitious conditioning (Herrnstein, 1966) should be more frequent in occurrence for pigeons, where the  effects of the occasional spurious reinforcement will only slowly extinguish through nonreinforcement, than for rats.  The IRT distributions obtained on DRL schedules often show a peak close to zero. The model in its present form does not  predict this extra peak. There are a number of possible ways to adapt the model to account for this peak, e.g. let the "1" and  "0" of Eqs. (3) and (5) be replaced by other values between 0.0 and 1.0 or, alternatively, assume that the pattern of stimuli  generated by a response occasionally fails to appear, so that sampling continues from the pattern associated with the  penultimate response. These alternatives are developed in Ambler (1972). Since the data used here to test the model does not  show the peak near zero, they are not developed further.  4. THE EXPERIMENT  Two human subjects were run on each of a random ratio, a random interval, and a DRL-like schedule. The subjects were  imprisoned in a small cubicle for ten daily sessions of 45 min each. The subjects were seated before a response box that  contained a single push button, a red pilot light, a green pilot light, and a counter. The green  SCHEDULES OF INTERRESPONSE TIME REINFORCEMENT 377  light came on to indicate a reinforcement (a correct response) and the red light to indicate a nonreinforcement. The counter  gave the total number of correct responses. The subjects were not informed of the schedule they were on, but simply told to  score as many points as possible, by pressing and releasing the button in some fashion. Subjects were paid at the rate of  .30 (-$0.75 American) per session.  The IRT's and a code for reinforcement/nonreinforcement were recorded on paper tape. The data from the tenth session was  regarded as asymptotic data and analyzed by grouping the IRT's into class intervals of one half-second width. The results for  each schedule are discussed separately in the next section.  5. RESULTS  A. CONCLUSION  The model given in the preceeding sections is, in spite of its discrete interpretation in stimulus sampling terms, essentially  a linear model. It describes learning by linear transformations of a bounded response strength function. It has had a fair  amount of success in describing the data collected. However, several problems do arise. This is often much more messy (i.e., multimodal)  than that obtained in the experiments described here. Some of these extra peaks could roughly be accounted for in a number of  possible ways.  Random ratio schedules have at least one peak, close to zero, which reflects the finite time required for the subject to  produce the response. This "response time," which was completely neglected here, may be the greatest influence on the IRT  distribution obtained under ratio schedules (Ray and McGill, 1964; Ambler, 1972).  Multimodal effects on other schedules could arise from at least two sources. One of these would be a failure to reset the  stimulus continuum after a response. The subject would then behave as if the previous response had not occurred. This would  lead, for example, to what are recorded as short IRT's on a DRL schedule. The other source of multimodal effects could lie in  the nature of the stimulus continuum itself. It has been tacitly assumed that the underlying stimuli are unique, in the  386 AMBLER  sense that they occur only once along the stimulus continuum. Some of the stimuli may, especially if they are genuinely  "temporal" stimuli, e.g. heartbeats, reappear at periodic positions along the continuum. Any such periodicity would presumably  manifest itself in a periodicity in the IRT distribution.  Sequential effects are somewhat difficult to handle with the model. These effects are, in contrast to the overall IRT  distribution, sensitive to the nature of the spread function. A general prediction (the proof is simple but tedious) is that  IRT's which follow reinforcement are on the average shorter than the overall mean IRT, while those which follow  nonreinforcement are on the average longer than the mean IRT. Such a property seems intuitively desirable, since it  corresponds with general expectations about the properties of reinforcement and nonreinforcement. Roughly stated,  reinforcement speeds up the rate of responding, while nonreinforcement slows it down. However, such behavior is maladaptive on  DRL schedules, where this property leads to the prediction that reinforcement is less likely to follow a reinforcement than it  is a nonreinforcement. This prediction contradicts the available evidence (Farmer and Schoenfeld, 1964).  Taken overall, the model described here has had a moderate success in handling the data presented and seems to merit further  investigation.
