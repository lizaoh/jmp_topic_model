Cross-Validation Methods 

Michael W. Browne 

The Ohio State University 

This paper gives a review of cross-validation methods. The original applications in multiple linear regression are considered first. It is shown how predictive accuracy depends on sample size and the number of predictor variables. Both two-sample and single-sample cross-validation indices are investigated. The application of cross-validation methods to the analysis of moment structures is then justified. An equivalence of a single-sample crossvalidation index and the Akaike information criterion is pointed out. It is seen that the optimal number of parameters suggested by both single-sample and two-sample cross-validation indices will depend on sample size. � 2000 Academic Press 

1. INTRODUCTION 

Cross-validation was originally employed to evaluate the predictive validity of linear regression equations used to forecast a performance criterion from scores on a battery of tests (e.g., Mosier, 1951). It was found that the multiple correlation coefficient within the original sample used to assign values to regression weights gave an optimistic impression of the predictive effectiveness of the regression equation when applied to future observations. In order to investigate this phenomenon the cross-validation procedure was employed. Two samples from the same population were drawn. The first, the calibration sample, was used to calibrate the regression equation, that is, to assign values to the regression weights. The weighted linear composite of predictors, with previously calibrated weights, was then correlated with the criterion in a second sample, the validation sample. The crossvalidation index so obtained yielded a realistic impression of the predictive effectiveness of the linear composite of tests. Other cross-validation indices, such as mean square errors, can also be used. 

This two-sample form of cross-validation can be wasteful because only about half of the available observations are used for calibration purposes leading to less effective calibrations. The remaining observations are required for the validation sample. Attempts to increase the number of observations available for calibration purposes have followed two main directions. In one, a single cross-validation with two samples of approximately the same size is replaced with repeated cross-validations based on the same data set with large calibration samples and small validation

In its simplest form, the leaving one out at a time method, this involves parti tioning a sample of size N into a calibration sample of size N&1 and a validation sample of size 1 and repeating the N process times. An average of the N crossvalidation index values is then used. Historical details and various extensions are given in Stone (1974) and Geisser (1975). The leaving one out at a time method can be extended to the leaving some out at a time method, where validation samples of size greater than one are used (Geisser, 1975). Neither the original two-sample form of cross-validation nor the leaving some out at a time method involves distributional assumptions about the data. 

In the other approach employed to increase the number of observations used for calibration purposes, the expected value of the cross-validation index is estimated directly from the calibration sample (e.g., Burket, 1963; Darlington, 1968; Browne, - 1975a, 1975b). Additional distributional assumptions not involved in the two sample approach have to be made for the evaluation of the expected value. No validation sample at all is required in this single sample approach. 

The two-sample cross-validation approach was originally intended for regression. It was applied to the analysis of models for covariance matrices in Cudeck and Browne (1983). Expected values of the cross-validation indices were subsequently obtained under normality assumptions in Browne and Cudeck (1989). This facilitated the use of the single sample approach mentioned above. Although these expected cross-validation indices were presented in the context of the analysis of covariance structures, they are applicable to the analysis of moment structures in general. It will be seen later that the single sample results are also applicable to models for mean structures including models for proportions. 

The Akaike information criterion (Akaike, 1973) or AIC is a general criterion for the assessment of the fit of probability models. De Leeuw (1989) has clearly pointed out in what sense the AIC is a single-sample estimate of an expected cross-validation criterion based on the Kullback�Leibler measure of the discrepancy between two distributions. A general framework for the assessment of the fit of probability models that includes the AIC is given by Linhart and Zucchini (1986) and by Zucchini (2000). In the analysis of moment structures, the single sample estimate of an expected cross-validation criterion given by Browne and Cudeck (1989) is equivalent to the AIC when maximum likelihood estimation is being employed. Stone (1977) has shown an asymptotic equivalence of the AIC to the leaving one out at a time cross-validation procedure in a particular situation where the data distribution assumed by the AIC is correctly specified. This distributional assumption is not required by the leaving one out at a time procedure. 

The aim of this paper is to present an objective evaluation of the use of crossvalidation for the assessment of structural models for moment matrices. Exactly what its purpose is and when it should be used will be examined. We shall also point out where cross-validation is not helpful and consider what it cannot be used for. 

Some preliminary comments on model selection are made in Section 2. Section 3 presents an overview of the use of cross-validation for the assessment of accuracy of prediction from a regression equation. This provides a background for its use in model selection. Two different indices of predictive accuracy are examined: a correlation index is considered in Subsection 3.1 and a mean squared error index

2. GENERAL CONSIDERATIONS IN MODEL SELECTION 

Before examining cross-validation and its applications in model selection, it will be convenient to consider the general topic of model assessment in practice and to specify clearly what is feasible and what is not. 

Every model we are likely to consider will be wrong to some extent. This implies that seeking the correct model is searching for an impossibility. Rather than seeking a nonexistent correct model, therefore, we should be seeking a model that is a plausible approximation to reality and conveys insight. A good model will aid one's understanding of the situation under investigation. For this to be possible it should (i) be clearly understood by the investigator and (ii) be appropriate for the situation investigated so that it should fit reasonably well. Many indices of model fit have been developed. These consider the second property of a good model but do not, and cannot, address the first. No index will ever measure the extent to which a model aids understanding. It is usually easier to understand a parsimonious model, that is, one with few parameters, so that parsimonious models are preferable. A fit index can encourage parsimony by incorporating a penalty for the number of parameters. It should be borne in mind, however, that choosing a model according to the value of a fit index incorporating a penalty for the number of parameters cannot guarantee a good model. Rather we hope that by seeking only amongst parsimonious models we are more likely to find a model that makes sense. Human judgment alone can evaluate this. No mechanical data analytic procedure for evaluating model fit should therefore either replace or override human judgment. 

It is difficult to evaluate a model on its own without any reference points. Consequently it is desirable to consider a set of several competing models simultaneously, compare them and come to a decision on which to retain. We shall therefore be concerned primarily with the choice of a model from a set of competing models rather than with a decision whether or not a single model is a good one. This choice of a model may be influenced by measures of fit but the final decision concerning which model to use must involve human judgment.

4. APPLICATIONS OF CROSS-VALIDATION FOR THE ASSESSMENT OF MODELS FOR MOMENT MATRICES 

The early uses of cross-validation were for the investigation of prediction equations but, more recently, they have been used for model selection purposes. This 

120 

MICHAEL W. BROWNE 

section will concentrate on the use of cross-validation in the investigation of models for moment matrices. Both applications of cross-validation involve a sample-based calibration of the model or assignment of values to parameters. In the choice of a prediction equation, the interpretation of parameters is not considered and the main emphasis is on accuracy of prediction. Moment models are not intended for prediction. The main emphasis of cross-validation then is to investigate the appropriateness of the sample-based calibration of a model for the population under consideration. A model can yield a good fit in the calibration sample, C, because peculiarities of the sample due to the effect of sampling are accounted for by the calibration of the model. This phenomenon is known as capitalization on chance and occurs particularly when the number of parameters is large and N C is small. It may be detected by examining the fit of the calibration of the model obtained from C to an independent validation sample, V. Since V will not have the same random peculiarities as C, the calibration obtained from C need not fit V well. The rationale underlying cross-validation in model assessment is that, if the calibration from C does not predict future observations reasonably well, one can assume that this calibration has been influenced by random peculiarities of C. It is then inadvisable to generalize findings based on C to the population. 

Because the effect of capitalization on chance is relative, and there is no critical value beyond which fit is unacceptable, it is helpful to carry out cross-validations on a set of competing models with different numbers of parameters. At least one of these competing models should have too few parameters and the saturated model, with the maximum number of parameters, should be included. The purpose of cross-validation no longer is to select calibrations to be used for predictive purposes, but to select calibrations that can be trusted because the effect of capitalization on chance is not noticeable. If N C is truly large, and the effect of random fluctuations in the data may be disregarded as unimportant, cross-validation will not be helpful.

The discrepancy function that will be referred to is an arbitrary function with the two previously specified properties. 4.7. Comparison between One- and Two-Sample Methods 

The two-sample cross-validation procedure involving a calibration sample and a validation sample has the disadvantage that it does not use all possible observations for calibration purposes resulting in a poorer calibration. On the other hand, the two-sample procedure also has advantages over the single-sample procedure. First, the two-sample procedure does not involve any distributional assumptions that may be unrealistic in some situations. Second, it is not restricted to the use of the correctly specified discrepancy functions of the single-sample procedure. These are used because they have convenient distributional properties, not because they represent error of approximation in a meaningful manner. Users of the two-sample procedure are free to choose any discrepancy function that they believe reflects error of approximation meaningfully. Finally the two-sample procedure may be used in conjunction with data snooping, that is in situations where sequential modifications are made to the model to improve its fit in the calibration sample. For example, model modifications may be suggested by the inspection of residuals obtained from the calibration sample. Data snooping results in selection bias (see Zucchini, 2000) that leads to an optimistic assessment of fit in the single-sample procedures. It does not affect the two-sample cross-validation procedure, provided 

128 

MICHAEL W. BROWNE 

that the validation sample is employed only after all model modifications have been tried and does not influence the choice of modifications.

5. CONCLUDING COMMENTS 

No numerical index computed from data can convey direct information about the interpretability of a model. Parsimonious models are, however, frequently more easily interpretable than highly parameterized models. Some indices, such as the RMSEA (Steiger, 1989, Browne 6 Cudeck, 1993) encourage parsimony of a model by penalizing 

130 

MICHAEL W. BROWNE 

models with many parameters. Hopefully, an interpretable model will more easily be found if the fit index one uses draws one's attention to parsimonious models. Crossvalidationindices are not, however, in the class of indices that seek parsimoniousmodels. While cross-validation indices, and their single sample variations such as the AIC, may sometimes appear to favor parsimonious models, this is not always true. The purpose of cross-validation is not to select a parsimonious model, but to encourage the selection of a model that yields a trustworthy calibration from the available sample. When the calibration sample is small the most accurate estimates of population moment matrices will tend to be provided by models with few parameters. In fact, if the calibration sample is sufficiently small, the most accurate estimates can be provided by models that are clearly oversimplified. When the calibration sample is large the converse is true. Expected values of cross-validation indices in (45) have an additive term that increases as the number of parameters increases, but decreases as sample size increases. If the calibration sample is sufficiently large, the effect of this additive term is negligible and highly parameterized models can be expected to yield the most favorable values of cross-validation indices (Section 4.8). 

Cross-validation procedures are appropriate for situations where no model in the set of competing models under consideration is perfectly correct. Any attempt to use cross-validation to detect which of a set of models is correct disregards the fundamental intent of the approach. This is to find a model that yields as small an overall discrepancy as is possible given a specific sample size, not to find a model with no error of approximation. In other words, the intent is to find a model that yields a calibration that is worth further examination despite a possibly small sample, not to seek a correct model with an unknown calibration. 

While the two-sample cross-validation procedure is wasteful in that more or less half of the available observations are omitted from the calibration process, it has a major advantage (Section 4.7) when it is used in conjunction with data snooping. Whereas the single-sample procedures are affected by selection bias induced by data snooping, the two-sample procedure is not.
