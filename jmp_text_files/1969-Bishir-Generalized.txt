This model considers the interaction of many simple Bush-Mosteller choice situations. INTRODUCTION  The mathematical models for learning proposed by Bush and Mosteller (1955) and by Estes (1950) have had considerable impact on  psychological learning theory since their introduction in the early 1950's. Briefly, using the Bush-Mosteller formulation, the  models consider a choice situation in which there is a set 67 of r mutually exclusive and exhaustive alternative choices A1 ,  A2 ,..., A,. . Whatever alternative occurs, it will have one of the m outcomes Ol , 02 ,..., 0 . For each choice of k and t,  the alternative-outcome pair (Ak , Ot) is called the event Ekt .  It is assumed that the set OL of alternatives is repeatedly presented to an organism in a sequence of trials. At the nth trial  the organism possesses a (column) probability vector p(n) whose kth component, pk(n), (1 < k <_ r) is the probability that  alternative Ak is chosen on trial n. We propose to generalize this model along the lines implicit in the description by Mitchie (1961) of a machine which is  capable of "learning" to play the game of tic-tac-toe. His machine, christened MENACE (Matchbox Educable Naughts and Crosses  Engine), consists of some three hundred match boxes containing a number of different colored beads, each box corresponding to  a particular configuration which the machine can encounter during the course of a play' of the game. The probabilistic element  is introduced by randomly selecting a bead from the appropriate box, the color of the bead indicating the position in which  the next mark is to be made. For each configuration encountered during the course of a particular play, probabilities are  altered by adding or removing beads depending upon the outcome of the play. Specifically, if the play is won, three additional  beads of the same color as that chosen are added, while if the play is lost, the chosen bead is not replaced. In case of a  tie, the bead is replaced and one additional bead inserted. Thus, winning and tying choices are "rewarded" and losing choices  "punished." Configurations which are not encountered incur no alteration of probability. Mitchie indicates that in a test  sequence of plays the machine learned to play a winning (or nonlosing) game.  In Sec. CONVERGENCE PROPERTIES  In order to establish that the model described above is an operative one, it is necessary to show that P, playing according to  the dictates of the model, will "learn" to make sequences of choices which lead to a win. In the subsequent development, the  following additional assumption wil be required:  128 BISHIR AND DREWES  11. Player P possesses a winning strategy. That is to say, there exists for P a way of playing (making his choices) by which  he can win no matter what his opponent may choose to do. Of course, this is not to say that P will win on a particular play,  as the probability structure allows for choices which are not optimal. However, Theorem 5 below guarantees that as the number  of plays increases, P's probability of winning approaches unity, regardless of initial assignments of probabilities.  In the setting of a particular game, the collection of winning sequences-that is, sequences from each point of which P may  win, regardless of O's choices, by making appropriate subsequent choices-will, in general, consist of two distinct types.  1. Those sequences from each point of which, owing to the structure and rules of the game, P can force a win. Of course, he  may fail to win by making undesirable choices.  2. Sequences for which a win occurs independently of the game structure, e.g., for which opponent O is programmed to lose, or  loses deliberately.  For our purposes it is not necessary to distinguish between these two types of sequences. It is possible that O may be  programmed to play in such a manner that sequences of Type 2 exist in the absence of those of Type 1. That is, if O played  "properly" he could ensure a win for himself, but his actual method of play allows P to win on certain sequences of choices. PSYCHOLOGICAL IMPLICATIONS  An experimental paradigm corresponding to the generalized learning model described herein can be developed as follows:  Consider a sequential learning situation characterized by at most N stages. At each stage, i, there is a collection of sets of  LINEAR LEARNING MODEL 137  response alternatives {{Ail!: j = l, 2,..., ri} where {A& _ {Ai,l , Ai.2 ,..., Ai.T;} is the set of response alternatives  corresponding to a particular stimulus situation encountered at the ith stage. For a given trial, the subject encounters a  specific sequence of sets of alternatives {A,}'1 {A2}'2 __* - --)- {An}'-. The set of alternatives encountered at the ith  stage is determined by the stimulus situation generated by previous choices.  A probability distribution is defined over each set of response alternatives, the specific pattern of alternatives chosen thus  being stochastically determined. The distribution of response probabilities over each of the sets of alternatives  corresponding to the stimulus situation encountered, is altered by linear operators whose specific form depends upon the  alternative chosen at stage i on trial n and the outcome of the trial.  The experimental paradigm then is seen as characterizing a sequential stage learning situation where the stimulus situation is  deliberately altered from stage to stage. The changing stimulus situation may be provided by an experimenter, machine, states  of nature, opposing subject-in short, any entity capable of generating responses which constitute the stimulus situation for  the subject.  In this context, the generalized learning model holds promise for the analysis of situations in which the subject is required  to develop a strategy to cope with a variety of stimulus situations. The effect of operator parameters on response convergence  can be observed by altering the elements of the operator matrices thereby allowing considerable flexibility in the  approximation of hypothetical underlying psychological processes.  An exemplary type of learning situation is trouble shooting, or the diagnosing of malfunctions, which is generally  sequentially performed. The set of response alternatives available to the subject at each stage is a function of the stimulus  situation provided by the equipment, usually in response to diagnostic tests made by the subject. Outcomes are defined as  mutually exclusive states of equipment performance determined according to external criteria. Learning in this context refers  to the development over trials of a strategy of response choices which for every possible stimulus situation results in the  desired outcomes.  The learning of complex mazes illustrates a further application of the theory. Here the organism can be thought of as P, the  sequence of configurations encountered during a trial being determined by the behavior at the choice points.s  The foregoing discussion is primarily intended to extend the domain of the generalized learning model beyond the physical  confines of board games to the more general game-theoretic approach.
