Signed Orders, Choice Probabilities, and Linear Polytopes 

Peter C. Fishburn 

AT 6 T Labs-Research 

Self-reflecting signed orders were proposed as an aid in estimating preference between subsets of items on the basis of limited information. The data of a signed order are preference comparisons that consider the desirability of excluding items from a subset as well as including items in a subset. This paper investigates signed orders within the setting of probabilistic choice and random utility theory. It focuses on linear signed order polytopes and their relationship to binary choice probabilities. The theory applies both to individual choice behavior and to group decision making. � 2001 Academic Press 

1. INTRODUCTION 

Self-reflecting signed orders were introduced in Fishburn (1992a) at the suggestion of Steven Brams as a basis for estimating preferences between subsets of items in a finite set S=[1, 2, ..., n], where i # S could signify a potential member of a committee, an item on a menu, a capability that might be included in a software package, or a project that might be funded by an agency. They were motivated by two practical concerns, a size problem and an interdependence problem. The size problem refers to the fact that the number of subsets of S can be huge even when S is of modest size (2[20] >1, 000, 000 for n=20), and unless very special conditions obtain it is unreasonable to suppose that a person could order by preference all subsets of S. The interdependence problem refers to the fact that subset values or utilities are often affected by substantial interdependencies among items, owing for example to substitutabilities, complementarities, and a desire for diversity or representativeness, whereas most tractable procedures for estimating subset preferences are based solely on individual item valuations or simple preference comparisons between items with no regard to evaluative interactions within subsets. Recent articles that augment references in Fishburn (1992a) to rules for extending simple preferences to subset preferences include Fishburn and LaValle (1993), Bossert,

6. DISCUSSION 

The primary aim of this paper has been to develop basic theory for linear signed order polytopes. In decision-theoretic terms, such a polytope describes all systems of binary choice probabilities or binary choice fractions of populations over ordered pairs from a set of items and their anti-items which satisfy p(x, y)+ p( y, x)=1 and are consistent with probability distributions over linear signed orders. Because the notion of self-reflecting signed orders is fairly new, their structures and their relationship to subset-preference extensions were described. Further background was provided for linear polytopes with emphasis on the extensively studied linear ordering polytopes. 

It was shown that the linear signed order polytope Qn with ground set [1, 2, ..., n] _ [1*, 2*, ..., n*] has dimension n[2] as a polytope in R[2][n][(2][n][&1)] and that a complete equation system for Qn is summarized by [ p(x, y)= p( y*, x*), p(x, y)+ p( y, x)=1]. The description of Qn is completed by a finite set of facetdefining inequalities. We identified all such FDIs for n=1 and n=2 and gave a possibly complete description of FDIs for n=3.

A primary question left open is whether there are other facet-defining inequalities for n=3. Of related interest is the discovery of FDIs that are nonvacuous only for larger values of n. In time, we might hope to have a complete FDI characterization of Q4 and perhaps even Q5 . 

Another important concern is the development of polytope theory for weak orders in which equation p(x, y)+ p( y, x)=1 is replaced by p(x, y)+ p( y, x)�1. This allows for a no-choice option or expressed indifference in binary comparisons on both individual and group levels. At the present time, very little seems to be known about what might be referred to as weak order polytopes.
