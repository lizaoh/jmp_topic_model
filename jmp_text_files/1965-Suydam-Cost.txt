JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY: 2, 171-179 (1965)  Effects of Cost and Gain Ratios, and Probability of Outcome on Ratings of Alternative Choices'  MARY M. SUYDAM  University of Massachusetts, Amherst, Massachusetts  On each of 45 trials, subjects were required to choose between two events and to rate the strength of their choice along a  9-point rating scale. The events on each trial represented a different combination of gain ratio, cost ratio, and probability  of occurrence. The experimental design provided for the control of effects due to repeated presentation of options, and no  exchange of chips or money took place. An analysis of variance of the rating scale data showed all main and interaction  effects to be significant (p G 0.001).  Two decision models are discussed in relation to their predictive power for the present data. A model based on the concept of  regret accurately described not only the rank ordering of the mean ratings but also the pattern of all main and interaction  effects.  The major purpose of the present study was to determine the effects of cost and gain ratios, and probability of outcomes on  choice behavior. In most previous experiments on choice behavior, the same two choices were presented on every trial. In this  experiment, each combination of cost, gain, and probability was presented just once. Another departure from the conditions of  most choice experiments was the replacement of simple choice between bets with a more sensitive dependent variable, that of  rating the strength of each choice.  A number of experiments on choice behavior (Edwards, 1956; Myers, Reilly, & Taub, 1961; Taub & Myers, 1961) have involved  nonsymmetric payoff matrices where either gain, cost or both were varied differentially. Under these conditions, the gain  received by the subject depended upon which event was correctly predicted, and the cost of an incorrect prediction also  depended on which event was predicted. Generally, it has been found that the asymptotic probability of event choice increases  as the difference in expected value (AEV) associated with the two responses increases.  1 This paper is based on a dissertation submitted in partial fulfillment of the requirements for the PhD. degree at the  University of Massachusetts, 1963. Edwards  (1956) has suggested that choice preference is a function of the difference in expected loss between two events. Loss is  defined as the amount which would have been obtained had the subject predicted the event which did occur minus the amount of  payoff for the subject's actual choice. His hypothesis accounted for the rank ordering of asymptotic probabilities of choice  both for his data (Edwards, 1956) and that of Taub and Myers (1961).  In the experiments just described, each group of subjects experienced the same payoff matrix repeatedly. Under repeated  presentation, choice is a function not only of payoff parameters but also of sequential effects, feedback, and set. Thus,  choice on a given trial is influenced by the pattern of stimuli, responses, and payoffs on immediately preceding trials  (Jarvik, 1951; Anderson, 1959; Anderson & Whalen, 1960; Myers & Fort, 1963). Also, as reported in a pilot study (Coombs &  Komorita, 1958), choice behavior of the subjects offered a few options repeatedly differed from that of the subjects offered  each of several options just once.  Sequential effects can be eliminated effectively by a technique of single presentation of options (Suydam & Myers, 1962). On  each trial, the subjects were required to choose between a gamble and a known alternative to gambling, and to rate the  strength of their choice. Until all bets were completed, the subjects were given no information regarding outcomes. The  single-presentation technique permitted the use of more levels of the independent variables with a larger number of subjects  than is usually feasible with the repeated-presentation technique. Despite the several differences in conditions under the two  techniques, Suydam and Myer's (1962) rating scale data paralleled those of other experiments with similar independent  variables, but with simple choice between events as the dependent measure (Myers & Sadler, 1960; Katz, 1962). Their findings  also confirmed previous results (Anderson & Whalen, 1960) which suggested that rating scales can provide a more sensitive  measure of preference than choice alone.  In the present study, the single-presentation technique was employed to investigate ratings of strength of choice as a  function of gain ratio, cost ratio, and probability of outcomes. Comparisons are made among data obtained from sequential  choice situations, and data from the present, single presentation situation. In addition, the applicability of two decision  models is considered.  METHOD  Experimental Design and Materials. Forty-five matrices representing 45 pairs of options were constructed by using all  combinations of the probabilities, cost ratios, and gain ratios shown in Table 1. Each matrix appeared at the top of a  separate page with the 9-point rating scale below. The matrices were arranged in random order and then assembled in 45-page  booklets. The order of the matrices in each booklet represented a different sequence of the original random order. At the beginning of the experimental session, the subjects were shown a sample matrix and told they were to decide  on each trial whether they preferred to bet on R or B to win. The several possible gains, losses, and odds were explained.  They were also told that the strength of each decision was to be indicated on the rating scale. It was emphasized that each  new decision was to be made independently from previous decisions. The subjects were also told that the object of the  experiment was to win as large an amount as possible, but that no actual exchange of money would take place.  The time allotted for completion of the booklets was 20 minutes. All the subjects were able to finish within this limit which  was predetermined on the basis of time records from pilot subjects. In order to show the subjects the results of their  decisions, several bets were run off on a roulette wheel at the end of the experimental session.  Subjects. Eighty-five males enrolled in the elementary psychology course at the University of Massachusetts were selected  randomly.  RESULTS AND DISCUSSION  Means and standard deviations of ratings are presented in Table 1; the means are plotted in the upper panel of Figure 1.  Described first are effects of cost and gain ratios, and probabilities on mean ratings. Then the relationships between ratings  and values predicted by two decision models are examined.  As shown in Fig. 1, mean ratings decreased as the probability of red (P) decreased, and as cost ratios (C) and gain ratios (G)  increased. In the analysis of variance on ratings, all three variables were significant (p < 0.001). At each combination of C  and G, the mean rating of Red (R) decreased as P decreased. Mean ratings decreased rapidly as P decreased to 0.5, then more  slowly as P approached 1. Mean ratings of R also decreased as the cost of an incorrect choice of R increased, and as the gain  on a correct choice of Black (B) increased.  The interactions of P and C, of P and G, of C and G, and of all three variables were also significant (p < 0.001). When P  equaled 0.9, mean ratings were highest for 1:1 C, with 1:5C and 1:10C following in order, but as P decreased, differences in  mean ratings as a function of C also decreased. Variability among the mean ratings at the three levels of G also decreased as  P decreased from 0.9 to 0.1. As C increased from 1:1 to 1:10, the effects of G decreased. Using similar variables in a sequential choice situation, Myers, Reilly, and Taub (1961) found that their data could be  described by the differences in Expected Value (dEV) associated with the two responses. They obtained a nearly linear  relationship between dEV values and asymptotic probability of choice. In contrast, the mean ratings of the present study were  a positively accelerated function of JEV values (see Table 1) with marked variability about the estimated regression line. The  differences in results between the two experiments are very likely due to differences between single  176 SUYDAM  and repeated-presentation techniques. In a sequential-choice situation, the subject must estimate event probabilities by  observing the sequence of events, but with the single-presentation technique each probability is explicitly stated.  Furthermore, in the sequential choice situation, the subject is informed immediately of the outcome of each trial, and is  reminded constantly of the effects of gain and cost by his growing or diminishing pile of chips. In contrast, the present  procedure eliminated feedback and monetary incentive: the subject made each choice in the same state of ignorance and was  aware that no real money was involved. Thus, in the present experiment, the subject was not "forced" to change his pattern of  behavior in response to a shrinking pile of chips nor was he required to learn probabilities over trials. Although the  relative effects of these procedural differences cannot be evaluated, such differences would be expected to affect choice  behavior and could account for the lack of fit of the AEV values.  One alternative to AEV is the Relative Expected Loss Minimization (RELM) rule proposed by Edwards (1956), and based on the  concept of loss or regret. The loss involved in risk taking is defined as the amount which would have been obtained had the  subject predicted the event which did occur minus the amount of payoff for the subject's actual choice. All further discussion, therefore, centers about the ELR model.  The ELR values accurately predicted not only the rank ordering of the mean ratings, but also the shape of all main and  interaction effects as shown in the lower panel of Fig. 1. The only notable difference between prediction and mean rating is  to be found under the 1:10C, 0.9P condition where 1:5G has a higher mean rating than 1:1 G (Fig. 1, upper panel). The Pearson  product-moment correlation between ELR values and mean ratings was 0.97; the regression line for ELR ratings had a slope of  0.94 and an intercept of 8.56.  The ELR model is, so far, the only one which has provided accurate descriptions of data in both single-presentation and  sequential-choice situations (Edwards, 1956; Taub & Myers, 1961). The author computed ELR values for two additional studies in  order to further assess the validity of the ELR rule. In the first study to be considered (Suydam & Myers, 1962), the subjects  were required to choose between a gamble with 0.50-0.50 probability, and a known alternative to gambling, and to rate the  strength of their choice. The ELR rule correctly predicted the tendency of the known alternative curves to converge as the  monetary level of the gamble increased. In the second experiment (Myers, Reilly, & Taub, 1961), the ELR values accurately  predicted the rank ordering of groups with respect to choice probability; the only notable inversions were under conditions of  0.50-0.50 probability.  The success of loss models in predicting choice behavior under both sequentialchoice and single-presentation techniques  suggests that ELR must be sensitive to factors which are similar under both conditions and be unaffected by those that are  dissimilar. The model takes into account tendencies to prefer high probability of gain to low, larger gain to smaller gain,  and accounts also for the cost associated with an  12  178 SUYDAM  incorrect choice. The predictive power of the ELR model depends not only upon the parameters included but also upon the manner  in which they are included. Contained in the ELR model are four desirable features of models for predicting choice behavior.  First, since the definition of EL requires a loss matrix containing no negative quantities, except for the trivial case where  all entries in the matrix are identical, a zero denominator is impossible. Second, ELR has limits of 1 and 0. The limits of 1  and 0 occur in the two-choice situation only when event probability is equal to 1 or 0, or when one choice dominates in the  sense that it is preferred regardless of outcome. From knowledge of the limiting cases, it follows that if the subjects reach  an optimal strategy (rating of 9, or 100 % choice of the lower EL event) only in those cases designated above, the theoretical  regression line relating mean rating and ELR will be:  Mean rating = 1.0 + 8.0 (ELR).  The computed values offer a close approximation:  Mean rating = 0.94 + 8.56 (ELR).  Third, as gain on the alternative event (E2) increases by equal amounts at constant probability and cost, the denominator of  ELR increases by equal amounts, and the ratio decreases in negatively accelerated fashion. For constant probability and gain,  equal increases in cost for an incorrect prediction of E, also increase the denominator of ELR by equal amounts to produce a  negatively accelerated decrease in the ratio. Therefore, ELR is a decreasing, negatively accelerated function of both amount  of gain associated with a correct prediction of E, , and amount of cost associated with an incorrect prediction of E, . Although the ELR model has been applicable in a number of choice situations, it lacks the explanatory concepts necessary to  represent a theory of behavior. However, in the absence of more adequate models it does serve as a helpful descriptive device.  Further, other models (Myers & Atkinson, 1964; Schoefller, 1962) involving the concept of regret have obtained experimental  support. These, together with the present work suggest that regret plays an important role in choice behavior.  EFFECTS OF COST AND GAIN RATIOS 179  REFERENCES  ANDERSON, N. H.
