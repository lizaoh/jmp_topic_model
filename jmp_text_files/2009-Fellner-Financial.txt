The rational choice approach is still the most frequently applied model to decision making under risk and a cornerstone of modern finance theory. The virtue of the model is straightforward: based on the knowledge of individuals’ risk preferences, the rational choice approach offers a (usually) unique prediction of investment behavior, i.e., a specific portfolio choice. However, actual heterogeneity of individual portfolio choices often are in violation with such clear-cut predictions (e.g.,  Barberis and Thaler (2003) ,  Camerer (1995)  and  Hirshleifer (2001) ).

An alternative is offered by the concept of bounded rationality. Unfortunately, the literature on bounded rationality is equally vast as vague in its use of the notion “bounded”. It frequently relates to so-called anomalies as well as decision biases and heuristics (e.g.,  Conlisk (1996)  and  Gigerenzer and Goldstein (1996) ). In this paper we will use the term “bounded rationality” as proposed in the original work of  Simon (1955) , suggesting satisficing rather than optimizing behavior.

                         1 
                         Please note that our definition is not all-inclusive; it does not consider  any  behavior as boundedly rational. Unlike, for instance,  Gigerenzer and Todd (2003) , we think that actual behavior can sometimes be unreasonable, necessitating teaching and learning. 
                       Previous research in this tradition has mostly focused on aspiration adaptation ( Sauermann & Selten, 1962 ), and explored the impact of aspirations in bilateral interactions, like bargaining (see, for instance,  Selten (1998) ,  Tietz (1992, 1997)  and  Tietz, Weber, Vidmajer and Wentzel (1978) ).

Although the original work by  Simon (1955)  has been extensively cited, hardly any attempts have been undertaken to test the applicability of bounded rationality, and the role of aspirations in particular, empirically. This study is–to the best of our knowledge–the first attempt to directly test the avail of aspirations. We do this for the domain of investment decisions, although the general approach is applicable in a broader sense. More specifically, the major novelty is that–unlike earlier studies (e.g.,  Todd and Miller (2003) ) that infer aspirations from choice behavior similarly to the revealed preference approach in economics ( Samuelson, 1948 )–we directly elicit aspirations, which allows us to formally define (non-)optimal satisficing and test it experimentally. We are particularly concerned with two questions: first, can the central concept of bounded rationality theory, i.e., the idea of aspiration levels, predict how portfolio choices are made? And does this prediction leave enough ambiguity, e.g., by allowing for set-valued rather than unique predictions, to account for individual heterogeneity? Second, is there any empirical relation between aspirations, in the framework of bounded rationality, and risk attitude in the rational choice tradition? Such a relation would provide an important foundation for developing a meaningful risk concept within bounded rationality theory.

We address these questions by designing an experimental investment game. Participants in this game can invest their monetary endowments in riskless bonds and risky assets. Bonds yield a positive, but certain return, whereas assets yield either a low or a high return (with equal probability).                          Aspirations are also central in the SP/A theory ( Lopes, 1987; Lopesc & Oden, 1999 ). According to this theory, a lottery’s attractiveness is evaluated with respect to the probability with which the outcome of the lottery meets a pre-defined aspiration level. The theory also employs a (decumulatively) weighted value rule, assuming maximizing behavior. In contrast, our approach only relies on aspirations in the tradition of  Simon (1955) , entailing no maximization at all. Thus, our approach seems simpler, requiring fewer assumptions and requirements (in the sense of Occam’s razor). 
                       Satisficing does not exclude optimality; in fact, it includes it as a limit case. Our approach therefore relies on a more basic definition of optimality than expected utility maximization.

In an attempt to relate bounded rationality and rational choice theory in the sense of expected utility maximization, we also analyze the investment situation and our data from the latter perspective. We exemplify this by imputing quite arbitrarily a specific cardinal utility function, whose unique parameter captures risk preferences and is inferred from the actual investment choice.

In the introduction we stated that our definition of bounded rationality distinguishes not only from perfect rationality (except for very simple decisions where the two may coincide) but also does not allow for all possible behavior. In this sense we allow for investors with non-satisfiable aspiration profiles and predict for them that they shy away from major risks since they do not fulfill the requirements of bounded rationality (pattern (1)). To ensure the financial salience of the investment task, we took the following steps: first, only one period was randomly selected for payment. Second, the credit taken for investment had to be paid back after each period, so that subjects only earned their (positive or negative) net investment returns. Third, subjects were instructed that possible monetary losses would have to be covered by completing a “working” task after the experiment. This task consisted of marking all letters ‘t’ in a text on German tax law. Each Euro lost was equivalent to half a page of work load. The maximum possible loss of 16 Euro therefore translated into searching eight pages, which would take about 35 min of extra time after the experiment and would ensure that losses had to be actually “suffered”. In the experiment, 17 subjects made losses and left the laboratory only after completing an additional search task.

This study presents a theoretical framework that renders aspirations and satisficing applicable to a simple investment task. By experimentally testing the model, the paper is–to the best of our knowledge–the first direct study of the actual practicality of bounded rationality theory, in the strict sense of  Simon (1955) .

An important insight from the theoretical analysis is that the separation of preferences and choice alternatives, on which the rational choice approach is based, may not hold for bounded rationality. Aspirations in our interpretation are not just “discrete utilities” that represent goals or achievement levels, but they also represent restrictions of the action space: the minimum return aspiration

Our experimental approach allows for a direct test of whether investments are appropriate to satisfy aspirations. The findings on aspirations are related to a rational choice approach and thus provide some insights into a possibly new way to define risk attitudes within the bounded rationality framework. Additionally, a natural way of classifying investor types according to the revealed steps of reasoning about aspirations and suitable investments is offered.

The overall findings are encouraging. Although aspiration choices are not incentivized, they can explain about 55% of investments. However, a constant proportion of about one fifth of stated aspirations is not in line with a minimum requisite of boundedly rational behavior. This evidence suggests that despite demanding seemingly less cognitive effort, stating state-contingent aspirations might require substantial learning. Another interesting finding is that aspiration data and risk attitude, measured by imputing cardinal utilities, are systematically related: less risk averse individuals have particularly low aspirations, but also desire ample improvements.

Our analysis is based on the main idea of bounded rationality theory, namely the concepts of aspiration formation and satisficing. Bounded rationality–skeptic scholars frequently criticize that the concept is intangible and lacks stringent predictions. Note, however, that also the rational choice perspective can conclude hardly anything forthright: to predict an optimal choice unambiguously not only the preferences or a cardinal utility function have to be specified but also its parameter(s). Therefore, both approaches rely on somewhat bold assumptions. The important difference lies in the kind of data the decision maker is supposed to deliver. Our claim is that aspiration data reflect a more natural way of decision making than the maximization calculus that is inherent in neoclassical utility theory and all its advancements.

                         We would like to stress that our descriptive model does not account for the cognitive process that generates satisficing behavior. To examine such an underlying process would require different approaches, such as using the mouselab technique ( Johnson, Payne, Bettman & Schkade, 1998; Johnson, Schulte-Mecklenbeck & Willemsen, 2008 ) and/or think-aloud protocols ( Ericsson & Simon, 1980 ).

In our view, bounded rationality has to be clearly separated not only from perfect rationality–except for simple decision tasks where both might coincide–but also from actual behavior which may not always be reasonable. Results of the experiment reveal that setting sensible aspirations and choosing actions accordingly may be less of a natural ability than expected and still requires learning. The observation that behavior is often not in line with minimum requirements of reason calls for advice-giving to boundedly rational clients. However, learning to satisfy reasonable aspirations can be easier achieved than learning to be a utility maximizer.

Certainly, we are just offering an initial step of further developing the theory of bounded rationality by using and testing it. Future research should proceed to more complex aspiration ladders by distinguishing, for instance, more, i.e., intermediate, states of the world. In fact, the basic framework of satisficing presented here has been generalized and extended (see, for instance,  Fellner et al. (2006a) ,  Fellner, Güth, and Martin (2006b) ,  Güth (2008)  and  Güth et al. (2008b) ), and introduced into other fields of decision making, such as sequential search ( Güth, Martin & Weiland, 2006 ) or social interaction ( Berninghaus, Güth, Levati & Qiu, 2006; Güth, Levati & Ploner, 2008a ). The aim of this paper was to further advance bounded rationality theory by making it more tangible and testing its predictive power, so that ultimately some of the initial speculations, for instance, on risk attitude and satisficing, can be replaced by more systematic empirical findings.
