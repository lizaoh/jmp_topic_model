INTRODUCTION  The discovery that two theories or classes of models are formally equivalent even though they were derived from different  assumptions is generally regarded as a severe handicap for empirical research, as it implies that there is no way of deciding  on the basis of data which theory is more appropriate. A well-known example is the controversy in cognitive psychology of  whether mental operations occur serially or in parallel. They appear more or less inconclusive now, after Townsend's (1972, 1974, 1976) demonstration that large classes  of serial and parallel models are equivalent, i.e., make identical predictions, as far as reaction time data are concerned,  and cannot be distinguished from each other unless additional constraints (e.g., stochastic independence, invariance across  experimental conditions, or selective influence) can be assumed to hold. Complete equivalence between serial and parallel  information processing models in the general case, i.e., without additional untestable assumptions, has been shown by Vorberg  (1977); Townsend and Ashby (1983) give a similar proof.  The purpose of this paper is to stress another, often neglected, aspect of equivalence problems which is beneficial for  theoretical work. If two theories are equivalent, and thus are indistinguishable from each other in their predictions, it does  not matter which theory is used to derive the predictions. However, whereas one theory may be quite intractable analytically,  its counterpart may lend itself easily to analysis. If this is the case, it may be worthwhile to exploit the equivalence  between the theories in order to translate from one theory into the other, to derive the predictions sought, and to translate  the results back into the original theory. Greeno and Steiner (1964) have used this approach to solve estimation problems in  Markov learning models.  Our aim in this paper is to demonstrate the approach of analyzing a model by constructing equivalent versions from it. In  order to make this general strategy clear, we study a particular model in depth. Therefore, we shall show how the equivalence  between the general classes of serial and parallel models (Townsend & Ashby, 1983; Vorberg, 1977) can be employed to analyze a  generalization of McGill's (1963) search model for retrieval from long term memory. This generalization has been suggested  repeatedly (e.g., Albert, 1972; Morrison, 1979; Reder & Shiffrin, 1971; Shiffrin, 1970) as one way to overcome some of the  obvious limitations of McGill's original model. With the exception of some early information-theoretic work by Shepard (1963)  and some Monte Carlo work (Reder & Shiffrin, 1971 ), it has resisted systematic treatment so far. The model describes a serial  search mechanism. Using the mapping theorem for equivalent serial and parallel models, we shall derive the corresponding  parallel model, which turns out to be surprisingly simple.  The outline of the paper is as follows: First, we shall give an intuitive argument for the parallel-serial equivalence result.  Next, the generalized McGill model is introduced and its serial and parallel versions are presented. Then, several testable  predictions are derived and compared to those from the original model and from another generalization proposed by Morrison  (1979). In the final section, we shall discuss problems of parameter estimation as well as two further extensions of the model.  Some further comments are in order. First, it will become obvious when the assumptions are stated that the model is an extreme  simplification that ignores many well-established findings which indicate the importance of organization in  GENERALIZATIONS OF MC GILL'S MODEL  long term memory. However, under carefully contrived experimental conditions that minimize the role of organizational factors,  the model may provide important insights into the dynamics of retrieval from long term memory. Thus, in spite of its narrow  scope and although by no means a competitor of the more ambitious largescale theories of memory like those of Anderson (1976)  or Raaijmakers and Shiffrin (1980, 1981), the model may be worth investigating experimentally or as a starting point for more  realistic theorizing. Second, it is not our intent to give a systematic survey of the literature, although we shall  occasionally mention experimental results bearing on the validity of the model. In presenting testable predictions and  suggestions for parameter estimation, we hope to motivate researchers to test the model rigorously.  2. PARALLEL AND SERIAL MODELS OF INFORMATION PROCESSING  For the purposes of this paper, it is sufficient to characterize an information processing system as serial if it processes  elements one at a time (operates on one symbol of a n-symbol array, solves one subproblem of a problem which consists of n  steps, etc.) and as parallel if it processes all elements simultaneously. As we shall consider the case only where the number  of elements to be processed, n, is fixed, no assumption is needed about how processing latencies depend on n. RANDOM SEARCH AMONG ITEMS WHICH DIFFER IN ACCESSIBILITY  McGill's (1963) search model is concerned with the temporal aspects of performance when a subject recalls a word list in a  free recall experiment or generates words from a given semantic category. The model pictures long terns memory as a totally  unorganized store in which random searches are performed. Apart from the fact that the model ignores contributions from short  term memory to performance (which may be acceptable, for example, when a delayed free recall test is used), the main objection  against it is that organization in long term memory, associative  6 VORBERG AND ULRICH  relations between concepts, and the like are neglected, the importance of which is evident from analyses of clustering of  responses or from inspection of the interresponse times in free recall of categorized lists (e.g., Patterson, Meltzer, &  Mandler, 1971; Shuell, 1969). Nevertheless, when considered as a limiting case that applies only under conditions which  minimize these factors, the model is remarkable in its ability to predict certain temporal properties of the retrieval from  long term memory.  Consider the following task that Bousfield and his associates (Bousfield & Sedgewick, 1944; Bousfield, Sedgewick, & Cohen,  1954) seems to have used first systematically. The subject is instructed to name, for example, as many four-legged animals as  she can remember. The main interest is on the temporal aspects of the retrieval from memory, for example, the time intervals  between successive recalls.  Let us assume that the subject confines her search to a restricted area in memory called the search area. Within that area  there are n relevant items which, when found, are recalled, plus an unknown number of irrelevant items (insects, fish, etc.).  Retrieval from memory consists of a sequence of random draws of items from the search area; each item drawn is checked to see  whether it is relevant and, if so, whether it has not been recalled before. Relevant items that have been recalled are marked  as used and replaced, such that the total number of items in the search area remains constant during the search whereas the  number of unrecalled relevant items decreases by one after each recall. In McGill's (1963) version of the model, all relevant  items are equally likely to be chosen on any draw; however, this is not a very plausible assumption, since some items (like  "dog" or "cat" in the example above) are more salient and tend to be produced earlier than others, as has been pointed out by  Shiffrin (1970). Therefore, we allow items to differ in accessibility. Let r; stand for the probability of choosing relevant  item i, i = 1, n, on a draw, where q = 1 - (r, + r, + - - - + r„) is the probability of drawing an irrelevant item. Each draw  and subsequent check is assumed to require an amount of time that is exponentially distributed with rate v. All times are  mutually independent. The search is assumed to continue until all n items have been found (modifications of this assumption  will be discussed later).  As formulated, the model describes a serial search mechanism that, as long as the search goes on, performs exactly one  operation at any instant. In the following sections, we derive several testable predictions for McGill's search model with unequal rates. We shall focus  on the number of items recalled by time t and on some properties of the interresponse times. The former dependent variable has  been studied in most applications of the model, mainly because it is easy to obtain; the latter requires more sophisticated  recording equipment, but provides a more detailed picture of the dynamics of retrieval from long term memory. Most of the  predictions under the equal rate assumption have been presented first by McGill (1963). Since there are several reports of the  merits and deficiencies of the simple model in free recall and word generation under constraints, we examine how the  predictions change if rate variability is allowed, and whether some of the known problems of the original model are fixed by  the proposed generalization.  4.
