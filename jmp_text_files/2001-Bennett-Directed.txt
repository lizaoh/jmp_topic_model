Directed Convergence in Stable Percept Acquisition 

Bruce M. Bennett and Rachel C. Lehman 

University of California, Irvine 

We view a perceptual capacity as a nondeductive inference, represented as a function from a set of premises to a set of conclusions. The application of the function to a single premise to produce a single conclusion is called a ``percept'' or ``instantaneous percept.'' We define a stable percept as a convergent sequence of instantaneous percepts. Assuming that the sets of premises and conclusions are metric spaces, we introduce a strategy for acquiring stable percepts, called directed convergence. We consider probabilistic inferences, where the premise and conclusion sets are spaces of probability measures, and in this context we study Bayesian probabilistic�recursive inference. In this type of Bayesian inference the premises are probability measures, and the prior as well as the posterior is updated nontrivially at each iteration. This type of Bayesian inference is distinguished from classical Bayesian statistical inference where the prior remains fixed, and the posterior evolves by conditioning on successively more punctual premises. We indicate how the directed convergence procedure may be implemented in the context of Bayesian probabilistic�recursive inference. We discuss how the L[�] metric can be used to give numerical control of this type of Bayesian directed convergence. � 2001 Academic Press

1. INTRODUCTION 

We imagine a system which makes inferences in response to inputs of some specified type. We call these inputs premises, and by definition an inference associates to each premise a conclusion. In this paper we are interested in the question of how an inferencing system arrives at stable inferences. 

We assume that the inputs are generated sequentially and that time is discrete. Here are some examples. First, we have the case of perceptual inference, where the system embodies a perceptual capacity to infer certain states of the environment given inputs to certain sensory receptors. Second, there is the development of a scientific hypothesis from input consisting of information garnered from experiments. Third, there is a card game where a player infers the cards in the opponent's hand given an input consisting of information about the player's own hand and the opponent's discards. The last two are examples of rational� deliberative inference (Skyrms, 1990). However, with a sufficiently broad interpretation of the terms ``environment'' and ``sensory receptors,'' it is hard to imagine a type of inference which could not somehow be considered ``perceptual,'' provided we don't put a priori restrictions on the time scale on which the inferences occur. For instance, we can view the development of a scientific hypothesis from experimental evidence as a perceptual inference whose ``perceptual instant'' is measured in years. In contrast, for the perceptual inference from retinal data of a rigid object in 3D, the perceptual instant may be only a fraction of a second. For the sake of convenience and terminological uniformity we will generally use perceptual terminology. So we will consider the conclusion of the inference to be a representation of the state of affairs in some environment, and we will frequently call these conclusions percepts. However, in order to emphasize the inferential nature of the system we will call inputs premises. 

Having chosen the appropriate time scale, we can think of a single inference as being instantaneous (occuring in one perceptual instant). The resulting instantaneous percept is transitory, i.e., there is no reason to suppose that this percept will persist as a mental representation or as an encryption in some type of short-term memory for much longer than the perceptual instant in which it was generated. In the generic situation a system receives a succession of unrelated premises and produces a corresponding succession of unrelated, instantaneous percepts. But whatever the relation may be among the various instantaneous percepts, if we take ``stable'' to mean ``persistent in time'' (with respect to the given time scale), then by definition no instantaneous percept is stable. 

Our viewpoint is that a stable percept is the conclusion of an inference��called the stable inference��which occupies a higher level in the ``perceptual hierarchy'' than does the original instantaneous inference. The notion of perceptual hierarchy that we are using here is based on the idea of a transitive relation ``�'' on a collection of inference systems, where A�B means that sets of conclusions of the inference system A generate premises for the inference system B. ( For more discussion on this notion of perceptual hierarchy we refer the reader to Bennett, Hoffman, and Prakash (1989, 1991).) In our present situation, a premise for the stable inference �a stable premise��is a sequence of instantaneous conclusions, and a

Thus, as we would expect, the stable inference takes place at a coarser time scale than does the instantaneous inference. In fact, the time necessary to acquire the premise for the stable inference is the same as the time necessary for an entire sequence of instantaneous inferences. 

Which sequences of instantaneous percepts serve as premises for the stable inference? For the stable inference to be deductively valid (in the sense of the definition to be given in Section 2), its premise (the stable premise) would have to be an infinite sequence, and then the stable percept would be that sequence's true and unique limit. But it is unreasonable to require that the sequence (the stable premise) actually be infinite, since we can't expect any premise to transduce an infinitude of information. Thus the stable inference has a nontrivially inductive quality (as opposed to being deductively valid), in that it infers a limit given only finitely many terms of a sequence. Intuitively, the key requirement is that this finite subsequence should appear to converge up to some degree of resolution, a degree which will depend in practice both on the resolution capability and on the psychological� environmental context. 

If the acquisition of stable percepts is a goal of perception, then the system will employ a strategy to increase the likelihood of inference sequences which have the stable premise property, namely, the ``appearance of convergence.'' The purpose of this paper is twofold. First, we introduce such a strategy, called directed convergence. We present the idea of directed convergence frameworks in Section 3, where the discussion presupposes only that we have an inferencing system whose set of possible premises and set of possible conclusions are metric spaces. In this case there is a natural way to make sense of what we mean by the appearance of convergence of a finite sequence of conclusions, namely, that the metric distance between successive conclusions decreases overall (at a sufficiently rapid rate). 

Our second major purpose in this paper is to study how directed convergence works in the important particular case of Bayesian recursive inference. In this kind of inference the instantaneous premise and conclusion sets (denoted respectively G and F) are the sets of probability measures on suitable ``configuration spaces'' Y and X; intuitively Y and X are what the premise and conclusion sets would be ``ideally'' if the inferences were made in a noise-free environment with perfect resolution.

(In Section 5 we present a brief review of the semantics of this type of Bayesian inference.) Then, given the previous conclusion (the prior) and a model of noise (the likelihood ), the system computes conditional probability (the posterior) and uses it to assign a new conclusion measure to a new premise measure. This new conclusion serves as the prior for the next step, and so forth. We discuss this in detail in Section 6. This updating procedure is truly recursive because both the prior and the posterior are recomputed at each step. In Step 6 we also explain how our recursive updating procedure differs fundamentally from the procedure which is usually known as ``classical Bayesian statistical inference,'' where the prior is essentially fixed. This fundamental difference arises at the level of the underlying probabilistic semantics of the two procedures, although in a technical sense the recursive procedure is a generalization of the classical one. In order to respect this difference, we use the term Bayesian recursive updating or Bayesian structural updating for our recursive updating procedure. 

In Section 7 we show that Bayesian recursive inference takes place in a directed convergence framework in the sense of Section 3, provided that the configuration spaces Y and X are complete separable metric spaces. This means that a Bayesian recursive inference system can in principle use a directed convergence strategy for the acquisition of stable percepts. This, together with the use of the L[�] metric presented in Section 7, is the main point of the paper. The restriction to complete separable metric spaces is actually mild, since almost all the spaces that we can readily conceptualize are essentially of this type, including all countable discrete spaces. The reason for this restriction is that when the configuration spaces Y and X are of this type, then so are the spaces of probability measures on Y and X, i.e., so are our premise and conclusion spaces G and F. In particular, G and F are metric spaces in this case. (This follows from the well-known theorems of Prokhorov (Prokhorov, 1956); see also Section 4). 

Finally, we show how to use an L[�] metric, in place of the Prokhorov metric, to get a particularly simple and effective numerical control of Bayesian directed convergence. We conclude with a theorem which shows that in the L[�] -controlled directed convergence procedure, convergence of the premise sequence is a necessary condition for the convergence of the conclusion sequence. This result, although very intuitively appealing, is far from clear in general. 

We return briefly to the question of classical versus structural�recursive Bayesian dynamics: In the context of Bayesian structural updating the directed convergence procedure is instantaneously Bayesian. Thus, instantaneously, it employs the same posterior as in classical Bayesian inference. In directed convergence this posterior is viewed as an operator from premise measures to conclusion measures, and in response to the input of a new premise measure a new conclusion measure is produced which serves as a new ``prior'' for the next Bayesian step of the procedure. In the classical case the inputs are points and not measures, and the posterior is a system of conditional probabilities conditioned on these points. When a punctual premise is acquired, the posterior associates to that point a probability measure. This new measure is not taken as a new prior to reset the posterior. Rather, the same posterior (which is associated to the original prior) is reused at each stage to generate a sequence of measures in response to a sequence of punctual premises.

Classically, it is assumed that the points in the premise sequence are random and governed by a given distribution; the question then is whether the corresponding sequence of conclusion measures converges. It is inconsistent with this scheme to selectively accept certain premises and reject others. In fact, the classical Bayesian analysis is designed to give information based on overall statistical regularities of premises (corresponding in principle to overall statistical regularities of the environment). In contrast, directed convergence provides criteria for the selective acceptance of premises, in order to find features which may be singular with respect to overall environmental statistics. For example, the birdwatcher's problem��to track one bird in a large flock��is singular to the statistical regularities of the flock as a whole. So in this sense it is not possible to formulate the problem in terms of classical Bayesian inference. 

Now, one can consider restricted versions of directed convergence where the premises are punctual, as in the classical case. But aside from this superficial similarity, for the same reasons as given above this no more fits into the classical Bayesian scheme than does the general case (where the premises can be arbitrary measures). Moreover, this restriction is both practically and mathematically unnatural. It is practically unnatural because perfect resolution and perfect unambiguousness are very rare in premise acquisition. Even in the case where the underlying configuration space (on which the premises are measures) is discrete, it is reasonable to assume that the premises are convex linear combinations of pointmeasures. And the directed convergence procedure is highly nonlinear (in the premises), so even in the discrete case a point-premise theory is of little help in a general analysis. In the case of nondiscrete configuration spaces, point premises are never acquired in practice. More than that, from a theoretical and computational viewpoint, allowing point premises prevents the use of the ``L[�] window'' which is an invaluable theoretical and computational technique. 

We assume that the reader is familiar with the basic mathematical ideas of probability and metric spaces. For the reader's convenience we review in Section 4 some definitions, terminology, and basic theorems related to measure theory and probability. A more detailed presentation of mathematical results related to this work is published separately in Bennett and Cohen (1999). 

A great deal of work (see Knill 6 Richards, 1996, for example) has been done in developing and studying Bayesian models of perceptual capacities viewed as instantaneous inferences, i.e., inferences with a single premise and a single conclusion. We remind the reader that our purpose in introducing directed convergence is not to study instantaneous inferences (Bayesian or otherwise), but rather to provide a stategy to obtain stable percepts given some model of an instantaneous perceptual capacity. This strategy is to be used in natural environments replete with noise and ubiquitous distractors. For example, consider a birdwatcher who wishes to track a certain type of sparrow in an observational environment in which there are many bugs and birds of various types, including the sparrow, flying about, disappearing behind trees and then reappearing, flying in front of each other, and so forth. We may assume that the birdwatcher already has an effective instantaneous sparrow-recognition capability; there remains the problem of ``locking on'' to the sparrow upon entering the environment. This is not a ``correspondence problem'' in

the usual sense of that term: it is not a problem of identifying corresponding points of a given 3D object in adjacent ``frames.'' Rather, it is a matter of a strategy to selectively acquire a sequence of percepts which coalesce into the representation of a relatively time-persistent phenomenon. We will present in detail a simplified version of this birdwatcher example beginning at the end of Section 5. 

We mention here another direction of investigation of conditions for the convergence of inference sequences, this time in the context of rational deliberation. Skyrms (extending earlier ideas of Good, Ramsey, Jeffrey, and Graves) has shown how belief in the value of knowledge imposes a coherence condition (``Condition (M)''��see [11] for details) on the updating procedure for sequences of probabilistic inferences. 2. GENERAL INFERENCING SYSTEMS AND STABLE PERCEPTS 

We consider a system which makes inferences in discrete time. The inputs to the system are called premises and the outputs are called conclusions. Sometimes we call the most recent output the ``current state'' of the system. One of the main examples of interest is the case of perception. Indeed, we may view a given perceptual capacity as an inferencing system, in which the premises represent incoming information from some sensory receptors, and the outputs, called percepts, represent the state of affairs in some environment. The intuition in this case is that the premises are transduced in some way from the environment and that the purpose of the inference is to output a representation which corresponds to the transduced environmental phenomena. 

In any case, an inferencing system is described mathematically as a mapping P: G � F from a set G of possible premises to a set F of possible conclusions (see Fig. 2). Given this, the assignment g [ P(g) for each g in G is called an inference; sometimes, the term inference refers to the whole function P. 

For any given type of inference there is usually a natural way to think of G and F as sets of propositions. For example, in the perceptual case the propositions assert that certain states of affairs hold in the environment or at the sensory receptors. There will usually be some natural way to define the logical operations of conjunction, disjunction, and so forth for the propositions in G and F (cf. Bennett, Hoffman, 6 Murthy, 1993). Moreover, it is reasonable to assume that these operations are ``respected'' by P; e.g., if 7 denotes conjunction, then P(a 7 b)= P(a) 7 P(b). To say that P respects the logical operations in this way is far weaker than saying that the inference described by P is deductively valid. Deductive validity means that if the premise proposition is true then the conclusion proposition is necessarily true, in the sense that the effect of P is computable by means of

We can imagine a situation where the premises are transduced from a chaotically changing environment so that there is little continuity of the premises from instant to instant. However, we are interested in the case where there are at least some relatively stable environmental features, and we may ask whether a sequence of perceptual inferences will result in a stable percept (Fig. 4). Ideally we might wish to define a stable percept to be a limit of a sequence of instantaneous percepts. This is based on the following intuitions: 

1. In order for a sequence of percepts to converge, it is likely (if not strictly necessary) that the corresponding sequence of premises converges. Here converges means with respect to the metrics on the spaces of conclusions and premises respectively. 

2. Consider a sequence of premises acquired spontaneously in a given environment. The convergence of this premise sequence (or even of a subsequence) obviously requires an abundance of arbitrarily closely related premises. And this is highly unlikely unless there is a stable feature of the environment which generates these premises via transduction. 

3. Suppose we have a convergent sequence of percepts whose corresponding premise sequence is also convergent and that these premises are generated by a stable environmental feature via transduction. Then the limit percept is likely to represent that stable environmental feature. 

We emphasize that the intuitions (1)�(3) above are not based on mathematical results which are valid in any generality and are intended only as a semantical support for the ideal definition of a stable percept as a limit of instantaneous percepts. In practice, however, we cannot expect the system to execute an infinite sequence of steps in order to arrive at a true limit. Therefore in practice a stable percept can

Note that a premise for the stable inference is a set of conclusions of the instantaneous inference, namely, the conclusions in the finite sequence. Having said this, the main issue becomes the following: how, in a feature-rich and semistable environment, can a premise sequence be acquired which arises from the transduction of a single stable enviornmental feature, among the many features which are present both stably and nonstably? Of course, stated in this way the question is purely rhetorical since, according to our perceptual semantics, from the perceiver's standpoint environmental features are a conclusion of inference. Thus, we ask how a premise sequence which gives rise to an apparently convergent sequence of percepts can be acquired (whatever reasonable definition is used for the ``appearance of convergence'')? We will say that such a premise sequence is distinguished. More realistically, we may ask: What computationally efficient strategies can be employed to increase the probability that distinguished premise-sequences are acquired? In other words, how can the system restrict itself, in a computationally efficient manner, to distinguished premise subsequences in the presence of ubiquitous unrelated premises. We here propose a procedure which accomplishes this, called directed convergence. In fact, we mean to suggest that this procedure may be effectively implemented in robotic or artificial intelligence systems and might be instantiated in various naturally occurring systems.

5. BAYESIAN INFERENCE IN THE PRESENCE OF NOISE 

We now consider the important special case to which we will apply the directed convergence ideas presented in Section 3. Namely, we consider inference in the presence of noise. 

Suppose that a given inference is represented in a noise-free idealized form with premise space Y and conclusion space X. This means that the points of Y correspond to inputs which are acquired with perfect resolution and unambiguity, and similarly the points of X correspond to conclusions which are perfectly discriminated. Thus, ideally, in the noise-free case it seems that the inference should be given by a function P: Y � X. But even in the noise-free case this is too naive. The reason is that there may be uncertainty about what is the correct conclusion even if the premise is perfectly unambiguous, a fundamental uncertainty of interpretation that has nothing to do with noise. Indeed, in both perception and cognition such uncertainty is ubiquitous. Therefore, the best we can expect in the noise-free case is that the conclusion, given the premise y, is not a point of X, but a probability measure on X which depends on y; denote it by '( y, } ). Birdwatcher Example: Bayesian Setup 

Consider a robotic birdwatcher in a forest, where there are large numbers of birds of two species, sparrows and catbirds. These species are quite similar in size and shape, but differ somewhat in color: the sparrows tend to be brownish and the catbirds tend to be grayish. This color difference, however, is not always sharp. The birdwatcher's goal is to track individual birds (of either species). The birds are flying randomly in and out of foliage, perching briefly and hopping about in the trees, and temporarily disappearing behind leaves and branches, with the two species mingling freely. Even if the birdwatcher is using some motion-based tracking algorithm to maximize the chance of following a single bird from frame to frame, due to the density of birds and the discontinuity and occlusion of their motions the chances are good that after several frames of successful tracking of a single bird the algorithm will fail: A bird of the other species will replace the original one in the tracking sequence. 6. BAYESIAN UPDATING: RECURSIVE AND CLASSICAL 

The Updating Formalism 

The type of Bayesian inference described here can be updated recursively in a natural way. With the notation and assumptions of Section 5, we begin with X, Y, N, and +0 , and we get the Bayes posterior P(+0 , N ) which gives the inference map * [ *P(+0 , N ) from P(Y) to P(X ). To simplify notation, let us denote this map by P0 . In accordance with the time index convention of Section 3, we will view +0 and the associated inference map P0 as arising at time t=0, but the argument * to which P0 is applied as arising at t=1. For this reason it is appropriate to denote the argument of the map P0 by *1 and to view the new measure *1P0 on X as a new prior +1 which arises at t=1 together with its associated inference map P1=P(+1 , N ). We remind the reader that N is time invariant. 

In this way, given a sequence of premise measures [*n]n , there is generated a sequence of priors [+n]n and the associated sequence of Bayesian posteriors, i.e., of inference maps [Pn]n , where Pn=P(+n , N) . It is appropriate to think of the inference map Pn as the ``learning strategy'' prepared at time n to be applied to a new premise *n+1 which will be acquired at time n+1. We may say that at each time n there is inferred a pair consisting of a new prior and its associated learning strategy. The aquisition of the premise *n+1 triggers the transition (+n , Pn) [ (+n+1 , Pn+1). This procedure is truly recursive, and we will call it Bayesian recursive updating or Bayesian structural updating. 

Updating in the Birdwatcher Example

Comparison of Classical and Recursive (Structural) Bayesian Inference 

The reader will note that Bayesian recursive inference is not the same as the classical Bayesian statistical inference from the statistics literature. It turns out that the recursive inference is a generalization of the classical, and we will make this precise below. In spite of this fact, however, there is a fundamental difference between the way that the apparatus of conditional probability is used in the two procedures. Classical Bayesian statistical inference is iterative rather than recursive in the following sense. In classical Bayesian inference, the inferencer has access to an infinite sequence of punctual premises, say, y1 , y2 , .... A sequence of updated interpretations is then generated (using the same fixed prior measure +0 on X together with the likelihood N) by conditioning on successively more of these yi's each time. This procedure also gives us a sequence of probability measures on X, but in a way which is truly iterative, not truly recursive. One can then study the convergence of these interpretation measures (Diaconis 6 Freedman, 1986). More

specifically, for each n the conditioning on ( y1 , ..., yn) is done with respect to the same ``overall'' measure \=+0 � N[�] on X_Y[�] . In the former, the updating is given by an inference map Y � P(X ), and in the latter the updating is given by an inference map P(Y ) � P(X). Intuitively, iteration in the classical case serves at best to increase resolution. In fact, the best case classically is where Bayesian consistency holds; for details on this we refer the reader to the excellent survey article by Diaconis 6 Freedman (1986). One case where the strong form of classical Bayesian consistency holds is the famous ``biased coin tossing'' example. 8. RESUME 

This paper has two themes. The first is to introduce directed convergence strategy in a general setting. The second is to consider directed convergence in the special case of Bayesian recursive inference. As we present this latter topic we carry along an example, the ``birdwatcher'' example, to illustrate the ideas as they develop. An important subtext of the Bayesian case is the use of the L[�] metric. 

We view an inference as a function P from a set G of premises to a set F of conclusions. An instantaneous inference is the application of P to a given premise to produce a given conclusion. A recursive inference system is a sequence of inferences parametrized by discrete time, where the inference map P at any instant depends on the conclusion at the previous instant. This dependency is expressed by a function

L from F to the set of inference maps; i.e., for + in F, L: + [ L(+)=P+ . In this context, directed convergence is a strategy for acquiring stable conclusions in an environment with many features, distractors, and noise. A stable conclusion is a convergent sequence of instantaneous conclusions, i.e., a sequence of elements of F: What time scale constitutes ``instantaneous'' is relative to the particular application. It is important to understand that, in general, a conclusion (instantaneous or stable) does not correspond to an overall statistical representation of an environmental gestalt, but rather to a particular feature or element on which the system might focus. 

The directed convergence strategy can be applied in any inference system where the premise set G and the conclusion set F for the inference are complete metric spaces. In this case the minimal requirements for the use of the strategy are the axioms of a ``directed convergence framework'' presented in Section 3. Such a framework consists of the data (F, G, L, N, B), where (1) F, G, L are as in the paragraph above, (2) N: F � G is a function which assigns to + in F the premise in G which would be transduced by an actual state of affairs in the environment corresponding to +, and (3) B is a nonnegative real-valued function on F (or more generally a sequence of such functions, one for each instant of time) which assigns a degree of belief to each conclusion. The main idea of directed convergence is to selectively accept or reject a premise * in G depending on its compatibility with the currently held conclusion +. This compatibility is measured in terms of the metric distance between * and +N, where N is a function from F to G that is specified as part of the data of the directed convergence framework (we adopt the convention of writing N as an operator on the right). Indeed, according to the interpretation of N, +N is the premise which would result if the conclusion + were correct. Thus, to the extent to which the observer believes in the current +, he will require that a premise * be metrically close to +N as a condition for its acceptance. The function B in the directed convergence framework translates the observer's belief in + into the minimum metric distance to +N that * must have in order for it to be accepted. In this case we say that the premise * confirms the current degree of belief. * is then used to update the current conclusion +, i.e., to replace + with P+(*). To reject * means to ignore it for purposes of updating. 

The principal example, and our main focus in this paper, is directed convergence in the case of Bayesian recursive inference, studied in Section 4ff. Here G and F are the spaces of probability measures on ``configuration spaces'' Y and X respectively. From the viewpoint of semantics the natural metrics here are the ``Prokhorov metrics'' (Section 4) which metrize the topology of vague convergence which is the topology of real-number measurement. In this context we can think of N as modeling noise; it correponds to the ``likelihood function'' in classical Bayesian analysis. Mathematically, it is a ``kernel'' from X to Y, i.e., a system of conditional probabilities on Y given points of X. The greater the belief in the current conclusion +, the closer a premise * must be to +N (in the Prokorov metric on G ) in order that it be accepted. The function L of the directed convergence framework is now given by the Bayesian ``posterior'' P+, N , which is associated to the current conclusion + as the ``prior'' and by N as the ``likelihood function.'' Mathematically, P+, N is a kernel from Y to X, i.e., a system of conditional probabilities on X given points

of Y. When viewed as an operator from G to F, P+, N is adjoint to N. To accept * means to replace + with *P+, N (as with N, we adopt the convention of writing P+, N as an operator on the right). This updated conclusion is then viewed as the prior for the next stage of the procedure, and in this manner a sequence of conclusions +n is generated in response to a sequence of premises *n (which have been successively accepted). 

Bayesian recursive inference, particularly with regard to directed convergence, differs significantly from classical Bayesian inference in both philosophy and technology (Section 6). In both cases the goal is to converge to a probability measure & given a sequence inputs. In the classical case, the procedure is to generate iteratively a succession of Bayesian posteriors obtained from a fixed prior + (and likelihood function N ), using a succession of punctual premises, which are assumed to be sampled from the distribution +N; it is hoped that this sequence of posteriors will converge to a reasonable &. In any case, to select or reject the punctual inputs as they appear, using any criterion whatsoever, is orthogonal to the goal and the procedure of the classical Bayesian approach. For this reason alone, directed convergence in the classical Bayesian setting seems to be meaningless. Additionally, in the classical case there are substantial technical differences from Bayesian recursive inference, in that (1) the inputs in the recursive case are probabilistic (not punctual), and (2) in the recursive case the prior itself is modified recursively; in fact, at each stage the conclusion measure is treated as a new prior for recomputing a Bayesian posterior kernel to be used to process the input measure at the next stage. 

In Bayesian recursive directed convergence, we have noted that the use of the Prokhorov metric (to metrize F and G ) is philosophically natural. However, it is computationally unwieldy and expensive, and for this reason it is convenient to seek other metrics which ``locally'' approximate the Prokhorov, but which are easier to use. The L[�] function space metric works well in this regard; this theory is presented in Section 7. The basic idea is that if we fix a measure +, say, on X, then we can associate to a function f on X the measure f& whose Radon Nikodym derivative (Section 4) with respect to + is f. This gives a 1�1 continuous map 8 from L[�] (X, +) to the space of measures on X. 8 has the nice property that the L[�] (X, +)-metric distance between the functions f and g is greater than or equal to the Prokhorov metric distance between the measures 8( f ) and 8( g). This means that one can very precisely control the Prokhorov metric convergence in the space of measures, by means of the L[�] (X, +)-metric calculations. Now, the L[�] (X, +) metric distance between the two functions f and g on X is essentially just the maximum value attained by | f &g| on X. As a consequence, so long as we restrict our attention to measures which are absolutely continuous with respect to a given &, we can control the directed convergence strategy by means of the observation of the maxima of functions, a task which is vastly easier than making Prokhorov metric calculations. Considering that such calculations must be made repeatedly in order to apply the strategy, the gain in efficiency is enormous. 

In the birdwatcher example the space X=[S, C] consists of two points, corresponding to two different species of birds, and the space Y is the interval [0, 1] representing a ``color space'' for premises which represent possible bird colors.

A conclusion, i.e., a probability measure on X, is an assignment of probabilities to S and C. We apply the L[�] theory to the birdwatcher example, and find the following: Suppose the birdwatcher executes a directed convergence strategy where the strongest possible confirmable degree of belief is held at each stage (in other words, the birdwatcher is as optimistic as possible!). Then we have the following results: (1) A stable conclusion will be acquired. (2) The stable conclusion will be extremal; i.e., it will represent a bird of extremal color, and hence (according to the setup of the example) one whose species affiliation is as unequivocal as possible. This means the conclusion is the probability measure on X which assigns probability 1 to either S or C and probability 0 to the other. (3) The stable conclusion will not be a ``phantom''; i.e., it will correspond to an actual bird with the extremal color, to which the observer has ``locked on.'' This result in this very special case comports well with our intuition about perception, namely, that in a complex environment there is a tendency to focus on the most extreme features. It remains to be seen if a general result of this type can be obtained mathematically. 

In this paper we have not addressed the issue of the speed of directed convergence. A rigorous treatment of this question requires exensive analysis, which utilizes precise assumptions about the nature of the degree of the belief function B and about the statistics of the environment. In the case of the birdwatcher example, assuming that the strongest possible degree of belief is held at each stage and assuming a uniform distribution of bird colors in the environment, preliminary and informal numerical data suggest reasonably rapid convergence to within a small neighborhood of the limit.
