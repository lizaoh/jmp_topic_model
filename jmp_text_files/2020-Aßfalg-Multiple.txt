The main objective of consensus theory is to estimate the shared attitudes, beliefs, or knowledge in a group of respondents ( Romney, Weller, & Batchelder, 1986 ). Such consensus can include the details of an unknown culture in anthropological surveys ( Weller, 2007 ), shared views among supporters of political parties ( Mueller & Veinott, 2008 ), or the details of a crime shared by eyewitnesses ( Waubert de Puiseau, Aßfalg, Erdfelder, & Bernstein, 2012; Waubert de Puiseau, Greving, Aßfalg, & Musch, 2017 ). As in latent-trait models, consensus theory postulates that respondents differ in their ability to express the consensus attitude, belief, or knowledge. However, models implementing consensus theory must also deliver estimates of the consensus responses—the answer key.

Applications of consensus theory have to operate within the, often narrow, boundaries of model limitations. Previous consensus models are restricted to one response format. This includes models for dichotomous, ordered categorical, or continuous responses (e.g.,  Anders & Batchelder, 2012, 2015; Anders, Oravecz, & Batchelder, 2014 ). The  variable response model  (VRM) removes this limitation by allowing applications to include continuous responses, unordered categorical responses or a mixture thereof within the same data set ( Aßfalg, 2018 ). Here, we continue the effort to remove limitations of consensus models by introducing the VRM2. The model accounts for ordered categorical responses such as levels of agreement expressed on a 5-point scale (see  Table 1 ). The VRM2 also accounts for multiple consensus groups and multiple latent traits underlying the response data. Each of these developments has been introduced in isolation to latent-trait models with known or unknown answer key (e.g.,  Anders & Batchelder, 2012, 2015; Anders et al., 2014; Mueller & Veinott, 2008; Reckase, 1997 ). The VRM2 combines these developments into a single framework and ensures that consensus applications can use much of the arsenal of consensus theory and latent-trait models within the same application.

In the next section, we reiterate the basic assumptions underlying the VRM ( Aßfalg, 2018 ) and present our extension of the model, the VRM2. We further present a Markov chain Monte Carlo algorithm to estimate the parameters of the VRM2 and discuss model-selection procedures. In a series of simulations, we test the VRM2’s ability to estimate answer keys and to detect the number of consensus groups and latent traits underlying the data. We conclude with an application of the VRM2 to the empirical data of a memory test with multiple consensus groups and latent traits.

In this section, we briefly reiterate the core assumptions of the VRM and suggest an extension of the previous model ( Aßfalg, 2018 ) to ordered categorical responses. We further apply ideas by  Anders and Batchelder (2012)  and  Reckase (1997)  to the VRM to account for multiple latent traits and multiple consensus groups underlying the responses, respectively. We refer to this extended version of the model as the VRM2.

The VRM extends well-known latent-trait models in which responses are either scored as correct or incorrect ( Birnbaum, 1968; Rasch, 1961 ). In these models, the probability of a correct response is a nondecreasing function of a latent trait. Conversely, in consensus theory, it may be impossible to determine what the correct response is. The group consensus may reflect group beliefs that are not testable and lack ties to known facts. However, in some applications, the consensus response may have a foundation in facts that either have been established or can be established in principle. One would hope that eyewitnesses reporting about a crime, for example, base their consensus testimony on the actual crime events. However, consensus theory does not guarantee such connections between consensus and testable facts. Eyewitnesses may exchange information, for example, and establish a consensus that deviates from the true crime events. Thus, for present purposes, we consider latent-trait models in terms of the consensus response rather than the correct response.

We aimed to increase the applicability of the VRM ( Aßfalg, 2018 ) by including existing concepts such as multiple consensus groups, multiple latent traits, and ordered categorical responses. However, we expect that most uses of the VRM2 will only include a subset of these features, tailored to a specific application. If all or some of the answer keys are known, the known answer key values may replace the answer key parameters in Eqs.  (4) and (11) . This is the application scenario for latent-trait models. Conversely, sample sizes are typically small if the answer key is unknown, for example, when the respondents are eyewitnesses and the answer key represents the details of a crime ( Waubert de Puiseau et al., 2012, 2017 ). In our simulations, steps 1 to 6 are executed 5000 times of which the first 4000 iterations are discarded as burn-in trials to let the parameters reach their stationary distributions before the actual sampling occurs. Of the remaining 1000 trials, we keep only every fifth trial to account for autocorrelations. We implemented the M–H algorithm for the VRM2 in the R language (R core team, 2019). The VRM2 program code is available online ( https://osf.io/n7ywh/ ). We further used R to implement the simulations, conduct the analyses and generate the figures of the following sections.

Extending the VRM2 to multiple consensus groups and multiple latent traits raises the question how users can determine the dimensionality of the VRM2 with respect to the number of consensus groups  G  and the number of latent traits  P  that underlie the data. Here, we discuss several information criteria for the selection of VRM2 models with different values of  G ,  P , or both. Of course, these criteria can inform model selection beyond selecting  G  and  P  as well. In general, all of these criteria include the model fit and a penalty term for the complexity of the model. However, the criteria differ, primarily in how the complexity of the model is determined. As we demonstrate in the following sections, the criteria can yield different conclusions about the dimensionality of the model.

The labeling problem has implications for parameter estimation. If the M–H algorithm we propose runs long enough, it should fully explore the parameters’ posterior distributions, including the modes in the posterior distributions induced by the labeling problem. Extracting answer-key estimates from such multi-mode distributions would require a careful and cumbersome analysis. However, in our simulation studies, we found that answer key estimates computed as the median (continuous or ordered categorical responses) or mode (unordered categorical responses) over the M–H parameter samples are good approximations to the true answer key. This suggests that the Markov chains are unlikely to explore more than one mode of the posterior distribution.

In our analyses, we had to solve the labeling problem (1) when comparing the results of one Markov chain with the true answer keys in a simulation study, (2) when performing pairwise comparisons of parameter estimates between chains with regard to all parameter types, and (3) when comparing the parameter estimates of one chain with known quantities in an empirical model validation study. Our approach was similar in all three scenarios. When we assessed answer keys, we tested whether switching the labels of the consensus groups resulted in a better fit of answer key estimates. Specifically, we always chose the labels that produced the highest correlation between the continuous answer key estimates of two chains or between the answer key estimates of one chain and the true answer keys. Thus, if overfitting were to occur, high parameter recovery for continuous answer keys should appear along with relatively low parameter recovery for ordered and unordered categorical answer keys.

We found that some starting values for parameters can lead to Markov chains that are slow to converge. Instead of waiting for these chains to converge by using very long chains, we propose a more pragmatic approach: We first sample multiple chains of the parameters with the M–H algorithm with different starting values for each chain. Note that BIC and AIC can be grouped in this analysis because their model-fit component is identical and the model-complexity component does not differentiate between chains of the same complexity. Finally, we compared the answer key estimates of the chain with the lowest information criterion with the true answer keys we used to generate the data. In all our analyses, we computed answer key estimates as the median answer key sample for continuous items, the median answer key appraisal sample for ordered categorical items, and as the mode of the answer key sample for unordered categorical items.

As a baseline for the VRM2 performance, we also computed answer key estimates based on equal-weights (EQW) aggregation of responses. For the EQW method, we computed the mean response, median response, and the modal response as the answer key estimate for continuous, ordered categorical, and unordered categorical responses, respectively. Although the EQW method is simple and computationally inexpensive, it can be very successful in estimating the answer key (e.g.,  Aßfalg, 2018; Waubert de Puiseau et al., 2012, 2017 ).

To evaluate the answer key estimates, we computed the correlation and mean absolute distance (MAD) between true and estimated answer keys for continuous and ordered categorical items. We further computed the proportion of matching true and estimated answer keys for unordered categorical items. Because there were sometimes two or three consensus groups underlying the response data but the EQW method considers only one answer key, we report the average answer key performance measures (e.g., proportion of matching keys) when the one key estimate is paired with each of the two or three true answer keys.  Fig. 4  includes boxplots of the answer key evaluations across the 180 data sets for each information criterion compared to the average result across the eight chains and the EQW result.

The VRM2 considerably outperformed the EQW method,demonstrating how inadequate the EQW method can be when multiple consensus groups are present. More importantly for present purposes, selecting the chain with the lowest information criterion outperformed the result of the average chain for all information criteria. The results were virtually identical between the information criteria. Because, overall, the results slightly favored WAIC over the other information criteria, we chose WAIC as a selection criterion between chains for the remainder of our analyses. However, BIC/AIC performed about equally well which suggests that, for practical purposes, it is sufficient to choose the chain with the greatest (log) likelihood.

Our final test of the VRM2 included an empirical data set in which respondents first read a text about a fictional story involving two ancient European tribes and then answered a set of questions that tested their memory of the text. Consequently, we treated the study text as the true answer key that the respondents tried to reproduce. We further included all three response types, two consensus groups, and two latent traits in the study, to test the new elements that separate the VRM2 from the VRM.

We induced two consensus groups and two latent traits by means of experimental manipulations. We created artificial consensus groups by changing the content of the text between respondents such that half of the respondents studied story 1 and the other half studied story 2. Importantly, the stories referred to the same set of questions for all respondents. Because respondents had studied different stories, the answer key for these questions differed between respondents. For example, some questions asked for the name of certain locations. Because respondents had studied different names for these locations, their answer key differed.

We further manipulated the respondents’ latent traits—in this context their memory ability—by omitting different parts of the study text for the respondents. We accomplished this by replacing one or more words at a time with a set of underscores where the words would normally be and instructed participants to memorize the study text as well as possible, despite the omissions. Some respondents thereby saw the information required to answer half of the questions but not for the other half and vice versa for the other respondents. Consequently, we aimed to induce two latent traits in our respondent sample. The memory test included 72 questions in total, of which 24 questions required a continuous numeric response, 24 questions required an ordered categorical response, and 24 questions required an unordered categorical response. Respondents studied the text in four parts. Each part contained information necessary to answer six continuous, six ordered categorical, and six unordered categorical questions in the memory test. However, half of that information was omitted for each item type (continuous, ordered categorical, and unordered categorical).

We recruited 118 English-speaking participants in our study via an online recruiting site (Prolific). Participants needed about 20 min to complete the study and received £2 (GBP) for their participation. After providing informed consent, the computer randomly assigned respondents to one of four groups resulting from the combinations of the stories respondents studied (story 1 vs. story 2) and the item set for which information was omitted in the text (set 1 vs. set 2). Respondents first practiced their task by reading a brief text about Mahatma Gandhi in a self-paced manner. Immediately after reading the text, all respondents received a six-question memory test including two items of each type (continuous, ordered categorical, and unordered categorical). The memory test was again self-paced. The practice text and memory test were identical for all respondents. Respondents provided numeric continuous responses by typing their response into an input field next to the question. All continuous responses had to be in the interval [0, 100]. Respondents further answered all categorical questions (ordered and unordered items) via radio buttons next to the response options. All categorical items in the study had five response alternatives. After answering the practice questions, the respondents studied the first of four parts of the main text and immediately afterwards answered 18 questions about that part. Afterwards, the participants completed another three such study–test pairs, before they were debriefed, thanked, and paid for their participation.

We moderately increased the number of samples compared to the simulation study to balance our need for precise estimates with the relatively high computation time necessary for the search algorithm to explore the model space.

We found that the VRM2 had placed respondents who studied answer key 2 and trait 1 items together with respondents who studied answer key 1 and trait 2 items into a common consensus group. The VRM2 assigned the remaining respondents into the second consensus group. Only two out of 118 respondents (1.7%) were not classified in this manner. We interpret this result as another form of labeling problem: Because all four experimental groups studied different items, the VRM2 was free to recombine the groups in a different way than we had labeled them in the experiment without impairing the fit of the model.

Next, we evaluated the item discrimination parameters. These parameters govern to what degree a latent trait will contribute to the probability of a consensus response for a specific item (see Eq.  (2) ). Because we had experimentally induced two latent traits that accounted for mutually exclusive item sets, we expected item discrimination parameters to follow a specific pattern: For the items studied by respondents in group 1, we expected the item discrimination parameters to be greater for one latent trait compared to the other; we expected the opposite pattern for the items studied by respondents in group 2. Indeed this is the pattern we observed.  Fig. 6  includes the estimated item discrimination parameters for items studied by respondents in group 1 ( Fig. 6 , top) and items studied by respondents in group 2 ( Fig. 6 , bottom). Black circles indicate item discrimination parameters the model assigned to trait 1 and grey triangles indicate item discrimination parameters the model assigned to trait 2. Note that we again fixed the item discrimination parameters for items 1 and 2 to values in {0.0, 1.0} prior to the parameter estimation to make the model identifiable (see “The Labeling Problem” section). Items 1 and 2 also happened to be studied by different respondent groups. This is a good example of how to fix the item-discrimination parameters based on marker items. However, such items require that the researcher knows which items are good representations of the hypothesized latent traits. If such marker items are unavailable, latent traits can be difficult to interpret ( Reckase, 1997 ).

Another way to evaluate the VRM2 arises from the respondents’ performance in the memory test as evaluated with the true answer keys. Specifically, we computed for all respondents (1) the negative standardized MAD between a respondent’s responses and the true continuous answer keys, (2) the negative standardized MAD between a respondents’ responses and the ordered categorical answer keys, and 3) the standardized proportion of a respondent’s responses that matched the unordered categorical answer keys. The standardization to zero mean and unit variance ensured the comparability of these observations. The negative sign on the MAD further ensured that for all three measures higher values indicated better performance. In a final step, we summed the three standardized scores to a total performance score. Importantly, we computed the total performance score separately for items respondents had studied and items that respondents had not studied. In  Fig. 7 , we matched total performance scores with the latent trait estimates for items that respondents had studied and those that they had not studied. We found that latent trait estimates correspond well with the respondents’ performance on items they had studied but latent trait estimates for unstudied items were poor. From  Fig. 5  it is clear that latent trait estimates for both studied and unstudied items are about equal across chains. This suggests that the poor performance of latent trait estimates for unstudied items is not due to a lack of convergence of parameter estimates. Instead, responses for unstudied items contain no information about interindividual differences in latent traits. Without such information, we suspect that the VRM2 fit the latent trait estimates to noise in a consistent manner when compared across chains.

As a final step in assessing the VRM 2 parameter estimates, we turned towards answer key parameters. As a measure of answer key estimation performance, we computed the correlation and MAD between continuous answer keys and their estimates. For unordered categorical answer keys, we computed the proportion of matching answer keys and answer-key estimates. Finally, in contrast to the simulation studies, we had to evaluate ordered categorical answer keys in a different manner. Because the true appraisals were unknown, we first estimated the consensus appraisals with the VRM2. Then we used the definition of the appraisal thresholds to compute the manifest categorical responses that would result from these consensus appraisals. We then computed the MAD between the answer keys and their manifest categorical estimates.  Table 7  lists indicators of the accuracy of answer key estimates.

Because there were two consensus groups underlying the response data but the EQW method suggested only one answer key, we report the average answer key performance measures (e.g., proportion of matching keys) when the one key estimate is paired with each of the two true answer keys. Finally, we added the performance of the average respondent to  Table 7  by first computing individual performances and then averaging them across all 118 respondents (the correlation average relies on Fisher’s transformation).

The comparison of the average respondent with the EQW method illustrates the benefit of basing estimates on groups rather than individuals—the wisdom of the crowd effect (e.g.,  Galton, 1907 ). However, the results in  Table 7  also demonstrate a clear advantage of consensus analysis over simple averages and majorities when estimating the answer key from response data. In a comparison of the EQW method and the VRM for a single consensus group,  Aßfalg (2018)  found that the VRM performed notably better. For unordered categorical answer keys, for example, the VRM achieved 16% more answer key estimates that matched the true answer keys compared to the EQW method. In the present case, the difference between these methods was even more pronounced and evident across all response types.

Compared to other consensus-theory approaches, the VRM accounts for continuous responses, categorical responses, or a mixture thereof within the same test, thus, increasing the flexibility of consensus-theory applications. However,  Aßfalg (2018)  listed two notable disadvantages of the VRM compared to other consensus-theory approaches: The VRM does not account for ordered categorical responses and cannot deal with multiple consensus groups. The VRM2, we propose here, removes both of these disadvantages. The VRM2 further accounts for multiple latent traits contributing to the response data, a feature proposed for latent-trait models but previously not included in consensus analysis ( Reckase, 1997 ).

Other limitations remain. The VRM2, as the VRM, does not specify what cognitive processes give rise to the responses. An exception is the theory by  Anders and Batchelder (2015)  according to which respondents represent discrete ordered entities on a continuous appraisal scale. The continuous appraisals then give rise to ordered categorical responses. Although we applied this idea to the VRM2, we did so because of its easy adaptability to the VRM rather than its theoretical appeal. The problem we address here is however general. Few attempts have been made to experimentally test the cognitive assumptions of consensus-theory models. Exceptions are parameter-validation studies that established that experimental manipulations directed at specific parameters indeed affect these parameters in the predicted direction. Such validation studies exist for latent traits and response tendencies (e.g.,  Aßfalg & Erdfelder, 2012; Waubert de Puiseau et al., 2012 ). However, consensus-theory research tends to neglect psychological theories and instead tends to focus on accurate measurement, primarily of the answer key (e.g.,  Aßfalg, 2018; Aßfalg & Erdfelder, 2012; Karabatsos & Batchelder, 2003; Waubert de Puiseau et al., 2012, 2017 ).

Despite this limitation, the VRM2 offers more functionality than any other consensus-theory implementation: the possibility to combine response formats, to account for multiple latent traits, and to account for multiple consensus groups within a single framework. By maximizing the functionality of the model, we aimed to minimize limitations for model applications.
