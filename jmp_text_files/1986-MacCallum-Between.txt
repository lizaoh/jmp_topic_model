GREGORY ASHBY  University of California at Santa Barbara  It is shown that an important subset of linear systems theory (LST) can be represented as a special case of covariance  structure modeling (CSM). Overviews of both LST and CSM are presented, along with an explanation and illustration of their  relationship. Possible modifications and extensions of LST are described based on the more general framework of CSM. An LST  model where all inputs, state variables, and outputs are latent variables is proposed. The potential use of CSM methodology to  carry out model fitting, parameter estimation, and hypothesis testing for certain LST models is discussed.  1986 Academic  Press, Inc.  INTRODUCTION  This paper discusses relationships between two quantitative techniques that were developed in different disciplines and for  different purposes. The first of these is linear systems theory (LST), which developed,in the hard sciences and is generally  used in modeling and developing physical systems. The second is covariance structure modeling (CSM), which evolved in the  social sciences as a method for testing models of relationships among variables. It will be shown that an important subset of  LST can be viewed as a special case of CSM, and the implications of this relationship for users of these techniques will be  discussed.  We expect that most readers of this paper will be familiar with either LST or CSM, but that few will be familiar with both.  Therefore, we will provide a conceptual and mathematical review of each theory prior to identifying specific relationships  between them. For a more detailed treatment, the reader is referred to standard texts such as  Chen (1970), Padulo and Arbib (1974), or Luenberger (1979).  LST is an extremely important and powerful method of mathematically modeling certain physical systems. It is used extensively  in the physical sciences and various fields of engineering. Its use in the social sciences has been more limited, although it  has been used to model phenomena such as social interaction (Thomas & Martin, 1976), human memory (e.g., Kohonen, 1977; Hinton  & Anderson, 1981), and organizational structure and change (Hummon, Doreian, & Teuter, 1975).  For our purposes, we are interested in the relationships LST specifies among three types of variables: inputs, outputs, and  states. An input is some type of force or influence applied to the system from some external source. In a physical system such  as an electrical circuit, for instance, an input would be the electrical current or voltage entering the circuit. In  psychological applications, an input may be a stimulus applied by an experimenter, or an independent variable. An output is  some measurable response of the system. In an electrical circuit, an output would be an electrical signal emerging from the  circuit. In psychological applications, outputs may be responses of subjects, or dependent variables.  Complex systems may have large numbers of inputs and outputs, where the multiple inputs are administered simultaneously with  each other, and the multiple outputs are also observed simultaneously. Thus, a system can be considered to be a "black box,"  with inputs administered and outputs observed, and with the relationship between inputs and outputs determined by what happens  inside the box. It is inside the system that the third type of variables, state variables, exists. These are variables that  are affected by the inputs and that, in turn, affect the outputs. The state variables define a "compact representation of the  system complete enough to allow us to predict, on the basis of the inputs, exactly what the outputs will be, and also to  update the state itself' (Padulo and Arbib, 1974).  For instance, in an electrical circuit, devices such as capacitors, inductors, and resistors alter the electrical flow through  the circuit. Typically, the state variables are defined as the current into an inductor and the voltage out of a capacitor.  These measures are dependent on the electrical flow into the circuit and they affect the flow out. In psychological  applications, state variables would correspond to characteristics of the subjects (or observational units) that act as  mediating variables between the inputs and outputs. Often they may not be observable, and the researcher may not even know how  many such variables exist. These issues are discussed further below.  One remaining critical aspect of systems, implicit in the duscussion above, is the issue of time. Systems operate over time,  and inputs, outputs, and states are all dynamic variables. Furthermore, this characteristic of a system can be represented in  terms of either "continuous" time or "discrete" time. The present paper is concerned with the latter model, in which the three  types of variables are defined in  SYSTEMS THEORY AND CAUSAL MODELING 3  terms of their values at discrete points in time, rather than being defined as varying continuously over time. It is important at this point to recognize that there are two very different ways in which LST can be employed. These two  approaches correspond closely to the potential use of LST in the physical sciences vs. the behavioral sciences. In  applications of LST in the physical sciences, the exact nature of the system is often known, because the system itself is  often a man-made physical device. As a result, the number of state variables is known, and the state variables themselves may  even be directly observable. Furthermore, the values of the coefficients in A*, B*, C*, and D* of Eqs. (1) and (2) are known.  In this case, LST can be used to design a system that will perform some desired transformation on a certain input or set of  inputs, or perhaps to determine quickly how a given system will respond to some specific input.  An alternative use of LST is to employ it as a model of a system whose properties are unknown. This use could be especially  relevant in the behavioral sciences. In this case, one can obtain measures of inputs and outputs, but the state variables may  be unobservable; even the number of state variables may be unknown. Furthermore, the values of the coefficients in A*, B*, C*,  and D* would not be known. For example, when LST is used to model human memory, the elements of the system are usually assumed  to be single neurons (or neuron-like units) and the state variables to be electrical activity in each cell. In this  illustration, the researcher cannot directly observe the state variables, does not have a good idea of how many exist, and  does not know the values of the coefficients in the model.  These implications have limited the use of LST in the behavioral sciences. Before the theory can be used to its full  potential, some method is needed for recovering the contents of the "black box" by observing only its inputs and outputs. One  major goal of this paper is to move toward a solution to this problem using tools readily available to the social scientist.  OVERVIEW OF COVARIANCE STRUCTURE MODELING  CSM is a very general method for testing models of relationships among variables. As discussed by Bentler (1980), it can be  viewed as a unification or merging of several relatively independent quantitative traditions: factor analysis and reliability  theory from the field of psychometrics; simultaneous equation models from econometrics; and path analysis in biometrics.  Joreskog (1974, 1977) receives primary credit for the theoretical development of CSM and for the development of the very  general and powerful LISREL computer program (Joreskog & Sorbom, 1984) that implements CSM. Excellent introductions to CSM are  provided by Bentler (1980) and Long (1983).  In its most general version, CSM involves both latent and measured variables. A latent variable (LV) is a hypothetical  construct that is usually not directly  SYSTEMS THEORY AND CAUSAL MODELING S  measurable. Typical LV's in psychology include attitudes, mental abilities, etc. A measured variable (MV) is, obviously, a  variable that is directly observed and measured. Often MV's are used as approximations to LV's, in which case the MV is called  an indicator of the LV. For instance, a score on an intelligence test (MV) can be viewed as an indicator, but not a perfect  measurement, of the LV "intelligence." When a model involves LV's, it is desirable to employ several indicators of each LV so  that a more reliable and valid representation of the LV's may be obtained.  Given these two types of variables, a covariance structure model can then be defined as an hypothesis of a specific pattern of  relationships among a set of MV's and LV's. To represent such a model mathematically, we must first define random vectors  containing LV's and MV's. Let ~ be a column vector containing n independent LV's ~,, b2,..., ~ The independent LV's are  those which, according to the investigator's hypothesis, are not affected by any other variables in the model. Let x be a  column vector containing x,, x2,..., xy, which are the q indicators of the independent LV's. These indicators will be referred  to as independent MV's. Let n be a column vector containing m dependent LV's n,, n2,..., nm. The dependent LV's are those  which, according to the investigator's hypothesis, are affected by at least one other variable in the model. Finally, let y be  a column vector containing y,, y2,..., yp, which are the p indicators of the dependent LV's. These indicators will be referred  to as dependent MV's. All MV's and LV's are represented in deviation form, so that all have expected values of zero.  CSM can then be defined in terms of a set of three matrix equations. It  is assumed that all error terms in both 6 and a have expected values of zero. Thus, the measurement model defines each MV as a  linear combination of the LV's, plus an error term. The vectors 6 and a are referred to as "errors in variables," in that  these error terms represent the degree to which each MV is not accounted for by the LV's. A critical point is that one will almost never wish to estimate all elements of all eight parameter matrices. Rather, one's  hypothesis will determine that many of these parameters have known values-usually zeroes. For instance, if each MV is  considered to be an indicator of only one LV, then each row of Ax and Ay will have only one non-zero value in it; that is each  row will have one parameter to be estimated, and remaining coefficients are assumed to be zero. Generalizing this idea, we can  define the eight matrices as containing several different types of parmeters. Fixed parameters are those whose values are  "known" according to the hypothesized model. Free parameters are those whose values are unknown and are to be estimated.  Constrained parameters are to be estimated under the constraint that they be equal to one or more other parameters; one's  model may include an hypothesis that one or more parameters have equal, but unknown values.  The general principle, then, is that, based on an hypothesized model of the relationships among the LV's and MV's, one can  define each element of the eight parameter matrices as either fixed, free, or constrained. Given such a model and a sample  covariance matrix for the MV's, it is possible to estimate the free and constrained parameters so as to achieve optimal fit of  the model to the data. It should be noted here that the capability for obtaining meaningful parameter estimates depends on the  model being identified; i.e., it must be the case that there exists a unique solution for each free and constrained parameter.  The identification issue can be difficult to deal with in practice. Readers are referred to Joreskog and Sorbom (1984) and  Long (1983) for further discussion.  8 MAC CALLUM AND ASHBY  Given a solution for an identified model, the remaining issue of paramount interest is the goodness of fit of the solution to  the data. There is considerable information available regarding goodness of fit, with good discussions of how to evaluate this  information provided by Joreskog and Sorbom (1984) and Bentler and Bonett (1980). If parameter estimation is done via the  method of maximum likelihood, the richness of the information is enhanced by the capability for statistical hypothesis  testing. In particular, one obtains asymptotically valid significance tests of two types: (a) an overall likelihood ratio Xz  test of the hypothesis that the model holds in the population, and (b) a significance test for each free or constrained  parameter. In addition, LISREL provides a "modification index" for each fixed zero parameter; the value of this index  represents the amount by which the overall Xz would improve (decrease) if the corresponding parameter were defined as free,  rather than fixed. If parameter estimation is done via the unweighted least squares method, statistical tests are not  available, but a variety of descriptive statistics is provided for assessing fit. Careful assessment of all of this  information about fit can aid an investigator in evaluating overall fit of the hypothesized model as well as in identifying  potentially useful changes in the model.  CSM has been applied successfully in a wide range of settings. For illustrations specifically using LISREL, the reader is  referred to Joreskog and Sorbom (1984). Other interesting applications in psychology include Bentler and Speckart (1979),  Maruyama and McGarvey (1980), Fiske, Kenny, and Taylor (1982), and Fredricks and Dossett (1983). It is necessary first to define the correspondence between the various types of variables in the two models. The inputs in LST  correspond to independent MV's in CSM. Furthermore, each of these MV's will be defined as equivalent to an independent LV;  that is, each input is considered to be an error-free indicator of an independent LV. In a similar fashion, the outputs in LST  correspond to dependent MV's in CSM, and each of these MV's will be defined as equivalent to a dependent LV. Finally, the  state variables in LST also correspond to dependent LV's. Our  observation of this phenomenon led us to consider the possibility that this model was not identified; i.e., that there did not  exist a unique solution for each free parameter. In a detailed investigation of the identification issue for this model, Lee  (1985) proved that the LST model, as we have represented it in the CSM framework, is not identified. That is, there will be an  infinite number of alternative solutions for the coefficients in A*, B*, C*, and D* which would yield an identical model  estimate of X. More specifically, Lee (1985) showed that, though the parameters in D* are always identified, the parameters in  A*, B*, and C* are not. Furthermore, if all of the parameters in any one of these three matrices are given fixed numerical  values (i.e., converted from free to fixed parameters), then the parameters in the other two matrices would be identified.  (For those readers somewhat familiar with LST, we note that the demonstration system here can be shown to be both controllable  and observable. Thus, the lack of either of these properties is not the source of the identification problem we encountered.)  This finding is related to results obtained by Rindskopf (1984) in a very different context. Rindskopf (1984) employed CSM  models that included what he called "phantom variables," which were defined as LV's with no indicators; the models included  paths both to and from these phantom variables. Rindskopf (1984) pointed out that these models were not identified, but that  products of some parameters were identified. In the present context, state variables are analogous to phantom variables, and  Lee's (1985) finding that fixing certain parameters causes others to be identified parallels Rindkopfs (1984) statement that,  though individual parameters are not all identified, certain products are.  This finding has important implifications for the fitting of LST models via LISREL. If parameters are not identified, then  their estimates are not unique or meaningful, and their approximate standard errors cannot be calculated (Joreskog  SYSTEMS THEORY AND CAUSAL MODELING 17  & Sorbom, 1984). Thus, in order to obtain interpretable parameter estimates and associated sigificance tests, the researcher  must impose over-identifying restrictions. Based on Lee's (1985) findings, this can be done by fixing the values of the  parameters in one of A*, B*, or C*. Though it may often be very difficult to define reasonable values for these parameters,  there may be cases in which there is adequate prior research or theoretical foundation to do so. Fortunately, it appears that  a better option is available. It has been shown (Lee, 1985) that the identification problem becomes less severe when error  terms are added to the LST model defined in Eqs. (1) and (2), thereby creating a stochastic model. The resulting model and the  identifiability of its parameters are discussed further below.  IMPLICATIONS AND DISCUSSION  We suggest that there are at least two important implications of the finding that the discrete time LST state-space model can  be represented as a special case of CSM. The first is that the greater generality of CSM provides a specific framework for  investigating some LST models that are more general than that specified in Eqs. (1) and (2). The second is the possibility of  using the methodological developments from the field of CSM as aids to the application of LST. Both of these implications will  now be discussed in detail. This process of constraining a single unidentified parameter to be equal to an identified  parameter results in identification of all parameters in this model. It should be noted that Lee (1985) also presents a demonstration of a LST model with correlated errors in equations. He finds  that identification problems are more severe, but that they can be resolved via judicious use of constraints, as in the model  with uncorrelated error terms. It is clear that any model of the type shown in Eqs. (22) and (23) will present identification  problems which must be confronted in order to take advantage of CSM methodology.  The possibility of incorporating error terms into the LST model has been considered in the LST literature. Meditch (1969), for  instance, presents a model that corresponds to our Eqs. (22) and (23). The only important difference is that his version of  our Eq. (23) does not include the D*U, term; this omission represents a commonly used variation of the state-space model. We  believe, however, that the CSM model may provide a more explicit and flexible framework for incorporating LST disturbances,  and we suggest that this approach could be very beneficial in the application of LST in the behavioral sciences, since it  represents a more realistic approach to modeling many behavioral science systems. It can be seen that the more general CSM approach provides an explicit  framework for hypothesizing other types of relationships. This can be accomplished easily by freeing coefficients outside the  A*, B*, C*, and D* blocks within these CSM matrices. Two points need to be emphasized regarding such an approach. First, this  should not be done whimsically; CSM is a confirmatory technique, and there should be prior substantive justification for  defining the pattern of fixed and free parameters. Second, identification problems are quite likely to arise if many  additional free parameters are allowed, since many different A*, B*, C*, and D* combinations can be found that are  input-output equivalent. For example, if a dependence is hypothesized between state variables that spans more than one time  unit, an alternative model, written in the form of Eqs. This  equivalent alternative will postulate more state variables than the model with more complex dependencies, but it will not be  possible to find a sequence of inputs for which the models predict different outputs. Thus, while such generalizations appear  promising, exact relationships between this approach and prior developments in LST remain to be determined.  There is one much more general possible extension of LST that remains to be discussed. This is perhaps the most important  extension of LST suggested by the present work and represents a direct merging of LST and CSM. To begin, note that when LST is  translated into the CSM framework, the two equations of the measurement model of CSM become, in effect, dummy equations.  Considering the example in Table 2, it can be seen that the first equation simply declares that each input is an error-free  indicator of an independent LV. The second equation declares that each output is an error-free indicator of a dependent LV,  and also that the state variables represent additional dependent LV's with no indicators. Thus, in this representation of LST,  each LV has either one perfect indicator or no indicators at all. There are no LV's as they are usually understood in the  behavioral sciences; i.e., hypothetical constructs that cannot be directly observed but which can be approximated via multiple  error-perturbed indicators.  We suggest that it may be quite useful to consider a generalization of LST that incorporates true LV's. In particular, given  the representation of LST within CSM as defined above and illustrated in Table 2, it becomes straightforward to define an LST  model in which any or all of the inputs, outputs, and state variables are true LV's, approximated by multiple indicators  containing errors of measurement.  To define such a model, we need only to generalize the two equations of the measurement model, given by Eqs. (5) and (6) and  illustrated in Table 2. In particular, in Eq. That is, the inputs, outputs, and state variables related by the system defined  in Eqs. (1) and (2) are now conceived of as LV's. To generalize this LV-LST model even further, we could employ the stochastic  version of LST represented in Eqs. (22) and (23). By defining the variables in Eqs. The LST model remains intact mathematically, but is stated in terms of LV's as a restricted version  of the structural model in CSM. This model is combined with the measurement model of CSM to relate the LV's to their  indicators. Furthermore, the overall model includes the two types of errors mentioned above; i.e., (a) errors in the system  equations, and (b) errors of measurement in the MV's.  We suggest that this LV-LST model may be especially relevant to potential applications of LST in the social sciences. Theories  about linear systems operating over time in psychology and other social sciences are likely to involve LV's as inputs,  outputs, and states. For instance, a model proposed by Bentler and Speckart (1979) concerns relationships among the LV's of  attitudes, subjective norms, intentions, and behaviors. If their model were extended across time points, it could possibly be  represented in the LST framework, with attitudes and norms being inputs, intentions being a state variable, and behavior being  an output. However, these variables are all LV's. They cannot be measured directly and exactly. One could, though, obtain  measures on several indicators of each of these constructs at each point in time. In such a case, the LV-LST model just  defined provides a basis for representing the entire measurement model along with the LST model, and for estimating all the  unknown parameters.  The reader should note that one could define a number of variations of this LV-LST model that could be of interest in various  situations. For instance, one could incorporate the notion of time-varying parameters into such a model. One could also allow  for the situation in which no indicators could be obtained for the state variables. The important point is that this model  represents a fairly general and flexible extension of LST that may be of value in the behavioral sciences. (We wish to note  that it may be possible to represent this LV-LST model within the  SYSTEMS THEORY AND CAUSAL MODELING 21  LST state-space model without true LV's. This may be done by considering all indicators to be either inputs or outputs and  increasing the number of state variables. We suggest, however, that the LV-LST model may be much more useful from a conceptual  and operational perspective.)  We now consider the second general implication of our work: the possible use of CSM methodology in the application of certain  LST models. We suggest that perhaps the greatest potential gains are in the areas of estimating parameters, testing  hypotheses, and evaluating goodness of fit. The LISREL computer program (Joreskog & Sorbom, 1984) has become the standard of  the field in CSM and is widely available. As pointed out earlier, it offers several different types of parameter estimates and  a variety of information about goodness of fit. Of particular interest would be the possibility of estimating parameters of a  linear system via the method of maximum likelihood. Such an approach would provide the capability of testing the significance  of each coefficient in the system. In addition, it would be possible to carry out a likelihood ratio Xz test of fit of the  model, and even to test the significance of the difference between certain types of competing models (Bentler & Bonett, 1980).  To our knowledge, such a general approach to maximum likelihood estimation and significance testing has not been presented in  the LST literature. However, it should be noted that such a capability has been developed for some important specialized  models. Of special interest here are time-series models, virtually all of which can be represented as special cases of the  general LST approach (e.g., Luenberger, 1979). Maximum likelihood estimation and associated statistical tests have been  developed for some time-series techniques, such as the Box-Jenkins model (Box & Jenkins, 1970; Priestly, 1981). However, our  work provides a basis for extending this to the state-space version of LST (and the more general models discussed above),  providing that the necessary conditions are met.  The validity and avilability of these various types of inferential statistics depend on three factors: (a) the sample  covariance matrix, S, being non-singular; (b) the model estimate of Z, obtained by substituting parameter estimates into Eq.  (12), being positive-definite; and (c) the assumption that the MV's (inputs and outputs) follow a multivariate normal  distribution in the population. The issue of the singularity of S may be a problem in some applications. Recall that S is of  order (i* +j*)(t* + 1), containing variances and covariances of all inputs and outputs across all time points. Thus, a  necessary condition for the non-singularity of S is that N>, (i* +j*)(t* + 1), where N is the "sample size;" i.e., the number  of input and output vectors observed. In complex physical systems with large numbers of inputs, outputs, and time points, it  may be unusual for this condition to be satisfied. It is difficult to say whether this issue will be more or less problematic  in applications of LST in the behavioral sciences. However, the researcher should certainly strive to satisfy this condition  since the use of the maximum likelihood approach clearly enriches the information available in a CSM solution.  We now consider the second condition mentioned above, the positive-definiteness of the model estimate of X. It can be shown  that, for the LST model represented by  22 MAC CALLUM AND ASHBY  Eqs. (1) and (2), this condition will never be satisfied. As Eq. (4) illustrates, Eqs. (1) and (2) can be be re-written in a  form that expresses the outputs as linear combinations of the inputs, omitting state variables from the equations. Thus,  according to the LST model, there will be exact linear dependencies between outputs and inputs, thereby always yielding a  singular model estimate of E. Therefore, it will never be possible to obtain maximum likelihood estimates of the parameters of  the LST model represented in Eqs. (1) and (2). However, we have presented a variation of this model that would eliminate this  problem. In the stochastic model defined by Eqs. (22) and (23), the problem described here would, as a rule, vanish, since  there would not exist perfect linear dependencies between outputs and inputs. We suggested earlier that the stochastic model  is more realistic in behavioral science settings. We now point out a further advantage of the stochastic model in that it  enables the use of maximum likelihood estimation along with the accompanying inferential statistical information.  The third requirement for the use of maximum likelihood estimation is the assumption that the inputs and outputs follow a  multivariate normal distribution in the population at each time point. Very little is known about the robustness of the  statistical tests when this assumption is violated. Under such conditions, Joreskog and Sorbom (1984) suggest conservative  interpretation and possibly the use of some techniques for obtaining robust estimates of variances and covariances prior to  fitting the model to the data. In the present context, the assumption of multivariate normality of the inputs and outputs at  each time point implies that the inputs and outputs are stochastic processes. We note that there is a substantial literature  on the topic of stochastic processes in LST (e.g., Papoulis, 1965; Meditch, 1969). Most of these developments focus on  treating the inputs as stochastic processes, but the representation of outputs as stochastic processes has also been  considered (Meditch, 1969). We suggest that the CSM maximum likelihood approach of considering both inputs and outputs to be  stochastic serves to combine both issues into one framework, and also provides an approach that will be appropriate in many  applications in the behavioral sciences.  We believe that, when these conditions for maximum likelihood are satisfied in practice, it will be advantageous to employ  this approach. If maximum likelihood estimation is not possible, one can still employ CSM methods to obtain least squares  parameter estimates. The capability for statistical inference is lost, but one can still estimate the parameters of a LST  model and emply descriptive measures of fit to evaluate its adequacy (Joreskog & Sorbom, 1984).  There are several other issues inherent in the process of estimating the parameters of a LST model that must be discussed at  least briefly. One involves the problem of defining the number of state variables. Whereas in physical systems the number of  state variables (k*) may be known exactly, in the social sciences the investigator may be able only to hypothesize the number.  In such situations, the issue of determining the value of k* becomes part of the process of fitting the model to the data. We  suggest obtaining a series of CSM solutions, one for each value of k* over a (small) range, and to assess the overall fit of  the model at each value of k*. We  SYSTEMS THEORY AND CAUSAL MODELING 23  speculate that fit would be poor with k* too small, but would improve substantially when the correct value of k* is employed.  Investigation of this or other possible approaches to this problem remains to be done.  Another issue inherent in the estimation process concerns the form of the A matrix from the LST model. The LST literature  discusses a number of possible canonical forms for A* (e.g., Chen, 1970). One that is particularly simple and interesting is  for A * to be diagonal. In this form, a system is defined as "uncoupled," meaning that the effects among the state variables  are restricted to any state variable at time t affecting only the same state variable at time (t + 1). Note that the system  represented by the solution in Table 5 was uncoupled. In estimating parameters of a linear system via the methods of CSM, one  can easily impose the restriction that the solution represent an uncoupled system by defining the off-diagonal elements of  each copy of A * as fixed zeroes, and estimating only the diagonal elements of A*. Similarly, one can restrict A* (or any of  the other parameter matrices, far that matter) to be of a variety of other specific forms by appropriately specifying fixed,  free, and constrained parameters.  Another issue related to the estimation process involves the distinction between time-varying and time-invariant systems.  Recall that the system defined by Eqs. (1) and (2) is time-invariant since A*, B*, C*, and D* do not vary across time. In a  time-varying system, these would be replaced by A*, B*, C*, and D*. To estimate the parameters of a time-invariant system, we  use the notion of constrained parameters, where we constrain the corresponding elements of the copies of A*, B*, C*, and D* to  be equal. To estimate the parameters of a time-varying system, we could simply drop these constraints, thus allowing A*, B*,  C*, and D* to vary across time points.  It is especially interesting to note that, under the maximum likelihood approach, it would be possible to inferentially test  the difference between the time-invariant and time-varying models. Let XTI with dfT1 be the result of the likelihood ratio  test for the time-invariant model, and let XTv with dfTv be the result for the time-varying model. Then, since the former  model is a restricted version of the latter, we can test the hypothesis that the two models are equivalent via XZ = XT I - XTv  with df = dfT, -dfTv . A significant result indicates that the time-varying model is significantly better. To our knowledge,  no capability for such a test exists in the LST literature.  It is interesting to note that in the LST literature the distinction between timevarying and time-invariant models is  generally presented as an "either/or" distinction. In fact, though, the CSM approach allows a middle ground. Considering the  illustration in Table 5, and recalling the concepts of free and constrained parameters, note that CSM does not require us to  choose between only the two options of (a) constraining all corresponding elements of A*, B*, C*, and D* to be equal across  time points, or (b) imposing no such constraints. Rather, we are allowed to hypothesize any particular pattern of constraints  involving subsets of these matrices or their elements. For instance, it would be simple to impose constraints in such a way as  to represent a system in which B* is time-invariant, but  24 MAC CALLUM AND ASHBY  A*, C*, and D* arc not, or in which some coefficients within these matrices are time-invariant and others are not. As  mentioned earlier, it is important to have a strong basis for such hypotheses. Thus, it can be seen that CSM provides a  framework for representing and testing LST models that have a mixture of timevarying and time-invariant parameters.  We have emphasized the generality and flexibility of using the CSM framework to fit certain LST models in the behavioral  sciences. We wish to point out, however, that there are some potentially problematic issues that may arise in practical  applications. One such issue involves the possibility that the covariance matrix to be analyzed may be very large. Recall that  the covariance matrix will be of order (i* + j*)(t* + 1). Though there is no theoretical limit to the size of the covariance  matrix, there are practical limits at the present time in that a system with a large number of inputs, outputs, or time points  will produce a very large covariance matrix and may require large amounts of computer memory and time for computer programs  such as LISREL to analyze. This problem may be somewhat alleviated via the further development of CSM computer programs, and  possibly by the use of an alternative mathematical framework for representing CSM (e.g., Bentler & Weeks, 1980; McArdle &  McDonald, 1984). Nevertheless, this issue is of practical concern at the present time.  A related issue involves the sample of observations from which the covariance matrix is obtained. The CSM literature  emphasizes the fact that large samples are required in order to obtain stable parameter estimates and valid significance tests  (e.g., Boomsma, 1983; Joreskog & Sorbom, 1984). In fitting an LST model, this means it is necesary to obtain a large number of  observations of the entire system of inputs and outputs across time points. The model is hypothesized to represent the  relationships among inputs, state variables, and outputs in the ensemble from which the sample is drawn. The observations that  make up the sample are considered to be independent observations drawn from that ensemble. The ensemble would consist of all  possible realizations of behavior of a single subject, or if subjects are assumed to be homogeneous, of the behavior of the  population of subjects. Given this sample, we have shown that one can obtain a sample covariance matrix for the inputs and  outputs, and then, using CSM, estimate the coefficients of the linear system. These coefficients, the elements of A*, B*, C*,  and D*, are considered to be population parameters. That is, in our approach, these parameters do not represent and cannot be  estimated from a single observation. Rather, they represent a population, and a large sample from that population is required  in order to obtain accurate estimates of them. This approach and view differ from some uses of LST in the physical sciences,  where system parameters can be determined based on a single error-free observation. Thus, the issue of sample size is not  really relevant for the deterministic model of Eqs. (1) and (2). But sample size is an important issue for the stochastic  model of Eqs. (22) and (23), whether the variables in this model are MV's or LV's. We consider this an important practical  matter because it is the model represented by Eqs. (22) and (23) that will be appropriate for use in many behavioral science  applications.  SYSTEMS THEORY AND CAUSAL MODELING 25  As a final point of discussion, we wish to note that there exists previous work in the application of CSM in the social  sciences that is tangentially related to our work. In particular, several authors (e.g., Dwyer, 1983; Fredericksen & Rotondo,  1979; Joreskog, 1979; Joreskog & Sorbom, 1977; Molenaar, 1985) have discussed the application of CSM to longitudinal data and  have employed models that bear some resemblance to LST (though there is no indication these authors were aware of LST per se).  Rather, these authors present a variety of specific models of representing relationships among variables over time. Though  none of these models correspond to LST, these authors do encounter some of the same problems and issues we have discussed;  e.g., the identification problem, the incorporation of errors in equations and errors of measurement, and the use of LV'S. By  comparison, we hope that our work will provide a useful general framework for defining and fitting models using the  state-space version of LST, and the various extensions of that model that we have suggested. We view this model and its  extensions as important special cases of CSM, which may be of substantial value in behavioral science investigations of  variables over time.  We believe that users of either LST or CSM can benefit from the developments presented here. The LST user can benefit from the  greater generality offered by CSM, providing mechanisms for extending LST in a variety of ways, as discussed above. The CSM  user can benefit from the definition of a very interesting and useful special case that may be applicable in a variety of  settings. Finally, each field may benefit from the vast literature and continued development of the other. We expect that  there are methodological issues and techniques in each of these fields that are of relevance and value to the other, but which  have not yet been recognized.  SUMMARY  We believe there are three important points to emphasize. First, it was shown that the discrete-time LST state-space model  defined by Eqs. (1) and (2) can be represented as a special case of CSM. Second, we emphasize that the greater generality of  the CSM approach offers an explicit and interesting framework for considering extensions of LST. The most interesting and  important such extension considered here is, we think, the development of a LV version of LST. In addition to defining inputs,  state variables, and outputs as LV's, this general model incorporates disturbance terms in the system equations [as shown in  Eqs. (22) and (23)] and errors of measurement in all MV's, and treats all MV's as stochastic processes. Though some of these  developments have been previously considered in the LST literature, the capability of CSM to incorporate all of these features  into one framework provides behavioral scientists with a flexible mathematical representation of a potentially valuable  version of LST. Finally, we emphasize the potential value in adapting the methodology of CSM to fitting LST models to data.  This process can include the application of a wide range of CSM procedures for  26 MAC CALLUM AND ASHBY  estimating parameters and assessing goodness of fit, as well as the use of CSM computer programs such as LISREL (Joreskog &  Sorbom, 1984), which has been widely used in some areas of the social sciences for several years.
