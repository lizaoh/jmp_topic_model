In 1977, Saaty proposed a scaling method for priorities based on the normalized, positive eigenvector corresponding to the largest eigenvalue of a matrix of paired comparisons. Originally designed for analyzing preferences expressed on a ratio scale, this technique was adapted by Jensen to handle ordinal,-“pick-the-winner” type, dominance data. It involves a coding parameter, a> 1, whose interpretation is akin to the sociometric concept of attenuation of influence transmitted through intermediaries developed by Katz. This paper examines the implications of Jensen’s proposal and illustrates the effect of the constant a as a comparison reliability factor on the ordinal ranking of alternatives. Jensen’s procedure is seen to provide a continuum of orderings between the Kendall scores, or total vote count ranking, and Wei’s solution to the tournament ranking problem described by Kendall. In this context, circumstances are also delineated where Saaty’s eigenvalue-based index of cardinal consistency is functionally related to a classical measure of ordinal consistency involving the number of circular triads in a respondent’s judgments. Beyond its intrinsic theoretical interest and practical value, it is argued that the methodology put forward by Jensen is a useful diagnostic tool for analyzing the ordinal structure of pairwise comparisons and for spotting potentially contentious applications of Saaty’s scaling procedure, © 1993 Academic Press, Inc.

In addition to providing intuitive support for Proposition 2.2, the preceding discussion clarifies the role of the coding parameter in Jensen’s adaptation of Saaty’s eigenvector method and may help to rationalize the occurrence of rank reversals in applications. By enhancing each victory a-fold and dampening the importance of the losses by the same multiplicative factor, this parameter effectively allows one to diminish the influence of multistep path connections on the determination of the final priority weights and on the ordinal ranking that ensues. Its interpretation would thus appear to be closely akin to the concept of “attenuation in influence transmitted through intermediaries,” introduced by Katz (1953) in the context of a sociometric analysis to account for the effective functioning of an existing link between individuals when evaluating their relative status in a group. Based on the resolution of an eigenvector problem, his paper proposed a status index which, through a functional dependence on an attenuation factor similar to the scalar a, took into consideration who chooses potential high-status members of a group, and not merely the number of times each individual is selected. To paraphrase Katz, the coding parameter a@>1 mvolved in Jensen’s proposal (or better yet, the quantity p=1-—1/a, which always lies between 0 and 1) might be described as a constant which “has the force of a probability of effectiveness of a single link.” Katz’s suggestion that the choice of this constant should depend on the context of a particular investigation, appears more appropriate still: assuming that the difficulty of making dominance judgments is relatively constant over all comparisons, the value of p could be assimilated, much in the manner proposed by Tritchler and Lockwood (1991), to a reliability index providing a global rating of judgment difficulty. Vested with this meaning, Jensen’s adaptation of Saaty’s method would then provide a mathematical model well suited to the analysis of context effects or individual differences across subjects having expressed identical ordinal preferences. To be truly meaningful, however, the estimation or selection of a (or p) would have to be based on quantitative evidence, in addition to the set of pairwise ordinal, dominance data. Suppose, for example, that in a food testing study on animals, various sets of stimuli affected mean response time but left the subject’s pattern of ordinal preferences unchanged. Under the assumption that easier or less confusing judgments are made faster, the choice of p= 1-—1/a values might be constrained to be inversely telated to mean response time, so that judgments considered to be harder or more uncertain would tend to make p (respectively a) closer to 0 (respectively 1). Depending on the nature of the ordinal inconsistencies in the animal’s responses, an application of the Jensen-Saaty algorithm might thus provide evidence for associating different rankings to different experimental conditions. As a second illustration, imagine that an individual has been asked to compare two at a time a certain number of leadership attributes expected of candidates running for office, expressing personal preferences each time on an ordinal, “pick-the-winner” scale. Such attributes might include charisma, experience, professional abilities, personal

integrity, and many others. To explore the potential impact of the position’s responsibility level on the respondent’s opinion of an appropriate leadership profile, the interviewee might then be asked, at a second stage, to provide an overall measure of strength in his/her feelings, depending on the responsibility level in the organizational hierarchy, such as Chair versus Dean and/or President in an academic institution, say. For further illustrations and an alternative approach which allows the effectiveness of links or reliability in judgment to vary between comparison pairs, the reader is referred to the paper by Tritchler and Lockwood (1991). 

Naturally, these considerations do not alleviate the selection or estimation of an appropriate value for the coding parameter in the absence of quantitative information rating the intensity of a respondent’s ordinal preferences, or the degree of difficulty attached to his/her choice task. The same criticism could actually be made of Katz’s attenuation factor and, for that matter, of any parameter involved in the definition of all-purpose stochastic models. Although the possibility of rank reversal raised by Example 2.1 shows that the initial use intended by Jensen (1986) for his adaptation of Saaty’s method may have been misguided, his proposal actually opens up a new path for modeling experimental data for which a global measure of the reliability of paired comparisons expressed on an ordinal, “pick-the-winner” scale, would be available. As will be seen in the final section of the present paper, it also constitutes a handy diagnostic tool for analyzing the ordinal structure of sets of pairwise comparisons expressed on a numerical ratio scale, prior to application of Saaty’s scaling procedure.

6, DISCUSSION 

Motivated in part by the work of Jensen (1986) and Uppuluri (1987), the investigation reported in this paper draws some of the implications of a proposed extension of Saaty’s scaling method which would make it amenable to deriving an ordinal ranking from pairwise preferences expressed on a “pick-the-winner,” ordinal scale. The extension involves expressing these subjective judgments on a three-point scale {1,a,1/a} for an appropriately selected value of a>1 (or perhaps p=1— i/a), applying Saaty’s eigenvector algorithm to extract a normalized, positive eigenvector, w*, and then declaring alternative j preferred to alternative i, denoted i <* j, whenever w? < w’. In the present authors’ estimation, this procedure has the potential to prove useful in situations where an agent is incapable or unwilling to supply paired comparison judgments on a ratio scale, but direct or indirect quantitative evidence can be used to assess the respondent’s intensity of preferences or difficulty of choice induced by context effects. Examples of that sort were given in Section 3, and other potential areas of application are listed by Tritchler and Lockwood (1991) in their paper concerned with the reliability of paired comparisons. 

As pointed out by Jensen (1986, p. 206) in the language of elections, 

Ratio-scale responses demand much more of voters than simply picking the winner from every pait. One danger is that different voters may apply the scale using different interpretations of the adjectives which describe points on the scale. (Likert scales suffer from the same problem.) 

Subject to this limitation, it seems clear that the expression of individual preferences on the finer ratio scale should be encouraged wherever meaningful and feasible. However, before rushing to analyze the resulting data with Saaty’s scaling method, or any other prioritization technique such as the logarithmic least squares solution, it might be wise to have a closer look at the ordinal structure of the response matrix to investigate the effect of potential inconsistencies of this nature on the
