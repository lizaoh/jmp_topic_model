INTRODUCTION  The random utility model is gradually gaining support as a viable description of discrete choice, explaining the observed  variability in the outcomes of identical choice experiments in terms of a probabilistic rather than deterministic mode of  rational behavior. Although it was originally conceived by Thurstone (1927) as the assignment of a random variable UX  measuring utility to each individual alternative x, Block and Marschak (1960) later demonstrated that a random utility model  is equivalently formulated as the assignment of a random (partial) order to the entire class of allowable alternatives.  Consequently, the random utility model has the same structure as a model of state-dependent utility, for which the  optimization is performed ex post to the realization of the random state variable. We obtain a stochastic choice function  which usually cannot be generated by the ex ante optimization of an expected utility function.  The random utility model was ushered into prominence in mathematical psychology by the recognition that Luce's "strict  utility" model (1959) constitutes a special case, because it satisfies one of Block's and Marschak's fundamental  characterizations (1960). The experimental shortcomings of the Luce model eventually inspired the random utility models of  Restle (1961), Tversky (1972), and Edgell and Geisler (1980) in the psychology literature. Econometric applications were  pioneered by McFadden (1973), who initially considered a multinomial logit model that is essentially equivalent to a  logarithmic scale transformation of the Luce model. Most of these contributions in both psychology and economics involve  parametric specifications of the general model leading to specialized experimental predictions.  The nonparametric approach of Block and Marschak was also continued by Falmagne (1978), who demonstrated that the  non-negativity of the BlockMarschak polynomials is a necessary and sufficient condition for representing a stochastic choice  function with a random utility model. Falmagne's theorem was generalized from univalent to multivalent choice functions by  Barbera and Pattianiak (1986), but the multivalence of a stochastic choice function obviously confounds the possibility of  empirical refutation. Nevertheless, their work further clarifies the relation between random utility and revealed preference,  suggesting that Fishburn's original investigation (1978) of this matter is somewhat inadequate outside the confines of the  Luce model. Unfortunately, Falmagne's condition is difficult to verify in practice, and a more common method of testing a  parametric specification of the random utility model involves finding sme notion of "stochastic transitivity" that can be  associated with it. In the next section, we demonstrate that if a stochastic choice function is ex post rational with respect  to a random utility function, then it is also ex ante rational and the ex ante revealed weak preference relation is  transitive. This result suggests that the transitivity of ex ante revealed preference is a useful concept of "stochastic  transitivity".  Perhaps the most serious criticism of the random utility model was recently expressed by Machina (1985), who argued that a  stochastic choice function is better modeled in terms of the ex ante optimization of a determinate utility function defined  upon lotteries instead of the ex post optimization of a random utility function defined upon discrete prizes. This hypothesis  is a plausible alternative for explaining individual choice behavior, but it carries less weight in econometric applications  involving the aggregate choice behavior of a population. In the third section, we demonstrate that ex ante transitive  rationality characterizes ex ante rationality with respect to a determinate utility function. Consequently, the random utility  model is a special case of the determinate utility model. In the fourth section, we characterize the additive determinate  utility model in terms of "acyclic" choice probabilities, and verify that the Luce model satisfies this condition.  2.
