ROSS  Department of Psychology, Stanford University, Stanford, California 94305  This article considers procedures for combining individual probability distributions that belong to some "family" into a  "group" probability distribution that belongs to the same family. The procedures considered are Vincentizing, in which  quantiles are averaged across distributions; generalized Vincentizing, in which the quantiles are transformed before  averaging; and pooling based on the distribution function or the probability density function. Some of these results are  applied to models oreaction time in psychological experiments.  INTRODUCTION  In this paper, we consider ways of combining individual probability distributions that belong to some "family" into a "group"  probability distribution, such that the group distribution belongs to the same family as the individual distributions.  Experimenters often collect a relatively small number of observations from some number of subjects and want to combine the  data, so as to increase the sample size, before testing their hypotheses. However, simply pooling the data is likely to result  in a group distribution that does not reflect the properties of the individual distributions, so that it would be  inappropriate to use the group distribution to test hypotheses about the properties of the individual distributions.  The problems encountered in using group data to make inferences about individuals were noted by learning theorists, among  others. For example, Estes (1956) proposed a classification of learning functions. One class consists of functions that are  "invariant" under simple averaging and are such that the parameters of the group curve are simply the means of the  corresponding parameters of the individual curves. For a second class of functions, the form is unchanged by combining data,  but the parameters of the group function are not simply arithmetic means of the individual parameters (e.g., they may be  geometric means). The final class Estes defined includes all other functions, i.e., those functions whose form is changed by  combining data from individual functions. We shall be concerned with the issue of combining individual probability  distributions and parallels to Estes' classes will be apparent.  This issue of combining individual probability distributions has been raised recently by Ratcliff (1979), who has suggested a  combination rule, called Vincentizing, that is based on the quantiles of the distributions. Some discussions of the invariance of probability distributions under a combination rule have emphasized the "shape" of the  distribution (e.g., AZosteller & Tukey, 1977). In this paper, we will define a "family" by some invariant features of the  individual distribution functions (d.f.'s) or the probability density functions (p.d.f.'s), rather than by the shape of the  functions (although, as Ratcliff (1979) points out, for distributions that do not take on a wide variety of shapes, such as  are posited as reaction time distributions, these two approaches are very similar).  Group distribution functions are of interest in at least two situations. As mentioned earlier, we often have only a small  number of observations from the individual distributions. One use of group distributions, then, is to improve the power of  tests that require a large number of observations (e.g., tests of changes in the tail or the skewness of a distribution). A  second situation in which group distributions may be used is when a specific distribution has been hypothesized, or has been  derived from hypotheses about underlying processes, and one wishes to look at changes in the parameters as a function of  experimental manipulations.  In the following sections, we will explore the conditions under which Vincentizing leads to a group distribution that belongs  to the same family as the individual distributions, and that depends on (group) parameters that are averages of the individual  parameters. We will also consider cases where a generalization of Vincentizing is the appropriate combination rule, and cases  where combination should be based on the d.f. or p.d.f., rather than the quantiles. Much of the impetus for this work comes  from an interest in reaction time distributions (e.g., Ratcliff, 1979), and many of the families to be considered in this  article are plausible reaction time models. One approach would be to assume that the individual  distributions belong to the same (unknown) family, and then to try to use the procedures implied by Theorems 3 and 4 by  finding the transformation, h(t), which renders all Q-Q plots linear. The desired transformation may not exist if the  individual distributions belong to different families and, even if it exists, it may not be an easily recognizable function.  So, to assess the feasibility of this approach, we used the computer to sample from distributions belonging to the same family  for which the desired transformation was known. We then examined how easy it was to identify the transformation.  To do this we considered families for which the desired transformation, h(t), belonged to the set {log t, to for u = -1, - s ,  z , 1, 2}, and we compared the value of some index of linearity for the desired transformation with the values for the other  transformations in the set. We used this set of transformations because log t behaves like the zeroth power of t (Tukey, 1977,  pp. 90-91), and the set is likely to contain an excellent approximation to the desired transformation in most situations.  Many factors are relevant in this search, including the particular distribution used to generate the samples, the number of  samples, the number of observations per sample, the heterogeneity (degree of variation of distribution parameters) across  samples, the number of points on a Q-Q plot, and the particular index of linearity used. We used three distributions, the  logistic, for which the desired transformation is h(t) = t, the Weibull, for which h(t) = log t, and the inverse exponential,  for which h(t) = t-1. We arbitrarily set the number of samples at 10, and varied the number of observations per sample from 20  to 60 to 100. In choosing, for example, the 10 logistic distributions, from each of which a sample would be drawn, we varied  either the centering parameter across distributions in some simulations, the scale parameter in some simulations, or both  centering and scale parameters across distributions. In each case, the range of parameter variation was either "small" or  "large." The number of points on a Q-Q plot was either 10 or 20. Finally, we considered four indices of linearity, two based  on the product moment correlation between the coordinates (Qik , Q;x), h = 1, 2,..., 10 or 20, of the points on each Q-O plot,  and two based on the average curvature of each 0-Q plot. CONCLUDING REMARKS  We have tried to show how appropriate procedures for combining individual distributions into a group distribution can be  derived from a knowledge of the family to which the distributions belong. When the family is unknown, one can search for a  transformation of the data that most nearly linearizes all Q-Q plots. If such a transformation can be found, an empirical  group distribution can be defined by Vincentizing the transformed data, and can be used for describing and testing group  differences. Additionally, information about the functional form of the true group distribution can be obtained by plotting  the obtained group quantiles against the quantiles of various theoretical distributions, and then seeing which theoretical  distribution yields a straight line.  The term "family" here refers to a set of functions of a variable t that are indexed by a vector parameter, O. That is,  members of a family, differ only with respect to 0. Our results can be described as characterizations of different families in  terms of the concatenation operations, for example, Vincentizing, under which the families are closed. Unfortunately, the term  "family" is not standard. The family characterized in Theorem 1 is already referred to as a "type" (see, for example, Feller  1966, p. 134),3 and the term "class" usually has a broader meaning than "family" as used here. Hopefully, the present usage  will prove helpful in other investigations.
