FISHBURN  Advanced Research Department, Research Analysis Corporation, McLean, Virginia  This paper explores the notions of stationary value mechanisms and stationary transition value mechanisms in time-dependent  processes. Contemporary utility theory serves as the basis of the discussion. After defining the two notions of stationarity  in terms of additive forms for an individual's utilities of histories of a process, they are compared to more general additive  forms for utility and to stationarity concepts in the context of probability theory and managerial economics.  Two axiomatizations of the stationary value mechanism and stationary transition value mechanism notions are then given in the  context of expected utility theory. The first formulation uses general finite gambles; the second uses only simple 50-50  gambles. Under both formulations it is shown what must be added to the axioms for a stationary value mechanism in order that  the utility function for `state utilities' possess a mathematical expectation property in situations where the `states' are  taken to be gambles.  INTRODUCTION  The purpose of this paper is to axiomatize and discuss the notions of stationary value mechanisms and stationary transition  value mechanisms in time-dependent processes. The idea of stationary value or utility is not new. In recent times Wold (1952),  Koopmans (1960), Koopmans, Diamond, and Williamson (1964) and Diamond (1965) have discussed notions related to those in this  paper in the context of consumer demand behavior over successive time periods. Mosteller and Nogee (1951) and Davidson,  Suppes, and Siegel (1957), among others, in attempting to measure utility experimentally, assumed stationarity in the utility  sense over the successive trials in choice-making experiments. The possibility of using stationary utilities and stationary  transition utilities in normative analysis of sequential decision problems has been noted by Fishburn (1965b, c).  The paper is divided into two main parts. The first part, comprising Secs. I, II, and III, defines the utility-theory notions  of stationary value mechanisms and stationary transition value mechanisms for discrete-time finite-horizon processes and then  compares these with stationary forms in probability theory and managerial economics.  The remainder of the paper then examines the stationary value notions in the context of expected utility theory. THE PROCESS  The type of process under consideration is characterized by an individual who is part of a "system" which at any given time is  in one "state" x of a set of states X. The set X may be composed of a finite or infinite number of states, which may be  unidimensional or multidimensional. In one interpretation, to be discussed in Sees. These gambles should not be  confused with gambles built upon histories, as described in Secs. IV and VII.  In the present discussion it is assumed that the individual (or experimenter, if the individual is part of a choice-making  experiment) is concerned with a horizon consisting of n stages or periods, perhaps of equal length, beyond the present. In  period zero (the present) the system is in state x,, assumed to be known.' In period 1 the system will make a one-step  transition from state xo to state xl (where xl may be identical to x,); in period 2 the system will make a one-step transition  from xl to x,; ... ; and in period n the system will make a one-step transition from xn_1 to state xn . Any unfolding of this  stochastic or time-dependent process from the given initial state xo to the state that occurs in period n will be called a  history of the process.  A history of the n-stage process is written as an n-dimensional vector in the form (xl ) X21 , xn), xi in X, i = 1, 2, -, n.  The initial state xo is not made explicit in this vector, but may be understood as given. To keep the discussion from becoming  too complicated, it is assumed that any vector of the form (xl , x2 , , xn) in the product set X x X x ... x X (n times) may  occur.  Because it does not affect the notions of this paper, we shall usually not be explicit about how much control the individual  exercises over the process. EXPECTED UTILITY THEORY WITH 50-50 GAMBLES  The idea of using 50-50 or even-chance gambles to define utility predates the general gambles approach by about 17 years, and  is due to Ramsey (1931, 1950). In more recent times Davidson, Suppes, and Siegel (1957) used this notion (and the notion of a  stationary value mechanism) extensively in their experiments concerned with measuring utility based on the 50-50 gambles idea.
