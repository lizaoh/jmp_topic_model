On the Interpretation of Response Time vs Onset Asynchrony Functions: Application to Dual-Task and Precue-Utilization Paradigms 

Wolfgang Schwarz and Anja Ischebeck 

University of Nijmegen 

The central bottleneck model of dual-task performance (H. Pashler and J. C. Johnston, 1998, Quarterly Journal of Experimental Psychology, 46A, 51�82) and the serial processing model of precue utilization (R. Gottsdanker, 1992, Acta Psychologica, 79, 21�43) are based on a common formal structure: They both represent response time as RT{=max(X&{, Y)+Z, where X, Y, Z denote the duration of certain processing stages specified by the models and { denotes the onset asynchrony (SOA) between two stimuli. We consider this model within a stochastic framework in which the stage durations are random variables following an arbitrary joint distribution and derive properties of the function relating E[RT{] to SOA. We present a distribution�free result which relates the slope of this function to the distribution of the random durations of the assumed processing stages. Our results allow for a direct, model-based interpretation of data from related experiments; specifically, they show how the slope of the SOA-function depends on experimental factors which selectively influence individual processing stages. We explain the implications of our results for models of dual-task performance and precue utilization and illustrate their application to data obtained by M. C. A disadvantage of this research strategy, though, is that it tends to treat developments stemming from different paradigms as diverse and separate; it thus may overlook the conceptual similarities which sometimes exist across different paradigms, e.g., similarities of the formal structure of models used to analyze and to interpret data from different paradigms. If such a correspondence between paradigms exists, tools and diagnostics used with one paradigm could then be translated into the context of another paradigm given that the correspondence between the two paradigms is properly understood. Of course, conceptual correspondence does not automatically imply that the same cognitive mechanism (e.g., the same neural population) is activated in both paradigms. 

The present note describes an example of this, namely the close conceptual correspondence between the PRP paradigm (Pashler 6 Johnston, 1998) and the precue�utilization paradigm (Gottsdanker, 1992), and the two major models which have been developed to explain findings within these two contexts. We point out that although the substantive focus and details of the experimental procedures are quite different with these paradigms, the formal structures behind important models used in their context are essentially identical, and we derive a general quantitative property characterizing this common formal structure. We then discuss in detail the application and interpretation of our results within the context of each of the two paradigms. 

The outline of this note is as follows. First we describe the PRP and the precueutilization paradigms, and we develop formal descriptions of the most influential model used with each of these paradigms. In both cases, it is not our aim to give exhaustive reviews of empirical findings within these two fields; rather we concentrate on the properties and predictions of the models. In Section 2 we derive our main proposition, discuss some of its consequences, present a detailed example, and relate it to a proposition derived by Ulrich and Miller (1997). In Section 3, we apply our results to the two paradigms, concentrating on the effects of factors which selectively influence the specific processing stages assumed by the models. 

The PRP Paradigm and the Central Bottleneck Model 

It is well established that our ability to rapidly respond to signals in close temporal succession is severely limited (for excellent reviews, see Pashler, 1994a, and Pashler 6 Johnston, 1998). In a standard PRP experiment subjects are presented with two stimuli, S1 and S2 , either simultaneously or in close temporal succession (with S1 preceding S2). Each of the stimuli requires a separate speeded choice response; the instructions particularly stress rapid responses to S1 . To minimize perceptual interference, many studies use bimodal stimuli; often, S1 is a tone and S2 is a character. An important independent variable in this paradigm is the time (SOA) from the onset of S1 to the onset of S2 ; the main dependent variable is the time (RT) required to respond to S2 as measured from the S2 onset. The principal result of this experiment then is a plot of the mean RT to S2 against the SOA (asynchronous onset), sometimes called the ``SOA function.''

For very short SOAs the slope of the SOA function may approach a value of &1 under some conditions, which means that each millisecond (ms) head start of S1 reduces the RT to S2 by the same amount. 

Descriptively, results like those in Fig. 1 indicate a general limitation of our ability to rapidly respond to separate signals presented in close temporal succession, but it is less obvious which psychological mechanisms are responsible for this limitation, and how it depends on further experimental manipulations. The model which has received the most attention, both empirical and theoretical, is the central bottleneck model originally proposed by Welford (1952) and considerably elaborated by Pashler and colleagues (Pashler, 1994a, 1999b; Pashler 6 Johnston,

1989, 1998; see also De Jong, 1993, 1995; McCann 6 Johnston, 1992; Ruthruff, Miller, 6 Lachmann, 1995; Schubert, 1999; and van Selst 6 Jolicoeur, 1994, 1997). These efforts have led to a very detailed picture of both the findings successfully explained by the model and some of its limitations. It is probably fair to say that the central bottleneck model can accommodate a wide range of findings from PRP experiments (reviewed in Pashler, 1994a, and Pashler 6 Johnston, 1998; see also Luck, 1998; Schubert, 1999; and van Selst 6 Jolicoeur, 1997) and that knowledge of its limitations (cf. De Jong, 1993, 1995; Meyer 6 Kieras, 1997; Pashler, 1994b; Pashler, Carrier, 6 Hoffman, 1993) has stimulated interesting alternative developments. 

The central bottleneck model is based on two general assumptions. First, it is assumed that each stimulus is processed in a serial sequence of (usually three) stages. During the earliest stage the stimulus is perceptually encoded and identified. Next, in the central stage the response appropriate to the previously identified stimulus is selected. Finally, during the last stage the previously selected response is activated and executed. Clearly (cf. Pashler, 1994a; Pashler 6 Johnston, 1989), each of these stages may in turn be subdivided further, using either a serial or a more complicated cognitive architecture. For the present purpose, however, all that really matters is that there is a central stage which is preceded and followed by preand post-central stages, respectively, and that these three stages are serially organized. The second principal assumption of the central bottleneck model is that the central stage can only select one response at a time, while (at least under the standard conditions of the PRP paradigm described above) the pre- and postbottleneck stages can operate in parallel to process the two stimuli. Given the instructional emphasis to rapidly respond to S1 , the model implies that sometimes the central stage of S2 processing will have to wait until the response for S1 is selected, especially for short SOAs. 

Pashler (1984; 1994a, 1994b; Pashler 6 Johnston, 1989, 1998) described important qualitative predictions derived from the central bottleneck model (influential earlier work introducing similar logic is due to Schweickert, 1980, and Schweickert 6 Townsend, 1989). Essentially, these predictions state how manipulations selectively influencing individual processing stages will interact with variations of SOA. For example, incrementing the duration of the perceptual stage of S2 processing should have smaller effects for short SOAs as compared to those for long SOAs. The reason is that for short SOAs S2 will often already be identified before the response for S1 has been selected. Thus, there will then be an idle period (slack) before the response to S2 can be selected. Therefore, prolonging the time required to encode and identify S2 will often simply reduce the duration of that idle period, but will not increase the RT to S2 . 

Clearly, it would be very informative to be able to directly relate the properties of the SOA function such as its slope to properties of the basic processing stage durations involved. Usually, predictions from the central bottleneck model are derived within a framework of deterministic stage durations (for an exception using simulations, see Ruthruff et al., 1995), which can be a useful tool for illustrating several qualitative model predictions. On the other hand, the deterministic framework is clearly not adequate to deal with genuinely stochastic aspects of the

More specifically, it would be highly informative to explicitly relate properties of the SOA function to properties of the joint distribution of the stage durations. Before we derive results from Eq. (1), we need to address a potential objection against the reasoning from which it was derived. Implicit to Eq. Thus, under this interpretation, S1 processing will always access the central stage first. A slightly different view is that Eq. (1) may be interpreted to mean that the central bottleneck is, in fact, occupied by whichever stimulus is encoded and identified first, but that under standard PRP conditions the probability for S2 to win this ``race towards the central bottleneck'' is negligible. In contrast to the first interpretation, under this view S2 processing would indeed occasionally enter the central stage first, if only the experimental conditions (short SOAs, S2 easier to identify than S1) would favor this outcome. Therefore, under the standard PRP conditions a number of precautions and diagnostics are used to ensure, and to test, that S1 processing did in fact always enter the central stage first (Pashler, 1994b). These precautions include usage of a S1 which is easier to encode and to identify than S2 , instructions stressing rapid responses to S1 , and a fixed temporal order of the stimuli. The Precue-Utilization Paradigm and the Serial Processing Model 

As described above, one of the principal assumptions of the central bottleneck model is that the organization of perceptual, central, and motor stages for each stimulus separately is serial, while across stimuli at least some of the processing stages are assumed to operate in parallel. Gottsdanker and his colleagues (1989, 1992; Gottsdanker 6 Shragg, 1985) have developed a so-called precue-utilization paradigm (see also Leonhard, 1958; Miller, 1982; Rosenbaum, 1980) which is aimed at testing some of the assumptions inherent to this serial processing architecture. More recently, essentially the same paradigm has gained considerable importance in the context of experiments in which the lateralized readiness potential is used to make inferences concerning the time course of hand-specific central response activation (Miller 6 Hackley, 1992; Osman, Bashore, Coles, Donchin, 6 Meyer, 1992). 

Specifically, Gottsdanker was interested in testing that a subject cannot, in fact, activate and execute a motor response unless and until the appropriate response has been selected during the preceding central processing stage; that is, he looked for evidence that there is no temporal overlap between the central and motor stages. Technically, he developed a go�nogo choice RT paradigm, in which a precue (Sc) indicates to the subject whether (in the case of a go trial) a left hand or a right hand response is required, while a second, go�nogo stimulus (Sg) indicates whether the current trial is a go or a nogo trial. In the standard design the probabilities of these trial events are balanced so that half of all trials are go trials, in which in turn each response is equally likely. Just as in the PRP paradigm, a major independent variable is the SOA from Sc to Sg , and the principal result consists of a plot of mean RT (as measured from go stimulus onset) in go trials as a function of SOA. Note, however, that with the precue-utilization paradigm, subjects give either just a single response (go trials) or no response at all (nogo trials) during any error�free trial, while any trial of a PRP experiment yields two response times (viz., one for each task), whose bivariate distribution is of considerable theoretical importance (Pashler, 1994b; De Jong, 1995). This implies that for a given number of trials the PRP paradigm yields four times as many RT data as the precue-utilization paradigm.

Gottsdanker (1989, 1992; Gottsdanker 6 Shragg, 1985) reasoned that increasing the SOA starting from 0 ms functionally corresponds to a transition from the choice RT involving stimulus Sc to the go�nogo RT involving stimulus Sg. For long SOAs, the subject will on almost any trial already have selected the potential response by the time the go�nogo stimulus Sg is presented. According to the serial model, in these cases the RT as measured from Sg onset reflects two additive components, the time required to encode and identify the go stimulus Sg and the time required to activate and execute the already selected response. In particular, RT should then not include any time consumed to select the response. On the other hand, for zero SOA the encoding and identification of the go stimulus should almost always have finished by the time that the response indicated by Sc has been selected. Thus, the selected response may then immediately be activated and executed, so that RT in this case should reflect the time required to encode and identify Sc , plus the time to select the appropriate response, plus the time to activate and execute this response. That is, according to this serial account, for zero SOA the RT should then just correspond to the ``standard'' choice RT involving Sc . Note that, according to this account, the go�nogo stimulus Sg just needs to be encoded and identified by the subject, while processing of the precue Sc additionally requires the central stage of response selection. Correspondingly, the durations C and M of the central and motor stages apply only for the processing of Sc , so we need not index them with respect to the eliciting stimulus; this contrasts with the central bottleneck model of dual-task performance where the individual processing of both S1 and S2 required all three stages. Note also that even within this serial conceptualization, at least some processing stages can be carried out in parallel; specifically, Sg can be encoded and identified during the perceptual and central processing of Sc .

4. DISCUSSION 

For the central bottleneck model, many of the predictions discussed above have been tested in great detail (Pashler, 1994a; Pashler 6 Johnston, 1998), especially the predictions that incrementing prebottleneck stages of S2 processing should have smaller effects for short SOAs as compared to long SOAs while effects arising at or after the bottleneck should be additive with SOA have been tested under a wide range of conditions and have been found to hold in many situations which involve stimuli in different modalities, fixed temporal stimulus order, and speeded manual choice tasks. In fact, assuming the central bottleneck model to hold, the qualitatively different predictions concerning increments of P2 vs increments of C2+M2 have been applied ``inversely,'' namely as a diagnostic tool to infer whether the locus of effects of factors varying Task 2 is before or at�after the central bottleneck (cf. Pashler, 1984; Ruthruff et al., 1995; van Selst 6 Jolicoeur, 1994). 

The formal similarity of the serial model of precue utilization to the central bottleneck model pointed out in the Introduction suggests corresponding model tests for the Eq. (3) model, too. There have, however, not been nearly as many empirical tests of these model predictions for the serial model of precue utilization. Application of the diagnostics implied by the central bottleneck model of dual-task performance might provide for stricter tests of the precue-utilization model and allow us to further develop the model. In particular, the prominent prediction of the central bottleneck model concerning the interaction of SOA with factors which increment the Task-2 prebottleneck stage duration corresponds to the analogous prediction that factors which make it harder to encode and identify the go stimulus Sg should 

476 

SCHWARZ AND ISCHEBECK 

have smaller effects for short SOAs when compared to long SOAs. As yet, this important prediction seems not to have been tested within the precue-utilization paradigm. 

In conclusion, the PRP paradigm and the precue-utilization paradigm address quite different substantive issues, but they share a similar experimental rationale, and this similarity is reflected in the common formal structure [viz., Eq. (2)] of the principal models used to analyze and interpret data from the two paradigms. In our view, the commonality of the formal model structures reflects a commonality in the theoretical claims of the two models, which is perhaps best captured by the well�known bottleneck metaphor. Essentially, all stages are assumed to proceed at normal, unaltered speed for different SOAs, but a certain stage requires the completion of two (or more) previous stages. All effects of SOA are thought to be caused by this imposed waiting time, as opposed to a slow down in any, some, or all of the stages. 

The correspondence of the two models also allows us to translate experimental techniques and important diagnostics across the two paradigms. In particular, the well-developed set of predicted factor interactions derived from the central bottleneck model (plus assumptions of selective influence) suggests new corresponding experimental tests of the serial model of precue utilization also. This is more than a purely formal, methodological parallel, as often the same stages are under examination in both paradigms. For example, varying the brightness of S2 or Sg should have similar effects in the two paradigms. 

Previous informal predictions derived from models of the two paradigms have often relied on qualitative arguments, derived within a deterministic framework. An exception to this is the deferred processing model of dual-task performance (Ollman, 1968; see also Luce, 1986, pp. 188�191). While similar in spirit to the present approach, it treats response times to S1 and S2 as holistic units, i.e., without any further breakdown into separate processing stages such as the perceptual, central, and response stages. Specifically, the deferred processing model claims that processing of S2 is started only after processing of S1 has finished completely and the SOA has elapsed, whatever comes later. Translated into the present notation, this amounts to identifying response time to Si with Ci , i=1, 2, and setting all other stage durations identically equal to zero. Inserting this special case into the present Eq. (9), we easily confirm Ollman's (1968) main result that the slope of the SOA function is equal to the negative survivor function of C1 , i.e., of the response time to S1 . 

The situation Ollman (1968) had in mind was one in which S1 required no overt response (cf. Davis, 1959; DeJong, 1993). However, even then it seems much more realistic to assume that at least part of S2 processing can already start while the perceptual and central processing of S1 is still ongoing. Using the same model assumptions, Luce (1986) also treats designs in which S1 requires a simple or even a choice response. Luce (1986, p. 188) himself spelled out in some detail the defects inherent to this formulation and realized that an adequate approach would have to start from a stage-oriented processing model for each task separately, although he did not pursue it. Given that most recent experimental research using the PRP paradigm has focused on studying the interaction pattern of experimental factors 

477 

INTERPRETATION OF RT VS SOA FUNCTIONS 

with SOA, a major disadvantage of the deferred decision model is that it is unable to predict simple or interaction effects due to factors which selectively influence stages before, at, or after the central bottleneck. Despite these severe limitations, the great importance of Ollman's and Luce's work is that they realized that analyzing the bottleneck model by treating random stage durations as if they were constants ``is incorrect and potentially misleading'' (Luce, 1986, p. 189). 

The present results explicitly take into account the generic trial-to-trial variability of stage durations and thus of RT. As most of our results are independent of restrictive distributional or independence assumptions, they are of sufficient generality to permit several relevant conclusions. First, our results confirm that qualitatively many of the ``deterministic'' predictions continue to hold if the stage durations are random variables. Second, they describe how these predictions are quantitatively modified by the trial-to-trial variability of the stage durations. Finally, they also illustrate how RT is influenced by genuinely stochastic mechanisms such as stage duration variability, or the positive and negative dependency of stage durations, which cannot be understood within a deterministic framework. Together, our results make more transparent, and more precise, the close conceptual correspondence between two important experimental paradigms; they could thus serve as a first step toward generalizing tools and insights across different experimental contexts.
