DEAR  System Development Corporation, Santa Monica, California  In a process that involves learning through the presentation of stimulus items in a sequence controlled by the experimenter or  teacher, it is of interest to determine stimulus presentation strategies that optimize the expected level of achievement of  the subjects. We consider an N-trial experiment in which, at each trial, a stimulus item is chosen for presentation from among  a given set of n items. The learning model used is the single-element model of the stimulus-sampling theory of learning.  A strategy is evaluated by an expected loss over terminal states of the subject. The single trial "locally optimal" strategy  is the one that chooses in the current trial that item which would minimize the expected loss at the end of that trial. Our  main result is that this strategy is in fact "globally optimal"; this strategy chooses in each trial that item for which the  current probability of being in the learned state is the least. In addition we show, for the case of presentations from a set  of two stimulus items, how to determine domains of common optimal strategies in the space of pairs of initial probabilities  that the two items have been learned.  INTRODUCTION  An optimization problem of considerable interest to mathematical learning theorists is the determination of an optimal  strategy for presentation of stimulus items out of a given set of such items in order to achieve certain teaching or learning  objectives. A presentation strategy is a complete specification of the choice of stimulus item for every trial in a learning  experiment, where the choice at any trial may be contingent on the presentations and responses to stimuli in all preceding  trials.  We consider learning experiments that consist of a fixed number of trials (nonsequential experiments) and a finite set of  stimulus items; for these there are only a finite number of strategies that can be realized. What we do is define for each  strategy an expected loss, or risk, over certain terminal joint states of the subject, and then take as an optimal strategy  one with minimum risk. An obvious, but typically computationally infeasible, method of selecting an optimal strategy would  consist of determining the risk for each member of the finite set of strategies and then selecting a strategy with minimum  risk. The main result of this paper is the demonstration that a certain simple rule is sufficient to generate an optimal  stimulus presentation strategy for the  19  ZO KARUSH AND DEAR  model of learning considered here (see Theorem I). This rule is to present in each trial that item for which the current  probability of being in the learned state is the least; this rule corresponds to a policy of local optimality in which we  choose at each trial the item so as to minimize the risk which would occur if the experiment were to end with that trial. In  addition we show, for the case of presentations from a set of two stimulus items, how to determine domains of common optimal  strategies in the space of pairs of a priori probabilities that the two items have been learned.  Of especial interest is the situation where the a priori probability of being in a learned state at the outset of the  experiment is zero for each of the several given items. In this case we show that the rule for generating an optimal strategy  reduces to comparisons of simple counts of correct and incorrect responses to the various items. We also show that in this  case the optimal strategy is independent of the remaining parameters of the model, parameters that might vary with the  individual subject; these parameters are the probability of transition from the unlearned to the learned state in a single  trial and the probability of guessing the correct response when in the unlearned state.  We use the one-element model of the stimulus sampling theory of learning to characterize the underlying learning process. In  this model, the learning process is viewed roughly as the formation within a subject of an association or connection between a  stimulus item and the response that the experimenter has designated as correct. When the connection between the stimulus item  and the response has been formed, the subject is said to be conditioned to respond correctly to that item or to be in the  conditioned state with respect to that item. The subject is either conditioned to an item or not conditioned to it; no other  state is allowed. Also upon presentation of a stimulus item, the subject responds in one of two possible ways, either  correctly or incorrectly.  In a stimulus sampling model it is assumed that the transition from the unconditioned to the conditioned state occurs during  the course of a particular trial. Thus, stimulus sampling models are often referred to as "all-or-none" models of learning. In  contrast, a number of other mathematical theories of learning assert an incremental change in probabilities of correct  response. [Prominent among the incremental models of learning are the so-called linear models of Bush and Mosteller (1955).]  An important advantage of the stimulus sampling theory is that it allows characterization of the learning process in terms of  small, finite sets of states of conditioning; however, in practically all models of the theory these states are unobservable.  Thus, in solving for an optimal stimulus presentation strategy one must base one's selection of items for presentation on the  partial information that is available from trial to trial in the history of an observable sequence of responses to a sequence  of item presentations. In such circumstances, it is quite natural to seek presentation strategies that are optimal according  to the Bayes principle, and consequently to summarize the  OPTIMAL STIMULUS PRESENTATION STRATEGY 21  partial information available in a sequence of observables as the a posteriori probability of a subject being in the various  states of conditioning.  Some key general references concerning stimulus sampling theory are Estes and Suppes (1959), Estes (1959), and Atkinson* and  Estes (1963). The special model that we examine here is issentially the same as the single-element model of paired-associate  learning that Bower (1961) has developed with the important difference that we incorporate the notion of stimulus presentation  strategies in our model.  Matheson (1964) has also considered optimum teaching procedures for experiments based on the one-element stimulus sampling  model. He uses a trial-by-trial reward structure, and for the special case in which the parameter that we call the guessing  probability y is zero, he presents an argument for a result analogous to our Theorem I. An N-trial experiment is, thereby, represented by a tree structure which begins with a single node (the initial  trial) and ends in 2' terminal nodes. Altogether it contains 2N - I(= 1 + 2 + --- + 2") nonterminal nodes, and at each of  these one of the n stimulus items A1 , AZ , --- , A,, must be specified. A stimulus presentation strategy is represented  graphically as a tree for which the choice of item has been made at each of these nodes; thus, there are in all, n2N-1  strategies to choose among in an N-trial experiment.  The optimality of a strategy will be determined with respect to a numerical criterion expressed as an expected loss or risk  involving the terminal states of the subject with respect to the n stimulus items. Given the learning model parameters 01  A10, ..., An y, 9) and the specification of a presentation strategy, we have sufficient information to determine the  probability of a subject arriving at any particular terminal node; i.e., following a given one of the 2' possible paths of  responses from the initial node to a terminal node of the strategy tree. Using a specified loss function defined on the joint  states of conditioning of a subject, we associate a risk with each terminal node. The risk of the whole strategy is then given  by the expectation of this risk over all terminal nodes of the strategy tree. PROOF. By Lemma 7, an optimal policy is initiated by presenting any one of the r items in question, say A; , at the first  trial. It should be noted that the proof just given subsumes the special case r = 0 in which all the initial probabilities are 0 or  larger. In this case, the optimal strategy is generated from the beginning by the rule of least current probability of  conditioning, and Theorem II asserts that all optimal strategies are generated by this rule for such a vector of initial  probabilities. But when r > 0, Theorem II does not characterize all optimal strategies, for it does not assert that an optimal  strategy must present the r items in question in the first r trials. As a matter of fact, an optimal strategy need not be so  restricted; e.g., the item selected at trial k, with k < r, can be made dependent upon the past history of responses up to  trial k. From the point of view of application, however, it does not seem worthwhile to pursue the characterization of optimal  strategies beyond the detail of Theorem II. DOMAINS WITH COMMON OPTIMAL STRATEGIES  The space of initial probabilities Ais the hyper-cube 0 < ai< 1, i = 1, 2, ..., n, in n-space. For given N, the number of  possible strategies is finite-hence, the hypercube can be partitioned into a finite number of domains such that all initial  points i]in the same domain have a common optimal strategy. It is a problem of general interest in multi-stage  decision-making to determine domains of common optimal strategy. Among other things, it provides an insight into the  sensitivity of an optimal strategy to exact values of initial probabilities. Also, the number of optimal strategies as a varies is typically orders-of-magnitude less than all possible strategies. Subdivisions of the space of a priori probabilities  are well-known modes of representing Bayes' strategies in statistical problems, such as sequential analysis problems (e.g.,  see Blackwell and Girshick, 1954, Chapters 9 and 10).  In this section of the paper we consider this problem for two items. We describe a method of determining the subdivision for  experiments terminating after an arbitrary number of trials N; the sequence of subdivisions is a nested sequence as N  increases. For experiments involving two stimulus items, graphic representation of the domains of common optimal strategies is  simplified by the fact that the space of a priori probabilities is the unit square. Illustrations of these subdivisions will  be presented here.
