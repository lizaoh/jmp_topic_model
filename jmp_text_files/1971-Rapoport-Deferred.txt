BURKHEIMER  Psychometric Laboratory, University of North Carolina, Chapel Hill, North Carolina 27514  Deferred decision theory assumes two simple hypotheses, observations that are purchased sequentially, losses for incorrect  terminal decisions, and a constant loss per observation which may depend on the true hypothesis. After every observation, the  decision maker may either make a terminal decision and incur a terminal loss if he is incorrect or purchase another  observation; his objective is to minimize subjectively expected loss.  The optimal decision policy is presented and numerical results are given for Gaussian and exponential stimulus models. The  theory is extended to the case where loss per observation varies from one trial to another. Predictions regarding statistics  obtained from data are derived, effects of discrepancies from the optimal decision policy are examined, and descriptive models  are proposed and compared to the normative model.  The elements of a static decision problem under uncertainty are a set of outcomes (states of nature), a probability  distribution on the set of outcomes, a set of decisions, and a set of rewards. Static decision theory considers a decision  maker (DM) confronted by a set of outcomes. Without knowing the true outcome, DM must make a decision the consequences of  which will depend on the outcome which actually obtains. Associated with each decision-outcome combination there is a  numerical value for DM called the reward. We shall assume that there exists a utility function, unique up to a linear  transformation, on the set of rewards, specify for each reward the negative of its utility, rather than its utility, and call  this number the loss. The DM's objective is to choose a decision, called an optimal decision, which minimizes the expected  loss.  Consider now a decision problem in which costly observations can be taken in sequence. A decision procedure which prescribes  taking a specified number of observations may not be optimal. To minimize expected loss, DM should exploit the structure of  the sequential decision process, deciding at each state, on the basis of the information thus far collected, whether to  purchase at least one more observation or to stop the process, make a terminal decision, and incur a certain loss if his  decision is incorrect. A Bayesian DM (Edwards, Lindman, and Savage, 1963), who wishes to minimize the subjectively expected  loss of the sequential decision process, will base his decision at each stage of the process on the losses associated with  terminal decisions, on  508  MODELS FOR DEFERRED DECISION MAKING 509  subjectively expected losses for future observations, and on information acquired thus far. He will attempt to balance the  loss for purchasing more observations against the expected gain in information from these observations.  Assuming that observations are discrete and are generated by a well-defined data generating process that is known to be in  either one or the other of two states, So and Sl, several kinds of sequential observation decision procedures may be  distinguished, depending upon (1) the approach taken in formulating the problem and determining the decision criteria  (classical vs. Bayesian), (2) the number of observations that can be purchased (unlimited vs. fixed in advance and known), (3)  the type of hypotheses that can be tested (simple vs. compound), and (4) the loss per observation (which may or may not depend  on S; , j = 0, 1).  In Wald's (1947) theory of sequential analysis the criteria employed by the DM to decide whether to make a terminal decision  or to continue observing are derived from the assumption that the classical type I and type 11 error probabilities are given.  The decision criteria are obtained regardless of the losses associated with the terminal decisions, the observation loss, and  the prior probability for hypothesis H; , which asserts that the data generating process is in state S; . Terminal losses,  observation losses, and subjective probabilities are explicitly considered by the deferred decision theory (Birdsall and  Roberts, 1965; Goode, 1962; Pollock, 1964), which, as we show below, extensively generalizes Wald's sequential analysis theory.  In Wald's theory the number of observations that can be purchased in a given sequence is unlimited. For many practical  situations this is a restrictive assumption because resources that can be invested in information purchasing are limited or  because of the existence of external conditions that cause a truncation of the sequential decision process. In deferred  decision theory (DDT), which is better suited for practical applications, the number of observations that can be purchased in  a given sequence is fixed in advance and known to DM. Another, more realistic, assumption is possible, namely, that the number  of observations that can be taken is fixed in advance but is unknown to the DM, who may only possess a probability  distribution over this number.  Simple and compound hypotheses have been studied by Wald's sequential analysis theory and by DDT. Simple DDT (Birdsall and  Roberts, 1965; Goode, 1962; Pollock, 1964) has developed optimal decision criteria, assuming that both hypotheses are simple,  i.e., that each of them determines uniquely the values of all the parameters of the data generating process. Composite DDT  (Roberts, 1967) has extended previous work to sequential problems in which at least one of the hypotheses is composite.  With only one important exception (Pollock, 1964), theories of sequential decision making have assumed a fixed cost (or loss)  per observation, which is independent of the state of nature. Sequential decision problems may be considered, where loss per  observation depends on S; . In this case, DM must consider the expected observation loss, since the actual loss per  observation can be determined only after a terminal decision has been made. For this reason, DM should be charged for all the  observations  510 RAPOPORT AND BURKHEIMER  that he has taken only after a terminal decision, whether correct or incorrect, is made. Otherwise, if he has to pay for each  observation as he takes it, he would only purchase the first observation to find out with certainty the true state of nature.  PROBLEM FORMULATION  We adopt the Bayesian view that probabilities are orderly opinions about uncertain events and are constantly revised by DM as  relevant information is gathered. From this standpoint a sequential decision problem is considered in which the parameter  space under either Ho or Hl includes one element only, the maximum number of observations is fixed in advance and known to the  DM, and loss per observation may depend on the true state of nature. To fix ideas and suggest applications to sequential  detection of signals in noise, the terminology of signal detectability theory (SDT) is used frequently. Following Pollock's  dissertation (1964), with only minor notational changes, the sequential decision problem is stated formally as fellows:  Stimulus Assumptions  1. A well-defined data generating process is known to be in either state S1 or So with prior (subjective) probabilities P and  1 - P, respectively.  2. An observation is made, the result of which is a random variable x, which has a known probability density functionf;(x) if  S; is the true state of nature. Observations are independent; they take place at unit time intervals (trials) and are recorded  by DM without error.  3. The likelihood ratio, L(x) =fi(x)/fu(x), is a monotone nondecreasing function of x in the set of points for which at least  one of fo(x) and fi(x) is positive (if fa(x) = 0 and fi(x) > 0, then L(x) is defined as +oo). (The latter assumption  considerably simplifies the presentation and is less restrictive than it seeems to be; the binomial, Poisson, exponential,  uniform, hypergeometric, and equal variance Gaussian distributions have monotone likelihood ratios.)  Response Assumptions  4. After every observation (including the zeroth) DM makes one of three decisions: Di : Si is the true state of nature, i = 0,  1, or W: Purchase another observation.  5. Decision Di is terminal. The losses to DM for making a terminal decision Dz , given that S; is the true state of nature,  are denoted by Ci; (i, j = 0, 1). Clo and Col are the losses associated with "false alarm" and "miss," respectively. Without  loss of generality (see Goode, 1962) assume that Coo = C, = 0 and Ci; _>_ 0.  6. The decision W continues the sequential process for at least one more trial, provided there are more observations to take.  The loss per observation may depend  MODELS FOR DEFERRED DECISION MAKING 511  on the state of nature; it is W; if S; is the true state of nature (Ci; and W; are measured in the same units).  7. The total number of observations that can be taken, denoted by N, is fixed in advance and known to DM.  The objective of DM is to minimize the subjectively expected loss of the process. The decision policy (i.e., the rule for  making decisions given a sequence of observations and knowledge of the parameters P, Ci; , W; , f;(x) and N) that achieves  this minimum is called the optimal policy.  Our first purpose in this paper is to present the optimal policy for the deferred decision problem. Although various  derivations of the optimal policy have been knwon to statisticians and electrical engineers, they have been fairly  inaccessible to psychologists because of their incompleteness (Good, 1962), compactness (Birdsall and Roberts, 1965), or lack  of circulation (Pollock, 1964). (But see, however, an excellent presentation by DeGroot, (1970).) We suspect that progress of  experimental research in sequential decision making has been hindered because psychologists have not familiarized themselves  with the DDT and the optimal policy. A recent attempt by Edwards (1965) to present normative models for sequential decision  problems, better known to psychologists as optional stopping problems, discusses only a few special cases, assuming N = oc in  each case. A cursory inspection of recently published experimental studies of sequential decision making (Becker, 1958;  Edwards and Slovic, 1965; Fried and Peterson, 1969; Pitz, 1968; Pitz, Reinhold, and Geller, 1969; Pruitt, 1961; Sanders and  Ter Linden, 1967; Wallsten, 1968; and other studies by Edwards' group) shows that, with few exceptions, these studies have  treated only the special case of two binomial distributions with an unlimited number of costly observations. Three studies  that limited the number of observations that could be purchased (Fried and Peterson, 1969; Pitz, 1968; Sanders and Ter Linden,  1967) employed approximations rather than the optimal policy to analyze their data.  The second purpose is to extend DDT to the case where observation losses are not fixed but may vary arbitrarily from one trial  to another. This extension is mathematically trivial despite Edwards' assertion that "nonlinear costs of observing complicate  things, usually intolerably (Edwards, 1965, p. 317)." Descriptive as well as normative reasons have motivated this extension.  It is known that when cost of observation is constant the optimal choice of decision criteria at a given trial is independent  of the cost of observations already paid. "Money spent observing is down the drain except insofar as the observations  purchased have moved the odds; you always consider your immediate situation as though you had not collected any information  previously (Edwards, 1965, p. 315)." As this point may be counterintuitive to many DMs, experienced and wealthy poker players  excluded, descriptive models of sequential decision behavior postulating the minimization of expected loss should allow for  nonconstant loss per observation. Indeed, Pitz (1968), Pitz et al (1969), and  512 RAPOPORT AND 13URKHEIMER  Wallsten (1968), noting that the decision criteria adopted by their subjects changed as the number of observations taken  increased, suggested that a possible explanation "may be found in a nonconstant utility for the cost of information (Pitz,  1968, p. 34)."  A third and equally important purpose of this paper is to suggest an approach to data analysis. Recursive equations for error  probability and expected number of observations are solved iteratively. These solutions are then used to study the sensitivity  of the optimal model and can be used to test certain model assumptions with empirical data. Normatively, a quantitative  analysis of the effects of discrepancies from the optimal model on the expected loss of the process has obvious merits,  especially if the costs of searching for and executing the optimal policy are high. Descriptively, sensitivity analysis is no  less important, since without it statistical comparisons of optimal policies with observed decisions typically yield limited,  uninteresting, or superficial conclusions.  Finally, employing the same recursive technique to compute the expected loss of the process, we shall propose two  "descriptive" models, examining the effect of discretizing the stimulus continuum or assuming a probabilistic response  mechanism on the optimal policy. DESCRIPTIVE MODELS  Theories of psychophysics as well as theories of preference incorporate both stimulus and response assumptions. SDT and DDT  assume a continuum of sensory states and a deterministic decision policy. In threshold theories (Krantz, 1969; Luce, 1963;  Norman, 1964), on the other hand, the sensory model involves two or more states, and the decision policy is probabilistic.  Combinations of continuous sensory model with probabilistic decision policy have also been explored (Lee, 1963, 1968). It is  528 RAPOPORT AND BURKHEINIER  worth emphasizing that sensory and response assumptions are independent, that "the choice of a continuous or discrete sensory  model and a deterministic or probabilistic response model are logically separate (Krantz, 1969, p. 311)."  In the next two sections the distinction between stimulus and response assumptions will be maintained in proposing alternative  descriptive theories to DDT. Our main purpose is to compare these theories to DDT in terms of the quantitative measure  introduced in the preceding section, namely, the proportional increase in expected loss. In the immediately following section  we shall assume a deterministic decision policy and an optimal choice of decision boundaries, as in DDT, and then investigate  how changing the stimulus assumptions by discretizing the stimulus continuum increases the expected loss. An opposite tack  will be taken in the second section; assuming a continuum of sensory states, as in DDT, we shall examine the increase in  expected loss resulting from the assumption of a probabilistic rather than a deterministic decision policy.  Discrete Stimulus Models  There have been several attempts to construct theories of psychophysics which propose quantization of the sensory continuum,  i.e., which assume an M-state sensory model, M > 2, rejecting the controversial assumption of SDT that the sensory information  used by DM is strictly continuous. The two-state sensory model proposed by Luce (1963) has been mentioned above. For a review  of other two-state sensory models (low-threshold or high-threshold) see Green and Swets (1966). The case of M = 3 has also  been suggested by Green (see Swets (1961)) and Krantz (1969). The neural-quantum theory (Green and Swets, 1966) also  incorporates an 117-state sensory model; it proposes that sensory representation of stimulus magnitude is discrete throughout  its range, and that many thresholds exist.  There are two additional reasons for assuming that x is a discrete variable. First, when x is a sensory signal, there may be  physical detection systems which are limited in their capability to measure x as a continuous variable, or which are incapable  of calculating and storing continuous likelihood functions (Pollock, 1964). Second, when sequential decision experiments, such  as the "book-bag and poker chip" experiments (Edwards, 1965), are conducted, discrimination on the part of DM can be safely  assumed to be perfect, and hence x can be assumed to be discrete.  As indicated by Green and Swets (1966) and exemplified by several psychophysical studies employing nonsequential decision  procedures, it may be difficult to distinguish between M-state sensory models and the continuous sensory model of SDT, even  when M is very small. Comparisons between these two types of models have been typically made qualitatively by comparing graphs  on ROC figures. The question arises if and to what extent the similarity between the two types of sensory models holds when  observations are purchased sequentially. TESTS OF RESPONSE MODELS  The results reported in the last two sections show how various stimulus and response models can be formulated and then optimal  decision boundaries and minimum expected losses computed for the deferred decision process. They also show how to compute the  efficiency of such models and how to compare them in terms of the resulting proportional increase in expected loss. The  specific models presented have not been tested with data and are not advanced as descriptive models to be seriously  considered. Rather, they only represent classes of models which may be considered to describe empirical data. While we have  indicated general techniques for constructing alternative models, no explicit scheme has been proposed for fitting the models  to data. Guidelines for testing deterministic response models are sketched below.  Since most psychological studies are concerned with testing response models, it is important that the stimulus assumptions of  DDT be realized. Some of the controversial assumptions of SDT concerning the sensory model can be successfully overcome in DDT  experiments by employing externally distributed stimuli (Kuboby, Rapoport, and Tversky, in press; Lee, 1963; Pitz et al,  1969). If this approach is taken and, in addition, learning effects are controlled by providing subjects with extensive  practice  536 RAPOPORT AND BURKHEIMER  with the stimulus distributions, the major remaining stimulus assumption is that of continuity. As we have shown above,  however, rather severe violations of this assumption lead to only relatively small departures from the optimal policy. Hence,  with externally distributed stimuli which are well known by the subjects, experimental results may provide a direct test of  response models.  The most basic issue in testing response models is to determine whether the response mechanism can be conveniently and  parsimoniously described as deterministic or whether probabilistic assumptions have to be invoked. When individual data from  deferred decision making experiments are collected we recommend first attempting to fit them with a deterministic response  model. The three most popular deterministic models are the fixed cut-off point model (DM makes a terminal decision if either  P, <_ y or Pn ~> 8), the fixed sample size model (DM takes a prespecified number of observations and makes a terminal decision  on the basis of all observations taken), and the "World Series" model (basically a combination of the two previous models,  see, e.g., Pitz et al (1969)). Of these models, only the first is consistent with the Bayesian orientation of this paper; the  latter two will not be considered here.
