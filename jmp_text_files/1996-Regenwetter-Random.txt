Random Utility Representations of Finite m-ary Relations 

Michel Regenwetter

Block and Marschak (1960, in Olkin et al. (Eds.), Contributions to probability and statistics (pp. 97�132). Stanford, CA: Stanford Univ. Press) discussed the relationship between a probability distribution over the strict linear rankings on a finite set C and a family of jointly distributed random variables indexed by C. The present paper generalizes the concept of random variable (random utility) representations to m-ary relations. It specifies conditions on a finite family of random variables that are sufficient to construct a probability distribution on a given collection of m-ary relations over the family's index set. Conversely, conditions are presented for a probability distribution on a collection of m-ary relations over a finite set C to induce (on a given sample space) a family of jointly distributed random variables indexed by C. Four random variable representations are discussed as illustrations of the general method. These are a semiorder model of approval voting, a probabilistic model for betweenness in magnitude judgments, a probabilistic model for political ranking data, and a probabilistic concatenation describing certainty equivalents for the joint receipt of gambles. The main theorems are compared to related results of Heyer and Niedere�e (1989, in E. E. Roskam (Ed.), Mathematical psychology in progress (pp. 99�112). 1. INTRODUCTION 

A frequent starting assumption in models of individual judgment and decision making is that each choice alternative may be represented by a strength random variable, often called a utility random variable. The choice or preference behavior of a sample of persons (or a sample of choices made by the same person) is then modeled as a function of the sampled joint outcomes of a family of jointly distributed random variables (on some specified or unspecified sample space). Such random utility choice models assume for instance that a voter or decision maker chooses the alternative that achieves the highest value in a realization of the utility vector. 

An alternative starting assumption states that each person's choice or judgment behavior is induced by an underlying preference relation of some (specified or unspecified) sort. In other words, a sample of decision makers is observed and

The standard empirical paradigms include binary choice (e.g., Fishburn, 1992; Koppen, 1995; Marley, 1990; Suck, 1992) and choosing one among several alternatives (Barbera� 6 Pattanaik, 1986; Falmagne, 1978). For a given empirical choice paradigmsuch as binary choice, thesetwoassumptions together give rise to three problems. One concerns the relationship between the two assumptions. This random variable representation problem (extended to m-ary relations) studies conditions that relate a family of jointly distributed random variables indexed by a set C of choice alternatives and a probability distribution over a class of m-ary relations on C. The second and third problems are typically referred to as characterization problems (Marley, 1990). They study conditions for a collection of choice probabilities of a given format to be induced either by a family of jointly distributed random variables or by a probability distribution on a class of relations, respectively. 

Solving the representation problem may reduce the two characterization problems to one. The representation problem is the main concern of the present paper and is studied here independently of the particular empirical paradigm that the representations are used for. The following two conventions are important in order to avoid possible confusions. (1) For any family of random variables mentioned here, I will assume that the sample space is known. (2) Similarly, when talking about a collection of relations I assume that it is known what that collection is.[1] The implications that the results have for cases where the collection of preference relations is a priori unknown, or where the random utility sample space is a priori unknown, will be discussed briefly. 

Probabilistic choice models typically involve all three problems introduced above and are routinely referred to as random utility models. The usual random utility models in the literature involve very difficult characterization problems. The first example is a semiorder model of approval voting. The second is a probabilistic model for betweenness (e.g., Suppes, Krantz, Luce, 6 Tversky, 1989) which arises when subjects are confronted with triples of magnitudes and are asked to tell the experimenter which element of each triple is between the remaining two. The third example is a probabilistic model for ranking data of the sort arising from the so-called ``feeling thermometer'' typically used in political surveys[2] (e.g., Miller, Kinder, Rosenstone, 6 NES, 1993), in which the respondents are asked to rate a number of political candidates or parties on some numerical scale. The joint receipt of gambles plays an important role in recent axiomatic theories of utility (Cho, Luce, 6 von Winterfeldt, 1994; Fishburn 6 Luce, 1995; Luce, 1991, 1995; Luce 6 Fishburn, 1991, 1995). 

These four examples give a glimpse of a network of unexplored empirical settings for random utility models on the one hand and of a spectrum of combinatoric interpretations for random variable representations on the other hand. The suggested approach to approval voting views the voters' decision process as governed by a probability distribution on the collection of semiorders over the candidates. Betweenness ratings are regarded as arising from a probability distribution on betweenness relations. Feeling thermometer data are interpreted as a probability distribution on the strict weak orders over a set of alternatives. Certainty equivalents for the joint receipt of gambles are modeled as a probability distribution over certain concatenations. 

All four paradigms are alternatively modeled in terms of the joint distribution of a family of random variables on some given sample space, which may or may not be identical for all four cases. The mathematical machinery presented below shows thereby how, in the case of one fixed family of random variables (on one fixed sample space), various choice and rating paradigms are interrelated through their random utility representations. In other words, one and the same family of jointly distributed random variables induces probability distributions on a palette of classes of preference relations, and may therefore provide a unifying framework for a variety of choice and scaling paradigms.

Strict Weak Orders and Feeling Thermometer Ratings 

A common tool in political opinion research is the feeling thermometer. The participants are asked questions such as: ``How do you feel towards presidential candidate X? If you feel indifferent, please mark a value of 50. If you strongly support X mark a value of 100, if you strongly oppose X mark a value of 0. You can use any value from 0 to 100'' (cf. Miller et al., 1993).

8. DISCUSSION AND SUMMARY 

Random utility models typically involve three kinds of problems, two characterization problems and a randomutility representation problem. One characterization problem concerns the relationship between choice or judgment probabilities of a given type and a probability distribution on relations of a certain kind over the choice alternatives. The other characterization problem concerns the relationship between choice or judgment probabilities and a family of utility random variables indexed by the choice alternatives. The representation problem concerns the relationship between the two characterization problems. More generally, though, regardless of the empirical choice paradigms under study, it concerns the relationship between a probability distribution on a class of relations over a set and a family of jointly distributed random variables indexed by the same set. 

Starting from the linear order case studied by Block and Marschak (1960), the present paper generalizes real random variable representations to finite Borel-realizable m-ary relations.It also gives a glimpse at the vast varietyof empirical paradigms where random utility models could naturally be studied. The examples demonstrate that the traditional random utility paradigms only touched the tip of the iceberg, and furthermore that there exist empirical paradigms with natural and quite simple random utility characterizations. 

The main theoretical results are related to results by Heyer and Niedere�e (1989, 1992). In the framework of their concept of a probabilistic relation, they give conditions that allow the translation of one characterization problem into the other, or vice versa. There are four main aspects with respect to which the present paper differs from theirs: the role of (1) noncoincidence and isomorphic embeddability, (2) probabilistic relations, (3) the underlying sample spaces, and (4) the real numbers. 

I will first discuss these four aspects and then summarize my findings. 

The Role of Noncoincidence and Isomorphic Embeddability 

Heyer and Niedere�e (1989, 1992) and also Niedere�e and Heyer (in press) show how noncoincidence goes hand in hand with isomorphic embeddability. As they study isomorphically embeddable cases in more detail, they emphasize noncoincidence somewhat more. 

From the probabilistic perspective of the present paper, noncoincidence is only one among many possible so-called 

generalized noncoincidence conditions which guarantee the probability mass over all relations to add up to one. As we saw, there are some interesting cases such as strict weak orders, semiorders, and betweenness relations, where generalized noncoincidence becomes a vacuous condition. In those cases, we may in fact like to allow for ties and explicitly violate noncoincidence. 

Onereason why theserelationshavereceivedlittleattention may be that reflexive and nonasymmetric relations, such as weak orders, create additional difficulties in the characterization problems of binary choice probabilities. Indeed, it seems that a random utility representation with a reflexive relation such as �on the reals causes paradoxical results: Given that two identical stimuli are presented as choice alternatives in a binary choice paradigm, the random utility representation (regardless of whether coincident or noncoincident) seems to suggest that each of the two would be chosen with probability one (or zero using formula (2)), rather than one half. This problem rests, however, not in the random utility representation, but rather in a suitable formulation of the characterization problems. If two ``equivalent'' stimuli are presented and are not perceived as equivalent with probability one, then the theory has to account for that and treat them as two different stimuli. Their physical equivalence may be accounted for by assuming that either is preferred to the other with probability one half, or that their utility random variables are independent and identically distributed. 

In the case of the joint receipt of gambles, generalized noncoincidence is strictly more than noncoincidence whenever we do not allow for the empty relation. Generalized noncoincidence also extends the representation to classes of structures that are not closed downward. 

The Role of Probabilistic Relations 

Heyer and Niedere�e formulate their characterization and random representation problems exclusively in terms of probabilistic relations. In contrast the present paper separates the characterization and the random representation problems. In fact, once we understand the relationship between a probability distribution on a class of relations and a family of random variables, we can derive from it the relationship between marginal probabilities of subsets of relations (i.e. the probabilistic relation derived from the probability distribution on the relations) and the family of random variables. Thus, for instance, there is only one random utility representation problem for linear orders on a given set, independent of the possible choice paradigms we try to explain using linearly ordered preferences. 

Also, it may be too restrictive to state characterization problems of choice or judgment probabilities only in terms of probabilistic relations of the same type and on the same set as the mixture of deterministic relations. For instance, in

The Role of the Underlying Sample Spaces 

Heyer and Niedere�e tend to leave the sample spaces implicit in some of their theorem statements. The sample spaces constructed in their proofs clearly suggest that the samplespace of therandom variablesand the indexing sample 

space of the relational structures are essentially identical, while the definition of a probabilistic relation completely omits a sample space and probability measures. A possible motivation for a single underlying abstract sample space would be that, in an experiment, subjects and their responses are sampled from some unspecified urn. This urn would then simultaneously serve as sample space for the utility random variables and�or the mixture of relational structures. However, if that is the intended meaning of the common sample space, it is not clear why the definition of a probabilistic relation does not explicitly incorporate this sample space as well. 

The approach of the present paper is to typically assume three sample spaces for a given random utility model. In fact, there exist three particularly natural choices: (1) The experiment generates a canonical sample space consisting of all possible choices and of their choice probabilities. (2) If we attempt to explain the choice probabilitiesby a probability distribution Pr on a finite collection R of m-ary relations, this introduces another canonical sample space, namely ( R, 2[R] , Pr). (3) Furthermore, when we attempt to explain the choice probabilities by a family of, say, N real random variables, this family has a canonical sample space, namely ( R[N] , B(R[N] ), P), where P is a probability measure. 

If, starting from the random utilities, the corresponding class of relations (on which we would like to derive a probability distribution) is a priori unspecified, we can take any family which is Borel-realizable w.r.t. some relation S on the reals. Conversely, starting from a probability distribution on a class of Borel-realizable relations, if the sample space on which we would like to construct the random utility representation is a priori unspecified, we can simply work with the default Borel-space (or take the class of relations as sample space, in analogy to Heyer and Niedere�e). 

As the present paper emphasizes, random utility representations and characterization problems can be studied and solved quite well even when the three sample spaces differ drastically. An important implication of this approach is that a given family of random variables on a given sample space induces probability distributions on a panoply of families of relations and can account for a panoply of choice and�or judgment probabilities of various formats. This provides a unifying framework for various combinatoric perspectives and various empirical paradigms (each with their own sample spaces) under the roof of a given family of random utilities. 

The Role of the Real Numbers 

Heyer and Niedere�e chose a higher level of generality and abstraction by representing their relational structures in some abstract set X and by studying X-valued random representations. In contrast, the present paper focuses its attention on the case of real representations (as does the 

232

standard representational measurement theory) and real valued random variables. 

The advantage of the more specific real valued case is that one can formulate very clearly the condition that guarantees all probabilities to be well defined: The (sufficient) condition is that the representing relation S on R be a Borel-set. Indeed, all standard random utility models in the literature have in common that the random variables are real valued and that the underlying relations are Borel-realizable. 

Summary 

Overall, starting from the traditional binary choice paradigm and its characterization problem in terms of linear orders, I have laid out the crucial mechanisms underlying Block and Marschak's (1960) random utility representation for linear orders. These mechanisms involve the fact that a given class of relations is Borel-realizable, i.e., can be homomorphically mapped into the real numbers endowed with a relation which is a Borel set. This allowed me in particular to formulate random utility representations of semiorders, betweenness relations, strict weak orders, certain concatenations, and in general Borel-realizable finite m-ary relations. Furthermore, not only can one and the same family of jointly distributed random variables be interpreted in a rich variety of combinatoric frameworks, these representations can also be used to simultaneously model a vast spectrum of empirical paradigms. I illustrated this with four examples: (1) a probabilistic model of approval voting based on semiordered preferences and a random utility counterpart of the model; (2) probabilistic betweenness and a random utility representation of betweenness; (3) a probabilistic strict weak order model of ranking data and its random utility counterpart; and (4) a probabilistic concatenation and its random variable representation. 

The bottom line is that essentially the same machinery as that proposed by Block and Marschak for linear orders and binary choices, which was extended by Heyer and Niedere�e to m-ary relations in the framework of probabilistic relational structures of type (k1 , ..., km), can easily be adapted and generalized to a vastly unexplored network of empirical paradigms, combinatoric structures, and random variable representations. 

The main challenge for most applications remains the solution of the corresponding characterization problems. However, these problems may also be overcome with empirical paradigms that provide sufficiently rich data to provide simpler characterizations. That there exist important paradigms with that feature was illustrated with a semiorder model for approval voting and a strict weak order model for feeling thermometer data. In these extreme cases the characterizations are in fact trivial (and the models vacuous without additional assumptions). 

Several questions about Heyer and Niedere�e (1989, 1992) were raised, some of which are addressed by Niedere�e and Heyer (in press).
