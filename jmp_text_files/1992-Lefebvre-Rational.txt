LEFEBVRE  School of Social Sciences, University of California  A hypothetical law governing the generation of aesthetically attractive proportions in music and geometry is introduced. This  law is formulated as follows: an attractive proportion x is a positive root of the equation  xl+'+2rx-x'-r=0,  where r is either k or 1/k, k is a natural number, and a is zero or one. If a=0, a root x corresponds to an attractive musical  interval; if a = 1, then a positive root x corresponds to an attractive geometrical ratio of line lengths. From this law we  formally deduced the Just Intonation set in music and the golden section and some other geometrical ratios which might also be  attractive. We then deduce this hypothetical law from a formal model of a subject with many-tiered retlexion. C0 1992 Academic  Press, Inc.  INTRODUCTION  The two topics addressed in the present paper have been of interest since ancient times. The first one concerns the nature of  musical intervals; the second the nature of the golden section value (g = (,/5 - 1)/2 z 0.618) which for centuries has been  considered a symbol of beauty and perfection.  0.1. Musical Intervals  According to legend, the relation between musical intervals (a pair of synchronous or consecutive sounds) and numbers was  established by Pythagoras in 6 B.C. He found that two identical strings (of a musical instrument) would sound harmonious if  their lengths were in the ratios of 1/2, 2/3, 3/4 (see, e.g., McClain, 1976). Let us note that the words "musical interval"  today denote the ratio of two sound frequencies that is the inverse ratio of the strings' length. The set of musical  intervals-known as Pythagorean Tuning-was built around this discovery and within the next millennium was believed to represent  the ultimate in euphony (see Table 0.1). However, musical systems based on integer ratios had a serious defect. They did not allow transposing a melody without  distortion: if the first sound was shifted, the entire system of musical relations was distorted. The problem of transposition  was solved in the 17th century with the introduction of an equally tempered scale. Because the intervals as ratios of integers  could not be preserved, they were reduced to approximations (Table 0.1). The new scale was constructed with 12 equidistant  logarithmic intervals. The intervals 4/7 and 7/8 could not be included in the new scale because there were no adequate  approximations for them (McClain, 1987).  From the Renaissance until the middle of the twentieth century it was held that only those musical intervals given by a ratio  of small natural numbers would be pleasing to hear (see Cohen, 1984; Meyer, 1962a). This conviction was supported by a  psycho-acoustical theory (Helmholtz, 1954) based on the physical notions of resonance and interaction of oscillations.  Nevertheless, a hypothesis of simple intervals was doubted. The roots of this doubt were not in the area of experimental  psychology, but rather in the limitations of the psycho-acoustical approach: "Now a certain degree of preference for simple  ratios is understandable when one is dealing with simultaneous tones that are intense enough to produce beats. However, when  applied to tones heard alternately the argument that simple ratios are preferred is simply numerological nonsense" (Ward,  1962, p. 679). Shepard (1982b) expressed a similar point of view. Such considerations motivated the experimental verification  of the hypothesis of simple ratios. The works by Ward (1954, 1962), Ward and Martin (1961), Elfner (1964), and Krumhansl and  Shepard (1979) suggest that subjects prefer intervals slightly deviating from the simple ones. For example, Ward (1954)  demonstrated that the subjects prefer not a pure octave expressed by the number 1/2, but rather a stretched one expressed by  the number 1/2.009. In addition, Ward and Martin came to the conclusion that "there is a reason to believe that very few  musicians can tell the difference between just intonation and equal temperament in melodic sequences, and even when they can,  they will prefer equal temperament" (Ward & Martin, 1961, p. 588). According to Meyer, these experiments do not undermine the  hypothesis of simple ratios: "A hearer of music who is not professionally prejudiced (as a performer, music critic,  researcher, etc.) can hear and enjoy melody in orchestral music even much more mistuned than an ordinary commercial piano,  because his brain interprets the auditory sensations just as the brain interprets geometrical design into the `optical  illusion' which were already well known to the ancient architects who built the famous Greek temples" (Meyer, 1962b, p. 128).  Therefore, if we take Meyer's point of view, then small systematic deviations from strict values do not indicate that "ideal"  values of preferred intervals are not simple ratios. Anticipating ourselves, we note that in the model which will be described  later, an important role is played by the assumption that musical intervals (taken without any context) are related to human  feelings. A hypothesis that such a relation may exist was suggested by Makeig (1982).  There have been theoretical attempts to support the hypothesis of simple ratios  as well as attempts to disprove it. For example, Boomsliter and Creel (1961, 1962) suggested a physiological explanation for  the hypothesis of simple ratios with the theory of "neural synchronization." Plomp and Levelt (1965) attempted to substantiate  this theory by modifying Helmholtz's ideas. Terhardt (1974) proposed a concept of virtual pitch based on the synthesis of  psycho-acoustic and gestalt notions. With the aid of this concept Terhardt developed an approach which would substantiate both  the hypothesis of simple ratios and subjects' preference of a stretched octave. On the other hand, Balzano (1980) used an  algebraic method to demonstrate that if an equal-tempered system of a 12-fold octave is represented as a cyclic group, then  formal analogues of diatonic scales are connected with the group automorphisms. In this approach, musical intervals are not  (with the exceptions of octave and unison) integral ratios. It is important to note that the appearance of Shepard's double  helix model (Shepard, 1982a, 1982b) shifted the problem of musical interval perception from psycho-acoustic into the domain of  cognitive psychology. As a result, the position of supporters of the simple ratios hypothesis was weakened, since they based  their arguments, in one way or another, on psycho-acoustic considerations.  If we accept the hypothesis of simple ratios we encounter a new problem which the theories analyzed above did not consider:  only some simple ratios are used (at least in the European culture). Let us look at the column of Just Intonation in Table  0.1. There we see the number 4/7, but not the number 5/7; we see 8/9 but not 7/9. Let us pose a question: Does Just Intonation  possess uniqueness as a set? In this paper we will provide some arguments in favor of this hypothesis without referring to any  psycho-acoustic ideas.  0.2. Geometrical Ratios  The opinion that the golden section has a special attractive power has persisted since ancient times (see, for example, Ghyka,  1946). Objective measurements also demonstrate that the golden section was used in ancient cultures more often than other  proportions (Colman & Coan, 1920; Doczi, 1981; Hambidge, 1920). The ratio ~/2/2 0.707 is the second most prevalent proportion  (but far less prevalent than the golden section). Ghyka (1946), in discussing how widespread various proportions in ancient  art were, cites data from a study of 120 ancient Greek vases from The Boston Museum. The data demonstrate that 96 times out of  120 vase shape is based on the golden section, 18 times on the proportion /2-/2, and 6 times on ~53/3. The golden section was  widely used by the artists of the Renaissance, for example, by Leonardo da Vinci (Archibald, 1920), and by our contemporaries,  such as Le Corbusier (1968).  Fechner (1876) was the first to try to experimentally verify the attractiveness of the golden section. The subjects were given  a set of ten rectangles with side proportions varying from 1/1 to 2/5 and asked to indicate the most and the least attractive  ones. Fechner found that the most attractive rectangle was the one with the golden section side length ratio. These results  were supported by Lalo (1908). However, later works, Davis (1933), Thompson (1946), Shipley, Daltman, and Steele (1947),  and Berlyne (1971), demonstrated that a choice of the most attractive rectangle is not stable and depends on conditions of the  particular experiment. In addition, Godkewitsch (1974) and Piehl (1976) found that such a choice also depends on the range of  rectangle side proportions (see also a discussion of these problems in Benjafield, 1985; Plug, 1980; and Valentine, 1962).  A common feature for the majority of experiments mentioned above is that the subjects were to choose an object from a given  set of objects. However, if we consider an experiment in which the subjects' task is to construct a "beautiful" object rather  than to choose it, we see different results. Svensson (1977) asked his subjects to draw five horizontal and five vertical  lines of different lengths and then to put a mark on each line in such a way that the resulting proportion would be the most  visually attractive. The maximum in the proportion distribution was close to the golden section ratio.  Another approach to the study of special properties of the golden section was implemented by Benjafield, Pomeroy, and Saunders  (1980). They demonstrated that the error in copying the golden section is significantly smaller than in copying the  proportions 2/3 and 3/4, and is equal to the error in copying the proportions 1/2.  Recently, an unexpected finding resulted in a study by Lockhead (1989). Subjects were given a sheet of paper with two dots on  it. The subjects were asked to put a pencil in the middle of an imaginary segment connecting these dots and move it to the  right or to the left until they felt that a dot placed where they are holding the pencil and the segment and closest to it  form "a group of dots" countervailing the other end of the segment. As a result the segments were divided into two unequal  parts. The data demonstrated that the average proportion for many subjects was close to the golden section ratio.  It is necessary to note that the experiments by Schiffman & Bobko (1978), Plug (1980), and Davis & Jahnke (1991), in which the  subjects generated ratios rather than choosing them, did not support the hypothesis of the golden section. Nevertheless, the  empirical facts and experimental data described above provide us with some support for the position that the golden section  indeed possesses attractiveness (outside of any special context). Let us emphasize that this attractiveness reveals itself  better in generating proportions than in perceiving them.  With the exception of Davis' observation, we could find no experimental data supporting the attractiveness hypothesis of the  proportion /2-/2. Davis' (1933) subjects were asked to draw a rectangle of the most pleasing proportions. He mentions  that the number /2-/2 corresponds to one of local maximums in the proportion distribution obtained in this experiment.  0.3. Approach  The main idea of our approach is to establish formal connections between the structure of human inner feelings, on the one  hand, and musical and geometric ratios, on the other. We can do this with the help of a theoretical model of a subject  who projects his inner states onto an external screen. We interpret these projections as ratios. Our model represents the  inner human domain: the theoretical subject has images of himself and of the world; he can also be aware that he has these  images. The subject and each element of his inner domain correspond to a variable. The value of the variable that corresponds  to the subject is interpreted as characterizing his state. The values of the variables that correspond to different elements  of his inner domain are interpreted as the intensity of the corresponding feelings. After postulating connections between the  variables we deduce the set of a subject's possible states. This set corresponds to a set of ratios. Further we demonstrate  that a musical (or geometric) proportion can be considered an information-carrying signal, whose function is to transmit a  state from one subject to another. We also demonstrate that with such an approach each proportion can be considered code for a  small natural number which allows us to evaluate for real musical intervals the boundaries for acceptable deviations from  ideal theoretical ratios. It has turned out that the acceptable range is much wider than the deviations seen in the European  tempered scale. We, therefore, propose the hypothesis that the theoretical values of musical intervals in cultures other than  European are also ratios of integers.  Since we put subjective phenomena at the foundation of our model, we have to answer the question: What is the source of  information regarding these phenomena? This source is our introspection understood in the broad sense of this word: the use of  not only our personal experience based on self-reflexion, but also of a general cultural picture of a person's inner life as  obtained from philosophy, psychology, psychiatry, and artistic literature. The word "introspection" has been extremely  unpopular in scientific psychology for more than 70 years. The main argument of the opponents of introspective methods in  psychology is that self-observation cannot be a source of information about the real state of the observer. We cannot disagree  with this critique. But why cannot introspection serve as a heuristic method helping us to construct theoretical models of a  human being together with his inner domain? Once the model has been constructed the arbitrariness disappears. The predictive  and explanatory power of such a model can be evaluated in the same ways as any behavioristic models. Of course, the variables  corresponding to measurable values must be distinguished from those which cannot be measured in principle. In our case, the  variable that can be measured corresponds to a subject's state, and those corresponding to his inner domain do not lend  themselves to instrumental measurement.  However, this situation is not unique in science. Quantum mechanics is in the same relation to reality. Its nucleus-wave  function cannot be revealed by means of experimental procedures; instead, it is postulated, and its introduction is justified  by the results it has produced. Our situation is similar: we postulate a certain structure for the inner human domain and as a  result we obtain the possibility to deduce experimentally verifiable hypotheses. But the model includes something more. For  psychiatrists and some philosophers, subjective phenomena are no less real than is behavior for a contemporary psychologist.  Thus, representing some phenomena of  human inner life with the help of a formal method can be of special interest for them.  The model described in this work is based on a more general model of a subject with multiple rank reflexion (V. A. Lefebvre,  1965a, 1965b, 1967, 1977a, 1977b, 1980, 1982, 1987, 1990a, 1990b). A discussion of various aspects of this model is given in  the works by Townsend (1983), Adams-Webber (1987), Bogatyrew (1989), Batchelder (1987), Kauffman (1987), Levitin (1987),  Sacksteder (1987), Rapoport (1982, 1987), Turner (1987), Wheeler (1987, 1990), and Zajonc (1987).  Independently of this model, Adams-Webber and Benjafield were developing the theory of Kelly (1955) and discovered  experimentally that subjects evaluating their acquaintances with the help of bipolar constructs (of the type wicked-kind) on  average chose a positive pole with the frequency 0.62 (Adams-Webber & Benjafield, 1973). Three years later they suggested a  hypothesis that the true value of this experimental number is the golden section (Benjafield & Adams-Webber, 1976). Further  studies concerned with verifying this hypothesis were conducted by AdamsWebber (1979, 1982, 1984, 1985a, 1985b), Benjafield  (1984), Benjafield and Green (1978), Schwartz and Garamoni (1986), Schwartz and Michelson (1987), Romany and Adams-Webber  (1981), Rigdon and Eptig (1982, Messick (1987), Marczewska (1983), Tuohy (1987), and Tuohy and Stradling (1987). All of these  studies support the idea that when the subjects evaluate people, they choose positive poles of bipolar constructs with  frequencies in the range 0.60-0.64. Similar results were obtained when subjects evaluated the conditions of their work  (Shalit, 1980) and when they evaluated inanimate objects (V. D. Lefebvre, 1990). V. A. Lefebvre (1985, 1987), V. A. Lefebvre,  V. D. Lefebvre, and Adams-Webber (1986) attempted to explain these results by using a multilayered model of the reflexing  subject. According to V. A. Lefebvre (1985), a precise theoretical value for the golden section corresponds to the case when  subjects evaluate inanimate objects rather than people (we provide a brief description of this explanation, because it  underlies the model of a subject generating musical and geometric proportions).  The general plan of the paper is as follows: in the first section, we introduce an equation for attractive proportions and  deduce its implications; in the second section, we describe our model and formally deduce equations for attractive  proportions; in the third section, we consider proportions as information-carrying signals.  1. In the subsection Model l, we give a modernized description of a  model elaborated earlier (V. A. The Metaphor  We assume that the subject is constructed in a manner similar to that of a Chinese doll or a Russian "matrioshka": Inside the  first subject is another subject which is an "image of the self" in the first subject, and inside this image of the self is  one more subject which is an "image of the self in the image of the self." The third subject does not have anything inside it.  (We must emphasize that this construction is purely metaphorical. It is a kind of mnemonic scheme that allows us to represent  a set of elements and relations that underlie the construction of our model in an easily observable form.)  Imagine now that each one of the three dolls has a pocket in which we can place an object. The object in the pocket of the  first doll is the "world affecting the subject"; the object in the pocket of the second doll is "an image of this world"; and  finally, the object in the pocket of the third doll is "an image of the world which belongs to the image of the self."  The cavity inside the first subject in which an image of the self and an image of the world are inserted is analogous to the  domain where human inner life takes place. In the framework of this paper, we will not discuss philosophical aspects  associated with the nature of this inner domain, its relation to the physical reality, to the brain, etc. These are important  questions, but the model can be constructed independently from them (see V. The States of Components  Every component at any moment is in one of the states from the set of states L, where L has the power of the continuum. We  separate a subset L1 from the set L which is called the subset of pure states, and each component of this subset is called a  pure state. This subset consists of two elements: we call one of them positive and the second one negative. A complementary  subset LZ = L - L1 is called the subset of mixed states, and each element of this subset is called a mixed state. There is a  oneto-one correspondence between the set L and the interval [0, 1 ], such that the positive element corresponds to 1, and the  negative to 0. Each component, independently of its initial state, can transform itself into one of the two pure states. This  transition is called reduction. The number x that corresponds to a state is a conditional probability that the component in  that state will transfer to the "positive" state if reduction takes place.  We introduce now the set E, which is in one-to-one correspondence with the interval [0, 1 ]. We call E a screen. The assumption that the main part of emotional phenomena is automatic awareness of them is based on a peculiarity of human  self understanding discovered by the Prague psychiatrist Max Levi in 1908 (see Reznik, 1969). One of his patients complained  that from time to time she lost the ability to experience emotions. However, the "emotional" form of these complaints  demonstrated that her ability to experience emotions remained intact. Levi explained this amazing phenomenon by saying that  this patient lost the ability to feel her own mental activity, to feel her own feelings, but she did not lose her ability to  have feelings per se.  The Levi case suggests to us that human feelings have a two-tiered structure: on the one hand, a person experiences certain  feelings, and on the other, his image of the self also experiences certain feelings. If this image suddenly loses its ability  to feel, the person becomes blind to his own feelings. He experiences. feelings but does not feel that he is experiencing them.  This particular two-tiered structure exists in our presentation of a subject as a formula (2.3). If we did not introduce x3,  our subject would be similar to Levi's patient: he would be able to experience feelings d and D, but he would not know that he  was experiencing any feeling. This is why we believe that a "healthy" subject must have cognizant images of the world and of  the self, that is, the images of images.  2.3.11. The Three Postulates (2)  1.
