JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 12, 114-135 (1975)  The Relative Judgment Theory of Two Choice Response Time  STEVE W. LINK  McMaster University, Hamilton, Ontario, Canada  Choice reaction times and response probabilities are analyzed in terms of a random walk decision process. The theory of  relative judgment which gives rise to this analysis assumes that a presented stimulus is compared by the subject to an  internal psychological standard or referent. Discrepancies between the stimulus and the referent are accumulated until one or  the other of two response thresholds is exceeded. Results from three experiments are examined in detail. In each experiment a  particular feature of the theory is tested and shown to be supported by the data. Methods of estimating the two parameters of  the theory are given and an appendix presents a summary of theoretical results.  INTRODUCTION  The Theory of Relative Judgment describes the mental process of choosing between two possible actions (R,, or RB), in response  to the presentation of a single stimulus. The major assumptions of the theory are as follows. Upon presentation of the  stimulus a subject compares over time physiologically transduced values of the stimulus to an internal psychological referent  or standard. During a unit of time, At, the difference so obtained is added to an accumulator of discrepancies. When the  accumulated discrepancy rises above a subject controlled threshold, response RA occurs. If the accumulated discrepancy falls  below a second subject controlled response threshold then response RB is made. If neither threshold is surpassed then another  discrepancy is accumulated.  The assumption of an internal referent has been a necessary component of many theories of psychophysical discrimination.  Peirce and Jastrow (1884), Thurstone (1927), Blackwell (1963), Luce (1963), and Green and Swets (1966), have each postulated  that comparison of a sensory value (or function of a sensory value) to a referent results in a classification of the stimulus  presented to the subject. TheTheory of Relative Judgment maintains that the referent adopted by a subject does not provide  immediate classification of the stimulus but rather contributes toward the eventual act of responding to the stimulus  presented. The present discussion centers on the application of Relative Judgment Theory to two  choice response time (CRT) with an emphasis on establishing the empirical predictions of the theory. The obtained predictions  specify relationships between response time and response probability that depend only upon two parameters and a constant. In  addition these predictions are virtually free from constraints imposed by the precise form of the probability density  functions (pdf's) that characterize the probabilistic behavior of a difference between the stimulus and the subject's  referent. Because of the distribution free character of the theory a wide range of experimental tests can be made by examining  changes in estimated parameter values of the theory as a function of experimenter controlled variables.  THE THEORY OF CHOICE REACTION TIME  We will assume that during an experimental trial either of two stimuli (S,, , SB) can be presented to a subject and that the  subject must correctly identify the stimulus by responding Ri to Si (i = A, B). The necessary components of the theory include  the processes of stimulus transduction, comparison, and decision, each of which contributes to the variability and duration of  the eventual response as measured by the frequencies of correct and erroneous responses together with correct and error  response times.  The process of stimulus transduction produces a psychological representation of the stimulus presented to the subject. Like  Thurstone (1927) we will assume that a psychological representation of Si is a variable defined on a psychological continuum,  X. Since the process of transduction may itself introduce random variation in the psychological representation of Si , values  of the transduced stimulus will be represented by a random variable Xi with probability density function fi(X). Both  variability in the physical stimulus and variability in the transduction process determine the distribution of Xi.  When only a single stimulus is presented to the subject, and a transduced value of the stimulus is produced, how is the  subject to determine whether the value of Xi is a characterization of S,, or SB ? The present theory shares with other  psychophysical theories the common assumption that a categorization of Xi is obtained by a comparison of Xi to a psychological  referent, X,. For example, the Theory of Signal Detection (Green & Swets, 1966) assumes a referent, called the criterion,  against which an obtained sensory representation of the stimulus is compared. Similarly Adaptation Level Theory (Helson, 1959)  supposes that the level of adaptation to the experimental situation is a referent against which the sampled stimulus is  compared. Naturally the referent X, need not have a fixed value but instead may be treated as a random variable with pdf f,(X)  and expected value EX, bounded by the expected values of X,, and XB (i.e., EXE < EX, < EX,,). Variability in the value of X,  will contribute to the  116 S. W. LINK  total variability faced by the subject when attempting to decide whether S,, or SB was the presented stimulus. In this regard we will make two assumptions: first, that the value of Xi is compared to X,. by  subtraction of X,. from Xi ; and second that the differences so obtained are accumulated over intervals of time. Naturally it is not necessary that the random walk begin at zero. A shift in the starting position to a new starting point C  increases the probability that the process terminates at A. The distances from the new starting position to the random walk  barriers at A and -A become A - C and A + C, respectively. Thus, a shift in starting position characterizes a simultaneous  change in threshold values for the two responses, A - C for R,, and A + C for RB . Presumably these biases toward one or the  other of two responses can be obtained through experimental manipulation of stimulus presentation probabilities or response  payoffs.  The probability density function for increments to the random walk determine how the accumulation of differences traverses the  distance to the terminal boundary from the initial position. In this regard an important class of random walk models is the  class of symmetric random walks (Stone, 1960). Estimates of BA and BC provide for a test of two normative theories of response bias. The first of these theories is the  statisticians' model for error minimization. The second model is the psychologists' model based on expectancy. When stimulus  presentation probabilities are varied, which should bias the subjects responses toward RA or RB , the error minimization and  expectancy theories of response bias produce distinctly different predictions concerning the optimal location of the random  walk starting position. A shift of half the distance toward A may provide correct response probabilities near unity.  Response Time Predictions  One of the well known effects in CRT is the systematic change in marginal RT as stimulus presentation probabilities are varied  (Audley, 1973). Generally, when both stimuli are presented randomly the mean RT is a maximum when the presentation  probability, 77, equals 0.50. Deviations of a from 0.50 result in expected marginal RTs that become smaller as the deviation  (either positive or negative) increases. This result has been taken as support for theories of CRT performance based upon  mixtures of RT distributions associated with different psychological states (e.g., Falmagne, 1965; Ollman, 1966; Yellott,  1971; Falmagne and Theios, 1969).  It is therefore of some interest that Relative Judgment Theory predicts a similar decline in marginal RT as rr deviates from  0.50. An Application of the Tests  Of the numerous choice response experiments reported in the literature few report  122 S. W. LINK  data in sufficient detail to test the proposed theory. Although error rates are often reported, few studies also report mean  error times. In fact, many experimenters have treated the existence of erroneous responding as an embarrassment rather than  the result of a process that may occasionally generate errors. In yet other studies various functions of correct and error  times are illustrated but the mean response times are seldom reported. Method  Four right-handed university students each volunteered to participate in five experimental sessions. Subjects were instructed  to initiate a trial by placing the index fingers of each hand on two solid state response keys. A response was to be made by  lifting one finger from a chosen response key. The response keys consisted of a metal center post slightly raised above a  surrounding metal ring. By passively resting his fingers on the response keys a subject immediately activated a response panel  buffer that was sampled at least once every 3001Lsec by the PDP 8/I computer that controlled stimulus randomization, visual  presentations on a calligraphically driven display screen, and the measurement of response times. After the initiation of the  trial the response keys were regularly monitored to determine whether the subject had inadvertantly lifted a finger from a  key. If a release occurred prior to the stimulus presentation (even if replaced prior to stimulus presentation) the trial was  omitted from the analysis of data.  At the initiation of a trial a subject was presented with a value of 7r indicating the probability that stimulus S,, would be  presented on that trial. Values of a ranging from .875 to .125 in steps of .125, were chosen by a psuedo-random number  generator and presented for one second. After a fixed delay of one second either S,, or SB was chosen randomly with a  probability near a and presented to the subject until a response occurred. The stimuli were two square figures approximately 1  cm on a side with the only feature distinguishing the figures being a diagonal line connecting either the lower left to upper  right corners or the lower right to the upper left corners. The display screen on which allvisual presentations occurred was  situated 1 m in front of the subject and all subjects were required to view the display with their heads supported by a chin  rest. Each session contained 375 trials and lasted approximately 30 min due in part to time between trials taken up by the  computer punching trial by trial results onto paper tape and the time taken for the subject to initiate a new trial.  For two subjects a indicated the probability of one stimulus while for the remaining two subjects Tr indicated the probability  of the other stimulus. Subjects were balanced across response keys so that when iT indicated the probability of a particular  stimulus, one subject responded correctly by releasing the right hand key while the other subject responded correctly by  release of the left hand response key.  126 S. It has been shown that changes in stimulus presentation probability promote changes in bias toward one response threshold  while simultaneously producing a bias away from the remaining response threshold. Neither of these preceding experiments,  however, was designed to determine whether the shift in bias due to presentation probability would remain constant across  changes in a second experimenter controlled variable. SUMMARY  The three experiments presented here have demonstrated the applicability of Relative Judgment Theory to two choice tasks in  which stimuli are potentially easily discriminable. The distinguishing feature of each experiment concerns the extrastimulus  factors that have a major influence upon performance. These factors influence, indeed determine, through their effects on the  decision process the frequencies of responses to one or the other of the two stimuli.  Response time serves as a measure of performance that covaries with response frequency. The extent to which Relative Judgment  Theory can be applied to data can easily be determined by simply plotting mean response time against a function of response  probability. In the three experiments herein reported the predicted linear fit was obtained. Once this linearity has been  established changes in performance across experimental conditions will be due entirely to changes in bias and response  A single experimental condition provides data from which estimates of bias, BC, and response thresholds, 9A, can be obtained.  The variation in these estimates across experimental conditions provides a means of examining the higher-order cognitive  processes that produce changes in the performance as extra-stimulus experimental variables are manipulated. The study of these  higher-order processes is of primary importance in psychology. Relative Judgment Theory contributes to this pursuit by  providing an investigatory tool that is easily understood and can be applied to a wide range of discrimination experiments.
