The method of triads is a natural extension of the method of paired comparisons. Unidimensional triadic judgments arise in situations where only one particular attribute of the stimulus is of immediate concern. The standard detection and discrimination paradigms, in practice, require the perceived difference between test stimuli to be either zero (no difference) or small (just noticeable). Thus, when large (or quite noticeable) difference sensations are also of interest to the investigator, the method of triads appears to be a suitable alternative. An example of the application of the method is found in  Krantz (1967) , where it was used to measure both small and large color differences within a single paradigm.

The representation of triadic (measurement) models, as these models will be referred to in the subsequent discussion, has been formalized and investigated by  Falmagne (1985, pp. 277–279)  under the heading “bisection”. The goals of the current work are (1) to elucidate the connections between models of triadic judgments and those of paired comparisons, and (2) to derive the analytic forms of some of these triadic models in the presence of Weber's law. The former is motivated by the idea that triadic judgments can be seen as paired comparisons of pairs of percepts, while the latter is motivated by the results obtained in works such as  Falmagne, Iverson, and Marcovici (1979)  in psychoacoustic research and  Falmagne and Iverson (1979) , which showed that the class of feasible choice models is considerably reduced when Weber's law holds.

The paper is organized as follows:  Section 2 , the triadic choice data and the paradigm which generates them are described, and the definition of a triadic probability is formulated.  Section 3 , probabilistic measurement models for triadic judgments are presented and related to bisection and discrimination models.  Section 4  formulates Weber's law in the context of triadic judgments and discusses the relationship between the law and models of triadic judgments.  Section 5 , two propositions are proved about the impact of Weber's law on triadic models.  Section 6  briefly summarizes the discussion and remarks on a couple of remaining open problems.

The results constitute partial solutions to exercises number 8–10 in Chapter 12 of  Falmagne's (1985)  
                      Elements of Psychophysical Theory . These exercises pose open problems where the reader is invited to investigate the impact of Weber's law on a general class of probabilistic measurement models for triadic judgments.

This assumption, in effect, reduces similarity judgments of triads to a paired comparison system. Having related Eq.  (1)  to models of discrimination and bisection paradigms, it is natural to ask if the model is testable and how well it summarizes a body of data. The first question can be more sharply posed and answered in two parts: (i) Exactly what constraints does representation (1) impose on data? The answer is that there are two ordinal constraints among triadic choice probabilities called the  joint double cancellation conditions  ( Sheu, 1990 ); (ii) Are there statistical methods available for testing such conditions? Though there is no tailor-made test for the conditions, Iverson and Falmagne (1985) showed how to construct such tests based on the maximum likelihood ratio principle. But a test along this line would require a large amount of data and simulation work. Recently,  Karabatsos and Sheu (2004)  applied Bayesian order-constrained inference to test these stochastic conditions. Unfortunately, statistical issues in the testing of the probabilistic measurement axioms are, in general, quite involved. For practical purposes, it seems that studying special models of Eq.  (1)  will be more rewarding at the present stage of theory development. On the other hand, certain readily verifiable empirical invariances often lead to powerful constraints on a class of models. For instance, in the presence of Weber's law the class of additive models for loudness summation data is greatly reduced ( Falmagne & Iverson, 1979 ). A second example is that in a balanced discrimination system ( Falmagne, 1985, p. 119 ), the so-called  bicancellation condition  must hold whenever Weber's law holds ( Falmagne, 1985, p. 128 ). I will now discuss particular models of Eq.  (1) . Weber's law as an empirical constraint for models of triadic judgments will be examined later.

The paper examines a Fechnerian-type representation for triadic choice probabilities in the form of Eq.  (1)  and derives two analytic forms for subcases of such a representation assuming that Weber's law holds. In addition, the probabilistic measurement models for triadic judgements have been related to models of discrimination and bisection. Given a body of choice data derived from the method of triads, the researcher might first check if Weber's law holds and then fit the particular forms of the models suggested by the above results to data.
