Draper Laboratories, Cambridge, Massachusetts  The Theory of Signal Detectability (TSD) has nearly replaced classical notions of the threshold because of its ability to  separate sensory and decision processes in weak-signal detection and recognition paradigms. The primary emphasis of recent  work has concentrated on the sensory rather than the decision aspects and almost all work has been exclusively at one signal  strength. We propose a model to describe behavior at different signal strengths based on subjective rather than objective  distributions. The model predicts performance at a constant likelihood ratio (LR) criterion (even though subjective  distributions are the basis for determining cutoff criteria) unless the observer adopts a Subjective Neyman-Pearson (SNP)  objective. Results from an experiment in visual discrimination suggest that some observers in fact operate at constant  objective LR's as signal strength is varied randomly over a wide range. The objective LR's of the other subjects changed  dramatically with signal strength, but this behavior is consistent with the use of a Subjective Neyman-Pearson (SNP) decision  rule and the linear relation between subjective and objective log LR's found in the studies of subjective probability.  1 Sponsored in part by NASA Grant NGR 22-009-733 from the Man-Machine Integration Branch, NASA Ames Research Center. ISSN 0022-2496  DECISION BEHAVIOR 47  INTRODUCTION  In this paper we consider in detail the decision aspects of the Theory of Signal Detectability (TSD) that has found wide  application in the psychophysics of the last two decades, particularly as a model in psychophysical detection and recogpition  tasks. The general details of the model are well known and may be found in standard monographs such as Green and Swets (1966).  As with the majority of expositions on TSD, we will consider here only the case of two alternative stimulus classes, which in  the theory are represented as hypotheses.  TSD, in common with the more modern of the threshold theories, has allowed the experimental separation of the sensory and  response elements of psychophysics. The sensory elements are typically assumed to be derived from the distributions of the  stimulus realizations on a psychological continuum. Observed behavior, then, is a joint consequence of the distance between  these distributions (the sensory element commonly referred to as signal strength) and a subjectively established criterion  (the response element) which determines the specific mapping from stimulus state to response state.  We are exclusively concerned here with the response element: How do observers select response criteria? In approaching this  problem, the first question that is usually asked is, On what continuum is a criterial value selected? In the normative  application of TSD, this continuum has been associated with the likelihood ratio (LR) or a monotonic function of the LR (see  Green & Swets, 1966), since it is well known that an LR decision rule is optimal for a variety of optimization goals, e.g.  (Green, 1960):  (1) Maximize percent correct;  (2) Maximize expected value;  (3) Maximize P(Hit) PP(False Alarm);  (4) Maximize a posteriori probability;  (S) Maximize P(Hit) at fixed P(False Alarm) (Neyman-Pearson objective).  The importance of an LR criterion level is given more weight by the fact that for fixed a priori probabilities, the optimal LR  criterion level is independent of signal strength for all of the objectives above except the Neyman-Pearson objective.  However, a wide variety of criteria selection processes have been proposed as alternatives to the normative application of  TSD. Parks (1966) has suggested that observers adjust decision criteria such that the probability associated with making a  particular response will correspond (within a multiplicative constant) to the probability with which the stimulus event  actually occurs. When the multiplicative constant equals 1, that is, the probability of saying a stimulus occurs equals the  probability that the stimulus is actually presented, this rule is referred to as the matching hypothesis. Thomas and Legge  (1970) explored this general model and showed, among other  48o/15/1-4  48 CURRY, NAGEL AND GAI  things, that the model can account for the observation that when a priori signal probability is varied in a detection or  recognition task, subjects perform in a conservative manner relative to the ideal observer. Smith (1970) has suggested that  observers in recognition experiments may choose LR criteria as a function of the particular memory strength for the  to-be-recognized items. Specifically, criteria will be selected such that the number of difficult decisions the observer must  make is kept below some critical value, which might be determined as a function of the payoff matrix. Here a difficult  decision is said to result when the observation on a particular trial results in an LR close to the chosen criterion value of  LR. It is important to note, however, that given the kind of process he envisions, it matters little whether the criteria)  continuum is likelihood ratio or some other function of the observation space, such as stimulus energy. It would be impossible  to distinguish one from another on the basis of observer behavior in many experiments.  Indeed, for experiments in which experimental conditions (e.g., signal strength, a priori probability, etc.) remain fixed from  trial-to-trial or over a block of trials, it is quite difficult to determine the manner in which observers choose decision  criteria since decision processes are confounded with sensory or other processing. There are two other classes of experiments,  however, in which it is at least conceptually possible to determine decision strategies. In the first class of experiments,  inspired by the normative version of TSD, response criteria are manipulated by changes in the payoff structure or a priori  signal probabilities (e.g., Egan, Schulman, & Greenberg, 1959; Tanner, Swets, & Green, 1956; Swets, Tanner, & Birdsall, 1961).  Results are generally as predicted by the model, although real observers tend to be somewhat more conservative than the  normative theory would have it.  In the second class of experiments, response strategies can be assessed because the signal and/or the noise strengths are  allowed to vary from one trial to the next. Although such signal situations bear more obvious resemblances to everyday  experience than the more common fixed-strength designs encountered in the literature, they have not received much systematic  study.  When mixed-strength designs do appear, they are usually associated with studies of human memory (Kinchla & Smyzer, 1967;  Smith, 1970; Parks, 1966; Healy & Jones, 1973), but other instances of multiple signal strength designs exist in the  psychophysical literature (Broadbent, 1971, p. 109; Markowitz & Swets, 1967; Pollack & Decker, 1958). In the two latter cases,  which are typical of multiple-strength psychophysical studies, signal strengths are blocked. That is, a fixed number of trials  (a block) is typically presented at any given strength before the strength is changed. This, of course, allows an observer to  use different response strategies for different blocks. We are interested in the more demanding case in which signals of mixed  strength may be expected at any time by the observer.  Healy and Jones (1973) review a number of studies which address the question of whether or not criteria change in experiments  that vary task difficulty (e.g., signal  DECISION BEHAVIOR 49  strength). They choose to consider a different index of criterion than the likelihood ratio, reasoning that in experiments in  which a priori signal probability changes, LR criteria do not provide a good index of whether an observer is being "lax" or  "strict" in making his decisions on a trial to trial basis. They prefer instead, to consider the ratio of the joint  probabilities of hit (that is the joint probability of signal occurring and saying yes) and false alarm as the proper  criterial index. If an observer keeps this criterion fixed as prior signal probability is varied, he will be holding the  conditional probability of a false alarm at a constant ratio to the conditional probability of a hit. This will not occur, of  course, if a constant likelihood ratio is the criterion as prior signal probability is varied.  It should be noted that Healy and Jones are concerned with what are commonly termed "Type II" detection or recognition  analyses. In such situations, the observers are asked to evaluate their own responses (typically to items in memory recall  experiments rather than an external stimulus as such). For memory recall experiments, in which signal strength is changed by  changing the retention interval between learning and recall, evidence can be found for both the contention that criteria (as  defined by Healy & Jones) vary as a function of signal strength (Healy & Jones, 1973) or are fixed when signal strength is  varied (Murdock, 1966). Intelligibility experiments, however, more typically display LR criterion change as a function of  changing signal strength (e.g., Pollack & Decker, 1958; Clarke, 1960). It should be pointed out that the most consistent  evidence for LR criterion change comes from those experiments in which signal trials are blocked. When trials with varying  signal strength are interspersed at random throughout an experimental session, as was done by Murdock (1966), the Type II  criterion described by Healy and Jones appears to remain fixed although calculated LR criteria varied across retention  interval.  Thus the question of how an observer chooses a criterion for a response is open. Especially little is known about such  processes when signal strength is varied at random from trial to trial. We propose a model for decision behavior in which the  observer is presumed to perform the optimal processing using subjective rather than objective distributions, as is usually the  case. This approach is analogous to the "misperception," as opposed to the "misaggregation," explanation of the conservatism  in decision makers' behavior (Rapoport & Wallsten, 1972): Bayes' rule is used correctly, but on subjective rather than  objective distributions. In support of the misaggregation hypothesis, Wheeler and Beach (1968) and Peterson, Du Charme, and  Edwards (1968) found better predictions of probability updating with subjective distributions than if they had used the  theoretical or objective distributions. We present some preliminary results in a visual discrimination experiment which  suggest that people make decisions on the basis of subjective distributions that do not coincide with the true or objective  stimulus distributions. One unequivocal finding is that for experimental situations in which signal strength is allowed to  vary from trial to trial, decisions for certain observers are not made on the basis of a constant likelihood ratio. Results  The observed P(H)-P(FA) data for each half of the experiment are given in Table 2 and the theoretical decision rule curves and  the raw data are shown in Fig. 5-9. A visual inspection of the raw data indicates some changes in LLR criteria between the  first and second half of the experiment, but perhaps the most unusual feature is the extreme departure from a constant LR  decision rule for some observers (e.g., Figs. 7, $, 9). These data follow the trend of increasing P(H) at nearly constant  P(FA) with a sudden decrease in P(FA) at the higher signal strengths.  The data were analyzed in detail by a general Maximum Likelihood program (Curry, 1975) which estimates the parameters, the  standard deviations of the estimates (from the Cramer--Rao bound), and a X2 goodness-of-fit measure. A subroutine to describe  the constant LR and Subjective Neyman-Pearson (SNP) decision rules was used to simultaneously estimate the seven (or eight)  parameters of the models [5 d' values, 2 z8 or g(Al) values, and b for the Subjective Neyman-Pearson rule] for each observer.  The results of these computations are shown in Table 3. These parameter estimates confirm the criterion shifts observed in the  raw data and indicate possible changes in decision rule from the first to the second half of the experiment for some subjects. DISCUSSION  The strongest conclusion to be drawn from the experimental result is that subjects operate with (objective) LR criterion  levels that vary with signal strength. Data similar in nature were tabulated by Kinchla and Smyzer (1967) and we have plotted  them in the P(H)-P(FA) plane in Fig. 10 as further evidence of this behavior. In both our visual discrimination data and the  visual perceptual memory data of Kinchla and Smyzer (in which the signal strengths were blocked) there is a tendency for the  observers to have high false alarm rates (compared to the typical auditory detection experiment) and to maintain a relatively  constant false alarm rate across signal strengths. The SEU Model  The most viable alternative to the Subjective Neyman-Pearson rule as an explanation of the observations would appear to be a  breakdown of the Subjective Expected Utility (SEU) theory. Of the three major assumptions comprising the SEU model (Tversky,  1967) the independence of utilities and subjective probabilities is critical in this situation. When the independence  principle is valid, the subjective LLR criterion level is determined by the log of the regret ratio  A8 = ln[(Uoo - Uo&(Uli - Uio)j,  (19)  where Uii is the utility of responding hypothesis i when hypothesis j is actually true. It is difficult to completely  eliminate the change in utilities as the source of variation without experimental evidence to the contrary. Although the  payoff matrix has been manipulated to alter criterion levels (Green & Swets, 1966) we are not aware of any utility  measurements in a psychophysical setting even at one signal strength, and results for multiple signal strengths would be  required here.  In spite of this lack of data, we feel that the change of utilities with signal strength (i.e., the breakdown of the SEU  model) is a less satisfactory explanation than the Subjective Neyman-Pearson rule for the following reasons. Wallsten (1970),  in explaining when the SEU model has been found to be valid, summarized the experimental efforts by suggesting that the SEU  model is more appropriate in "simple" rather than "complex" decision situations. Although the line of demarcation between  "simple" and "complex" decisions must necessarily be a fuzzy one, the decisions reported here must surely lie toward the  "simple" end of the continuum. Furthermore, unusual changes in utilities would be necessary to explain the observed results.  An examination of the data in Figs. 6-9 indicates that the log of the regret ratio (and the LLR) is nonmonotonic with signal  strength and even changes sign in some instances. Although this is consistent with a SNP rule, the utilities would have to  change in a manner such that  (1) a "right" (or "left") response is perferred at longer distances,  DECISION BEHAVIOR 67  (2) the utilities change to strengthen this preference for "right" or "left" at intermediate distances, and  (3) the utilities change again to bias responses in the opposite direction at nearer distances.  Response Ambiguity  There is a possible response ambiguity in the SNP decision rule when more than two response; categories are available. If two  criteria for three responses (as in the present case) are being used, one with constant P(FA), the other with constant P(H),  there is one signal strength at which the decision rule curves may intersect. For signal strengths higher than this there is a  response ambiguity for stimulus realizations between LLR criteria for each category boundary: For such realizations, a "noise"  response is indicated for constant P(FA) criterion, but a "signal" response is indicated for constant P(H) criterion. Some  evidence for this is shown in Fig. 6 for the P(H)-P(FA) performance for observer JO's first-half data (open symbols). The data  are consistent with both a constant P(H) and P(FA) criteria, and all data agree with the theoretical prediction. Since  observer JO changed his strategy to two constant P(FA) criteria for the second half of the experiment, and since all the other  subjects exhibiting Neyman-Pearson type of behavior adopted criteria of the same type (constant P(.FA)), it is conjectured  that the choice of asymmetrical criteria may be a direct result of the response ambiguity, and the choices of criteria are  made to reduce or eliminate the ambiguities.  The Experimental Data  A note of caution is in order concerning the data reported here. Since five signal strengths were intermixed, a very long  experiment would have been required to collect the number of responses usually obtained in single strength detection and  recognition experiments. Such an experiment would be long enough to question the observer's ability to maintain constant  criteria for the entire experiment. Moreover, we felt that the experimental resources for this new paradigm would prove more  insightful if allocated to several subjects in shorter experiments rather than to one or two subjects in longer experiments.  In view of the changes observed between the first and second halves of the experiment the data should be considered as highly  suggestive and most of the conclusions as tentative, requiring further confirmation. A difficulty with multiple signal  strength experiments is that the subject may have to maintain as many as m(n - 1) criteria for m signal strengths and n  responses over the entire experiment, whereas he need only maintain (n - 1) criteria over l Imth of the, total experiment when  the signal strengths are blocked.  (7$ CURRY, NAGEL AND CAI  SUMMARY  We have examined decision behavior with multiple signal strength stimuli and proposed a quantitative model in which subjective  (rather than objective) distributions play a key role. The model predicts that for constant a priori probabilities the  performance of the observer should lie along a constant (objective) LR curve unless a Neyman-Pearson objective is being used.  The experimental evidence from a visual discrimination task shows that the LR criterion level can change with signal strength.  The most plausible alternative to a Subjective Neyman-Pearson (SNP) rule is the breakdown of the SEU model, but other  experimental evidence suggests that this is unlikely in the "simple" decision setting used here, and moreover, the required  utility changes would be most unusual to explain the observations. Four of the five observers exhibited behavior consistent  with a SNP decision rule and it is conjectured that criteria of the same type (constant P(FA) for these observers) were  adopted to avoid response ambiguities that would otherwise exist.
