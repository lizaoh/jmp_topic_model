JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY: 4, 301-315 (1967)  A Generalized Urn Scheme for Simple Learning with a Continuum of Responses'  BARRY C. ARNOLD  Iowa State University, Ames, Iowa  A generalization of the classical urn scheme is suggested for the case of simple learning with a continuum of responses. The  behavior of the new model under simple noncontingent and simple contingent reinforcement is analyzed. Sufficient conditions  for ergodic behavior in the case of contingent reinforcement are developed. The paper concludes with an empirical comparison  of the linear model and the correlated urn scheme.  1. INTRODUCTION  A generalization of the finite-response linear model was suggested (Suppes, 1959) to cover the case of simple learning with a  continuum of responses and reinforcements; and subsequently, analogous generalizations of the pattern- and component-stimulus  sampling models were proposed (Suppes, 1960). Some studies of the empirical validity othese models have appeared (e.g.,  Suppes and Rouanet, 1964; Suppes, Rouanet, Levine, and Frankmann, 1964) which have revealed that the models perform  unsatisfactorily when predicting sequential dependencies. Nevertheless, for noncontingent reinforcement, the unconditional  response distribution predicted by the linear and stimulus sampling models are asymptotically in good correspondence with  observed distributions. The correlated urn scheme introduced here predicts the same asymptotic response distribution as do  earlier models; but it predicts different asymptotic response distributions conditioned on preceding responses and/or  reinforcements.  One of the earliest models suggested to explain observed response variations in a simple learning situation was the urn  scheme. The urn scheme for the case of two response alternatives may be described as follows. The subject's responses, which  may be numbered I and 2, are governed by selection of a ball from an urn of variable composition. At time zero the urn  contains n red balls and m white balls. If response 1 is reinforced on a trial, then w red balls and x white balls are added  to the urn. If response 2 is reinforced, then y red balls and z white balls are added. The subject  1 This paper is based on the author's Ph. D. thesis (Arnold, 1965). The reader is referred to Feller (1957, Chap. 5) for a discussion of some of the properties of this type of  stochastic process, and to Audley and Jonkheere (1956) for a discussion of some generalizations of this model.  A little reflection will show that the above scheme results in a commutative operator model; i.e., the order of reinforcements  is immaterial. Unfortunately, observation refutes the validity of such models. The order does matter, in that recent  reinforcements have more effect than those in the distant past. The simplest adjustment, and the one which will be exploited,  is to make the probability of drawing a ball depend on the length of time that the ball has been in the urn. This yields what  we call a correlated urn scheme. The notation and derivations that follow will be for the case of a continuum of responses and  reinforcements, but it will be clear that they simplify to cover the two-response case also.  The correlated urn scheme will be defined in Section 2; Sections 3 and 4 will be devoted to a detailed analysis of its  behavior under simple noncontingent and simple contingent reinforcement schedules. Section 5 will be concerned with an  empirical comparison of the correlated urn scheme with the linear model.  2. THE CORRELATED URN SCHEME  This model is applicable to simple learning situations.
