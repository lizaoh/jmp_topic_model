LORDAHL2  University of Miami, Coral Gables, Florida 33124  An hypothesis model of stable-state (no feedback) choice behavior is developed for a sequential prediction task. In three  studies, an hypothesis set for the application of the model is developed, a maximum likelihood fit of the model to data is  made, and a priori predictions from the fits to new data are tested. The model involves 40 different parameters, corresponding  to particular hypotheses related to perceived pattern structures in the stimulus sequences, and justifications for such a  complex model are given.  One may consider the behavior of human subjects in a two-choice prediction task to be dependent upon the perception of pattern  structure in preceeding events and upon the expectations that such patterning will continue. This point of view leads to a  number of assumptions which differ from many made in traditional stimulus sampling models for sequential prediction  experiments (See Neimark and Estes, 1967). For example, the effective stimulus resulting from some fixed sequence of binary  events may differ from subject to subject, or from one presentation of the sequence to another for the same subject, depending  upon the perception of the sequence. This reconceptualization of the effective stimulus is already frequent in the literature.  Restle (1961, 1966, 1967) has proposed that runs of the same event serve as powerful stimuli in two-choice probability  learning situations. Gambino and Myers (1967) and Rose and Vitz (1966) also have used this approach, with the additional  consideration of single alternations as a perceived structure in the Gambino and Myers article.  To illustrate the sorts of hypotheses to be used in this paper, the sequence "12212112" may be examined. If a subject were to  perceive (encode) this sequence as 1221-2112, then a possible hypothesis is that the initial sequence of length four  alternates with its complement, and the prediction, of the next two digits, would be 12. A major difficulty with viewing sequential predictions as based on perceived patterning and hypothesis testing is the large  number of possible structures one might propose as effective in a given situation. Gambino and Myers (1967, p. 417) refer to  this problem when discussing the inclusion of a single alternation feature in their model, which ". . . raises the unpleasant  possibility of having to postulate a host of expectancy vectors, one for each possible type of pattern that might be  encountered . . ." They reject the need for any large array of postulated hypotheses by interpreting available evidence as  showing that runs are the most important effective stimuli in probability learning experiments, and with the hope that only a  few additional factors will be needed to account for most of the observed variance.  In the present paper, a model with a rather large number of structural factors will be presented. This runs counter to the  position taken by Gambino and Myers, as well as to the more usual tendency to start with simple models and add to them only  gradually. It is my position that much of human behavior, and in particular certain types of sequential predictions, involves  hypothesis testing and, that unpleasant possibility, a host of different cognitive structures.  In order that this position not be considered as pure conjecture, consider a small portion of the results of the first  experiment, which will be presented later. Forty subjects were asked to predict the next two digits when given the sequence  "22111222," (no feedback was ever given). Then each subject was asked to explain the reason for his response. The variety of  verbal responses is of relevance; each of the following was given by two or more of the subjects, in different words than used  here, of course: (a) the initial sequence of length six starts over (at position number seven) and repeats; (b) runs of "1"  and "2" alternate, with the length of the runs increasing by one each time; (c) the pattern is length 10 and symmetric, with  the last half containing the complement digits of the first half; (d) the pattern starts in the middle and alternates between  a double alternation and a triple alternation; (e) the pattern starts in the middle and consists of alternating runs of length  3 (triple alternation); (f) the whole sequence starts over and repeats; (g) no reason. Moreover, seven other different pattern  structures were mentioned once each.  If human subjects perform in typical probability learning experiments by looking for pattern structures and testing hypotheses  compatible with their perceptions, one may suggest that the task is ill suited to the appropriate analysis of the behavior  measured. It is certainly difficult to specify a perceptual starting point in the sequence of hundreds of trials from which  the subject may generate his next hypothesis. Moreover, memory span and perceived structure may be closely related, again  making assumptions about the perceived beginning of a sequence difficult.  It is highly probable that the amount and kind of hypothesis testing which occurs  PREDICTIONS OF BINARY EVENTS 341  in a probability learning experiment change as a function of trials. Except for the use of learnable or partly learnable  sequences, the usual "random" ordering of events allows for no completely valid hypothesis. It is possible then that after  some moderate number of trials, subjects no longer search for and use the exact hypotheses to be presented in this paper.  However, it is hoped that the methods illustrated here may be adapted to situations in which different classes of hypotheses  are used by subjects.  The experimental situation to be treated is one in which no feedback is given. In the context of sequential predictions, this  means that subjects predict events but neither event ever occurs. Under the assumption that no systematic changes in  perceptual tendencies occur, this would be a stable-state condition. The results to be given may be considered as normative  measures of pattern perception of binary sequences. METHOD  Stimuli and General Procedures  Sequences of the digits 1 and 2 were used as stimuli, with each sequence consisting of eight digits. The 256 such sequences  were divided into two sets of 128, where each sequence in one set was matched by its complement sequence in the other set.  From one of these basic sets of 128 sequences, four sets of 20 sequences were selected by sampling without replacement. The  single and double alternation sequences were actually selected in advance and the remaining 78 sequences were selected by the  sampling procedure. The sequences used are given in Table 1. Each of the four sets was also used with the complements of each  sequence to control for any bias caused by the particular digits forming a sequence.  In each of the three experiments, any subject was presented with one of the sets of 20 sequences, in printed form on a single  page. The basic instructions were to predict the next two digits in the sequence. Subjects were college sophomores taking  introductory psychology. They were informed that more than one answer might be correct on a given sequence, that each sequence  was to be viewed as a new problem, unrelated to the other sequences, and that some of the sequences were easy and some  difficult. The instructions implied that a correct answer was always possible but also indicated that no feedback would be  given during the experimental session.  In Experiments 1 and 2, each set of 20 sequences was presented in 20 different orders, based on a 20 by 20 Latin square,  whereas only five different orderings of the sequences were used in Experiment 3.  Hypothesis Generation  Experiment 1 was conducted in order to generate a set of hypotheses related to the stimulus sequences. Stimulus set number 2  (sequences 21 through 40, in Table 1) was used. With the  344 LORDAHL  20 orders for the basic sets and their complements, there were 40 possible stimulus lists. One subject was given each of these  lists, subjects being run individually, and the basic instructions were modified so as to require each subject to give a  reason for his response before proceeding to the next sequence. The experimenter questioned the subject in order to reduce  ambiguities, and also asked on each trial how the sequence continued beyond the two digits predicted.  The verbal responses were classified and tabulated, with statements judged to be logically equivalent being pooled. The high  frequency reasons were selected for the hypothesis set used to test the model. However, any hypothesis not applicable to more  than one sequence used in Experiment 2 was discarded. In two cases, an hypothesis was formed from verbalizations which clearly  were not related except that they yielded identical response predictions. No hypothesis was selected which would yield more  than one prediction of the next two digits for a sequence of length eight. RESULTS  The Hypotheses  From the verbal reports of Experiment 1, 39 hypotheses were selected which met the criteria: (a) each hypothesis, when  applicable to a binary sequence of length eight, could logically be used to predict only one response (of length two); (b)  each hypothesis was applicable to at least two of the sequences used in Experiment 2; and (c) each hypothesis was reported by  at least two different subjects in Experiment 1 as being used (two exceptions were made, as described earlier).  The resulting 39 hypotheses are summarized in Table 2, in which the hypotheses have been classified into seven types.3 This  classification was somewhat difficult in that certain hypotheses in the set have at least two characteristics used in defining  other classes. For example, some hypotheses involve both the concepts of symmetry and complement sequences. Class VII was  formed to incorporate these complex hypotheses.  Class I hypotheses are those in which the sequence of eight events may be viewed as an initial pattern of events of a given  length which starts over at some point and is repeated. For instance, the sequence 21121211 may be perceived as a repeating  sequence of length five (21121) which starts over at the sixth digit.  It should be mentioned that some of the hypotheses in this class are divided into two specific factors for purposes of  preserving accuracy in the quantitative analysis. Initial repeating sequences of lengths five, six, and seven are separated  into two hypotheses each, depending upon whether or not the last and first digits in the pattern are the same. When the first  and last digits are the same, it was thought that there would be greater difficulty in perceiving this hypothesis than when  these two were different. In the former case, the perception of such a pattern would require a subject to "break" a run of at  least length two somewhere within the run. Class III includes hypotheses very similar to those in Class I, except that the pattern following the initial sequence is the  complement of the initial one. Again, for the most part, differences between the hypotheses within this class are based on the  length of the initial pattern. A case in which the initial sequence of length four is seen to alternate with its complement is  a separate hypothesis from that pertaining to an initial sequence of length five perceived to alternate with its complement.  Class IV hypotheses are those for which the sequence is perceived to be bilaterally symmetric. For instance, the sequence  12212121 may be thought to be a portion of the entire sequence 12212121221, which has the property of being symmetric about  the sixth digit.  Class V contains four hypotheses which are not so easy to describe or define as is the case with the preceding classes.  Included are special types of runs and alternation tendencies verbally described by subjects, and one hypothesis which is a  construction of the author and which is meant to summarize a number of diverse verbalizations which lead to the same predicted  responses on those sequences to which it is applicable. A good example from this class is what might be called a  superalternation hypothesis. In the sequence 11122212, a number of subjects report perceiving a single full cycle of triple  alternation (total length of six) followed by single alternations of sufficient number to equal the total length of the first  cycle. Clearly, a precise and inclusive definition for this type of tendency is quite difficult at the present state of  knowledge about pattern perception. For this reason, this hypothesis, and some like it, were defined very narrowly, with a  good deal of experimenter judgment being exercised. Validation of the Model  Experiment 3 was designed to be a more satisfactory test of the model than the goodness of fit to Experiment 2 data. However, since a large number of parameters were employed, a basic question must be entertained. Is a  model with so many parameters needed? Clearly, one's expectations of the degree of predictiveness in a "good" model will  influence the answer. The measures shown in Table 8 suggest that certain types of hypotheses are far more potent than others.  However, the a value for a given hypothesis cannot, alone, be used to judge the importance of that hypothesis in terms of  predictable variance of a model with and without that factor. The frequency of applicability must also be taken into account.  For example, the random (residual) component has an a of .042, far lower than the class total for alternating runs, but the  relative frequency of applicability of the random component results in an estimate of about .29 for the proportion of times  that responses were based on the random factor compared to a corresponding value of only .12 for alternating runs. Moreover,  the results presented here are, in a sense, normative, in that the two events have been sampled with equal probabilities for  the sequences. Were one to consider other experimental conditions, where one event is more frequent than the other, or in  which first order conditional probabilities are employed, then certain sequences become more likely and the relative  importance of certain classes of hypotheses is substantially increased.  It is argued here that a fairly large number of factors must be incorporated into any general model of the type illustrated.  One reason for this is that the hypotheses used here are often correlated in their predictions for the typical set of  sequences. As a result, estimates from a model with fewer parameters are likely to be inflated by measurement of real, but  omitted, factors correlated with those hypotheses retained in the reduced model. Similarly, if one attempts to reduce the  number of parameters by using classes of hypotheses as the variables of a model, resultant fits to certain individual  sequences will be in gross error. This is implied by the quite different effective weights for a number of hypotheses within  the same class. For instance, within Class I, the average effective weight for H4 and H~ , both related to an initial sequence  of length six which starts over, is about .31, while the corresponding average for H6 and H, , dealing with sequences of  length seven, is less than .07 (Table 3).  At a different level of evaluation the same argument for numerous variables in a model of choice behavior may be made. The  verbalizations of subjects, as a dependent variable, can be treated by certain modifications of the model with some hope of  reasonable fits. Indeed, such data provided the hypothesis set for the model and cannot easily be ignored in the selection of  hypotheses for a model of this type, unless one were willing to include hundreds of imaginable factors from an a priori  analysis of the stimulus sequences.  The general success of the model in the validation study, where predictions were  PREDICTIONS OF BINARY EVENTS 359  made on the basis of existing parameter estimates to new sequences tested with different subjects, should not be interpreted  as direct evidence that 30 to 40 different factors need be considered for good predictions. A number of individual hypotheses  could have been dropped without reducing the proportion of variance predicted by the model to any great degree. But dropping  any moderately large group of these hypotheses in making predictions would have reduced the sum of weights (D;'s) for some  sequences sufficiently to inflate the effective weights of the remaining factors. In addition, were such a group of H's  dropped prior to parameter estimation, predictions (and fits) from the reduced model might have been considerably worse.  One characteristic of the present approach which should be mentioned is that a synthesis of the data has been attempted more  than an analysis. It appears to be quite difficult to suggest a form of data analysis which would uncover any sizable number  of the hypotheses as defined here. Statistical analysis of general properties of the sequences may show some relationships to  the data, but specific sequences within some statistically defined class are likely to have response probabilities far  different from the general class averages. For example, in the data from Experiment 2, those sequences which contained six of  one event and two of the other resulted in an average of 34% of the responses being two of the more frequent event. However,  sequence 19, which meets this specification, had only 4 % of the subjects so responding. A number of other illustrations of  this type are possible, but let us treat this point as a passing comment.  The present results suggest that an analysis of probability learning may require a complex model of stimulus structure, as  perceived by subjects. Table 8 shows that Class II hypotheses, involving alternating runs, are a potent factor; however, it  should be noted that two of the hypotheses in this class, numbers 20 and 21, have to do with progressive increases and/or  decreases in run lengths. Moreover, the high weights for Classes I and VI, repeating initial patterns and patterning by pairs  of digits, indicate that subjects not only perceive and use "simple" structuring but, when possible, may redefine the basic  elements forming such structures, using groups of digits as building blocks.  Bower and Winzenz (1969) have provided strong evidence that the encoding of stimuli into groups of digits plays an important  role in the recall and recognition of information. Although they did not deal with binary sequences, it does not seem  completely inappropriate to view their results as suggesting similar effects in binary choice behavior. Specifically, one may  argue that the effectiveness of repeated stimulus sequences, such as runs of a given length, may depend completely upon  whether or not a subject has perceived the stimulus in terms of runs of events.  It seems that the present assessment technique could be used with subjects who have completed some fixed number of trials in a  probability learning task in order to study the amount and kind of hypothesis behavior following such procedures. A somewhat  better procedure than used here, for such a purpose, would be to present  360 LORDAHL  the sequence elements successively. A difficulty with this method is clear: the judged applicability of a given hypothesis to  a given sequence remains strictly valid only if the subjects can recall the sequence. Without parametric data on the retention  of different binary sequences, especially as related to different methods of encoding such sequences, additional changes in  procedure would be required. For example, subjects might be required to repeat the sequences prior to making predictions.  Certainly, a natural extension of the hypothesis model to a learning situation would be to a learnable-sequence task. With  slight extrapolation of Bjork's results (1968), one might expect an all-or-none hypothesis testing model to be a possibility  in such a task. The present methods provide a means for establishing an hypothesis set which would need to be used in such a  model.  Finally, the question of individual differences warrants comment. Although the results summarized in Table 9 show that only  small departures from the assumptions of independent response choices and equal sampling probabilities exist in the present  case, one can image situations wherein gross individual differences might obtain. For experimental purposes, one could  probably reduce individual differences by instructing subjects as to the set of possible hypotheses to be considered.  On the other hand, individual differences may be of interest for their own sake. In such cases, the sum of log response  likelihoods may serve as an index for identifying subgroups of subjects with different perceptual sets. Within such subgroups,  the present methods should be quite suitable for measurement of the hypotheses used. In this regard, it should be reported  that separate parameter estimates for men and women on the Experiment 2 data produced very similar results.  It is possible to find solutions of the form used here for single subjects, but this would involve enormous computer time. The  reason that sample solutions for single subjects are not presented here is that subjects have responded to only 20 sequences,  an insufficient number, as arranged, for estimating the full set of 40 a's.
