1. INTRODUCTION 

Let A and X be nonempty sets. They may be respectively interpreted as a set of subjects and a set of stimuli in an experiment or simply sets of objects. The real number system will be denoted by ( R , <) . A numerical characterization of a subset P of A×X was first obtained and discussed by Ducamp and Falmagne (1969) when the sets A and X are finite. Their characterization yields two functions, f: A Q R and g: X Q R, such that, for all (a, x) ¥ A×X , 

(a, x) ¥ P . f(a) < g(x), 

where the functions f and g define what is called a Guttman scale after Guttman (1944). 

A complete numerical characterization of this type for arbitrary A and X was later obtained by Doignon, Ducamp, and Falmagne (1984). They have shown that when A and X are countable, a necessary and sufficient condition for the representation is that P is a biorder , defined in Section 3, and P also has the following equivalent representation: there are two functions, f Œ : A Q R and g Œ : X Q R, such that, for all (a, x) ¥ A×X , 

(a, x) ¥ P . f Œ (a) [ g Œ (x).

In general, the equivalence fails to hold when A and X are arbitrary. Those two numerical characterizations of P will be called biordered numerical representations . 

The aim of the paper is to examine several necessary and sufficient conditions for real interval representability of P , which generalizes biordered numerical representations in the sense that each object in A and X is assigned a half-bounded real interval in place of a real number. Let J a and J ‘ be respectively the set of all half-bounded real intervals in R that are unbounded below and the set of all halfbounded real intervals in R that are unbounded above. Elements of J a consist of (− . , a) and (− . , a] for all a ¥ R, and elements of J ‘ consist of (a,+ . ) and [a,+ . ) for all a ¥ R. 

We shall consider two types of real interval representations. The first type, called exclusive interval representation , is concerned with the existence of two mappings, F from A into J a and F[ˆ] from X into J ‘ , such that, for all (a, x) ¥ A×X , 

(a, x) ¥ P . F(a) 5 F[ˆ ] (x)= ” . 

This says that objects in A and X are respectively assigned half-bounded intervals in J a and J ‘ , so that (a, x) belongs to P if and only if object a ’s interval and object x ’s interval have no intersection; that is, the former lies completely to the left of the latter on the real line. 

The second one, called inclusive interval representation , is concerned with the existence of two mappings, F from A into J a and G from X into J a , such that, for all (a, x) ¥ A×X , 

(a, x) ¥ P . F(a) … G(x), 

where … means proper inclusion. This says that objects in A and X are assigned real intervals that are bounded above and unbounded below, so that (a, x) belongs to P if and only if object x ’s interval properly includes object a ’s interval. A pair (J a , … ) is said to be a linearly ordered set , since the binary relation … on J a is a complete weak order. Note that the real number system ( R , < ) , which is also a linearly ordered set, is order-isomorphic to a proper subset of (J a , … ) . 

When A and X are identical, P is a usual binary relation on X and an irreflexive biorder P is called an interval order , where irreflexivity of P means that (x, x) ¨ P for all x ¥ X . Traditionally, real interval representability of an interval order has been concerned with assignment of bounded real intervals to objects in X . Fishburn (1970, 1973) asked under what conditions objects or stimuli under consideration can be mapped into the set of all bounded real intervals so that one object’s interval completely lies to the right of another object’s interval on the real line if and only if the first object is judged to possess more of some specified property than the second object. A necessary and sufficient condition for a countable set of objects was given by Fishburn (1970). When the set of intervals is restricted to the set of all closed real intervals, Fishburn (1973) and Doignon et al. (1984) obtained necessary and sufficient conditions for an arbitrary set of objects. Only sufficient conditions for general bounded real interval representations were provided by Fishburn (1973). On the other hand, Fishburn (1985, Chap. 2) and Doignon, Ducamp, and Falmagne

(1987) have shown that necessary and sufficient conditions are the same as ones for the countable case when bounded intervals in an arbitrary linearly ordered set are used as interval representations. 

One motivation to study bounded real interval representability may be that interval length has an interpretation such as the size of a sensory or instrumental threshold that prevents the detection of small differences or deviation in the relevant property from target or reference objects to objects under comparison. However, this interpretation is necessarily but not sufficiently applicable to the bounded real interval representations, since many numerical representational forms have been examined in which the size of a threshold may depend not only on target objects but also on objects under comparison (see, for example, Abbas & Vincke, 1993; Agoev & Aleskerov, 1993; Nakamura, 1990, 2000; Rodriguez-Palmero, 1997; and many others). 

Historically, real interval representability of P … X×X was first explored by Luce (1956) who introduced the concept of a semiorder, a special case of an interval order. Later developments of real interval representability of a semiordered set (X, P) include Scott and Suppes (1958) for the finite case, in which all intervals can be of equal length, Manders (1981) for the countable case, and Fishburn (1973) and Swistak (1980) for the arbitrary case. 

The paper is organized as follows. Section 2 considers two real interval representations (inclusive and exclusive) and their lexicographic counterparts. Then representational conditions are investigated to show how those two interval representations are related to each other. Numerical examples are also provided to clarify the discussion. In Section 3, we introduce a joint order–denseness condition and show the main theorems that give necessary and sufficient conditions for the existence of each of the inclusive and exclusive interval representations. Necessary and sufficient conditions for compatibility of those two interval representations are also presented. Section 4 considers real interval representations for an irreflexive biorder, that is, an interval order, in which all results are obtained as corollaries of the theorems in Section 2. Numerical examples are also presented. Necessary and sufficient conditions for irreflexive interval representability of interval orders and semiorders are investigated and discussed in Section 5. A necessary and sufficient condition for the general bounded real interval representation, which is unsolved so far, is shown as a corollary of one of the main theorems in Section 3. Section 6 provides proofs of the main theorems in Section 3.
