BENTLER  University of California, Los Angeles, California 90024  AND  SIK-YUM LEE  The Chinese University, Hong Kong  The differential calculus for scalars is used to develop theorems for a calculus of functions of matrices. No appeal to scalar  notation is necessary in the resulting calculus, so that the given chain and matrix product rules have wide applicability to  matrix theory and models. The chain and product rules of scalar calculus become a special case of the matrix rules. The  methods are illustrated by application to a generalized stochastic process model potentially relevant to long- and short-term  memory.  Although multivariable calculus has become a standard mathematical topic in recent years (e.g., Chorlton, 1976; Curtis, 1972),  the differential calculus of matrix functions is not yet a mature enough field to have developed general matrix calculus rules  that reduce to the standard pragmatic rules of scalar calculus when the matrices involved are of order one. It is by no means  an easy task to move from scalar calculus to matrix calculus. For example, the obvious matrix generalization of the scalar  chain rule dxldx = (dx/dy)(dyf dz), in which the scalars are replaced by matrices, can yield incorrect results.  Simple basic matrix calculus rules would not only be highly general, in that they can reduce to scalar calculus rules, but  they can be of immense pragmatic value in differentiating functions of complicated matrix products. For example, consider the  function of five matrices, trace[(A & B) C(D * E)]-1, to be partially differentiated with respect to each of the elements of  the various matrices. (A 0 B) is the Kronecker matrix product (ai;B) and (D * E) is the Hadamard product (di;ei;). Functions  such as these arise in various fields from biometrics to statistics, including mathematical psychology, and frequently  represent applications of optimization theory (e.g., Hestenes, 1975). Statistical estimation of the parameters of  these models has been hindered by an incomplete matrix calculus. While it is possible in principle to perform differentiation  involving such models using the currently available scalar calculus, only the truly dedicated person would undertake the task.  Standard texts on matrix theory (e.g., Gantmacher, 1959; Graybill, 1969) do not provide general methods for such problems, and  it is only recently that a matrix calculus powerful enough to handle a wide range of problems has been developed (e.g.,  McDonald & Swaminathan, 1973; Macrae, 1974). The existing calculus, however, cannot handle complex matrix problems such as  differentiation involving Kronecker products, and a basic theorem of the McDonald-Swaminathan calculus can yield incorrect  results. In this paper we develop several new theorems that can serve as a foundation for a general matrix calculus. The above theorems provide the basis for a differential calculus of  matrix functions. While a single matrix counterpart exists to the scalar chain rule, at least three matrix counterparts exist  to the product rule. A given applied problem, for example in nonlinear optimization, may require various of the above theorems  as well as a variety of results derivable from them.
