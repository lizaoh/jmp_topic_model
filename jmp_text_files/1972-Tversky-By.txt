JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 9, 341-367 (1972  Choice by Elimination  AMOS TVERSKY*  The Hebrew University of Jerusalem  Choice is analyzed as a probabilistic process of successive eliminations. Within this general framework, the present paper  characterizes a choice model interpretable as a process of elimination by aspects. It is shown that this model resolves the  major difficulties associated with the assumption of independence from irrelevant alternatives, that it generalizes the choice  models of Luce (1959) and Restle (1961), and that it is a random utility model. Testable consequences of this model are  derived and some of its ramifications are explored.  The observed inconsistency and the reported uncertainty associated with individual choice behavior have led to theoretical  developments which treat choice as a probabilistic process. Two classes of models, or representations, called random utility  models and constant utility models, have been investigated. Random utility models assume that the utility, or the value, of  each alternative undergoes random fluctuations, and that the alternative with the highest momentary value is selected.  Constant utility models, on the other hand, express choice as a probabilistic function of the (constant) scale value assigned  to each of the alternatives. The two types of representations differ with respect to the locus of the probabilistic element in  the choice process. Random utility representations attribute uncertainty to the determination of value, while constant utility  representations attribute uncertainty to the decision rule. The two types of representations, however, are not incompatible:  some (though not all) choice processes can be represented as either a random or a constant utility model. (For a systematic  review of probabilistic theories of preference, see Luce and Suppes, 1965.)  This paper investigates a third class of probabilistic models of choice, called elimination models, in which choice is viewed  as a sequential elimination process. To motivate the present development we discuss, in Section l, some major problems  associated with probabilistic preference models. Theorem 1 provides a necessary and sufficient condition for simple scalability which is formulated purely in terms of choice  probabilities and is, therefore, directly testable. It extends the previous results of Krantz (1964, Appendix A) and of  Tversky and Russo (1969).  Theoretical considerations and empirical studies show that simple scalability is not valid in general. The arguments against  simple scalability are reviewed elsewhere (Tversky, 1972). Here, we merely illustrate by examples some of the difficulties  encountered by simple scalability. Let T = {x, y, z}, and suppose that all binary choice probabilities equal 2. Assume further  that x and y are duplicates of each other (e.g., two copies of the same book) while z differs markedly from x and y. Under  CHOICE BY ELIMINATION 345  these conditions, one expects that P(z, T) = z while P(x, T) = P(y, T) = I because in the presence of z, the two duplicates, x  and y, are treated as one alternative. Data support this intuition. Order independence, on the other hand, implies that all  trinary choice probabilities equal a. This example, due to Debreu (1960), shows that two alternatives (y and z) which are  equivalent in one context [i.e., P(y; z) _ 2] are not equivalent in another context [i.e., P(y, T) :~L- P(z, T)], contrary to  simple scalability. Most work on random utility models has assumed that the random variables associated with  the alternatives are independent. Models of this type are called independent random utility models. As in the case of simple  scalability, the assumption of independence among the random variables which represent the alternatives is not valid, in  general. The above discussion shows that neither simple scalability nor the independent random utility model is acceptable in general,  and that in order to account for the above examples, a choice model must incorporate the structural dependence among the  alternatives. The rest of this section is devoted to interpretation and discussion of the representation formulated in Theorem 2. This  representation is interpreted as a process of elimination by aspects. Suppose that each alternative consists of a set of  aspects or components, and that each aspect possesses a weight. Suppose further that at each stage in the process one selects  an aspect (from those included in the available alternatives) with probability that is proportional to its weight. The  selection of an aspect eliminates all the alternatives that do not include the selected aspect and the process continues until  all alternatives, save one, are eliminated.  In contemplating a dinner at a restaurant, for example, the first aspect selected may be sea food: this eliminates all  restaurants that do not serve acceptable sea food. Given the remaining alternatives, another aspect-say a price level-is  selected, and all restaurants that exceed the selected price level are eliminated. The process continues until only one  restaurant that includes all the selected aspects remains. This model of elimination by aspects is similar in some respects to  the lexicographic and the conjunctive  350  TVERSKY  models. It differs from them in that, due to its probabilistic nature, the criteria for elimination (i.e., the selected  aspects) and the order in which they are applied vary from one occasion to another and are not determined in advance. From these data Coombs estimated, for various pairs  of shades, the probability that one shade is judged closer than the other to the S's ideal gray. The results show that pairs  of shades whose elements are either both darker or lighter than the S's ideal-called unilateral pairs-result in more extreme  choice probabilities than bilateral pairs where one element is darker and the other element is lighter than the S's ideal.  This finding confirms the random utility model formulated by Coombs, where the random variable associated with each of the  stimuli is the sum of two normally distributed independent random variables: one representing the S's ideal, and the second  representing the distance between the given stimulus and the S's ideal. Consequently, random fluctuations in the S's ideal  affect the bilateral comparisons but not the unilateral ones, which are expected, therefore, to yield more extreme choice  probabilities. Thus, the ARA model can be viewed as a generalization of the random utility model proposed by Coombs (1964).  The ARA model differs from the EBA model in that the aspects are represented by random variables rather than by constants, and  choice is described as a comparison  366 TVERSKY  of sums of random variables rather than as a sequential elimination process. Both models, however, attribute the dependence  among choice alternatives to the common aspects shared by them. Thus, both models account for dependence among alternatives  while preserving independence among aspects. As a consequence, the two models share the following testable property:  P(x, A) = 0 iff there exists y E A such that P(x; y) = 0.  In other words, P(x, A) > 0 if and only if P(x; y) > 0 for all y in A. This condition does not hold in the general random  utility model although it holds in the independent random utility model.  In the preceding investigation of choice models the data base was confined to choice probabilities. Furthermore, the  association of the relevant collections of aspects with the respective subsets of T permits us to interpret the EBA (or the  ARA) model in terms of aspects without making any assumptions about the specific alternatives under study. Nevertheless, in  the discussion of concrete examples some additional assumptions were introduced. For instance, we have made the "natural"  assumptions that all aspects of Paris are included in Paris*, and that the weight of the $1 bonus is small relative to that of  the entire trip. The introduction of such assumptions, based on prior analysis of the alternatives, serves to reduce the  number of parameters of the model and facilitates its empirical interpretation. These considerations suggest the following  problem: Suppose a set of alternatives satisfies the EBA (or the ARA) model. Suppose further that the alternatives in question  are ordered, with respect to their overall value, and that the ordering satisfies the additive conjoint measurement model (see  Krantz, Luce, Suppes, and Tversky, 1971) relative to some appropriate product structure. Under the additive model, the scale  value of an alternative is expressible as an additive function of the scale values of its components. What, then, is the  relation between the measurement of the components derived from the additive composition model based on judgments of value,  and the measurement of the aspects derived from the EBA model on the basis of choice probabilities? A theoretical analysis  that captures the structural relationships between the two classes of models would enhance their applicability and provide a  more comprehensive account of judgment and choice.
