Recently, models of perceptual classification which have been successful in describing choice probabilities, have been augmented with processing time assumptions that have allowed them to be equally successful in predicting response times in these tasks as well (e.g.,  Ashby, 2000 ;  Ashby & Maddox, 1994 ;  Maddox & Ashby, 1996 ;  Murdock, 1985 ;  Nosofsky & Palmeri, 1997 ). This has been an important advance in that historically, response time developments have proceeded largely independently of research on choice probability with only a few notable attempts at integration (e.g.,  Link & Heath, 1975 ;  Ratcliff, 1978 ).

The research in response time analysis of psychological processing has provided a plethora of technologies in answering such questions as what is the mental organization of processes underlying specific tasks (e.g.,  Schweickert, 1978 ;  Schweickert & Townsend, 1989 ;  Townsend, 1984 ;  Townsend & Schweickert, 1989 ), what are the capacity demands required by those tasks ( Townsend & Nozawa, 1995 ), is there any interaction between processes that are operating ( Dzhafarov, 1999 ;  Townsend & Thomas, 1994 ), etc. None of these addresses the issue of what is being processed and how the information representation might be relevant (for an exception see  Schweickert, 1985 ). On the other hand, the literature on perceptual classification has at its center issues of representation and similarity; resting on those concepts to provide the explanation for much of the empirical phenomena (for a review, see  Ashby, 1992 ;  Nosofsky, 1992 ). What is missing in many of the earlier attempts to simultaneously account for response times and accuracy is a sufficient model for the psychological representation of the stimulus set which could be used to derive similarity (although  Ratcliff, 1978 , could be adapted for this). Two classes of models that are currently being debated incorporate some of the best of both worlds. Signal detection models and their multidimensional generalizations with processing time assumptions ( Ashby, 2000 ;  Ashby & Maddox, 1994 ;  Ashby & Townsend, 1986 ;  Green & Swets, 1966 ;  Murdock, 1985 ;  Townsend & Wenger, 2004 ; for a different approach to modeling accuracy and response time in a single framework, see  Hu, 2001 ) and a random walk generalization of an exemplar model of classification ( Nosofsky & Palmeri, 1997 , see also  Lamberts, 1995 , for a related exemplar-based approach) unite a perceptual representation with processing time components that promise to fill the current gap between the two literatures.

In all of these models, however, there is an unexplored issue regarding their performance in an additive factors reponse-time (RT) paradigm. This is important because the added structure to accommodate perceptual representation and the reliance of decision processes on this representation presents intriguing consequences for the interpretation of results in previous applications of Sternberg's additive factors technique. In this paper, simple applications of these frameworks will be applied to a hypothetical two-choice reaction time experiment embedded in a factorial design originally proposed by  Sternberg (1969)  and extended by a number of investigators ( Cortese & Dzhafarov, 1996 ;  Dzhafarov & Cortese, 1996 ;  Dzhafarov & Schweickert, 1995 ;  Schweickert, 1978 ;  Schweickert & Townsend, 1989 ;  Townsend 1984 ;  Townsend & Schweickert, 1989 ). The goal will be to examine the joint consequences of model parameter adjustments and model architecture on the predicted (non) additivity of empirical factors.

The next section describes the basic additive-factors method and the types of problems to which it was originally applied. Following that, the signal detection models with response time assumptions and the exemplar-based random walk model are described. After this section, the theoretical predictions made by each model for factor influences and their interactions is provided. Finally, a brief review of the empirical research employing the additive-factors paradigm in the decade or so following its introduction reveals phenomena that may be reinterpreted in light of the present theoretical findings. In particular, the locus of the effect of stimulus probability generated a great deal of interest—especially the fact that probability often interacted with factors thought to influence early stages of perception. This paradox and other similar findings are consistent with predictions made by models of perception that include response time assumptions.

Originally, the additive-factors method of  Sternberg (1969, for a review, 1998 ;  Schweickert, 1978 ) was designed as a means of verifying the existence of sequentially arranged processing stages. It was thought that experimental factors, such as stimulus contrast, could be found that would selectively influence individual processes in the chain. If so, when varied factorially, their effects on processing time would be additive. To illustrate the concept and define some essential terms, assume the most basic 2×2 factorial design. Since the first appearance of the additive-factors technology, significant developments of the theory and scope of its application have appeared ( Dzhafarov, 1999, 2001 ;  Dzhafarov & Schweickert, 1995 ;  Schweickert, 1978 ;  Schweickert & Townsend, 1989 ;  Townsend, 1984 ;  Townsend & Nozawa, 1995 ;  Townsend & Schweickert, 1989 ). Much of this effort has gone toward using observed interactions to identify alternative architectures (e.g., parallel, graphical networks, etc.) rather than to signify a failure in selective influence (for an exception,  Townsend & Thomas, 1994 ). These efforts have also extended the analysis to other distributional statistics beyond the mean RTs, such as cumulative distribution functions and survivor functions ( Schweickert, Giorgini, & Dzhafarov, 2000 ;  Townsend & Nozawa, 1995 ;  Townsend & Wenger, 2004 ), and log accuracy ( Schweickert, 1992 ).

The present work puts forth a reconceptualization of the issue of factorial effects by placing them in the context of processing models of perceptual identification and classification. When reasonable assumptions regarding how experimental factors influence parameters of a processing model are made, together with plausible assumptions regarding the optimality of decision processes, predictions regarding if and how factors interact in a typical additive-factors experiment can be offered. This work demonstrates that obtained interactions can result naturally from the inherent reliance of decision time processing on the perceptual representation of the stimulus, a possibility not explicitly considered in the original formulation of the additive-factors logic. As will be seen in a later section, the consideration of a detailed perceptual representation as driving processing time will provide an alternative account of the source of paradoxical observed factorial interactions in the literature.

                      d . It is common to collect other component times (such as motor execution) in a residual random variable  T 
                      r  which is assumed to be stochastically independent of the critical decision time variable and unaffected by the factors that are explored here (e.g.,  Ratcliff & Smith, 2004 ). The overall RT is then the sum of  T 
                      d  and  T 
                      r . Hence, this section develops theoretical predictions made by the different models for the expected decision time,  E ( T 
                      d ), of a classification or identification response. Second-order difference predictions for RT are equivalent to those for  E ( T

The model described in  Palmer, Huk, and Shadlen (2005)  is essentially equivalent to this one but uses a simpler decision process.

This model was proposed to account for choice probability and response time in a classification task in which multiple stimuli were mapped onto one of two categories ( Nosofsky & Palmeri, 1997 ). It is adapted to the two-stimulus, two-response situation by assuming a probabilistic representation for each stimulus ( Ashby & Lee, 1993 ), specifically, a univariate normal in a manner inspired by Ennis’ (e.g.,  Ennis, Palen, & Mullen, 1988 ) adaptation of the deterministic multidimensional scaling—choice models of identification. Hence, on a trial, the perceived stimulus is a sample drawn from a distribution. In this case, each percept is treated as an “exemplar” in a categorization task. Evidence for either a  R

In order to derive interaction contrast predictions, one must make reasonable assumptions regarding how empirical factors influence model behavior. We will look at three categories of empirical factors, those that affect stimulus quality (such as luminance contrast or masking), those that affect stimulus dissimilarity, and those that affect stimulus probability. These factors were chosen as they are ubiquitous in the empirical literature using the additive-factors method and they are perhaps among the easiest to manipulate in typical experiments. Some general facts regarding the main (or first-order) effects of these factors on observed RT are known. A high-quality stimulus will be responded to more quickly than a low-quality stimulus. When stimulus probability is manipulated, the higher probability stimulus is responded to more quickly (e.g.,  Miller & Pachella, 1973 ). In general, the greater the degree of dissimilarity, the easier the discrimination is, and hence, the more quickly it should be made (e.g.,  Shwartz, Pomerantz, & Egeth, 1977 ).

These empirical effects are manifest in the three modeling frameworks as influences on model parameters. In all three modeling frameworks, the stimulus is modeled as a probability distribution of perceptual effects (see above model descriptions), which is assumed to be univariate normal. The decision criteria can be found numerically, for different diffusion parameter values, by maximizing  (9)  and solving for  A  and  B . In the analyses below, this method of determining the decision criteria was used for all cases considered, even when probability was not explicitly manipulated. One of the interesting side consequences of using optimality to determine  A  and  B  was the observation that changes in dissimilarity and quality (manifested by changing stimulus means and variances) affected the criteria especially for high costs of waiting. Specifically, when discriminability improved, whether achieved by an increase in the difference between the perceptual means or a decrease in the perceptual variance, the optimal decision criteria moved outward.

Given the finite sampling nature of the EBRW it was impossible to implement Edward's analysis for this model. That is, the step probabilities and times are all functions of the contents of long-term memory which is a finite list of experienced exemplars (see Eqs.  (5)–(7) ). Hence, an alternative strategy of implementing probability effects was necessary.  Schweickert (1992)  suggested that, in a random walk, the probability of a stimulus should affect ( B /( A + B )) but not ∣ A + B ∣. Specifically, if  S 
                         1  were more likely, both criteria would shift in response to this unbalance so that  A  is closer to 0 than before and  B  is farther away. In addition, deadline pressure (which might be thought to impact a cost of waiting,  U ), should influence ∣ A + B ∣ but not ( B /( A + B )). We will see that the probability conjecture holds but the cost of waiting influences both ∣ A + B ∣ and ( B /( A + B )) for the stochastic SDT analysis.

The primary agenda of this study was to discover the consequences for factorial interactions of incorporating models of perceptual decision into the response time framework. That is, when reasonable assumptions are made regarding how empirical factors are manifest in model parameters and performance, what are the implications for factorial additivity? As can be seen in  Table 1 , on several occasions nonzero interactions of factors are predicted. The mostly additive with some slight underadditive effects of quality and dissimilarity are also consistent with at least one of the models, the stochastic SDT. Models that predict a strong underadditivity for these factors (SDT-RT distance and, perhaps, the EBRW) would be ruled out by the observations in  Table 2 . One important property of  Table 2  is the observance that quality and probability were found to interact yielding overadditivity. A long dialog ensued regarding the interpretation of this interaction with the argument framed in terms of indicating whether probability may or may not affect encoding (e.g.,  DeJong & Sanders, 1986 ;  Miller & Pachella, 1973 ;  Pachella & Miller, 1976 ;  Sanders, 1970 ;  Stanovich & Pachella, 1977 ). The debate was abandoned, it seems, perhaps to due lack of clear resolution. The present results provide at least one answer in that when a full consideration of the decision process is given, the interaction of these two factors results naturally. Interestingly, the interpretation given by  Pachella and Miller (1976)  of their results is quite compatible with the present modeling analysis. In that work, they concluded that stimulus probability has its effect “during the process that derives the name of the stimulus from the visual representation” ( Pachella & Miller, 1976, p. 29 ); that is, from a decisional process that takes the perceptual input and identifies it. 
                         4 
                      
                      
                         4 
                         
                            Sanders (1990)  argued that the additive-factors method entails the assumption of constant stage output. Specifically, there should be no variability in perceptual output. This requirement runs counter to much of what is observed (e.g.,  Ashby & Lee, 1993 ) and is rejected in formal models of perceptual classification such as those studied here which have been quite successful in describing substantial amounts of data ( Ratcliff & Smith, 2004 ).

An astute reader may notice the absence of data regarding the combination of dissimilarity and probability in  Table 2 . Though a study may exist somewhere in the vast additive-factors literature, this author could not find it. Ongoing work in my laboratory suggests that the issue of these two factors is complex. It appears that the empirical form of the interaction may depend on how probability is manipulated within the experiment ( Thomas & Sholl, 2004 ,  Thomas, 2005, in preparation ).

Townsend and Thomas (1994)  considered the impact of a stochastic dependency between serially arranged stages on the presence or absence of an interaction. One phenomenon that was discussed was termed a  single factor reversal . A single factor reversal occurs when a factor that, when increased, though believed to reduce the processing time of its associated stage, nevertheless, increases overall RT. In that work, such a finding was taken as support for the existence of a negative dependence between a subsequent stage and the stage directly influenced by the factor producing the reversal. The present work provides another example of how such a reversal might occur. Consider the factor of stimulus quality. Its influence on processing was implemented as a change in the perceptual variance. That is, when quality of a stimulus is improved, the perceptual variance would be reduced.  Fig. 11 
                          plots the expected decision time as  d ′ is increased where this increase in discriminability is the result of a reduction in perceptual variance. Oddly, within the SDT–RT-distance framework, such a decrease in perceptual variability results in a slowing of decision time processing. This is because, with high variability, some percepts are very far from the decision bound leading the overall expectation to be reduced. Alternatively, when variance is lower, more percepts are closer to the decision bound resulting in slower decision times.

Fig. 12 
                          shows similar graphs for the stochastic SDT. Here the expected decision time is plotted as a function of perceptual variance for different values of the cost of waiting. When the cost of waiting is relatively low, decision time increases with perceptual variance, a result more in line with our intuition. However, as can be seen in the figure, if the cost of waiting is high, increases in variability can lead to a reduction in the expected decision time. If one starts with a high variance and reduces it, when the cost of waiting is high, a paradoxical increase in decision time can result. Both the findings in  Figs. 11 and 12  provide the opportunity for the observance of a single factor reversal at the hands of stimulus quality, if the nonselective effects on decision outweigh any other factor's enhancement of processing speed. Within the parameters investigated, no such paradoxical predictions of variance effects on decision were obtained for the EBRW (see  Fig. 13 
                         ).

Finally, en route to the interaction constrast results from the stochastic SDT, a test of the conjectures in  Schweickert (1992)  was possible. Recall that Schweickert suggested that for random walks, the probability of a stimulus should affect ( B /( A + B )) but not ∣ A + B ∣. In addition, deadline pressure (which might be thought to impact a cost of waiting,  U ), should influence ∣ A + B ∣ but not ( B /( A + B )).  Table 3 
                          presents the results of the analysis determining the decision criteria given changes in the cost of waiting and stimulus probability where it was assumed that the observer is maximizing the expected value of a trial (Eq.  (9) ).

As can be seen in the last two columns of the table, the first assertion regarding the effect of changing stimulus probability has been confirmed by this analysis. When stimulus probability changes ( P ( S 
                         1 ) is the probability of stimulus  S 
                         1 ), only the ratio of  B  to  A + B  changes but not ∣ A + B ∣. However, the second conjecture was not supported. When the cost of waiting changes (which presumably would be the result of changing deadline pressures), both the ratio of  B  to  A + B  and ∣ A + B ∣ changes, the latter changing as intuition would suggest—the greater the cost of waiting, the tighter in the decision criteria. The impact on ( B /( A + B )) of changes in  U  suggests that, as the cost of waiting increases, the least probable stimulus is even less desirable, all other things being equal.

The present work underscores the necessity of considering the perceptual representation and the decisional processes that operate on it before drawing conclusions regarding the consequences of factorial interactions obtained in the traditional additive factors paradigm. Future work will extend the present analyses of perceptual classification models into other response time statistics such as distributional (or survivor) contrasts to ascertain whether some model identifiability might result. It was hinted earlier that even in the present case, the failure to observe underadditivity for the factor pair quality and dissimilarity may rule out the SDT–RT distance model (and perhaps, the EBRW) as both of these evidence large underadditive predictions throughout wide ranges of their parameter values. Additionally, empirical work is in progress to examine the remaining unstudied combination of dissimilarity and probability with some intriguing preliminary findings having been obtained ( Thomas & Sholl, 2004 ). A different tack but worthy of pursuit is to examine the issue of  parameter selective influence  and what might be obtained from taking this perspective. Parameter selective influence refers to the property whereby an empirical factor's effect on processing is selective to a single parameter or set of separable parameters within a model. 
                         5

                       Functions of the data that the model is designed to predict may be “linearizable” in the model parameters. If so, one may be able to use factorial logic, combined with the assumption of parameter selective influence, to confirm or refute a model's basic structure. This line of reasoning will be further developed in future work.
