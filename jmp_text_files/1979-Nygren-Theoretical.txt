NYGFEN  Department of Psychology, Ohio State University, Columbus, Ohio 43210  Implicit within the acceptance of most multidimensional scaling models as accurate  representations of an individual cognitive structure for a set of complex stimuli, is the  acceptance of the more 5 eneral Additive Difference Model (ADM). A theoretical frame-  work for testing the ordinal properties of the ADM for dissimilarities data is presented and  is illustrated for a set of three-outcome gambles. Paired comparison dissimilarity judgments  were obtained for two sets of gambles. Judgments from one set were first analyzed using  the ALSCAL individual differences scaling model. Based on four highly interpretable  dimensions derived from this analysis, a predicted set of dimensions were obtained for  each subject for the second set of gambles. The ordinal properties of the ADM necessary  for interdimensional additivity and intradimensional subtractivity were then tested for  each subject second set of data via a new computer-based algorithm, ADDIMOD. The  tests indicated that the ADM was rejected. Although violations of the axioms were  significantly less than what would be expected by chance, for only one subject was the  model clearly supported. It is argued that while multidimensional scaling models may be  useful as data reduction techniques, they do not reflect the perceptual processes used by  individuals to form judgments of similarity. Implications for further study of multi-  dimensional scaling models are offered and discussed.  Recent developments in multidimensional scaling models (Carroll, 1972; Carroll &  Chang, 1970; Takane, Young, & de Leeuw, 1977; Young, Takane, & Lewyckyj, 1978) have  prompted their increased usage in the study of individual differencesin perceived similarities  and differences among sets of psychologically interesting stimuli. These models have  provided a means of attempting to discover the subjective dimensions used by individuals  in their evaluations of complex stimuli in such areas as face perception (Milord, 1978),  semantic distance and memory (Rips, Shoben, & Smith, 1973), perception of political  candidates (Nygren & Jones, 1977; Sherman & Ross, 1972), perception of geometric  figures (Krantz & Tversky, 1975), and gambling behavior (Nygren, 1977), the sub-  stantive topic of this paper. In these studies the power of multidimensional scaling models  as data reduction techniques has been clearly demonstrated. Generally, however, applica-  tions of these scaling models have been restricted to interpretations of derived psycholog-  ical dimensions and individual differences estimates (e.g., the subject weights in the  Carroll and Chang (1970) INDSCAL model). Except for the work of Krantz and Tversky  (1975) there has been no major attempt in the substantive areas mentioned above to assess  53  0022-2496/79/040053-2SO2.00/0  Copyright 0 1979 by Academic Press, Inc. The validity of the representations derived from these multidimensional scaling models  rests on a set of necessary and sufficient conditions that suggest the means by which the  judgments of perceived dissimilarity are formed (Beals, Krantz, & Tversky, 1968). These  conditions form the basis for a psychological processing model referred to by Tversky  (1969) as the Additive Difference Model (ADM). G iven that a collection of stimuli are  perceived as varying along a set of relevant psychological dimensions, it is proposed that  these dimensions form the underlying basis for a multidimensional cognitive structure  that the individual decision maker imposes on the stimuli. The model asserts that two  alternatives are judged first by a perceptual process involving intradimensional subtractive  comparisons and then by an additive evaluative process across all relevant dimensions.  The result is the individual response judgment.  On intuitive as well as theoretical grounds the ADM seems appealing as a potential  descriptive model for judgments of perceived dissimilarity aqng alternatives that can be  considered to be multidimensional in nature. Historically, the risky decision making  literature and the gambling literature in particular have been inundated with various forms  of expectation models beginning with the axiomatization of von Neumann and  Morganstern (1944). Recent experimental evidence, however, has cast doubt on the  validity of these models as descriptions of gambling behavior and has led to the alternative  characterization of risky decision alternatives as multidimensional stimuli, where the  relevant dimensions are based on attributes of the gambles such as probabilities and  payoffs, (Nygren, 1977; Payne, 1973, 1975; Slavic & Lichenstein, 1968). If gambles are,  in fact, perceived as multidimensional stimuli, then it would seem that the ADM is a  viable cognitive processing model for judgments of dissimilarity deserving of investigation.  The focus of this paper, then, is twofold, having both a methodological and sub-  stantive base. Since most of the currently popular multidimensional scaling models for  similarity judgments implicitly assume the validity of the ADM, an error theory associated  with the crucial axioms of the ADM was devloped. A random response model for dissim-  ilarity judgments was proposed in order to obtain for each axiom of the ADM the  probabilities of satisfying and violating these axioms by chance. Second, two sets of 16  gambles were constructed. For the first set, the traditional multidimensional scaling  approach was taken to determine if gambles are perceived as multidimensional stimuli,  and, if so, what relevant objective dimensions of the gambles the judgments of dissimi-  larity are based on. The scaling of the stimuli was not, then, an attempt to measure the  psychological transformations imposed on these objective dimensions, but rather to  hypothesize a set of relevant objective dimensions for the second set of gambles for each  individual subject. If the derived individual differences scaling solution obtained from a  multidimensional scaling model such as ALSCAL is representative of the cognitive  structure imposed on the gambles by an individual, then the dissimilarity judgments for  the second set of gambles should satisfy the axioms of the ADM when based on the  predicted dimensions obtained from the Set I gambles. ADDIMOD, AXMTEST, and the ADM Axiom System  Krantz and Tversky (1975) in a study of perception of rectangles proposed a method  for evaluating the presence of systematic violations of the ADM axioms in a set of  similarity judgments at the individual subject level. They focused their attention on  simple equality and ordering predictions and on tests of monotonic&y and elative  straightnessimplied by the ADM. The present paper takes a somewhat different  approach to testing the ADM. This approach is based on the development of an error  theory for the ADM axioms and a computer programming algorithm, ADDIMOD  (Nygren, 1978), which searches for potential tests of four ADM axioms, Axioms 3-6 in  the Beals et al. (1968) presentation, for a given set of stimuli. ADDIMOD checks each  individual subject dissimilarities data for violations of each test. These probabilities were derived via an independent computer  algorithm, AXMTEST, developed by the author.  In a theoretical sense one violation of an axiom constitutes evidence for rejection of that  axiom. In practice, however, one would expect some errors within an individual data  matrix even if the underlying ADM is valid. The development of an error theory associated  with Axioms 3-6 is particularly difficult since each dissimilarity judgment is involved  in a number of tests of each axiom. This implies that some subsets of tests may not be  independent. However, for any one particular test of an axiom, the chance probability  associated with each of the possible ordinal relationships for a given axiom can be readily  computed. Since all tests may not be independent, these values only approximate the  expected proportions of errors and successes in random data. As will be shown, however,  the probability values will be very close approximations since the ratio of the number of  independent tests to dependent tests is very large, even for small stimulus designs.  In the general theory it will be assumed that paired comparison dissimilarity judgments  are either directly obtained or are derived for each individual for all pairs of n stimuli. APPLICATION OF THE ADM TO PERCEIVED SIMILARITY AMONG GAMBLES  Recent reviews of the decision making literature (Payne, 1973; Rapoport & Wallsten,  1972) reflect an increased interest in alternatives to expectation models in the study of?66 THOMAS E. NYGREN  risky decision making behavior. One of the major alternative approaches to decision  making under risk was first proposed by Slavic and Lichtenstein (1968). They suggested  that rather than viewing gambles in terms of their underlying distributions, it might prove  more beneficial to characterize them as multidimensional stimuli with the outcomes and  probabilities as the relevant psychological variables. Slavic and Lichtenstein proposed a  specific set of four characteristics of duplex gambles as the dimensions along which  judgments are made. These dimensions consisted of the probabilities of winning (pW)  and losing ( pL), and the amounts to be won ( $W) and lost ( $L).  In the study to be described below, two sets of 16 three-outcome gambles varying in  Pw,P,, Kv, and $L were constructed. Subjects were asked to make dissimilarity judg-  ments for all pairs of gambles. One set of the subjectdata was used as input to ALSCAL  in an attempt to hypothesize the objective dimensions used by subjects in making their  judgments. ALSCAL, like the more widely used individual differences model, INDSCAL  (Carroll & Chang, 1970), derives a Group Stimulus Space which includes as component  axes the aggregate of the dimensions used by all subjects. Like INDSCAL, the derived  ALSCAL dimensions cannot be rotated since the model assumes that each individual  applies unique saliences or weights to the dimensions-reflecting his or her unique  perceptions of similarities and differences among the stimuli. Both INDSCAL and  ALSCAL are special cases of the ADM in that they employ the familiar Euclidean metric.  ALSCAL differs from INDSCAL in one very important respect. It need not assume that  the subjectsdata are interval scaled; ordinal or nominal measurement is sufficient. In  addition, the analysis can be made ubject conditional,allowing a unique monotonic  transformation to be applied to each subject data.  If ALSCAL or any other multidimensional scaling model is to be used as a representa-  tion of a cognitive processing model describing how and on what basis individuals  actually perceive and evaluate multidimensional stimuli, then as a minimal test of their  validity the properties inherent in the ADM should be examined. This was the intent of  the present study. On the basis of the derived ALSCAL stimulus space for one set of  gambles and the derived weights for each subject on the dimensions, a predicted set of  relevant objective dimensions for the second set of gambles were obtained for each  subject. If the ALSCAL estimates provide a valid representation of an individual  cognitive structure, then the ADM axioms should be satisfied by each subject dissimi-  larities ratings for the second set of stimuli. The stimuli used in the study provide an  excellent means of testing the axioms for each subject, since a set of gambles could be  explicitly formed to vary systematically along the hypothesized dimensions of p, , p, ,  $ W? and $L to allow for adequate tests. NYGREN  DISCUSSION  A theoretical framework was developed for assessing the validity of the Additive  Difference Model as a cognitive processing model for dissimilarity judgments. The ADM  is of central importance in the scaling literature in that it is implicitly assumed by most  of the currently popular multidimensional scaling models. It has been argued in the  multidimensional scaling literature (cf. Carroll, 1972) that individual differences models  employing weighted Euclidean distances (i.e., ALSCAL or INDSCAL) result in a  spatial representation of the stimuli that defines a unique dimensional orientation in a  goodness-of-fit sense. It is further argued that these dimensions should be the psycholo-  gically meaningful dimensions used by the subjects. The interpretability of the derived  dimensions for the gambles used in this study and the correspondence between derived  subject weights and subject protocols suggest that the ALSCAL representation does,  at least at a global level, reflect individual differences in judgments of dissimilarity among  the gambles. As a data reduction technique, the ALSCAL model has an advantage over  traditional scaling models. In addition to its intuitively appealing means of representing  the data, the model individual differences estimates coupled with preferential choice  data may prove useful in generating hypotheses about gambling behavior.  Yet, there is a major disadvantage of using the scaling models like ALSCAL as data  reduction techniques that the present study clearly demonstrates. At best one is only  indirectly assessing the validity of the underlying ADM as a psychological processing  model. There is a seemingly perplexing incongruity between the traditional subjective  indices of goodness-of-fit of the scaling model and the violations of the ADM axioms in  the present study. It is, of course, difficult to directly compare violations of the axioms  with goodness-of-fit measures, since the magnitude of the violations should be considered  in addition to the actual frequencies. Nevertheless, without knowledge of the tests of the  ADM axioms, the traditional indices of (a) interpretability of dimensions, (b) very high  hivalues, (c) few violations of the triangular inequality, and (d) correspondence  between subject protocols and the weight estimates, of themselves, would lead to the  conclusion that the ALSCAL model and implicitly the ADM are being fit very well.  The results from the tests of the ADM axioms at the individual subject level, however,  indicate that none of these indices may be adequate for assessing the validity of the ADM  as a processing model.  With respect to the interpretability of the derived dimensions for the Set I gambles,  there is a strong correspondence between these dimensions and the objective dimensions  of the gambles cited earlier. It appears that although these dimensions may reflect the  the objective characteristics of the gambles actually used to form the dissimilarity  judgments, they were not utilized in the way proposed by the ADM. For example, one  puzzling aspect of most of the subjectsweight estimates from the ALSCAL analysis is  that all three probability dimensions are weighted highly. With each gamble being  represented by three probabilities there should only be two degrees of freedom associated  with this information, and thus only two probability dimensions being used by a given  subject. One possible explanation for this discrepancy is that the weight estimates are  simply not indicative of the subjectsjudgmental processes. A more interesting explanation?ADDITIVE DIFFERENCE MODEL 15  in light of the subjectsprotocols, however, is that subjects may use all three dimensions for  evaluating differences in some pairs of gambles in a lexicographic fashion. In particular,  the Odds, and the p,/p, dimensions may have been used in this manner. When, for  a pair of gambles p, = p, , one of these dimensions is irrelevant. When p, # p, , a  subject may first approximate the perceived difference between the gambles using the  simple Odds dimension and then make a further distinction on the basis of the pw/pL  dimension. Such a change in decision rules, when applied, could lead to violations of the  ADM axioms. Since the same rule would not be applied to all pairs, the ALSCAL  procedure could still successfully extract both dimensions, although not without some  deviations-as suggested by the projections of the stimuli on the Odds dimension in  Fig. 2.  The question still remains as to how the goodness-of-fit measure (i.e., the phi index)  can be so high for individual subjects if the ADM axioms are being violated. The answer  appears to lie in the examination of the triangular inequality axiom. As was shown  earlier, even for random data generated from Eq. (6), the expected proportion of violations  in a subject data set for this axiom is less than 0.10. It is reasonable, then, to expect that  when this data is analyzed via a model such as ALSCAL that the phi index would be very  high, even if the resultant dimensions were uninterpretable. As a means of assessing this  hypothesis, data generated via Eq. (6) for 50 random subjects were input to the ALSCAL  program. Using the program FIX procedure, the derived configuration of Figs. 1 and 2  were also input and kept constant in order to see how well the random subjectsdata could  be fit to this configuration. As expected, the derived weights were approximately equal in  value across all four dimensions for each random subject. However, the phi values were  only slightly less than those obtained for the 16 real subjects. These values ranged from  0.918 to 0.928 with a mean of 0.920. Hence, it is possible that the ALSCAL procedure may  indicate that the data are being fit very well by the model even if the ADM axioms are  substantially violated.  CONCLUSION  In testing the ADM axioms for the gambles employed in this study, a procedure was  developed whereby observed data could be compared with data generated from a random  response model. It can be argued that the random response probabilities of errors lead to  rather weak null hypotheses for testing the fit of the data to the axioms. It seems clear that  subjects, though they may not be using anADM processing model, are also not responding  randomly. Hence, one would expect fewer than the predicted chance number of violations  of the axioms as long as subjects were evaluating the stimuli in some dimensionally-  organized fashion. Nevertheless, it is argued that the error theory proposed here can prove  to be quite useful in evaluating the ADM properties for several reasons. First, the  random error proportions can be used to evaluate the fit of the scaling model in a more  objective manner than can the more traditional criteria like the phi index. Secondly, since  each axiom can be tested separately, the procedure outlined here affords a means of  examining interdimensional additivity and intradimensional subtractivity independently.?76 THOMAS E. NYGREN  Finally, it is possible via this procedure to examine empirically under what circumstances  all or some of the axioms are supported or violated. Further development and investigation  of the ADM along these lines may prove to be as enlightening to the understanding of  perception of similarity among complex stimuli as has been the computer-based procedures  for extracting the psychological dimensions themselves.
