INTRODUCTION  In many discrete-time control or decision-making processes in such diverse fields as engineering, communication, economics,  and psychology, either physical control devices or people are called upon to execute sequences of interdependent decisions.  These decisions are made under various states of uncertainty regarding underlying physical and environmental conditions. The  conditions may range from detailed knowledge of the parameters of the process, when, for example, the probability distribution  over all possible outcomes is completely specified or assumed known, to complete ignorance of the process parameters.  Multistage decision-making processes of this kind involve most or all of the following characteristics: (1) decisions are made  sequentially in time, one on each stage of the process; (2) the task specifications or, more generally, the environmental  conditions may change from one stage to another; (3) changes in the environment may occur either probabilistically or  deterministically, and either independently of or as a function of previous decisions; (4) decisions are subject to certain  physical or psychological constraints, for example constraints on the decision maker's (DM) information processing; and (5)  since information available for later decisions may be contingent upon the outcomes of earlier decisions, implications of any  decision may reach far into the future (Rapoport, 1975).  169  Copyright Q 1977 by Academic Press, Inc. Experiments on multistage decision behavior designed to investigate how people plan, organize, and manifest segments of their  behavior as sequences of interrelated decisions over time have been briefly reviewed by Rapoport and Wallsten (1972) and more  recently and extensively by Rapoport (1975). A variety of research paradigms have been employed in these experiments, ranging  from an attractive one-person game designed by Toda (1962) to explore the logic underlying planned survival behavior to more  specific and task-oriented experiments conducted by Rapoport and his associates (e.g., Rapoport, 1967, 1968; Rapoport &  Calder, 1972; Rapoport & Tversky, 1970), who have sacrificed the imaginativeness and generality of Toda's approach in favor of  tractability of solutions and ease of implementation. The most versatile among the latter paradigms has been the multistage  betting game (MBG) designed by Rapoport and Jones (1970), and later employed by Rapoport, Jones, and Kahan (1970), Rapoport  (1970), Gordon (1970), Levinsohn (1973), and Kleiter and Wimmer (1974).  MBG allows the experimental manipulation of several variables of interest. These include most of the primary variables  controlled in static decision-making experiments, such as the number of betting alternatives and their associated  probabilities, the DM's informational pattern, the rate of return on the DM's bets, and the duration of the process. Equally  important, the MBG possesses a simple structure, which is exploited below for deriving alternative tractable models.  In modeling multistage betting behavior, an important and difficult problem is the specification of what criterion (or  criteria) the DM satisfies or attempts to satisfy in determining the composition of his bets on each stage. Among others,  these criteria include maximization of expected utility, minimization of risk or minimization of playing time. Additionally,  the DM may attempt to maximize the probability of attaining a prespecified fortune (Ross, 1974), or he may specify a desired  increase in wealth and then determine the betting policy that minimizes the number of plays required to achieve this increase  (Epstein, 1967). Yet another criterion is to specify the number of plays in the game, N, and then maximize the fortune after N  plays. Individual differences in choosing among objective criteria are not unlikely, and the possibility of within-game  alternation among various criteria by the same DM due to learning or changes in capital also should not be discounted.  Moreover, because of the notorious insensitivity of optimization models to deviations therefrom, even payment with real money,  that is contingent upon the DM's performance, cannot guarantee that DM's will adhere to the objective criterion specified by  the experimenter.  It seems warranted, therefore, to assume alternative objective criteria for the same decision task. Given this assumption, one  may derive the decision policies optimizing these criteria, test the DM's observed decisions against each of the resulting  models, and reject or modify one or more of the models. The choice of objective decision criteria should be limited to those  criteria that have been supported in previous studies or that have a psychologically reasonable justification. This approach  to modeling betting behavior is adopted in the present study.  MULTISTAGE BETTING 171  THE MBG PARADIGM  MBG is a discrete-time multistage decision task. Several experiments have been conducted employing the MBG paradigm, in which the values of pi , ri , m, and N have been  systematically varied. The results of these experiments have weakly supported the probability-matching prediction of the  logarithmic utility model as a first order and crude approximation to betting proportions averaged over plays and subjects  (Gordon, 1970; Levinsohn, 1973; Rapoport & Jones, 1970). Experimental evidence also pointed to systematic individual  differences (Rapoport, 1970), and, in violation of any proportional Markov betting policy, to systematic effects of capital  size on yi,lxn for both group and individual data (Gordon, 1970; Rapoport, & Jones, 1970; Rapoport, et al., 1970) as well as  negative recency effects (gambler's fallacy) in a few cases.  Most of the experiments employing the MBG paradigm, have concentrated on the analysis of group rather than individual data.  Also, since all of these experiments had been conducted before betting policies (4) and (6) were proposed, only proportional  Markov betting policies were tested. Effects of capital size on the proportion of capital bet as well as the weaker negative  recency effects were noted and discussed but not used to generate or test alternative models. Other attributes shared by all  the previous MBG studies have been their requirements of (a) equal returns, and (b) favorable gambling conditions. The distinction between favorable vs. unfavorable betting conditions alluded to above, which separates the two main  experimental conditions in the present study, warrants clarification. In psychological experiments on decision making under  uncertainty, when the DM must choose one of several alternatives, a gamble is considered as favorable or not depending on  whether or not its EV is positive. In casino games or in multistage gambling tasks in which decisions are not interdependent,  the same distinction seems to be maintained. Thus, blackjack, the side bet in Nevada-style Baccarat, roulette, and the wheel  of fortune are all classified as favorable games, since "all may offer the player positive expectation" (Thorp, 1969, p. 273).  According to Ferguson, "Loosely speaking, games are favorable if there occasionally occur favorable situations" (1965, p.  795). The example given by Ferguson to demonstrate his distinction is also in terms of the DM's EV.  But when a detailed examination of multistage gambling games is undertaken, the distinction between favorable and unfavorable  games emerges as somewhat paradoxical. It turns out that expectation is defined not only in terms of the known probabilities  and rates of return but also with respect to some fixed betting policy, which, and this is the crux of the matter, neither  does nor is intended to maximize EV. As noted by Thorp (1969), and demonstrated by the 2-alternative N-stage MBG example  discussed above, a decision policy maximizing EV accelerates, though not necessarily maximizes, the probability of ruin, and  one minimizing the probability of bankruptcy has the unsatisfactory consequence that it also diminishes, although not  necessarily minimizes EV. A betting policy is sought, which is intermediate in some sense between minimizing the probability  of ruin and maximizing expectation. The gambling situation may then be classified as favorable or not depending on whether or  not EV is positive conditional on this betting policy.  To demonstrate the dependency of the classification of betting conditions on the prespecified decision policy, consider an  N-stage 2-alternative MBG with pl = .7, p2 = .3, and r1 = r2 = 1.5. Supposing the DM adheres to betting policy (1), which  maximizes EV. Under this policy,  E(XN+i I xi) = xi(1.05)N.  Yet, despite a 5 % increase in the DM's expected fortune, some may not refer to the betting conditions of this game as  favorable. Although it maximizes EV, betting policy (1) will lead to bankruptcy with probability 1 - (.7)N, which converges to  unity as N increases. Were he to adopt an intermediate betting policy, such as betting policy (2), the DM's  expected fortune may also decrease, but at a slower rate. Favorable vs unfavorable betting conditions are explicated in the present study relative to betting policy (2), which  maximizes E[log(xN+1) I xl]. This betting policy has certain desirable properties, which render it particularly suitable for  classifying MBG conditions. First, among the betting policies discussed above, which are intermediate between minimization of  ruin and maximization of EV, it is the only parameter free policy. If a parameter-dependent betting policy were to be used for  the classification, a game would be classified as favorable or not, depending upon the values assumed by the parameter.  Second, betting policy (2) has been shown in previous studies to provide a first-order approximation to mean data. Third, and  not less importantly, this policy has been recommended by Kelly (1956), Murphy (1965), and Thorp (1969) as very reasonable,  and, in addition to the maximization of expected utility, has been derived from other models in which the notion of utility  does not appear at all (Breiman, 1961).  The primary purpose of the present study is to test the six models described above when multistage gambling is conducted under  either favorable or unfavorable conditions. These betting conditions have been chosen both because they are inherently  interesting and because they allow for a more stringent test of the models.  METHOD  Subjects  Six undergraduate male students from the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill participated in the experiment. They were  recruited by posted advertisements for a computer-controlled multiple-session decision-making experiment with financial  rewards contingent upon performance. Each of the subjects served individually in six experimental sessions, each of which  lasted about 90 min.  Task  Administration of the MBG was controlled by a PDP-$ computer to which six independent teletypewriters were connected, each of  which was located in a separate soundproof cubicle. Seated in front of a teletypewriter, each subject received a set of  printed instructions for a 3-alternative MBG with the values of pi and N unknown.  The three alternatives of the MBG were identified as Blue, Red, and Green, and the task was then described to the subject as  follows:  "Imagine a box that is known to contain 100 marbles. Some of the marbles are Red, some of them are Blue, and the rest of the  marbles are Green. The box is given a thorough shake and then one marble is drawn out. Before you draw you must place wagers  on a Red marble, or a Blue marble, or a Green marble being drawn. These wagers represent  180 RAPOPORT ET AL.  some division of your available capital for that draw." Several examples of various possible wagers were then given, including  an equal split of the capital and wagering all capital on a single color. The subject was then told that when a marble of a  particular color was drawn, his wager on this color would be doubled and returned to him as his capital for the next trial,  whereas the wagers on the other two colors would be lost.  The following steps constituted a single trial. First, the trial number and the initial capital for the trial were printed on  the subject's teletypewriter followed by the message "Favored Order?" The subject responded by ranking the three colors in  terms of his estimated likelihood of drawing them on that trial. He did that by typing "B" for Blue, "R" for Red, and "G" for  Green. Following the subject's ranking, the words "BLUE," "RED," and "GREEN" were printed one at a time in the order specified  by the subject. After printing a color name, there was a delay until the subject typed his wager on that color. Wagers were  placed on only two colors. For the third-ranked color, the sum of the two wagers was subtracted by the computer from the  subject's capital for the trial and the difference, if nonnegative, was printed as the subject's wager on that color. If this  difference was negative an error message was printed and the subject was asked to place new bets. After three nonnegative  wagers had been placed on the three colors such that they summed up to the total capital for the stage, the word "OK ?" was  printed, requiring the subject to approve this set of wagers. If he replied negatively, by typing "N," the trial was restarted  allowing the subject to place a new set of wagers. After a positive response, by typing "Y" for "Yes," one of the three colors  was selected randomly, using the stored values of pi , and the subject received a message informing him of the winning color  as well as of his capital for the next stage.  When a game terminated, the subject was asked to estimate the percentages of Blue, Red, and Green marbles in the next game.  His responses were typed as three nonnegative integers summing to 100.  Procedure  The subject was informed of the following features of the game: (a) that sampling was with replacement; (b) that he was to  participate in several games in each session, with the number of trials varying considerably from one game to another, but  with the mixture of marbles remaining the same over all games and all sessions; (c) that the sequence of draws was randomly  predetermined and stored in the computer, and, hence, that his bets or ordering of the three alternatives could not affect the  draws; (d) that a game would terminate when he lost all of his capital for that game or after a predetermined but unknown  number of trials N, whichever came first; and (e) that successive games were independent.  While reading the instructions each subject was led through a 3-alternative 4-stage example game for which he was not paid.  The experimental games played for profit followed immediately.  Six games were presented in each of the six experimental sessions. The maximum number of trials for each game and each session  is presented in Table 1. Each session allowed up to 120 trials (with the exception of session 2, in which a computer error  allowed only 115 trials). DISCUSSION  The results of the present study strongly resemble previous MBG results. The individual differences in betting proportions are  of the same order of magnitude as those reported by Rapoport (1970) in a 3-alternative MBG. Capital size effects in condition  F (Table 9 and Figures 2 through 4) are also similar in form to those obtained previously under favorable betting conditions,  with the proportion of capital bet on the most likely alternative typically decreasing and the proportion bet on the least  likely alternative increasing as capital increases.  The major independent variable of the present study was the return rate. The difference between ri = 2 in condition U and ri =  3 in condition F, though it might seem minor or inconsequential, resulted in two phenomenologically different gambling tasks.  In condition' U, if a subject conservatively allocated his capital equally among the three alternatives, he suffered a mean  33.3% reduction in EV on each stage. A probabilitymatching betting policy (2) resulted in a mean 11.2% reduction in EV per  stage. Betting all capital on Blue on each stage caused a 14% increase in EV, while at the same time consistently increasing  the probability of bankruptcy. In condition U the primary concern, as most of the subjects expressed it spontaneously, often  in a complaining or frustrated manner, was to minimize the rate of inevitable capital reduction or merely to survive in the  game. Given that N changed drastically from one game to another (Table 1), the subject's desire was for a game to terminate as  soon as possible so as to proceed to a subsequent and hopefully shorter game. In condition F, on the other hand, the  experimental atmosphere was considerably more relaxed and friendly. As subjects almost inevitably increased their capital in  each game, they desired longer games and more games within a session, thereby providing more opportunities for increasing  profit.  194 RAPOPORT ET AL.  These substantial differences between unfavorable and favorable conditions yielded a significant but weak condition effect  (Table 6). On the average, subjects lowered their bets on Blue and, correspondingly, increased their bets on Green, with  almost no substantial changes in Red, as they switched from condition U to F (Table 7). Given the difference between the two  betting conditions, as well as previously reported radical changes in the pattern of choices while switching from positive to  negative EV static bets (Edwards, 1954, 1962), the magnitude of the mean difference reported in Table 7 is surprisingly small.  Moreover, the multivariate condition effect reported in Table 6, which is barely significant, is confounded with the  significant capital size effects reported for each subject and each condition in conjunction with the differences in the  capital size boundaries between the two conditions (Table 8). If the proportions of capital bet on a particular color were  compared between conditions U and F for a restricted range of capital size, say 200 < x < 2000, the differences between  conditions would often decrease or disappear.  A second major finding was the superiority of the logarithmic utility model over the other models, in particular the  single-stage negative exponential and minimization of risk models. A detailed stage-by-stage examination of the predicted and  observed proportions bet showed that betting policies (4) and (6) typically overpredicted the proportions bet on Blue for  small capital and underpredicted them for high capital. For the Green alternative exactly the opposite pattern of  discrepancies was noted. In other words, the prescribed functions relating y*,,lx,, to x taper off too quickly as xn increases.  The unimpressive performance of betting policies (4) and (6), as measured by the mean absolute deviation scores (Table 11), in  conjunction with the pattern of discrepancies noted above, might result from the fact that both policies assume a single-stage  decision horizon. There were three major reasons against implementing this suggestion. The first had to do with the geometrically increasing  computer costs in obtaining numerical solutions as n increases. This problem, common to the dynamic programming solution of  recursive equations, has been known as the curse of dimensionality (Bellman, 1961). A second reason is that the experimental  design employed in the present study did not allow for independent assessment of n for individual subjects. With two free  parameters to be estimated from the data, B and n, for which no validation procedures were available, we would be moving  rapidly from model testing to curve fitting. The third, reason was the relative success of the logarithmic utility model in  accounting for the results, which suggested an alternative approach to model development.  The logarithmic or Beinoullian utility model has provided a first-order approximation to betting results obtained in a wide  range of MBG studies in which the number and probabilities of the alternatives have been systematically manipulated (e.g:,  Rapoport & Jones, 1970; Rapoport et al., 1970; Gordon, 1970; Levinsohn, 1973). A tractable and parsimonious model is required that accounts for the gross features of the  data found in the present as well as previous MBG studies, namely, systematic capital size effects, probability matching for  high capital (at least for the case ri = r, the only one that has been investigated), and individual differences. Sequential  effects due to homogeneous runs of wins or losses, to the extent that they have been found in MBG studies, have been  considerably smaller in magnitude than capital size effects. Moreover, they have been frequently inconsistent, with negative  recency effects found for some subjects and positive recency effects for others (Gordon, 1970; and the present study). The  probability learning literature (e.g., Edwards, 1961, Lindman & Edwards, 1961) suggests that these effects may change in time  or disappear over long sequences of learning trials.
