MARLEY  Department of Psychology, McGill University, Montreal, Quebec  Consider the data obtained from a series of absolute judgment and pair comparison experiments, the stimuli being  "unidimensional" in some suitable sense. A number of models that have been proposed for the analysis of such data have the  following common foundation: repeated presentations of a particular stimulus lead to a (univariate) distribution of sensory  effects, and changes in a subject's behavior under fixed stimulating, but different motivating, conditions are due to changes  in various decision criteria relative to the sensory distributions, and not to changes in these distributions. A particular  model is then obtained by making specific assumptions about the form of the sensory distributions, and the type of decision  criteria. We demonstrate several strong predictions of the basic model, when minimal constraints are placed on the form of the  sensory distributions.  INTRODUCTION  A broad class of models dealing with sensory processes have the following common properties. Repeated presentations of a  particular stimulus in the domain of interest leads to a (multivariate) distribution of sensory effects, the variability of  these effects being due to various kinds of internal and/or external "noise". In any given experimental situation, the subject  divides the total sensory space into regions that are suited to the task that he has to perform, and bases his response on a  particular trial on the region in which the sensory effect lies on that trial. Particular models are obtained by making  explicit assumptions concerning the form of the sensory distributions, and the type of decision rules that the subject uses to  subdivide the space into regions. In this paper, we will be mainly interested in the constraints placed on the data when only  very general restrictions are placed on the form of the underlying distributions. Unfortunately, our results to date apply  only to the case of a univariate distribution of sensory effects; even in this case, we only have fairly complete results in  the case of absolute judgment experiments. Partial results are stated for pair comparison experiments, and none at all for  other kinds of experiments, such as those  * The research reported in this paper was begun whilst the author was a Killam Fellow at the Center for Advanced Study in  Theoretical Psychology, University of Alberta, Edmonton.  554  REPRESENTATION OF ISOSENSITIVITY CURVES 555  involving judgments of similarity, or of "same" versus "different". We therefore ask: Given a (finite) set of (fallible) data for a single subject in a series of absolute judgment experiments  with various pairs of stimulus presentations taken from some fixed set X of stimuli, can we construct underlying distributions  such that the data are "fit" by these distributions in the way suggested above. The question of fit is tricky, and we are not  yet prepared to tackle it. We answer the less difficult, but none the less interesting, question of when a set of error-free  points in the unit square is compatible with the above model. We study this in two cases: first, when we have a complete set  of data in the sense that if the data is representable in the above way, then there is a value of the criterion c  corresponding to each data point, and conversely a data point corresponding to each criterion value; second, when we have a  finite set of data that is representable in the sense that each data point corresponds to a possible value of the criterion c,  but we do not necessarily have a data point corresponding to every criterion value.  We then discuss similar problems that arise in connection with absolute judgment experiments with more than two stimulus  presentations, forced choice experiments, and pair comparison experiments. Also, we discuss whether certain other models in  the literature, which at first consideration do not seem to be of the above form, are in fact compatible with the present  ideas.
