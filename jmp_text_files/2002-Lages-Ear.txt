Ear Decomposition for Pair Comparison Data 

Martin Lages 

University of Glasgow 

An efficient graph-theoretical decomposition technique is introduced that treats inconsistencies in behavioral data as systematic adaptations rather than random errors. This technique, which is known as ear decomposition, reduces inconsistencies in any binary data set to a basis of directed cycles. Such a basis characterizes the data set in terms of inconsistencies and its size offers an improved measure of internal consistency. In two examples it is illustrated how different implementations of the ear decomposition technique can help to identify choices that are critical for violations of transitivity. © 2001 Elsevier Science (USA) 

Key Words: choice behavior; consistency; intransitivity; decision making. 

1. INTRODUCTION 

A graph-theoretical decomposition—known as ear decomposition—is applied to pair comparison data. The ear decomposition is especially suitable when the data sample is relatively small but contains a considerable number of intransitive observations. Missing or repeated observations pose no problem. The technique finds a minimal number of cyclic substructures characterizing any binary data set in terms of its internal consistency. Additional aspects that are related to the emergence of inconsistent choices in a pair comparison are easily implemented and can help to identify critical decisions. 

The paper is subdivided into four sections that are organized as follows. In the Introduction the analysis of inconsistencies is motivated in the context of individual choice behavior. It is well known that fundamental principles of classical binary choice models are exposed to systematic violations. Describing human decision behavior in terms of algebraic models has a longer tradition than psychology as an empirical science and dates back to the eighteenth century. For example, the mathematician Daniel Bernoulli (1738) laid down the foundations of expected utility, the majority rule traces back to Condorcet (1785), who applied this rule in the context of social choice, and the weighted sets of differences rule was used by Benjamin Franklin (1772) in the context of individual choice. Since then researchers in disciplines such as mathematics, economics, philosophy, and psychology have been involved in decision research. 

In general, choice behavior may be described as the specification of a nonempty subset X of objects from a set S , with finite or infinite cardinality, where every possible subset of the set denotes a possible choice. Consequently, the complete choice behavior of an individual is only captured if all chosen subsets of alternatives from all possible subsets of S were collected. This is clearly impracticable and a number of suitable restrictions are usually imposed. Then, of course, choice behavior is reduced to a finite subcollection of choices. Choosing a single object out of a set of objects is more common than selecting two or more alternatives. The latter may be considered as repeated choice of single alternatives from the same set. In fact, binary choice or choosing a single object out of two in a forced choice pair comparison is regarded as a valid way of assessing individual choice behavior. In the usual two-alternative forced choice (2-AFC) procedure a person is forced to make a sequence of choices between pairs of objects. Clearly, under this paradigm choice behavior satisfies weak connectedness and asymmetry , for distinct alternatives a, b ¥ S satisfy either a P b or b P a where P denotes the preference relation (cf., Roberts, 1979). The preference relation is often said to be a subset of the cartesian product of the set of alternatives, written as P ı S×S . Hence, in the case of a standard forced choice pair comparison the preference relation is an asymmetric and weakly connected subset of the cartesian product. This restriction is easily overlooked because it is convenient to assume that choice behavior is at least asymmetric and therefore unaffected by the experimental paradigm. The restriction to asymmetric and weakly connected relations in a forced choice pair comparison gives a trivial example of how the experimental paradigm affects choice behavior-simply because preference reversals and missing preferences are excluded by definition.

Despite their presupposed properties, forced choice pair comparisons[1] have been used in numerous empirical investigations and are closely related to the attempt to find an axiomatization of preference in terms of binary relations (Neumann & Morgenstern, 1944). By imposing constraints or axioms on the cartesian product an ordered structure or preference relation is defined. When defining an ordered structure it is important whether the set of alternatives S is considered as a set of finite or infinite cardinality and whether the objects in the set are represented as single- or as multiattribute alternatives. In the following only finite sets of multiattribute alternatives will be of interest. 

Rational choice behavior has been considered as optimal choice, emphasizing the economic origin of decision research. Optimal choice maximizes the outcome of one or more choices. This interpretation requires an interval scale where different outcomes can be compared quantitatively. Such a scale is the utility scale that was originally defined in terms of money but forms a more abstract construct since the days of Cramer (1728) and Bernoulli (1738). The interval scale requires stronger assumptions than the more fundamental ordinal scale, which only preserves the ordering of objects (Cantor, 1895; Stevens, 1946). In the following the assumptions for a representation on ordinal scale are of primary interest and rational choice behavior is discussed exclusively in terms of ordinal preference relations. 

Transitivity of choice is a necessary condition for the existence of an ordinal utility scale. Together with connectedness, transitivity is also a sufficient condition for the existence of such a scale, provided the number of alternatives is finite or countable.[2] Because transitivity is such a fundamental property for the ordinal representation of choice behavior it has been labeled the cornerstone of normative and descriptive decision theories (Edwards, 1954; Luce, 1990; for an exception on ratio scale see, however, Fishburn, 1982, 1991). Indeed, persons behave irrationally if they violate transitivity in their choice behavior and the following example is commonly cited: Assume a person chooses intransitively by preferring alternative a over alternative b , b over c , and c over a . Then, it can be argued that this person is willing to pay a certain amount of money to exchange a for b , another amount to exchange c for a , and a third amount to exchange b for c . Thereby, the person loses money but ends up with the same alternative. Obviously, the circular argument a P b , b P c , and c P a , also known as the ‘‘money pump’’ or ‘‘dutch book,’’ can be used recursively and extends to more than three alternatives. 

On the other hand, if we think of choices in terms of preference and indifference, written as a R b , b R c , and c R a , the phenomenon of intransitive choices may be explained as indifference between alternatives. This can be modeled by the equivalence between indifferent alternatives, written as a ’ b , b ’ c , and c ’ a .[3] However, it has been shown that indifference is not necessarily transitive and therefore not an

He discussed the following example: Suppose a boy is indifferent about receiving as a gift either a pony or a bicycle. He will undoubtedly prefer the bicycle with a bell attached to the bicycle without a bell. But he is still likely to be indifferent about the bicycle with bell and the pony. Another axiom that has been associated with rational choice and that has implications for the representation on ordinal scale is asymmetry. If a person prefers a over b , he or she should not prefer b over a at the same time. Again, the phenomenon of preference reversals may be explained by indifference, but usually only preference reversals between equivalent alternatives are believed to be nonsystematic and a possible reason for indifference or limited discriminatory ability. As mentioned before, in a standard forced choice pair comparison preference reversals are ruled out by the experimental design unless comparisons are repeated. 

Preference reversals and intransitivity of preference may be considered as violations of rational choice behavior especially when equivalence and indiscriminability between multiattribute alternatives can be excluded as a possible explanation. Both phenomena, reversals and intransitivity, are related and have a common feature in the language of graph theory. They can be described as directed cycles connecting two, three, or more vertices in a digraph. It is suggested here that inconsistent or irrational choice is conveniently described as directed k -cycles, including directed 2-cycles for preference reversals, rather than a violation of stronger principles that may be derived from axiom systems of expected utility theory and its generalizations (Savage, 1954). This graph-theoretical definition of intransitive preference is exploited in the next section. Violations of asymmetry and transitivity, that is preference reversals and intransitive preferences, are considered as irrational choice behavior. To acknowledge this simplistic view of rationality the rather general term inconsistent choice instead of irrational choice is used throughout and simply refers to preference cycles of any length. Note that this view is not as restrictive as limited discriminability between objects. Graphical representations of various inconsistencies are given in Fig. 1. Graph A on the left-hand side shows a preference reversal (2-dicycle), an intransitive triple (3-dicycle) is depicted in Graph B, and preference cycles of lengths 3 and 4 (3 and 4-dicycle) appear in Graph C on the right-hand side. It is argued that longer preference cycles are less convincingly interpreted in terms of (intransitive) indifference because all alternatives that belong to a directed cycle need to be indifferent. This means that otherwise perfectly ordered alternatives become indifferent as the result of a single violation. To explain d R a in Graph C, for example, would require that a ’ b ’ c ’ d are indifferent. On the contrary, it seems more plausible to assume that the preference order between a , b , c , and d is valid and that the choice d P a marks some altered information processing reversing this preference.[4]

There are a number of empirical phenomena that are known to occur among multiattribute alternatives and that have some potential to create inconsistent choice (May, 1954; Grether & Plott, 1979; Lichtenstein & Slovic, 1971). For instance, choice behavior is affected if an additional outcome or attribute is added to the alternatives (Ellsberg, 1961) or if an alternative is added to a set of multiattribute alternatives. It was recognized that the frequency of choosing alternatives can be either reduced or increased (Huber, Payne, & Puto, 1982; Kahneman & Tversky, 1979; Wedell, 1991) depending on certain attributes alternatives share among each other. The systematic selection of a set of multiattribute alternatives due to similarity (Rumelhart & Greeno, 1971) or difference in attractiveness also showed a considerable effect on individual decision behavior (Albert, Aschenbrenner, & Schmalhofer, 1989). 

As the number of empirically verified effects or biases increases, the requirements for decision models become more and more demanding. It seems reasonable to assume that most alternatives are multiattribute and that decision-making processes can vary from one choice trial to the next because the alternatives themselves provide changing contexts. Adaptive choice heuristics are plausible candidates that can explain some of the inconsistent preferences (Aschenbrenner, 1981; Dawes, 1979; Dawes & Corrigan, 1974; Gigerenzer & Goldstein, 1996; Payne, 1976; Schmalhofer, Albert, Aschenbrenner & Gertzen, 1986; Simon, 1956, 1959; Tversky, 1969). Unfortunately, these models, and in fact all multiattribute models, require a lot of domain-specific assumptions before they can explain choice behavior. In addition to the underlying decision rule the attributes, their values, and often their weights need to be assessed before any of these models can be applied. 

In the next section a nonprobabilistic approach is suggested that focuses on inconsistent choice behavior. Variants of the ear decomposition are discussed that impose weak assumptions on the processing of domain-specific attributes. Inconsistencies are handled as internal errors signalling adaptive choice behavior. 

A number of nonprobabilistic techniques have been put forward that relate to the idea of decomposing a structure of binary relations into substructures. In the case of preference data these substructures usually correspond to attributes or dimensions of the underlying decision process. In general, the substructures, which adhere to a

In a theoretical paper, Doignon, Ducamp, and Falmagne (1984) showed that an arbitrary relation R that is defined on the cartesian product of two finite sets R ı S×D can be expressed by the intersection of |R[¯] | biorders Bi .[5] Koppen (1987) investigated reductions and algorithms that compute the minimal number of biorders for data matrices of moderate size using tools of graph theory. He used the fact that the minimal number of biorders or bidimension of a relation is identical to the chromatic number of an associated hypergraph. 

In a more general approach Chubb (1986) reported an algebraic technique that can reduce preference matrices to a 4-core of relations to find the minimal number of dimensions. In a paper that explicitly relates to choice behavior, Barthélemy (1990) suggested a decomposition into substructures that refer to different points of view of the decision maker. This decomposition follows from a lexicographic decision rule that includes in its generalized form both the conjunctive and the disjunctive rule as well as decision rules with thresholds. The main goal was a minimization of the number of substructures: the lexicographic sum of a minimal number of partial orders explaining all preference relations, including intransitive preference cycles. Barthélemy did not suggest an explicit decomposition technique that determines the underlying partial orders but derived upper bounds for the minimal number of partial orders. In another approach Doignon (1995) and Doignon and Falmagne (1997) investigated the family of all possible semiorders on a given set: If a family of semiorders is well graded it can be described by a permutahydron where stepwise changes of semiorders can be studied in a stochastic learning process operating on this lattice structure. 

The following critical remarks apply to the decompositions mentioned so far. Similar to deterministic models they are based on a normative rationale that is supposed to hold for each substructure. In principle, the combination of substructures could model adaptive choice behavior but to my knowledge none of the decompositions have been empirically tested. The reasons for the lack of applications are quickly explained. These models are new to decision research and their application to choice behavior has not been at the center of interest. More important, no efficient techniques for the identification of substructures were developed. This problem remains unresolved because minimal solutions for these decompositions are computationally hard to find and not necessarily unique. Depending on the empirical preference structure, there may be many sets of substructures, each of which can explain the preference structure. As a consequence techniques for the previously mentioned decompositions, if available, would be difficult to test. This is particularly true because these decompositions have at least one trivial but adequate solution and are based on preference relations as the only variable observed. So far no additional aspects have been proposed or incorporated into existing decomposition models. In this paper ear decomposition techniques are discussed that work efficiently and provide a minimal solution. It is shown that under suitable constraints the solution is even unique.

2. EAR DECOMPOSITION FOR DIRECTED GRAPHS 

There are three equivalent ways to represent a collection of binary relations between distinct objects: ordered sets, graphs, and vectors. Each representation refers to a different theoretical approach, namely order theory, graph theory, and algebra. In the following, graphs and diagrams are used to exemplify structures, whereas theoretical results are stated in terms of sets and vectors. (cf. Bergé, 1973; Biggs, 1993). 

A directed graph D , abbreviated to digraph , consists of a finite set V(D) of vertices together with a set E(D) of oriented edges or arcs . Their cardinalities are |E(D)|=m and |V(D)|=n . A straightforward definition of inconsistent and intransitive relations in a pair comparison is provided by directed cycles , abbreviated to dicycles .[6]

In the following the ear decomposition is introduced, which can decompose strong components of a digraph. Folklore in mathematics tells that the name was derived from the graphical illustration of an ear decomposition as depicted in Fig. 3. The idea of an undirected ear structure was first suggested by Whitney (1932, 1933) and has been useful in proving many graph-theoretical results (e.g., Lova´sz & Plummer, 1986). 

A digraph is strong if and only if it can be built from a vertex by sequentially adding arcs and by subdividing arcs. This property leads to the definition of an ear decomposition . 

Definition 2. An ear decomposition of a digraph D is a sequence E0, ..., Ek= D of subdigraphs of D such that E0 consists of a single vertex and Ei arises from Ei−1 by adding a directed path Pi whose endvertices (which are not necessarily distinct) belong to Ei−1 while the arcs and intermediate vertices of Pi do not. 

The paths Pi are called directed ear paths . It is possible to obtain a basis of directed cycles from an ear decomposition by completing each ear to a directed cycle using a directed path in the strong component. Such a collection of directed cycles is called directed ear basis . 3. APPLICATION TO BINARY CHOICE 

In the following it is argued that intransitive choices in a pair comparison do not occur randomly but in response to the alternatives that provide changing contexts of choice. The sources of intransitivities are therefore highly subjective and domainspecific eluding efforts to capture intransitivities by a simple decision rule. 

Consider, for example, a person who chooses between magazines in a pair comparison paradigm. In the first few choices he or she may develop a strong preference for magazines with a detailed TV preview. When confronted with a magazine that has a large section on cooking recipes he or she realizes that this is another attribute of importance. Taking this attribute into account would have reversed some earlier choices but generates intransitive preferences now. The difficulty is to identify the choice that has been critical and caused a shift in viewpoint. In the following, two variants of the ear decomposition are suggested that can help to identify critical choices without assessing domain-specific information by costly tracing methods, without introducing strong assumptions about the decision strategy, and without running into computational problems. 

Various constraints can be implemented to refine the ear decomposition (e.g., Franzblau, 1999). Finding an ear basis with a minimal number of arcs gives the least redundant solution and is suggested first. 

Definition 4. An ear decomposition is called minimal if it has an ear basis with a minimal number of arcs. 

A minimal ear decomposition provides a reduced search space for critical arcs , that is a set of choices that appear frequently in intransitivities. A single arc that appears in a number of ear dicycles may indicate that this choice is responsible for all intransitivities generated by these ear dicycles. In general however, the minimal number of critical arcs corresponds to the ‘‘acyclic subgraph problem,’’ a computational problem that is N P -complete. It is also related to finding Slater’s i , the minimum number of arc reversals to obtain a transitive tournament (Jünger, 1985;

In the following it is illustrated how the ear decomposition can determine intransitivities as they occur in a sequence of choice trials. This refined decomposition ensures that up to a given choice trial all intransitivities are captured by ear dicycles. Whenever a subsequent choice creates intransitivities new ear dicycles are generated. Subsequent inspection of these ear dicycles reveals the earliest choices that can explain all intransitivities within the data set. 

The algorithm selects the earliest choices when searching a new ear path and when completing the ear path to an ear dicycle. Thereby the assumption is made that early choices are more likely to be critical than later choices because decisions tend to be more consistent as the pair comparison progresses. A plausible justification is that during the first trials the decision maker is not yet familiar with the alternatives and therefore likes to update his or her heuristic. In later trials the

It was demonstrated that the ear decomposition can overcome three problems. (1) Unlike other algebraic decomposition techniques it works efficiently and finds a solution in polynomial time. (2) It can provide a minimal solution in the sense that the ear dicycles form a basis of the cycle space. (3) The minimal ear decomposition provides the smallest search space to identify critical arcs. If the arcs are indexed then the solution of the labeled ear decomposition is unique. 

4. CONCLUSION 

Directed ear bases can characterize inconsistent binary data and the size of the ear bases provides an improved measure of internal consistency. The ear decomposition is efficient, its solution minimal, and, if the sequence of trials is taken into account, unique. It is therefore suggested that pair comparison data should be analyzed in terms of directed ear bases when assessing internal inconsistency. First implementations in PROLOG perform minimal or labeled ear decompositions on any binary data set. The program is called EAR, which stands for Error Analysis and Reduction, and can be obtained from the author. 

Identifying critical choices within an ear basis can help to understand idiosyncratic or domain-specific aspects of choice behavior. If a particular choice is at the center of many intransitive choices (within or between subjects) then it seems reasonable to assume that this choice can alter the decision process. Similarly, in a sequence of choice-trials early choices may be responsible for intransitivities that include later choices. 

To empirically validate these techniques it can be tested whether the introduction of modified alternatives can predict ear dicycles and critical choices. Altered decision processes are expected if the corresponding choice trial conveys new or modified information about the alternatives or the choice task itself. It should be possible to identify these choices by the appropriate ear decomposition technique. Moreover, pair comparison paradigms with varying sequences of choice trials should affect internal consistency. It has been shown, for example, that internal consistency is increased when pairs in a pair comparison are arranged in blocks so that all alternatives appear within a block of choices (Lages, 1999).

One of the major challenges in the field of social sciences is the understanding of error in human choice behavior (Luce, 1996). The probabilistic approach is often unsatisfying because it identifies error with randomness. A probabilistic choice model, for example, describes preferences between lotteries or gambles by a probabilistic preference relation between alternatives. Thereby, the alternatives are represented in terms of probabilities subjecting observed preferences to randomness in choice and randomness in utility. Even in situations where choices are repeated it is possible that a series of ear decompositions can provide more detailed information about choice behavior than collecting frequencies in a probabilistic model. 

In general, deterministic and probabilistic theories address the problem of structure and variability in behavioral data. The dilemma is that classical deterministic approaches are inflexible and do not account for variability whereas probabilistic approaches often do not provide a desirable qualitative representation of changing behavior. The ear decomposition and related decomposition methods on the other hand could reveal the source of variability without losing their qualitative appeal. It is hoped that further developments and applications of decomposition techniques will help to establish nonprobabilistic models of error in decision research and the social sciences.
