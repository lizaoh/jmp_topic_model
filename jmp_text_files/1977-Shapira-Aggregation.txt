JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 16, 60-67 (1977)  On the Aggregation Across Subjects in Analyzing Individual Choice Behavior  ZUR SHAPIRA AND ITZHAK VENEZIA  Jerusalem School of Business, Hebrew University, Jerusalem, Israel  Authors dealing with models of individual choice behavior often point out the pitfalls of using group data to test such  models. However, it is possible to derive properties of the choice behavior of the population based on these models of  individual choice behavior. The present paper develops a procedure for the aggregation of choice data which takes into account  individual differences. The utility of using aggregated choice data and the properties of the present procedure in comparison  to other methods which utilized group data in analyzing choice behavior are discussed.  Using group data may not be adequate in testing models of individual choice behavior. This was demonstrated by a simple  example provided by Luce (1959, p. 8) showing how group averages can violate a choice axiom which is not violated by any one  individual of the same group. Consequently, several studies of individual choice behavior (see, e.g., Tversky, 1972) analyzed  data within individuals only.  In many cases, however, individual choice behavior has to be inferred from group data. The underlying assumption is that there  is some common rule of choice which is employed by all individuals.  Several authors dealing with constant utility models (CUM) had to assume that all individuals have the same scale values, in  order to estimate these scales for the alternatives chosen. This assumption is made in Case V of Thurstone's (1927) model, in  Bock and Jones' (1968) elaborations of the Thurstonian model, and in a recent study conducted by Rumelhart and Greeno (1971).  Authors dealing with random utility models (RUM) did not make such a strict assumption. They assumed (cf. Luce & Suppes, 1965;  McFadden, 1974) that the utility function of each individual (i.e., the scale values) is a random variable consisting of a  population utility and a random element with an expectation equal to zero. Marschak (1960) and Luce & Suppes (1965) have shown  that if the random variable has the Weibull distribution, then the probability that an alternative would be chosen from a set  of alternatives depends only on the population utility, which is common to all individuals. McFadden (1974) used this property  in conjunction with the Logit procedure to estimate the parameters of several choice models.  In this paper we present a method to estimate the parameters of individual choice behavior models in the context of CUM. The  present method has an advantage over previous studies in this area since it is not assumed that all individuals have the same  scale values. (The present method also has an advantage over previous treatments of the  60  Copyright 1977 by Academic Press, Inc.  All rights of reproduction in any form reserved. Instead we assume that the scale values of each individual are obtained by sampling from some distribution. This method  resembles the random coefficient regression model in Econometrics (cf., Akkina, 1974; Swamy, 1971; and Theil, 1971). However,  since the choice models we consider are nonlinear in the parameters to be estimated (the scale values), the mean of the choice  function depends on the specific sampling distribution. Presenting the exact formula of the mean is quite difficult for most  sampling distributions, also it is not usually known which distribution is appropriate, hence it may be advantageous to  approximate the mean. Second-order approximations (to be defined below) will be used to represent the means of the choice  functions. METHOD  The model was tested using two sources of paired-comparison choice data: 1. Data published by Rumelhart and Greeno (1971); 11.  data collected in a study which was conducted by the present authors.  I. Rumelhart and Greeno's (1971) Study  In their study each of 234 subjects made 36 pairwise choices among nine well-known personalities. The proportions of time each  personality was preferred over the others were calculated (see Rumelhart and Greeno, 1971, p. 379, Table 3). These proportions  were subjected to the present procedure to derive a set of scale values for the nine personalities.  The similarities between subgroups of personalities played a major role in their study, but is not dealt with at all in the  present model. Hence, an experiment was conducted to test the model in a situation where the similarity factor was controlled  II. The Present Study  Subjects: The subjects were 80 undergraduate students at the Hebrew University, who were taking an introductory course in  Business Administration.  Procedure: The subjects basically followed the procedure of Rumelhart and Greeno (1971). Each subject was required to make 28  pairwise choices. The stimuli presented were the names of eight well-known politicians in Israel. The 28 choices constituted  all the possible pairs formed by the stimuli. The names of the politicians were: Y. Allon, M. Begin, M. Dayan, A. Eban, S.  Peres, Y. Rabin, A. Sharon, and I. Using data within individuals  is recommended primarily for testing models of individual choice behavior (see, e.g., Tversky, 1972). Group data, on the other  hand, may not be appropriate for this purpose, but may be advantageous in other situations where one wishes to predict the  choice behavior of some aggregate group of people based on the model of the individual. Such a case is the attempt to predict  market shares in the area of consumer choice behavior.  Within the context of group data analysis there are two different options. First, one may use group averages the way Rumelhart  and Greeno (1971) did, and second, one may utilize an appropriate aggregation procedure. The procedure discussed in this paper  follows this second avenue. It has an advantage over previous procedures that dealt with the issue of aggregated data. First,  it takes into account individual differences and does not assume that all individuals have the same scale values (such as Case  V of the Thurstonian model). Second, it does not assume a a specific distribution of the random element in the utility  function of the individual (cf. McFadden's 1974, use of the Weibull distribution, and Bass, Jeuland, & Wright's, 1976, use of  the gamma distribution, for which no support by data has been given).  Also, the present model is within the CUM context and thus differs from other attempts to aggregate individual data (see, e.g.  McFadden, 1974) which dealt with RUM. Finally, as indicated above, the present method can be easily applied to other choice  models.  In sum, it was shown that group data may be utilized to analyze individual choice behavior if an appropirate aggregation and  approximation procedure is used.
