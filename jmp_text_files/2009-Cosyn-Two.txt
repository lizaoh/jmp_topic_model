At the inception, the ambition of Knowledge Space Theory was to offer a sound combinatoric framework to design an efficient machine for the assessment of knowledge (see  Doignon and Falmagne (1985, 1999) ). In this context, mastery with regard to a specific field of knowledge is tested by a collection of question types, or ‘items’. The field of knowledge, or ‘domain’, is simply identified with the full collection of items. A student’s mastery, or ‘knowledge state’, is the subset of items that she can answer correctly. The family of feasible knowledge states defines the ‘knowledge structure’.

In the following, we briefly make the point that the importance of the above two conditions on knowledge structures largely derives from the original emphasis of the theory on  knowledge assessment . We shall then argue that, if the motivation is in modeling  knowledge acquisition , then weaker versions of the requirement of closure under union and well-gradedness, respectively, should be substituted. However, we shall see that, used in conjunction, our new requirements are quite powerful and are equivalent to the old conditions.

The condition of closure under union has historically been the main requirement on a knowledge structure. It is a fairly reasonable expectation on the family of knowledge states: if there is a student knowing exactly the items in  
                         K 
                       and another knowing exactly the items in  
                         L 
                      , then we may expect that there could be a student knowing exactly the items in  
                         K 
                         ∪ 
                         L 
                      . For instance, the two students could talk to each other and bring their knowledge state to  
                         K 
                         ∪ 
                         L 
                      . Maybe more importantly, this algebraic requirement allows one to design computerized procedures to build a knowledge structure by tapping human expertize ( Kambouri, Koppen, Villano, and Falmagne (1994) ,  Koppen and Doignon (1988)  and  Müller (1989) ; see also  Düntsch and Gediga (1996) , for an extension of such procedures to general knowledge structures).

Well-gradedness is another often required condition on a knowledge structure. Among other things, a knowledge structure which is well-graded has the remarkable property that any knowledge state is completely determined by its inner and outer fringes ( Definition 2.1  below, Theorem 2.8 in  Doignon and Falmagne (1999) . This property is very useful in reporting the output of an assessment procedure which matches the student’s performance with a knowledge state.

The two axioms on a knowledge structure which are going to be introduced in this section came from an effort to answer the following question: If we were to consider a knowledge structure as a way to model a student’s learning, what would be the a priori requirements that it should satisfy? In other words, we will put aside the conditions traditionally added to a knowledge structure when one was concerned with practical means to build it and use it for the purpose of assessing knowledge. In particular, we will momentarily abandon the conditions of closure under union and well-gradedness for weaker conditions which have a stronger appeal from the viewpoint of learning.

Though slightly weaker than  Corollary 2.3 , the following corollary is the main statement from a pedagogical standpoint.  
                         Corollary 2.4 
                         
                            A knowledge structure is learning smooth and outer fringe coherent if and only if it is a well-graded knowledge space.

A knowledge structure is learning smooth and outer fringe coherent if and only if it is a well-graded knowledge space.

The two axioms described in this note can be posed as a minimal set of requirements for a knowledge structure whose emphasis is the modeling of students’ learning. The fact that well-graded knowledge spaces are equivalent to such ‘learning structure’ provides a stronger pedagogical ground for their use in the application of knowledge structure theory

                         In the spirit of the results of this note, the use of the term ‘learning space’ to designate a well-graded knowledge space has already trickled in the literature  Eppstein (2008)  and  Eppstein, Falmagne, and Ovchinnikov (2008) .

Partial or related results can be found in the literature, though they sprang from different sets of motivations. Learning smoothness is closely related to the concepts of  learning path  and  gradation  (see, for instance, Definition 2.1 in  Doignon and Falmagne (1999) ). In particular, learning smoothness is equivalent to the requirement that all learning paths are gradations. Theorem 2.10 in  Doignon and Falmagne (1999)  states that a knowledge space is well-graded if and only if all learning paths are gradations.  Koppen (1998)  introduces the notion of accessibility from a purely algebraic perspective while studying surmise mappings. His Lemma 4.4 states that a knowledge space is well-graded if and only if it is accessible.

Related results are found in the literature on antimatroids (see, for instance,  Korte, Lovász, and Schrader (1991) ). There, an accessible structure is known as an  accessible set system , and outer fringe coherence is known as the  interval property without upper bounds . An accessible set system having the interval property without upper bounds is known as an  antimatroid . They are shown to be closed under union, though it seems that the notion of well-gradedness is ignored.
