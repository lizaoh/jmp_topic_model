BACKGROUND  Saaty, in a number of references (1980-1984), introduced a scaling method for paired comparison dominance judgments that  entails scaling of a paired comparison reciprocal and n-dimensional response matrix [R] by using the normalized principal  right (PR) eigenvector corresponding to the maximum eigenvalue ~ of [R]. The ri elements of [R] are typically ratio-scaled  responses in a judgment task concerning perceived dominance of alternative i over j, e.g., dominance judgments as to utility,  preference, probability, beauty, taste, performance, etc. As in virtually all previous papers, reciprocal rji = 1/ru responses  are assumed such that for n comparison alternatives only n(n- 1)/2 judgments need be elicited. Saaty's contribution stimulated  hundreds of published and unpublished applications that utilize the innovative and very popular eigenvector method (EM) of  scaling [R ]. Some firms such as Woods Gordon in Canada, a member of Arthur Young International, advertise consulting services  in applying Saaty's scaling and Analytic Hierarchy Process methods. If rij depicts any element in the response matrix [R ], the respondent is said to have perfect (cardinal) consistency if rik =  rurik for all element pairs. In particular, the u; eigenvector scores will  be compared with w,?' optimal scores based upon a criterion of minimum aggregate least squares deviations of observed  inconsistent ru responses from adjusted w,* = w* /w* consistent responses.  Saaty implicitly assumes uU = ui/uj replaces rte responses, where e(uu) = ru - uij depicts the implicit EM consistency  adjustment. The EM aggregate error sum of squares  ESS = (ru - uu)Z,  is not in general the minimum ESS that converts [R ] to a rank one matrix. In other words, the [U] matrix comprised of uij  surrogates for ru responses is not generally the optimal rank one matrix under a least squares criterion. In contrast, the LSM  scaling of [R] yields w* solutions that minimize ESS. Nearly all applications by Saaty and his  colleagues have been in a bounded [v < r;j < 9] response scale where 9 is the maximal dominance of i relative to j and 9 is  the maximal dominance of j relative to i. The adjectives used in this Saaty response scale (SRS), as utilized in numerous  prior studies, are repeated in numerous references, e.g., Saaty (1980, pp. 53-54). For example, suppose r12 = 9 or r23 = 9 at SRS upper bounds. The respondent  trying to be perfectly consistent would then want to let ri3 = rl2r23 = 81, but the SRS upper bound for any ru, including r,3,  is 9. Therefore, the respondent is forced to be inconsistent if r,2 = r23 = 9 judgments are retained.  Unbounded response scales have rarely been employed, although Jensen (1981, 1982, 1983b) proposes the use of odds ratio  responses. This (0 < rid < oo) scale overcomes the forced inconsistency artifact of the SRS. However, respondents are still  likely to be inconsistent even when allowed greater freedom using the odds ratio scale in place of the SRS scale.  4. NONUNIQUENESS OF LSM  In practice, LSM and EM generally provide unique scaling solutions to scaling of [R ] matrices and the corresponding implicit  consistency adjustments. It is, however, possible to specify [R] matrices that have certain symmetries and high levels of  response inconsistency that result in multiple LSM solutions yielding minimum least square error. I discuss and illustrate  these circumstances in Jensen (1984). Nonuniqueness is easily detected and is not likely to arise in practice, especially when  the CR < 0.10 rule of thumb is applied to consistency adjustments. THE "BEST" SCALING METHOD CANNOT, IN GENERAL,  BE RESOLVED FROM INFORMATION IN [R] ALONE  Saaty and Vargas (1984) compare eigenvalue method (EM), least squares method (LSM), and logarithmic least squares (LLSM)  approaches to adjusting for response inconsistency in [R ]. They strongly favor EM and go so far as to contend in Saaty and  Vargas (1984, p. 24) that: "In fact, it (EM) is the only method that should be used when the data are not entirely  consistent." In this paper I argue otherwise. Under certain criteria EM is a very good method and LSM fares badly. For  example, if rik > 1, then it is more likely that (UJ/uk) > 1 than (w*/w,*) > 1 at moderate to high levels of response  inconsistency. If such rank preservation is deemed the most important criterion, then neither EM nor LSM are the best  approaches for consistency adjustment. Both EM and LSM may reverse element or row rankings. It is shown in Jensen (1984) that  approaches other than EM are better for assuring rank preservation.  Under other criteria EM is only fair or even poor relative to LSM. This paper, for example, demonstrates that LSM is far  superior to EM in yielding consistency adjusted surrogates for rjk responses that are contained within the original response  bounds imposed upon respondent judgments. LSM is optimal under a criterion to minimize Euclidean distances between original  responses and their consistencyadjusted surrogates.  There are countless varieties of consistency adjustment (scaling) methods and criteria under which consistency adjustments may  be evaluated. Inconsistent [R] response matrices do not contain enough information for resolving which method or criterion is  optimal, e.g., we cannot get enough information from [R] alone for resolving whether EM is better than LSM or vice versa. Up  to now, published studies, including my own, appear to have assumed that since EM has been used in nearly every AHP study that  it is ipso facto the "only" method.  330 ROBERT E. JENSEN  My point in this paper is that it is important to compare alternate methods, especially LSM versus EM. LSM is primarily  concerned with magnitudes of consistency adjustments whereas EM is mainly concerned with preserving ordinal priorities. The  analyst should first examine whether LSM versus EM scalings differ substantially. If so, then the analyst must seek or specify  added information as to whether magnitude, ranking, or some other criterion should dominate the choice of a scaling method for  [R]. In such instances, this information cannot be gleaned from [R ] itself.  Choices of scaling methods other than EM or LSM should proceed along similar lines. The analyst must determine what is desired  from the scaled outcomes and how they are to be used. Possibly, various scaling outcomes should be presented in the Analytic  Hierarchy. Process analysis to judge the sensitivities of the ultimate composition outcomes upon choices of scaling methods.  In my view, there is much in favor of the LSM method. I am not advocating that it is the best. or only appropriate method. Nor  am I advocating that it be chosen as "the" method for any study. I am proposing that it nearly always be included along with  EM and other scaling methods so that the advantages of LSM discussed in this paper are not overlooked in the "bootstrap"  process of matrix scaling and consistency adjustment.  The presentation of alternative scalings of [R] matrices may confuse readers and decision makers somewhat. But it may confuse  them constructively by drawing their attention to implicit assumptions underlying alternative scaling methods and the  sensitivities of outcomes to choice of a particular metric. The LSM does have a key advantage when explaining consistency  adjustments since it is usually quite easy to comprehend magnitudes of adjustment in terms of Euclidean distance.  8. SUMMARY AND CONCLUSION  The early contributions of Saaty have resulted in hundreds of applications of the PR-eigenvector of [R] scaling method (EM)  for Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP) analysis. In this paper it is demonstrated that the u, components of the PReigenvector of  [R] are relatively sensitive to inconsistent responses when [R] is not rank one. The revised u,; = u;/u; consistent responses  implicit in using u; scaled weights, therefore, lead to very large E(uu) = r;j + u,; consistency adjustments whose ESS  combined error may differ substantially from minimum ESS solutions even for low levels of response inconsistency.  In this paper it is proposed that wi weights giving rise to e(wu) = ru - w consistency adjustments under the LSM method have a  number of important advantages over the u, weights. The w * = w */wf ratios comprise the matrix [ W* ] of rank one whose  LS-eigenvector yields w * components. The LS-eigenvector of [W*] minimizes the ESS or r;j consistency adjustments. The error  reduction may be substantive even when CR < 0.10. For example, in Table 2 the u, weights give rise to consistency adjustment  squared error of ESS = 187.9, whereas the w* optimal  ALTERNATIVE SCALING METHOD 331  weights reduce this to ESS = 58.2. This savings is substantial even when the CR = 0.077 suggests inconsistency of response is  rather minor. Also, the u; versus w* weights are substantively different for the U.S. and Japan even though the CR = 0.077  small value suggests insignificant inconsistency of response. Both row priority magnitude and rankings may differ under EM  versus LSM approaches.  It is, therefore, proposed that analysts do not automatically adopt the EM scaling technique without at least considering the  differences between the resulting u; weights vis-a-vis the w* least square optimal weights obtained by the LSM technique. In  this paper it is demonstrated that, because of higher degrees of freedom, w* weights yield implicit consistency adjustments  that are less extreme than the ui weights obtained in the PR-eigenvector of [R ]. The PR-eigenvector of [R ] transforms the [R  ] matrix to a rank one [U], whereas the LS-eigenvector approach transforms [R] to a rank one [W*] matrix using an approach  with more degrees of freedom such that [W*] is "closer" to the original [R] judgments of the respondent in terms of subtracted  differences squared.  Important conclusions in the paper are:  (1) The ESS error reduction by using w* weights relative to the popular u; weights may be substantive even when the CR < 0.10  rule of thumb suggests inconsistency is minor. (3) The analyst who uses the ut weights may be implicitly making much larger "arbitrary" consistency adjustments than  necessary.
