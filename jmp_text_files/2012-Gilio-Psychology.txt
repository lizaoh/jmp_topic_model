Probabilistic analyses from a subjectivist, or Bayesian, point of view have been increasingly applied in cognitive psychology over recent years, and these analyses have led to a new  probabilistic paradigm  in the psychology of reasoning ( Chater & Oaksford, 2009; Over, 2009 ). Probability theory, and not extensional logic, supplies the computational level and formal theory of reasoning in the new paradigm ( Oaksford & Chater, 2007, 2009; Pfeifer & Kleiter, 2010 ). For example, suppose a doctor we trust says that the following disjunction about our son John is highly probable:

(1) John has measles or he has meningitis.

We might in this case infer that the following conditional is also highly probable:

(2) If John does not have measles then he has meningitis.

In this paper, we will argue that the inference of (2) from (1) is reasonably strong under the assumption that the doctor has general grounds for asserting (1). That is, she does not know that John has measles and infer (1) from that knowledge, but has inferred (1) from symptoms that point equally to measles and meningitis. In this kind of case, we shall say that the doctor has a  non-constructive  justification of (1).

In contrast, suppose that there has been a measles outbreak at John’s school, and he then gets a rash himself. We infer without going to the doctor that he probably has measles. From this conclusion, (1) immediately follows. That is, inferring  
                         A 
                       
                      or  
                      
                         B 
                       from  
                         A

Now if inferring (2) from (1)  were  also a logically valid inference, we could infer (2) directly from our belief that John has measles by the transitivity of logically valid inference. But that will be a totally unjustified inference given only the evidence that there is measles at his school and he has a rash. In this case, it will be more probable, for instance, to infer that John has a heat rash if he does not have measles. A heat rash is a quite a common condition, and meningitis is rare. If the  only  justification for asserting or believing (1) is a belief that John has measles, we shall say that (1) has a  constructive  justification. Our argument will be that inferring (2) from (1) is a weak inference when (1) has a constructive justification, but is a strong inference when (1) has a non-constructive justification.

We shall clarify the distinction between constructive and non-constructive justification much more fully in what follows. But note now what our response would be if we found that the only reason our doctor had for asserting (1) was her belief that John had measles and a desire to frighten us into paying for more medical tests. We would certainly feel that she had seriously misled us and lose confidence in the strength of the inference from (1) to (2). Our aim in this paper is to specify precisely when inferring  if not -

Recent work finds that the conditional probability response tends to increase as people make more and more judgments of this type, and this change tends to be from the conjunctive probability response to the conditional probability response ( Fugard et al., 2011 ). There is also a developmental trend. Yet another striking result is that people who give the conditional probability response, rather than the conjunctive one, are of higher cognitive ability, as measured by a standard intelligence test ( Evans, Handley, Neilens, & Over, 2007 ; see also ( Oberauer, Geiger, Fischer, & Weidenfeld, 2007; Politzer et al., 2010 ), on individual differences). One explanation of these results is that people of higher cognitive abilities, and at later developmental stages, are better able to process a conditional fully in a Ramsey test (Evans and Over, 2004).

Most justifications and inferences outside of pure mathematics, whether constructive or non-constructive, end in conclusions with some degree of probability that fails short of certainty. A simple example of this type can be easily given. Think of an actual pack of cards and a game that is being played with it, in which cards are laid on a table and tricks taken. Suppose that someone who has been watching the game states that the following disjunction is highly probable about a player’s hand:

(4) There is an Ace in the hand or there is a King in the hand.

The ordinary pragmatic presupposition would be that this speaker has a general reason for asserting (4) with high confidence, given the run of the cards, without a belief that one of the cards, the Ace or the King, is much more likely to be in the hand than the other. In this first kind of case, where there is a  non-constructive  probabilistic justification of (2), we argue that the following conditional is also quite probable:

(5) If there is not an Ace in the hand, there is a King in the hand.

It would be very misleading for (4) to be asserted merely because the speaker had caught sight, for a brief moment, of one of the player’s cards that looked like an Ace. Of course, people do sometimes make pragmatically misleading assertions, and the speaker in this second kind of case does have good reason to believe (4), which is grounded in perception and confidence in the left disjunct of (4). Here where is a  constructive  justification of (4), we argue that (5) could be quite improbable.

We have defined constructivity relative to a justification of a disjunction. A disjunction could of course have more than one justification, and one of these could be constructive and another non-constructive. We will leave consideration of more complex examples of multiple justifications for a single disjunction to future research. However, we would stress that the new probabilistic paradigm in the psychology of reasoning has a great advantage in being able to study epistemologically rich contexts in which probability judgments can be based on arguments, justifications, and evidence of many types and sources. We would also argue that out examples clearly suggest how experiments could be designed to investigate these contexts and test our proposals.

Most existing experimental research on the relation between disjunctions and conditionals does not manipulate justifications or probabilities (for the exception, see  Over et al., 2010 , with results in line with our analysis here).
