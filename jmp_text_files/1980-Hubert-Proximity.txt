Based on a set measure f (D) and a proximity function on S x S, two separate evaluation tasks,  referred to as confirmatory and exploratory, are considered. In a confirmatory task the subset D is identified a priori and f  (D) is compared against the distribution of f () over all subsets containing the same number of objects. The exploratory task,  on the other hand, treats f(-) as an objective function to be optimized over all subsets of a given size. Examples of these  two notions include the assessment of symmetry, cluster compactness, and the extent to which D satisfies the error-free  conditions for a hierarchical model or a unidimensional scale.  INTRODUCTION  Most data reduction procedures currently used in the behavioral sciences can be characterized very broadly as formal methods  for choosing a particular structure from some given class that will describe an available data set the "best." For example,  starting with a measure of proximity between objects from some set S, hierarchical clustering is concerned with locating an  ultrametric (Johnson, 1967), additive tree analysis searches for a function on S X S satisfying a certain four-point condition  (Sattath & Tversky, 1977; Cunningham, 1978), seriation is keyed to finding a matrix having what is called the Robinson pattern  (Kendall, 1971), and so on. These properties that distinguish the various data reduction techniques are algebraic and are  typically presented as defining characteristics of a final representation. Only in a very heuristic sense can these properties  also be used to describe data subject to error, since error-free conditions will usually fail even for a data set only  minimally different from some ideal characterization. It is reasonable to expect, however, that the "closer" a data set is to  satisfying a specific algebraic condition, the better will be the ultimate representation constructed by some appropriate  analysis technique. Some work is available in the scaling and clustering context on adequacy of representation, but unfortunately,  this literature is algorithm specific (e.g., Klahr, 1969; Baker & Hubert, 1976). Thus, for instance, no general techniques  have been proposed that could test the complex conjecture that a given proximity measure is really an ultrametric up to an  acceptable level of error, or even, for that matter, whether an arbitrary measure could be considered symmetric. It may be  obvious that statistical schemes for evaluating complete data sets and the degree to which they conform to a given algebraic  property are desirable, but at the same time, they would probably be very difficult to develop in any generally convincing  manner. In short, even though truly comprehensive methods would obviously be of value, we may be forced to design evaluation  strategies subject to very stringent constraints on the type of proximities and structures that are considered.  As an alternative to attacking the difficult task of assessing global structure, it is possible to evaluate variation in  structure within an object set if all the available data could be used to define an operational population (see Ramsay, 1978,  for a related discussion of conditional confidence regions in multidimensional scaling). Some of these ideas will be discussed  in the context of cluster analysis and several closely related areas, but as noted later, this evaluation strategy is not  limited to these specific problem types. It should become very apparent that the technique we present is general and could be  used with most data reduction schemes that have a combinatorial base or in which conditions for an error-free representation  are algebraic. As a cryptic summary, one of our major emphases will be on a confirmatory inference stategy for evaluating the  degree to which a subset of S satisfies some conjectured property. This latter property is defined in terms of the proximities  between objects within the given subset or from objects within the subset to objects outside. In particular, the reference  could be to some simple characteristic such as symmetry or to a more complex algebraic condition that is necessary for a  perfect representation in terms of some given structure (cf. Shepard, 1978).  Although our original motivation is from confirmatory hypotheses testing, our discussion does extend beyond the task of merely  observing variation in combinatorial structure. In particular, we include the related optimization problem of identifying  those parts of a proximity matrix, defined by subsets of S, that demonstrate the most extreme levels of the property being  assessed. These applications can be best labeled as "exploratory" as opposed to "confirmatory" and fall under the general  topic of combinatorial optimization.  BACKGROUND: JOHNSON'S SIMPLE CLUSTER STATISTIC  Confiirmatory Applications  As a convenient way to introduce the conceptual framework to be developed in some detail, we first review briefly the concept  of a simple cluster statistic developed in an unpublished paper by Johnson (1968). Although we will choose to work with  several different compactness measures in our later numerical examples, Johnson's work, as  ANALYZING PROXIMITY MATRICES 249  well as some related literature in graph theory (e.g., Bloemena, 1964), provide the historical bases for our discussion. For  this reason, Johnson's proposal is a natural place to begin.  Given an object set S = {Ol , 02 ,..., 0 and a proximity measure q(-, -) on S X S, each subset D C_ S of size K can be given  an index of compactness defined by the average of proximities within D minus the average of the proximities between D and S -  D. The major purpose of Johnson's original paper was to provide the mean and variance of this measure P under the assumption  that q(-, -) was symmetric and D was chosen at random from all subsets of size K. Consequently, the basic framework proposed  by Johnson can be used to develop a formal test of the a priori conjecture that a particular subset should display an unusual  degree of compactness as measured by the index 1', or for that matter, by any of a host of indices. Assuming that all subsets  of size K are equally likely and based either on a belief in asymptotic normality using the available mean and variance  parameters, or more convincingly, on complete enumeration over all (i') subsets of size K, we typically hope that an observed  index I' could be declared significant. In other words, if we wish to assert that it is very unlikely that the conjectured  subset was chosen at random, P should be sufficiently extreme with respect to an exact distribution of l', or possibly, to an  assumed normal approximation.  Summarizing, we are interested in some notion of compactness for a subset D C S that is operationalized through a particular  measure. The distribution of this index over all (it) possible subsets then serves as a reference distribution to evaluate the  size of the observed value of the measure for the subset D; thus, we would wish to reject at a very small significance level  the hypothesis that D was actually chosen by chance from the (ie) possible alternatives. As we will see, this same scheme of  index evaluation can be extended to several other data analysis areas that depend on specific underlying algebraic conditions  by merely varying the meaning of compactness and how it is measured. In all cases, the evaluation of the salience of a subset  with respect to a property assumes that the subset was identified independently of the given proximities, e.g., from theory or  possibly from a second data set. They key to the inference strategy itself lies in the fact that proximity data are assumed to  be available between all elements in S even though the initial conjecture relates to only a smaller subset. Using this broader  background information, a statistical evaluation can then be carried out by comparing the degree to which the property holds  in the given subset to what could be expected from other subsets of the same size that might have been formed from the larger  parent set S. (If some proximity data are missing, it is still possible to develop appropriate compactness measures if care is  taken to normalize each term of the chosen statistic by the number of proximities that are defined. In constructing the  reference distribution, however, these normalizations would have to vary from subset to subset.)  The heavy computational burden of complete enumeration or the problem of relying on a conjectured asymptotic normality can be  partially avoided by approximating the complete reference distribution using a random sample from it. Such a strategy is  rather well known and has been demonstrated in several related contexts, e.g., Cliff & Ord (1973), Hope (1968), and Hubert  (1979). In fact, a later example will provide a numerical illustration of this approximate testing procedure. Alternatively,  the first four moments  250 LAWRENCE J. There is one important cautionary note regarding this scheme of inference on subsets that should be stressed at the outset.  The comparison of a given index against the distribution obtained for all possible subsets of the same size implicitly  presupposes that some variation in the index is present. This variation, however, may be nonexistent for particular data sets  and/or for particular index definitions. In such cases, subset evaluation is meaningless and alternative analysis procedures  must be sought.  Exploratory Applications  Even though Johnson proposed his cluster statistic I' and its associated reference distribution as a way of evaluating the  compactness of a given subset D in a confirmatory manner, the statistic itself can also serve as an objective function in an  exploratory sense. Here, we would wish to identify those K-object clusters that optimize the index, and thus, our aim is to  locate those subsets that are the most compact with respect to the index I'. It is obvious that once a complete enumeration is  available, it would be a very simple matter to search through the list for the most extreme cases. If n is large, however, the  computational burden of complete enumeration may be so great that heuristic optimization techniques are of much more practical  importance, i.e., optimization strategies that do not guarantee optimality but which are powerful enough to provide  satisfactory results with reasonable computational efforts.  As one very representative heuristic strategy that will be used later, suppose we start with a randomly selected subset of  size K and consider all the K(n - K) possible alternatives of removing one object from the subset and adding a second from the  complementary set. Searching through the list of possibilities in some fixed way, we carry out the first identified  interchange that modifies the index I' in the appropriate direction (e.g., an interchange that decreases I') and repeat the  process with the newly formed subset. If this procedure is continued, a local optimum will eventually be achieved. Moreover,  given enough randomly selected starting points, most of the local optima should be located, and ideally, the global optimum or  optima would be present in this list as well.  Using the motivating background of Johnson's cluster statistic I', we now move to several uses of the subset compactness  notion, both in the exploratory and confirmatory contexts. To provide some continuity to the present discussion, however, the  first application is a simple variation on Johnson's original cluster statistic that deals with the concept of symmetry. In  this first example as well as later, the level at which some property holds for the subset D (for instance, compactness in  Johnson's sense) will be indexed by a function f (D). Thus, f (-) is a mapping from the set of all possible subsets formed  from S to the real numbers and will be used in measuring the salience of a subset with respect to a given property. In a  confirmatory context the subset D is typically identified by supplementary information and we wish to assess f (D) with  respect to all possible subsets of the same size; in the exploratory context, f(-) is merely used as an objective function to  be optimized over all possible subsets of size K. ANALYZING PROXIMITY MATRICES 255  EXAMPLE (C)-HIERARCHICAL CLUSTERING  In recent years, hierarchical clustering has become a very familiar data reduction strategy in the behavioral sciences as well  as an attractive topic for researchers interested in methodology. Briefly, the more common hierarchical clustering methods  construct a sequence of partitions for some given object set S based on a symmetric real-valued (proximity) function defined  on S X S. The hierarchy includes two trivial end pointsa partition that has all objects from S grouped together within one  single class and a second in which all objects are completely separated into distinct classes. Starting with this latter end  point, each partition in the hierarchy can be formed by uniting two or more object classes from the preceding partition in the  sequence. Since excellent summaries of the clustering literature already exist, the reader is merely referred to Hartigan  (1975) and Sneath & Sokal (1973) for background detail not presented here.  Assuming the desirability of hierarchical clustering, it may be important to evaluate variation in the adequacy of the model  for subsets of S. Specifically, we mention below the possible effects of "aberrant" objects on the adequacy of a hierarchical  representation and propose a method for trimming the set S before a hierarchical clustering algorithm is used. The latter may  be considered loosely analogous to a broad class of statistical techniques designed for the robust estimation of a population  parameter, and in particular, those methods that depend on eliminating extreme observations from a sample prior to the  calculation of an estimate. Here, objects are trimmed from the set to be clustered that do not conform to the dominant  structure imposed on the remaining objects. Consequently, if an appropriate subset measure could be defined for indexing the  closeness to a perfect hierarchical representation, the exploratory facet of our paradigm would appear to be a natural way to  approach the trimming problem.  Aberrant Objects  Whenever the general robustness properties of any statistical technique are discussed, one of the major areas of interest must  be the effect of aberrant or wild observations on the final results. In a similar manner, an assessment of robustness for a  particular hierarchical clustering scheme should be concerned with the effect of objects that do not fit the typical structure  represented by a majority of the other objects, assuming that such a majority structure is present. Most hierarchical  clustering procedures, unfortunately, are very sensitive to such outliers and may even try to capitalize on such data in  obtaining the mandatory sequence of partitions. Thus, for this special case, the measure indicates how far the binary relation  on D X D is from being an equivalence relation. DISCUSSION  It should be apparent that the possibility of different index specification is almost endless, and if we wish, the function f  (D) can be tailored to questions of importance in a particular substantive area. For instance, in geography when q(Oi , 0;)  refers to the number of individuals who move from place Oi to place 0; , large values of f (D) _ Lr0ti.D,0fS-D q(Oi , O;)  would denote regions of substantial out-migration, large values of f (D) = Y_1,..,-D,OicD q(Oi , O;) would be assigned to  regions of substantial in-migration, and the statistic f (D) = LO{CD,O1CS-D (q(Oi , Oj) - q(0; , Oi)) would index the degree  to which the subregion D loses more than it gains.  Other variations on our previous examples would replace three-object functions by four-object functions that could then be  used over all possible pairs of proximities and not only over those pairs that contain a common entity. In this way, the  four-point condition used in a perfect representation as an additive tree could be placed within our framework as well as the  four-object functions proposed by Johnson (1973) in his approach to nonmetric multidimensional scaling. In fact, f (D) could  be defined through some graph-theoretic property of the subset D or be based on a goodness-of-fit statistic calculated after  an optimization process is carried out on the subset D and the associated within subset proximities, e.g., some type of  scaling or a cluster analysis.  Irrespective of the specific index chosen or the given proximities, our confirmatory strategy allows an evaluation of the  relative size of an index for a given subset D. It is assumed that D is conjectured to have a large (or small) value of f (D)  based on considerations apart from an inspection of the available proximities. In arguing that this conjecture has some  credibility, we follow the same type of inference method that justifies the randomization approach to the two-independent  sample problem basic to nonparametric statistics (see Example (D)). Specifically, if f (D) can be shown unusually large (or  small) with respect to the distribution off(,) evaluated over all (equally likely) possible subsets having the same number of  objects as D, then technically, the hypothesis that D was randomly selected from this pool can be rejected. In turn, such a  rejection would lend support to the reasoning that went into the initial identification of D as being potentially unusual with  respect to the property being considered.  Although the various indices or their generalizations that could be developed on subsets have a natural confirmatory usage, it  is important to remember that they can always serve a dual exploratory purpose. In general, these optimization tasks can be  characterized in a very comprehensive combinatorial optimization paradigm known as quadratic assignment or its extensions to  cubic and quartic assignment. Unfortunately, the complexity of the optimization is such that some recent work in computer  science (on NP-completeness) suggests that "good" algorithms for finding optimal solutions are probably impossible to  construct. Obviously, because of this negative result, heuristic procedures of the type illustrated in our examples are of  great practical interest.
