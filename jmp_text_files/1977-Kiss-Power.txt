The theory is based on a few  simple assumptions and draws its conclusions from an analysis of limits on computational activity. These limits are a delay  limit and a processing power limit. The theory explains published findings on memory span, measurement of spatial numerosity  (and subitization), total-time effects, and the behavior of RT curves for the Sternberg item recognition experiment.  For at least 30 years computational models for cognition have been objects of serious investigation (McCulloch and Pitts,  1943). We continue in this spirit by developing a quantitative theory for cognition that has just a few simple assumptions and  yet explains many different empirical findings in the area of human performance (Fitts and Posner, 1967). For example, it  treats two of the three topics discussed by Miller (1956), namely, memory span and subitization, but also speaks to the nature  of performance curves in the Sternberg item recognition experiment (Sternberg, 1966) and to total-time effects in learning. The paper has three main divisions. In the first, an illustrative example is introduced and used as a vehicle for describing  many important ideas. We have labeled this didactic example the Stonemason Paradigm. The main points we make through it are  the introduction of delay and size complexities, computational work and power, the use of cyclic and acyclic machines for  carrying out tasks, and limitations on the execution times of tasks that are imposed by machines.  In the second main division we present a leisurely introduction to two computational models for mental activity. These are  logic circuits and sequential machines, which are acyclic and cyclic machines, respectively. We also present two computational  measures of the complexity of tasks and derive some of their properties. And finally, we develop computational limits on the  performance of tasks on computing machines.  The third major division of the paper is concerned with the psychological application of the computational ideas and with the  development of our theory. APPLICATIONS OF THE PROCESSING POWER CONCEPT IN COGNITION  Having outlined a set of concepts which provide a framework for a computational approach to cognition, we now turn to a  detailed discussion of the psychological aspects of our theory. We show that this computational approach leads to a  parsimonious, yet broadly applicable theory, encompassing many aspects of cognition.  Since our theory is concerned chiefly with the constraints that apply to the performance of tasks by humans, it is expected to  be relevant to most topics traditionally treated in the human performance area of psychology, i.e., attention, memory,  reaction time, and the like.  Limitations on human performance often manifest themselves in the existence of a span, that is to say a limiting value of some  performance index. Common examples are the memory span, usually defined as a length of the longest list of items that can be  recalled with a probability of 0.5 (or in some cases 1.0), and the attention span defined as the largest number of objects one  can simultaneously attend to (Hamilton, 1859; James, 1890). The idea of attention span has inspired a number of studies of the  phenomenon of subitizing (for reviews see Woodworth and Schlosberg, 1954; Klahr, 1973).  George Miller, in his seminal paper on the "Magical number seven, plus or minus two" (Miller, 1956) recognized the existence  of such limits in memory span, subitizing, and absolute judgment and attempted to construct a unified explanation on the basis  of information measurement. That attempt has failed and Miller has preferred to "withhold judgment. Perhaps there is something  deep and profound behind all these sevens, something just calling out for us to discover it."  We show that limits on memory span and subitizing can be given a unified interpretation using the theory proposed in this  paper. In particular, we interpret the limits as the points where the computing power delivered to the task first reaches the  power the mental computer can provide.  It is also shown that the theory accounts for some challenging relationships between memory span and other indices of  performance like reading rate.  Finally we demonstrate that the different segments of a performance curve and the changeover between them provide a uniform  explanation for the variety of outcomes which have been observed in the Sternberg (1966) item recognition type experiments.  The Limited-Power Postulate  As argued in the previous sections, we wish to represent mental activity as computation, directed at the evaluation of  functions. These functions represent the tasks set to subjects in cognitive psychology experiments, or those carried out in  everyday life.  In order to fix ideas, we assume that the computations are carried out by a machine which is functionally equivalent to the  human system. By this functional equivalence  48o/i6/i-6  80 KISS AND SAVAGE  we mean that the input-output mapping realized by the machine is identical to that of the human system, down to a certain  level of detail. The level of detail is dependent on the nature of the application. Our theory is neutral with respect to the  physical realization of the machine in the real human system. For this reason our theory could find application in both the  physiological and psychological areas, although in this paper we deal exclusively with the psychological domain.  We thus speak of "the mental computer" and whenever we do, we mean the functionally equivalent idealized computing machine  which, for the task in hand, could replace the human system.  Our theory does not make strong assumptions about the structure of the human cognitive system. Nevertheless, the reader might  wish to form an image of an assembly of cooperating computing machines, some logic circuits, some sequential machines,  utilizing various forms of memory to store information. We are intentionally not explicit about the nature and specialization  of these machines. The usual division into input (sensory, perceptual), output (motor), and central processors suits our  purposes well.  Among the various forms of memory which are available to such a system we wish to make an essential distinction between static  and dynamic memory. The idea could also be well captured by the volatile versus nonvolatile distinction. Static memory holds  information indefinitely without requiring processing work, while dynamic memory holds information only for a fixed duration  unless work is done to refresh it periodically. The reader should note that with this distinction we take sides in the decay  or interference issue, committing ourselves to a decay interpretation of forgetting.  Our fundamental postulate can now be stated as follows: The processing power of the mental computer is limited. This statement  needs to be qualified in three ways. First, the power limit may undergo short-term fluctuations due to motivational and  physiological influences. Second, the power limit is subject to short- or long-term change by learning processes which can  cause structural changes in the machine, making it better adapted to the task performed. Third, the power limit can be task  dependent due to a mismatch between task and computer, as outlined in the two preceding sections. Subject to these  qualifications, the postulate implies that the rate at which the mental computer can do work on a task cannot exceed some  finite limit.  Our strategy now is to apply this fundamental postulate to a number of well-known phenomena in cognition. First let us look in more detail at the characteristics of dynamic memory which we assume requires processing work in order to  preserve its volatile contents. We adopt the commonly made assumption that memory traces have a strength which decays with  time. The applications treated in this paper do not require us to postulate any particular decay function, although in other  applications this may become necessary. We simply assume that a memory trace remains useable for p sec and disappears after  this time unless refreshed. The idea of rehearsal, widely used in the literature, is a clear instance of what we mean by the  refresh operation. Over a period of t sec the memory trace thus has to be refreshed t/p times. As before, we assume that a  memory trace for an item in a memory experiment consists of b binary features. Each of these features requires a certain  amount of work ar to be performed in order to refresh it. Since there are b features, the total work spent in refreshing an  item is arb, and the work required to keep it alive over t seconds is arbt/p. CONCLUSIONS  We have presented a computational theory for human cognitive performance which is characterized by a small number of  assumptions. A consequence of our theory is that cognition occurs under two restrictions, a delay limit and a processing power  limit. We  LIMITS ON HUMAN PERFORMANCE  89  have demonstrated that the combination of the two limits provides a qualitative explanation for RT curves in the Sternberg  item recognition experiment and in the measurement of spatial numerosity. Furthermore, an excellent log-linear fit is shown  for published data on the Sternberg experiment.  The processing power limit and a few simple assumptions about the amount of work necessary to input and output items and to  refresh dynamic memory have been shown to be sufficient to explain short term memory span and limits on reading rate and to  obtain a good fit with published data on memory span, and rate-span relationships. Adding an assumption on the amount of work  needed to place items in static memory, we have developed a good qualitative explanation for total-time effects in learning.  The fact that our theory has few basic assumptions and yet broad scope suggests that many other findings in cognitive  psychology can be understood using it. We are currently investigating attention, choice reaction time, error-speed tradeoffs,  serial position effects, and same and difference findings in the context of this theory.  REFERENCES  BowER, G.
