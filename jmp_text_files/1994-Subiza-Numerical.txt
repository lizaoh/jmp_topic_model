Numerical Representation of Acyclic Preferences* 

BEGONA SUBIZA 

University of Alicante, Alicante, Spain 

In this paper it is shown that, under certain conditions on a preference relation defined on a set X, there exists a numerical representation for this relation by means of set-valued real functions. This kind of representation extends both the usual utility function and the representation by means of two real functions. When the relation is defined on a topological space, the continuity of this representation is also discussed. © 1994 Academic Press, Inc. 

1, INTRODUCTION 

A classical result on representations of binary relations is that of Ellenberg (1941) which proves that a continuous preorder > is representable by means of a real function (utility function) in such a way that x > y if and only if u(x) > u(y). Such a representation is useful because it gives us complete information about the binary relation and it is easier to work with a real function (for example, we can transfer the problem of getting maximal elements of the relation to the easier problem of getting maxima of a rea! function). 

In a similar way, when the binary relation is an interval order, several authors (e.g., Fishburn, 1973; Bridges, 1983; Doignon, Ducamp and Falmagne, 1984; Chateauneuf, 1987) study a more general numerical representation by means of two functions (x > y if and only if w(x) > v{))). 

When the binary relation is acyclic such representations do not exist and only “weak” numerical representations can be found (for instance, a real function u(.) such that x > y implies u(x) > u(y); see Peleg, 1970). But this kind of representation does not give complete information about the binary relation. 

The purpose of this paper is to provide a new numerical representation by means of set-valued real functions {utility correspondences) for acyclic preference relations. The utility correspondence is a generalization of the utility function as well as the representation by means of two real functions.

The former result is not necessarily true when ¥ is an uncountable set: the lexicographical order in R x {0, 1} is a preorder, therefore it is an acyclic preference relation; but since this relation has no indifferent elements, in the case whereby a utility correspondence exists, its supremum function must be a utility function. However, it is well known that, for this preference relation, no utility function can be constructed. 

Therefore additional conditions are needed in order to extend the result from the countable case to the uncountable one. Furthermore, when the preference relation is defined on a topological space, it is useful to find conditions on the continuity of the supremum function. 

In order to get the existence and continuity of utility functions and representations by means of two functions for preorders or interval-orders on topological spaces, two different types of conditions are used in the literature. FINAL REMARKS 

This paper provides a numerical representation for acyclic relations which is a generalization of the utility function as well as of the representation by means of two real functions. 

The results presented on the existence of utility correspondence are completely parallel to those of the other known numerical representations. First, when X is a

countable set, the existence of a utility correspondence is equivalent to the relation being acyclic (Propostion 1), in the same way as the existence of a utility function is equivalent to the relation being a preorder (Cantor, 1895), and representations by means of two functions are equivalent to the relation being an interval order (Fishburn, 1970), Second, if X¥ is a topological space, in order to get the existence and continuity of the utility correspondence, conditions on continuity and separability of the preference relation are needed, as well as in the case of preorders and interval orders (e.g., Eilenberg, 1941; Bridges, 1983; Chateauneuf, 1987). 

On the other hand, when a preference relation admits a utility correspondence, the supremum function is a numerical representation like the one defined in Peleg’s theorem (Theorem 3). Thus we can ensure the existence of such a “weak” representation in all the cases in which the utility correspondence exists. In particular, Theorem 4 is a generalization of Peleg’s result, since, if the relation is a partial order, it is acyclic and >> = >.
