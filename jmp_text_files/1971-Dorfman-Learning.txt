JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 8, 264-284 (1971)  A Learning Model for a Continuum of Sensory States'  DONALD D. DORFMAN AND MICHAEL BIDERMAN  The University of Iowa, Iowa City, Iowa 52240  This paper investigated a learning model for signal detection which assumes a continuum of sensory states. The model, a  generalization of a model originally proposed by Kac (1962, 1969), and selected special cases of this model were evaluated in  a 2AFC signal detection task. The general model and its associated special models are quite tractable from the point of view  of parameter estimation, and the axioms of the models generate tractable functions for the theoretical probabilities on each  trial. Of the special models evaluated, the Kac error-correction model gave the poorest fit on the average.  Signal detection theory is generally assumed to have a static decision process (e.g., see Green and Swets, 1966). However,  there is substantial evidence that the observer's behavior is not static but dynamic (Atkinson, Carterette, and Kinchla, 1962;  Atkinson and Kinchla, 1965; Friedman, Carterette, Nakatani, and Ahumada, 1968). Learning models have been introduced to  account for these dynamic effects in signal detection, but these models have not generally assumed a continuum of sensory  states. For instance, Atkinson and Kinchla (1965) have developed a learning model for forcedchoice signal detection in which  they assumed three sensory states, and a decision process determined by a Bush-Mosteller learning model (Bush and Mosteller,  1955). Similarly, Friedman et al. (1968) devised a set of learning models for yes-no signal detection in which they also  assumed three sensory states, and the Bush-Mosteller learning model for the decision process.  Signal detection theory has had wide application in sensory psychophysics. Since a continuum of sensory states is assumed in  signal detection theory, it would be of great value to investigate learning models which are appropriate for a continuum of  sensory states. Although learning models have been proposed for a continuum of sensory states (Kac, 1962; Sklansky, 1963;  Schoeffier, 1965), there has been no attempt to fit such models to signal detection data. It appears that such models have not  been investigated, because it is generally assumed that they are mathematically intractable (Green and Swets, 1966; Luce,  1964). The major goal of the present paper is to investigate a learning model for a continuum of sensory states, which is  tractable from  1 This research was supported in part by a grant from the National Science Foundation, GS-1466.  264  CONTINUOUS SENSORY STATE LEARNING MODEL 265  the point of view of parameter estimation, and whose axioms generate tractable functions for the theoretical probabilities on  each trial.  The model to be proposed and investigated is a generalization of a model originally put forward by Kac (1962, 1969). As in  signal detection theory, Kac assumed a continuum of sensory states, and a decision threshold or cutoff. Whereas in signal  detection theory, a fixed cutoff is assumed, Kac assumed that the cutoff changed when an error was made. Specifically, he  assumed that the cutoff increased by a fixed constant if the observer erred when a noise was presented, and decreased by a  fixed constant if the observer erred when a signal was presented. His model may be characterized as a random walk with  infinitely many states (Kac, 1962). Since there are four possible events in a signal detection experiment-an event for each  cell of the payoff matrix-it seems reasonable to assume a more general model, one having a parameter for each of these four  events. Any special model of this general model can also be investigated, and tested against the general model by means of a  likelihood ratio test (Kendall and Stuart, 1961). There are a variety of numerical methods available for the maximization of the above likelihood function (e.g., see Hooke and  Jeeves, 1961; Kelly, 1967; Traub, 1964; Rao, 1952, 1965). We employ a mixed strategy (Traub, 1964) involving both the method  of scoring (Rao, 1952), and a slight modification of the pattern search procedure of Hooke and Jeeves (1961). The method of  scoring is an adaptation of the NewtonRaphson method in which the expected values of the second partial derivatives are used,  and has been successful elsewhere (Dorfman and Alf, 1968, 1969; Ogilvie and Creelman, 1968). Exploration with various  procedures for maximizing the likelihood function presented in (6) revealed that the mixed strategy was the most efficient  procedure. It produced very rapid convergence in all of the cases studied. Some details on the speed of convergence for this  procedure will be discussed later in this paper, as it relates to the particular experimental data reported.  The mixed strategy used here involves iterating with the method of scoring until either the criterion for maximization is  achieved or a "failure" occurs. Maximization is achieved when the largest change in the estimates on successive iterations is  less than 5 X 10-5. A "failure" occurs when the value of logL on any iteration plus a small constant, is less than logL on the  preceding iteration. When a "failure" occurs, the pattern-search procedure is used for a fixed number of iterations, and then  the method of scoring is re-employed. This alternation of methods continues until the criterion of maximization is achieved.  Exploration with separate use of the method of scoring and the pattern-search procedure revealed that both procedures always  converge to the same solution within an acceptable margin of error. The mixed strategy, however, substantially increases the  speed of convergence, and ensures convergence with poor initial estimates.  APPLICATION OF THE MODEL  It was decided to initially apply the model and its associated special models to the two-alternative forced choice task (2AFC)  where a priori probability of an interval occurrence was manipulated. In a future paper, the yes-no task will be investigated.  The 2AFC task was chosen in this initial investigation because it would appear to require the fewest number of parameters to  be estimated from the data. Such is the case for two reasons. First, the 2AFC task appears to be less prone to a response  preference than the yes-no task. For instance, Friedman et al. (1968) obtained a strong response preference or bias in favor  of the "no" response in the yes-no signal detection task. A review of the data of the 2AFC experiments shows no evidence of a  response preference in favor of one of the two intervals (see Atkinson, Carterette, and Kinchla, 1962, 1964; Atkinson and  Kinchla, 1965; Carterette, Friedman, and  CONTINUOUS SENSORY STATE LEARNING MODEL 269  Wyman, 1966). Secondly, the 2AFC task has fewer sensory parameters to be estimated from the data since the density functions  for the difference distributions are theoretically identical except for a location parameter (see Green and Swets, 1966, p.  68). Consequently, the ratio of the variances of the two density functions is theoretically equal to one (Green and Swets,  1966). In the yes-no task, this variance ratio must be estimated from the data.  EXPERIMENT  Method  Design. A 2AFC acoustic simple detection task was employed with noise continuously present throughout the experimental  sessions. The subject was instructed to report by pressing the appropriate button, in which of two 100 msec observation  intervals a 1000 Hz sinusoidal tone was presented. The probability that the signal appeared in interval one was manipulated.  Call this probability, P(S,). Three probabilities were used, P(Sr) = 0.1, 0.5, and 0.9. There were three test sessions of 400  trials each, with each test session on a different day, and each test session contained a different P(S.). The temporal  sequence of P(S,)'s was varied across subjects. Correct feedback was provided on all trials.  Apparatus. The experiment was controlled automatically by a Grason-Stadler Modular Programming System (Series 1200), and the  stimulus and response events were recorded on paper tape by a BRS tape punch. Subjects were run individually in a soundproof  booth (IAC, Model 1202-A). The tone was generated by a Hewlett-Packard Function Generator (Model 3300-A), and was gated with a  Grason-Stadler electronic switch set for equal rise and decay times of 25 msec. The white background noise was band-pass  filtered between 125 and 4000 Hz with a Kronhite Filter (Model 314). Subjects listened monaurally through one phone of a  PDR-10 headphone set.  Procedure. Subjects were run individually. They were seated in front of a panel containing a red warning light, a yellow  observation-interval light and two green feedback lights, one for interval one, and one for interval two. Each subject  participated in five experimental sessions, two training sessions, and three test sessions. In all five sessions, the simple  2AFC detection procedure was used with a 1000 Hz tone serving as signal. The first training session consisted of the  presentation of five different levels of EINO in an arbitrary order, each level of EINO being presented for a continuous block  of 100 trials. The P(SI) equalled 0.5. The psychometric function obtained from this training session was used to choose a  level of EINO for the remaining four sessions. The EIND was chosen so as to give approximately 76 % correct responses in the  2AFC task for a d' of about one. The second training session was identical in procedure to the three test sessions with P(Sl)  equal to 0.5. Correct feedback was provided on all trials of the training sessions as well as the test sessions. Before each  of the experimental sessions, there were five 0.5 sec previews of the tone in noise at a signal level substantially higher  than the level used in the test sessions. In the final four experimental sessions, the tone was previewed at a level exactly  10 db higher than that used in the experimental session.  Each trial contained the following sequence of events: a 0.1 sec warning light, a 0.5 sec delay, a 0.1 sec observation  interval, a 0.5 sec delay, a 0.1 sec second observation interval, a 2.5 sec response interval, a 1 sec feedback light, and a 1  sec intertrial interval. For three of the subjects, the warning light was 0.5 sec in duration. The subject was instructed to  respond after the termination of the second observation interval. Procedures developed  for maximum-likelihood estimation of sensitivity make this static assumption (Dorfman and Alf, 1968; Ogilvie and Creelman,  1968). It has been pointed out that these dynamic effects depress estimates of sensitivity (e.g., see Green and Swets, 1966,  p. 411). In principle, if Model 4 is a reasonably  CONTINUOUS SENSORY STATE LEARNING MODEL 281  accurate characterization of this dynamic process, then it should provide an estimate of sensitivity independent of dynamic  effects. With this consideration in mind, maximum-likelihood estimates of sensitivity were obtained assuming a static  criterion, and these estimates of sensitivity were compared with the estimates of sensitivity obtained from Model 4, the most  accurate model. The average sensitivity of the fifteen subjects was 1.62 as estimated from Model 4, and 1.54 as estimated from  the static decision model. Estimates of sensitivity were larger for Model 4 in all 15 cases, and the largest difference was  0.161. The correlation between the two estimates of sensitivity was 0.991. As expected, a t-test showed that the mean  sensitivity was significantly greater for the learning model than for the static model (t = 6.89, p < 0.001, df = 14). Efficiency of the Maximization Procedure  As mentioned in the introduction, a mixed strategy was employed, the two strategies being the method of scoring (Rao, 1952),  and a pattern-search procedure (Hooke and Jeeves, 1961). We have found, however, that given a reasonable initial estimate of  a, e.g., a = 1.50, and di; = 0 as arbitrary initial estimates for all dii , convergence to the solution always occurred  directly with the method of scoring, with no intervention of the pattern-search subroutine. With these initial estimates, the  method of scoring produced convergence with all fifteen subjects, and all models. Also, exploration with a wide variety of  initial estimates showed that irrespective of the initial estimates used, the same solution was always obtained within an  acceptable margin of error. The initial estimates only affected the rate of convergence. For instance, in an investigation on  Model 1, eleven different vectors of initial estimates were used with each subject,  282 DORFMAN AND BIDERMAN  including the maximum-likelihood estimates obtained from Model 4 as initial estimates of the three parameters. Evaluation of Model 4  The most powerful method for evaluating the goodness of fit of a model is the likelihood ratio method. With the likelihood  ratio method, a model is always evaluated against a more general model. The most general model for this experimental situation  has a parameter, P,(R;,, I Sin), for each trial. Since only one observation is available on each trial, this model cannot be  used. Another approach would be to propose a somewhat more general model than Model 4, and evaluate Model 4 against this  model. Since Model 4 already has five parameters (four learning parameters and one sensitivity parameter) to be estimated from  the data, it is questionable whether such a procedure would be particularly fruitful at this stage of investigation. A more  fruitful strategy would be to compare the fit of Model 4 with the fit of other learning models with a similar number of  parameters. For instance, Atkinson, Bower, and Crothers (1965) have suggested a five parameter learning model (also four  learning parameters and one sensitivity parameter) as a natural extension of the learning model proposed by Atkinson and  Kinchla (1965). It would be quite useful to compare this model, and other such models with Model 4. Comparison of Model 4 with  other models will be the subject of a future investigation.  CONCLUSION  The models investigated in this paper are identical to signal detection theory with respect to the sensory assumptions in that  they assume a continuum of sensory states. With respect to the decision process, they are quite different. The decision  process of signal detection theory is based upon statistical decision theory. Within this framework, the decisionmaker in a  signal-detection task is assumed to divide a likelihoodratio continuum into two subsets, and bases his decisions upon this  partition. In contrast, the present models assume that the subject changes his criterion as a function of the trial-by-trial  results of his decisions. The data reviewed earlier (e.g., Atkinson  CONTINUOUS SENSORY STATE LEARNING MODEL 283  et al., 1965; Friedman et al., 1968), and the data presented in this paper would appear to give greater support to a dynamic  decision process than to the static decision process customarily assumed in signal detection theory.  Finally, it should be noted that Model 4 and its associated special models specify a group of nonlinear-operator models (e.g.,  see Luce, 1959, for a discussion of some nonlinear learning models). Given that one stimulus is used during the experiment,  i.e., Sl = S2 , then we have a set of possible nonlinear-operator learning models as an alternative to the classical  linear-operator learning models (e.g., see Bush and Mosteller, 1955). Since the nonlinear models studied in this paper are  quite tractable from the point of view of parameter estimation, they should merit consideration apart from their possible  application to problems in signal detection.
