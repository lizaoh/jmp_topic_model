BOCKENHOLT  The University of Illinois  A class of logistic models for analyzing multivariate paired-comparison experiments with and without ties is proposed. These  models facilitate fitting of the Bradley-Terry-Luce model or the Davidson model to more than one response variable, and they  model the association structure among the multiple response variables. Additional design-related effects, such as order  effects within pairs or group treatment effects, may be tested. Parameters of the models may be expressed as linear functions  of concomitant variables. The approach is exemplified by analyzing data from two consumer preference experiments. f 1988  Academic Press, Inc.  Important contributions to paired-comparison methodology have been made in experimental design, nonparametric statistics, and,  more recently, multidimensional scaling (for an overview see (Bradley, 1984; I. Bockenholt & Gaul, 1986)). In particular,  multidimensional scaling models for analyzing pairwise choice data have been suggested by De Soete, Carroll, and DeSarbo  (1986), DeSarbo, Oliver, and De Soete (1986), DeSarbo, De Soete, and Jedidi (1987), and I. Bockenholt and Gaul (1986). By  rendering a spatial representation of individuals and stimuli, these models account for the potentially multidimensional  nature of stimuli derived from the analysis of univariate preference judgments.  In contrast, this article focuses on methods for analyzing multivariate judgments in paired-comparison experiments (Davidson  and Bradley, 1969; Fienberg and Larntz, 1976; Davoodzadeh and Beaver, 1983; Beaver, 1983). Multivariate preference judgments  are commonly obtained, for example, in large scale consumer studies. In these studies it is customary to ask for preference  responses to specific object attributes such as aftertaste, sweetness, or color in the case of soft drinks. We may analyze  each set of paired-comparison data corresponding to a single object attribute by applying one of the many paired-comparison  models that are available. However, such an approach neglects the relationships among the different preference judgments. In  particular, for rich stimuli it may be very useful to model these relationships in order to understand the perceptual  processes underlying choice behavior. LOGIT REPRESENTATION 45  The paired-comparison model suggested by Bradley and Terry (1952) and Luce (1959) is adopted for providing a unidimensional  representation of the (aggregated) paired-comparison data for each of the multiple responses. This Bradley-Terry-Luce (BTL)  model has received considerable attention over the past few years in connection with the analysis of categorical data.  Log-linear representations of the BTL model and the various extensions of it have been suggested by Fienberg and Larntz  (1976), Fienberg (1979), Koehler and Ridpath (1982), van Putten (1982), and Beaver (1983). These modifications of the BTL  model have profited from the more general work on log-linear models. As a result, estimation and test procedures based on  straightforward maximum likelihood methods can be applied.  This paper complements these approaches by proposing a parsimonious and flexible class of logistic models for analyzing  multivariate paired-comparison experiments. A logistic formulation is advantageous when relationships between dependent or  response variables and independent or explanatory variables are considered. The flexibility of the suggested logistic  regression approach and its numerical efficiency in comparison to a log-linear representation becomes apparent when ordered or  continuous explanatory variables are taken into account in an "external" analysis of the data (cf. Cooper & Nakanishi, 1983).  Moreover, the logistic model parameterization is straightforward and involves no new derivations or proofs. Some new models  and corresponding test procedures are presented, however, that include previously suggested log-linear representations as  special cases.  A modified version of the multinomial response model introduced by Bock (1970, 1975) is used as a general framework for  deriving various paired-comparison models. Within this framework we present several multivariate paired-comparison  formulations which reduce to the corresponding univariate models when only one response variable is considered. These  formulations are extensions of multivariate paired-comparison models suggested by Davidson and Bradley (1969), Fienberg and  Larntz (1976), Davoodzadeh and Beaver (1982), and Beaver (1983). The simple structure of the suggested response models  facilitates both straightforward testing of the effects of additional design-related variables, such as within-pair order  effects (Davidson & Beaver, 1977), and/or analyzing experiments in which pairedcomparison scale parameters are some linear  function of a set of concomitant variables (Cooper & Nakanishi, 1983; Kousgaard, 1984; Mattenklott, Sehr, & Miescke, 1982).  For situations which allow an expression of no preference, the model proposed by Davidson (1970) is applied. In addition,  various new ways of modeling relationships among the response variables are suggested.  1. MODELING ASSOCIATIONS AMONG RESPONSE VARIABLES  Models for analyzing associations between responses in multivariate paired-comparison experiments have been first suggested by  Davidson and Bradley (1969, 1970), and later extended by Fienberg and Larntz (1976), Davoodzadeh and Beaver (1982), and Beaver  (1983). What these approaches have in common is that the model structure is separated into two components; one for fitting the  BTL, the Rao-Kupper, or the Davidson model (Rao and Kupper, 1967; Davidson, 1970) to each response variable under  independence, and one for modeling the association structure among the p response variables. Specifically, Pearson's 02  coefficient and the log-odds ratio have been suggested for describing the association between two attributes. An important  assumption of these models is that the association between two attributes is the same for all paired comparisons. In the  presented logistic framework we first relax this assumption in order to account for possible associations between attributes  in each pairwise comparison and test later whether it is appropriate to constrain the association coefficients to be equal for  all object pairs. An overall satisfactory fit of a model is, of course, only a first step when  analyzing the data. Usually, additional detailed tests are necessary for models with a large number of parameters before one  can conclude that the selected model provides an appropriate representation of the data. In particular, for multivariate  paired-comparison models overall LR tests are not always diagnostic in detecting systematic deviations of the model from the  data.  One of the implications of the BTL model is that strong stochastic transitivity  LOGIT REPRESENTATION SS  (SST) must be satisfied; that is, if p(a --> b) z and p(b -> c) > z then p(a -> c) max { p(a --> b), p(b - c) }. Violations of  SST lead usually to significant LR statistics for univariate responses. The identification of a particular object, or object  pair, is then easily facilitated by the tests presented above. However, the overall LIZ test for multivariate  paired-comparison models is less diagnostic for identifying violations of SST. Suppose, for example, we analyze  paired-comparison data based on two responses. The BTL model may provide an exceptionally good fit for one response and a  mediocre to poor fit for the second response variable. As a result, the overall fit for both responses may be fairly  satisfactory, thus masking the poor fit of the second response variable.  Another example for the potentially insufficient diagnosticity of the overall LIZ test is related to the fit of the  associations among the response variables. When the assumption of equal associations among the responses is justified, we may  reduce the number of parameters for a two-variable experiment, for instance, from n to 1, where n is the number of paired  comparisons. Moreover, when the relationships between the responses are well represented by a single association coefficient,  the gained n - 1 degrees of freedom for the overall fit of the model may mask systematic deviations of the BTL model part from  the paired-comparison data. In general, we may thus conclude that detailed separate tests of the BTL part, the association  part, and other parts of the multivariate model structure are useful even when the overall fit is quite satisfactory. VIII. DISCUSSION  The presented class of logistic models provides a flexible and computationally convenient approach for analyzing multivariate  paired-comparison judgments of the form commonly made in choice situations outside the laboratory (e.g., purchas-  LOGIT REPRESENTATION 61  ing decisions). We have shown how to incorporate the BTL model or the Davidson model for each response variable, how to model  the association structure among the response variables, and how to test additional design-related effects that may influence  preference behavior. However, many more applications of this multivariate logistic framework are possible. For example, the  BTL model with ordered responses (Tutz, 1986) and its special case, the Rao-Kupper model (Rao & Kupper, 1967), can be  represented with the proposed logistic framework as well.  It is well known that the BTL model does not provide an appropriate representation of the data if strong stochastic  transitivity (SST) is violated. Violations of SST typically result when information processing patterns vary among paired  comparisons. Judges may, for example, consider several attributes simultaneously when choosing between two objects on the  basis of a multifaceted criterion like acceptance, or attractiveness. Hence, one attribute may receive greater weight in one  comparison of a pair of objects than in another; as a result, intransitivities which considerably worsen the fit of the model  will occur (see (Tversky, 1969; Jackson, 1970)). By asking for a comparison on each attribute separately, we may both reduce  the likelihood of this effect and minimize the occurrence of these kinds of intransitivities.  Thus, the BTL model may be more appropriate for analyzing paired-comparison data when preference responses to some specific  object attributes are obtained. In addition, the association coefficients provide useful information about the underlying  structure among these response vaiables. Moreover, analyzing the relationship among the attributes may help to explain why an  overall preference judgment cannot be represented by a BTL model. This approach is limited, of course, by fatigue effects that  may occur when too many attributes are evaluated. In general, the multivariate paired-comparison models presented here offer a  complementary approach to more complex scaling procedures. They simplify the judgmental task and compare favorably in terms of  the assumptions required.  The approach of separating the multivariate model structure into two components, one for fitting a paired-comparison model to  each response variable under independence, and one for modeling the association structure among the response variables, is not  limited to the BTL framework. Alternative pairedcomparison models such as unfolding models (see, e.g., (Sixtl, 1973)),  Thurstonian models (Bock & Jones, 1968), more recently developed multidimensional pairedcomparison models (De Soete, Carroll,  & DeSarbo, 1986; DeSarbo, Oliver, & De Soete, 1986; DeSarbo, De Soete, & Jedidi, 1987; 1. Bockenholt & Gaul, 1986), and models  based on an analysis of covariance structures (Takane, 1987; Bloxom, 1972) can represent the data more appropriately since  most of these models do not require strong stochastic transitivity, and/or make provisions for modeling individual difference  components. Likewise, for experiments which allow for ordered preference responses, we may apply graded paired-comparison  models (see, e.g., (Tutz, 1986) ).  It thus seems promising for further research to extend these paired-comparison models in order to account for the important  information about the relations  62 U. Moreover, the possibility of including information about  the alternatives in choice models provides valuable additional tests necessary for understanding the perceptual processes  which govern choice.
