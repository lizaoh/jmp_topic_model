This article develops a general purpose mathematical framework for modeling context-dependent choice behavior, an empirical paradigm with a long tradition in psychology. While flexible, the proposed framework can be leveraged to make precise predictions about choice behavior, including predictions that are more parsimonious than those of traditional models. Its mathematical precision not only allows the framework to accommodate well-known empirical phenomena, but also to dissect theoretical nuances, and to derive novel empirical predictions about  how  choice behavior depends on context.

A sizeable experimental literature documents that people sometimes behave like Charlie and violate regularity (e.g.,  Huber, Payne, & Puto, 1982, 2014; Simonson, 1989; Trueblood, Brown, & Heathcote, 2014; Tversky & Simonson, 1993 ). This has led many researchers to conclude that a class of models known as random utility models cannot adequately describe choice behavior: standard formulations of these models imply regularity.

In this article, we show how standard random utility models can readily be generalized to allow for violations of regularity. We also show how generalizing the models is not synonymous with relaxing them: The resulting random utility models can be context-dependent, violate regularity, and nonetheless be highly parsimonious. On the basis of simple examples that draw on the asymmetric dominance paradigm ( Huber et al., 1982 ), we demonstrate how to construct context-dependent random utility models. Casting these models as context-dependent random preference models, we explain how to characterize them mathematically. We also report an illustrative experiment to show how to assess the theoretical parsimony and empirical goodness-of-fit of such models, and how to compare their descriptive performance. In sum, we provide a general framework for constructing, characterizing, and testing context-dependent random utility models.

The most well-known evidence for violations of regularity comes from experimental studies of ‘context effects,’ which suggest that the attractiveness of choice options may depend on the configuration of the choice set. That is, a particular option may appear more or less attractive depending on the available alternatives. Three context effects have famously proven to replicate across a variety of decision settings: the attraction ( Huber et al., 1982 ), compromise ( Simonson, 1989 ), and similarity effects ( Tversky, 1972 ). We focus on the attraction effect, which directly yields regularity violations, as a case in point. 
                         1 
                      
                      
                         1 
                         The similarity effect, while a context effect, does not constitute a violation of regularity, and whether a compromise effect violates regularity depends on the exact paradigm. An attraction effect represents a regularity violation in all standard paradigms used to study the effect.

Experimental studies of the attraction effect usually employ the asymmetric dominance paradigm ( Huber et al., 1982 ). The paradigm features a core choice set with two options, neither of which dominates the other. When a “decoy option” – which is similar to and clearly dominated by just one of the core options – is added to the set, choice proportions shift in the direction of the “target” option that asymmetrically dominates it, leading to violations of regularity. This finding has received much attention because of the challenges it raises for standard formulations of utility and rational choice more generally.

A sizeable experimental literature documents attraction effects in a variety of domains. Typical studies involve hypothetical consumer products, but the effect has also been demonstrated in choices between perceptual stimuli ( Trueblood, Brown, Heathcote, & Busemeyer, 2013 ), and even in studies of animal behavior ( Latty & Beekman, 2011; Lea & Ryan, 2015; Shafir, Waite, & Smith, 2002 ). Moreover, researchers have found the effect in products ranging from TV sets to paper towels, and both in between-subject (e.g.,  Frederick, Lee, & Baskin, 2014; Huber et al., 1982; Wedell, 1991 ) and within-subject designs (e.g.,  Lehmann & Pan, 1994; Pettibone & Wedell, 2000; Trueblood et al., 2013 ).

(2014)  recently argued that the attraction effect may be restricted to stylized product representations that describe the products numerically (see also  Yang & Lynn, 2014 ). Many researchers concede that other factors play an important role in the explaining the effect’s (lack of) robustness ( Berkowitsch, Scheibehenne, & Rieskamp, 2014; Simonson, 2014; Trueblood, Brown, & Heathcote, 2015 ). In its strong form, however, Frederick et al.’s argument has met considerable resistance ( Huber et al., 2014; Simonson, 2014 ).

A second line of experimental research that purports violations of regularity has focused on evidence for intransitivity using binary choice paradigms. Regularity implies a set of conditions on choice probabilities known as the “triangle inequalities” that play a key role in probabilistic models of transitive preference (see  McCausland & Marley, 2013 , for a detailed discussion, and  Sprumont, 2022 , for a recent, related result). But intransitive preferences may be less pervasive than previously thought (see, e.g.  Birnbaum & Gutierrez, 2007; Dai, 2017; Regenwetter et al., 2018; Regenwetter, Dana, & Davis-Stober, 2011; Regenwetter & Davis-Stober, 2012; Regenwetter, Davis-Stober, Smeulders, Fields, & Wang, 2021 ). Context-dependent transitive preferences can account for seemingly irrational choice caused by aggregation artifacts in experimental paradigms that vary the presentation order of stimulus attributes ( Davis-Stober, Park, Brown, & Regenwetter, 2016 ). And context-dependent transitive preferences can even account for the robust cyclical patterns observed in choice behavior when prior knowledge is limited and choices depend heavily on contextual inferences ( Müller-Trede, Sher, & McKenzie, 2015 ).

Inferences based on characteristics of the available options and on the composition of the choice set have also been invoked as an explanation for the attraction effect and other context effects ( Prelec, Wernerfelt, & Zettelmeyer, 1997; Wernerfelt, 1995 ), sometimes in conjunction with detailed cognitive theories of information processing ( Ronayne & Brown, 2017 ). At the same time, the robust evidence against regularity inspired a variety of psychological theories of context-dependent decision making. According to one influential interpretation, context effects provide evidence for heuristic processes in decision making ( Ariely & Wallsten, 1995; Dhar & Simonson, 2003; Hedgcock, Rao, & Chen, 2009; Simonson, 1989 ). Alternatively, the effects may be driven by other aspects of cognition such as associative processes ( Bhatia, 2013 ), attention ( Trueblood et al., 2014 ), lateral inhibition ( Roe, Busemeyer, & Townsend, 2001 ), or by how multiple agents within an organism aggregate their preferences ( Bergner, Oppenheimer, & Detre, 2019 ). From these psychological perspectives, context effects and the regularity violations they represent are often claimed to be irreconcilable with rational theories of choice in general ( Shafir & LeBoeuf, 2002 ), and with (random) utility theory in particular ( Rieskamp, Busemeyer, & Mellers, 2006; Simonson, 1989; Tversky & Simonson, 1993 ).

In what follows, we scrutinize this claim from a formal perspective. Our treatment examines the interconnections between regularity, context dependence, and random utility theory through the lens of polyhedral combinatorics. It suggests that context effects and context dependence may not pose such a problem for random utility theory after all. On the contrary, context-dependent random utility models and their associated random preference models may offer a precise and parsimonious account of context-dependent choice behavior, including decoy effects. We begin with the commonly used “best-choice” paradigm, in which the decision maker chooses their preferred option out of a choice set that is being offered to them.

While we rely on polyhedral combinatorics throughout this article, we keep the main text as accessible as possible, and relegate the formal details of convex geometric derivations to the  Appendix . We also place an emphasis on simple examples that illustrate the general method and provide an intuition for its mathematics (see, e.g., Section  4.2  and  Fig. 1 ). In these examples, we focus on the attraction effect, which allows us to concentrate on small choice sets and low-dimensional models. Similar examples could be constructed for the similarity and compromise effects and other context-dependent choice patterns. Indeed, our approach is sufficiently general that it may in principle be applied to any number of preference states and in any dimension — limitations are confined to computational, experimental, or statistical considerations. Our examples show how this general mathematical framework can be leveraged to yield highly parsimonious as well as novel behavioral predictions.

Classical random utility and random preference models are prescriptively appealing. They are also descriptively powerful ( McCausland, Davis-Stober, Marley, Park, & Brown, 2020 ). But the pervasive context-dependence of decision making reviewed above remains a challenge to their descriptive validity ( Kahneman & Tversky, 2000; Slovic, 1995 ). In what follows, we move beyond the classical models and introduce context-dependent random utility and random preference models. We make two key innovations. First, the primitives in our models are joint preference rankings or joint utilities across two or more contexts. Second, we require choice probabilities in a given context to exist only for those choice sets from which choices are made in that context. The resulting models may or may not satisfy regularity and other testable properties of the classical models, as we illustrate with several models in the setting of the TV purchase example from the Introduction. In our first example, we draw on multi-attribute utility theory to construct a simple context-dependent random preference model. Our approach builds on  Müller-Trede et al. (2015) , who showed how random preference models may be generalized to accommodate context-dependent preferences while maintaining their prescriptive appeal.

In general, distribution-free random utility models and their associated random preference models, whether context-dependent or not, form convex polytopes. The polyhedral perspective provides a useful tool to study probabilistic choice at high levels of generality (cf.  Regenwetter, Dana, & Davis-Stober, 2010; Regenwetter et al., 2011; Regenwetter & Davis-Stober, 2012 ). As we have illustrated, regularity conditions correspond to geometric constraints on polytopes, and the polytopes that describe the classic random utility model satisfy these particular constraints. Example 1 shows how to formulate context-dependent random utility models that imply a different set of geometric constraints, and that may – and in some cases must – violate regularity. The geometric representation also makes clear that models with or without regularity can vary greatly in terms of their dimensionality as well as their location and volume in the space that they span. In other words, depending on the particular constraints they impose, the models can range from highly restrictive to extremely permissive.

The general formulation of context-dependent random utility can accommodate a wide range of different models. Before we can apply this kind of model, we must discuss appropriate statistical tests. One approach is to impose additional distributional constraints. For example, “order-statistics” models constrain the distribution over preference states ( Critchlow, Fligner, & Verducci, 1991, 1993; Marden, 1995 ). Alternatively, “econometric” models including the well-known “probit”, and “(multinomial) logit” models constrain the joint distribution of random utilities (e.g.,  Louviere, Hensher, & Swait, 2000; Train, 2009 ). These models are computationally tractable, and have a successful track record in analyzing discrete choice data and in out-of-sample predictions. However, they generally cannot account for the attraction effect and other context effects. To address this shortcoming,  Rooderkerk, Van Heerde, and Bijmolt (2011)  developed an econometric model that allows (random) utilities to be context-dependent.

Our approach avoids such distributional assumptions and thereby supplies a nonparametric alternative to these commonly used distributional models. Models with multiple simultaneous order-constraints – like the facet-defining inequalities of a polytope – are difficult to tackle statistically. This is because maximum likelihood methods must accommodate that point estimates may lie on the boundary of the parameter space (in one or more facets of the polytope), where likelihood statistics have idiosyncratic distributions. We do not know of any commercial statistical software that can handle this complication. But recent breakthroughs in mathematical statistics have made frequentist and Bayesian order-constrained inference tractable ( Davis-Stober, 2009; Klugkist & Hoijtink, 2007; Myung, Karabatsos, & Iverson, 2005; Regenwetter et al., 2014; Silvapulle & Sen, 2005 ). These techniques have found successful applications in modeling choice behavior already (e.g.,  Cavagnaro & Davis-Stober, 2014; Guo & Regenwetter, 2014; McCausland & Marley, 2014; Regenwetter & Davis-Stober, 2012; Smeulders, Davis-Stober, Regenwetter, & Spieksma, 2018 ). Ongoing research in this area promises to soon permit order-constrained data analytics for polytopes of several hundred dimensions. Importantly, these methods can be used for any convex polytope, including those characterized by context-dependent random utility models, as long as the algorithm is provided with a suitable mathematical description of the polytope.

An influential Bayesian modeling approach associates a prior distribution with the polytope representing a particular model ( Myung et al., 2005 ). That is, the restrictions on choice probabilities imposed by a (random utility) model correspond to restrictions on the support of a prior distribution. Priors are otherwise uninformative in that they are uniform over the region in which they receive positive support. Bayes Factors compare the posterior for each of the models with the posterior of an unconstrained “encompassing” model that allows any combinations of choice probabilities. As a result, the Bayes factor for any model is bounded from above by the inverse of its volume. This property of Bayesian order-constrained inference turns out to matter in our experiment, which we report next.

An experimental replication of the attraction effect in best-choice provides a basic proof of concept. The experiment builds on the TV purchase example, which is loosely based on a widely used set of stimuli created by  Huber et al. (1982) . We illustrate our method using the classical model as well as the four simple context-dependent random utility models we have constructed. Even though these context-dependent models are engineered to capture insights from existing work on asymmetric dominance, they also yield new testable predictions. In particular, we show how the additional pairwise choice among competitor and decoy gives us additional valuable information to differentiate between the simple context-dependent models we have constructed as illustrations of the theoretical framework. This documents the potential scientific power of our approach: Even this slight modification in the empirical paradigm, while still yielding an extremely simple experiment, already adds diagnostic value.

Experimental work by behavioral decision researchers has catalogued the pervasive context dependence of decision making (e.g.,  Kahneman & Tversky, 2000; Payne, Bettman, & Schkade, 1999; Slovic, 1995 ). Such context dependence and the violations of regularity it entails are often brought forward as damning evidence against rational choice theory in general, and random utility models in particular. This article reconciles random utility theory and context-dependent choice. We have shown how to construct parsimonious random utility models that are context-dependent and may violate regularity and other coherence principles implied by classical models. The mathematical framework we propose is sufficiently general to capture many forms of context dependence: It does not place any a priori constraints on operationalizations of ‘context,’ on the number of permissible preference states, or on model dimensionality.

Our framework’s mathematical foundations lie in polyhedral combinatorics. Polyhedral methods have seen successful applications in operations research ( Grötschel, Jünger, & Reinelt, 1984; Grötschel, Lovász, & Schrijver, 2012 ) and marketing ( Toubia, Hauser, & Simester, 2004; Toubia, Simester, Hauser, & Dahan, 2003 ). The virtues of a geometric perspective on decision-theoretic models have also long been recognized ( Iverson & Falmagne, 1985; Morrison, 1963 ). The geometry of decision theory is garnering increased attention as mathematical and statistical advances have widened the scope for its applications (e.g.  Cavagnaro & Davis-Stober, 2014; Dai, 2017; Fiorini, 2004; Guo & Regenwetter, 2014; McCausland & Marley, 2013; Myung et al., 2005; Regenwetter et al., 2011; Regenwetter & Davis-Stober, 2012; Regenwetter & Robinson, 2017; Sarafoglou, Haaf, Ly, Gronau, Wagenmakers, & Marsman, 2023; Tsetsos, Moran, Moreland, Chater, Usher, & Summerfield, 2016 ). Our treatment shows that classical and context-dependent random utility models are both characterized by convex polytopes. Their descriptive performance in empirical settings may thus be assessed and compared with methods of order-constrained inference ( Davis-Stober, 2009; Heck & Davis-Stober, 2019; Klugkist & Hoijtink, 2007; McCausland & Marley, 2014; Silvapulle & Sen, 2005 ).

Our simple experiment illustrates how to leverage the models’ polyhedral geometry in a statistical analysis. To our knowledge, the attraction effect we observe is also the first recorded violation of the classical random utility model in a probability space of more than two dimensions that is not confined to binary choice. This complements other recent and ongoing quantitative tests of the classical model ( McCausland et al., 2020; Müller-Trede et al., 2015; Regenwetter et al., 2011 ). Despite its simplicity, our experiment also provides an insight into the attraction effect that goes beyond a mere illustration of our modeling approach and beyond a mere violation of the classical model. The Bayesian model comparison favors a model that defines context as binary versus best-choice from the full set over an analogous model that defines context via the presence or the absence of the decoy option. This is consistent with leading explanations for the attraction effect, including accounts based on contextual inferences ( Prelec et al., 1997 ) as well as  Tversky and Simonson (1993)  componential context model.

We propose a general theoretical framework, not a specific theory of context dependence. Modelers seeking to apply our framework can use a variety of core theories to identify the relevant operationalization of ‘context’ as well as which context-dependent preferences are permissible. This is illustrated in  Models 1a  and  1b , which draw on multi-attribute utility ( von Winterfeldt & Edwards, 1986 ) and options-as-information ( Sher & McKenzie, 2014 ). We chose these particular theories because, much like classical utility theory, they yield linear orders, and they emphasize the consistent valuation of goals and priorities. The illustration thus shows how our framework lends itself naturally not just to descriptive models, but also to nuanced normative and prescriptive models. By formally reconciling rationality, context dependence, and behavioral heterogeneity, such models could play an important role in the reinvigorated rationality debate in psychology and behavioral research ( Frederick, 2015; Huber et al., 2014; McKenzie, 2003; Oaksford & Chater, 2007; Sher et al., 2022; Tenenbaum, Kemp, Griffiths, & Goodman, 2011 ).  Model 1b  in particular, as the most restrictive of the models we consider, underscores how revising rational benchmarks can yield models that are both normatively appealing and highly parsimonious.

In other experimental paradigms, our framework could adjudicate between competing psychological accounts of empirical phenomena. Take the well-known paradigm on ‘decisions from experience,’ for instance. In that paradigm, participants have been reported to under-weight small probabilities that are learned experientially, in contrast to the over-weighting of small probabilities that are explicitly described ( Hertwig, Barron, Weber, & Erev, 2004  see also Regenwetter & Robinson, 2017). The under-weighting of small probabilities in experiential learning in these studies could reflect the under-sampling of rare events and thus a downward bias regarding the probability with which they occur ( Hadar & Fox, 2009 ). Or, first-hand experience with the rarity represented by a small probability could lead participants to weight it differently even though their beliefs about probabilities are unbiased. Interestingly, the latter, but not the former account, predicts a regularity violation in a novel experimental paradigm: Consider a ‘risky’ gamble offering a small probability of a large prize and a ‘safe’ gamble offering a larger probability of a smaller prize. The safe gamble should be relatively less attractive in two-alternative forced choice than when the two gambles are presented alongside a third, ‘decoy,’ gamble which offers the same small probability as the risky gamble, but of only a medium-sized prize and, importantly, for which this probability is both described and experienced. In this paradigm, under-sampling predicts that regularity will be satisfied, whereas differential weighting with unbiased beliefs allows regularity to be violated. Our framework could jointly test which of the two psychological accounts is favored in a series of several such decision problems. As discussed above, joint modeling across multiple stimulus sets is amenable to order-constrained inference and can render each of these competing predictions highly parsimonious.

In closing, we wish to emphasize that the context-dependent preferences in our framework need not be linear orders (like those in  Definition 3 ) for the geometric perspective to apply: Our approach could be extended to any core theory that specifies at least a unique best option for each choice set featured in each context. This includes, for example, various heuristics , even ones that give rise to intransitive or incomplete preferences. Indeed, perhaps our framework’s greatest strength is the ease with which it accommodates completely different descriptive, prescriptive, and normative theories. It sets these theories on an equal mathematical footing, and enables direct theoretical and empirical comparisons between them. Together with other methodological innovations such as the new methods of order-constrained inference and the use of best–worst choice in experimental design, it opens the door to a host of novel, highly diagnostic paradigms that promise to shed new light on the nature of context dependence in decision making.
