Imagine you are faced with a decision in which you have the utmost confidence in your expertise. Perhaps you are a financial expert deciding to how much money to invest in a new opportunity. You consult a colleague for advice, and their opinion differs from yours. Do you rethink your approach, or stick with the plan? Or maybe you are a beginner level chess player practicing your skills. You are about to make a move, but you consult a game solving algorithm first, which suggests an entirely different move. Which move do you make?

People are faced with choices every day in which advice is available that can aid their decision-making. Despite a strong consensus that advice tends to benefit decision performance ( Bonaccio & Dalal, 2006; Himmelstein & Budescu, 2022; Soll & Mannes, 2011; Yaniv & Milyavsky, 2007 ), there is also evidence that people fail to utilize advice optimally. For example, people will often make a discrete choice between their own opinion and advice, even when averaging the two is clearly a superior strategy ( Soll & Larrick, 2009 ). People also tend to egocentrically trim—to discount advice that differs strongly from their prior beliefs ( Allahverdyan & Galstyan, 2014; Bonaccio & Dalal, 2006; Schultze, Rakotoarisoa, & Schulz-Hardt, 2015; Wang & Du, 2018; Yaniv, 1997, 2004 ). In cases where people receive advice from multiple sources, they may fail to appropriately weight the different sources of information ( Soll & Larrick, 2009; Yaniv & Milyavsky, 2007 ). People can also show non-normative preferences for or against information if they believe it comes from a particular source, such as a statistical algorithm ( Dietvorst, Simmons, & Massey, 2015; Logg, Minson, & Moore, 2019; Prahl & Van Swol, 2017 ).

Advice utilization is often formally studied in the context of Judge Advisor Systems (JAS) ( Bonaccio & Dalal, 2006; Himmelstein & Budescu, 2022; Logg et al., 2019; Schultze et al., 2015; Sniezek & Van Swol, 2001; Yaniv, 2004 ). This application of the dual hurdle model is  both  methodologically and descriptively novel. It solves the boundary inflation and also proposes a theoretical accounting of the psychological process by which people choose or average ( Soll & Larrick, 2009 ). Decision makers must first make a discrete decision between declining, adopting, or compromising with advice. Was one’s prior belief so well informed already that advice should be ignored? Was the advisor so much better informed that its judgment is expected to dominate one’s own? Or do both inputs contain some information that needs to be averaged with greater deliberation? Declining and adopting represent fully discrete choosing outcomes. The compromise decision entails a second, more effortful continuous averaging judgment.

This approach has the potential to generate unique insights previously unavailable to researchers. The model can potentially describe how different aspects of a JAS influence different stages of the decision process. For example, perhaps a particular predictor influences the initial discrete decision, causing judges to compromise more (or less), but not the extent of the averaging judgment when they do. Given that averaging is typically a dominant strategy to choosing ( Soll & Larrick, 2009 ), such a result could have crucial implications for designing interventions to help advice-takers optimize their judgments. Furthermore, if predictors indeed have different effects on the proposed discrete and continuous judgment processes, it would provide clear support for the model as a theoretical description of the psychology of advice taking.

There are related computational models in the literature, often referred to as zero-or-one inflated beta (ZOIB) models, designed to capture boundary inflation for continuous proportions ( Liu & Kong, 2015; Ospina & Ferrari, 2012 ). These models imply the same distribution, and estimate parameters that can be transformed to produce equivalent interpretations, but have a slightly different computational structure. A brief treatment and comparison between the dual hurdle model and ZOIB models in the context of advice utilization can be found in the supplementary materials.

In JAS studies, judges are often exposed to several trials. Treating each observation as independent is typically inappropriate, as different observations from the same judge will tend to be more similar to one another than to other observations. This requires estimating parameters that define the judge-level grouping, or random, effects. This clustering, as well as the non-linear nature of the model structure, pose challenges to standard maximum likelihood estimation algorithms.

Bayesian estimation methods based on Markov Chain or Hamiltonian Monte Carlo are well suited for handling both of these challenges ( Bürkner, 2017; McElreath, 2018 ). Rather than partial random effects into an error term, Bayesian methods treat group-level effects as parameters of interest (aka latent variables). Likewise, these estimation algorithms are more flexible in linking linear combinations of predictors (also interpretable as latent variables) to non-Guassian probability distributions, such as the beta distribution. These methods simulate the posterior distributions of the model parameters, typically under diffuse priors, allowing for inferences about how the posterior predictive distributions of the response variable vary as a function of the predictors.

The purpose of this paper is to demonstrate the practical application of the dual hurdle approach to modeling advice utilization. To accomplish this, data from three previous studies were reanalyzed using the dual hurdle framework: Experiment 4 from  Logg et al. (2019) , Experiment 3 from  Schultze et al. (2015) , and Experiment 1 from  Himmelstein and Budescu (2022) . These experiments represent a variety of popular and distinct, but overlapping topics in the advice taking literature. In each case, graphical analysis of the posterior predictive distribution of the dual hurdle model both confirms and expands on the findings of the original studies in important new ways. The code that produced each analysis can be found in the supplementary materials.

Several past studies have suggested that people prefer to receive assistance from human rather than algorithmic sources, in a phenomenon dubbed  algorithm aversion  ( Burton, Stein, & Jensen, 2020; Castelo, Bos, & Lehmann, 2019; Dietvorst et al., 2015; Kaufmann & Budescu, 2020; Prahl & Van Swol, 2017 ). However, a series of studies recently challenged this notion, demonstrating several cases in which people preferred algorithmic advice to human advice ( algorithm appreciation ,  Logg et al. 2019 ).

Experiment 4 of  Logg et al. (2019)  tested whether judges with no specialized domain expertise would show more algorithm appreciation than expert judges. Their results indicated expert judges showed less advice utilization regardless of task domain, and were relatively indifferent to whether the advice came from a human or an algorithm. Lay judges both showed greater advice utilization in general, and also greater utilization of algorithmic advice than human advice.

However, this study did not separate cases where judges declined or adopted advice from cases where they compromised. In reanalyzing this data, we can demonstrate that there was indeed inflation on these boundary values. We can also obtain a more granular understanding of the decision process by analyzing the discrete decision (decline, adopt or compromise) and continuous judgment (averaging magnitude) separately.

The study included four judgment tasks. In the first, participants were simply asked to guess the weight of an individual based on a photograph. The second involved a forecast about whether Tesla motors would deliver at least 80,000 electric vehicles within the calendar year of 2016. The last two involved forecasting geopolitical events, and were the questions designed to match the domain expertise of the expert participants. One was a forecast about whether the US or an EU member country would impose sanctions on another country as a result of a cyber attack before the end of 2016, and the other was about the likelihood of Brexit occurring before July 2017.

The study design followed a standard JAS setup: participants were asked to express a judgment, offered advice, and then given an opportunity to revise their beliefs. The source of advice participants received was manipulated between subjects as either “the estimate of an algorithm” or “the estimate of another person/the average estimate of forecasters in a forecasting tournament”. The advice was identical across advice conditions, and designed to be accurate and believable. For more details on the experiment, see  Logg et al. (2019) .

There are both objective and subjective components to couducting graphical analyses of posterior predictive distributions from Bayesian models, and the extent to which we can make truly objective claims relies largely on the degree of uncertainty in the distributions. For example, there is very little overlap between the probability of decline for algorithmic and human advice for lay participants ( Fig. 3 a). Thus we can say with a high degree of confidence lay participants were less likely to decline algorithmic advice. However, the sample of experts was much smaller, leading to much wider posterior predictive distributions. Regardless of whether the posterior predictive means were similar for algorithms and humans (e.g. for the Weight item) or somewhat different (e.g. the Tesla item), because both distributions largely overlap, it is hard to say with confidence there are meaningful differences in either case.

This is, in part, a result of the boundary inflations, which are the methodological reason the dual hurdle model is more appropriate for analyzing such data than standard linear models. In these cases, one should consider both the bulk in the central mode of the distribution, as well as differences in the posterior predictive means (represented by points). Take the weight item for example. For lay participants, the center of the distribution is clearly higher for algorithmic than human advice. Likewise, for expert participants, the difference in the posterior predictive means is much more different than for the other items. This suggests there are differences in how both groups averaged their beliefs with the different advisors for this item.

Overall, the results of the dual hurdle model paint a clear psychological picture: algorithm appreciation is driven much more by the initial discrete decision to decline, adopt, or compromise than it is by the continuous averaging judgments. We see clear and strong effects, particularly for lay participants, in the effect of advice source on this discrete decision, while we see much more modest effects for belief revision magnitude. These results highlight the dual hurdle model’s utility in describing different aspects of the belief revision process, and provide evidence that belief revision is indeed a combination of discrete and continuous cognitive processes as the model posits.

These results are consistent with those reported in  Logg et al. (2019) , but provide new, more granular insight about the mechanisms behind them. There was a clear effect of advice source on lay participants’ decision to either decline or compromise with advice ( Fig. 3 ). For experts, the picture is less clear, as the posterior predictive distributions are so wide. However, the posterior probability of lay participants declining algorithmic advice is squarely in the lower tail of the posterior probability of experts declining, suggesting experts tended to decline advice (particularly algorithmic advice) more frequently than lay participants.

Interestingly, there does appear to be a small but consistent effect of advice source on adoption. Though the rates of adoption were much smaller than decline and compromise, the posterior predictive distributions indicate they were more likely when the advice was algorithmic than when it was from a human source, providing additional evidence for algorithm appreciation for both lay and expert participants.

When participants compromised, we again find that lay participants put greater weight on advice than experts ( Fig. 4 ). The effects of advice source were much smaller, but lay participants also utilized algorithmic advice slightly more than human advice when they compromised. Experts, on the other hand, did not, with one exception: the weight item. For the non-domain specific judgment, experts did show algorithm appreciation in the magnitude of their belief revision, even though they utilized advice at a similar frequency to the other items. This suggests that when not relying on their expertise, experts were more persuaded by algorithmic than human advice, though in general they were less willing to cross the initial hurdle and average, and more inclined to simply rely on their own judgments entirely regardless of question domain.

A common result in advice utilization research is that judges egocentrically trim: they increasingly discount advice as a function of its divergence from their initial beliefs ( Allahverdyan & Galstyan, 2014; Bonaccio & Dalal, 2006; Wang & Du, 2018; Yaniv, 1997, 2004 ). However, some recent studies have challenged this notion as well, finding that judges also discount advice that is very similar to their own beliefs ( Ecken & Pibernik, 2016; Himmelstein & Budescu, 2022; Schultze et al., 2015 ). This reversal produces a curvilinear pattern, where advice is most utilized when it moderately differs from the initial judgment.

The study once again followed a standard JAS setup, this time with 60 trials per participant. In each trial, participants were asked to estimate the distance between two European capital cities in kilometers. In the high competence condition, participants always saw the 7th ranked advisor, while in the low competence condition they always saw the 78th ranked advisor

A failsafe mechanism was built in for cases where the advice would be unrealistic or obviously deceptive, where instead of systematic advice, participants saw randomly generated advice, leading to 42 (1.8%) trials being excluded from the analysis. One of the primary goals of  Schultze et al. (2015)  was to systematically study the egocentric trimming effect: that people more heavily discount more distant advice. Instead, they found a curvilinear relationship between distance and advice utilization, which was explained in the context of Social Judgment Theory ( Sherif & Hovland, 1961 ). These new results present a compelling explanation for this pattern, and highlight the utility of the dual hurdle model in separating the decision process into discrete and continuous processes.

In general, when the distance between one’s prior belief and the advice was small, people frequently declined the advice ( Fig. 5 ). Presumably, rather than go through the process of making a minor revision in light of advice that was very similar to their own beliefs, they instead treated it as confirmatory. Put another way: the smaller the distance between

On the other hand, the magnitude of belief revision in cases where people compromised with advice ( Fig. 6 ) shows the opposite effect, and the expected pattern of egocentric trimming. In the continuous averaging stage, people weighted advice that was more similar to their own beliefs more heavily, and discounted distant advice more heavily.

These patterns were consistent for both levels of advisor competence. Unsurprisingly, people were generally more likely to decline advice from less competent advisors, and discounted advice from less competent advisors more heavily. There also appears to be a small effect such that people were more likely to fully adopt advice from competent advisors.

These results once again highlight the remarkable potential of the dual hurdle model to take an effect that appears one way in the aggregate, and disentangle it into two separate effects that occur during different stages of the decision process. Instead of a single curvilinear effect of distance on belief revision, the dual hurdle model suggests there are, in fact, two separate effects that act in opposition to one another, which, when viewed jointly, lead to this apparent reversal.

A third study combined several of the key features from  Logg et al. (2019)  and  Schultze et al. (2015) . The goal of Experiment 1 in  Himmelstein and Budescu (2022)  was to determine whether judges utilized advice differently depending on whether it came from a human expert or statistical algorithm for different forecasting domains: economics and politics. These were similar to the business and forecasting items described in Experiment 4 of  Logg et al. (2019) . While the judges expressed self-reported preferences for algorithmic advice for economic forecasts and human advice for political forecasts, this preference was not apparent in their utilization of advice in practice. Instead, features related to the (dis)similarity between judges’ prior beliefs and the advice seemed to be most associated with advice utilization. Consistent with the findings of  Schultze et al. (2015) , there was a curvilinear effect of distance on the probability that judges would revise their beliefs.

Additionally, the experiment from  Himmelstein and Budescu (2022)  involved multinomial probability judgments, such that several mutually exclusive events were associated with each forecast (i.e. the probability that one among several candidates might win an election). This made it possible to measure other features of similarity between a judge’s initial belief and the advice. These include how peaked the two distributions were compared to one another (an implicit measure of relative confidence) and the rank order agreement in the likelihood of the events. These similarity features can provide further insight into what, in particular, about belief-advice similarity drives advice utilization.

Like the previous studies described, Experiment 1 from  Himmelstein and Budescu (2022)  followed a standard JAS procedure. Participants first made an initial forecast by assigning probabilities across either two or four mutually exclusive and exhaustive bins associated with a forecasting question. These bins represented all possible outcomes for a forecasting problem (e.g., the probability each given candidate running for a particular office will win the election). Participants were then shown an advisory forecast, and offered an opportunity to revise their predictions.

The advice was described as being generated by a “human expert” or “statistical algorithm”, with each person viewing half of all items for each advice source. The advice itself was generated from a prior pilot study, in which different participants forecasted the same questions. The average values of across forecasts from the pilot were used as the advice values for both the human expert and statistical algorithm conditions, to ensure the advice was sensible, equivalent regardless of condition, and likely to be accurate based on the principle of the Wisdom of Crowds ( Atanasov et al., 2017; Budescu & Chen, 2014; Himmelstein, Atanasov, & Budescu, 2021; Surowiecki, 2005 ).

The task consisted of 16 items (for a list of items, see supplementary materials from  Himmelstein and Budescu 2022 ). Eight items involved economic events (i.e. What will the price of sugar be in November?) and eight involved political events (i.e. What will the approval rate of Japan’s cabinet be in September?).

Results regarding the experimental manipulations are largely consistent with  Himmelstein and Budescu (2022) ’s conclusion of a null result, with one exception. While there was no clear effect of advice source, item domain, or time horizon on the continuous averaging judgment ( Fig. 8 ), participants did decline algorithmic advice less for the long time horizon ( Fig. 7 ). While the distributions for human and algorithmic advice do partially overlap, there is clearly some evidence that participants were more willing to utilize algorithmic than human advice for longer time horizons. This result is consistent with  Logg et al. (2019) ’s finding of algorithm appreciation.

This previously undetected result demonstrates yet another benefit to the granularity of inference made available by the dual hurdle model. One possibility is that  Himmelstein and Budescu (2022) ’s long time horizon was not sufficiently long to detect a pronounced effect without the inferential precision made available by the dual hurdle model. Forecasting time horizons can range for years or decades in some cases ( Himmelstein et al., in press ), but the long time horizon used in this study was only 10 weeks long. That algorithmic advisors may be better appreciated as forecasting time horizons increase is a result that bears more careful study in future research, which should include even longer time horizons moving forward. However, without the results of the dual hurdle model, this potential research direction may have remained undiscovered, and researchers would not have known to concentrate their search on the initial discrete decision, rather than the continuous averaging judgment.

As in the previous two studies, the dual hurdle model went beyond mere confirmation of the existing theoretical results. It was able to tease out how different features of the JAS; whether they be experimental manipulations, aspects of the judges’ prior beliefs, or the advice itself; influenced the both the discrete and continuous decision processes separately.

The analyses presented thus far demonstrate how different predictors influence either or both of the discrete or continuous decision processes posited by the dual hurdle model. They show what factors influence how people decide between declining, adopting, or compromising; and also what factors influence how people weigh their continuous averaging judgments.

However, the possibility remains that different people utilize different strategies when taking advice. Perhaps some people are more inclined to average, while others are more inclined to choose ( Soll & Larrick, 2009 ). Maybe it is a small minority of people who drive the small but persistent adoption hurdle, while others always decline or compromise.

These results indicate a large amount of diversity in belief revision strategy. In all three studies, the distributions for the decline and compromise intercepts are long, uniform, and in  Himmelstein and Budescu (2022)  even slightly concave, indicating there were a plurality of participants who tended heavily towards either strategy, rather than strategies that mixed different discrete decisions.

In  Schultze et al. (2015) , participants tended to compromise more than decline, but both distributions were still quite long, indicating a large amount of strategic variability between the two options. However, all of the intercepts for adopt were concentrated near 0, meaning almost all participants adopted at uniformly low rates.

In the other two studies, the intercepts for decline and compromise spanned nearly the entire range of possible outcomes, indicating even larger strategic variability between these two options. And while the intercepts for adopt were also concentrated near 0, in both cases there appear to be a small minority of participants who adopted at a much higher rate.

It is possible that these differences reflect differences in the populations ( Schultze et al. 2015  used students while the other two studies used online panel members and volunteer national security experts) or the task type (the geographical distance estimates in  Schultze et al. 2015  are quite different from the geopolitical prediction tasks in the other two studies). The latter explanation has established theoretical grounding in the literature: judges tend to weight advice more heavily when tasks are perceived as more difficult ( Gino & Moore, 2007 ). Making predictions about geopolitical events are almost certainly more difficult than guessing the distances between major cities, though this intuition would require additional research to confirm. Regardless, it is clear that there was substantial diversity from person to person in the initial discrete decision stage

This paper represents both a methodological innovation for how advice utilization is studied as well as an important theoretical advancement in understanding the cognitive process behind it. The dual hurdle approach can not only account for boundary inflation in response variables such as  
                         
                            W 
                            O 
                            A 
                         
                      , but it is a novel and well supported theoretical model that aptly describes underlying psychology of judges in judge advisor systems. In all three studies, this approach yielded new, more nuanced insights regarding the mechanisms driving advice utilization than prior gold standard methods.

For example, these results suggest the algorithm appreciation observed by  Logg et al. (2019)  was driven by greater willingness to compromise with algorithmic advice, rather than how heavily it was weighted in continuous averaging judgments. This is an important distinction that can easily be missed by standard models: averaging (or compromising) one’s belief with advice is typically a strictly dominant strategy to choosing between the two ( Soll & Larrick, 2009 ). The lay participants were more inclined to compromise with algorithmic advice than with human advice, and also more inclined to do so than expert participants were. Interestingly, both groups were also more likely to adopt algorithmic advice than human advice, providing further novel evidence of algorithmic appreciation previously undetected in these data. However, there was little evidence of algorithm aversion or appreciation once either group of participants cleared the initial hurdle of deciding to average rather than choose.

The dual hurdle model likewise uncovered new algorithm appreciation results previously undetected by  Himmelstein and Budescu (2022) . One possible interpretation of their original conclusions is that they represent a failed replication of  Logg et al. (2019) ’s finding of algorithm appreciation, as they report no differences in advice utilization by source of information. However, the dual hurdle model was able to uncover an interaction between advice source and time horizon during the initial discrete decision: algorithm appreciation occurred for longer but not shorter time horizons. Importantly, this is the same stage in the decision process that drove algorithm appreciation in the  Logg et al. (2019)  data, indicating the  Himmelstein and Budescu (2022)  study represents a successful replication of their work after all. It appears that algorithm appreciation is sensitive to increasing time horizons for forecasting problems. This effect, which was not detected prior to the dual hurdle analysis, warrants additional investigation in future research on algorithmic advice taking, particularly for even longer time horizons.

The dual hurdle model can also provide explanations for apparent reversals in the decision making process that linear models cannot.  Schultze et al. (2015)  described one such reversal when they found that judges discounted advice that was either too similar or too dissimilar to their prior beliefs. However, rather than a single judgmental process, the results of the dual hurdle model indicate this reversal results from two separate decision processes. The discounting of highly similar advice occurred during the discrete decision stage, where judges were more likely to simply decline advice that was very similar to their prior beliefs. A likely explanation is that people consider such information as confirmatory, rather than meaningfully distinct, consistent with  Schultze et al. (2015) ’s Social Judgment Theory-based explanation. The discounting of dissimilar advice, on the other hand, occurred during the continuous averaging process. Averages between prior beliefs and advice tended to lean more heavily on the former as the distance between the two sources of information increased. This effect in the averaging stage is consistent with well established patterns of egocentric trimming in the advice taking literature ( Allahverdyan & Galstyan, 2014; Bonaccio & Dalal, 2006; Wang & Du, 2018; Yaniv, 1997, 2004 ).

Himmelstein and Budescu (2022)  also conducted several analyses involving belief-advice similarity, and once again the dual hurdle model provided novel insights. Some effects, such as the interaction between distance and agreement, were squarely focused in the discrete decision stage, while others, such as the interaction between agreement and relative confidence, were more influential on the continuous averaging process.

This theme is present throughout the results of all three studies that were reanalyzed: different predictors were influential during different stages of the decision process. Some were highly influential on the initial discrete decision between declining, adopting or compromising; while others were more influential on the continuous averaging process. In some cases, predictors were influential at both stages, but had very different effects. This pattern provides compelling evidence for the dual hurdle model as more than a mere methodological tool, but also a strong descriptive theoretical model of the psychology of advice taking as well.
