INTRODUCTION  The axioms of Savage's classical normative decision theory (Savage, 1972) are of two sorts. The qualitative axioms have the  character of directly forbidding certain preference schemes, thereby making the definition of a qualitative probability  relation possible. The refinement axioms do not directly forbid preference schemes, but rather constrain the qualitative  probability and the choice of states and consequences so as to insure a convenient mathematical representation. A similar  distinction can be made with respect to every Bayesian decision model. Qualitative axioms typically involve transitivity, some  form of the sure-thing principle, and some form of the dominance principle. Refinement axioms typically require the existence  of arbitrarily fine partitions of some suitable kind.  In this paper we develop a method for determining the absolute strengths of qualitative preference axioms. The idea is quite  simple. We first define a space of preference schemes. For a given qualitative axiom P; we then calculate the number pi of  preference schemes satisfying this axiom. The strength s; of P; is then defined to be pi/p, where p is the total number of  preference schemes. Greater strength corresponds to a smaller number of admissible preference schemes, and hence to a smaller  number si. This method can also be extended to determine the relative strenghts. If pii is the number of schemes satisfying  the conjunction Pi & Pj, then we can define the strength of Pj relative to P; as p;;/pi. The numbers pi might also be  interpreted as the probability of randomly drawing a preference scheme satisfying Pi from the space of all preference schemes.  General numerical results are contained in part I. We define a preference scheme as a totally ordered preference system over IF in which no two acts are equivalent in  preference. In other words, a preference scheme is a strict-preference system. Our reasons for studying preference schemes are  twofold: First, the space of preference schemes is simpler than the space of preference systems in which acts can be  equivalent in preference. A preference system of the latter kind can be regarded as an equivalence class of preference  schemes. In this sense preference schemes are logically more primitive. Second, preference schemes are empirically more  primitive in the following sense. When we ask a subject to choose between alternatives f and g, he will choose either f or g.  We can never ascertain from this single choice that the subject is indifferent between f and g. This would only become visible  on subsequent choices. For example, we might offer the alternatives f, g + S, and f + 6, g; where S is an arbitrarily small  gain. Equivalence in preference is in fact an interpretation of successive choice behavior. We note that the advantages of  confining attention to preference schemes could also be obtained by considering versions of the qualitative axioms in which  the preference inequalities were all strict. Theorem 2 shows that the sure-thing principle and dominance are quite strong relative to transitivity. This is important for  the following reason. Eventhough transitivity is quite strong, there is a simple heuristic rule which can help the decision  maker conform to this postulate. The decision maker simply has to list the available acts in order of descending (or  ascending) preference. He is not required to compare the consequences of various actions in specific states. No such simple  rule exists for dominance and the sure-thing principle. Theorem 2 suggests that the decision maker still faces a substantial  combinatorial problem even after he has eliminated all nontransitive preference schemes.  CONCLUSION  Although subjectivists tend to think that their notion of rational preference is very liberal, if not permissive, there is  ample evidence that their qualitative preference axioms are empirically false (Allais (1953); Ellsberg (1961); Kahneman and  Tversky (1979); Tversky (1969)). Either the qualitative preference axioms are normatively invalid and people voluntarily  violate them; or else the axioms are normatively valid and people sometimes make "preference errors" analogous to inference  errors commonly observed in everyday reasoning. There is some evidence that violators of the qualitative axioms tend to amend  their preferences when the violations are pointed  446 COOKE AND DRAAISMA  out to them, and that more experienced executives commit fewer violations than less experienced executives (MacCrimmon  (1968)). These latter results support the interpretation according to which the preference axioms are normatively valid. If  the axioms are normatively valid, then the results of this paper may be helpful in studying the ways in which people make  preference errors.  For example, MacCrimmon (1968) presented subjects with the six binary combinations of four basic acts and recorded the number  of intransitivities. In 114 subject-experiments, 8 subjects had intransitivities. Intransitive subjects were invited to change  their preferences, but the transitive subjects were apparently not issued a similar invitation. It is possible that some  transitive subjects were choosing according to some idiosyncratic rule which "accidently" resulted in transitive preference  schemes. In this experiment there were 64 (= 26) preference schemes available to each of the subjects, of which only 24 (=4!)  were transitive. Given the low a priori probability of transitive preference schemes (3/8 in this case), the low number of  intransitive subjects becomes more significant.
