Multidimensional scaling (MDS) is a long-established and widely-used statistical method for finding a spatial representation of a set of objects, based on the similarities between those objects ( Borg & Groenen, 2005; Cox & Cox, 1994; Schiffman, Reynolds, Young, & Carroll, 1981 ). MDS represents objects as points in a low-dimensional space, so that the similarity between each pair of objects corresponds to the distance between the points representing them, with more similar objects being nearer each other. Used as a statistical method, MDS has been applied throughout the natural and human sciences as a dimensionality-reduction method, providing useful representations and visualizations of the relationships between objects. In these sort of applications, the required similarities or proximities between each pair of objects are often found by automated methods, such as calculating the correlations or distances between vectors of information representing the objects. For example, the similarities of text documents can be derived from counts of the number of keywords they have in common, or the similarity between countries might be based on (standardized) differences in properties like their landmass, GDP, population, and so on.

The MDS model, however, has its origins in theories of human mental representation ( Shepard, 1974 ). MDS representations have provided psychologically meaningful representations of many stimulus domains, especially low-level perceptual domains such as colors, phonemes, and simple perceptual forms ( Shepard, 1980 ).  Shepard (1987)  developed a formal theory of the key cognitive process of stimulus generalization based on MDS representations, and MDS models are widely used to represent stimuli in cognitive models of identification, categorization, and learning ( Nosofsky, 1992 ). Used as a psychological model of stimulus representation, the similarities or proximities between each pair of stimuli are usually based on human judgments of similarity. These behavioral judgments can take the form of ratings on a scale, or be derived from various decision-making tasks, such as triadic comparison ( Romney, Brewer, & Batchelder, 1993 ), or identification or confusion tasks ( Miller & Nicely, 1955 ).

When MDS is used as a statistical method of dimensionality reduction, there is little conceptual scope to consider individual differences, because the similarities are calculated from known underlying quantitative descriptions. When MDS is used as a psychological model of representations, however, the question of individual differences becomes important. Different people may represent the similarities between stimuli differently, and a good model of human mental representation should account for these differences. One well-established generalization of the MDS model that include individual differences is the INDSCAL model ( Carroll & Chang, 1970; Takane, Young, & De Leeuw, 1977 ), which still assumes a single spatial representation, but allows individual differences by placing subject-specific weights on each dimension. Thus, differences in judged similarities between stimuli are explained by different people placing different emphasis on the dimensions. Another approach to allowing individual differences in MDS representations is provided by latent-mixture modeling ( Lee, 2008; Winsberg & De Soete, 1993 ), in which there are multiple spatial representations, each corresponding to a sub-group of subjects. Recently,  Bocci and Vichi (2011)  proposed a generalization of the MDS called K-INDSCAL, which effectively combines the INDSCAL and latent-mixture models of individual differences. K-INDSCAL is a  
                         K 
                       latent-class mixture of INDSCAL, and so includes the possibility of different spatial representations for groups of subjects, as well as dimension weights for individual subjects within each group.

While the K-INDSCAL model is an important theoretical development, the implementation reported by  Bocci and Vichi (2011)  uses classical estimation methods based on least-squares optimization. For a model with the complexity and richness of K-INDSCAL, this is potentially limiting, because it does not allow for the measurement of uncertainty in inferences. Given the model allows both group-level and individual-level differences in stimulus representation, most data will be at least partly consistent with different representational possibilities, and it is important to understand what can and cannot be inferred about individual differences. The ability to handle uncertainty in a complete and coherent way is the cornerstone of the Bayesian approach to statistical inference ( Jaynes, 2003; Lindley, 1972 ). There is a modest and growing literature on Bayesian methods for MDS modeling ( Bakker & Poole, 2013; Lee, 2008; Oh & Raftery, 2001; Okada & Mayekawa, 2011; Okada & Shigemasu, 2010; Park, DeSarbo, & Liechty, 2008 ), but it has yet to be applied to the K-INDSCAL model. There is also a literature on using Bayesian methods to understand individual differences in cognitive processes related to similarity judgment, including category learning ( Bartlema, Lee, Wetzels, & Vanpaemel, 2014 ), memory ( Dennis, Lee, & Kinnell, 2008 ), and decision-making ( Lee, 2015; Scheibehenne, Rieskamp, & Wagenmakers, 2013; van Ravenzwaaij, Dutilh, & Wagenmakers, 2011 ).

Accordingly, the goal of this paper is to develop, evaluate, and apply a Bayesian approach for the K-INDSCAL model, using modern computational Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods. In the next section, the Bayesian K-INDSCAL model is formally defined, and we develop a two-step post-processing method that can be applied to MCMC samples taken from its posterior to deal with indeterminacy issues. We report the results of a small simulation study using artificially generated data to check that inferences are possible from the sorts of data typically available in psychology. We then present three applications of the proposed Bayesian method using previously considered psychological data. The three applications involve the taste of colas, images of cats, and colors of different hues, and collectively highlight the flexibility of the model to consider both group-level and individual-level differences in representation. Finally, we discuss the current contributions and future directions for the modeling approach.

Our proposed Bayesian method is an extension of previous work by  Oh and Raftery (2001) . These authors developed a Bayesian approach for applying a standard MDS model to data. Our Bayesian approach for applying the model to data relies on computational MCMC methods for sampling from the joint posterior distribution. We implemented the model using the free and open-source software Stan and its interface to the R computing language ( Stan Development Team, 2015a,b ). Stan applies the so-called No-U-Turn Sampler ( Hoffman & Gelman, 2014 ) to draw samples from the joint posterior distribution.

The Stan code implementation of the model is provided in the  Appendix . One noteworthy feature of the implementation is that the mixture component of the model, involving the possible multiple latent representations, is achieved by explicitly building the components into the log probability density of the model, and inference about latent-class indicator parameters is achieved using a soft-max approach. These implementation features are standard for Stan, but differ a little from the statistical description of the model we have presented, and from how it might be implemented in alternative graphical modeling software such as JAGS ( Plummer, 2003 ).

Drawing inferences about the model parameters from posterior samples requires addressing two different identification issues. One issue stems from the fact that the likelihood function is invariant under permutations of the class labels; this is called the “label-switching” issue ( Redner & Walker, 1984 ). The other issue stems from the fact that the weighted Euclidean distance is invariant under translation and reflection.

In order to select the number of classes  
                            K 
                         , it would be desirable if the number of classes was inferred using fully Bayesian methods. The Bayes factor ( Kass & Raftery, 1995 ) is the obvious candidate, giving the evidence the similarity data provide for representational models with different numbers of classes. However, while conceptually straightforward, it is a difficult computational problem to estimate the desired Bayes factors. The tractable Savage–Dickey method ( Dickey et al., 1970; Wagenmakers, Lodewyckx, Kuriyal, & Grasman, 2010 ) cannot be used, because in a lower-dimensional MDS representation is usually not nested in a higher-dimensional representations. It is interesting to note that subjects K and L are also recorded as having a color deficiency, but this is not inferred by the current analysis. Whether these subjects have a different sort of color deficiency, or whether their condition is milder and so behaviorally not evident, is an interesting question for future modeling.

The first contribution of our work is to demonstrate the different sorts of individual differences that can be found using a model that allows both group-level and individual-level differences. Collectively, the cola, cat, and color analyses show the potential of the K-INDSCAL model to investigate individual differences in the way people represent stimuli. The inferred model for the cola data identifies two very different ways that groups of subjects organize their representations, but shows little evidence of individual differences in dimension weights within these two qualitatively different groups. The inferred model for the cat data identifies not just two different representational organizations, but large individual differences in the weight given to the dimensions by individuals. The inferred model for the color data explains all of the individual differences associated with color-blindness in terms of dimensional attention, consistent with theory, and more simply than previous analyses that identified two different representational organizations.

The second contribution of our work is to set the K-INDSCAL model within a Bayesian framework. The obvious advantage of this approach is the complete and coherent handling of uncertainty in inference, and this advantage is clear in our applications. The posterior distributions for spatial representations, dimensional weights, and latent-class memberships show how much certainty is reasonable in drawing conclusions. This makes it possible to assess psychologically important properties of spatial representations, such as whether one potential cluster of stimuli is separated from another potential cluster, or whether a dimension weight clearly indicates a subject is giving greater attention to one dimension than another. We also used more specific Bayesian capabilities when appropriate in the analyses, such as exploring the disagreement between Bayesian and classical inferences about group membership for the cola data using posterior predictive assessment conditional on the conflicting inferences. There are, however, a number of potential advantages of Bayesian inference not used in our analyses, which should be incorporated in future work. The determination of the number of latent classes, and the dimensionality of those latent classes, for example, are model selection problems that are well addressed conceptually by Bayes factors ( Kass & Raftery, 1995 ). Unfortunately, Bayes factors are difficult to approximate computationally for the models we consider, and we relied on more heuristic methods. Future work should try and solve all of the parameter inference and model selection challenges raised by models like K-INDSCAL in fully Bayesian ways. In addition, although we have argued that our modeling results produce stimulus dimensions that have natural interpretations, these interpretations were mostly subjective and post-hoc. Future work should take a more systematic and empirical approach to the development and evaluation of these interpretations. For example, Bayesian methods allow for predictions about people’s behavior in tasks that assess the meaning and salience of stimulus dimensions, such as categorization tasks that manipulate the attention given to psychologically separable dimensions ( Nosofsky, 1984; Nosofsky & Palmeri, 1996 ).

We think the most important direction for future work, however, lies at the intersection of the individual differences modeling and Bayesian inference methods we have considered. One of the important possibilities that Bayesian methods afford cognitive modeling is the possibility of developing theoretically richer and more complete models ( Lee, 2011 ). These possibilities arise from the ability of Bayesian methods to make inferences naturally and scalable for more complicated models, including hierarchical and mixture models. The K-INDSCAL model is a latent-mixture model, and so the applications we have presented already make some use of Bayesian capabilities. But many further modeling extensions are worth exploring, and could be pursued within the current Bayesian approach. It would be possibly for example, to consider hierarchical structure in the dimension weights, providing a more detailed and interpretable psychological model of these individual differences. A more ambitious extension would consider incorporating models of decision making to the current models of representation. Our approach made the standard MDS modeling assumption that a matrix of similarities or proximities is the basis for inference. A more complete cognitive model, however, would provide an account of the raw behavioral data, involving the ratings or others decisions individual subjects made trial-by-trial. This sort of generative approach–attempting to model how latent representations and processes produce behavior–is well suited to Bayesian inference on probabilistic models.
