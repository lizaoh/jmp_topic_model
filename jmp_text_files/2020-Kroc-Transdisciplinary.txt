There are five widely used measurement error models in the statistical sciences: the classical test theory model in psychometrics; the errors independent of true scores model in economics; the classical measurement error (or errors in variables) model predominantly used by statisticians, epidemiologists, and econometricians; the Berkson measurement error model used in studies of instrumental variables in, for example, epidemiology; and finally, the weak errors in variables model common in econometrics.

The motivation for this paper is twofold: (1) to explicitly define, describe, and compare these five similar measurement error models in a single framework; (2) to explicate the differences between these different models with the intention of clarifying when one particular model might be most appropriate for applied purposes. These models have become discipline-specific in their use and description. To our knowledge, whether and how these models relate have not been explicitly investigated. The purpose of this paper is to present and prove a sequence of propositions ( Propositions 5.1–5.9 ) that describe the connection between these five models, as well as their connection to an additional model formerly introduced in  Kroc and Zumbo (2018)  that generalizes all of them.

The need for such clarity becomes apparent when one reviews the classical test theory (CTT) literature, which is littered with false characterizations of its measurement error model. For example,  Fife, Mendoza, and Terry (2012, p. 864)  incorrectly state that classical test theory defines that true scores and errors are independent, when in fact this characterizes what we refer to as the  errors independent of true scores  (EIT) model common in econometrics. As we show in this paper, these models are  not  directly comparable. Under certain conditions (see  Proposition 5.3 ), the EIT model is a stronger version of the CTT model, but in such a case it imposes an extremely rigid measurement error structure on the population of individuals. Such a structure is not usually reasonable for psychometric applications as it does not allow, for example, for some group of individuals to be subject to more or less measurement error variance than some other group of individuals (see Section  5 ).

Another more recent example of CTT mischaracterization comes from  Chalmers (2018, p. 1057) . In this piece, the author misspecifies the CTT model as what we refer to in this paper as the  weak errors in variables  (WEV) model. This measurement error model is characterized by the conditions that the expected value of the random error should be zero, as well as the covariance between the errors and the true scores. Both of these conditions are  consequences  of the CTT model (see  Propositions 5.4 and 5.6 ), but the CTT idea of measurement error induces a far richer structure than that of the WEV model. In particular, the WEV specification is not strong enough to ensure that random errors balance on every individual in the research population. Moreover, it is not even strong enough to ensure that errors cancel out over a group of individuals with the same true score (see  Proposition 5.6 ). Such a measurement error model makes little sense in a psychometrics setting.

It is important to note that the focus of this paper is on the mathematical structure of the models and not on estimation or inference. As a consequence, we are required to lay a common mathematical foundation in which to discuss the similarities and differences of these models. This naturally imposes the need to use the language of measure theory. Such a need has been previously recognized and exploited to great advantage, for example in  Zimmerman (1975)  and  Zimmerman and Zumbo (2001) .

We recognize that measure theory is not a common language among non-mathematicians. However, it is absolutely necessary for establishing the connections and distinctions between the measurement error models considered in this paper in full generality, for three main reasons.

This article is organized as follows. First, the six measurement error models will be formally described. In the next section, the relationships between the six measurement error models will be described and the reasons for why these six measurement models are both unique and partially nested will be indicated. Next, in the Discussion section, we take a broader look at the usefulness of the relation between the models for both measurement theorists and practitioners. The final section contains the general mathematical propositions, and proofs thereof, arising from our analysis.

Given the common conceptual interplay between classical test theory and item response theory (IRT), it is important to note here that in no way is the CTT model equivalent, or even necessarily comparable, to the IRT measurement model. In particular, IRT is not a measurement  error  model according to the definition we employ in this paper. It is true that certain IRT models (e.g. the family of 1- and 2-parameter IRT models) can mimic certain features and derivative quantities of the CTT model via certain factor analytic formulations (most notably, the concepts of standard error of measurement and reliability), but such formulations do not recover the axiomatic error structure of the CTT model ( Raykov & Marcoulides, 2016; Takane & de Leeuw, 1987 ). We are unaware of how the IRT model can be written in such a way as to recover this error structure, or the exchangeability condition of the CTT model (see Discussion).

Indeed, there does not seem to be a single, unified notion of what does and does not constitute IRT. Some authors are extremely broad in their specification; e.g.  Van der Linden and Hambleton (1997)  say that IRT comprises any model “relating the probability of an examinee’s response to a test item to an underlying ability”. This definition is so general that it could arguably encompass every measurement error model considered in this paper. On the other hand, some authors (e.g.  Lord, 1980; Wright, 1984 ) have argued that IRT includes models only as restrictive as the 1-, 2-, and 3-parameter logistic models or ogives.

However, it is true that most practitioners of IRT agree on at least a few key features of the theory. We believe that  Stout (2002)  summarizes this stance quite clearly. In particular, various descriptions of IRT seem to universally subscribe to some notion of  local independence  of test items. Using Stout’s parlance, a test

Section  5  gives the mathematical reasons for why the six measurement error models of  (1) – (8)  are unique and partially nested. However, it is instructive to consider these differences in the context of the actual experiments or observational phenomena under which they should be applied. This aligns well with the general philosophy of generalizability theory that aims to understand measurement processes through an experimental design framework ( Li, Shavelson, Yin, & Wiley, 2015; Shavelson & Webb, 1991 ).

One way to accomplish this is to consider on what space the errors in a particular model balance. More precisely, we can ask what sample units are  exchangeable  under a given model (see  Draper, Hodges, Mallows, & Pregibon, 1993  for a thorough discussion of exchangeability)? Such exchangeability can be mathematically explicated via an appropriate conditional expectation condition (see Section  5.1 ).

Each of the measurement error models we have considered partitions the theoretical responses (equivalently, the theoretical errors) in different ways to achieve different levels of exchangeability of sample units. The resulting exchangeability condition(s) then suggest various experiments that could be conducted in practice to justify the mathematical assumptions of the corresponding measurement error model. Unfortunately, such experiments are usually only possible if one invokes additional assumptions to connect the observed measurement to the true value it attempts to quantify. The reason for this is that we are never, in practice, in a position to actually observe these true quantities and, consequently, cannot observe the errors associated to a particular proxy (i.e. measurement) for them. That is, from the context of sample data, measurement models of the form  
                         
                            X 
                            = 
                            T 
                            + 
                            E 
                         
                       are underspecified.

For the CTT model, the error terms must balance on the individual; this is the requirement that the expected value of the error is zero over all possible measurements of  each particular  individual. The assignment-to-individual function plays a crucial role here, as it defines the set of events that are invariant only under repeated measurement of the  same  individual. Within the context of psychometrics, this type of error balance makes sense: we are concerned with making inferences on an individual’s abilities or traits. Therefore, to avoid the possibility of “bias” from our measurement process, we must assume (or justify) that our observables will balance upon repeated measurements (i.e. repeated testing) for each and every individual simultaneously.

Here, we have used the term “bias” in its colloquial sense, not in its statistical sense. Statistically,  bias  is a property of an estimator relative to its estimand; however, we do not consider estimation of any kind in this paper. Instead, the colloquial “bias” is meant only to convey that the measurement process is accurate on average for the true score under consideration. The more appropriate terminology for this phenomenon is to refer to such a measurement as  calibrated  to the particular true score ( Kroc & Zumbo, 2018; Rosenbaum & Rubin, 1984; Wald, 1940 ).

To formally test this calibration assumption experimentally would require the introduction of additional untestable assumptions. For example, one could propose a parallel test or item, then experimentally examine whether or not both the original test and this parallel test generate the same true score (statistically speaking) for each individual. Of course, this proposition requires invoking the strong assumption about a parallel test, which is itself untestable under our current assumptions. The difficulty in validating the assumptions of the CTT model experimentally, without invoking other strong assumptions, lies in the fact that our errors must be exchangeable over the individual.

In contrast, the CME model assumes that errors balance only on the sample units that generate the same true score. This setup is ideally suited for calibrated inference on a population, where it is unimportant that we are assured our measurement process will, on average and over repeated applications, measure the true score of an individual without error, only that this process will not mismeasure the average true score over the subpopulations that share the same true score. This is a weaker form of exchangeability than that imposed by the CTT model. In the CME context, individuals are exchangeable as long as they share the same true value of the latent trait being measured. To experimentally check this, however, would require knowing apriori how to group individuals by the true value of this latent trait. In practice, one would have to assume such a grouping before one could check if the observed errors balanced over these groups.

Similarly, the BME model assumes that errors balance on the sample units that generate the same observed score. Again, this is a setup that is suitable for making population-level inferences only, since many sample units are assumed to be exchangeable, but no individual sample unit is necessarily assumed to be exchangeable with itself upon repeated measurements.

The EIT model is also very similar to the CME model and in fact insists on the exact same type of exchangeability of sample units. The difference here is that a further distributional assumption is made on the error terms themselves. Not only are the errors expected to balance over sample units sharing the same true score, the error distributions are required to be identical across  all  possible values of the true score. The added assumption implies nothing additional about which units are or are not exchangeable; therefore, it does not affect the would-be targets of inference. This distributional assumption can be exploited, however, to make stronger and/or more efficient inferences, as long as it can be reasonably justified. Note though that  none  of the other measurement error models we have described require any kind of particular distributional structure to the error terms beyond the basic exchangeability conditions. In particular, no parametric assumptions are required of these models. It is interesting to note though that it is somewhat common for practitioners in the test theory and econometrics disciplines to impose a Normal structure to the errors of the EIT or CTT models (see, for example,  Fuller, 2009 , or  Cappalleri, Lundy, & Hays, 2014 ). Such an assumption is mathematically unnecessary. It is also very likely to be difficult or impossible to justify in practice.

Both the WEV and WCE models only require that measurement errors balance over the  entire  population of interest. These models allow for the possibility that there may be particular subpopulations where calibrated measurements are inherently impossible (such a measurement error model is often referred to as  differential ). This may be the case, for example, if survey respondents are prone to exaggerate their opinions on controversial items: there would be no way to justify the assumption that measurement error will balance over any fixed true score, but it may indeed be reasonable to assume that the exaggeration miscalibration introduced by negatively-keyed and positively-keyed respondents will essentially cancel out. Such a situation is suited for the WEV and WCE models.

While we show that the WCE model is explicitly the weakest of the measurement error models considered in this paper, this should not be taken as an endorsement to use this model ubiquitously. In fact, applied researchers should always choose the strongest model that is appropriate for their data processes of interest, and thus allow the exploitation of the richest error structure possible. The WCE model may often be this strongest model (see  Kroc & Zumbo, 2018  for examples), but it should not be the default choice. Indeed, as the discussion of the previous paragraphs suggests, it is the exchangeability conditions of the particular research problem of interest that should dictate the choice of measurement error model.

It is important to note too that any quantities derived from the above models may require their own assumptions to make sense. In practice, any of these measurement error models are usually employed in the service of estimation and inference. While estimation goals can often be accomplished without assumptions beyond those of the appropriate model, when the goals turn inferential, additional assumptions are usually needed. This is not so surprising given that all the models discussed here impose conditions on the  average  structure of the measurement errors in question, but they do not impose conditions on the second-order distributional structure of the errors. Inference requires some quantitative way to control the variability of the errors in question; thus, further assumptions beyond those necessary to define the basic models will be required.

For instance, the classical mathematical object of  reliability  derived of the CTT model requires that both the true score and the error be square-integrable ( Zimmerman & Zumbo, 2001 ). This is an additional assumption that is not required of the original CTT model, but one that is crucial in the inferential framework for the classical test theory.

Finally, we remark that the common error models examined in this paper do not exhaust the full space of linearly additive measurement error models. As noted in  Kroc and Zumbo (2018) , and as is apparent from the footnote in the proof of  Proposition 5.5 , all of these models require integrable error variables. However, it is quite conceivable that such an assumption may not always be reasonable, especially in studies of financial markets, special education (e.g. dyslexia, autism), or exceptionality wherein the focus is on individuals with high or low scores (high or low achievers). In these contexts, ostensible “outliers” are actually of greatest interest. Thus, if one wishes to understand how these extreme responses relate to the spectrum of “typical” responses, presumably via a single mathematical model, heavy-tailed measurement errors are likely to play a key role.
