INTRODUCTION  A key conceptual problem associated with quantitative methods is that of discerning which statements involving measured values  are true merely because of artifacts of measurement procedures, and which are true by virtue of facts about the world,  correctly represented by measured values.  Well-known attempts to make this discrimination are the theory of scale types and admissible statistics (Stevens, 1946, 1957;  Adams, Fagot & Robinson, 1965), and the theory of dimensional invariance in physics (Bridgeman, 1931; Causey, 1969; Luce,  One of the goals of representational measurement theory has been to provide a foundation for such attempts-a detailed general  theory of how "facts about the world" are related to measurement values. The representational theory, exemplified by the  treatise of Krantz, Luce, Suppes and Tversky (1971), describes the deficient statements as those lacking certain invariance  properties, depending on the uniqueness of representations. Representations, and assignments of measurement values, are at  best unique only up to composition with certain endomorphisms; deterInination of these gives an indication of the degree to  which data determine the numerical relations of measurement values in a given measurement model.  In Section 2 we briefly recapitulate the argument for requiring invariance, to motivate later work and pinpoint the relevant  endomorphisms. In Sections 3 and 4, we characterise automorphisms and endomorphisms associated with constant jnd (just  noticeable difference) representations of semiorders and indifference structures.  In Section 5, we show a novel representation theorem for countable semiorders. The classification of possible  representing structures bears on a second conceptual problem of quantitative method, hitherto less tractable than that of  meaningfulness described above. The problem is to give a priori methods for selecting a measurement model, or at least an  account of why a given model is appropriate which goes deeper than the traditional "it works" and "quantitative methods make  computation and objective reporting possible." This problem appears unapproachable in general (to the point of not making  sense?) but using concepts from model theory (mathematical logic) it is possible to obtain some insight into the range of  options remaining once major decisions about a model-choice of basic relational conceptualisation and of the general nature of  the numerical correspondence desired-have been made.  Earlier investigations of the possibilities remaining-namely, choice of representing structure subject to certain  constraints-based on similar assumptions about major decisions being made in advance are Scala (1975) for utility  representation and Narens (1974b) for extensive measurement. In Manders (1977, 1979), I study necessary axioms for  representability from this point of view. Assuming the (firstorder) theory of the representing structure, but not the actual  choice of this structure, is fixed in advance, I give a general theory about how the collection of (first-order) axioms  necessary for representability depends on further properties of a representing structure. It is surprising and puzzling that  the results, which applications show to be quite sharp, require only that representing structures be infinite-in particular,  the notion of numerical structure plays no role.  Our classification of representations of countable semiorders gives elaborate information about how the collection of  necessary axioms for representability may vary depending on the choice of representing structure within a first-order class;  this will be developed elsewhere. DISCUSSION  (1) To see the significance of an analysis such as that in the preceding sections, let us give a brief account of the role a  representation might play. Suppose, as in Section 2, that we can adequately model the aspects of the world (say observational)  relevant in a given situation by treating certain entities as objects or unanalysed individuals and specifying for each of a  fixed repertoire of relations whether or not it held of each n-tuple of objects.  To assert that this relational system is homomorphically embeddable in a numerical structure is to give a (partial) theory of  the phenomena modeled by the relational system. This is perhaps clearest in examples from physics, such as the assignments of  numerical values to position and mass of particles. The choice of numerical domain implicitly specifies the possible relations  among objects. That the theory has a commitment to all possibilities corresponding to values in the numerical domain is  already clear for positions in Newtonian mechanics, and becomes similarly clear for mass in relativistic theory. (Mass being a  function of velocity, a continuum of mass values, corresponding to the continuum of possible velocity values, is assumed  possible.) This commitment is essential to the content of the theory. For example, no finite system of relations (geometric  and otherwise) between actual objects would suffice as a conceptual basis for a formulation of Newtonian mechanics. The  formulation in terms of co-ordinates and quantities gives the theory and results which it does because of the range of  possibilities taken into account by the quantitative formalism. Even the same choice of laws, if possible at all, would lead  to different outcomes on a different domain, such as that obtained by replacing the real numbers by the rational numbers. The  success of the theory counts not just in support of the laws, but also in support of the choice of numerical domain.  Historically, the support for numerical domains constructed from Re has grown so strong that one can hardly conceive of other  domains being used for physical  244 KENNETH L. MANDERS  theories, and does not perceive the choice of domain as the substantive part of the content of a theory which it is.  Of course we do not mean to suggest that the actual choice of the real numbers (e.g., as given by some definition in set  theory) is essential to the content of physical theory: any domain which is isomorphic with respect to the relevant relations  would be just as good. For physics, the relevant relations include the order, addition and multiplication of real numbers.  (2) Now the import of Propositions 11 and 12 (and their analogues for Re instead of Ra) may be seen. Assume that we are  considering merely semiorder structure, rather than semiorder structure together with further relations. The assertion of  homomorphic embeddability in (Ra, Pa) for a given jnd function 6 provides a theoretical conception of the domain and  relationships underlying the phenomena being studied. (4) The reader will have noticed the great difference between representations of finite and denumerable-infinite systems of  data. Finite systems satisfying the semiorder axioms have constant jnd representations; infinite systems may require highly  complex jnd functions. An objection arises: any actual body of observed data is finite. Why worry about infinite systems of  data?  The objection cannot simply be accepted, for it would seem to apply to such apparently noncontroversial cases as the choice of  Archimedean numerical structures in classical geometry and physics, and the opposite choice of nonarchimedean utility  representations in certain cases (e.g., value of human life vs finite combinations of ordinary commodities). Finite  observational data by itself could not determine either of these choices, and so some account of how they could be arrived at  is called for.  In our view observation, for example of spatial relations between objects, is best  480/24/3-5  246 KENNETH L. MANDERS  analysed as a process in which successive observations bear certain relationships to one another. Rather than observing  separate finite spatial configurations at each stage, observations at later stages determine in what ways additional objects  may be incorporated into previous configurations. Such a process of continued observation leads (questions of justification or  inductive procedure aside) to qualitative conclusions about what would be observed if more and more individuals were to be  considered. For example, the reader can easily imagine sequences of observations which would lead to the conclusion (say, in a  psychological sense) that the line segment connecting any two points would turn out to be less than some finite multiple of a  given unit segment, or the conclusion that a subject would consider the value of a human life to be greater than that of any  finite combination of ordinary commodities.  Of course, human beings come to such conclusions after a finite number of obserlogical consequences of some finite body of  observations leads to the notorious philosophical problem of induction. On the other hand, there is a mathematically rigorous  interpretation of the conclusions which follow from an infinite sequence of experimental outcomes.  An infinite sequence of observations may be identified (mathematically) with a countable structure, with as domain the  observed individuals and as relations the observed relations. One might think that this limit structure fails to reflect the  dynamical aspect of observation suggested above, but the quantificational properties of the structure can reflect much of the  dynamics. For example, if space is such that one can always find a new point between two previously observed points and  collinear with these, then the limit structure corresponding to a suitable sequence of observations in which such intermediate  points are consistently explored will reflect this because collinear collections of points will be densely ordered.  Above, we outlined the view that choice of a numerical representing structure forms a substantive part of a quantitative  theory, by providing a hypothesis about what configurations of individuals are possible. As illustrated in the previous  section, countable structures (such as the limit structures of sequences of observations just discussed) pose nontrivial  requirements on putative numerical representing structures, for example, requiring very complex jnd functions. These  requirements (as expressed by indicating the class of structures in which representation is possible) constitute our  mathematically rigorous formulation of "the conclusions which follow from an infinite sequence of observations." That finite  observations themselves do not pose such requirements finds philosophical expression in the problem of induction, and  mathematical expression in the Scott-Suppes result that any finite semiorder has a constant jnd representation (and analogous  theorems for other measurement models).  (5) Let me end on a note of puzzlement. The interpretation of numerical domains as presenting exactly the possible empirical  states of affairs appears to work rather well - in physical theory. I consider it at greater length in Manders (a). But in  psychological measurement, the domain of individuals potentially to, be represented is conceived of as quite small compared  with mar, and representations are viewed as  jnd REPRESENTATIONS OF SEMIORDERS 247  homomorphisms into. Presumably, these views are sound and entirely appropriate in psychological theory. But insofar as my  interpretation of physics is correct, the model of quantitative method according to which representations are not necessarily  onto is a model of a quantitative method quite different from that of physics. And if so, the task remains to provide  interpretations of numerical domains in quantitative psychological theory which would clarify the meaning of such theory in  the way that (I hope) the physical interpretation of numerical domains clarifies the role of those domains in physical theory.  Hopefully, such interpretations would make clear how measurement values in psychological theory occur in psychologically  significant theoretical structures. By themselves, representation theorems into (as above, or as in Krantz et al.) do not  provide such interpretations; they only show that certain formal preconditions for interpretation are satisfied.
