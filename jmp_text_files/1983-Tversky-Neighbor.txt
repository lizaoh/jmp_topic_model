The second moment of N(i, n) offers a measure of the centrality of  the ensemble. The use of centrality as a diagnostic statistic for multidimensional scaling is explored.  1. INTRODUCTION  Proximity data in general and nearest neighbor relations in particular are common in psychology as well as in other social and  biological sciences. Group structure is often investigated by sociometric ratings in which each person indicates which member  of the group he or she likes best, prefers to work with, or meets most frequently. These data are used to study the centrality  or popularity of particular individuals as well as the sociometric structure of the group as a whole (see, e.g., Coleman,  1964). In cognitive psychology, the organization of memory is sometimes explored by investigating the stimuli that are most  frequently confused with a given target in a recall or a recognition test (see, e.g., Shepard, 1980). And the perceived  structure of semantic categories, such as animal or furniture, can be revealed by judgments of typicality or similarity  between the instances of the category (see, e.g., Rosch, 1975). In many situations, however, it is difficult  to obtain a complete proximity ordering because the object set is too large or because quartenary comparisons (a is closer to  b than c is to d) are either not meaningful or difficult to perform. Indeed, many proximity relations observed in the social  sciences (e.g., free association or sociometric ratings) do not provide a proximity order, only nearest neighbor data. Such  data are also common in biology and ecology because it is considerably easier to identify the nearest neighbor of an element  in a population (e.g., the nearest tree to a given tree in a forest) and to measure its distance than to measure all  interpoint distances; see Ripley (1981, Chaps. 7 and 8) and references therein. Because nearest neighbor relations arise  naturally in many contexts, we analyze their formal properties and investigate their asymptotic behavior under certain  statistical models. In particular, we introduce a centrality statistic C that can be used to describe nearest neighbor  relations and to diagnose spatial representations of proximity data.  We begin with an ensemble of objects, or points, denoted by S = {0, 1,..., n } and a nearest neighbor relation N that  associates each point with its nearest neighbor. Thus, iN. means that i is the closest neighbor of j in S. We assume that N is  irreflexive so that a point cannot be its own nearest neighbor. We also assume that there are no ties, or else that ties are  broken at random, so that each point has a single nearest neighbor. In general N is not symmetric; iNj may hold without JNi  signifying that i is the closest point to j, but there is some point k such that i is closer to k than to j. At the other extreme, suppose every member of  some group S is the closest friend of his closest friend so that N is symmetric. In fact, it is impossible to arrange points in the plane so that one point is the nearest  neighbor of 6 others. The maximal number of points in the plane that can have the same nearest neighbor is 5. The center and  the vertices of a perfect (or nearly perfect) pentagon achieve this value; in a perfect hexagon the "radius" is no longer  shorter than the edges. More generally, the dimensionality d of the solution space sets an upper bound, denoted Md, on the  maximal value of N(i, n). It is easy to see that in the unidimensional case a point can be the nearest neighbor of at most 2  points, so M, = 2. As we have just noted, M2 = 5, and it is not difficult to show that M3 = 11. The values of Md for higher  dimensions were discussed by Leech and Sloane (1971).  Although high values of N(i, n) may be used to rule out two- or three-dimensional representations, Md increases rapidly with  d, hence it is of limited practical use for assessing the adequacy of high-dimensional representations. To obtain a more  powerful diagnostic method, we append to the geometric assumptions about the solution space some statistical assumptions about  the underlying distribution of points.  When the nearest neighbor relation N is generated by some random point process, the centrality of the random point process  could be described by parameters such as the variance Var[N(i, n)], or the second moment, E[N(i, n)2], where E denotes  expectation. In the models described below all points are exchangeable and E[N(i, n)] = 1 for all i = 0,..., n, hence, E[N(i,  n)2] = Var[N(i, n)] + 1. By exchangeability, E[N(i, n)2] does not depend on i and we denote it by y(n). Clearly, y(n)=E(C),  and the statistic C then is a natural estimator of the centrality parameter y(n). A statistic equivalent to C (transformed to  make its range independent of n) was introduced by Coleman (1964, Sec. 14.1) as a measure of hierarchization. The  distributional properties of C and N(i, n) under the simple model of random choice of nearest neighbor are easily obtained.  These properties were used by Coleman to investigate the hypothesis that the sociometric ratings, obtained by Moreno and  Jennings (1945), were generated by an independent random choice model.  There are many contexts in which models other than random choice are relevant. Of particular interest are random geometric  models where the nearest neighbor relation is generated by a distance function in a d-dimensional Euclidean space. Such  spatial models arise naturally in connection with multidimensional scaling (see e.g., Shepard, Romney, & Nerlove, 1972) and in  the analysis of spatial phenomena such as the distribution of trees in a forest or molecules in a solution (Ripley, 1981).  Nearest neighbor relations also arise in psychometric data. Consider an aptitude battery consisting of n + 1 tests. The  results of the ith test are described by a vector X(`) = (X(1'),..., Xd)), where Xk') is the score of individual k on test i,  1 < k < d. The distance between tests i and j can be defined in terms of the correlation coefficient, or in terms of the  Euclidean distance, between the vectors of scores.  240 TVERSKY, RINOTT, AND NEWMAN  In recent mathematical work (Newman, Rinott, & Tversky, 1983; Newman & Rinott, Note 2) we investigated the asymptotic  properties of N(i, n) and C under several statistical and geometric models. IMPLICATIONS AND APPLICATIONS  Our interest in the nearest neighbor relation grew from the study of multidimensional scaling in which objects are typically  represented as points in some lowdimensional space so that the order of the distances between the points matches the  dissimilarity order between the respective objects. The mathematical analysis of nearest neighbor relations may provide new  tools for evaluating spatial representations of proximity data.  As was noted earlier, the dimensionality of the solution space sets an upper bound on the maximal value of N(i, n). If N(i, n)  > 5 the data cannot be perfectly represented in two dimensions; if N(i, n) > 11, more than three dimensions are required, etc.  (Leech and Sloane, 1971). These constraints are purely geometric, they do not depend on the distribution of the points. The  mathematical development reviewed in Section II incorporates both geometric and statistical assumptions. It assumes that the  objects can be treated as a sample of iid points from some continuous distribution (not necessarily uniform) in a  d-dimensional space and that the nearest neighbor relation is determined by the lp (power) metric, p > 1, or by the  correlation between the variables. The geometric assumptions are analyzed in Beals,  POINT PROCESS APPLICATIONS 247  Krantz, and Tversky (1968); the psychological interpretation of the statistical assumptions is discussed in Schwarz and  Tversky (1980, Section III).  The statistical assumptions introduced in this paper allow the derivation of the asymptotic distribution of N(i, n) and the  expected value of C. Unlike the geometric bound Md, which is highly sensitive to d, the present results show that under  certain assumptions when the number of points n is large relative to the dimension d, the expected degree of centrality is  practically insensitive to the dimensionality of the space. Furthermore, when n is large, the nearest neighbor relation is  determined locally, hence the statistical properties of N(i, n) are essentially independent of the probability density of the  underlying distribution of points. (Schwarz and Tversky (1980) obtained similar results for measures of reciprocity, such as  the proportion of points for which the nearest neighbor relation N is symmetric; see also Cox (1981).) For example, points  generated by a uniform distribution and points generated by a process that produces spatial clusters give rise to the same  asymptotic distribution of N(i, n). For the same reason, similar values of C are expected for dense and for sparse regions of  the space. Nearest neighbor data for several populations of plants, reported by Clark and Evans (1955), provide instructive  examples in which N(i, n) approximate the Poisson (.l = 1) distribution, while the spatial distribution of the plants exhibit  a considerable degree of clustering.  The limit theorems reviewed in this paper suggest the following conclusions. If the number of iid points is sufficiently large  in relation to the number of dimensions then N(i, n) approximates a Poisson distribution and C is close to 2 as implied by (6)  and (7). An observed value of C that is substantially larger than 2 therefore may signify either (i) a failure of the  statistical assumptions in a relatively low-dimensional space produced by a nonrandom configuration of points, or (ii) a  relatively highdimensional space and an underlying distribution with high kurtosis. Theoretical and computational results  indicate that the latter case does not arise commonly in standard models. Hence, the degree of centrality for random samples  in a space of reasonable dimensionality is rarely much greater than 2.  If the dimensionality is known (e.g., when nearest neighbors are determined by actual distances in two- or three-dimensional  space, or when d corresponds to sample size as in the aptitude-test example) the C-statistic can be used to test the  statistical assumption that the points can be viewed as a random sample from some underlying distribution. Suppose, for  example, that the points are the vertices of disjoint pentagons with one point in the middle of each pentagon. Such a  structure may correspond to a set of camp sites consisting of six tents per site, where the leader's tent is in the center of  the pentagon. The C-value for each site then is (5' + 1 ')/6 = 4.3, which is also the C-statistic for the entire ensemble. The  application of multidimensional scaling to such data is expected to recover the original configuration of tents and to match  the observed value of C. The observation of high C in the data as well as in the corresponding (low-dimensional) spatial  representation, therefore, provides evidence against the iid assumption.
