Mathematical Psychology can be subdivided into several research areas, e.g. Batchelder (2010), butwithout question the largest area is the development of cognitive models in the so-called softer areas of cognition. These areas include learning, memory, categorization, concept acquisition, choice response time, and attention, and they are referred to as â€˜softâ€™ because unlike vision, audition, and psychophysics, they lack corresponding physical science substrates connected with them. Some would include decision making in the soft category, although, unlike the others, there is another science, namely economics, connected to this area. Today, of course, there are researchers attempting to connect all of these areas to cognitive neuroscience; however, I can confidently assert that such efforts were almost completely absent in the early days of cognitive modeling. I believe that it is indisputable that Estesâ€™ early approach to mathematical modeling in these softer areas has led to the establishment of standards for this sort of work that remain operative today. Estesâ€™ stimulus sampling theory, e.g. Atkinson and Estes (1963), was certainly a precursor to later approaches to constructing cognitive models, e.g. Healey et al. (1992a,b); however, inmy view, it is his approach tomodeling rather than the particular models that he created, that will end up having a lasting effect. Estesâ€™ approach was to select experimental paradigms with explicit data structures that directly revealed phenomena in an area. Examples include probability learning experiments, e.g. Estes (1964), the RTT procedure in paired-associate learning, e.g. Estes, Hopkins, and Crothers (1960), and a paradigm to evaluate what is initially available in immediate visual memory for letters, e.g. Estes and Taylor (1964). The next step in Estesâ€™ approach was to construct statistical models for these experimental paradigms. All suchmodels specified hypothetical cognitive structures alongwith associated parameters that could predict the statistical structure of the data, and consequently the models were evaluated on their relative ability to closely fit data. Often there were several models that could predict data patterns in a particular paradigm, and the goal then became one of selecting the model that best predicted the data, and rejecting or augmenting themodels that failed in this contest. As a graduate student at Stanford, I recall many exciting timeswhen a statistical analysis showed that a particular cognitive model appeared to give a better fit to a particular set of data than a competitor, and this sort of excitement continued and still continues decades after Estesâ€™ early successes of this sort. As I reflect on the evolution of cognitive modeling from the 1960s to present, I am struck by how much of this work could be characterized in this way. For example, in standard experimental paradigms like simple recognition memory, free recall, choice response time, and categorization, there are dozens of models that have been invented by many different psychologists to predict data in which one model squares off against another. Of course, to evaluate the fit of a model to a set of data involving multiple subjects responding to multiple items, one has to make assumptions about whether or not the parameters are identical across subjects and/or like type items, and in addition whether or not the successive responses of the subjects to the items are realizations of independent events. The usual assumption in most applications of cognitive modeling from the 1950s up to the early 2000s was to assume all the observations to like-type items were independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.), which to my mind today seems absurd on its face. Estes was aware of these issues even in the 1950s, and Estes (1956) is a famous article describing the problems that can occur if heterogeneity is not properly modeled. He pointed the way to identifying this problem, and others published articles similarly exhibiting how things can go very wrong if inappropriate sampling assumptions are made.atical Psychology 59 (2014) 3â€“5 An extremely important step in overcoming these problems has been the incorporation of Bayesian hierarchical modeling, e.g. Lee (2011), which is a natural statistical way to handle heterogeneity. Bayesian statistics goes back for several centuries, and in the past half century or so it has become the standard approach to statistical inference in the statistical sciences. However, it is only in the last ten or so years that cognitive modelers have begun to pose their models hierarchically and employ computational Bayesian inference to evaluate them. Another issue that modelers face when applying Estesâ€™ ap- proach to cognitive modeling in very complex situations is exactly how to decide if one model fits the data better than another. Un- der i.i.d. assumptions,modelers began to use some classical asymp- totic measures like the Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) and the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC), but their use requires that one be able to obtain the likelihood function for the model. When the models became very complex, theorists often resorted to com- puter simulation to show that onemodel could fit a particular data pattern better than another. A seminal addition to the Estes ap- proach of comparing the fit of cognitivemodels camewhenmodel- ers learned about work in model selection in statistics, e.g. Myung et al. (2000). Since then it has become clear in the cognitive mod- eling community that some models are better able to fit data than others by the functional formof their specifications rather than just by some simple goodness-of-fit measure. The statistically relevant term is â€˜overfitting,â€™ and such models are able to wrap around sta- tistical noise as well as the signal in the data, and a proper compar- ison of models therefore requires that one incorporate a penalty for the overfitting potential of a model in evaluating its fit. Conse- quently those following the Estes approach to cognitive modeling today often employ either Bayesian or classical frequentist model selection measures when comparing their models. With these relative recent additions to the Estesâ€™ approach to cognitive modeling, things now seem to be on a solid statistical footing. Recent books and conference proceedings reveal that cognitivemodeling in the Estes style is here to stay, and it has even begun to spread into new areas of cognition, including cognitive neuroscience. To my knowledge, Estes never applied modern model selection and Bayesian methods to his ownmodeling work. Estes himself was very conservative in the sense that it took a great deal of evidence to lead him to change his views. This is a good scientific trait; however, I regret that he was not alive for a few more years so that we could see his reactions to the current level of statistical sophistication that characterizes cognitive modeling today. In any event, I strongly believe that the received approach to constructing and testing cognitive models in the softer areas of cognition is due to Estes. It evolved from his early modeling work, and it continued not only in his later work but also more importantly by the many psychologists that he has influenced along the way. It is this area where Estesâ€™ influence will have a lasting influence on the field of psychology, and his foundational work will continue to inspire new generations to move the field forward.
