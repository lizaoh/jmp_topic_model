JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 25, 185 (1982)  A Response to Yellott and Ahumada's Review of "Visual Perception: Theory and Practice" by Terry Caelli  TERRY CAELLI  The University of Newcastle  In the recent review of another book the author (Caelli, 1981) suggests that there are four pertinent questions we can ask of  a book. One, does it supply the reader with new, relevant information and ideas? Two, is it creative insofar as it generates  new constructs (for example, applications of mathematics) in the area? Three, is it integrated, or, is there a theme? Four, is  it of value for the money? Usually one tries to answer these questions in some detail in an extended review.  Unfortunately, in my opinion, Yellott and Ahumada have not satisfactorily answered these questions with respect to the  author's book. Their review consists almost entirely of error listing, with only two paragraphs dealing explicitly with the  substantive issues of the book. Furthermore, their inference that these errors are only a sample of many more errors is simply  not true. This is not to excuse the fact that this first printing has a number of errors due to poor galley-proof checking,  the author having had no access to others for double checking.  However, as is even implied by the reviewers, the book does deal with topics other than errors and their review of the  applications of systems theory, geometry, and stochastic processes to vision would also have been useful.  Let me simply conclude by saying that the author noted such errors long before these reviewers, and the corrections are in the  process of being made. Maybe once officially published these reviewers would be prepared to actually review the book's  substantive contents.  REFERENCES  CAELLI, T.
