JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 28, 43-72 (1984)  Generality of a Strength Model for Three Conditions of Repeated Recall  ALEX CHERRY WILKINSON AND RONALD KOESTLER  University of Wisconsin-Madison  A Markovian model of repeated recall is presented that is based on four memory processes: (a) a presentation increment that  strengthens the memory trace of a word when the word is shown to a subject, (b) a recall increment that strengthens the trace  when the subject successfully recalls the word, (c) a cueing decrement that weakens the trace as contextual cues for  retrieving a word change over successive trials of attempted recall, and (d) a sampling rule that translates the strength of a  memory trace into a probability of recall. Mathematically, the model predicts the relative frequencies of the recall patterns  that are defined by sequences of recalls and forgets for individual words over trials. According to the model, the  recallability of a word depends on its state, which is a function of (a) whether the word has been recalled for the first  time, and if so, then (b) how many consecutive forgets of the word immediately precede the current trial, (c) how many  successful recalls of the word have accumulated, and (d) how many previous trials of attempted recall have elapsed. Paradigms  that were well described by the model were (a) no reminding, in which words were presented once and then recalled repeatedly  without additional presentations; (b) restricted reminding, in which words were presented before each trial of attempted  recall up to their first successful recall but not thereafter; and (c) complete reminding, in which all words were presented  before each attempted recall, whether previously recalled or not. Differences in recall among these conditions were explained  in terms of differences in values of the model's parameters.  GENERALITY OF A STRENGTH MODEL FOR  THREE CONDITIONS OF REPEATED RECALL  In a recent article (Wilkinson & Koestler, 1983) we presented a theory of repeated recall, formalized the theory in a  mathematical model, and demonstrated the validity of the model for human subjects over a wide range of ages. In this article,  we investigate the generality of our model by applying it to the recall condition of our previous research and to two new  conditions. The three conditions in the present study were all versions of repeated free recall. All rights of reproduction in any form reserved.  44 WILKINSON AND KOESTLER  Repeated recall is a process of successively attempting to recall an entire set of items (Buschke, 1977), with a period of  delay or interference separating the attempts. Our previous research involved repeated recall of words that were presented  once prior to the initial attempt to recall, but were not presented again before any of the subsequent attempts. The data of  interest were patterns of recalls (R) and forgets (F) for individual words across the several trials of attempted recall. One  such pattern, for example, is RFRRF, which designates recall on all but the second and fifth trials. Different recall patterns  occur with different frequencies, and our mathematical model predicted the relative frequencies of the various patterns. The  model, and hence the psychological theory that generated it, was supported by good agreement between predicted and empirical  frequencies. The theory is explicated in Wilkinson and Koestler (1983). Here we shall summarize the theory, concentrating on  four concepts that generated hypotheses concerning differences between recall conditions.  Our first major concept is that when a word is given to be remembered, a trace of the word in memory may have its strength  boosted. This presentation increment is not guaranteed; it is probabilistic. The trace is assumed to exist in memory prior to  presentation, because the subject knows the word. A boost in the strength of the trace occurs when new associations are formed  that link the trace to cues for retrieving it. We assume the presentation increment to be probabilistic because the new  associations may or may not be formed successfully. If and only if they are formed successfully, the word is stored in memory.  An increment in the strength of the trace may occur each time a word is presented. With multiple presentations, it is possible  that a single trace has its strength incremented repeatedly (as in Raaijmakers & Shiffrin, 1981) or that several different  traces of the same word receive increments in their individual strengths (as in Bower, 1967). We did not try to differentiate  these two possibilities empirically. Rather, we tested the general concept of a presentation increment by using recall  conditions that varied in the frequency with which words were presented.  We assume that retrieval is contextually cued (Estes, 1955; Raaijmakers & Shiffrin, 1981; Tulving & Thomson, 1973). When a  word is stored, at least one association is formed that links the word to attributes of the current environmental and  psychological context (Bower, 1981; Smith, 1979). The environmental context consists of perceptible features of the physical  setting in which the words are presented, including general features such as ambient noise or light and specific ones such as  the shape of a chair or the design of the wallpaper. The psychological context consists of the subject's emotional or  physiological state and ideas or plans that the subject is thinking about. Significantly, the phychological context for a  given word at a particular time may include other words that are active in memory at that time (Raaijmakers & Shiffrin, 1981).  The psychological context is the major concern of the present research, for we kept the environmental context the same  throughout a subject's participation in the experiment.  Recall of a word that was successfully stored in associative memory depends on cues generated from context at the time of an  attempt to recall. Importantly, during the period intervening between storage of a word and subsequent attempts to recall it,  MODEL OF RECALL 45  the associations available for retrieving the word may change. One cause of a change in associations is that the context is  assumed to be dynamic (Smith, Glenberg, & Bjork, 1978; Glenberg, Bradley, Kraus, & Renzaglia, 1983). Some attributes of the  context at the time of storage may be lost and unrecoverable; others may be lost but then be reinstated by methods such as  imagery; others may have remained active continuously since the word was presented; and still other cues may be wholly new.  Thus, contextual cues evolve inexorably over time. Change in contextual cues tends to diminish the probability of recalling a  word, because the context at the time of attempted recall is likely to include some new, irrelevant cues and to exclude some  of the cues that were associated with the word at the time of its storage. This cueing decrement is our second major concept.  To measure the severity of this decrement, our mathematical model used the number of trials intervening since the last  successful recall of a word.  A changing context is not the only cause of a change in the associations available in memory for retrieving a word. Another  cause is that successful recall of a word generates new associations. The newly generated associations may link the recalled  word to other recently recalled words or to other features of the current context. In effect, successful recall boosts the  associative strength of a memory trace (Raaijmakers & Shiffrin, 1981). This recall increment is our third major concept. Our  mathematical model measured the magnitude of the increment by estimating how much the probability of recall increased when the  number of previous recalls increased.  Our last major concept is a sampling rule, which we adapted from Raaijmakers and Shiffrin (1981) and from Luce (1959).  According to the sampling rule, an item's probability of recall is determined by the number of associative pathways that  include the item. Specifically, the probability is equal to the proportion of the total number of associative pathways through  memory that include the item. We assume that in a given context, one of many possible pathways through memory is chosen. How a  particular path is chosen depends on a process of search and retrieval. We make no assumptions about the nature of this  process. We merely assume that one path is sampled from many that are possible and that the probability of recalling a given  item is the probability of sampling a path containing that item. An important consequence of the sampling rule is that adding  new associative pathways to a particular item will cause an increase in that item's likelihood of recall only if the new paths  to the item outweigh other new paths to other items. Thus, increments caused by presentation or recall for a given word  interact with increments for other words and with cueing decrements; in effect, the sampling rule is the arbiter of these  interactions.  The conditions of repeated recall for the present study were derived from Buschke (1977). In the conventional condition, which  we shall call complete reminding, one list of words had its order randomized and was presented in full before each attempted  recall. In the condition of restricted reminding, the words that had never been recalled on any previous trial were presented  in a newly randomized order before each attempted recall. Finally, in the condition with no reminding, which we had used in  our previous research, the words were presented only before the first  480/28/1-4  46 WILKINSON AND KOESTLER  attempted recall. One objective of the present study was to ascertain whether our mathematical model would fit data well for  all three of these conditions.  A second objective was to determine whether differences among the conditions in parameters of the mathematical model were  predictable from the concepts of recall increment, presentation increment, and cueing decrement and from the sampling rule.  The three reminding conditions are interesting because they differ in presentation frequency and produce marked differences in  amount of recall; in addition, there are subtle but predictable differences in cueing decrements. These differences can be  expressed as predictions concerning parameters in our mathematical model. We shall present the predictions in detail when we  come to the relevant data. We begin, however, by specifying the model.  MATHEMATICAL MODEL  The model that we define in this section is slightly modified from the one we used previously (Wilkinson & Koestler, 1983).  The major characteristics of the model are: (a) It is Markovian, with probabilities for discrete transitions that derive from  an underlying continuum of memory strength. (b) Certain probabilities in the model specify the likelihood of recalling a word  for the first time on a given trial. (c) After a word's first recall, the probability of recalling it again exhibits three  effects and can be represented, therefore, as varying in three dimensions. (d) The three effects are constrained to be  additive on a log-odds scale. (e) The probabilities of first recall and the probabilities for the three effects can be  expressed as four independent, monotonic functions.  Description of Parameters  The mathematical model is defined, in part, by a wedge of probabilities. The wedge forms the corner of a cube in a  three-dimensional space, as illustrated in Fig. 1. The dimensions correspond to three memory functions, which we call the  discrepancy, accumulation, and relativity functions. In addition to the wedge, there is also a line of probabilities that  corresponds to a fourth memory function. This line lies outside the three-dimensonal space and is separate from the wedge; it  is the first recall function, which specifies the probability of recalling a word for the first time on a given trial. Memory  functions within the three-dimensional wedge, in contrast, concern the probability of recalling a word on trials after the  word's first recall.  It is convenient to begin with the probabilities in the wedge. Mathematically, a parameter in the wedge is computed from three  predictor variables that specify a history of preceding recalls or forgets. The predictor variables are leading forgets (i),  cumulative prior recalls (j), and number of previous attempts to recall (k). Probability of recall is determined by these  variables and is designated r(i, j, k). Not all combinations of the predictor variables can occur. For example, with two  previous recall attempts there can be zero, one, or two cumulative prior recalls but not more. Markov Equation  For a word that has already been recalled once, the probability of the word being recalled on a given trial depends on its  pattern of leading forgets, cumulative prior recalls, and previous attempted recalls. For example, the pattern RFRF designates  a word remembered on trials one and three but forgotten on trials two and four. This word's probability of being recalled on  trial five is r(1, 2, 4), because it has a pattern of one leading forget, two cumulative prior recalls, and four previous  attempts to recall. There is another pattern FRRF which, although different from RFRF, has the same values on the three  predictor variables. Its probability of being recalled on trial five is also r(l, 2, 4). Thus a single r(i,j, k) parameter may  apply to more than one pattern. METHOD  Subjects  The subjects were 52 undergraduate students enrolled in introductory psychology. Each subject earned course credit for  participating in the experiment.  Word Lists  A subject recalled three 30-word lists, each under a different recall condition. Orders of both lists and conditions were  randomly assigned for each subject. The words in a list were all one or two syllabes long, and were drawn from Battig and  Montague (1969), with each word on a list taken from a different semantic category.  Procedure  Testing took place in a single session lasting 45 to 60 min. The subject sat in a sound-attenuated booth looking at a CRT  monitor that was controlled by a microcomputer. The experimenter was outside the booth at a terminal connected to the  computer, allowing him to input subject responses and to monitor the progress of the experiment. An intercom system allowed  the experimenter to hear and speak to the subject.  Subjects saw the words on the CRT monitor. The response portion of every trial was tape recorded. The recorder was turned on  and off by the computer, ensuring that no response was missed. Timing was controlled by a clock in the computer with an error  of f 8 msec.  Presentation of a list of words began when the subject responded to the visual prompt "Say ready to begin." A countdown of  numerals from 5 to 1 initiated the presentation; then came the words of the list, presented in a random order. Each number in  the countdown was displayed for 500 msec, and the subject was instructed to read each one aloud. Each word was displayed for  400 msec and then masked with C! characters for 100 msec, resulting in an interword interval of 500 msec. The subject was to  say each word aloud once as it was presented.  After presentation of the last word on a list, there were five trials, which differed across the three conditions. All the  conditions had in common a three-part cycle consisting of an initial interference task, a middle period of attempted recall,  and a  MODEL OF RECALL 53  final interference task. The conditions differed with respect to what followed the final interference task. In the no  reminding condition, the three-part cycle for one trial was followed immediately by the three-part cycle for the next trial.  In the complete reminding condition, the three-part cycle was followed by presentation of the entire list of words. In the  restricted reminding condition, a partial list consisting of only the words not recalled on any previous trial followed each  The initial interference task in each three-part cycle lasted 30 sec. It began with the prompt "Read the following numbers  aloud," which was presented for 1 sec and was followed by 58 random numbers between 1 and 100, each of which was presented for  500 msec. The subject was to read each number aloud. Subjects were monitored by the experimenter to ensure that they were  trying to read each number as instructed.  The middle period of attempted recall began when the subject was shown the message "Begin recall of all the words." Sixty  seconds were allowed for recall, and the subject was instructed to use the entire 60 sec to recall as many of the words as  possible. Recall was oral, and the subject was instructed that order of recall was unimportant.  To begin the final interference task, the subject was shown the prompt "Solve the following math problems," followed by five  arithmetic problems. Paper and pencil were available, and the subject had 40 sec to complete the problems. However, the  problems were sufficiently difficult that the subject could not finish them in the allotted time. The purpose of the problems  was to distract the subject from rehearsing the list while the experimenter entered into the computer the words that the  subject had recalled during the preceding recall period. The subject was unaware of the purpose of this interference task.  The general procedure was explained to the subject verbally at the start of the experiment, and each condition was preceded by  additional explanation. The subject took a break between conditions, resting until ready to continue.  Statistical Analyses  To test the Markov model and its various simplifications, we performed two kinds of maximum likelihood tests. In the first  kind of test, we computed maximum likelihood estimates of the parameters and assessed goodness of fit with the likelihood  ratio chi-square. This statistic compared (a) the likelihood of the data under some model, given maximum likelihood estimates  of the model's parameters, and (b) the likelihood of the data, given a saturated model that had no degrees of freedom and fit  the data exactly (Wilkinson & Koestler, 1983, Eq. (B2)). Goodness of fit was measured in this way for a number of models.  Every model was a special case of the general model described above. The individual models differed because certain parameters  were sometimes constrained to be equal across two or three of the reminding conditions, and because we sometimes constrianed  certain parameters to have fixed numerical values. When the likelihood ratio chi-square was computed for a pair of models, one  of which imposed constraints on the other and thereby simplified it, a second kind of test was computed. This second kind of  test determined whether the simplifying constraints caused a significant loss in goodness of fit. The test was  54 WILKINSON AND KOESTLER  performed by subtracting the chi-square values for the two models. The difference in chi-squares is itself distributed  asymptotically as chi-square with degrees of freedom equal to the number of additional constraints imposed in the simplified  model (see, e.g., Mood, Graybill, & Boes, 1974, pp. 419-421).  The data for which we computed the chi-square statistics were frequencies pooled over words and subjects. Strictly speaking,  tests based on these statistics assumed independence of observations that were pooled together. This assumption would be  correct if the parameter values for all individual subjects and individual words were equal. We had too few data from single  subjects and single words, however, to estimate their individual parameters.  All the pooled fits and estimates of parameters were computed with iterative algorithms programmed from standard references on  optimization methods (Bazaraa & Shetty, 1979; Jacoby, Kowalik, & Pizzo, 1972). The algorithm we used in most cases was the  Powell-Zangwill method of conjugate directions. Unless stated otherwise, all statistical tests were conducted with a nominal  significance level of .05. Second, an  60 WILKINSON AND KOESTLER  individual function was assumed to be log-linear or one-step; if either assumption were valid, two parameters would be  required for the function. The log-linear and one-step assumptions were tested separately for each function. If either  assumption resulted in no significant loss in goodness of fit, then the assumption that gave the better fit was accepted as  valid. Finally, if any function had been found to be loglinear or one-step, it was next assumed to be null, requiring only one  parameter. The assumption of a null function was accepted as valid if it led to no significant loss in goodness of fit. We begin with an overview that explains in general how recall  increments, presentation increments, cueing decrements, and the sampling rule, which are the four main concepts of the model,  affected recall in each of the three reminding conditions. Then we explain in detail how these concepts were expected to  affect the shapes of the first recall, discrepancy, accumulation, and relativity functions.  Overview  Our preliminary data showed that recall was poorest with no reminding, was much better with restricted reminding, and was, by  a modest amount, better still with complete reminding (see Fig. 2). Data from the observed frequencies of recall patterns  showed, however, that no reminding was superior to the other two conditions in one respect. The probability of recalling a  word consistently on every trial was .14 with no reminding but .08 with both restricted and complete reminding (see Table 1).  These results may seem paradoxical, but the paradox is resolved by qualitative predictions that follow from the model. The  predictions are summarized in Table 4.  Consider the effects of recall increments, presentation increments, and cueing decrements. On all three counts, no reminding  is the condition least conducive to successful recall. It yields the fewest recall increments because the level of recall is  so low, and affords the fewest presentation increments. It also produces the strongest cueing decrements because in the  absence of any reminding, the most recent context associated with an unrecalled word is always at least one trial old. The  condition of restricted reminding, in contrast, is intermediate on all three counts. It produces an intermediate amount of  recall, hence a moderate rate of recall increments, and it offers presentation increments continuously up to the trial of a  word's first recall. There is little competition among words, because the few that are relatively strong have a high  probability of recall but the rest do not. The result is a tendency for a word to be recalled consistenly on every trial or  not at all. In contrast, with restricted or complete reminding, the recall increment given to a recalled word is opposed by  presentation increments given to other words. Furthermore, the cueing decrement for an unrecalled word can be counteracted if  the word is presented anew. The result is that the strengths of recalled and unrecalled words do not diverge greatly, and no  subset of words is likely to be recalled consistently and exclusively.  This analysis of the results is coarse and descriptive. We can make it more precise and analytical by considering in detail  each of the four memory functions in the model. In doing so, we shall rely heavily on the sampling rule, which gives the  relationship between recall probability and memory strength. We shall refer to the strength ratio, t/T, which is readily  shown, using Eq. (1), to be equal to r/(1 - r) , where r is the probability of recall. Thus, recall probability increases  whenever the strength ratio does.  First Recall  The probability of recalling a given word for the first time after i previous trials depends on the word's relative strength,  r(i, 0, i), as given by the sampling rule in Eq. (1). The first recall function specifies how this probability changes as i  increases. Using the sampling rule, we can describe the function with t(i, 0, i) and t(i + 1, 0, i + 1), which are the given  word's own absolute strengths on two successive trials, and with T(i, 0, i) and T(i + 1, 0, i + 1), which are the summed  absolute strengths of other words on the same two trials. Then  r(i, 0, i) = t(i, 0, i)/ [t(i, 0, i) + T(i, 0, i)]  and  r(i+1,0,i+1)=t(i+1,0,i+1)/[t(i+1,0,i+1)+T(i+1,0,i+1)]. The number of  leading trials on which a word was consecutively forgotten is the variable that defines the domain of the discrepancy function. Expressed in terms of memory strengths under the sampling  rule, these probabilities are  r(i, j, k) = t(i, j, k)/ [t(i, j, k) + T(i, j, k)]  and  r(i,j,k+ 1)=t(i,j,k+ 1)/[t(i,j,k+ 1)+T(i,j,k+ 1) ].  Our predictions for the relativity function are explained by describing how recall increments, presentation increments, and  cueing decrements cause memory strength to change from t(i, j, k) to t(i, j, k + 1) and from T(i, j, k) to T(i, j, k + 1). If the strength ratio changes at  all, the change would most likely be a decline, because the denominator gets both recall and presentation increments while the  numerator gets only presentation increments. To some degree, recall increments and  cueing decrements are offsetting for T with restricted reminding, as they are with no reminding. In addition, however,  presentation increments raise T with restricted reminding. The net effect on T should be an increase. Consequently, the  strength ratio should decline, and the relativity function should have a negative slope. EVALUATING THE MODEL  A perspective for evaluating our model can be taken from questions that were once posed to Bush and Mosteller (1955, p. 333).  "We have often been asked," they wrote, "to explain the sense in which our general model, or one of our specific models,  represents anything more than curve-fitting. Furthermore, we have been asked to explain the sense in which the parameters of  the model are psychologically meaningful." To these two familiar questions, we add a third: How does our model relate to other  formal models of memory that are currently viable?  The question of curve-fitting can be given a cautious answer. If caution were our only concern, we would be reluctant to claim  anything more for the model than that it ably describes sequential dependencies in repeated recall by fitting four memory  functions on the log-odds scale. The descriptive sufficiency of the model is important, one could reasonably argue, because it  draws attention to orderly properties of memory that must eventually be given a theoretical accounting. Like the serial  position curve, our memory functions are data to be explained. Significantly, their existence as data in need of explanation  would not have been known without the model.  If we relax the cautious attitude in favor of a more hopeful one, we can address the question of psychological meaningfulness  by asserting that the model implicitly provides at least the outline of a theoretical explanation for the memory functions.  The sampling rule and the concepts of recall and presentation increments and cueing decrement are the cornerstones of a  psychological theory of memory. To be sure, they do not provide all conceivable details concerning the processes by which  information is stored in memory and subsequently retrieved. They are silent, for example, about processes by which memory  might be searched during attempts to retrieve.  70 WILKINSON AND KOESTLER  Nevertheless, they are sufficient to generate qualitative predictions of the sort we tested for the memory functions of the  mathematical model. Without the model, the concepts would have been difficult to study empirically. In particular, the  concepts themselves imply that the recallability of a word depends on tradeoffs among various factors that strengthen or  weaken a memory trace. Without a model to quantify the tradeoffs, however, the implications would be untestable.  Other models, admittedly, also provide well-quantified and testable predictions about memory. One class of such models  contains the various Markov models of two-stage learning (Brainerd, Howe, & Desrochers, 1982; Greeno, James, DaPolito, &  Polson, 1978; Levine & Burke, 1972). A similarity between a two-stage model and ours is that both are now known to provide a  good account of recall for the condition that we have called complete reminding, with a comparable number of free parameters.  A direct comparison of the two models on a single set of data has not been done, however, and we suspect that data definitely  showing one model to be better than the other may be hard to secure. Furthermore, any similarity between the two models is  outweighed by many differences between them.  One difference concerns the kinds of data that the models can fit. The two-stage model is meant for experimental conditions in  which a subject begins without knowing certain items and gradually learns all of them to a strict criterion of mastery over a  long, theoretically unlimited series of trials. Thus, the model applies to the condition of complete reminding, in which  complete learning should occur eventually if the number of trials is allowed to be arbitrarily large. It also applies to  conditions such as paired associate learning and avoidance conditioning. Our model, in contrast, is meant for conditions in  which recall is attempted repeatedly over a fixed number of trials. Thus, it applies to conditions such as restricted and no  reminding that are not within the domain of the two-stage model, and to a fixed number of trials in any condition for which  the two-stage model would be applicable with unlimited trials.  Another difference between the two kinds of models concerns how memory traces become recallable. The two-stage model assumes a  trace to be in one of three states: not recallable, partially recallable with some probability strictly between zero and one,  or perfectly recallable. The process of learning is defined to be a series of abrupt transitions between these states, with no  transition allowed from any given state to one of lower recallability. - With this definition of learning, transitions from  unrecallability to partial recallability can be interpreted as storage of new information in memory, and transitions from  partial to complete recallability can be viewed as learning to retrieve the information coinsistently. Our model, in contrast,  assumes that a trace may have any of a large number of states, depending on its history of presentations, recalls, and  forgets. In a given state, a trace has a specifiable strength, and the ratio of this strength to other current strengths  determines the recallability of the trace. Thus, the states in our model map onto a continuum of recallability. Although our  model is technically Markovian and as such is concerned with transitions between discrete states, its fundamental purpose is  to explain changes in recallability along the continuum. One trenchant comparison between our model and the two-stage model,  therefore, is that ours looks keenly at the details of partial  MODEL OF RECALL  71  recallability, while the other blurs some of these details by taking a broader perspective spanning all the stages of learning  from initial ignorance to eventual mastery.  Models of two-stage learning are but one class of models with which ours can be compared. Another class is computer simulation  of memory (Anderson, 1976; Anderson & Bower, 1973; Eich, 1982; Metcalfe & Murdock, 1981; Raaijmakers & Shiffrin, 1981). Within  this class, the most pertinent comparison is with the SAMS model of Raaijmakers and Shiffrin, which is like our model in  taking recall memory as its domain. Importantly, concepts closely related to our sampling rule, our recall and presentation  increments, and our cueing decrement are all embodied in SAMS. The two models differ in the manner of implementing these  concepts, but they are qualitatively comparable.  The differences in implementation provide a revealing perspective on the strengths and weaknesses of our model. One difference  is that in SAMS, increments and decrements are applied as actual changes in the numerical values of associative strengths in a  simulated memory, but in our model they are inferred from data by comparing estimated parameters for conditions that differ in  the frequency of recalls and presentations. In SAMS, therefore, the qualitative predictions that we derived from Tables 5-8  could be quantified by having the computer dump the numerical values from its simulated memory. The presence of this  capability in SAMS is one of its strengths, and its absence in our model is a weakness. On the other side of the ledger,  however, our model has strength in an area where SAMS is weak. Given data from repeated recall, our model can be tested  rigorously for goodness of fit and is therefore statistically rejectable. SAMS, in contrast, is meant to be a simulation of  recall in a large number of experimental conditions, and its main criterion of adequacy is the number of conditions for which  it provides a reasonable fit to human data. Some uncertainty is inescapable concerning the number of conditions that must be  well fit for the model to be considered adequate and the number of poor fits that can be tolerated before the model is judged  to be too limited in scope. This ambiguity in standards for judging the adequacy of a model is a price to be paid when the  model aims for generality. Our model avoids the ambiguity by paying, instead, the price of specificity.  REFERENCES  ANDERSON, J. R. (1976). Language, memory, and thought. Hillsdale, NJ: Erlbaum.  ANDERSON, J. R., & BOWER, G.
