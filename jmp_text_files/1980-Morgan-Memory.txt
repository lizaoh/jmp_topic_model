JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 21, 30-52 (1980)  Short-Term Memory Models for Choice Behavior  BYRON J. T. MORGAN AND CHRIS ROBERTSON  Mathematical Institute, University of Kent, Canterbury, Kent, England  In this paper we consider the effect of imposing memory restrictions on the accumulator and random-walk models of choice  behavior outlined by Audley (Psychological Review, 1960, 67, 1-15). Specifically, it is assumed that subjects apply criteria  to the numbers of impulses received, and that if the criteria are not satisfied by the time w impulses have been received,  then thereafter the criteria are only applied to w impulses, which could, for example, be the last w impulses received. Exact  and approximate mathematical approaches and simulation techniques are presented in detail. The results obtained demonstrate  that both the restricted accumulator and the restricted random-walk models can make qualitative predictions in line with much  recent data.  1. INTRODUCTION  Consider a subject choosing between two objects or stimuli; we might assume that the subject is receiving impulses from each  stimulus, and that a stimulus is chosen when the number of impulses received from it by the subject satisfies some specified  stopping rule before the number of impulses received by the subject from the other stimulus satisfies a stopping rule, which  may be different from that for the first stimulus. Models of choice behavior based on such an assumption have been considered  by Audley (1960), Audley and Pike (1965), Bower (1959), Estes (1960), Laberge (1962), and Vickers (1970). Recent reviews and  discussions of these models can be found in Broadbent (1971, pp. 287-296), Broadbent (1973, Chap. 2), and Laming (1968, pp.  22-25), which is also a rich source of references to related work. These "impulses," of course, have not been simply invented  for the expediency of constructing a mathematical model, but rather are shorthand for, for example, "vicarious trial and error  responses," as sometimes observed in rats in mazes (Audley, 1960), "incipient responses," such as may occur in humans before  deciding to rise in the morning (Audley, 1970), or even representations of an hypothetical likelihood ratio for testing  whether one stimulus is, in some sense, greater than another (Vickers, 1970). In Pike (1973) they result from multiple  observations during signal detection.  The relationship of these models to data has been considered by, for example, Audley (1970), Audley and Mercer (1968),  Petrusic and Jamieson (1978), Pike (1968), and Vickers (1970). In the experiment described by Audley and Mercer, for example,  the stimuli were the colors blue and green, and subjects, presented with stimulus colors in the blue-green range, had to  decide whether the stimuli were "blue" or "green." Audley (1970) referred to the motor conflict data of Wollen (1963), while  Pike (1968) illustrated the data of Cross and Lane (1962), Kellogg (1931), and Laberge (1963), as  0022-2496/80/010030-23$02.00/0  Copyright 1980 by Academic Press, Inc. Petrusic and Jamieson (1978) and Vickers (1970) similarly produced  their own data.  Stopping rules are usually the same for each stimulus, and three rules that have received much attention are:  (a) A stimulus is chosen when the number of impulses received from it by the subject reaches some threshold value k (in this  paper k will denote a positive integer) before the number of impulses received by the subject from the other stimulus reaches  k. The model with this stopping rule has been termed the accumulator model.  (b) A stimulus is chosen when an unbroken run of k impulses from the stimulus is received by the subject, and that is the  first run of k impulses of either type received by the subject. The model with this stopping rule has been termed the runs  model.  (c) A stimulus is chosen when the number of impulses received from it by the subject first exceeds the number of impulses  received from the other stimulus by k, and at no stage has the number of impulses received from the other stimulus exceeded  the number of impulses from the first stimulus by k. The model with this stopping rule has been termed the random-walk model.  The criterion, k, is usually the same for each stimulus: see Pike (1968) and Petrusic and Jamieson (1978) for an investigation  and discussion when this is not the case.  Varying k, for each of the above three stopping rules, results in slightly different models. The way in which different models  have been compared, and also matched against data, is by means of the mean latency/probability function (LPF, using the  notation of Pike (1968); cf., Petrusic and Jamieson (1978) who use LFC) which is simply a graph of the mean latency for a  chosen stimulus, plotted against the probability of that stimulus having been chosen. For each stopping rule and each k there  is precisely on LPF. Different points on the LPF result from changes in the experiment, thus altering the subject's response  probability, but not the subject's value of k. Several LPFs, together with the analysis leading to these curves, can be found  in Audley and Pike (1965). Accumulator LPFs have the characteristic property that mean latencies decrease monotonically as  choice probability increases. For all other LPFs, small and large choice probabilities have mean latencies less than the mean  latency for a choice probability (P) of 0.5; the random-walk curves are symmetrical about P = 0.5 and the runs curves tend to  symmetry about P = 0.5 as k increases. Empirical LPFs can be found, e.g., in Pike (1968) and Petrusic and Jamieson (1978), and  they frequently suggest some form of compromise between the accumulator curves and the more symmetric ones of the runs and  random-walk models, in that mean latencies for values of P = P < 0.5 tend to be appreciably greater than the mean latencies  for P = 1 - P, without the LPF being monotonically decreasing; see, e.g., the "(4, 3) exact" curve of Fig. 1. Vickers (1970)  was able to obtain theoretical curves of this kind by associating a magnitude with each stimulus, and then considering an  accumulator model, his main analytic tool being simulation; Petrusic and Jamieson (1978) also adapted an accumulator model, by  supposing that subjects guess.  In this research we were interested in the effect of imposing memory restrictions on the accumulator and random-walk models.  Such a restriction has been discussed by Broadbent  32 MORGAN AND ROBERTSON  (1973, p. 298) and Laming (1968, p. 93), and Audley (personal communication) has mentioned circumstances in which memory  limitations are likely to play a prominent role. For example, when difficult discriminations are being made a relatively long  time to choose may result, in which case it seems reasonable to suppose that recent evidence only will determine the final  decision, possibly because of definite limits on the time span of some perceptual processes.  Specifically, we have assumed that the subject applies the accumulator and randomwalk stopping rules to, at most, w impulses,  for some w: if the criterion has not been satisfied when the first w impulses have been received, then we assume that  subsequently the subject only considers, at each stage, w impulses. These could either be the last w impulses received (we  shall call this "fixed forgetting"), or an incoming new impulse could be supposed to displace one of the currently held w  impulses at random (we shall call this "random forgetting"). It is quite clear that no such short-term memory versions of runs  models can be distinguished from the customary runs models. The fixed forgetting models are analogous to the idea of a  "push-down store"-see Atkinson et al. (1967) and cf. also Atkinson and Shiffrin (1971)-and there are similarities here with  the work of Green and Luce (1974).  The resulting algebra proved to be formidable, and, in keeping with current ideas of short-term memory (see, e.g., Baddeley,  1976) we only illustrate the LPFs for values of w < 6. In general, models will be referred to by a (w, k) pair.  2. DISCUSSION  The main aim of this paper has been to investigate the effect, on the LPFs, of imposing a short-term memory limitation on the  accumulator and random-walk models. Much of the paper has been concerned with analytic approaches to these modified models.  While it is interesting to observe how the modifications affect the LPFs producing, in most cases, LPFs more in accord with  experimental data than the LPFs that these models produce without such a memory limitation, it must be emphasized that these  are primarily academic findings. It is interesting, however, to note that with a memory limitation both the fixed forgetting  and random forgetting accumulator models can give rise to the two main qualitative types of LPF, viz., monotonic decreasing,  as choice probability increases, and with small mean latencies for small and large choice probabilities; it is tempting to  speculate that the change between these two types of LPF that Petrusic and Jamieson (1978) observed occurring with practice  could be due to w increasing with practice (see Fig. 5).  The utility of work such as this must lie in demonstrating the type of mechanisms  480/2111-4  46 MORGAN AND ROBERTSON  that might have given rise to the observed data. There are many other possibilities that remain to be considered and which may  also qualitatively describe the available data equally well; for example, in keeping with the idea of a limitation on memory  one might suppose that a subject behaves according to either of the three main models mentioned in Section 1, and if the  criterion has not been satisfied when w impulses have been received, then the subject bases his choice, say, solely on the  next impulse to occur-for example, choosing stimulus 1 if the next impulse is R.  The difficulty, implicit in the results of this paper, lies in trying to distinguish, in any particular case, which of the  possible models best describes the data and/or the actual behavior of the subject. The region that is likely to be the most  diagnostic in this respect is for small values of p, which unfortunately, is the region in which there are the least  observations overall, leading to the greatest variability in estimators of latency and probability of choice (cf. the  simulations of Fig. 1). One possible way of distinguishing between different models may lie in the consideration of latency  distributions, rather than just mean latencies, though there are obvious problems in obtaining suitable data on latency  distributions. It would be of interest to try to incorporate some of the ideas of Ratcliff (1979), in this respect. Miller and  Greeno (1978) have proposed goodness-of-fit tests for models of latency and choice, but their tests assume normal latency  distributions, and the effect of skewed latency distributions, which simulations indicate do result from models such as those  of this paper, for example, has yet to be investigated.
