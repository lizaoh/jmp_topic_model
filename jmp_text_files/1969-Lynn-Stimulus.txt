JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY: 6, 277-285 (1969)  A Stimulus Sampling Model for Simultaneous Discrimination Learning'  ELIZABETH LYNN  San Diego State College, San Diego, California 92115  A one-element stimulus sampling model is presented, in which it is assumed that the stimulus element is not necessarily  sampled on every trial. This model gives a good account of the performance of monkeys on four simultaneous discrimination  tasks, and is superior to a comparable linear model and to several models which predict asymptotically perfect learning. It is  suggested that the systematic nature of the discrepancies requires modification of the assumption concerning the initial  conditioning state of the element.  A number of proposals have been advanced for encompassing discrimination learning within the framework of a mathematical  model. Characteristically, these have been concerned either with spatial discriminations or with successive object  discriminations. The only systematic effort directed toward the important area of simultaneous object discrimination learning  has been that of Restle (1955, 1958, 1961, 1962). His models dealt with a broad range of intraproblem and interproblem  phenomena. While these models described well the gross aspects of empirical data, their complexity precluded the type of  detailed analysis which has been useful, both for the evaluation of models and for the study of phenomena. The model presented  in this paper sacrifices breadth of application for tractability in derivation of detailed predictions.  It has also been characteristic of mathematical models of learning, that asymptotically perfect learning is assumed. That is,  when one response is consistently reinforced, and all other responses consistently nonreinforced, the probability of  occurrence of the reinforced response goes to one. This prediction is patently not tenable in certain important areas of  application, and is removed in the present model.  Specifically, the discrimination learning situation is conceptualized as a pairedassociate task, each pair of discriminanda  comprising a stimulus to which one of two responses is to be learned. Predictions from this uncertain sampling (US) model are compared with data from rhesus monkeys,  obtained by using two experimental techniques: (a) a single pair of discriminanda presented for a number of trials in the  standard discrimination learning procedure, and (b) several stimulus pairs presented in the paired-associate paradigm,  treating pairs as stimulus items in a list.  THE MODEL  The following set of assumptions comprise the US model for discrimination learning:  (1) Each pair of discriminanda may be represented by a single stimulus element.  (2) On every trial the element is sampled with probability s. With probability (1 - s) no element is sampled.  (3) The element is in either of two conditioning states: Cl (conditioned to the correct response, Al), or Ca (not  conditioned). Prior to the first trial, the element is in Ca .  (4) On each trial, if the element is in state Co and is sampled, then the probability of a transition to state Cl is a  constant c > 0. If the element is in state Cl , or if the element is not sampled, then its conditioning state cannot change.  (5) If the element is in state Cl and is sampled, the probability of the response Al is one. If the element is in state Co ,  or is not sampled, the probability of the response A1 is iz. (g)  METHOD  Subjects. Five adolescent rhesus monkeys were used. These animals had had considerable laboratory experience, including  extensive training on simultaneous discrimination of both stereometric and planometric stimuli, over a period of about four  years.  Apparatus and Materials. Ten stereometric objects, differing in shape, were cut from -41 in. plywood and painted red. All had  a surface area of approximately 4 sq. in. Ten planometric objects were prepared, consisting of squares of construction paper  differing in color, mounted on 3 by 3 in. squares of white posterboard. The color patches were 3 sq. in. in area.  Testing was done in the Wisconsin General Test Apparatus (WGTA). This apparatus is essentially as described by Harlow (1949).  In outline it consists of a cage occupied by the monkey during testing; an opaque screen directly in front of the cage, which  is lowered between trials; a sliding wooden tray in which there are two depressions, or food wells; and, directly before the  experimenter, a one-way vision screen through which the monkey's choice may be observed.  Design and Procedure. A discrimination problem consisted of two stimuli differing either in shape (stereometric) or in color  (planometric). In any testing session, the problems were presented either successively or concurrently. These were combined to  yield four types of discrimination  STIMULUS SAMPLING MODEL 281  task; successive stereometric (SS), successive planometric (SP), concurrent stereometric (CS), and concurrent planometric (CP).  A day's testing involved five problems, formed by random pairing of the 10 stimuli, either planometric or stereometric. For  successive presentation, the subject was given 25 trials on a problem, with the correct object appearing on the left or right  according to a prearranged random sequence. The next problem was then introduced, again for 25 trials. For concurrent  presentation, the pairs of objects comprising the day's problems were presented in random order, 1 trial being given on each  problem before the next trial on any problem. The pairs were presented in a different random order on each of 25 runs through  the set.  Each subject was given four test sessions on each task type, with one session per day. Order of problems and task types was  the same for all subjects. All stimuli were designated correct and incorrect an equal number of times during the experiment,  and no pairing of the stimuli was used more than once. A noncorrection technique was used throughout, and standard WGTA  procedure (Harlow, 1949) was followed. This is  probably a minor flaw at the present stage of development. In general, though, and in view of the unpredicted variability, the  fit of the model is quite as satisfactory as is usually found with the one element model, but examination of the pattern of  discrepancies is illuminating. Errors before the first success tend to be higher than predicted from the model, again consistent with the observation of a  higher than predicted repetition rate. The sampling  parameter may be evaluated by fitting the one parameter (O) model which is identical to the US model with s = 1.0. For the  present application, the learning curve for the O model is given by  p(Al..) = 1 - I1 - c)n-1.  Replacing c in the above expression by c/N, we have the learning curve for a multielement pattern model with N independently  sampled patterns. Replacing c by csf N yields the learning curve for a component model with samples of size s from a  population of N stimulus elements.  The same Newton-Raphson iterative procedure was used to obtain least squares estimates of this learning parameter. These  estimates are shown in Table 1, and the  predicted learning curves in Fig. 1. It is immediately apparent that these models, which predict asymptotically perfect  performance under the conditions of the present experiment, are quite inadequate for these data, even though it is not assumed  that the asymptote has been reached. In view of this poor fit, sequential statistics were not considered.  One model which is in many respects quite similar to the US model is the single operator linear model. In this model it is  assumed that response probabilities are transformed on each trial by the single operator,  P(A,,n+1) = aP(A,,,,) + (I - a) A.  This assumption leads to the result (retaining the assumption that P(A,,l) = 2),  P(A,,n) = A - (A - 1) an-1.  The parameter A is commonly assumed to be 1, yielding a linear analog to the O model. If A is retained as a free parameter, we  have a linear form of the US model. The asymptotic probability of a correct response is A, and the learning curve is identical  to that of the US model, with A = 1(1 + s) and a = g. Clearly then, if the models are to be distinguished, it must be on the  basis of other aspects of the data.  One characteristic of the linear model, which is not shared by the US model, is that the responses over trials are  statistically independent, and the covariances are therefore zero. From this, together with the equivalence of the learning  curves, it follows that the expected total errors in k trials is the same for the linear model as for the US model, but that  the variance of the total errors is smaller. Inasmuch as the observed variances were greater than predicted by the US model,  this is no improvement, but it has already been noted that this is probably not a critical statistic. The fact of underestimation of response repetition tendencies still remains, however. It would appear that a major source of  this difficulty probably lies in the entirely unrealistic assumption that the stimulus element is initially in state Co .  Certainly, if the model is to apply more generally, then some situations, e.g., generalization tests, must admit of a positive  probability that the element is initially in the conditioned state, while others, e.g., discrimination reversal, require a  third conditioning state, C21 in which the element is conditioned to the nonreinforced response, A2 . If this modification is  made, along with the further assumption that with a random selection of pairs, as in the present experiment, the element is  equally likely to be initially in states Cl and C21 then the response probabilities, P(AZ,n), are unchanged, but the  repetition probabilities, P(Aj,j,,,+1), are increased, and the alternation probabilities, P(Aj,A;,n+i), are correspondingly  decreased, as a function of this initial state probability. Without the assumption of equal initial probabilities for the  states Cl and C2 , the response probabilities P(Ai,,) are also changed.
