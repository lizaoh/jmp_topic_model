DISCUSSION  Our aim in this paper has been to state and compare increasingly general axioms for a comparative probability relation on the  subsets of { 1, ..., n } which imply that there is a unique probability measure P on the subsets of 11, ..., n } that agrees  with > . Simple sufficient conditions for uniqueness such as Van Lier's UA3 and the regular-measures generalization UA4  require only de Finetti's basic qualitative probability axioms to obtain the existence of a uniquely agreeing measure.  However, the general uniqueness axiom UA5 also requires a generalized additivity axiom such as A4+ to form a complete set of  necessary and sufficient conditions on > for the existence of a uniquely agreeing P.  The unique P's that can be obtained by satisfaction of UA3 and UA4 can be described by nondecreasing finite sequences of  nonnegative integers in fairly simple ways. There appears to be no comparable representation characterization for UA5 since  the unique atom probabilities for the general case depend on the existence of a nonnegative solution to a set of n - 1 linear  equations that are linearly independent. We have noted that the ratio of the number of unique solutions that satisfy UA4 to  the number that satisfy UA5 approaches 0 as n gets large, and suspect that the same thing is true for UA3 compared to UA4.  It may be wondered whether there are simple generalizations of UA4 that capture some of the irregular measures but are  considerably less complex than UA5. There is, of course, an array of uniqueness conditions between UA4 and UA5. For example,  it is easy to state a condition that allows the first four terms of an integer representation to be 1223 or 1234 or a regular  sequence and then proceeds by regular extension for succeeding xi. However, we know of no generalization of UA4 that is as  intuitively straightforward as that condition, and leave this as an open question along with Conjecture 1.  Finally, we note that the finite uniqueness question for subjective probability examined here forms part of a larger study of  finite uniqueness in the theory of measurement. Elsewhere we consider uniqueness for representations of finite algebraic  difference structures (Fishburn et al., 1988; Fishburn, Odlyzko, & Roberts, 1989), for finite additive conjoint measurement  (Fishburn & Roberts, 1988), and for finite extensive measurement (Fishburn & Roberts, 1989). The last of these presents a  survey of results on finite uniqueness.  Note added in proof. It has been brought to our attention that Michael A.
