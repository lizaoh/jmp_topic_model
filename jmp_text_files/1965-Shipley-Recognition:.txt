SHIPLEY  University of Pennsylvania, Philadelphia, Pennsylvania  An experiment is described in which each of 3 observers participated in 12 different detection and recognition tasks; relevant  choice models based on Luce's work are examined. The stimulus parameter from a two-alternative forced-choice task is shown to  be related to the stimulus parameter from the corresponding yes-no detection task by a distance representation. An assumption  is made to relate stimulus parameters from recognition tasks to stimulus parameters from detection tasks; again a distance  representation is used and the predicted relation is supported by the data. Several extensions of the choice models to  composite tasks that require both recognition and detection are examined. Multistage choice models in which recognition occurs  first and is followed by detection are judged most adequate. Data from tasks with uncertainty in one of two aspects of the  stimulus are compared with data from composite tasks to evaluate the assumption that covert responses actually occur in the  former tasks and influence the overt responses. With one exception, response proportions summed over the irrelevant response  from the composite tasks are similar to response proportions from the tasks with uncertainty. The exception, recognition when  no signal is presented on one-half the trials, yields response proportions similar to those found in the simple recognition  task. In general, the covert response assumption appears to be supported whenever both stages of choice are required. The first issue is the interrelation of performance on  yes-no and forced-choice detection tasks and recognition tasks. In a forced-choice procedure several signal intervals are  defined for the observer; the signal is presented in exactly one interval and the observer's task is to choose the interval  that he believes contained the signal.  The first theoretical attack on the problem of human detection of signals in a noise background was made with statistical  decision theory coupled with an assumption that resembles Thurstone's discriminal processes (Tanner and Swets, 1954;  Licklider, 1959; Green, 1960). This approach known as the theory of signal detectability treats the yes-no and forced-choice  procedures and extracts from each a parameter that represents the observer's sensitivity to the signal in noise as well as a  representation for response bias. The stimulus parameters from the two different detection tasks are related by the assumption  that they can be represented in Euclidean space. The signal detectability theorists have considered recognition tasks in which the signals vary qualitatively, usually in  frequency from trial to trial and on each trial the observer attempts to name the signal which he believes was presented  (Tanner, 1956). The signal detectability theorists study recognition when a noise background is used. Two kinds of signal have  been used: signals of sufficient intensity to be perfectly detectable in a detection task and signals which are imperfectly  detectable. With imperfectly detectable signals the observer's ability to recognize is related to his detection performance by  a spatial representation.  It will be argued in the discussion that an analogous spatial representation permits the interrelation of recognition. and  detection tasks by the choice model. We shall present a second assumption to relate q's from different experiments which  applies when Eq. 25 does not apply. This second assumption applies to experiments which have some but not all stimulus  presentations in common. For instance: a yes-no detection experiment in which the stimulus set consists of Sl and N, a yes-no  detection experiment in which the stimulus set consists of S2 and N, and a recognition experiment in which the stimulus set  consists of Sl and S2. This second assumption, together with the assumption expressed in Eq. 25, will permit us to relate  stimulus parameters from all experiments reported in this paper.  Consideration of the effect of uncertainty of the nature of a given stimulus presentation upon performance will lead us to the  next issue of interest. The observer's certainty that the signal possesses some specific property has been varied in detection  tasks. In human acoustical signal detection the size of the set of possible signal frequencies has been varied. In some  experiments the signal is always of the same frequency, the observer is informed of this prior to the experiment, and the  observer is said to be certain of the signal frequency. In other experiments on a given trial the signal is one of two or more  possible frequencies, the observer is informed of the possibilities prior to the experiment but on a given trial he is  uncertain of the frequency of the signal. The general finding is that the observer's ability to detect decreases with an  increase in his uncertainty with respect to the signal frequency, i.e., with an increase in the number of possible  frequencies. To account for the decrement in detection performance with uncertainty of the signal frequency signal  detectability theorists made additional assumptions about the observer. They assumed he functions either as a single filter  with a center frequency that shifts or as several narrow band filters. See Green (1958) for a further explication of the  signal detectability position and for a summary of relevant experimental data.  Another way to account for the decrement in detection performance with an increase in the observer's uncertainty is to assume  that the observer makes covert recognition responses on every trial as well as overt detection responses (Shipley, 1960). The  probability of a given detection response, for instance the selection of the first interval  284 SHIPLEY  in a forced-choice task with two possible signal frequencies A and B, is the sum of the probability that the observer decides  signal A is in the first interval and the probability that he decides signal B is in the first interval. The covert response  assumption is independent of any model of choice behavior. Predictions of detection choice probabilities with uncertainty  require a model for choice behavior where both detection and recognition responses occur overtly. Such models are called  composite models.  This brings us to our second issue, choice models for composite tasks. Choice models have been written for composite tasks  (Shipley, 1960; Luce, 1963) but these models will be judged inadequate on the basis of the data reported here. After  consideration of the original models a more adequate choice model is presented. This latter model is based upon the assumption  that the observer makes his decision in stages in a composite task; first he recognizes and thep he detects on each trial.  The third issue we will examine is the validity of the covert response assumption. This assumption can be evaluated directly  by comparison of relative frequency of choices in different experimental tasks. For instance, the frequencies of detection  responses in a forced-choice detection task with frequency uncertain are compared to the frequencies of detection responses in  a composite forced-choice detection and recognition task. Responses which indicate the same detection-choice are combined. As  has been reported elsewhere (Luce, 1963, p. 184) the results support the covert response assumption when detection response  probabilities are compared. In this paper recognition response probabilities are also compared. The tenability of the  assumption varies with the detection task. It appears to hold for forced-choice tasks but not for yes-no taks. It will be  argued that the necessity for a recognition choice prior to a detection choice accounts for covert responses. Evidence that  favors the covert response assumption will be found only when two choices must be made prior to an overt response.  Throughout our evaluations of various choice models we will attempt to fit the models to the data with stimulus parameters  which are functions of two independent values. These values correspond to the two signals used: 500 cps and 1100 cps. Each  presentation of a given frequency is at the same intensity, although the two frequencies differ in intensity. The use of two  independent values is a demand that each presentation of a given signal in a single interval has the same effect no matter how  complicated the experiment. Our over-all judgment of the choice theory approach is that it is consistent with this demand.  DESIGN  Twelve experimental conditions were run. APPARATUS AND PROCEDURE  The data were collected in the Psychophysical Laboratory of the Electronic Defense Group, University of Michigan, using their  audio channel, measurement technique, and programmer which have been described elsewhere (Green, Birdsall, and Tanner, 1957).  Briefly, the apparatus permitted the simultaneous presentation of signals in noise to as many as four observers, who listened  over earphones in a sound-treated room. Each observer sat in a booth facing a panel of lights, a set of keys, and a chart of  the conditions and payoffs similar to Table 1. A light flashed to indicate the start of a trial; a white light flashed for the  duration of each signal interval; and a red light signified the response period. Finally, one of several lights flashed to  indicate which response was in fact correct. To respond, the observer pushed the key corresponding to his decision. Only the  first key pushed during the response period was recorded.  With the exception of Conditions 9 and 10 the feedback contained the same information as the response set. In Condition 9 one  of two recognition responses, F, or FL , was possible but the observers were told which of the three possible stimulus  presentations occurred: S, , S, or N. In Condition 10, both recognition and detection were required, a total of four possible  responses, again the observer was told which of three possible stimulus presentations occurred: S, , S, , or N. For detection  when the frequency was uncertain (Conditions 5 and 11), the feedback specified only the correct detection response; and for  recognition when the temporal location of the signal was uncertain (Condition 6), the feedback specified only the correct  recognition response.  Each signal interval lasted 500 msec; the response period lasted 550 msec; and the light indicating the correct response, 500  msec. The next trial began 1500 msec after the end of the response period. Trials with a single signal interval lasted a total  of 3050 msec, and those with two intervals, 3550 msec.  The two signal frequencies used were Signal One, 500 cps, and Signal Two, 1100 cps. Signals began at the same time as the  light indicating the signal interval and lasted for 100 msec, S, was presented at 0.0023 V, SZ at 0.0026 V, and the wideband  noise at 0.0435 V, all measurements across the terminals of the earphones. On the assumption that the noise bandwidth is  10,000 cycles, the signal energy to noise power density ratio, EINO , is 14.0 for Signal One and 17.9 for Signal Two. A  constant level of noise was on throughout each experimental session. Stimulation was binaural.  DETECTION AND RECOGNITION  287  The frequencies, intensities, and durations were selected on the basis of previous work at Michigan, Two considerations guided  the choices: a desire to obtain a large decrement in detection with frequency uncertainty and a desire to collect data rapidly  with little discomfort to the observers.  Two random selectors, which sampled the output of noise tubes, were used to program the experiment electronically. For the  simpler detection tasks, one random selector selected the interval in the forced-choice procedure and whether or not a signal  occurred in the yes-no procedure. In the simple recognition task, a single random selector decided which of the two  frequencies occurred. All other conditions used two independent random selectors, one to select the frequency and the other to  control the interval or the presence of the signal. In all cases, the two alternatives of each selector were equally likely.  With forced-choice procedures, all stimulus alternatives were equally likely; with yes-no procedures, trials without signals  were as likely to occur as trials with signals.  The selector outputs were also run through a coincidence network that controlled the answer lights and counters which gave a  cumulative record of the correct responses.  On each day, each condition was presented for an uninterrupted series. The daily order of conditions was randomly determined.  The length of the series was proportional to the total number of trials for that condition, which in turn was approximately  the number of possible outcomes times 400. A break occurred between each series during which the observers were told how many  times they had been correct and which condition would follow.  Some disagreement is found in the totals of responses to presentations of a given stimulus recorded for each observer. This  could reflect either a failure of the observer to respond or a failure of the equipment to record. We do not know if the  failures are systematically related to the observer's choice of response. However, the discrepancies are small; the difference  between the mean number of presentations for all three observers and the number for a given observer never exceeds 0.4 % of  the mean. Hence we have used the recorded responses to compute proportions of choices and essentially ignored all failures to  respond.  The experiment, following 10 days of practice, took 40 days. Let us consider a situation in which the sequence recognition followed by detection seems intuitively compelling. Imagine a  signal detection task with uncertainty of the modality of the signal. The signal can be either visual or auditory. It is  difficult to imagine an observer who makes a "yes" response with confidence but with no idea of the modality. Recognition in  the sense of selection of the channel would seem natural before a detection response is made.  We believe the same considerations hold for acoustical signals of sufficiently different frequencies. Before the observer can  make a detection decision he must decide which aspects or cues of the situation to use-those that suggest a presentation of Sl  or those that suggest a presentation of S2 . On the other hand if both signals were qualitatively similar, for instance of  different intensity but of the same frequency, presumably the observer would not need to decide which cues to attend to before  making a detection decision. Similarly in the composite forced-choice  task the observer first decides for each interval which frequency seems most likely and, having selected the cues for each  interval, he compares them and then selects the interval that he believes contained the signal. This intuitive interpretation  of a two-stage choice model is of course subject to direct empirical evaluation; two signals of the same frequency and  different intensity could be employed. The two-stage model predicts no decrement in detection with uncertainty of the signal  intensity. Here the evaluation will be confined to an attempt to fit the two-stage model to the composite data. CONCLUSION  We have examined the data from twelve tasks for three observers. We have attempted to interrelate the data from all these  tasks with choice models. As previously reported, a distance representation of stimulus parameters relates stimulus parameters  from yes-no and forced-choice detection tasks. Another distance representation is assumed here to relate stimulus parameters  from detection and recognition tasks. The data support this assumption also. To account for behavior in composite detection and  DETECTION AND RECOGNITION 307  recognition tasks we have assumed here that an observer makes his decision in stages, first recognition and then detection.  Simple choice models are used at each stage and good agreement is found between data and predictions. To account for behavior  in conditions with uncertainty we use the covert response assumption: both detection and recognition occur covertly when only  one choice is required overtly. The data from composite tasks and from tasks with uncertainty seems in good agreement for  three of four comparisons. The exception is to be expected if recognition precedes detection in the composite procedures.
