INTRODUCTION  A basic concept at the core of understanding is the reciprocal property. Essentially this property asserts that when comparing  two stones according to weight with the aid of the hands and one stone is judged to be five times heavier than the other, then  the other is automatically one fifth the weight of the first. Both stones participate in the judgment, the smaller one serving  as a unit of reference. All our senses are capable of making such comparisons and so is our mind in comparing abstract ideas  with respect to common properties. From such comparisons among objects in pairs, one derives a relative scale of measurement  among the objects. Reciprocal comparison is an inherent ability of all human beings which enables us to scale things  encountered in practice.  The logical question then is: Is our judgment sufficiently accurate to ensure that if stone A is five times heavier than stone  B and stone B is three times heavier than stone C that we would judge A to be fifteen times heavier than C? Most likely not.  To improve the accuracy of the scale derived from the paired comparisons we should also compare A with C. We would then need  to say something about the inconsistency in performing comparisons. In general if ai; indicates the relative dominance of  object i over object j when comparing n objects in pairs, the comparisons are said to be consistent if the following relation  holds:  aiia;k = aik for all i, j, k = 1, 2,..., n. Transitivity is difficult to ensure in practice unless one keeps a careful tally of all comparisons. Forcing transitivity  would be in violation of spontaneity in making comparisons. In addition, when a problem is new and its relations are  uncertain, one is never really sure that some of the order relations are the right ones and should be followed to force the  others to conform. Hence, one must introduce other comparisons independently to enrich the information base. What is needed is  a theory that does not assume transitivity and remains valid despite intransitivity, though perhaps not fully applicable. Our first task is to derive the functional form of K(s, t). First we assume that K(s, t) is consistent and has a smoothness  property in both s and t. This leads to a general form for a consistent kernel K(s, t).  We show that if we have a reciprocal kernel and consider its corresponding response function, that function itself may be used  to construct a consistent kernel which is a ratio of this response function in each of the two variables of the reciprocal  kernel. Thus to every reciprocal kernel there corresponds a consistent kernel. Hence, it suffices to derive the general form  of a consistent kernel and that of its response function.  The foregoing ideas can be illustrated in terms of a simple mechanism as follows. Let us consider a single neuron that fires  in response to a stimulus. The intensity of firing of the neuron depends on the intensity of the stimulus. Each firing gives  rise to a single response function. Experimental observations (Kandel and Schwartz, 1981) show that for a given stimulus a  neuron may fire several times. This is equivalent to an accumulation of outputs for all the firings. In addition, it has been  observed that the same stimulus intensity need not cause the neuron to fire with the same frequency and hence has associated  with it a frequency distribution of firings. Thus the range of values of a stimulus generates a family of distributions of  firings. The response of a neuron to a stimulus is averaged over the range of that stimulus. Analytically, this average  response can be approximated by a sum of the maximum frequencies of firing for a finite set of values which form a partition  of the range of the stimulus. The quality of the approximation depends on the number of points forming the partition. Thus the  firings of a neuron give rise to a linear combination of responses. CONCLUSION  We have shown that the principal eigenfunction solution of Fredholm's equation of the second kind with a reciprocal kernel as  a model of stimulus-response phenomena, is given by the gamma-like function s"e-fls. We also proved that these functions are  dense in the space of continuous functions on finite intervals. Finally we have indicated that density can be regarded as a  useful property for explaining how response to diverse stimuli can be synthesized to approximate in the T-norm to meaningful  perception of sense data. Elsewhere we have extended this synthesis to response in hierarchic and feedback network structures.  REFERENCES  ACHIESER, N. 1. (1956). Theory of approximation, New York: Ungar.  KANDEL, E.
