JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 21, 53-65 (1980)  A Generalization of the Yule-Simon Model, with Special Reference to Word Association Tests and Neural Cell Assembly Formation  P. LANSKI AND T. RADIL-WEISS  Institute of Physiology, Czechoslovak Academy of Sciences, Prague, Czechoslovakia  A generalization of the Yule-Simon model is suggested and such related questions as inversion problems and nonequilibrium  behavior are solved. The generalization follows the work of Haight and Jones (Journal of Mathematical Psychology, 1974, 11,  237-244) and thus special reference is made to word association tests. Some new possible applications of the presented model  are offered, namely, in the field of neural cell assemblies.  INTRODUCTION  The Yule-Simon model was introduced by Simon (1955) to describe the distribution of word frequencies and by Yule (1924) as the  limiting case of a distribution in mathematical genetics. Discussion has been raised about its suitability (see, e.g.,  Mandelbrot, 1959; Simon, 1960; Herdan, 1961) on the other hand it has started to be used (e.g., Prasad, 1957; Kendall, 1961;  Haight, 1966; Haight & Jones, 1974, Sichel, 1975).  It is the purpose of this paper to generalize the Yule-Simon model and in this way to make it more flexible and applicable.  This generalization not only yields the Yule-Simon distribution, but some other distributions can be achieved by changing the  variable conditions of the model. An inversion problem is solved, and nonequilibrium behavior is considered.  The paper represents an extension of the work of Haight and Jones (1974). For the sake of simplicity, some of the same  terminology, notation, and basic exposition of the problem will be used. Let us review the definitions necessary for further  work.  An infinite population of subjects presented sequentially with a qualitative stimulus is considered. It is supposed that each  subject replies with a well-defined qualitative response. Let k = 1, 2,... denote the number of subjects who have been  interviewed.  The random variable q,(n) is defined for each fixed k as follows: q,(n) = proportion of responses in classes of size n, n = 1,  2,... .  The limiting behavior of the expectation of these proportions is investigated in this paper.  One illustrative example of the definition of the quantity q,(n) for the word association test is presented in Haight and  Jones (1974) together with examples of other obviously analogous phenomena which can be described by the Yule-Simon model.  Readers who are not familiar with the topic of the above-cited paper will find the definition of q,(n) explained in detail in  the following section. That section also presents the main ideas of the Yule-Simon model as well as its generalization.  53  0022-2496/80/010053-13$02.00/0 Copyright 1980 by Academic Press, Inc. NEURAL CELL ASSEMBLY FORMATION  Another possible application of the suggested model is the theory of memory trace formations. The human brain cannot be  considered a collection of a large number of isolated identically operating neurons, but an organization of differently  interacting neurons. For that reason functional (and not necessarily morphological) connections of neurons seem to be more  important than their individual characteristics. It was suggested by Hebb (1949) that the "elements of thought" can be  imagined as "cell assemblies" or micronets and this idea was used by other authors (Grossberg, 1969, 1974; Jacobson, 1976).  For example the point pi defined by simple behavioral unit ri in M of Grossberg's embedding field theory can be considered as  a cell assembly. In a recent paper Jacobson (1976) compared two independent theories of the cell arrangements: multicell vs  singlecell theories. He finally favors the former one as more reasonable. Surveys of assembly theories can be found in  Caianiello (1968), Conrad et al. (1974), and Scott (1977). It is usual that the number of neurons in an assembly (the size of  assembly) is assumed to be constant or is not taken into consideration. However, the number of neurons in cell assemblies can  play some role as it is variable and it can in some way reflect the "strength" of the assembly.  Let us find the probability distribution that a neuron randomly sampled from a large number of neuronal assemblies comes from  an assembly of given size and the probability distribution of the number of neurons in an assembly (the size of assembly) by  applying the generalized Yule-Simon model. Assuming that an infinite population of neurons is sequentially distributed into  assemblies, k = 1, 2,... denotes the number of neurons which have been assigned. CONCLUSION  A classical field of application of the presented model is the description of word association tests (Haight and Jones, 1974).  The model could also be used in various behavioral situations characterized by the selection of a certain reaction or  behavioral pattern. The model is suitable to describe situations that could be called, in a sense, "free will situations".  Their typical feature is the possibility of responding to a stimulus in an a priori undetermined way by any of a practically  infinite set of reactions.  However, it has to be emphasized that the validity of the model is restricted by the condition that the probability of a new  response a does not change with increasing number of stimuli.  It should be noted that the model and, namely, its presented generalization can be used in the simulation of dynamical  systems. For example, the output of a neuron consists of a sequence of impulses. These impulses occur often in bursts and  there are a number of models describing this clustering phenomena, e.g., Ekholm (1972), Cox and Lewis (1966), Smith and Smith  (1965). It can be easily imagined how to translate the postulates of the given model into the terms of neuronal modeling. The  same is true for many familiar types of "all-or-none" events or phenomena in psychophysiology, like instrumental reactions  (bar pressing, etc.), self-stimulation of the brain in animals, vocalization behavior over time, etc.  Another possible suitable field of application is the description of bunching in rural road traffic. The Yule-Simon  distribution is used there (Miller, 1961; Taylor et al., 1974). This distribution is called the one-parameter Miller  distribution and it provides a good fit to observed data; however, there is no satisfactory derivation in terms of vehicle  behavior, as is pointed by Gipps (1977).  The existing bunching models assume that each driver has assigned his "free will speed" for given highway section. This speed  is changed for physical reasons only, e.g. the impossibility of passing due to hills, curves, oncoming traffic, etc. Thus the  clusters emerge in this way. However, it can be supposed that the driver chooses himself to travel in a bunch of given size  rather then is constrained to do it by highway and traffic factors. The existing bunching models do not admit such voluntary  bunching, not even in the situation when the speed of the bunch corresponds approximately to the desired speed of the  approaching driver. In such a case it seems to be more convenient for the majority of drivers to travel within a bunch of  given size and they do not leave the bunch even when there is a possibility of doing so. Bunches of large size induce  unpleasant traveling, but their incidence in the Yule-Simon model has a very low probability. Thus, applying the Yule-Simon  model in this situation, we can explain the close fit of the distribution by psychological reasoning more credibly than by  physical reasoning.
