Training takes place in complex environments. Typically, there are many different tasks which need to be learned; each task can be performed at one of several different levels of proficiency and each level of proficiency within a given task can be trained in one of various different ways. Much is known about what tasks need to be trained in order to achieve a particular objective, what methods are best for training a particular level of a particular task, and what measures should be used to evaluate training. Curiously, given that the tasks, methods, and measures have been selected, very little is known about how to determine which level of which task it is best to train in each session. In this article, a framework for pursuing the optimization of training schedules which are sensitive to (dynamic) and not sensitive to (static) the session by session (trial by trial) progress of the learner is developed. The framework takes as its starting point the early state models of learning first proposed within mathematical psychology in the 1950s. We show that the state models can be used to predict how performance will vary as a function of the scheduling of training trials. Practically, it is important to consider the effect of changes in the scheduling of training trials because such changes can substantially reduce the time it takes any given individual to learn a composite skill. Theoretically, it is important to consider the effect of changes in the scheduling of training trials because such changes can potentially provide the answers to a number of questions central to research in training. We conclude that the state models of learning provide both of the hoped for practical and theoretical benefits. ] 1996 Academic Press, Inc.

The learning and attention models were developed in different contexts. Below, we show that the learning models can be applied not only in the rather limited context in which they were developed, but also in the richer context that the research on attention models suggests will bear the most fruit. Learning models. Among the earliest quantitative models of learning are those proposed by Estes (1950). Central to the models is the assumption that a stimulus is defined by a set of elements. Various of the elements are sampled on each trial. A response is made based on the states of the sampled elements. Models with these features were labeled as stimulus sampling models. Typically, they were used to explain the learning of paired associates. At the outset, we want to describe the simplest of the stimulus sampling models, the single element model. This model was first discussed by Estes (1959) and then studied more thoroughly by Bower (1961, 1962). 

Briefly, it is assumed that each stimulus consists of a single element which is sampled on every trial (or session). Before any training trials have been completed (i.e., at the start of trial 1), each element is unconditioned, C0=1. After n training trials have been completed (i.e., at the start of trial n+1, n�1), each element is in either one of two conditioning states, Cn=2 or Cn=1 (conditioned or not conditioned to the correct response). So, for example, the state of conditioning after one training trial has been completed (at the beginning of the second trial) is indicated by the value of C1 . The response Rn after the completion of n training trials to a stimulus is coded either as correct or incorrect. So, for example, the response R1 after completing one training trial to the stimulus in trial 2 will be coded either as correct or as incorrect. The state of conditioning after n training trials determines in part whether subjects respond correctly (Rn=2) or incorrectly (Rn=1) to the stimulus on the next trial. If an element is conditioned, then a correct response is made with probability one, i.e., P(Rn=2 | Cn=2)=1; otherwise, if an element is unconditioned, subjects must guess. If subjects guess, they are correct with probability g, i.e., P(Rn=2 | Cn=1)=g, and incorrect with probability 

1&g, i.e., P(Rn=1|Cn=1)=1&g. Changes in the state of conditioning occur from trial to trial. Specifically, the probability is p that an element which has remained unconditioned for n training trials becomes conditioned after the next training trial, i.e. P(Cn+1=2 | Cn=1)=p. An element which is conditioned after n training trials remains conditioned with probability one, i.e., P(Cn+1= 2 | Cn=2)=1.

Attention models. Much of the research over the past 20 years in the area of training leaves aside a discussion of the more quantitative state models of training. Instead, this research focuses on the factors which make it possible to automate some behaviors but not others. Behaviors which can be automated are performed very efficiently, frequently are insensitive to increases in load, and generally do not appear to require the active attention of subjects (Schneider 6 Shiffrin, 1977; Shiffrin 6 Schneider, 1977; though see Fisher, 1982, 1984). Recently, several investigators have argued that training fails in some cases because too little attention has been paid to the distinction between

(behavioral) components of training which can and cannot be automated (e.g., Fisk 6 Eggemeir, 1988; Fisk 6 Gallini, 1989; Regian 6 Schneider, 1990; Schneider, 1985). 

Briefly, in order to understand what makes it possible to automate a component it is necessary to define consistent and inconsistent components. A component is defined as consistent if during the execution of the component the mapping between the input (stimulus) and the output (response) remains the same from trial to trial. A component is defined as inconsistent if during the execution of the component the mapping between the input and the output varies from one trial to the next. Shiffrin 6 Schneider (1977) find that it is the consistently mapped components that can be automated; the inconsistently (variably) mapped components cannot be automated. Perhaps more importantly, at least from the standpoint of training, investigators have found repeatedly that the consistent components of complex skills will benefit from extensive practice much more than the inconsistent (varied) components (Eggemeir, Fisk, Robbins, 6 Lawless, 1988; Fisk 6 Eboch, 1989; Fisk 6 Lloyd, 1988; Fisk, Oransky, 6 Skedsvold, 1988; Myers 6 Fisk, 1987). In short, in order to predict whether training will have a large effect on performance, one needs to know whether the component being trained can be automated. And in order to know whether the component being trained can be automated, one needs to know whether it is a consistent or inconsistent component. 

Learning and attention models. Given the clear advantage that comes from training consistent components, it becomes especially important to determine whether state models can describe the acquisition of proficiency for such components. The immediate answer is yes since the original tasks for which these models were developed do map stimuli consistently to responses. However, many of the simple psychomotor tasks which have been used to test automaticity theory do not appear similar to the original paired-associate tasks used to test the state models, at least at first glance. This dissimilarity makes it unclear how one might apply the state models to the simple psychomotor tasks. For example, when subjects are asked to search for a target in a background of distractors, it is not clear what the various states of proficiency might be. One possibility recently suggested by Fisher 6 Tanner (1991) is that with enough practice subjects learn progressively more efficient strategies for comparing the target with each of the distracters. Each comparison strategy can be thought of as a different state. Such an explanation can be extended to memory search as well. Thus, it may be the case that state models can be used to explain skill acquisition in many simple psychomotor tasks. When accuracy is the dependent variable, state models yield this measure directly. However, when response time is the dependent variable, an assumption must be made about the length of time that it takes an 

individual to execute a component at each different level of proficiency. This assumption must be consistent with the power law of practice (Newell 6 Rosenbloom, 1981). A discussion of this law would take us too far afield at this point. Thus, we will assume, at least temporarily, that accuracy is the dependent variable. 

More generally, for complex tasks, it has been argued that the learning of such tasks takes place in stages (Fleishman 6 Hempel, 1955; Fleishman, 1962). These stages have included verbal, perceptual, conceptual, and spatial processing as well as psychomotor coordination. In this context, it might reasonably be assumed that performance in each of the stages can be characterized as being in one of several states (levels of proficiency). Recently, Ackerman (1987) has argued that only three different processes predominate during the learning of a task. Furthermore, he has argued that these processes become proficient in a prescribed order. Again, one might assume that the performance of each process can be characterized as being in one of several states (levels of proficiency). Furthermore, it is easy to incorporate into the underlying state models the orderly progression of learning seen in the data. In summary, we believe that for some relatively large number of both simple and complex tasks, it is the case that state models are plausible candidate descriptions of the acquisition process where we assume that each different state of a stage in the model corresponds to a different level of proficiency. 

Training Schedules, Training Scenarios, and the Benefit Function 

We now want to identify the training schedule that brings each subject to the highest level of proficiency on each of the tasks to be learned in the limited time available for training. Here, we formally define a training schedule as a specification of which task and which method of training should be given to subjects in each session. It turns out that we will need to differentiate between static and dynamic training schedules. A training schedule is a static one if the task and method to be used in a particular session do not depend on the history of responses prior to the session. A training schedule is a dynamic one if the task and method to be used in a particular session do depend on the history of responses prior to the session. It turns out that we will also need to differentiate among training scenarios. Specifically, in this context, it is useful to distinguish among three different complex training scenarios: multiple task scenarios (two or more tasks; only two levels of proficiency for each task), multiple level scenarios (one task; three or more levels of proficiency), and mixed scenarios (two or more tasks; three or more levels of proficiency for at least one task). We will speak at length here only about optimal training schedules for the first two scenarios.

This completes our general remarks. We now want to develop efficient procedures which can be used to identify the optimal training schedule. We want to derive expressions for the probability that an individual being trained using the optimal schedule will be proficient at all tasks being trained. And we want to show how the implementation of optimal schedules can potentially reduce the costs of training (through a reduction in training time), sometimes by quite substantial amounts.

Above we assumed that it was not possible to monitor an individual's responses or that to do so was prohibitively expensive. Suppose now that we can monitor the response on every trial. Then, at least in some cases, the response that an individual makes when asked to perform a particular task may reveal his or her level of proficiency. So, for example, if a maintenance procedure consists of 10 steps and all steps are performed correctly, then the individual involved is presumably proficient. DISCUSSION 

Training is a multibillion dollar industry. Most of the research has focused on the design of instructional strategies which derive from a deeper understanding of the learning process. There is a general agreement that this is a key to developing effective, individualized instructional systems (Gagne 6 Dick, 1983; Snow, 1982). Curiously, given the high cost of most training programs, very little of the research has addressed, formally, the general optimization problem discussed in this article. We have argued that the solution to this problem also serves as a key to developing effective instruction. In short, although much is known about how to select the tasks and methods used to train particular individuals, very little is known about how to schedule the tasks and methods. In this article, we have tried to fill this gap by setting forth a framework for conceptualizing the optimization of training and then showing within this framework exactly what has to be accomplished. 

The framework takes as its starting point the state models of paired-associate learning first proposed back in the 1950s. It is argued here that these same models can be used to describe the course of skill acquisition. We went on to discuss both the procedures that can be used to identify the optimal (or locally optimal) schedule and the potential benefits of using an optimal as opposed to suboptimal training schedule. In all cases the procedures were easy to implement. And, at least for the particular examples discussed here, the optimal procedures were seen to lead to much higher probabilities of joint (full) proficiency in a fixed number of sessions than were the suboptimal procedures. In some cases, the increases were on the order of 900. Of course, all of the examples used to date were hypothetical ones and therefore are only suggestive of the potential improvements one might obtain. Lorton (1972) finds that the optimization methods discussed above provide real improvements, not just hypothetical ones. Specifically. Lorton compared Karush and Dear's (1966) algorithm with the standard algorithm in an experiment where elementary school children were being taught spelling. Recall that Karush and Dear's algorithm is detailed in Theorem 3. The standard algorithm requires that one randomly order all stimuli and then present the stimuli one at a time. After each stimulus has been presented once the procedure is repeated an integral number of times. Subjects in Lorton's experiment spelled roughly 960 of the words correctly using the Karush and Dear algorithm and 860 of the words correctly using the standard procedure. This improvement is considerably smaller than the improvements noted in our hypothetical examples. However, Lorton assumed that the probability a word would be spelled correctly was constant across words. Clearly, such an assumption is unrealistic. It is the failure of this same assumption which may well explain why Dear et al. (1967) failed to obtain an advantage

In summary, both theory and experiment indicate that by focusing more precisely on the sequencing of training trials one can increase substantially the probability that subjects will be jointly proficient at the end of training, although there is still some question about which model of learning should serve as the basis for the optimization. Below, we want to argue that by focusing on the sequencing of training trials, one can potentially provide answers to several key questions that have long been central to the training community. These questions include: (1) Will the training sequence which improves performance the fastest be the one which makes later retention most likely? (2) Is it better to train a task by parts or as a whole? (3) Will redundant training improve retention and, if so, how much redundant training is required to achieve a given level of proficiency? 

Acquisition versus Retention 

Recently, Schmidt 6 Bjork (1992) have argued that in many circumstances it is a mistake to focus solely on the speed with which a given method of training leads to the desired level of proficiency. 

In particular, they argue that in some cases the method which speeds learning in the short term may actually hinder learning in the long term. As an example, Schmidt and Bjork cite the work of Shea 6 Morgan (1979). Shea and Morgan ran subjects in one of three motor tasks. In blocked practice, the trials for a given task were practiced together. In random practice, the trials were intermixed. Shea and Morgan found that subjects were much faster throughout training in the blocked practice. Yet, when subjects were returned for retention testing 10 days after the end of training, subjects trained using random practice performed much better in random testing than did subjects trained using blocked practice. Furthermore, subjects trained using random practice performed significantly better in blocked testing than did subjects trained using blocked practice. 

The models as outlined earlier in the article would not predict such an effect. However, it is easy enough to modify them so that such an effect does emerge. Specifically,

And let MF represent the matrix of transition probabilities when a task is not trained in a given session (some other task is being trained or a time of sufficient length intervenes between trials; this is the forgetting matrix). Part�Whole Training 

A number of investigators have asked whether it is better to train a complex skill as a whole or to separate it into its components and train the components individually (e.g., Briggs 6 Naylor, 1962). Theoretically, it has been argued that in general, part training should be better than training the entire skill (Carlson, Khoo, 6 Elliott, 1990). CONCLUSION 

Formal learning models first appeared in the literature almost half a century ago. They lead to specific predictions about the identity of the training schedule that will optimize the rate at which individuals achieve proficiency on any given set of tasks. However, curiously no one has worked out these predictions explicitly for the complete range of training 

scenarios and associated training schedules. When this is done, it is clear that a number of questions central to research on training can potentially be answered. Furthermore, when this is done, it is clear from a theoretical standpoint that major improvements can be made to existing training schedules. In fact, several studies indicate that the improvements predicted in theory do occur in practice (Atkinson 6 Paulson, 1972; Lorton, 1972). What makes us particularly excited about these improvements is that they can generally be achieved without any additional costs due to modifications to the existing methods of training. All that is required in most cases is a change in the number of times a particular task is trained using a particular method and, possibly, a change in when that task is trained. Of course, the potential advantages that we describe depend on the fundamental correctness of the models that we have been using. In many cases, one might want to modify the assumptions to the models. As long as in the resulting models the progress of an individual from session to session is still a Markov chain (or, more broadly, a Markov process), the general approach we have used in the article can be extended nicely to the modified model. Above, we talked about two possible modifications which keep intact the general approach.[1] And even when changes are made to the model of learning such that it no longer can be characterized as a Markov process, the approach taken here may provide some insight into the approach needed for other models (Atkinson and Paulson). Clearly, much remains to be done.

In the first case, the probability of joint proficiency is again the product of the probabilities that each task becomes conditioned in a single session. In the second case, the probability of joint proficiency is obtained by replacing the conditioning probability of the task which requires two sessions by the product of this conditioning probability and its complement. This must be summed over all tasks since any task can require two sessions to train. Adding together the probabilities for the first and second cases yields Eq. (4) with '={+1. If a task which has been learned is retrained once, the probability of joint proficiency will not contain the second sum. Thus, retraining is suboptimal. Furthermore, it is clear that it does not matter in what order the tasks are selected for training as long as one is selected which previously has not be learned, and so on.
