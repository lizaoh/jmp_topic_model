The hazard function  λ ( x ) for a continuous probability distribution describes the instantaneous risk of an event occurring at  x , given that the event has yet to occur, i.e., 
                         
                            (1) 
                            λ(x)= 
                                  f(x) 
                                  1−F(x) 
                               , 
                         
                      where  f ( x ) is the probability density function (pdf) and  F ( x ) is the cumulative probability. Thus,  λ ( x ) is a function describing the continually reconditionalized immediate likelihood of a critical event. Because the critical event can be quite general, hazard functions play an important role in several scientific disciplines, e.g., in the health sciences ( Klein & Moeschberger, 1997 ), and in actuarial and reliability studies ( Bain, 1978 ). In psychology, the completion of a cognitive task or the loss of a memory trace are two salient examples of critical events that can be described by hazard functions. In fact, hazard functions have played a key role in the mathematical psychology of response time, e.g.,  Luce (1986)  and  Thomas (1971) , as well as in the work on the dynamics of memory storage, viz.,  Chechile (1987) . In addition,  Townsend and Ashby (1978, 1983)  and  Townsend and Nozawa (1995)  have utilized hazard functions in their modeling of the capacity of an information processing system. Other psychologists have explored the use of hazard functions to discriminate between underlying statistical models, e.g.,  Ashby, Tein, and Balakrishnan (1993) ,  Bloxom (1984) ,  Burbeck and Luce (1982) ,  Colonius (1988) , and  Maddox, Ashby, and Gottlob (1998) .  Luce (1986)  observed that some probability distributions with rather similar cumulative distributions can have dramatically different hazard functions.  Van Zandt (2002)  demonstrated this point by comparing the Wald and ex-Gaussian distributions, which have similar  F ( x ) and  f ( x ) functions, but the Wald distribution has a peaked hazard function; whereas the ex-Gaussian distribution has a monotonic increasing hazard function.

Although hazard functions are valuable for understanding stochastic processes, they also present their own set of difficulties. Let us first consider empirical hazard functions, i.e., estimates of  λ ( x ) based on sample data. Hazard estimates require values for both the probability density (pdf) and the cumulative probability.  Van Zandt (2000, 2002)  explores in detail the problems with density estimation. Regardless of the method utilized for estimating  f ( x ), there will be error and a possible bias. Moreover, in the right tail of the probability distribution,  F ( x ) is approaching 1; thus the denominator of the hazard ratio is approaching 0. Consequently, there is an enormous amplification of the density error in computing hazard.

Although there are serious difficulties, there are methods for conducting a statistical analysis of hazard functions.  Patel and Read (1975)  have provided bounds on estimates of the conditional moments of the order statistics when it is assumed that the distribution has monotonic increasing hazard.  Marshall and Proschan (1965)  provided maximum likelihood estimates for hazard under the assumption of monotonic increasing hazard.  Gupta and Panchapakesan (1974)  review these and other statistical results that follow from the assumption of monotonic increasing hazard. However, a key purpose for estimating empirical hazard is to determine whether the function is monotonic increasing, monotonic decreasing, peaked, multimodal, etc. Consequently, the assumption that there is monotonic increasing hazard is a strong claim that undermines the motivation for obtaining an empirical hazard function in the first place. For psychological applications it would seem more suitable to utilize nonparametric methods that do not specify the general shape of the hazard function in advance.

Nelson (1972)  and  Aalen (1978)  have provided a nonparametric method for estimating the cumulative hazard.  Klein and Moeschberger (1997)  discuss how the Nelson–Aalen estimator and its derivative, the estimated hazard, can be modified by various kernel smoothing procedures. Yet, the Nelson–Aalen estimates have increasing error with larger  x  values.  Klein and Moeschberger (1997)  also caution that the estimated hazard is likely to be biased. Other nonparametric approaches for estimating hazard have been developed. For example, penalized likelihood estimation ( Senthilselvan, 1987 ) has been adapted for estimating the hazard rates. However,  Van Zandt (2002)  points out that this procedure can also lead to errors in the early portion of the hazard function as well as biases for large  x .

There is another problem with empirical hazard functions. This problem pertains to the limited range for evaluating the empirical hazard rate. In cases where there is an infinite domain of support for  x , the behavior of the hazard function will never be assessed for all  x . For example, suppose the empirical hazard within the range of collected data is monotonic increasing. Despite this finding, we still cannot conclude that the hazard function is monotonic increasing for larger  x  values that are outside the observed range. Perhaps for much larger  x  values, the density decreases more rapidly than 1− F ( x ), i.e., the hazard might have a peak at a sufficiently large  x .

Surely additional progress will be made on nonparametric methods for estimating empirical hazard. Also, it would be informative if the estimated hazard within the range of recorded values has a reliable peak. In general, however, it is not likely that all of the above problems will be circumvented any time soon. In light of these formidable problems, empirical hazard functions will not be considered further in this paper.

Given the aforementioned problems with empirical hazard functions, the question might arise as to the value of hazard functions. In this paper, we will show that hazard functions and univariate probability distributions are dual problem domains, i.e., given a hazard function then there will be a corresponding mathematical measure-theoretic representation, and conversely given a probability distribution there will be a corresponding hazard function. The linkage between these domains provides a means for creating new probability distributions that correspond to hazard profiles that might be important for psychology but heretofore unexplored. Examples of novel distributions that are linked to hazard functions will be developed later. Thus, it is possible for the mathematical psychologist to develop a model by means of the hazard function and determine later the distribution function linked to the hazard model. In general, hazard functions are important theoretically because they provide a means for understanding the dynamics of the risk of a “critical event” occurring.

In this paper the primary focus will be ascertaining information about the hazard function given the specification of a univariate probability distribution. A mathematical rather than a computational approach will be utilized to explore the properties of hazard functions. A computational approach involves the numeric calculation of  λ ( x ). However, there are serious problems with computing selective hazard values as a means of discovering the properties of the hazard function. As with empirical hazard functions, hazard cannot be evaluated for all  x  values if the domain of support is infinite. Perhaps outside the range of  x  values studied, the behavior of the hazard function changes. A mathematical argument is required to draw clear conclusions regarding the properties of the hazard function for all  x . Moreover, there is a serious problem of a loss of significant figures in computing differences of hazard values. Without a mathematical argument, a hazard function determined by the computational approach is suspect, and with a mathematical argument a computational assessment is unnecessary. Consequently, in this paper the focus is on developing mathematical methods for assessing the hazard function.

Thomas (1971)  and  Luce (1986)  have provided some useful analytic tools and theorems for the mathematical analysis of hazard functions. In this paper, the set of relevant mathematical tools will be reviewed and expanded in an effort to provide in a common notation a more comprehensive collection of results that can be useful for hazard function analysis. In many cases where the results have been previously established, citations to the earlier work will be provided. Even in the case of previously established results, proofs will be provided to enable the deeper understanding of the rationale and necessary conditions for the mathematical result.

It is easy to see how a mixture of distributions can arise in psychology, even on an individual participant basis. For example,  Chechile (1987)  described the type of mixture that can occur in memory research. Some memory traces can be very strong whereas other traces might be rather fragile. In fact, some memories are so vivid that they are retained over the life of the individual; whereas other traces are lost within seconds.  Chechile (2001b)  has also provided response time data that demonstrated a characteristic bimodal distribution that is reflective of a mixture. Such mixtures can occur if the participant is inattentive during brief periods over the course of a long testing session.  Burbeck and Luce (1982) ,  Yantis, Meyer, and Smith (1991) , and  Van Zandt and Ratcliff (1995)  have also discussed mixtures of processes in response time data. In general there is no guarantee that there is a single, homogeneous stochastic process. Mixtures can and do occur in psychological experiments. Consequently, it is important to consider hazard analysis for mixtures.

A number of interesting questions arise in regard to the hazard of systems of  n  components. Suppose that the critical event for the system occurs if any of the components change state. An example of this type of system is a memory trace with  n  necessary components; the memory becomes insufficiently stored and incomplete whenever one of the  n  components is lost (see  Chechile, 1987 ). In the area of response time models, a comparable system could be race models where the process terminates whenever one of the parallel processes is completed, e.g.,  Mordkoff and Yantis (1991) ,  Egeth, Folk, and Mullin (1989) . Systems of this type will be referred to as disjunctive hazard systems—i.e., the failure of any component results in system failure or alternatively completion of any parallel component results in a recorded response.

Another entirely different system is also possible. Suppose that the critical event does not fail until all of the components fail. An example from the memory perspective of this type of system would be if there are multiple copies of the “target”, and the memory of the target only fails when all the copies are lost. The  Hintzman (1986,1988 ) multiple-trace model would be an example of a system of this type. We will refer to systems of this type as conjunctive hazard systems.  Bain (1978)  has provided some of the key mathematical results for disjunctive and conjunctive systems.

Another example of generating a novel probability distribution comes from the problem of describing the dynamics of memory storage, i.e.,  Chechile (1987) . The model represented by  (9) and (10)  treated time as the variable that affected the dynamic changes in storage dynamics. However, in most experiments time is correlated perfectly with the number of intervening items in the retention interval, i.e., the retention interval is filled with items at a constant rate. Experimentally it is possible to vary the rate of interpolated items in the retention interval in order to understand if it is time or the number of items that affects memory.  Chechile (1987, exp. 1)  found that it was the number of intervening items and not time that affected trace storage. The number of items is a discrete variable; hence, there should be a discrete distribution counterpart to the model represented in  (9) and (10) . The geometric distribution belongs both to the set of discrete distributions with nonincreasing hazard and to the set of discrete distributions with nondecreasing hazard. From Theorem 15, it is established that the mixture of geometric distributions has decreasing hazard. Consequently, it is not generally true that a mixture of discrete distributions that have nondecreasing hazard is nondecreasing. Consider the example of a mixture of two Poisson components. Let the components have weights of  θ =0.7 and 1− θ =0.3. Also, let the Poisson parameter values of the components be  τ =0.7 and 10, respectively. The hazard function of this mixture has a peak at  k =1, a valley at  k =4, and second peak at  k =10. Moreover, a single Poisson distribution is a PF 2  function (see the proof of  Corollary 11 ). This example demonstrates that mixtures of discrete PF 2  distributions are not PF 2  functions. Thus, a mixture of discrete distributions with increasing hazard has similar complexity as does a mixture of continuous distributions with increasing hazard.

Let us illustrate the differences between disjunctive and conjunctive systems by considering a system of three independent and identically distributed components which have a geometric distribution with a component hazard of 0.25. For a disjunctive arrangement, the hazard via  Theorem 16  is 0.578 for all  k  values. Note that the disjunctive system of identical components with a geometric distribution is also a geometric distribution with a much larger hazard. However, for a conjunctive arrangement, the hazard for  k  values of 1, 2, 4, 8, and 16 are 0.047, 0.109, 0.183, 0.232, and 0.248, respectively. As with continuous conjunctive systems, the reduction of the system hazard rate over that of the individual components is limited to smaller values in the domain of support, i.e., smaller  k  values.

There has been considerable theoretical interest in hazard functions both inside and outside of psychology. Often probability distributions that are highly similar have quite different hazard functions. Numerical calculation of a hazard function is problematic because we are often confronted with an infinite domain to explore and the calculations involve the division of two very small quantities. Consequently, it is important to have available a set of mathematical tools for analyzing the hazard function.

This paper reviews and further develops the set of mathematical methods for studying hazard functions. In the process some insights can be obtained. For example, if an investigator has credible empirical evidence that the hazard function has multiple peaks, then the investigator should (a) suspect that there is a mixture process involved, and (b) the mixture components have nondecreasing hazard functions. Moreover, for both continuous and discrete probability distributions, hazard models can play an important role in the development of novel probability distributions. These probability distributions can be generated because the investigators modeled the hazard function directly.
