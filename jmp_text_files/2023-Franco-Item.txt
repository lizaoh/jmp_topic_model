The additive conjoint measurement theory (ACMT;  Luce & Tukey, 1964 ) is an abstract theory of measurement that shows that observed data with additive structures result in interval measures. More specifically, if two components (i.e., predictor variables) have an additive effect on a response (i.e., criterion variable), it is possible to imply an interval scale for all the three variables. Formally ( Krantz, 1968 ), an additive structure exists for data with two components (or attributes, or variables; the terms are used interchangeably in the literature, sensitive to the context and to the tradition of the field) whenever it is possible to represent the data using a measurement scale  f  of two components  A  and  X,  with elements  a  and  x , respectively, where

The ACMT has been extensively used in psychophysics (e.g.,  Steingrimsson, 2009, 2011 ), one of the predecessors of the field of psychometrics. Despite of the prior influences of psychophysics on psychometrics, the importance of measurement theory and its implications for psychometric modeling is still underdeveloped ( Cliff, 1992; Heene, 2013; Markus, 2021; Michell, 2008a ). In a notable exception,  Perline et al. (1979)  argued that the  Rasch (1960)  model can be thought of a stochastic version of the ACMT. To interpret the Rasch model as an instance of the ACMT, the difficulties of the items and the respondents’ parameters are interpreted as the two underlying components. Based on the relation between the Rasch model and the ACMT, several authors in the field of psychometrics have argued that the Rasch model is the only appropriate Item Response Theory (IRT) model for developing fundamental (i.e., interval or ratio) measures in social sciences (e.g.,  Andrich, 1988; Bond et al., 2020; Borsboom, 2005; Scheiblechner, 2009; Wright, 1999 ). On the side of measurement theory, because of ACMT’s popularity ( Michell, 2008a, 2008b ), it is commonly assumed that an additive structure is a necessary condition for developing fundamental measures. However,  Luce et al. (1990) , chapter 19) present empirical conditions that can be used to derive models that do not require an additive structure of the data to derive fundamental measures. It is worth noting that the family of logistic IRT models also do not necessarily assume an additive structure of the data ( Cliff, 1992; Michell, 2008a ). However, the estimates of the models for the true score also do not necessarily result in fundamental measures ( Heene, 2013; Scheiblechner, 2009 ).

Therefore, one relevant implication of measurement theory that is underdeveloped in psychometric modeling is the development of IRT models that do not assume an additive structure, but are proven to result in interval or ratio estimates for the true scores (as long as the model holds for the data).  Heene (2013)  revisits ( Rasch, 1979 ) to remember its readers that the Rasch model was not developed to answer any specific empirical observation, but rather as an exercise on “Rach’s own mathematical playground”. In the spirit of Rasch intentions, the main aim of the present study is to develop IRT models derived from measurement theories other than the ACMT. More specifically, as there are several measurement theories that, in principle, could be used to this end, we aim to: (i) present the theoretical basis of the Rasch model and its relations to psychophysics’ models of utility; (ii) give a brief exposition on the measurement theories presented in  Fishburn (1974, 1975) , some of which do not require an additive structure; and (iii) derive IRT models from these measurement theories, as well as Bayesian implementations of these models. We also present two empirical examples to compare how well these IRT models fit to real data.

In addition to deriving new IRT models, we also discuss theoretical interpretations regarding the models’ capability of generating fundamental measures of the true scores of the respondents. The rest of this paper is structured as follows. In the next section, we present some measurement theories of probabilistic utility models and how these theories are related to the development of the first IRT models. The third section is dedicated to a brief discussion on additive conjoint measurement and its relations to the Rasch model, as well as to present the five measurement theories of  Fishburn (1974, 1975)  and how values can be set to develop identifiable IRT models. Next, we derive the IRT models from the exposed measurement theories and provide non-informative priors to fit these models with Bayesian methods. The fifth and sixth sections are dedicated to empirical examples with real data. The paper ends with a discussion and some concluding remarks on the main implications of the present study.

These attributes can be anything, such as the luminance of stimuli presented in each eye for the measurement of brightness ( Steingrimsson, 2009 ), or even mass and velocity for the measurement of momentum ( Krantz et al., 1971 , p. 267). In the context of psychometrics, the attributes are usually assumed ( Pfanzagl, 1973 , chapter 11) to represent the respondents’ dispositions (i.e., true scores;  A ) and the items difficulty or easiness ( X ).

Krantz et al. (1971, chapter 6) , presented conditions that must hold for the ACMT to be a valid measurement model for the analyzed data. The same authors, ( Luce et al., 1990 , chapter 19), discussed the case of nondecomposable conjoint measures. This latter type of models occurs when there is more than one utility, or latent, function relating any attribute to the final evaluation of the biorder system. An implication of nondecomposable models is that their structures will be nonadditive, but will also imply fundamental measures. This may be helpful in situations where the studied behavior is nonmonotonically related to the studied construct, or when form of the relation between behavior and construct changes depending on the attributes.

The five measurement theories proposed by  Fishburn (1974)  have had only a few applications in the literature (e.g.,  Eliashberg & Hauser, 1985 ) and none in psychometrics (to the best of our knowledge). To give an example of a context where the pattern of the P-MFM could hypothetically happen, one could think about the assessment of prejudice ( Matthews & Levin, 2012 ). People with a higher level of prejudice may be more prone to “over-compensate” their responses in questions that indicate higher levels of prejudice if social desirability (e.g.,  Rattazzi and Volpato (2003) ) simultaneously influence their responses. A similar pattern could also hypothetically happen if implicit and explicit aspects of prejudice affect the responses in a given task (e.g.,  Brauer et al. (2000) ), but in an “antagonist” manner. In both cases, the response pattern is due to consequences of multidimensional aspects of the data that the researcher did not, or could not, control for. An intuitive psychometric interpretation of the I-MFM is to think of it as an especial case of the two-parameter logistic model ( Swaminathan & Gifford, 1985 ) with two restrictions. The first restriction is that the discrimination parameter is perfectly and negatively correlated with the difficulty parameter. The second restriction is that the discriminations are assumed to be positive for all the items. Of course, the I-MFM does not estimate discrimination parameters, but its IRFs behave like the described especial case of the two-parameter logistic model. In terms of psychological processes, the I-MFM is similar to psychophysical findings based on the Weber law ( Murray & Link, 2021 ): small discrepancies are easier to identify when stimuli have a smaller magnitude of an attribute than when stimuli have a larger magnitude of an attribute.

A context where the I-MFM could hypothetically hold is in the assessment of academic performance ( Tian & Sun, 2018 ). In this context, the family of the logistic models are commonly used (e.g.,  Hambleton et al. (2000) ). However, one could also hypothesize that it is reasonable to assume that: (i) because there are just a few individuals in the higher end of the true score, difficult items will naturally have lower discrimination; (ii) some items can be so difficult that almost everyone will be incapable of getting it right; and (iii) because the easiest items are expected to be answered by almost everyone, they have larger discrimination. Of course, two, three, and four-parameter logistic models are more flexible in estimating data with these same properties. However, they do this by using more parameters, adding more complexity to the estimation process. A study on the performance of the I-MFM, in comparison with the Rasch model, is discussed in our second empirical example.

The form of the AM-FM is like a combination of the P-MFM and the I-MFM. Comparing the AM-FM to a restricted two-parameter logistic model, it works similarly as if the discrimination parameter is perfectly and quadratically associated with the difficulty parameter. Therefore, from a specific level of difficulty, it is possible to see that the relation between the true score with the probability of an answer equals to “1” is inverted, and it gets steeper as the “difficulty” of the item increases. In terms of its psychological interpretation, the AM-FM is actually similar to a restricted two-parameter logistic model. The AM-FM should provide a better fit (in comparison to exclusively monotonic models) when the set of items have items with both negative and positive contents with regards to the expected direction of the relation between the latent variable and the observed responses.

Finally, it is worth noting that the application of these models to existing data may not be straightforward or even appropriate in some cases. The main reason for that relies on the fact that psychological instruments are usually developed with a monotonic latent-observable relation as the theoretical framework (e.g.,  Flake and Fried (2020) ). Therefore, instructions, response scales, and peoples’ reasoning to decide on their response may be biased in favor of monotonic models. Of course, this is just a hypothesis and it needs to be tested with empirical data. To demonstrate a possible way of testing this hypothesis, we will present two empirical examples. The first example comes from social psychology and deals with excuse giving, as framed according to  Weiner ’s attribution theory ( 1986 ). The second example is focused on a subsample of a large-scale educational assessment.

According to  Weiner ’s attribution theory ( 1986 ), excuses can be classified regarding their locus of control. Excuses with external causes are those that attribute situational (and usually uncontrollable) influences to a social fault. Excuses with internal causes are those that attribute dispositional (and usually controllable) influences to a social fault. In the excuse giving context, if one has committed a social fault and the maintenance of the relationship is something desired, one will tend to use external over internal excuses, as they signal that the negative consequences were not intentional ( Weiner, 2006 ). However, because excuses are used when an individual commits a social fault, and because individuals that are more interested in maintaining a relationship are less likely to commit a social fault, it is reasonable to expect that the relation between the need to maintain a relationship is nonmonotonically related to the probability of an individual saying that they would a specific excuse in a specific context.

Franco and Iglesias (2017)  asked 92 undergraduate students, with an average age of 21 years ( SD  
                            
                               = 
                             2.98), to judge if they would, or would not, use a specific excuse in a specific context, as presented in  Table 1 . The authors created 16 excuses that were evaluated by 4 judges, with 4 excuses for each social context, created based on examples from  Weiner (2006) . The contexts were related to: (i) being late for an appointment; (ii) missing an appointment; (iii) having a poor performance on a task; and (iv) harming someone. Explicit relationship types (e.g., friendships) were avoided to decrease the probability of bias on the response of the participants. The judges agreed that half of the excuses were not appropriate to the locus of control or to the context and, therefore, those excuses were excluded from the final task.

To analyze these data,  Franco and Iglesias (2017)  used a Bayesian latent-mixture model, trying to identify groups of individuals that were more or less motivated to excuse themselves. In this empirical example, we used Bayesian methods to fit the Rasch model, the P-MFM, the I-MFM, and the AM-FM. The other IRT models derived from  Fishburn (1974)  theories (i.e., AFM, and MFM) were not included because they do not have any clear theoretical application to this dataset or because they are mathematically equivalent to the Rasch model. The same reasoning applies to why we did not include the SIRM in this analysis. To compare the performance of the models, we used two main strategies: the first based on overall fit; and the second based on item-fit-like statistics.

In the first strategy, we assessed the overall performance of the models using the posterior distribution of their deviances (i.e., –2 times the log-likelihood). These distributions were centered using the same reference value (i.e., the smallest MAP estimate considering all the models). Then, the centered deviances were. used to calculate ( Makowski et al., 2019 ): (i) the probability of direction (pD); (ii) MAP-based  
                               p 
                            -value of the posterior distribution; (iii) a binary decision variable of the inclusion or not of the smallest MAP estimate of the deviances within each model 95% highest density interval (HDI); and (iv) Bayes factors (BFs). For all these statistics, the reference value was set as 0, due to the centering based on the smallest MAP estimate of the deviance. We did not use AIC or BIC because the number of parameters, as well as the sample sizes, is equal for all the models. The densities of the posteriors were calculated using kernel density estimates with the number of equally spaced points equal to the sample size and the bandwidth chosen adaptively using  Sheather and Jones (1991)  method.

In the second strategy, the performance of each item in each model was assessed using the posterior predictive distributions of the fitted models ( Kruschke, 2015 ). More specifically, we calculated five accuracy-based performance metrics for each item: (i) accuracy (in the Guttman scaling literature known as the reproducibility coefficient;  Guttman, 1944 ); (ii) sensitivity; (iii) specificity; (iv) positive likelihood ratio (LR+); and (v) negative likelihood ratio (LR–). The accuracy is an overall measure of how well the responses fit to the patterns that can be expected by the fitted model. The sensitivity is a measure of how well the correct responses fit to the patterns that can be expected by the fitted model. Similarly, the specificity assesses the appropriateness of the predictions regarding wrong responses. The LR+ and LR– statistics are equivalent to the sensitivity and specificity, respectively, but represented as odds ratio. Because we were using the posterior predictive distributions of the fitted models, we could actually calculate the posterior distributions for each one of these metrics, and the corresponding 95% HDI of each metric, using the formulas in  Table 2 .

JAGS will also use slice sampling ( Neal, 2003 ) when there are discrete parameters and when the prior and the posterior distributions are not conjugate, being the sampler automatically chosen by the program. It is possible to see that the P-MFM model has a “fan-like” format: it basically always makes the same predictions for the probability of wrong answers, but has larger variance for the probability of a correct response. The I-MFM model looks like a two-parameter logistic model where the difficulty is fixed, but the discrimination is being estimated. Finally, the AM-FM model is a combination of the P-MFM and the I-MFM: it also has a “fan-like” format, but with a less prominent restriction. At the same time, it is quite similar to a two-parameter logistic model with fixed difficulty and free discrimination. Therefore, from a qualitative perspective, it reasonable to conclude that the models provide similar conclusions about the relation of the latent variable and the observed responses. However, the small differences in the form of the IRFs allow the models to identify different aspects of the patterns found in data.

To illustrate the differences between the proposed IRT models, we used a random sample of 500 respondents from an administration of the Brazilian National High School Exam ( Exame Nacional do Ensino Médio , ENEM;  Andriola, 2011; INEP, 2021 ). It consists of four subtests: languages; human sciences; natural sciences; and mathematics. Each subtest is analyzed separately and contains 45 items. In this example, we used the languages subtest of the ENEM. This subtest was chosen because it is less skewed and presents less outliers in its sum scores than the other three subtests. The main aim of this study was to develop Item Response Theory (IRT) models derived from the nondecomposable measurement theories presented in  Fishburn (1974) . More specifically, we aimed to: (i) present the theoretical basis of the Rasch model and its relations to psychophysics’ models of utility; (ii) give a brief exposition on the measurement theories presented in  Fishburn (1974, 1975) , some of which do not require an additive structure; and (iii) derive IRT models from these measurement theories, as well as Bayesian implementations of these models. We also presented two empirical examples to show that the new models have the potential to identify patterns in data that are not possible to be identified with the Rasch model. The new models accomplished this without the need to add more parameters.

The main take home message of the present study is that, if the assumptions of data structure hold, despite of what is asserted by some authors ( Borsboom, 2005 ), to have an additive structure is not a mandatory condition for a model to be able to estimate scores that are fundamental (i.e., measured in a ratio or interval scale) and, therefore, properties such as their means and standard deviations are meaningful. This conclusion is derived directly from the fact that if a measurement theory is valid, which is the case for the nondecomposable  Fishburn (1974, 1975)  models, and there is a fitting procedure for the measurement theory, then meaningful scores can be estimated. This approach, for the best of our knowledge, has not been applied in psychometrics so far.

Regarding our empirical examples, the results indicated that the new models are at least as efficient as the Rasch model in describing the data. This is a trivial finding when one considers the family of logistic models in the IRT literature ( Hambleton et al., 2000; Sijtsma & Van der Ark, 2021 ). However, it should be noted that in this tradition, a better fit is usually attained by increasing the number of parameters which, sometimes, makes the interpretation of the estimates of the true scores not meaningful ( Michell, 2008b; Scheiblechner, 2009 ). This issue was discussed, for example, by  Golino et al. (2021) , who argued that the Three-Parameter Logistic Model (3-PLM), despite giving a better overall fit to the ENEM data, does not result in scores that can be said to be truly in an interval scale. Therefore, as argued by some authors (e.g.,  Barrett (2008), Michell (2008a)  and  Sijtsma (2012) ), model fit should not be the only property assessed when one is evaluating the appropriateness of a model to explain or describe some data.

We emphasize that our results are of course dependent on our decisions regarding how to deal with the necessary restrictions of the  Fishburn (1974, 1975)  models. These decisions were arbitrary and different proposals could result in quite distinct results. Finally, it is worth to reinforce that measurement theory, despite its relevance for evaluating the validity of IRT models, has had little impact on psychometric practices. This point was discussed extensively by  Michell (2008a, 2008b, 2021a, 2021b)  and others (e.g.,  Cliff (1992), Heene (2013)  and  Sijtsma (2012) ). In this scenario, the present study offers an alternative for those willing to combine measurement theory and psychometrics: the implementation of psychometric models based on less known measurement theories. There are many measurement theories (e.g.,  Bouyssou and Pirlot (1999, 2002), Fishburn (1991), Luce and Narens (1983, 1985), Narens and Luce (1986, 1993)  and  Tversky (1967) ) for nonadditive data structures that still have no equivalent IRT models. Analyses for the adequacy of additivity in a data structure (e.g.,  Nygren (1980) ) are also not widely implemented in psychometric practice, and extensions of this type of analyses could be useful to provide further evidence on the appropriateness of applying additive, or nonadditive, IRT models to psychometric data.
