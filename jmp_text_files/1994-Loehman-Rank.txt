Rank Dependent Expected Utility: Stochastic Dominance, Risk Preference, and Certainty Equivalence 

EpNA LOEHMAN 

Purdue University 

This paper examines a type of nonexpected utility called “expected utility theory with rank dependent probabilities’ (EURDP) with respect to implications regarding dominance tests and risk aversion. EURDP uses two behavioral functions to represent preference: a utility function over outcomes and a transformation function applied to the cumulative distribution function. Properties of these functions are identified which make the preference order consistent with second-order stochastic dominance. Properties of the transformation function are shown to have interpretations in terms of optimism, pessimism, and risk aversion. Some well-known experiments are reexamined to make inferences about the nature of the transformation function. © 1994 Academic Press, Inc. 

This paper examines a non-expected utility theory—“expected utility with rank dependent probability” (EURDP) (Chew, Karni, and Safra, 1987) or “anticipated utility” (Quiggin, 1981, 1982)—with respect to risk aversion and other preference properties. One advantage of EURDP, compared to expected utility, is that it admits a larger class of behavioral types than just pure risk aversion or risk seeking. As shown here, the properties of the underlying functions in EURDP determine risk preference. Several well known experiments are reexamined here to derive implications about the nature of the underlying functions in EURDP. 

Another important property of EURDP (Wakker, 1989) is that it is consistent with first-order stochastic dominance (Bawa, 1975). Here, the relationships of both first- and second-order stochastic dominance to EURDP are examined. Results regarding dominance similar to those obtained for expected utility are derived here for EURDP. Dominance is defined below, following a detailed description of EURDP and a comparison to other nonexpected utility theories.

The original definition of anticipated utility by Quiggin was in terms of discrete lotteries. A more recent definition by Chew et al. (1987) was in continuous terms. Although psychology experiments are usually in terms of discrete lotteries, economic and financial applications frequently involve continuous outcomes, for example, the return on an investment.

STOCHASTIC DOMINANCE 

Below, stochastic dominance is defined. Later, it is related to “attitude toward fate” and “risk aversion.” Both risk attitude and risk aversion are described in terms of properties of S. 

The concept of stochastic dominance has been closely tied to expected utility theory and risk aversion in economics and finance theory and applications (Rothschild and Stiglitz, 1970; Hadar and Russell, 1969; Bawa, 1975; Hanoch and Levy, 1969). The linkage between dominance tests and preferences for expected utility has been used in economics and finance applications to determine an undominated set from a given set of risky choices. 

The same concept is used in insurance literature (Promislow, 1987) to compare risks based on the work of Hardy, Littlewood, and Polya; they work compared sums of n-dimensional vectors and use the term “majorize” instead of second-order dominance. 

Monotonicity, one axiom of expected utility (Luce and Raiffa, 1967), is one type of first-order dominance: if two lotteries have the same two possible outcomes (zero Or a positive value), but one of them has a greater probability of the positive outcome, then the lottery with the higher probability will dominate. First-order dominance also includes the case when the two lotteries have the same probabilities, but there is a greater positive outcome in one lottery; then, the lottery with the greater outcome will dominate. 

Experiments have indicated the consistency of stochastic dominance tests with risk preference. Preference rarely contradicts first-order dominance in experiments (MacCrimmon and Larson, 1979; Tversky and Kahneman, 1985). Work by Lopes (1984) supports the consistency of second-order dominance with preference for lotteries over gains. In Lopes’ experiments, subjects compared lotteries with the same mean values. Judgements of their riskiness were shown to be correlated with the relative positions of their Lorenz curves (ranking by the Lorentz curve when the mean is constant is equivalent to ranking by second-order stochastic dominance; Foster, 1985). Lopes also found that more risk averse subjects preferred less risky lotteries. 

The definitions of first- and second-order dominance below apply to both continuous and discrete lotteries. Firstand second-order dominance concern the partial ordering of pairs of cumulative distribution functions (c.d.f’s) for outcomes x defined on a common range [m, M]. This section gives a behavioral interpretation in terms of optimism and pessimism to properties of the transformation function S and relates these to dominance. Optimism and pessimism are risk attitudes different from risk

A behavioral reason for transformation of probabilities given by Lopes (1987) is that, while information regarding lotteries presented to a subject during an experiment is “objective,” objectivity does not imply that a subject will take probabilities to be predictive of outcomes particularly if the lottery is to be played only once. Thus, the expected value of a lottery may say little about its “anticipated value.” 

Hey (1984) first introduced the notions of optimism and pessimism in economic decisionmaking and described them as “attitudes toward fate” separate from risk aversion. A pessimist was defined to be a person who increases the probability of unfavorable events and reduces the probability of favorable events, whereas an optimist does the opposite. Therefore, a pessimist would reduce the expected value of a lottery whereas an optimist would increase the expected value of a lottery. However, Hey only considered first-order dominance in characterizing optimism and pessimism. Quiggin (1981) confirmed these modes of behavior in a limited experiment using certainty equivalents. 

Optimism and pessism can be differentiated according to the nature of dominance relations between an objective distribution and its subjective transformation.

THE LINK BETWEEN STOCHASTIC DOMINANCE AND RISK PREFERENCE FOR EURDP 

Results given below concern the relation between stochastic dominance and risk preference for alternative properties for functions underlying EURDP. Risk aversion results are special cases of dominance results. . 

The link between dominance tests and expected utility depends on the nature of the underlying utility function (Bawa, 1975). For a monotone utility function (preferring more to less}, expected utility and first-order stochastic dominance will rank pairs of lotteries consistently. For a concave utility function, expected utility and second-order stochastic dominance will rank pairs of lotteries consistently. Proofs of these relations for expected utility given by Bawa were based on integration by parts. Similar proofs for EURDP, by type of utility function and probability transformation, are given below. (Note that Bawa proved that dominance was both necessary and sufficient for consistency with expected utility ranking for classes of utility functions, whereas here only sufficiency is proved.) 

The definition of risk aversion here is similar to the traditional definition, except that it is relative to a given set of lotteries y. That is, risk preferences may be described differently for different y; e.g., if y is restricted to lotteries over gains, or to lotteries over losses, or to lotteries over mixed gains and losses.

CONCLUSIONS 

Ideally, a theory of risk preference should explain observed behavior, permit a variety of behavior types, and have well-defined mathematical properties. This paper demonstrates that EURDP has such properties. 

For EURDP, behavior 1s categorized not only in terms of the utility function but also in terms of probability transformation. “Attitude toward fate” is an interpretation of probability transformation; transformations of different types include pessimism, optimism, and weak optimism and pessimism, respectively associated with concave, convex, and symmetric transformations. Concavity of this transformation was shown to imply risk aversion if the utility function is either linear or concave. However, the theory allows other types of behavior besides risk aversion. 

One desirable property shown here is that both discrete and continuous lotteries can be represented by the same theory. Another important mathematical property of EURDP is its correspondence with stochastic dominance tests. The implications of dominance testing is somewhat different than in economic and financial applications for expected utility. In such applications, dominance has been used to reduce the choice set, given the assumption of risk aversion. Here, there is no presumption of risk aversion. Instead, as illustrated here, dominance can be applied to make inferences about the nature of preference from observed choices. 

Some well-known experiments were reexamined to determine implied properties for EURDP, The Allais experiment reveals that contradictions of expected utility can be explained by both concavity and convexity of S. Kahneman and Tversky’s reflection experiment about gains and losses implies that utility has a different concavity for gains and losses, with S symmetric, convex, or concave. The Ellsberg Paradox indicates that predominantly S is not symmetric. 

Dominance can be applied to design better experimental tests based on the results given in this paper. For example, experiments of the Allais and Kahneman and Tversky types with lotteries designed to satisfy stochastic dominance and varying money amounts and probabilities could be used to identify the nature of u and S more completely. 

Obviously, no theory may predict behavior perfectly in all occasions. New paradoxes may be found for EURDP (similar to those found by Yaari, 1987). A less restrictive theory than EURDP—e.g., allowing subjective weights to depend on
