In 1959, after spending a summer studying with J. Kemeny, a mathematician whose specialty was Markov chains (e.g.   Kemeny & Snell, 1959 ), Estes published a comprehensive treatment of that topic for learning and memory researchers. The proximal reason for that essay was to expand the mathematical foundations of stimulus sampling theory to deal with what, at the time, was known as the small  
                      N 
                    problem. In stimulus sampling theory, a set of  
                      N 
                    stimulus elements is randomly sampled on each trial of a learning experiment, and following reinforcement or feedback or a study trial, the elements change their conditioning states in an all-or-none fashion. As long as  
                      N 
                    was assumed to be large, as it traditionally had been, the law of large numbers applied, and the ordinary mathematics of probability theory could be used to derive quantitative predictions from stimulus sampling theory. However, there were certain types of experiments, such as discrimination learning, for which it seemed plausible that  
                      N 
                    must be small—so that ordinary probability theory could no longer be used. In such circumstances, predictions could be derived by implementing stimulus sampling theory in finite Markov chains, and Estes developed the necessary mathematical machinery to accomplish that. During the ensuing years, those developments were widely used by other authors (e.g.   Bower, 1961; Greeno & Steiner, 1964; Restle, 1962; Waugh & Smith, 1962 ), but not to derive predictions from stimulus sampling theory. Instead, finite Markov chains provided a natural framework for handling the all-or-none learning demonstrations that began to appear in the literature at about the same time as Estes’ essay (e.g.   Estes, 1960; Rock, 1958; Trabasso, 1963 ).

All-or-none learning refers to discrete changes in empirical response probabilities, rather than in theoretical entities, such as stimulus elements. Specifically, at the level of individual items, the probability of a correct response only takes on a small set of values, such as 0, some constant  
                      0 
                      < 
                      p 
                      < 
                      1 
                   , and 1. In the span of a few years, experiments in which subjects received multiple opportunities to study a focal list and to retrieve it produced data of that sort. Some tasks, such as associative recognition ( Bower, 1961 ) and concept identification ( Restle, 1962; Trabasso, 1963 ), were found to be one-stage Markovian; that is, variability in the probability of a correct response consisted of an initial unlearned state  
                      U 
                   , in which the probability was some constant  
                      0 
                      < 
                      p 
                      < 
                      1 
                   , and a terminal learned state  
                      L 
                   , in which the probability was 1. Other tasks, such as associative recall ( Greeno, 1968 ) and free recall ( Waugh & Smith, 1962 ), were found to be two-stage Markovian; that is, variability in the probability of a correct response consisted of an initial unlearned state  
                      U 
                   , in which the probability was 0, a partially learned state  
                      P 
                   , in which the probability was some constant  
                      0 
                      < 
                      p 
                      < 
                      1 
                   , and a terminal learned state  
                      L 
                   , in which the probability was 1. Using the developments in  Estes (1959) , it was a simple matter to derive the sampling distributions of a plethora of statistics for these models (e.g., trial number of the last error, trial number of first success, and auto-correlation of errors) and to use them to conduct rigorous quantitative evaluations of one- and two-stage Markov models for the indicated paradigms. Consequently, Markov chains dominated mathematical modeling work on basic memory paradigms for some years (for reviews, see   Brainerd, Howe, & Desrochers, 1982; Greeno, 1974 ). Ultimately, they faded from view as more complex modeling efforts were mounted, with the appearance of global matching models of memory being a prominent case in point (for a review, see   Clark & Gronlund, 1996 ).

Recently, however, there has been a resurgence of Markovian analyses of memory, for reasons not unlike those that motivated  Estes (1960)  essay—namely, the need for a mathematical framework that will help us gain leverage on important theoretical problems. In this instance, the theoretical motivation is supplied by dual-process conceptions of episodic memory. According to such conceptions (e.g.   Jacoby, 1991; Mandler, 1980 ), two forms of retrieval control memory for the events of our lives. The first is a recollective form, in which retrieval of episodic memories is accompanied by realistic phenomenology—vivid restatement of events’ prior occurrences and details of the contexts in which events were experienced. The second is a nonrecollective form, which confers high confidence that the events were experienced but is not accompanied by realistic phenomenology. The distinction between recollective and nonrecollective forms of retrieval has been exploited, particularly over the last three decades, to explain memory deficits in patients with brain lesions ( Schacter & Tulving, 1994 ) and neurodegenerative diseases ( Bugaiska, Morson, Moulin, & Souchay, 2011 ), brain activity during encoding and retrieval ( Diana, Yonelinas, & Ranganath, 2007; Ranganath, 2010 ), memory development ( Gomes & Brainerd, 2013; Reyna & Mills, 2007 ), and the effects of various experimental variables on memory, such as list length ( Cary & Reder, 2003 ), word frequency ( Gardiner & Java, 1990; Guttentag & Carroll, 1997 ), time of testing ( Knowlton & Squire, 1995; Tulving, 1985 ), and subjects’ level of attention during study ( Gardiner & Parkin, 1990; Yonelinas, 2001 ), among others (for a review, see   Yonelinas, 2002 )).

Dual-process distinctions have been studied primarily with old/new item recognition. To do that, old/new recognition has been enriched with metacognitive tasks that supposedly disentangle its recollective and nonrecollective components. Remember/know judgments ( Tulving, 1985 ) are far and away the most commonly used procedure, but confidence judgments, which allow recollective and nonrecollective components of the receiver operating characteristic (ROC) to be estimated ( Yonelinas, 1994 ), and inclusion versus exclusion instructions, which allow the recollective and nonrecollective parameters of the process-dissociation model ( Jacoby, 1991 ) to be estimated, have also been used. There are serious validity challenges to these recognition methodologies, however, and that has led to renewed interest in Markov models  of recall  as a response to the challenges—all of which forms the substance of the present paper.

The paper consists of four main sections. The first sketches traditional recognition-based approaches to measuring dual memory processes, summarizes recent validity criticisms of those approaches, and discusses the need for alternative measurement procedures that respond to the criticisms. The second section presents a recall-based response that we have implemented in recent experimentation. This approach uses fuzzy-trace theory’s (FTT) distinction between verbatim and gist traces of experience (e.g.   Brainerd & Reyna, 2004; Reyna & Brainerd, 2011 ) to interpret the states of two-stage Markov chains in such a way that measures of dual memory processes can be extracted from raw recall data, without the need of additional metacognitive tasks. In the third section, the mathematical machinery for this family of models (matrix representations and fit tests) is developed. In the fourth section, two worked illustrations of how these techniques can be applied experimentally to answer theoretical questions about episodic memory are presented. The first focuses on how factor analysis of parameter spaces over large numbers of data sets can be used to test the theoretical assumptions that lie behind this family of models. The second shows how the model can be extended to study dual memory processes in an important domain in which virtually no data on them are currently available, namely child-to-adult development.

That episodic memory is controlled by two distinct forms of retrieval, recollective and nonrecollective, is, of course, one of the touchstones of contemporary memory research.  Strong (1913)  is usually credited with being the first to provide an experimental demonstration of this distinction, but current interest can be traced to a theoretical paper by  Mandler (1980) . Strong reported a surprisingly modern recognition experiment, in which his subjects studied a list of target words and then responded to an old/new recognition test. After that, subjects were asked to review the items that they had accepted as old, both hits and false alarms, to introspect on the phenomenology that each provoked, and to write a brief description of that phenomenology. Despite great variability in the specifics of what subjects wrote, Strong noticed that the responses were of two basic sorts. Sometimes, old responses were justified by describing realistic details that had been associated with their presentation (e.g., that a subject’s stomach had growled or that a subject had remembered a dental appointment), whereas at other times, old responses were justified by assertions of confidence that were not backed up by realistic details (e.g., “just know it was on the list”, “must have seen it”). Strong also noticed that the first type of report, now called recollection, was overwhelmingly stimulated by hits, whereas false alarms primarily stimulated the second type of report, now called familiarity. In his later paper, Mandler reviewed experimental demonstrations of the recollection-familiarity distinction and provided compelling anecdotal illustrations, such as the well-known butcher on the bus example. He also developed a simple multinomial model of how recollection and familiarity affect hit rates, in which their effects were jointly independent and additive.

The modern literature on recollection and familiarity preserves two key features of  Strong’s (1913)  original demonstration: Old/new recognition is the focal memory paradigm, and the two forms of retrieval are measured by asking subjects to introspect on the phenomenological qualities of remembering (called metacognitive judgment nowadays).  Tulving’s (1985)  remember/know procedure is the most popular form of metacognitive judgment. For instance, when we recently reviewed the published literature on this procedure, we found that it contained over 1000 data sets. Two other procedures that have been extensively used are  Jacoby’s (1991)  process-dissociation methodology and  Yonelinas (1994)  dual-process ROC methodology. Together, these procedures have spawned vast behavioral and neuroscience literatures on recollection and familiarity. The former has been focused on identifying manipulations that dissociate the two forms of retrieval (e.g., word frequency, testing delays; see  Yonelinas, 2002 ), while the latter has been focused on identifying different brain regions that are active during one versus the other (e.g., the perirhinal cortex versus the parahippocampal cortex; see  Ranganath, 2010 ). The distinction between recollection and familiarity is also integral to contemporary explanations of memory declines in healthy aging and neurocognitive impairment (e.g.   Reyna & Mills, 2007; Schacter & Slotnick, 2004 ).

Of late, however, these standard methodologies have been the focus of mounting validity criticisms. Beginning with an article by  Donaldson (1996) , these criticisms are extensive for the most widely used methodology, remember/know judgments ( Dunn, 2004, 2008; Rotello, Macmillan, & Reeder, 2004 ). After reviewing several remember/know experiments, Donaldson concluded that the types of effects that were thought to support a dual-process view of retrieval could be handled by a one-process signal detection model. The model’s key feature was to posit that old/new responses are governed by two decision criteria, a conservative one for remember judgments and a liberal one for know judgments.  Dunn (2004)  independently reviewed a large set of remember/know experiments, using them to evaluate five arguments for rejecting one-process signal detection models. He showed that each argument was flawed and concluded that the remember/know data in his sample of experiments provided no grounds for rejecting one-process models. Later,  Dunn (2008)  demonstrated that if two retrieval processes control old/new recognition, remember/know data should be bidimensional when decision criteria are held constant, but in fact, they are one dimensional.

Major critiques of the other two methodologies, process dissociation and ROC, have also appeared. Concerning process dissociation, for example,  Ratcliff, Van Zandt, and McKoon (1995)  noted that if this procedure actually separates two distinct retrieval operations, it should not be possible to recover estimates of its recollection and familiarity parameters from a one-process model. They conducted a simulation analysis of reported estimates of the procedure’s recollection and familiarity parameters, using a well-known one-process model ( Gillund & Shiffrin, 1984 ) and found that the prediction was wrong; estimates of the recollection and familiarity parameters were recovered. Turning to the dual-process ROC procedure,  Wixted (2007)  compared its fits to those of a signal detection model in which the noise and noise + signal theoretical distributions have unequal variances ( Glanzer, Kim, Hilford, & Adams, 1999 ). Although the unequal variance signal detection model is compatible with a dual-process account in which the contributions of recollection and familiarity are combined into a unidimensional strength variable, it is also compatible with simpler, single-process accounts. However, on the grounds of parsimony, the latter (single-process) interpretation is preferred over the former (dual-process) as the reported ROC analyses did not differentiate between the two interpretations. Wixted found that whenever fit was different for the dual-process and unequal variance models, it was better for the unequal variance model, thus indicating that ROC analyses are compatible with single-process accounts.

A more recent criticism of all of these procedures centers on their inapplicability to certain subject populations that are of great interest in memory research—particularly, normal children and older adults with neurocognitive impairment ( Brainerd, Reyna, & Howe, 2009 ). In contrast to the vast number of remember/know experiments in the main stream adult literature, only a single study with normal children has been published ( Billingsley, Smith, & McAndrews, 2002 ) and only a handful of studies with impaired older adults ( Bugaiska et al., 2011 ). Likewise, virtually no research using the other two procedures has been reported with such subjects. The dearth of research is due to the fact that remember/know, process dissociation, and ROC are high-burden methodologies that are beyond the capabilities of most subjects in these populations. With remember/know, for instance, subjects must read and comprehend complex instructions telling them how to introspect on their mental states, and they must implement those instructions accurately on recognition tests. Ghetti, Mirandola, Angelini, Cornoldi, and Ciaramelli (2008; cited in  Ghetti, 2008 ) found that children could not follow even simplified versions of these instructions, and similar problems have been reported in studies of impaired older adults ( Brainerd, Aydin, & Reyna, 2012 ).

In a seminal review,  Malmberg (2008)  surveyed the bulk of the dual process recognition literature. He concluded that the enterprise of separating dual-retrieval processes with old/new item recognition had misfired, that standard procedures for effecting such separation are demonstrably unreliable, and that contrary to  Mandler’s (1980)  proposals, item recognition is controlled by a single familiarity process. Such conclusions leave us with a major methodological uncertainty: If the conventional tactic of enriching item recognition with metacognitive judgments fails to separate the recollective and nonrecollective forms of retrieval, how then can they be disentangled and quantified?

A solution that we have explored ( Brainerd, Aydin et al., 2012; Brainerd & Reyna, 2010; Brainerd et al., 2009 ) is to switch to recall-based measurement, which has two advantages relative to recognition. First and foremost, the uncertainty about whether and under what conditions single or dual processes control retrieval is avoided. Unlike recognition, it has long been known that dual-process models deliver better fits than one-process models to the data of associative, free, and cued recall experiments ( Bower & Theios, 1964; Greeno, 1968; Greeno, James, & DaPolito, 1971; Kintsch & Morris, 1965 ). Second, the high-burden problem with metacognitive judgments is also avoided, allowing research on dual retrieval processes to be extended to populations with cognitive limitations. In that connection, as we shall see, the recall solution extracts measurements of recollective and nonrecollective retrieval  directly  from the error-success data of recall tests, without metacognitive intervention. Consequently, it can be applied to any population that performs recall tests reliably, which includes young children and older adults with neurocognitive impairments. With children, there is a long-standing developmental literature in which associative, free, and cued recall have been used to chart normal memory development, from the preschool years onward (for reviews, see   Bjorklund & Muir, 1988; Schneider & Pressley, 1997 ). With older adults, recall tests are standard fare in the neuropsychological batteries that are used to diagnose cognitive impairment and dementia (e.g.   Langa et al., 2005; Morris et al., 1989 ), and indeed, performance on such tests is the best single predictor of these diagnoses ( Peterson et al., 1999 ).

The error-success data of recall paradigms are well fit by a two-stage Markov model whose general form was first developed by  Greeno (1968) . Over trials, the level of recall for an individual list item is described by three discrete performance states, with all-or-none escape from the first and second states—hence, “two-stage”. The probability of successful recall of an item is effectively zero before the first study cycle. This is the initial no-recall state  
                         U 
                      . After the first study cycle or any subsequent study cycle, an item in state  
                         U 
                       can remain there, can escape to the second partial-recall state  
                         P 
                      , in which the probability of successful recall is some constant  
                         0 
                         < 
                         p 
                         < 
                         1 
                      , or it can escape to the third perfect-recall state  
                         L 
                      , where the probability of successful recall is 1. After the first study cycle or any subsequent cycle, an item in state  
                         P 
                       can remain there or can escape to state  
                         L 
                      .

As memory models go, a rather minimal assumption is all that is required to use two-stage Markov chains to measure dual retrieval processes—namely, that these processes can be mapped with occupancy of the two states in which the probability of successful recall is greater than 0. The specific assumption ( Brainerd et al., 2009 ) is that recollective and nonrecollective retrieval can be mapped with occupancy of  
                         L 
                       and  
                         P 
                      , respectively. That interpretation was based on FTT’s conception of the different types of memory traces that support recollective versus nonrecollective retrieval. In that conception, recollective retrieval is supported by verbatim traces whose content can be directly accessed and simply read out of consciousness on a recall test, whereas reconstructive retrieval processes traces of partial-identifying information about items (usually called gist traces). The more detailed theoretical interpretations are as follows.

In state  
                         L 
                       recollective retrieval is referred to as “direct access” because this retrieval operation accesses verbatim traces of a list items’ prior presentation without searching through traces of other items. Consequently, it is the faster of the two operations. It is also the more accurate of the two because it supports errorless recall. Here, the idea is that when an item’s verbatim trace is directly accessed, its surface form and contextual details of its presentation are consciously reinstated, so that the item can be recalled by merely reading it out of consciousness. The fact that an item’s surface form is accompanied by vivid, realistic contextual details is what makes direct access a recollective operation, of course. Although direct access has speed and accuracy advantages, it has two limitations. The first is that during the initial phases of learning, verbatim traces are quite sensitive to sources of interference that are generated by recall tests, and the second is that because of such sensitivity, direct access rapidly degrades over the course of a recall test ( Barnhardt, Choi, Gerkens, & Smith, 2006; Brainerd & Reyna, 1993 ). This means that recall cannot rely exclusively on direct access until learning is so far advanced that the verbatim traces that have been stored are robust enough to survive the interference that recall tests generate ( Brainerd et al., 2009 ). Such reliance is not even an option in most recall experiments, which usually involve only a single study-test trial.

Turning to recall in state  
                         P 
                      , reconstruction is a nonrecollective operation and is accompanied by a slave judgment operation that performs familiarity checks on reconstructed items before outputting them. Reconstruction avoids the interference obstacle to direct access by regenerating list items from stable episodic traces of partial-identifying information, particularly semantic information (e.g., “clothing” for  shirt ). Such information is still available when verbatim traces are not, such as at the end of free recall protocols ( Brainerd & Reyna, 1993 ), when output interference is maximal ( Roediger & Schmidt, 1980 ), and after long intervals between study and test ( Brainerd, Reyna, & Brandse, 1995; Toglia, Neuschatz, & Goodwin, 1999 ). That subjects are able to reconstruct items from partial-identifying information is a fact, not a hypothesis, that is well established in the literatures on the tip-of-the-tongue phenomenon and feelings of knowing. In both literatures, subjects have been found to access various forms of partial information about list items and then process it in order to regenerate the items themselves (see   Brown & McNeill, 1966; Hicks & Marsh, 2002; Koriat, 1993, 1995; Schacter & Worling, 1985 ). With respect to semantic features in particular,  Koriat, Levy-Sardot, Edry, and De Marcas (2003)  found that subjects could access the semantic features in  Osgood’s (1952)  model of meaning before recalling the corresponding items. In the present conception, reconstruction uses partial-identifying features to generate sets of matching candidate items (e.g.,   shirt, coat, shoes, dress ) that are small enough to be processed rapidly on a recall test. Because such sets will normally include non-targets ( coat, shoes, dress ), a judgment operation performs pre-output familiarity checks on reconstructed items, in order to avoid high intrusion rates. Familiarity judgment is therefore a slave operation because it only operates on reconstructed items. Note that this is how the recall model incorporates the familiarity operation of dual-process theories of recognition.

Although nonrecollective retrieval avoids the interference problem that besets direct access, it poses a problem of its own. It is error-prone. On analogy to the familiarity operation of recognition theories, which permits false alarms, non-targets will sometimes survive the familiarity check and be passed on for recall. It follows that the advantage of recollection (errorless recall) ameliorates the problem posed by reconstruction, and the advantage of reconstruction (interference resistance) ameliorates the problem posed by recollection. Relying on both retrieval operations, then, increases net recall by compensating to some extent for each operation’s limitations.

Now, we turn to worked applications of the dual-retrieval model to recall data, in order to illustrate how it can be exploited to answer theoretical questions. This section consists of two subsections. In the first, we present findings from factor analyses of a large corpus of data sets from published and unpublished recall experiments that have accumulated since the early 1980s. In the second section, we sketch some applications of the model to the study of memory development between early childhood and adolescence, which illustrate how the present approach overcomes the high-burden problem with traditional metacognitive judgment methods of measuring dual processes.

Factor analyses of these sets of parameter estimates supplied validity tests of a sort that is rarely reported for memory models. More particularly, the factor analyses evaluated FTT’s mapping of different retrieval processes with the states of a two-stage Markov chain, across data sets that varied as to experimental conditions and subjects’ memory abilities. Suppose that, indeed, recall involves two distinct retrieval operations plus a slave familiarity operation, that recollective retrieval controls recall in state  
                            L

                            P

                            D

                            R

Next, we consider an application of the dual-retrieval model to memory development during childhood and adolescence. As mentioned, a major gap in the literature is that virtually no findings on the early development of dual processes have been reported, owing to the high-burden aspect of paradigms such as remember/know, process dissociation, and dual-process ROC. Also as mentioned, the dual-retrieval model avoids this limitation by extracting measurements of recollective and nonrecollective processes directly from the error-success data of recall tests. We illustrate how this advantage can be exploited by using the model to analyze data from some experiments reported by  Brainerd and Howe (1982) .

Summing up, this worked example illustrates the analytical steps that are involved in identifying which retrieval processes are responsible for developmental trends in performance on recall tests. In this particular example, those analyses converged on two simple conclusions. First, all of the age improvement in retrieval processes occurred after age 7. Second, the retrieval locus of that improvement was quite narrow; it was confined to improvements in the recollective operation, though there was some evidence of an increasing tendency for reconstructed items to become more familiar over trials.

On the surface, modern dual process ideas about episodic memory–the verbatim and gist traces of FTT or the item and knowledge traces of Shiffrin’s retrieving effectively from memory theory (e.g.   Shiffrin & Steyvers, 1997 ), for instance–are rather far afield from the ideas about conditioning states of stimulus elements that motivated  Estes’ (1959)  exegesis of Markov models. However, the underlying motivation for implementing such models is the same in both spheres, something that Estes would have been the first to recognize. That motivation is the desire for a simple, elegant mathematical framework in which important theoretical processes can be quantified and their ability to explain experimental effects can be studied systematically.

The present application of Markov models to the prototypical recall paradigms of the memory laboratory has both historical roots and contemporary features. On the historical side, it takes advantage of one of our field’s classic data archives. According to that archive, the error-success sequences of recall tasks are two-stage Markovian to close approximations because these models give quite good accounts of the fine-grain structure of such sequences. These models, then, supply an empirically grounded pathway to quantifying theoretical ideas about the processes that control recall. On the contemporary side, the present application resolves three problems that are posed by the currently dominant approach to measuring dual retrieval processes, which consists of making certain types of metacognitive judgments about old/new recognition decisions. The first two problems, which have been much discussed in the mainstream memory literature (e.g.   Malmberg, 2008 ), are that (a) recognition may involve only a single familiarity operation and (b) even if it involves dual retrieval operations, metacognitive judgment tasks are not reliable methods of separating them. The third problem, which has cropped up in the developmental and neuropsychological literatures, is that metacognitive tasks are high-burden methodologies that are beyond the capabilities of young children and older adults with neurocognitive impairments. Nevertheless, the measurement of dual retrieval processes is of great interest with those populations because these processes have been invoked to explain their characteristic memory deficits.

To date, the application of two-stage Markov chains to the study of dual retrieval processes has proceeded along two lines. The first, which was illustrated in this article, is the analysis and interpretation of a corpus of previously reported recall experiments. Across that corpus, there is variability in important characteristics of subjects (e.g., chronological age, normal versus disabled), list content (e.g., categorized versus unrelated words, pictures versus words), and recall paradigm (e.g., associative versus free, immediate versus delayed) that can advance theoretical understanding of what model parameters are measuring. The other line of research is the more traditional one of conducting new experiments that incorporate manipulations that are designed to test hypotheses about what the parameters are measuring, which leads to improved theoretical conceptions of dual retrieval processes.

Notwithstanding that these lines of research are recent ones, some instructive findings have already accumulated. This is not the place to undertake a comprehensive review of them, but we will mention a smattering of results that bear on three problems. The first is the possibility that, as with recognition, recall may involve only a one-stage operation. In that connection, we have studied a pair of one-stage alternatives to the dual-retrieval model of recall (panels B and C of  Fig. 1 ). Fit tests have repeatedly rejected one-stage models at very high levels of confidence, whereas fit tests have failed to reject the dual-retrieval model. Beyond this, factor analyses of parameter estimates from large numbers of data sets have been in broad agreement with the notion that two distinct retrieval processes and a slave judgment operation figure in recall.

The second problem, that metacognitive judgment tasks may be unreliable methods of measuring dual retrieval processes, vanishes with the recall approach. That is because familiar recall paradigms are not altered in any way, and nothing other than the usual sequences of errors and successes supply inputs to the measurement process. The novel feature is analytical rather than methodological: One or more versions of the five Markov chains are used to split sequences into components in which reconstruction is operating or direct access is operating or neither is operating. Model parameters then index the mix of these components in particular data sets, which forms the basis for evaluating hypotheses about recollective and nonrecollective retrieval.

The third problem, that metacognitive judgment tasks are too demanding for certain populations that are of considerable theoretical interest, also vanishes with the recall approach. The populations in question, young children and impaired older adults, are quite capable of performing standard recall tasks. Indeed, ironically, the literature on memory development between the preschool years and young adulthood is dominated by recall experiments because recall exhibits marked age improvements while recognition does not (for reviews, see   Schneider & Bjorklund, 1998; Schneider & Pressley, 1997 ). Likewise, recall experiments prevail in the literature on memory declines in elderly individuals who have transitioned to cognitive impairment or dementia, for similar reasons: Declines are far more apparent for recall than for recognition. Of course, the fact that recall tasks are within the capabilities of children and impaired older adults does not establish that the dual-retrieval model is as applicable to their recall data as to the data of subjects who do not have major cognitive limitations. It seems to be, however. This has been one of the foci of experimentation with the dual-retrieval model, and good model fits have been obtained in associative, free, and cued recall experiments with younger and older children, healthy older adults, older adults with mild cognitive impairment diagnoses, and older adults with dementia diagnoses.

In sum, the recall approach can be fairly said to still be on its shake-out cruise, but the results have been promising so far. The dual-retrieval model has delivered good fits to recall data throughout the human life span, and the behavior of its parameters has been broadly consistent with theoretical conceptions of recollective and nonrecollective retrieval. The preliminary evidence, then, points to the conclusion that this model is a productive addition to existing techniques for disentangling recollective and nonrecollective retrieval and exploring their respective contributions to episodic memory.
