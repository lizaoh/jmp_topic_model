JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 311, 338-365 (1987)  Grid Analysis: Continuing the Search for a Metric of Shape  ERNEST GREENE AND PETER WAKSMAN  Departments of Psychology and Mathematics, University of Southern California, Los Angeles, California 90081  A model of form perception is proposed, based on the concept that shape properties can be assessed by the measurement of the  dimensions of a form, taken at regular intervals over several directions in a grid of lines. These measurements are then  collected into a frequency distribution, which serves as a translationally invariant summary of shape properties for purposes  of perceptual judgment. The present article illustrates the basic approach using simple convex forms, with primary emphasis on  grid properties and the plausibility of the model with respect to biological substrates. An abundance of careful thought and research has been devoted to the analysis of shape, and yet no clear principles have  emerged which explain how we know what form is being viewed. Classic work by Attneave (1951, 1954) and others has attempted to  explain recognition as a function of the information contained in the stimulus display, but this approach raises questions as  to what constitutes the relevant information, as discussed in greater detail by Michels and Zusne (1965). Indeed, it may be  said that the essential challenge for a theory of form perception will be to formulate a clear model of how the physical and  objective characteristics of a figure correspond to psychological criteria which determine the perceived shape.  The most comprehensive efforts to quantify perception of shape have come from the application of multidimensional scaling  methods using random polygons. Multidimensional analysis presumes that some of the shape properties are qualitatively  distinct, and can be thought of as "dimensions" of the figure. For a given set of shapes, the relevant dimensions as well as  the scale of distances along each dimension are derived by having subjects make judgments of overall similarity of members of  the set. It is generally assumed that scale distances and dimensions alike reflect measurable characteristics of the figure,  and that the recognition of a particular shape is based on the determination of the scale value in each of the dimensions. In  other words, evaluation and recognition of a particular shape requires that it be judged using these dimensions, with its  "weight" along each dimension being determined, and the collection of such weights providing a summary of the shape. SHAPE METRIC 339  While this method seems plausible in theory, in practice it has been difficult to specify which attributes of the figure  provide the critical information. For example, using measurements such as size of angle, length of side, area, and axial  positioning, as well as algebraic combinations of these values, Brown and Owen (1967) found that 12 dimensions were required  to account for most of the variation among the values even for relatively simple, four-sided polygons.  A comprehensive theory of form perception should also address questions of "motion invariance"-specifying how translation,  rotation, and magnification of the figure will affect the measures which are taken. Ever since the introduction of the  "Erlanger program" (Klein, 1903), it has been understood that the "shape" of an object is defined by those measures which do  not change when the object is moved in relation to the measurement system. Since we judge shapes to be unchanged by  magnification, lateral movement, and (to an extent) by rotation, the value of a given theory will be determined, in part, by  the degree to which it can recognize forms in the presence of such motions.  In this paper we describe a formal method for measuring shape, which provides a summary reflecting the distinctive attributes  of simple geometric shapes in a quantitative way. The procedure requires the calculation of a frequency distribution of the  distances which are measured systematically across the form (from border to border) along the lines of a grid-more  specifically, along the lines of a hexagonal grid. The concepts behind the method are derived from geometric probability  (Waksman, 1985) and follow in the tradition of Novikoff (1962 ). Although there are some similarities to the procedures  suggested by Zusne (1970), as discussed below, the elaboration of such methods on a grid is new. This theory should be of  interest for the development of recognition algorithms because of the simplicity and effectiveness of the mathematics.  Preliminary experiments for artificial pattern recognition have been encouraging (Waksman, 1987b). It also provides a basis  for evaluating human shape judgement, and thus may serve as an impetus and guide to further research.  SUMMARIZING SHAPE USING GRID ANALYSIS  There are many interesting problems associated with the early stages of visual processing, such as how the system transforms  shadings and textures to allow perception of a sharp boundary. In this paper we assume that such early processing has been  accomplished, and that the problem is to provide a quantitative measurement of the shape so that it may be recognized. We  further restrict the discussion to the measurement of two-dimensional, solid convex shapes. The mathematical basis for  generalizing to non-convex shapes has been presented by Waksman (1986, 1987a, b), and will be elaborated as a model for human  perception in a subsequent paper.  As a general frame of reference for the analysis to be performed, the form  340 GREENE AND WAKSMAN  stimulus can be thought of as being projected upon a flat (or hyperbolic) "analysis field." This field is cross-connected by a  grid lines which serve as communication links between the internal elements of the field and the summary operations which lie  (at least figuratively) at the border of the field.  It should be noted that the explanations and illustrations of the present paper are made using a three-axis grid. As we will  discuss subsequently, there is reason to believe that the biological substrates of human perception use a three-axis grid.  From a mathematical standpoint, the process of line-measurement and summary is the same whatever the number of axes chosen,  though the number of axes and their arrangement have an effect on the results of that analysis.  The drawing in Fig. 1 portrays the key elements of the present approach, with the shape stimulus being shown as a translucent  gray so that grid segments and points of intersection can be seen. A count of the number of occluded grid points is taken  along each line, which provides an estimate of the distance through the form along each line. The estimate is made by counting  all grid points lying completely within the boundary of the form; one additional point which lies on or very near the boundary  can be counted, but not two. This provides estimates of the distance from edge to edge at regular intervals. These values are  then used as the independent variable in a histogram which plots the frequency of occurrences as a function of the possible  distance measurements. We will see that the histogram reflects unique properties of the shape, and thus provides an efficient  "global summary."  For the disk shown in Fig. 1 we find that 1 line crosses in 4 units, 1 in 5 units, 2 in 6 units, 9 in 7 units, ..., 15 lines  cross in 25 units, and 13 in 26 units. It is no coincidence that so many lines cross in 25 or 26 units. The disproportionate  number of such lines and other characteristics of the histogram provide a distinct "signature" for the disk being analyzed. As  indicated above and illustrated in Fig. 1, this information is summarized by a frequency distribution where for each possible  number of distance unit (the independent variable), one plots the number of occurrences of lines crossing in this given number  of units (the dependent variable). COMPARISON OF HISTOGRAMS FOR SIMILARITY AND MOTION INVARIANCE  The goal is to develop a method for quantifying shape so that one can compare shapes for their degree of similarity, as well  as for the effects of motion. The method of measurement described above provides a summary of the shape in the form of a  frequency distribution (the histogram), and this greatly simplifies the task. Since a histogram is a function of the variable  n rather than a two-dimensional image, any number of alternative comparison procedures may be used to derive a single value  which reflects the degree of similarity between any two histograms. We generally judge shapes as being unchanged when their position is changed in the visual field.  Thus a triangle may be moved without changing the perception that its "shape" is the same. In any of the above examples the  form stimulus can be moved by translation without producing much change in the corresponding histograms. For the disk of Fig.  1, the large number of lines crossing in the maximum possible distance reflects the fact that this distance is most likely,  and this fact is not change by movement of the disk. Figure 3 illustrates the extent to which the histogram summary is  invariant with translation. An ellipse can be moved laterally to any arbitrary position on the grid (or placed deliberately so  as to ensure a modified alignment relative to grid lines), yet the histogram changes very little. Calculation of the  mean-square difference for the ellipses shown at 3.1 and 3.2 yields a value of 6. This extremely low value (see below)  confirms that the histograms for the initial and shifted histograms are very similar.  Rotation is not invariant. Figure 4 illustrates the effect of tilting the forms shown in Figs. 2 and 3. The histograms of  tilted forms can be compared to those shown for the previous figures. The mean-square differences for the untilted versus  tilted shapes are as follows:  2.1 vs 4.1 (square) =34 2.2 vs 4.2 (triangle) = 8 3.1 vs 4.3 (ellipse) = 19.  The similarity value for the triangles is quite low, which conforms to our judgment that these forms are fairly comparable.  The value for the ellipses is moderately low, but comparison of the unrotated and rotated squares suggests that they should  not be perceived as being very similar.  In general we have learned to expect that a good theory of form perception will be invariant with respect to all  motion-rotation as well as translation. When it was found that the present measurements vary with rotation, the initial  impulse was to look for a correctional procedure which would produce histogram values (or comparison values) which are the  same for comparisons among rotated and unrotated objects. However, additional consideration of this matter suggests that a  proper model of human judgment should not consider these forms equivalent. The work of Rock (1973, 1974), Cooper and Shepard  (1978), and Jolicoeur and Kosslyn (1983) point up the separability of rotational judgment from other form properties, and the  need to propose specific mental operations for comparing rotated and unrotated objects. From common experience, we are aware  that a shape may appear different when it is seen in a tilted or upside-down position, and we often have separate names for  the two forms (e.g., square vs diamond). In that vein, it is interesting to note that the mean-square comparison of the tilted  square with the untilted triangle (4.1 vs 2.2) yields a similarity value of 26, suggesting that the tilted square looks more  "triangular" than it does square. This may in fact correspond to our perceptions. ALTERNATIVE GRID DESIGNS  There is a variety of alternative types of grid that are of mathematical interest. The process of calculating a frequency  distribution for a form located in a grid is the same regardless of the type of grid which is chosen, but each grid has its  own peculiar properties with respect to translation or rotation of the form. These are briefly noted to provide a broader  context for discussing the advantages of the three-axis grid as a model of human perception.  (a) Irregular grid: This is a grid consisting of random lines with no bias in their position or orientation, such as could be  generated by a stochastic line-process (Santalo, 1979; Davidson, 1974). It has the best properties with respect to motion  358 GREENE AND WAKSMAN  invariance, and excludes the kind of rotational anomaly observed for objects in a regular grid. One objection to this grid  design for modeling perception is that it is not consistent with the hexagonal packing of visual receptors. Another is that it  does not explain our ability to distinguish forms in different tilts; for example, it would not provide a differential summary  as a basis for judging square and diamond shapes as being distinct.  (b) Regular grid: This is grid consisting of the superposition of two, three, or more families of uniformly space parallel  lines. The three-axis grid has received adequate attention in previous examples. The two-axis grid has similar properties but  the rotational variance is much more pronounced. Generally, for more axes there is a greater degree of the rotational  invariance of the measurement; however, even with two or three axes the grids produce a great deal of translational invariance.  (c) Radial grids: Such a grid consists of one or more centers, with regular or irregular spacing, from which the lines radiate  like spokes from a "wheel hub" (in the terminology of Dow et al., 1984). This grid has good translational invariance  properties, and if the lines radiating from the centers have no bias in their direction, the measurements will also be  approximately invariant under rotation.  As a mathematical possibility it is worth considering the case of a radial grid with a single center with lines radiating from  it. In this grid an object rotated about some center will have no invariance in its histogram summary unless the center of  rotation coincides with the center of the grid, and not even then if there is a bias in the direction of the radiating lines.  In no case will there be any translational invariance.  Given a particular type of grid there are several ways of improving the quality of the frequency distribution which summarizes  the grid analysis. One way is to increase the number of lines of the grid, although care must be taken with the units of  distance so that the span of the field of analysis remains the same.  Another way to improve the summary histogram without changing the grid is to propose that small "eye movements" have the  effect of jiggling the location of the object on the grid and averaging the corresponding histograms. This sort of successive  sampling could increase the sample size and provide for smoothing of the histogram. The smoothed histogram would also be more  invariant with translational motion of the form. Translational invariance should be nearly perfect when eye movement is  greater than the distance between adjacent parallel lines in the grid, and this suggests that an efficient system would not  "jiggle" any more than is necessary to ensure overlapping positions of neighboring points in the grid (i.e., half the Nyquist  limit). We note that the size of involuntary eye movement (tremor) is approximately what would be needed to provide optimal  data for histogram smoothing. While perception of form may occur without such movement, there is reason to believe that the  quality of judgment is improved by allowing eye scanning and perhaps also tremor. We consider the fact that eye movement would  improve the measurement process as one of the real strengths of the present theory or of any such theory based on  probabilities. The analysis of shape by  the statistics of distance measurements along lines has been applied by Wong and Steppe (1969) in the field of computer  vision, and this approach has been familiar in integral geometry for several years (Santalo, 1979). However, because of a mode  of thinking based on the direct location of points rather than on such indirect line-measurements, this method has received  less attention than it deserves.  Additionally, the calculations are difficult in the abstract. By studying a finite grid of lines one can calculate frequency  distributions explicitly for a variety of different shapes, providing some practical insight into this method. For the simple  shapes that have been examined, the frequency distributions are not bell-shaped, but rather have characteristics which are  specific to the forms being measured.  It may not be clear the extent to which the frequency distribution contains information about the object measured. It is  known, for example, that the shape of an object can be reconstructed from its Radon transform where distance information is  recorded from each line, all lines in the plane are used, and the location of the line used is retained as part of the  information. However, the frequency distribution ignores which line gives which measurement and so it would seem that its  calculation implies a substantial loss of shape information. Surprisingly this is not the case. We know of only one example  (Mallows & Clark, 1970) of two different (non-congruent) shapes with the same frequency distribution for distances over all  lines. It is not generally understood that such examples are very rare, though one can prove that examples of different forms  with the same measurement are the exception rather than the rule (Waksman 1983, 1985). Thus generally the shape is uniquely  determined by the frequency distribution and there is no loss of information. In particular the radon transform contains no  more information than the frequency distribution except for the information about the object's location. A summary decision  may not require this information, and indeed may require that location be made irrelevant to the decisional process. The same  holds true for nonconvex objects, though the measurement process which generates the frequency distribution is more  complicated and no longer directly relates to the radon transform.  Unfortunately we are not using all lines in the plane, only a finite sample of such lines; we need to be clear on the relation  to all lines. In integral geometry the set of all lines is understood as coming equipped with an "invariant measure" that  replaces the notion of "counting" when finite sets of all lines are replaced by infinite sets of lines (Santalo, 1979, p. 27).  In that context a finite grid of lines is a systematic random sample of lines and the frequency distribution approximates a  pure of "true" distribution which is defined in terms of all lines. More specifically, let X be the finite collection of lines  in a bounded grid. PLAUSIBILITY OF A THREE-AXIS CORTICAL LATTICE  As indicated above, the present model is not limited to a particular grid design. The analysis of shape may be accomplished  with a grid having any number of axes, or with a patchwork quilt of mixed design. However, as a basic model we favor a  three-axis grid as being most consistent with general principles of cell packing. A clear hexagonal packing of cones is seen  in photographs of foveal retina in Macaque monkey (Miller, 1979). The most plausible concept for the sampling of images,  therefore, would be from the points which lie along the three axes of the hexagonal array.  At the level of the cortex, there is reason to believe that communications among cells may occur through a regular lattice.  Several investigators have described shifts in orientation preference of single neurons using diagrams which show alignment of  the preferred orientation. Mitcheson and Crick (1982) have formulated a model in which the responses of single units are  coordinated through an extended four-axis lattice. RELATION TO OTHER MODELS  As indicated above, the foundations for the model come from geometric probability, as reviewed by Santalo (1979). Some of  these concepts have been applied to problems of machine vision (Novikoff, 1962; Wong & Steppe, 1968). Sutherland (1957)  suggested the possibility that the radon transform could be used as the basis for shape recognition, and Zusne (1970) further  noted the possibility of  362 GREENE AND WAKSMAN  using the moments of these histograms (taken separately or in combination) as potential contributors to the psychological  judgment of form.  Analysis of shape and pattern using Fourier transforms has become popular in recent years. Casasent (1985) describes methods  for real-time recognition of figures using coherent light processors, and Ma et al. (1986) describe fast Fourier descriptors  which allow translational, scale, and rotational invariance. The computations required by these procedures seem ponderous, and  the models have not been formally evaluated in relation to human judgment.  The use of Fourier transform is implied by theories of spatial frequency analysis-often selective cell response is postulated  as a means for providing Fourier (spatial frequency) descriptors. However, few investigators are specific about how the shape  of an object is to be identified. Ginsberg (1971, 1978) suggests that shape properties are especially reflected in the  low-frequency components of the stimulus. Some have taken exception to this generalization (e.g., Janez, 1984), but aside from  the question of evidence, the model can be faulted for failing to provide particulars about how to process the information  that has been derived from the image. For example, how does one distinguish between a square of side length d, and a circle of  diameter d? Measurement over two axes (as is usually done) will show similar patterns of low-frequency components. Are we to  assume that analysis over more than two axes is needed to define the particular shape? Is the shape definable as a summary of  these several profiles, and is recognition based on a match with a collection of tabled values for the Fourier transforms?  The choice of number of axes seems analogous to the considerations given above, of which type of grid should be used. We think  the spatial frequency approach would be more workable (explaining such things as shape invariance under translation), if the  measurement process were followed by the calculation of a frequency distribution. If this were done, the "spatial frequency"  theories would have more in common with the methods we have proposed.  Some methods of shape analysis (e.g., Hoffman & Dodwell, 1985) place an emphasis on the description of the boundary. Two  shapes which have the same curvature descriptor (Lie function) will be classified as being equivalent, but it is unclear how  differences in curvature would be judged. By contrast with such an ordered collection of local descriptors, we make use of an  ordered collection of global descriptors-the value of the histogram at each n is a count of all pairs of (collinear) boundary  points which lie n distance units apart. Such a global analysis avoids some of the computational difficulties of boundary  descriptors and provides an explicit method for comparison among shapes.  It has also been suggested (e.g., Biederman, 1985) that the visual system can extract important "features" of a shape. A  summary of shape might be a list or table of such characteristics, with discrimination among shapes being accomplished by  cross-tabulation of the entries in the table. For example, the summary could be an array of numbers where each position in the  array corresponds to a specific feature and the numerical value at that position specifies whether it is present in the shape  under analysis. Such a feature array would not depend on the location of the object,  SHAPE METRIC 363  and similarity among shapes could be accomplished by comparing different arrays-position by position. The present model can be  included under this general approach, where a single family of features (the boundary-to-boundary distances) is measured. The  magnitudes of these features are reflected in the histogram, which can be stored as a linear array of (tabled) values. The  analysis of the boundary-toboundary distances as key feature dimensions is computationally simple, requiring only the counting  and sorting of data gathered from an array of sensors. The method has intuitive appeal, avoiding many questions of how to  weigh or combine more complex feature sets. It also advances the problem to a new level: establishing what comparison methods  match our perceptual judgments.  In Euclidean geometry two shapes are defined as being alike (congruent) if one can be moved and superposed on the other. Such  congruence can be evaluated by digitizing the image of the shape and its background, and then determining the correspondence  of elements on a point-by-point basis. Many computational algorithms for processing of shape information make use of such  proceduresrelying on the high speed and memory capacity of electronic systems to accomplish the "movement" and comparison of  points in an exhaustive manner. However, aside from the issue of how to find the best motion for comparison when  superimposition is not exact, it is not clear how to evaluate departure from match. In other words, the question of how to  define similarity of non-congruent shapes is not specified by classical geometry. We have addressed this issue by providing a  method for quantifying and summarizing shapes, and also for comparing the degree of similarity of those summaries. It may be  said that these methods provide a specification of superposition failure, wherein the best motion for partial superposition is  already implicit in the calculation of the histogram (Waksman, 1987a).  CONCLUSION  The model proposes that the shape of an object is evaluated by taking measurements of its span at regular intervals, as though  the form had been placed on a grid lattice which could provide these values in several directions. The measured values are  then sorted to form a histogram, with the frequency of observed distance being the dependent variable. Our initial model  combines the values derived from each axis to form a single histogram, thus providing a method for quantifying and comparing  shapes for similarity and for motion invariance.  The model reduces a two-dimensional image stimulus into a one-dimensional histogram in which the information is reflected in  the magnitude of successive values. Our method draws upon previous suggestions that one can derive useful information about  the shape of an object by measuring the border-to-border span at regular intervals. We then add the concept that the  measurements should be summarized as a probability distribution, plotting the frequency of measured distances as the dependent  variable. Other unique aspects of our approach include discrete sampling, use of a three-axis grid, and use of an average of  the values  364 GREENE AND WAKSMAN  across all axes in forming the probability distribution. By deriving a single probability distribution for each shape, we are  provided with a summary which greatly simplifies the process of classification and comparison. Classification can be  accomplished by comparing the distribution to a tabled set of prototype values, or with the values of the derived moments.  Judgment of similarity among forms can be accomplished by direct comparison of the distributions using the mean-square  difference, the cumulative absolute difference, or some other strategy-the best model being one which is consistent with  principles of information flow and comparison in networks of neurons.
