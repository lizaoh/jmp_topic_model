In order to summarize a student's performance by an evaluative score, it is common to assess his or her performance on separate subtests, and to then simply compute the arithmetic mean from the obtained scores on the subtests. Although regarding the average as an overall score is common, there is no “a priori” reason to do so, and in some cases it may be better to weight the subtest scores (e.g., according to their importance) and compute their weighted arithmetic mean. However, when the subtest scores reflect percentages, it may be better to compute the (weighted) geometric mean. In other cases, such as rating shops or department stores by different attributes (e.g., favourability of prices, variety of their stock, or quality of service), one may wonder how, and by what mechanisms, individuals form an overall evaluative rating of a store from a set of separate ratings of attributes. Possible combinations of these prior attributes can be represented by among others the (a) (weighted) arithmetic mean, (b) (weighted) geometric mean, (c) (weighted) exponential mean or (d) total sum-score. Even if two persons apply the same type of “formula” for combining information, they may weight the attributes differently. On the other hand, if the weights attached to the attributes are the same, personal “formulas” may be different.

In this paper we show that two conditions, reflexivity and  n -variable bisymmetry, determine a general class of rules of combination or information integration: that of quasilinear means, to which the above types of average belong. The main part of the paper contains a number of experiments designed to test the second, crucial condition for the applicability of this general class: that of  n -variable bisymmetry. These tests give supportive evidence that people can use combination methods, equivalent to quasilinear means, for such intra-personal multi-attribute evaluation tasks.

A well-known and important psychophysical scaling process is bisection. Its experimental prototype consists of presenting the subject two different stimuli with the task to find a third stimulus—the bisecting stimulus—in the middle of the two presented stimuli ( Stevens, 1955, 1957, 1975 ;  Fagot, 1963 ). Bisection is also formalized by the functional equations of bisymmetry and reflexivity ( Pfanzagl, 1968/1971 ). This formalization is motivated by the various concepts of means (as mentioned above) and asserts the internal consistency of taking midpoints. Bisymmetry operations have received much attention in the context of conjoint measurement for bisection systems and have been studied by  Torgerson (1958) ,  Pfanzagl (1968/1971) ,  Krantz, Luce, Suppes, and Tversky (1971) ,  Coombs, Dawes, and Twersky (1981) ,  Falmagne (1985)  and  Luce, Suppes, Krantz, and Twersky (1990) . In the context of information integration it has been studied by  Anderson, (1962, 1970, 1991) , among others. The bisymmetry property for two variables and some generalizations of bisymmetry have been studied extensively, and for a good review of relevant work concerning bisymmetry and its applications see  Aczél (1997) .

Testing reflexivity is straightforward, but testing the key element of bisection—that is bisymmetry—needs some care. In order to test 2-variables bisymmetry by experiments, some input values  x ,  y ,  z  and  w  must be selected by the experimenter. Then the overall values of  B ( x ,  y ),  B ( z ,  w ),  B ( x ,  z ) and  B ( y ,  w ) are produced (according to specific instructions) by each subject, and finally the two “cross–overall” values  B ( B ( x ,  y ),  B ( z ,  w )) and  B ( B ( x ,  z ), B ( y ,  w )) are also produced by each subject. Although the input values  x ,  y ,  z  and  w  are the same for each subject, the reported overall evaluations  B ( x ,  y ),  B ( z ,  w ),  B ( x ,  z ),  B ( y ,  w ),  B ( B ( x ,  y ), B ( z ,  w )) and  B ( B ( x ,  z ), B ( y ,  w )) may differ per subject (even the function  B  may differ). Altogether six overall values are produced by each subject, and to validate 2-variables bisymmetry, the bisymmetry equality  B ( B ( x ,  y ), B ( z ,  w ))= B ( B ( x ,  z ), B ( y ,  w )) should hold for each subject (independently of their personal  B  function). Taking a random sample of subjects and allowing random discrepancies from the equality of bisymmetry, then e.g., by applying the  t -test or Wilcoxon-test (depending on the distributional assumptions of the difference), it is possible to test whether bisymmetry holds in the population represented by the sample. It is worth noting that in the context of information integration, 2-variables bisection is tested by variants of monotone analysis of variance (see experiments in, e.g.,  Carterette & Anderson, 1979 ;  Anderson, 1977 ;  Weiss, 1975 ).

Examples of quasilinear means ( Aczél, 1966 ) include the weighted arithmetic, geometric, harmonic, root-mean-square and exponential means. In economics, for linking microeconomic and macroeconomic levels, some related types of functions, called functional equations of aggregation (see, e.g.  Pokroff, 1978 ) have been used extensively ( Green, 1964 ;  Gorman, 1968 ;  Aczél & Maksa, 1996, 1995  and  Aczél, 1997 , among others).

We have seen above that if, next to reflexivity,  n -variables bisymmetry is valid for a population of subjects, then their overall evaluation and information integration across different attributes corresponds to quasilinear means. Hence testing  n- variables bisymmetry is crucial for the relevance of quasilinear means for such evaluation processes. This is what we have tried to do in the next section.

In the case of bisymmetry, the difference between  BISR  and  BISC  should be equal to zero for each test person. However, because responses from a test person may contain some disturbances, it would be too rigid to let bisymmetry be determined by whether the difference between  BISR  and  BISC  (denoted by DIFF) is equal to zero or not. Hence, to clarify that the resulting data fit the bisymmetry assumption, we present several pertinent statistics across the subjects. A good fit of data to the bisymmetry assumption would imply that the mean value of DIFF is not significantly different from zero. Of course, basic testing can be done without the assumption and associated testing of normality, by using a non-parametric test, such as the Wilcoxon one sample test. However, here we also test specifically the additional normality assumption implying the more stringent test setup reported. A  t -test (with 2-tailed significance) is performed to test whether the mean of DIFF is equal to zero. In each of our experiments, the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test (with 2-tailed significance) showed non-significance from normality for the distribution of DIFF. Also, the ratio of the mean of the absolute value of DIFF (denoted by “ absdiff ”) and the theoretical (which may differ from the observed range) range of DIFF (denoted by “ fullrange ”) is computed and this value gives an overall picture of the  deviance from bisymmetry  (denoted by “ devbis ”). In each of the experiments, the input stimuli are given in comparable scale values. In Experiment 1, it is the length of the line representing hypothetical students’ performances on different history subjects. The performances of the different history subjects may reflect qualitatively different knowledge depending on the subjects, but are scaled on a joint performance scale. The actual scale values are varied, and have no special meaning. It is supposed that only the scale values, and not the qualitative background (which is actually unknown), of the history subjects are relevant in making an overall evaluative judgement, and the obtained overall evaluative judgement also assumed to be given on the same performance scale. A simple joint performance scale could be the percentage of correct responses to different tests of factual knowledge on each of the three history subject. In this simple case, the percentage of correct responses of a student is the only information used for making an overall judgement. These assumptions are common in schools when a student is evaluated by a teacher, but also applied e.g., in marketing research, when the attribute-wise utility scales are supposed to be comparable. In Experiment 2, the input scale values (a number scale from 0 to 10) refer to the “goodness” of a store on different attributes (prices, variety of stock, and quality of service). Similarly to Experiment 1, it is assumed that the scale values contain all the information necessary for making an overall value judgement. For example, an input value 4 of the attribute “prices” may refer to a complex qualitative price structure of the store in question, but its “goodness” is scored 4 relatively to other stores. The same value 4 on the attribute “quality of service” may refer to an acceptable service, but its “goodness” is scored 4. Similar remarks can be made about the variety of stock. A respondent makes an overall value judgement based only on the “goodness” values on the attributes, and the overt judgement is also a “goodness” value. In Experiment 3, the scale values represent “goodness” of positive personal characteristics, and in Experiment 4, it is “chance” of happiness.

The conclusions from this work will be provided after the experiments have been described.

The context of the experiment is a familiar one, corresponding to a situation where students are evaluated by their performances. The three input stimuli are the hypothetical performances on different sub-tasks and these performances are represented by the lengths of line segments on a computer screen.

Results : From the responses to the training and accuracy section, we computed for each subject, a kind of inaccuracy measure. First, we computed the differences between the person's responses and the corresponding six standards, and then took the maximum of the absolute values of these differences, and this final value was regarded as a measure of a test person's inaccuracy. The mean value and the standard deviation of this measure of inaccuracy over the sample was 1.35 and 0.67, respectively. The range of the possible inaccuracies is 100 (i.e., from 0 to 100), hence, the sample mean of the observed inaccuracy compared to the theoretical range is rather low.

The mean of DIFF was −0.5 (std=2.92, min=−6, max=4) and the data show a very good statistical fit with the bisymmetry assumption ( t -value=−0.763, df=19, 95% conf. int.i.=(−1.87, 0.87), sign>0.45). Also, the mean of  absdiff  (=2.2), compared to the full range of DIFF ( fullrange =200) is rather low ( devbis =0.011). It is important to note that the difference between the mean value of the training inaccuracy score (=1.35) and  absdiff  (=2.2) is less than 1, that is, less than the unit (“pixel”) of the measure (line length). For a more detailed description of the design, procedure, dataset and results, see  Fig. 4 . We may summarise, that based on the subjects rather accurate responses, the bisymmetry condition holds in this experiment.

It is worth noting that  Anderson (1977)  found that for lengths of rods the bisection of 2-variable model failed. Yet, contrary to his finding, our Experiment 1 validated a 3-variable bisymmetry model. The reason for the different findings may be that in our experiment the participants had to evaluate the overall performance of hypothetical students, and the modality of the input was the length of a line on a computer screen (representing the performance on specific topics). In Anderson's experiment, the task was to directly bisection a length of rods, hence, the two tasks were different.

Experiment 1 was done in a laboratory environment, with a relatively easy task (evaluating student's overall knowledge). For the second experiment we turned to a more complex setting in the form of a field experiment. The key element of the task was evaluating a store's overall quality. This kind of task is probably experienced frequently (either consciously or unconsciously) by people, and choosing it was motivated also by an earlier study of supermarket choices done by  Louviere and Meyer (1981) . Using a specific regression model, they found that consumers stated that prices, selection and convenience were the most important decision attributes (more or less equally important). The core assumption of the bisection process is bisymmetry. It is important to note that linear combination of “input scores” satisfies bisymmetry, but bisymmetry is also compatible with a much larger class of averaging rules. In most of the linear structural models, bisymmetry is implicitly assumed.

Although the present data came from only four experiments with relatively small sample sizes, the results were convincing enough to confirm bisymmetry. Given that bisymmetry holds, the corresponding mathematical model suggests that the subjects are “averaging” information, as previously suggested by Anderson. From our theory, it follows that respondents do this in a way analogous to taking one of the many averaging functional forms within the class of quasilinear means. However, the results do not suggest which particular linear (or quasilinear) combination of the input scores is the best. It is even possible that different respondents use different integration rules. In  Table 6 
                      , it can be seen that the so-called “obvious” arithmetical mean, as common to previous works on information integration, is not the general form of integration. We computed the arithmetic means of the rows and columns for the corresponding designs in each of the experiments, and performed  t -tests to test the equality of these arithmetical means and the actual responses.

The substantive amount of the standard deviations of the responses across persons also shows that there may not be a “unique” integration function, therefore it may be different for each subject. The demonstrated  n -variables bisymmetry does not allow for a more specific conclusion on this point. The different forms of these functions will be the topic of another paper. We observed that training leads to stability of bisymmetry judgements and that increasing the number of inputs from 3 to 5 does not increase discrepancy from bisymmetry.
