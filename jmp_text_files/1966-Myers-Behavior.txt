HEUCKEROTH  University of Massachusetts, Amherst, Massachusetts  Two linear models and a finite Markov model were tested against data obtained from a noncontingent two-choice experiment with  differential payoff matrices. The relative merits of the three models were considered as was the problem of parameter  identification, the relationship between the parameters of the model and those of the experimental situation. Identifying the  parameters of each model with the regret associated with trial outcomes resulted in good data fits.  The stochastic model literature includes several investigations of the effects of reward upon choice behavior (e.g., Atkinson,  1961, 1962; Bush and Mosteller, 1955, Ch. 13; Bush and Wilson, 1956; Estes, 1962; Myers and Atkinson, 1964). These papers have  focused either entirely or primarily upon the symmetric payoff case in which all correct predictions result in the same gain,  and all incorrect predictions result in the same loss (or in nonreward). This research might be extended by eliminating the  requirement of symmetry, permitting the magnitude of gains and losses to vary depending upon which correct or incorrect  response occurred. This differential payoff paradigm is considered in the present paper; data obtained under several payoff  conditions provide a test for two linear models and a finite Markov model. The relative merits of these models is only one  issue to be considered; equally important is the problem of parameter identification, the relationship between the parameters  of the model and those of the experimental situation. Before discussing the three models and their fits to data, we will  consider one proposal for identifying the parameters of these models. Several studies (Edwards, 1956; Myers, Suydam, and Gambino, 1965; Schoefer, 1962; Suydam, 1965; Taub and Myers, 1961) suggest  that choice behavior may be a function of the regret matrix. In the ensuing presentation and test of three stochastic models,  we will carry this approach to a logical consequence by assuming that changes in conditioning strength are a function of the  regret experienced by the subject. The weak-strong model assumes the existence of a population of N stimulus  elements, each of which is conditioned to exactly one response on any trial. It is further assumed that a single element is  randomly sampled from the population on each trial, and that the subject's response depends upon the conditioning state of the  sampled element. These assumptions are common to other stimulus sampling models (Atkinson and Estes, 1963); the weak-strong  model differs from its predecessors in the assumption that an element may be either weakly or strongly conditioned to a  response. If the element is either weakly or strongly conditioned to response Ai , that response will be made with probability  one. Transitions of elements among states are assumed to occur according to the following rules:  (a) Stimulus elements that are not sampled on a trial do not change their conditioning state.  (b) If event Ei occurs, then (i) if the sampled element is strongly conditioned to the Ai response, it remains so, and (ii) if  the sampled element is weakly conditioned to the Ai response, there is a probability [, that it becomes strongly conditioned.  (c) If event E; occurs (i :7~j), then (i) if the sampled element is strongly conditioned to Ai, there is a probability Si that  it becomes weakly conditioned to Ai , and (ii) if the sampled element is weakly conditioned to Ai , there is a probability Si  that it becomes weakly conditioned to A; . Although the fits are good, it is conceivable that they might be improved if the restrictions in our estimation procedure were  weakened. One possibility is that N, the number of elements in the weak-strong model, is a function of some aspect of the  payoff matrix. In accord with this assumption, we re-estimated the parameters allowing four different estimates of N, one for  each payoff matrix; N was still assumed to be constant over values of 7r. Since all four estimates of N were close to 2.49,  the estimate in Table 1, there is essentially no improvement in fit to compensate for the loss of three additional degrees of  freedom.  A second assumption which leads to a modification of the estimation procedure is that the effect of regret is a function of  7r. Permitting two sets of parameter estimates, one at each level of 7r, we find that the average absolute deviation has  -decreased  CHOICE AND REWARD 467  slightly for all three models. The D-values are now .032 for the weak-strong model, .018 for the reinforcement-extinction  model, and .025 for the secondary reinforcement model. This improvement in fit may be misleading; since additional parameters  have now been estimated, degrees of freedom have been lost. Using the index suggested earlier, D/df, we obtain .0018, .0025,  and .0013 for the reinforcement-extinction, secondary reinforcement, and weak-strong models, respectively. These index values  do not appear much smaller than those reported earlier when parameters were held constant over 7r. However, the decrease in  our index is 14 % for the reinforcementextinction model and 19 % for the weak-strong model. Viewed in this manner, it appears  that our parameters are somewhat dependent upon the value of 7T, a result which is consistent with previous analyses of other  models (e.g., Estes and Straughan, 1954).  DISCUSSION  Comparison of the models. A major purpose of the present investigation was an evaluation of the relative merits of three  models. On the basis of the present data set, we can only conclude that all three models deserve further consideration. When  adjustments are made for the number of degrees of freedom, the weak-strong model provides a slightly better fit than do the  linear models. However, the differences are too slight to warrant any claims of superiority of the weak-strong model until the  result can be shown to occur consistently in a number of data sets.  A particulary interesting result is the finding that the linear models do yield reasonable fits to data. Mathematical  psychologists have done little with the linear models whose operators depend on both the preceding response and event. To our  knowledge, only the secondary reinforcement model has previously been tested in a probability learning situation (Bush and  Wilson, 1956). Presumably, the lack of mathematical tractability, as evidenced by the impossibility of obtaining closed  expressions for the moments of the response probability distribution, has proven a deterrent to tests of these models. The  results of our investigation, together with those of Bush and Wilson, suggest that the difficulties in testing these linear  models are not insurmountable and that further investigations are warranted.  The regret hypothesis. This research was also designed to provide data on the adequacy of identifying parameters of the model  with regret associated with trial outcomes. We approached this problem by imposing on the parameter estimation process several  restrictions that followed from the values of the regrets. Despite these restrictions, good fits to data were obtained. While  it is true that alternative identifications, leading to other restrictions, might have resulted in equally good, or better,  fits, it is important to note the existence of other data that imply that choice behavior is a function of regret.  468 MYERS, SUYDAM AND HEUCKEROTH  Experiments in which subjects chose between a risky option and a sure thing (e.g., Katz, 1962; Myers and Sadler, 1960; Myers  et al., 1965) have consistently obtained probabilities of risk taking which cannot be accounted for by expected values of  payoffs, but are consistent with the expected regrets encountered. In a recent signaldetection experiment, Taub (1965) exposed  subjects to slides containing both highand low-value targets. Proportion of correct detections clearly varied as a function of  regret, but not as a function of the actual payoff. Other studies of choice behavior reporting results consistent with a  regret formulation have been performed by Edwards (1956), Schoeffler (1962), Suydam (1965), and Taub and Myers (1961). These  data consistently suggest that the subject is influenced by his payoff relative to what he might have won. The results of the  present investigation indicate that this viewpoint can successfully be incorporated into stochastic models for choice under  payoff.  REFERENCES  ANDERSON, N. H.
