JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 28, 467-478 (1984)  A Statistical Approach to Saaty's Scaling Method for Priorities  PIET DE JONG  University of British Columbia  This paper investigates the logarithmic least squares (LLSM) approach to Saaty's (Journal of Mathematical Psychology, 1977, 5,  234-281) scaling method for priorities in hierarchical structures. It is argued that statistical criteria are important in  deciding the scaling method controversy. It is shown that LLSM is statistically optimal under a number of realistic and  practical models. Variances and covariances of parameter estimates are derived. The covariance matrix associated with overall  priority differences is also developed. These results allow for a significance analysis of apparent priority  differences. 984 Academic Press, Inc.  1. INTRODUCTION  Saaty (1977a) introduced a method for deriving "priority weights" associated with a set of mutually exclusive alternatives.  The "priority weights," or simply "priorities," are positive numbers adding to one which reflect the importance of  alternatives, judged in the light of various criteria. The method is an imaginative technique which has found diverse  application in areas such as marketing (Wind and Saaty, 1980), health planning (Lusk, 1979), transport studies (Saaty, 1977b),  and personnel appointment (Lootsma, 1980).  The details of the method are described in Saaty (1980). Since the appearance of Saaty's (1977a) paper, a variety of authors  have considered alternative scaling techniques to the originally proposed eigenvector method. These alternative techniques  include the least squares method (see, e.g., Jensen, 1984), and logarithmic least squares method (LLSM) considered by  Rabinowitz (1976), de Graan (1980), Fichtner (1983), and Williams and Crawford (in press). All these methods yield a rank one  approximation to a paired comparison matrix.  All methods coincide when the comparison matrix is consistent, i.e., of rank one. When there is inconsistency, different  methods generally give rise to different rank one approximations. An important question then becomes: Which scaling method is  best? Recent papers by Jensen (1984) and Saaty and Vargas (1984) have come to grips with this problem by considering the  empirical and mathematical properties of the various methods. The problem of rank reversal is an important issue in deciding on an appropriate scaling method. Jensen (1984) and Saaty and  Vargas (1984) deal with this issue. A different important issue concerns the statistical properties of the various methods.  Statistical properties are important on two accounts. First, they force a consideration of an appropriate statistical model.  In the present context, a statistical description of inconsistent pairwise comparison matrices becomes paramount. Second,  statistical properties permit statistical inference procedures to be applied. This is necessary for a significance analysis of  apparent priority differences.  This paper considers statistical properties associated with LLSM. It is shown that LLSM has statistical optimality properties  under specific models for pairwise comparison. This emphasis on LLSM is not to imply that other methods are not statistically  optimal under an appropriate model. Indeed it is hoped that the current contribution stimulates interest in alternative  statistical formulations.  The specific contributions of this paper are as follows: First, following de Graan (1980) and Williams and Crawford (in  press), it is observed that LLSM is statistically efficient and unbiased under a basic model for comparisons. However, it is  further shown that LLSM retains its optimality under a practically realistic extension of the basic model. The extension  allows for correlation between responses, as is likely in practice. Second, variances and covariances of estimated parameters  are derived for the extended model. Third, the correlation across different comparison matrices (with the same alternatives)  is modelled and it is shown that LLSM retains its optimality. Where there is replication (corresponding to different  individual judges, for example), it is shown that, under certain simplifying assumptions, the average of individually  calculated LLSM solutions is optimal. The joint covariance matrix of the estimators is derived as well as moment estimators  for disturbance variances. Finally we derive the covariance matrix for LLSM priority estimates when they are derived as  suggested by Saaty, by combining priorities corresponding to different cirteria using the priority vector of the criteria.  2. If there are m criteria then the m eigenvectors are linearly combined. The weights in this linear combination constitute the  eigenvector extracted from the comparison matrix generated from comparison of the criteria. LLSM generates p(l), p(2),...,  P(m), where the argument denotes the criterion. These p(k) vector estimates can be arranged columnwise in an n X m matrix  P = { P(I ), P(2),..., P(m) }. SUMMARY AND CONCLUSIONS  This paper has considered LLSM under a number of models which serve as a statistical description of inconsistency in paired  comparison matrices. It was shown that LLSM estimators, or an average of LLSM estimators, are best linear and unbiased in the  context of these models. Variance-covariance matrices associated with the estimators, as well as combined estimators, were  derived. The results enable statistical inference to be performed for paired comparison data. The results are also of some  relevance to the eigenvalue method of extracting priorities, for it may be shown that the LLSM solutions are a first-order  approximation to these eigenvector solutions.
