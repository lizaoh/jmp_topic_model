MEYER  Department of Psychology, University of Pittsburgh, Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania 15213  A Bayesian procedure was developed to estimate separately the probability of sufficient storage, B, , and the probability of  successful retrieval, B,. , on an individual subject basis. The method involves a probabilistic analysis of the subject's  performance on a task that randomly intermixes recall with recognition trials. Two experiments using the Brown-Peterson  paradigm were performed to demonstrate and validate the estimation procedure. The experiments showed that both 8, and 8,  decrease as a function of increasing retention interval. The results of both experiments suggest the necessity of a two-factor  theory for short-term memory.  A fundamental methodological deficiency exists in the empirical study of memory in that recall failures cannot be analyzed to  determine what fraction of the forgotten information is due to storage failures and what fraction is due to retrieval  difficulties. If the relative contributions of storage and retrieval could be quantitatively estimated in a wide class of  experimental tasks and conditions, then clearly those estimates would provide useful information in the development of  theories of the storage and retrieval mechanisms in memory.  The problem of separating storage and retrieval forgetting components is particularly acute in the area of short-term memory  since current theories conflict in regard to the relative importance of storage and retrieval. On the storage side, a number  of theories employ concepts like a short-term store or a temporary trace (e.g., Waugh & Norman, 1965; Atkinson & Shiffrin,  1968; Morton, 1970; Norman & Rumelhart, 1970; Reitman, 1970; Wickelgren, 1973). These theories generally hold that the  information is lost in nonrehearsing interpolated interval because of a loss of information from the short-term store and  because of a failure to transfer the information to a permanent long-term  Copyright 1976 by Academic Press, Inc. However, on the retrieval side, a number of theorists argue that short-term memory effects are merely a result of  retroactive and proactive interference between the trace of the target item and the traces of other recently presented  material (e.g., Keppel & Underwood, 1962; Melton, 1963). In fact, if storage and retrieval factors cannot be separated then  there is no logical reason to exclude the concept of a separate short-term retrieval system rather than a short-term store.  The primary purpose of the present article is to outline and demonstrate a statistical procedure to separate storage and  retrieval components. Such a procedure would be an important methodological tool and would lead the way to empirical answers  for questions about the details of memory processing.  Experimental paradigms. With slight modification, several standard memory tasks like that of the Brown-Peterson, serial probe  and the paired-associate can be used to separate storage and retrieval components. In general the modification involves  supplementing the usual recall trials with forced-choice recognition trials. In addition, on each recognition trial it is  necessary to obtain a three point confidence judgement (with a 3 rating denoting certain knowledge and highest confidence and  a I rating denoting no knowledge and lowest confidence).  On any trial the subject will receive either a recall or a recognition test, but care should be taken to ensure that the  subjects have no clue prior to the testing time as to the type of test procedure that will be used on that trial. The reason  for this precaution is due to a necessary assumption in the separation procedure that the storage processes are the same on  both recall and recognition test trials. This assumption, however, would not be reasonable if the subjects knew prior to the  testing time how they were to be tested on that trial. Consequently, recall and recognition test trials must be randomized  throughout the experimental session so as to ensure the homogeneity of storage processes on recall and recognition test trials.  Given the type of experimental paradigm mentioned above then the separation procedure involves constructing a probabilistic  task analysis of the recall, old recognition, and distractor recognition trials. Storage and retrieval factors appear as  probabilities in the task analysis along with several other probabilities related to the task. Storage and retrieval will have  been separated when each parameter in the task analysis model has been estimated.  Definition of storage and retrieval. While it seems reasonable that both storage and retrieval can continuously vary in  strength or effectiveness it is convenient to dichotomize both factors. Storage will be dichotomized as either sufficient or  insufficient. Fractional storage or no information storage will be grouped and characterized as insufficient since in both  cases the subjects would not have recalled the entire target item. Thus on any trial the subject either has sufficient or  insufficient storage concerning the target information. Now across many trials the proportion of times that the subject  sufficiently stores the target information will be defined as 9s , the probability  BAYESIAN PROCEDURE 271  of storage. Notice that the question of what is stored is not being addressed here but just the question: "How frequently was  something sufficiently stored ?"  Given that sufficient storage has occurred on a trial then retrieval can be dichotomized into successful retrieval of all of  the stored information and unsuccessful or incomplete retrieval. Across the trials where there is sufficient storage the  proportion of times that the subject successfully retrieves the information will be defined as B, , the probability of  retrieval. EXPERIMENT 1  The purpose of the first experiment is to study the storage and retrieval changes that occur in the Brown-Peterson paradigm.  As recall decreases with increasing retention interval as demonstrated by many studies, e.g., Peterson and Peterson (1959),  Murdock (1961), Keppel and Underwood (1962), then how much of the decrement is due to storage changes and how much is due to  retrieval changes?  In addition, two other experimental variables related to the interpolated activity in the Brown-Peterson task were  investigated. The first variable is the amount of information processing during the interpolated interval and it has been  found by Posner and Rossman (1965) and Kroll and Kellicutt (1972) to affect performance. More specifically, in an interpolated  task of digit shadowing, the subject reduces zero bits of information, but in a counting backwards task the subject will  reduce several  BAYESIAN PROCEDURE 283  bits of information. The Posner and Kroll studies have found that greater information processing in the interpolated interval  reduces recall, but the question still remains as to the mechanism of the effect in terms of storage and retrieval factors.  The other experimental variable that will be investigated in this study is the degree of similarity between the target  material and the interpolated material. Bruning and Schappe (1965), Kothurkar (1968), Levy and Jowaisas (1971), Loess and  McBurney (1965), and Spring (1968) have found that increased similarity in the Brown-Peterson paradigm decreases retention. It  is still, however, an unanswered question as to whether the similarity factor affects storage or retrieval or both.  Method  Subjects. Thirty-six subjects were randomly assigned to one of three experimental conditions of equal size. All subjects were  students at the University of Pittsburgh and were tested individually. The subjects were paid for their participation in the  experiment. The money paid to each subject was based on the rate of one cent for each correct response made by the subject  with the maximum possible payment being $9.60.  Materials and apparatus. The target items and recognition distractors were from a pool of 1280 consonant trigrams with Witmer  association value from 21 to 45%. Other materials were four-digit numbers and letter sequences with no number or letter  sequence being used more than once. All materials were presented auditorially by means of a two-channel Concord tape recorder.  The subjects listened to recorded tapes by means of Pioneer SE-30 headphones. The target items and test cues were recorded on  channel one of a tape in a female voice paced by a metronome clicking at the rate of two clicks per second. Also recorded on  that channel were low and high pitch sounds produced by a xylophone and used as ready and rating response signals to the  subject. The low pitch sound was a low C note and the high pitch sound was a high E note. On the second channel of the tape  was recorded the interpolated material in a male voice paced by the recorded metronome clicking from the first channel.  Another earphone allowed the experimenter also to listen to the first channel of the tape while the experiment was in progress.  Design. The three experimental conditions differed in the interpolated task. Group CB subjects repeated a four-digit number  that was presented auditorially one digit at-a-time and then counted backwards by three from the four-digit number. Group DS  subjects verbally shadowed or repeated digits presented auditorially one-at-a-time throughout the interpolated interval. Group  LS subjects verbally shadowed letters presented auditorially one-at-a-time throughout the interpolated interval. All subjects  were run in a basic Brown-Peterson paradigm and were tested at 0, 4, 8, or 12 sec after the off-set of the target material. At  each retention interval, all  284 CHECHILE AND MEYER  subjects received 240 trials. The 240 trials were divided into 80 recall trials, 80 recognition trials on the target items,  and 80 recognition trials on the distractor items. Sixteen of the 80 distractor items shared one letter with the target  whereas the remainder of the distractor items had no letters in common with the target item. Each subject received 240  Brown-Peterson trials per day for 4 days. Consequently, each subject received a total of 960 trials. The 240 trials per day  were divided into 60 trials at each retention interval. Each retention interval was run as a block. The order of the four  retention interval blocks was counter-balanced across the 4 days by a random 4 x 4 Latin square. Within each block the type of  test trial was randomized under the condition that a of the trials were recall, I of the trials were recognition on the target  item, and 3 of the trials were recognition on a distractor.  Procedure. Each subject was individually tested for about 1 h per day for 4 days. At the beginning of each daily test session,  the subject listened to recorded instructions. Following the instructions, the subjects were given three practice trials on  each day, followed by the test session. Each trial of the test sessions began with a low-pitch recorded sound which served as  a ready signal. One second later, the letters of the trigram were read in the female voice for 1.5 sec and followed  immediately by the interpolated material in the male voice. Following the interpolated activity of either 0, 4, 8, or 12 sec,  the subjects were given the output cues for 1.5 sec in a female voice. The subjects were allowed 2 sec to respond and a  high-pitch recorded sound marked the end of the 2 sec period. The subjects were then given 2 sec to rate their recognition  responses and finally given a 1 sec rest interval before the next trial. On the completion of the entire experiment the  subjects were paid for their participation.  Results  Since model 4 is more general, it was first used for parameter estimates. Discussion  Validity of estimates. There are several indications that the parameter estimates behave in a reasonable fashion. For example,  given that there was an overall increase in forgetting as a function of retention interval, it would have been an unreasonable  result if the storage and retrieval components of forgetting behaved erratically or with decreasing functions. However, both  the Ps and P,. components behave in a credible fashion in that neither factor has erratic nor decreasing retention functions.  The 0, and 09- estimates also seem reasonable for guessing parameters. For example, both B9 and 0g- are greater than a 50%  "chance" level. According to the model, these guessing parameters are a mixture of blind guesses based on no information and  educated guesses based on fractional information, so it is to be expected, considering the educated guesses, that the  parameters are higher than "chance." Furthermore, 0gis greater than 0, . This outcome is also reasonable, considering the  times when there is fractional storage. It should be easier to reject a distractor then to accept an old item since only one  mismatch is sufficient to reject a distractor but one match is not necessarily sufficient to accept an old item. Also if the  subject misperceived the target item then it is likely for a subject to guess incorrectly on old recognition but to guess  correctly on distractor recognition. Lastly, the retention functions of the parameters seem reasonable in that they are  invariant for the DS and CB conditions and decreasing in the LS condition. The decrease in the LS could be explained by a  decrease in the proportion of "educated" guesses with increasing retention interval.  Another indication of the validity of the parameter estimates comes from the comparison between the two types of distractor  items. At each retention interval dissimilar distractors had a lower number ofalse alarms attesting to the fact that  distractor similarity did change recognition behavior. However, the only parameter that changed as a result of distractor  similarity was the distractor recognition guessing parameter, 09. Indeed, distractor similarity should not and did not  affect 03 and 0,. since the type of false test item ought not affect storage and retrieval processes. If less attention  is required in the CB condition with the highly practiced subjects then it is likely that the subjects were rehearsing more in  the CB condition. The major difference between the conditions in  regard to storage seems to be that the CB group does not show storage losses over the first 4 sec. In fact the storage losses  after the 4 sec retention interval increase at the same rate for both conditions. A relatively high rehearsal rate in the  earlier part of the interpolated task could explain the 4 sec delay before storage losses develop. These hypothesized  rehearsals could have also additionally "strengthened" the memory trace and explained why retrieval is better for the CB group.  In terms of overall forgetting, the major difference between the DS and LS groups is a faster rate of forgetting for the LS  condition. Also the groups did not differ reliably at the longer 12 sec retention interval. Furthermore, retrieval alone seems  to account for the difference in the forgetting rate. These data are thus only weak support for the detrimental effect of  similarity observed in previous studies, e.g., Bruning and Schappe (1965), Kothurkar (1968), Levy and Jowaisas (1971), Loess  and McBurney (1965), and Spring (1968). More pronounced differences due to the similarity variable were found with less  practical subjects and perhaps the highly practiced subjects of the present study overcame the difficulties of letter  shadowing.  EXPERIMENT 2  One purpose of the second experiment is to investigate the same interpolated activities as in the previous study except with  less practiced subjects and for longer retention intervals. Additionally, the separation procedure is more practical if it can  be done in fewer trials. Also in this study another experimental variable is introduced in order to check further on the  validity of the parameter estimates. More specifically, the time allowed for recall is a variable that should have no effect  on storage and should only affect retrieval and thus should provide an additional validity test. Discussion  The important new finding of the second experiment is the invariance of storage with respect to the retrieval time  manipulation. This finding strongly indicates the validity of the parameter estimates. The only important assumption of the  separation procedure is the assumption concerning no retrieval difficulties on old recognition testing when there is  sufficient storage. All other assumptions are relatively minor and inconsequential. However, the consequences of the above  assumption being incorrect are that the storage parameter in the model includes also retrieval processes. If this were so then  the storage parameter, 0, , would have changed as a function of the retrieval time manipulation. However, the Bs parameter was  invariant with respect to retrieval time thus strongly suggesting the validity of the assumption of no retrieval difficulties  in old recognition when there is sufficient storage. Obviously the greater retrieval time resulted in a more extensive memory  search and hence improved the probability of successful retrieval.  The results of the second experiment also showed a more pronounced effect for the similarity and this effect is mainly  attributable to the storage component. Also these less practiced subjects failed to show the superiority for counting  backwards that was found in the first experiment. Thus these data have replicated the similarity effect found by Bruning and  Schappe (1965), Kothurkar (1968), Levy and Jowaisas (1971), Loess and McBurney (1965), and Spring (1968), but these data still  have not replicated the Posner and Rossman (1965) and the Kroll and Kellicutt (1972) finding concerning the information  processing variable. The information processing effect seems thus to be a more subtle effect.  Perhaps the simplest explanation for the effects of interpolated activity is that these tasks may differ in regard to the  attention required by the subject and when less  294 CHECHILE AND MEYER  attention is required then rehearsals can occur resulting in improved storage. If the storage is substantially improved such  as for the CB group in Experiment 1 then the memory trace is stronger and more easily retrieved as well.  GENERAL DISCUSSION  The parameter estimates from both experiments demonstrate that both storage and retrieval failures are involved in short-term  memory. Clearly then no single factor theory of short-term can be correct and presently no good two factor theory exists.  Obviously there are many possible blends of the two factors, e.g., a short- and longterm store combined with interference  notions or possible both a short and long storage and retrieval systems, etc. The purpose of the present paper is not to  explicate a particular two factor short-term memory theory but to show the necessity for such a theory. Had the results of  this study shown one factor involvement in short-term memory then further use of the separation procedure would be  unnecessary. Since, however, both factors are involved in short-term memory, there are many phenomena that should now be  re-examined using the separation procedure so as to isolate the locus of the phenomena in terms of storage and retrieval  processes. After such experiments the direction towards an accurate two-factor theory will be clearer.  REFERENCES  ATKINSON, R., & SHIFFRIN, R. Human memory: A proposed system and its control processes. In K.
