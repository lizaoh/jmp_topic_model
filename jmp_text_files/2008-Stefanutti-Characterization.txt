In knowledge space theory (KST) ( Doignon & Falmagne, 1985; Falmagne, Doignon, Koppen, Villano, & Johannesen, 1990 ) a knowledge structure is a deterministic representation of the implications among the items in a given set  
                         Q

                         K

                         Q 
                      , including  
                         Q 
                       and the empty set, each of which represents a possible knowledge state in a population of students. From a certain perspective it can be thought as a formal representation of the “a-priori” information that a teacher could have about the knowledge state of a student. This prior information makes, in general, more efficient the assessment of the knowledge state of a student, in the sense of saving a lot of questions to the teacher. Concrete procedures for the efficient assessment of knowledge by means of a knowledge structure have been proposed by  Falmagne and Doignon (1988a,b) . The fundamental idea at the core of such procedures is that the (correct or wrong) answers of a student to a subset

Theorem 9  shows that pairwise and global independence are equivalent in a quasi-ordinal knowledge space. However the question remains open of characterizing the family of all those structures for which this theorem holds true. The question is thus: are there any other types of knowledge structures outside the family of the quasi-ordinal ones in which pairwise independence corresponds to global independence? Although interesting, this question will not be pursued in the present paper.

Perhaps an interesting application of the theory developed in this paper (and what actually motivated it) is in connection with probabilistic knowledge structures ( Falmagne & Doignon, 1988a ). A probabilistic knowledge structure (PKS) is a triple  
                         
                            ( 
                            Q 
                            , 
                            K 
                            , 
                            π 
                            ) 
                         
                       where  
                         
                            ( 
                            Q 
                            , 
                            K 
                            ) 
                         
                       is a (deterministic) knowledge structure, and  
                         π 
                         : 
                         K 
                         → 
                         R 
                       is a probability distribution on the knowledge states. Usually a researcher constructs a deterministic knowledge structure (DKS) and validates a probabilistic counterpart of the DKS, i.e. a PKS. Since what is constructed and what is validated are two distinct (even though related) objects, it is desirable to have clearly and easily interpretable relationships between them. One such relationships concerns independence. The concept of  stochastic independence  naturally arises in probabilistic settings. Probabilistic knowledge structures are not an exception. In this connection, for instance,  Doignon and Falmagne (1999)  have introduced the concept of (stochastically)  independent substructures  of a probabilistic knowledge structure. On the other hand the type of independence considered in this paper is of a deterministic (set-theoretical or algebraic) nature. How these two types of independence are related one another? Is there any correspondence between them when moving from a DKS to a PKS? The problem is that of having consistent information about independence when moving from the deterministic to the probabilistic level. This question has been addressed by  Stefanutti (2006) , and the answer is that, in general, there is no correspondence between the two types of independence. This means, for instance, that two subsets of items could be (deterministically) independent in a DKS but not (stochastically) independent in the corresponding PKS. If so, the results of an empirical validation could lead to interpretations that are in contrast with the assumptions (about independence) on which the deterministic model was based. On the other hand there exists a special case of a PKS, called a  logistic knowledge structure  for which, indeed, such a correspondence holds. For the details of this model the reader is referred to  Stefanutti (2006) .

The decomposition algorithm given in Section  4 , and the theory on which it is based, could have applications outside canonical knowledge space theory. For instance it could be used in the analysis of empirical datasets. If the dataset is a Boolean matrix with rows representing objects, cases or individuals, and columns representing features or attributes, one could be interested in finding dependencies among attributes in the dataset. As mentioned in the introduction of this paper, this is a typical application of formal concept analysis (see e.g.  Ganter and Wille (1999a,b) ). Besides this, FCA provides a number of ways of decomposing a concept lattice, or the formal context on which it is based, into simpler parts (see, in this connection  Wille, 1987 ). However, to the best of our knowledge there seems to be no method aimed at a decomposition of a context into independent factors in the sense specified in this paper. Nonetheless it could be of interest to check whether the full set of attributes (or the full set of objects) can be partitioned into a collection of independent subsets.
