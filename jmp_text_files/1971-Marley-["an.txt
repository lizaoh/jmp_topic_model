JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 8, 481-488 (1971)  An Observable Property of a Generalization of Kinchla's Diffusion Model of Perceptual Memory  A. A. J. MARLEY*  McGill University, Montreal, Canada  A model has recently been presented that can be applied to any discrimination task in which an observer has to "remember" the  value of a stimulus observed at one time in order to compare it with a stimulus value observed later in time. Here we present  a slightly more general version of this model, and discuss its implications for performance change as we vary the length of  time the observer has to "remember" the earlier stimulus presentation.  INTRODUCTION  Kinchla and Allan (1969) have presented a model that can be applied to any discrimination task in which an observer has to  "remember" thevalueof a stimulus observed at one time in order to compare it with a stimulus value observed later in time. In  this note, we present a slightly more general version of this model, and demonstrate that if this version is applicable, then  the data obtained from various experiments must be interrelated in a particular way. The particular relations, or "observable  properties", of this model do not hold for similar models. However, these relations are stated in such a way that there are  natural generalizations that might be investigated for other models.  We now turn to a detailed discussion of the relevant experimental design and data. We have a set of stimuli X, each element of  X being of the same (finite) duration.' Each stimulus presentation in the experiments of interest consists of a stimulus v E X  beginning at time r, followed by a stimulus w E X beginning at time r + t, t > 0, this sequence being denoted (v, t, w>. Suppose we carry out a series of such experiments, and we wish to say something about the data. We can consider a particular  model similar to that of, say, Kinchla and Allan (1969), estimate parameters, and measure the goodness of fit of the model. An  alternative approach is to discover a particular prediction or "observable property" of a class of models, this prediction  being stated directly in terms of observable quantities. If this prediction is not upheld, we can reject this class of models;  and if it is upheld, we have some direct evidence in favor of this class of models, more so perhaps than we would have from a  goodness of fit test.  Of course, we might require some kind of statistical test to evaluate whether or not the property holds to a satisfactory  degree, so the distinction we are attempting to make is not based on the role of estimation and statistical procedures per se;  we will return to what the distinction is in the Discussion, after we have presented an example of an observable property. A MULTIVARIATE VERSION OF KINCHLA'S MODEL  Consider an experiment using x, y E X, separated by a time t, and look at the case when z occurs followed by w after time t,  z, w E {x, y}. The basic assumptions of the model are that on repeated presentations of the z, w pair, there arises a  (multivariate) distribution of internal states associated with z (respectively, w), and a (multivariate) distribution of  errors or distortions due to the time delay t. On each trial, the subject "stores" the internal state associated with z on  that trial; this value is distorted over the time t by the addition of the error term on that trial; the final value at the  end of time t is then compared to the input value associated with w on that trial; if the "distance" (defined below) between  these terms is less than some constant a, the subject responds "same", otherwise he responds "different". To  really test the condition, we should study situations in which it is most likely to fail. What are these situations ? To  explore this question, we consider experiments similar to those reported in Kinchla and Allan (1969). In such experiments,  each element of the set X of possible stimulus presentations is a spot of light on a (two-dimensional) plane surface in front  of the subject; each light is of the same size and intensity, the lights differing only in their location on the plane  surface. Each experiment is designed as in the introduction, with two of the lights serving as stimulus presentations. We now  consider which triples of lights and time separations are most likely to give a strong test of Condition 1 with, say, 8 = 0.  To invalidate Condition 1, we must generate points with e > max(a, c), f < min(b, d). By suitable manipulation of payoffs  and/or presentation probabilities, we can probably get one of these pairs of inequalities to hold, say the first. Given this  constraint, when is the second inequality most likely to hold ? Suppose that the mapping from the frontal plane to internal  states is in some sense "natural"; then we assume that in any experiment of the form [v, w : t], the various choice  probabilities depend on the Euclidean distance, d(v, zv), between v, w on the frontal plane, and on some function of t. The  multivariate model introduced above is essentially of this form. So b (respectively d, respectively f ) will be a function of  d(x, y) (respectively d(y, z), respectively d(x, z)). Suppose we fix d(x, y), d(y, z), and ask what arrangement of the points  x, y, z (with these two constraints) gives the smallest value of f Now f is the probability of (erroneously) saying "same" to  the sequence <,x, g(r, s), z . We expect f to decrease as d(x, z) increases, so with d(x, y), d(y, z), fixed, we expect f to  be smallest when x, y, z lie on a straight line with y between x and z; for in this case, d(x, z) = d(x, y) -{- d(y, z), which  is the largest possible value for d(x, z) with the given constraints on d(x, y), d(y, z). Thus, to test Condition 1 with 8 =  0, take three points x, z on a straight line with y between x and z, manipulate the payoffs and/or presentation probabilities  to get e > max(a, c), then examine the relation of f to min(b, d); further consideration might also suggest taking d(x, y) =  d(y, z), and r = s.  The difference between fitting a model, and judging whether an observable property holds, should now be clear. In studying a  model, we do not normally try to find a situation in which the model is most likely to fail, partly because we do not usually  know what this situation will look like. With an observable property, we try to construct such a situation. The above  discussion illustrates that it can be tricky to decide on just the right situation to test a given observable property, and we  will not therefore invest much effort in looking at a particular property unless we feel that it is a useful discriminator  between various models. We do not claim to have shown this for Condition 1; rather it is meant to be illustrative of an  approach.  Although the idea of observable properties is not new, few experiments have been designed with the explicit aim of challenging  such properties.
