The uncertainty paradigm has been offered as a means of empirically verifying the independence of processing of stimulus components in a discrimination task (e.g.,   MacMillan & Creelman, 2005 ;  Magnussen & Greenlee, 1997 ;  Pelli, 1985 ;  Thomas, Magnussen, & Greenlee, 2000 ;  Thomas & Olzak, 1996 ;  Wickens, 2002 ). The canonical structure of the task, as it pertains to multi-component independence, uses a two-component stimulus, such as two superimposed sine-wave gratings of differing orientations ( Thomas & Olzak, 1996 ) or an oval of fixed height and luminance with the “components” of color (hue) and shape (width,  Weerda, Vallines, Thomas, Rutschmann, & Greenlee, 2006 ).  Fig. 1 
                    diagrams a perceptual representation of the task as it is studied here. Each component can differ from an implicit ( Morgan, Watamaniuk, & McKee, 2000 ) standard along a dimension of interest such as spatial frequency in the case of the gratings, or in color or shape in the case of ovals either by an increment (relative to the standard) or a decrement. At least two conditions are needed. In the  certainty  condition, the observer knows in advance which of the two stimulus components will contain the change. In the  uncertainty  condition, the observer does not know which stimulus component will contain the difference from standard and must monitor information from both components. The observer’s task is to indicate, when given a single stimulus on a trial, whether there was an increment in a component or a decrement. It has been asserted that, if the two components are processed by separate and statistically independent mechanisms, then performance must drop in the  uncertainty  condition relative to  certainty  (e.g.,   Thomas & Olzak, 1996 ) due to the combining of sources of noise. This is generally true; the specific amount that can be expected, however, depends crucially on the decision process the observer uses and the variance properties of the perceptual distributions of the stimulus components as this paper will illustrate. There has been some confusion regarding the exact amount of performance decrement to expect in this paradigm stemming from an underspecified or inaccurate analysis of the decision process (see e.g., Appendix of  Thomas & Olzak, 1996 ). As has been observed by Ashby and his colleagues ( Ashby & Gott, 1988; Ashby & Perrin, 1988; Ashby & Townsend, 1986; Maddox, 1992; Silbert & Thomas, 2013; Thomas, 1995, 1996, 1999, 2003 ), a complete understanding of perceptual mechanisms requires a full specification of the decision process the observer may use in the given task.

This model assumes that separate decisions are made for each component regarding whether an increment or decrement has occurred and that these separate decisions are combined using a logical connective such as “OR” or “AND” in the  uncertainty  condition. 
                            2 
                         
                         
                            2 
                            This decision strategy has also been referred to as “independent” decisions in the literature (e.g.,   Ashby & Gott, 1988 ). We avoid this terminology so as not to confuse the concept of a separate decisions mechanism with statistical independence of the component percepts. In later publications, Ashby and his colleagues refer to this decision strategy as decisional separability (e.g.,   Ashby & Lee, 1991 ;  Ashby & Maddox, 1992 ;  Kadlec & Townsend, 1992a,b ;  Maddox & Ashby, 1993 ;  Thomas, 1995, 2001 ). 
                          Specifically, there is a criterion on each perceptual dimension that is used to determine if the percept suggests that the corresponding component has experienced an increment or not. If the participant only requires one of the components to register an increment, thus using an “OR” connective, we refer to this as a  liberal DS  strategy. If he or she demands that both percepts exceed their respective criteria, thus using an “AND” connective, we refer to this as a  conservative DS  strategy. 
                            3 
                         
                         
                            3 
                            One might object to the explicit consideration of both components in the decision rule given that the design yields stimuli which depart from the standard value only along one dimension. However, in the uncertainty condition, by design, the observer does not know which component will contain the change and thus, if using a DS strategy, must maintain in memory two decision criteria that would come into play on each trial. Because of the stochastic nature of the percept, it is conceivable that extreme values are sampled on both components even though only one of them actually contained the change. One could argue that, given the stimulus configuration, the liberal strategy is more consistent with the observer’s goals. Which DS strategy is actually used would be an empirical question. We consider both possibilities so as not to rule out plausible alternatives a priori.

A significant question for researchers of face perception is to what extent does processing of one facial attribute influence the processing of another (cf.  Maurer, Le Grand, & Mondloch, 2002 ;  Tanaka & Gordon, 2011 ;  Thomas, 2001 ;  Wenger & Townsend, 2002  for a recent review). There is a very large literature on this question and we do not claim to present a definitive answer here; however, we illustrate the utility of the uncertainty paradigm as another potential methodology to aid in the study of perceptual interactions by demonstrating that some of the fundamental observations found in face processing appear in this paradigm as well. One such finding, due to  Thomas (2001)  using the identification task and a Garner speeded classification task, was the appearance of a perceptual (negative) correlation between eye separation and nose length using semi-schematic faces. This is a within stimulus perceptual interaction ( Ashby & Townsend, 1986 ) that describes how the perceived value of one feature may vary as a function of the perceived value of another across multiple presentations of the same face. The perceived value (that is, perceptually sampled value within multiple presentations of the same face) of one feature varied in a negatively correlated way with the perceptually sampled value of the other—a lack of perceptual independence. In addition, she found no interaction between these features across physical levels; that is, when the actual physical stimulus levels changed, a finding of perceptual separability. Physical variation of nose length did not alter either the perceptual identification of eye separation nor its speeded classification when the observer was instructed to selectively attend to eye separation (and vice versa). The absence of featural interactions across levels of a physical attribute has been observed in several similar paradigms (e.g.,   Wenger & Ingvalson, 2002 ). However, in the  Thomas (2001)  study, response times to identify one of these features was affected by the absence versus presence of the other; that is, there was a lack of existence separability. It mattered whether the face had eyes on it for the identification of nose length but not what the eye separation actually was—at least in the marginal (across stimulus) case. The conjunction of the findings of within stimulus perceptual correlation and across stimulus invariance or separability makes these facial features an ideal case with which to test the uncertainty predictions derived above. The full uncertainty paradigm included four different conditions manifest as trial blocks carried out on separate days: certainty, control, uncertainty, and finally a (3 × 3) full factorial design. In the certainty, control, and uncertainty conditions, participants were to make a decision regarding whether (a) eye separation or (b) nose width differed from a standard (neutral ‘0’) value. That is, they had to decide whether a feature showed evidence for either an  increment  (“yes” response) or a  decrement  (“no” response) from the standard. To reiterate, in the certainty condition and in the control condition, the observer knows which component (the eyes or the nose) will contain the change. However, in the uncertainty condition, which component differs from the standard on each trial is unknown.

Each trial in these conditions followed the same general sequence. Each trial would begin with a black dot appearing on a gray background, prompting the participant to initiate a trial by pressing either button on the mouse pad in front of them. Next, a fixation cross (+) appeared in the center of the computer screen (500 ms) followed by one of the faces randomly drawn from that block’s relevant stimulus set for a duration of 750 ms. Participants were instructed to make a “yes” (right click) response by clicking the right mouse button with their right index finger if the feature in question constituted an increment, and a “no” response (left click) if the feature indicated a decrement. Feedback was provided after each response and trials timed out after 3000 ms. Observers were provided with 20 practice trials not counted in the subsequent data analysis at the beginning of each block in order to orient them to the purpose of the task.

The certainty condition consisted of two (sub) blocks presented on the same day in one experimental session (approximately 45–60 min). This condition was referred to as the “certainty” condition because one dimension (i.e., the nose size in the example above) was fixed and the participants needed to only focus on the eyes. Thus, one type of trials included a block in which the nose size was fixed to the standard ‘0’ value, and observers were required to make a “yes” response if the eye separation was incremented and a “no” response otherwise (faces 4 and 6 in  Fig. 5 ). In the other block, eye separation was fixed to the standard value, and this time observers were required to make a “yes” response to the incremented or large nose width (faces 2 and 8). Both the certainty conditions consisted of 200 total trials: 100 in which the increment (on eye separation or nose width) was present, and another 100 in which it was absent (i.e., a decrement relative to the 0 value). One observer received the certainty task on the eyes first, while the other received the certainty on the nose condition first. The same two observers participated in controlled versions of the two certainty conditions on a separate day. The controlled certainty conditions were exactly the same in terms of directions and number of trials, except that in the control blocks, the feature not attended to (i.e., the one that was set to a neutral value before) was blurred out using the stamp tool in Adobe Photoshop. The observers participated in the control conditions subsequent to participation in the aforementioned certainty conditions.

Next, the uncertainty condition required the participant to make a “yes” response if either (a) the eye separation (i.e., an increment on the  
                            x 
                         -dimension) showed evidence for an increment beyond the 0 value, or the nose size was “large” (i.e., an increment on the  
                            y

The uncertainty block consisted of 400 total trials: 100 which showed an increment on eye separation (neutral nose width), another 100 showing an increment in nose width (neutral eye separation) (i.e., “yes” responses), 100 trials with a neutral or standard nose width and decrement on eye separation, and finally 100 trials with neutral eye separation and a decrement on nose width (i.e., “no” responses).

The 3 × 3 full factorial identification design was carried out on a separate day in order to estimate the perceptual representation by fitting models based upon the Gaussian General Recognition Theory. This process is described briefly in the results section. The stimuli consisted of each of the 9 possible stimulus configurations ( Fig. 5 , each of the 3 levels of eye separation: narrow, neutral, wide; paired with each of the 3 levels of nose width: small, neutral, wide). Participants were presented with 720 total randomized experimental trials, and received exactly 80 trials from each of the 9 stimulus types. Similar to the conditions described previously, each trial began with a black dot appearing on a gray background, prompting the participant to initiate a trial by pressing any numbered key on the keyboard. Next, a fixation cross (+) appeared in the center of the computer monitor (500 ms) followed by one of the nine possible faces from the stimulus set for a duration of 1000 ms. This time, participants were required to respond by pressing one of the 9 numbered buttons on the keyboard in front of them that corresponded to the face displayed on the screen. The keys were laid out in the following way: number 1 represented the lowest value for nose width and eye separation, 2 represented neutral eye separation and the lowest value for nose width, and 3 represented the highest value for eye separation and the lowest value for nose width. The next row (keys 4, 5, and 6) had the same ascending values for eye separation, although the nose width was neutral in each case. Finally, the top row (keys 7, 8, and 9) included the face stimuli with the largest or “incremented” nose width (e.g., face ‘9’ had the widest eye separation and largest nose width). This procedure encourages the observer to use independent decisions (decisional separability) for this task (Olzak and Wickens, 1992), an assumption that is difficult to separately test in identification paradigms ( Silbert & Thomas, 2013; Thomas & Silbert, 2014; Wickens, 1992 ). Trials timed out after 5000 ms post stimulus offset. Observers were provided with one practice block (720 trials total; 80 from each stimuli) not included in the data analysis. This was done in order to maximize task familiarity and to help ensure response stability.

Our stimuli had face outlines always present but the value of the irrelevant feature in the control condition was not discernable, having been blurred. The enhancement of feature memorability in the classic studies may rely solely on an overall face context rather than specific values for featural attributes. Details of how this logic works can be found in  Ashby and Lee (1991) ,  Silbert, Townsend, and Lentz (2009) ,  Thomas (2001, 2002)  among others. These models will assume that percepts are bivariate normal (Gaussian) in distribution. For the identification task, we also assume that observers partition the two dimensional space into 9 response regions using decision criteria parallel to the perceptual axes—the assumption of decisional separability as it manifests in an identification task. Fitting a Gaussian GRT model to a 9 by 9 confusion matrix entails the estimation of several parameters for each stimulus (a mean and variance on dimension 1, a mean and variance on dimension 2, and a correlation parameter) and two decision criteria for each dimension. To be identifiable, one needs to fix the scale on each dimension by fixing the variance parameters of one of the stimuli to be 1. Also, without loss of generality, one can locate, say, the face with the lowest value on both attributes, at the origin. Models differ with respect to constraints placed on parameter relationships, such as if all variances are set to 1 or if perceptual separability holds, etc. Perceptual separability, in this framework, is met if and only if the marginal density functions for one component are invariant across physical levels of the other component (see  Ashby & Townsend, 1986 ), which, given the assumption of normality entails only mean and variance parameters. More ‘general’ models can be formed by relaxing constraints (Thomas, 2002). If one model can be obtained from another by relaxing a parameter assumption, we say that the latter model is nested within the former, more general model. For example, one model may assume across stimulus perceptual separability but allows all stimulus correlations to be different from each other while another may assume perceptual separability as well but fixes the correlations to be equal to each other (though they can be different from 0). If one uses maximum likelihood methods ( Myung, 2003 ) to estimate parameters, this nesting provides for a hypothesis test using a likelihood ratio test statistic,

Signal detection framework versus process model . We have described how to interpret patterns of performance in terms of the language of multidimensional signal detection theory. It is conceivable, and no doubt of central interest, to examine what types of process theories, with more detailed mechanisms, that can be proposed to produce responses consistent with these results. In our view, the language of signal detection offers a framework, analogous to other statistical frameworks, such as ANOVA or SEM, within with the predictions of other models can be understood. Examples of this relationship between process architecture and a resulting detection representation can be found in  Ashby (2000)  and  Thomas (2006)  among others. We know of several well-articulated, neurally inspired, models of visual integration whose predictions are frequently described using the language of detection theory (e.g.,  Graham, 1989 ,  Olzak & Thomas, 1999 ). We offer the present results in that spirit and encourage model builders such as these to draw connections between their theories and the present results.
