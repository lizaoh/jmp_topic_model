Stochastic Token Theory* 

J.-Cl. Falmagne 

University of California, Irvine 

This paper presents a theory encompassing a collection of stochastic models developed earlier and concerning the evolution of subjective preferences under the influence of a stream of elementary events. The theory relies on two basic concepts. (1) The states : Each individual in the population of reference is regarded as evolving in real time through a sequence of internal states. These states can be probed by interviewing the subjects at some arbitrarily chosen times t1 , ..., tn. Each of these interviews yields a response which is a reflection of the current state. (2) The tokens: The individual is subjected to a stochastic stream of unobservable or unrecorded ``particles'' of information, called tokens. Each token has the potential of modifying the current state, and is accordingly formalized by a transformation on the set of states. The core of the theory and the major concern of this paper is the particular semigroup generated by all such transformations. Combinatorial and stochastic axioms are formulated which lead to testable conditions. The theorems of this paper generalize earlier results by the author and others. ] 1997 Academic Press

The results established here generalize those of the papers just cited, and may also have broader application in view of the abstract nature of the theory. 

This theory is organized around two fundamental ideas. 

The states. At any time t, each individual in a relevant population is in a ``state'' belonging to a finite class S. These states are either fully or partly observable. In one example (Section 2), S is the set of linear orders on a finite set A of alternatives, and the researcher may ask the subjects in a sample to provide, at some arbitrarily chosen times t1 , t2 , ..., tn their personal momentary rankings (with possible ties) of the alternatives in A. If the researcher only requires the subjects to give the top k alternatives in their momentary ranking, then the responses are not identified with the states, but only with a feature of them. In other examples, S is the set of semiorders (or partial orders, biorders, interval orders, weak orders) on the set A of alternatives (see Sections 3 and 4). In developing the general theory in Section 1, however, we make no assumptions regarding the nature of the states. Rather, these states are points in a structure which is entirely specified by a particular set of transformations on S (see below). 

The stochastic stream of tokens. The individuals are subjected to a stochastic stream of elementary messages, which we have called ``tokens.'' The potential effect of a single token is to transform the subject's current state into an ``adjacent'' state consistent with the information carried by the token. I do not assume that the tokens can be reliably observed by the researcher. In principle, however, their combined effect can be assessed through a statistical analysis of the subject's responses. (An example of such an analysis, based on empirical data, is given by Regenwetter et al., 1995). Formally, each token is identified with a function mapping the set S of states into itself. In the following, we do not distinguish between a token and its representing function. A key concept arises when the collection T of all tokens generates a particular semigroup of transformations on S in which each token has a unique ``reverse'' (intuitively, another token capable of annulling the effect of a given token). Studying this semigroup is the major concern of this paper. Axioms specify the semigroup and regulate the stochastic occurrence of the tokens. The structure of the semigroup ensures that asymptotic predictions can be obtained in the form of the longterm probabilities of the states. 

The literature on discrete longitudinal data (or ``panel'' data) is large but only peripherally relevant to the focus of this paper which, as mentioned above, is the particular semigroup of transformations on the set of states. Accordingly, we shall not review this literature (see Chamberlain, 1980, and Heckman, 1978, for examples, and e.g. Parzen, 1994, for the general theory). 

Section 1 contains the combinatoric concepts concerning the states and the tokens. Sections 2�4 are devoted to examples in which the states are binary relations formalizing the subjects' stance about the alternatives. The cases of the linear orders, semiorders, and weak orders are discussed in detail. Stochastic assumptions and results are formulated in Section 5. The discussion in Section 6 reviews the principal tenets of the theory and considers possible extensions to other situations.

1.21. Remarks. (a) In the last two theorems, we uncovered the facts that, in the framework of our definitions, the content family associated with the states of a medium was 2-graded, that all the states had content of the same size, and that any state was defined by its content. We naturally think about the reverse construction. Namely, it is - possible to start from a 2 graded family of sets in which all the sets have the same size and construct the medium associated with it. An example is given in the next section. General conditions under which such a construction is feasible can be investigated, but we shall not consider this question here. 

(b) In a medium (S, T), the set T of tokens can be regarded as the set of generators of a semigroup T* of transformations on S, the operation being the function composition. The elements of T* are all the functions generated from T by all the function compositions. In general, the semigroup generated by T need not have a neutral element. Indeed, from the Axioms [M1]�[M4] defining a medium, we cannot derive the existence of a particular message which is ineffective for all states S. (For a counterexample, take a medium with two states and two tokens which are mutual reverses.)

2. THE STATES AS LINEAR ORDERS 

Theorem 1.12 implies that the group of all permutations on a finite set A (the symmetric group) is not a medium. A tempting construction would be to take the set of rankings of A (i.e., strict linear orders on A��transitive, asymmetric, and connected) as the set S of states, and the set of all transpositions of the corresponding symmetric group as the set T of tokens. It is easy to check that the pair (S, T) is a token system. By Theorem 1.12, however, it is not a medium because the transpositions are 1�1 functions. Note that, in this interpretation, a token is its own reverse. For empirical reasons, this case is nevertheless worthy of attention. Suppose, for instance, that subjects have been asked to rank the major candidates in an election. Thus, as above, the states are all the possible rankings of the candidates. The tokens are the pieces of information which might alter an individual's ranking in some fashion. The occurrence of a particular token { might induce a subject to 

believe that ``Candidate A is better than Candidate B,'' which would result in altering that subject's current ranking R accordingly. One would expect the reverse of that particular token { to convey the message that ``Candidate B is better than Candidate A'' (or possibly ``Candidate A is not better than Candidate B'') which would have the current ranking R$ change back to the original ranking R. More generally, a token and its reverse should normally be identified by different functions on the set of states. Each token { in the set T of all tokens is identified with a transformation S [ S{ on the set of states. repeatedly probing the state at selected times. The evolution of the states is axiomatized as a stochastic process. The focus of the paper is the semigroup of transformations generated by the tokens through function composition. These transformations are called messages. In the general case, no assumptions are made concerning the internal structure of the states. Rather, four axioms are postulated on the pair (S, T), which is then called a medium. A major consequence of these axioms is that any two straight messages producing the same state V from a given state S have the same content (i.e., contain the same tokens) (Theorem 1.14). This fact justifies the endowment of the state itself with a ``content,'' via the definition: The content S� of a state S is the set of all tokens each of which may occur in any straight message producing S (see Definition 1.15). It then turns out that all the states have contents of the same size (Theorem 1.17). Examining the consequences of the axioms shows that a state behaves as a filled box whose content undergoes recurrent minute alterations due to the occurrence of the tokens. More precisely, the effect of a token {, if any, is to replace {~ by { in the content of the state. 

While the concept of a medium may seem abstract and far remote from applications, it is easily specialized into concrete models, offering useful tools for the analysis of the data. Indeed, this concept forms the core of a stochastic theory for a class of empirical situations where individuals in a sample are asked to indicate their preference concerning a set A of alternatives (say, candidates in a political election) at times t1 , ..., tn , .... For example, all the individuals are asked to order the alternatives at appropriately chosen times. The set S of states is then the set of all possible orders on A. We cannot realistically suppose that we could ever get a complete or even representative record of all the influences that may affect an individual's current order. Nevertheless, we must assume that such influences exist. In the model, they are formalized by a stochastic stream of unobservable tokens. From a pilosophy of science standpoint, the status of the tokens resembles that of the elementary particles of statistical mechanics in that their statistical effects can be computed using the equations of the theory, but the computation does not depend on actual recordings of particular tokens. 

In typical applications, the number of distinct tokens (i.e. the number of elements in T) will be considerably smaller - that the number of states, or especially n tuples of states. This feature of the theory is critical because the states are at least partly observable, while the tokens are theoretical constructs to which numerical parameters are attached. Evidently, we want to explain as many independent observable quantities with as few theoretical parameters as it is possible. Consider the case of the semiorders on a set of 4 elements (see Section 3), for instance. There are 183 states, and 2_12=24 tokens. Suppose that the subjects have been interviewed twice, and that Eq. data. These data are made of 183[2] &1=33, 488 independent frequencies to be explained by a couple of dozens token parameters %ij and the density parameter * of the Poisson process. Note that the values of these parameters can be interpreted empirically (cf. Regenwetter et al., 1995). 

The role of the tokens in the theory may also evoke that of the letters of an alphabet in the theory of formal languages. The presentation of the letters successively transform the states of the decoder. The number of such letters in the alphabet is typically very small, while the number of potential states may be much larger. This comparison is not superficial because a token system has some features in common with a semiautomaton in the sense of automata theory (cf. Chomsky, 1963; Ginzburg, 1968). There are important differences, however. In particular, Axioms [M1]�[M4] are central to our developments, but play (as far as I know) little or no role in automata theory. 

Several special cases of the theory have been described in Sections 2�4. The last model, in which the states are all the weak orders on a set of three elements, has in fact been successfully applied by Regenwetter et al. (1995) to the National Election Study panel data from the 1992 Bush� Clinton�Perot campaign (Miller et al., 1993).
