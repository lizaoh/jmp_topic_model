A Statistical Look at Saaty’s Method of Estimating Pairwise Preferences Expressed on a Ratio Scale 

CHRISTIAN GENEST AND LOUIS-PAUL RIVEST 

Université Laval, Québec 

This article is concerned with the problem of estimating n 22 object or attribute weights from a set of n(n ~—1)/2 pairwise preferences expressed on a ratio scale. Saaty’s classical solution to this problem (Saaty, 1977, Journal of Mathematical Psychology, 15, 234-281) is to use as an estimate the normalized positive eigenvector corresponding to the Perron eigenvalue of the binary comparison matrix. An alternative procedure, rooted in the statistical theory of linear models, involves minimizing the total sum of square deviations measured on the logarithmic scale (de Jong, 1984, Journal of Mathematical Psychology, 28, 467-478; Crawford and Williams, 1985, Journal of Mathematical Psychology, 29, 387-405). In recent years, extensive simulation studies have revealed that the two methods have a tendency to produce similar estimates. This observation is theoretically strengthened here. In addition, a statistically valid interpretation is provided for Saaty’s eigenvalue-based index of cardinal consistency in a set of responses. This makes it possible to derive an approximate distribution for this quantity under reasonable assumptions and ultimately feads to a critical examination of the assumptions underlying the so-called 10% cut-off rule recommended by Saaty for identifying response matrices that exhibit too large a degree of cardinal inconsistency. © 1994 Academic Press, Inc.

Though there is a wide consensus in the literature that the eigenvector method and the logarithmic least squares solution are the most performant estimation procedures currently available, these approaches are obviously not the only ones that could be used for scaling ratio judgments. A number of other possibilities, mostly based on the minimization of specific loss functions, are surveyed by Blankmeyer (1987). It is also clear at the outset that the search for a universally superior estimation method is futile: the work of Saaty and Vargas (1984a, 1984b) and others has established that each method is best according to some criterion, usually one it explicitly seeks to optimize. 

By statistical standards, however, it would seem difficult to lend much credence to an ad hoc procedure such as Saaty’s eigenvector method of scaling ratio judgments. Because it ignores completely the stochastic structure of the data, it would not be expected to yield consistently reliable estimates. 6. SAATY’S TEST OF CARDINAL CONSISTENCY REEXAMINED 

Traditionally, theoreticians and practitioners of the analytic hierarchy process have paid more attention to the problem of detecting unacceptable levels of inconsistency in a set of pairwise comparisons than to the question of testing for the existence of genuine differential preferences in the agent’s judgments, as discussed previously. This concern can be traced back all the way to Saaty’s original article, where the author speaks of the necessity (Saaty, 1977, p. 238) of developping a statistic for testing the hypothesis Hy: »=0, that some inconsistency is present in a respondent’s numerical expression of preference judgments. Interpreted in the narrow sense, this would amount to testing for ¢? =0 in terms of linear model (3), a hypothesis most likely to be rejected in practice. However, the spirit of Saaty’s suggestion was to ensure, through some objective criterion, that a collection of pairwise comparisons was not so incoherent as to forbid any attempt to analyze the collected data. The 10% cut-off rule that he suggested for rejecting response matrices that exhibit too large a degree of cardinal inconsistency thus would seem to be more akin to a test of the unilateral hypothesis Hy: ¢? <o?2, that the background variability or “noise” in the responses does not exceed some predetermined threshold value ¢Z. It will now be explained why this correspondence is justified, and how this can help in an understanding of the mechanisms that are at work in Saaty’s procedure. 

In his original articie, Saaty (1977) actually entertained two methods of testing for an excessive degree of inconsistency in a collection of paired comparisons. His first proposal, referred to as a “qualitative test of consistency,” was based on a statistical analogy with the x distribution. It involved declaring a set of responses inconsistent when the observed value of 4 exceeds 2. However, this suggestion does not appear to have ever been followed up or theoretically pursued in the literature. The second proposal, later termed the 10% cut-off rule, was to reject as too inconsistent a response matrix whose corresponding value of ~ would be larger than 10% of the so-called random index yo, defined as the average value of Saaty’s eigenvalue-based consistency measure for matrices of a given size, n, whose entries would be chosen at random within an admissible range. Although Saaty’s 10% cut-off rule appears reasonable at first sight and has been used successfully in hundreds of applications to date, its somewhat arbitrary character has been underscored by many authors in the literature.
