JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 31, 192-202 (1987)  A Conjoint Grassmann Structure for Testing the Additivity of Binocular Color Mixtures  HANS IRTEL  Universitdt Regensburg  A measurement structure for binocular color mixtures is developed, based on the principles that explain monocular matches of  additive mixtures of color stimuli. Binocular color mixtures are created by presenting different stimuli to two eyes, such  that they fuse and result in a single impression of color. The measurement structure makes it possible to compare binocular  color mixtures with physical mixtures of color stimuli. The two types of mixture are characterized as compatible, if the  binocular mixture creates a color, which is identical to the vectorial sum of the colors of the respective monocular  components. The axioms of the measurement structure are experimental questions, suitable for testing the additivity of  binocular color mixtures. 1987 Academic Press, Inc.  Measurement theory may be considered as a valuable tool for the conceptualization of large bodies of data. In this case the  axioms of a measurement structure are empirical statements that embody the results of numerous possible or actually conducted  experiments. A nice example is Krantz's (1975a) definition of the Grassmann structure. Its basic principles already had been  formulated by Grassmann (1853) and numerous experiments have supported their validity thereafter (Wyszecki & Stiles, 1982).  Krantz's axiomatization, however, states the empirical assumptions that underly the psychological concept of color space much  more precisely than Grassmann did. It also separates unnecessary assumptionslike Grassmann's fourth law on brightness  additivity-from those assumptions that really are relevant for the concept of color space. In addition, the Grassmann  structure provides a basic language for the discussion of further concepts of color theory, as in Krantz's (1975b) paper on  opponent colors theory.  The purpose of this paper is to show that measurement theory not only may be used to conceptualize well-known experiments, but  also may be used to find the best experiments that have to be done in order to answer some scientific question. This will be  demonstrated with a well-known problem in color theory: binocular color mixtures. Suppose we present to a subject two  independent stimulus situations, one for each eye, such that both fuse and create a single image. If the two components of  such a dichoptic stimulus differ in color, then the fused color will generally be different from both stimulus colors as they  appear under monocular observation. This type of color mixture has been called binocular color 192  0022-2496/87 $3.00  Copyright 1987 by Academic Press, Inc.  All rights of reproduction in any form reserved. Whereas Helmhotz (1910) doubted that binocular color mixture is possible at all, Hering (1920) was able to show that  homogeneous mixtures may occur if the components of a dichoptic stimulus have identical contours but differ in color only. In  order to get stable fusion even with short presentation times, however, the color difference of the dichoptic components  should not exceed a certain limit (Ikeda & Nakashima, 1980). A first review on binocular color mixture is given by Dawson  (1917) who cites many references that clearly establish the phenomenon. The main question then was how binocular color  mixtures may differ from the usual physical mixtures of color stimuli. Several authors conducted experiments to answer this  question (Trendelenburg, 1923; Livshitz, 1940; Thomas, Dimmick, & Luria, 1961; de Weert & Levelt, 1976).  None of these papers, however, made quite clear what exactly the notion "difference between binocular color mixture and  physical mixture of color stimuli" should mean. The usual mixture of color stimuli is a purely physical operation on the set  of color stimuli-summation of radiant energy-that creates a new color stimulus. The binocular mixture of colors, however, is a  psychological operation that maps pairs of color stimuli onto a set of sensations: colors. This paper is devoted to precisely  formulating how binocular color mixtures have to be compared with mixtures of color stimuli in order to find a sensible way to  investigate differences between the two. This is done on the basis of Krantz's (1975a) concept of a Grassmann structure. Since  the main point of this paper is to extend the simple Grassmann structure to a conjoint system for binocular matches, the  axioms and the representation theorem for Grassmann structures as they are given by Krantz (1975a) have to be presented. The essential point of a binocular Grassmann structure is the additivity of binocular color mixtures. Within the context of a  binocular Grassmann structure Axiom A4 essentially states that binocular mixtures and physical mixtures commute on the level  of monocular equivalence classes. This is best seen in a symmetric binocular Grassmann structure. If the structure is not  symmetric, however, then the physical mixture and the binocular one will not lead to the same color, even if binocular  mixtures are additive. In this case the two eyes will have different psychophysical functions 0, and 'V2. De Weert and Levelt  (1976) actually did find this for some of their subjects and their model also allows different psychophysical functions for  the two eyes. In this case, however, a failure to observe the binocular match of (x, y) and (x (B y, x p+ y)-as de Weert and  Levelt did-does not mean that binocular mixtures are not additive.  The axiomatic definition of a binocular Grassmann structure shows that Axioms A4 and A5 are the best way to compare binocular  color mixtures and physical mixtures of color stimuli. If they are satisfied the two types of mixtures may be called  compatible. This compatibility can be explained in the following way: Suppose we have a binocular match (a, p)(b, q) and for  some stimuli a' and p', (a') equals 02(p'). Then we must also have a match between (a (D, a', p) and (b, q (B2 p'). So the  color described by 0,(a') or 02(p') may either be added to the left eye using stimulus a' or to the right eye, using stimulus  p'. The result will be the same  198 HANS IRTEL  binocular color in both cases, since binocular mixtures and physical mixtures commute on the level of metameric equivalence  classes.  The binocular Grassmann structure is a form of additive conjoint measurement (Luce & Tukey, 1964), where the psychophysical  functions involved are vectorial. It is easy to see that the main axioms of a unidimensional additive conjoint  structure-independence and double cancellation-are satisfied here. The uniqueness result of Theorem T4 is somewhat stronger  than in unidimensional additive conjoint measurement. Both functions 01 and 0, map into the same vector space V and are unique  up to the same linear transformation T. This is due to the fact that a Grassmann structure has a natural zero point, the null  stimulus, that is mapped onto the zero vector. The binocular Grassmann structure incorporates all constraints of a monocular  one. This is shown by the following theorem. EXPERIMENT  The following experiment presents an empirical test of the most critical condition of a binocular Grassmann structure, Axiom  A4. The subject's task is to create com-  A CONJOINT GRASSMANN STRUCTURE 199  plete binocular color matches. On any trial three stimuli a, p, and q are fixed and the subject has to construct a fourth  stimulus b, such that (a, p) and (b, q) produce the same binocular color. For testing Axiom A4 a dichoptic stimulus (c, w) is  then added to (a, p) and the single stimulus w is added to q, resulting in (a (9 c, p +Q w) and q Q+ w. Now the subject has to  produce a stimulus b*, such that (a @ c, p Q+ w) and (b*, q (D w) give the same binocular color mixture. If Axiom A4 holds  empirically, then b* and b(@ c have to have the same coordinates in a 3-dimensional color space.  Stimuli  The stimuli are displayed on a 19 in color monitor (BARCO CDCT 51/3) using a refresh rate of 50 Hz in noninterlaced video  mode. The red, green, and blue channels of the monitor are controlled by 8-bit digital-to-analog converters connected to a  real-time computer. Each color channel's signal may be varied in 256 steps from zero to maximal intensity for each pixel.  Local variations of the monitor's energy output and nonlinearities in the tube's response function are corrected by software  control of the monitor's input signal. The color coordinates of the stimuli are computed from single color channel luminance  measurements, based on the manufacturer's CIE phosphor coordinates. Luminance is measured at eye position using a photometer  with very precise V(A)-calibration (LMT 1000, Lichtmesstechnik, Berlin). Presenting many different stimuli is greatly  simplified by using a color monitor. In order to present a stimulus of the form a(@ b, it is not necessary to physically  superimpose a and b, but the parameters for a O+ b can be computed numerically from those of a and b and pesented directly.  The stimulus components on the screen are rectangles appearing under a visual angle of 0.61'x0.55' on a black background with  luminance of less than 0.01 cd/m2. The left eye's stimuli are displayed on the left half and the right eye's stimuli on the  right half of the screen. A haploscope is used to create a single virtual image and ensure proper fusion of the two  independent components of each dichoptic stimulus. On each trial four stimulus elements are displayed: [(a, p), (b, q)]. The  components a and b are seen by the left eye only, p and q are seen by the right eye only. The elements of each pair (a, p) and  (b, q) are displayed on corresponding positions in the visual field in order to create homogeneous binocular color mixtures.  The components a and p are displayed above b and q with a 0.55' gap and a thin fixation mark (7.7 cd/mz) between them. Similar  fixation marks are above and below the stimulus fields. In order to prevent adaptation the stimuli are continuously switched  on and off during the experiment. On time is 1 s and off time is 3 s. The fixation marks are always visible. A chin rest is  used to control the subjects's eye position at an effective distance of 110 cm from the display.  The bottom stimulus element of the left eye (b of [(a, p), (b, q)] is variable and under control of the subject. Six switches  are available to the subject to adjust the stimulus color, one for each direction of the red/green, blue/yellow, and the  brightness component. The subject's task is to adjust the stimulus b such that (a, p)  200 HANS IRTEL  and (b, q) appear to be equal in color. A seventh switch is used to indicate final color equivalence of the dichoptic stimuli.  Six different stimulus quadruples are used. Since de Weert and Levelt (1974) found a strong nonmonotonicity of binocular  brightness (the so-called Fechner's Paradox) if stimulus intensities for the two eyes are different and one is below 10 cd/mz,  luminance is kept approximately equal for all stimuli (between 17 and 35 cd/mz). The colors of the dichoptic components are  distributed over the whole range of colors available on a television monitor. The initial coordinates of the adjustable  stimulus are determined by a random procedure, not too far from the result to be expected by an averaging behavior, but  clearly different from a binocular match. A more detailed description of the apparatus and a complete list of all stimulus  coordinates may be found in Irtel (1982, 1985).  Procedure and Subjects  The initial stimulus presented on each trial is a dichoptic pair [(a, p), (b, q)] which does not match in color. The subject  adjusts the stimulus component b until b; is found, such that (a, p) and (b;, q) completely match. This is done for each of  the six initial quadruples and repeated between 4 and 10 times, depending on a subject's variance. The arithmetic mean of the  stimulus coordinates in CIE UCS 1960 color coordinates are taken as final values b* for each binocular equivalence. For the  second part of the experiment a dichoptic pair (c, w) is added to both sides of a binocular equivalence and the adjustments  are repeated with (a p+ c, p O+ w) and (d, q p+ w) as initial stimuli. The initial value of d is again selected randomly.  Repeated adjustments and averaging of the results leads to the final value of d*.  Four hours of training are required for each subject. One session starts with 5 min of dark adaptation and lasts approximately  50 min with a 10 min rest. Each subject works between 20 and 25 h. Data from five subjects are reported here. They are  students of psychology between 22 and 28 years old, not wearing glasses, and having normal color vision.  RESULTS  The results of the adjustments are two sets of final stimulus coordinates. Thus binocular colour mixture  is not compatible with physical mixture of color stimuli. This result is in accordance with the results of de Weert and Levelt  (1976), who, however, used much stronger assumptions to test the compatibility hypothesis. Even if Axiom A4 fails, however,  Axiom A5 remains as an interesting empirical condition in its own, since the binocular Grassmann structure may be applied to  unidimensional attributes of colors like brightness or saturation also. In that case, however, the benefit of Axiom A4, to map  the binocular and the physical combination onto the same operation in a linear vector space, is lost. Axiom A5 is a  homogeneity condition and may be added to the axioms of a unidimensional additive conjoint structure in order to restrict the  range of possible psychophysical functions . A useful result for this possibility is given by Luce (1977) who showed that if  A, and A Z are real intervals, 0 = 01 + Y'2 with 01 and 02 sufficiently smooth, then 0, and Y'2 have to be power functions if  Axioms A2 and A5 hold.
