INTRODUCTION  When a subject expresses his preferences among objects x, y, z,..., he might at one time say he prefers x to y and, at another  time, y to x. As pointed out by Roberts (1979), "in many cases, there is a pattern to the inconsistencies and, although a  subject may not be absolutely consistent, he may be probabilistically consistent." The theory of probabilistic consistency  (see, e.g., Luce & Suppes, 1965) proposes several ways for modelizing consistency between preference relations of a subject at  * Address reprints requests and comments to J.-P. One way is to associate, to each ordered pair (x, y), the proportion p(x, y) of times  the subject says he prefers x to y, and to state conditions on the resulting valued relation. A second approach focuses on the  chain (nested family) of relations R;. derived by  xR. y-p(x, y) > %.  In a third and more accurate way, the data are recorded in the (not necessarily nested) family of relations R; obtained at  different times i. Axioms on the R; define consistency in various senses.  Motivated by the first two approaches, Roberts (1971) studied several consistency conditions on chains of semiorders (a type  of relation used in psychology in order to capture the idea of a threshold). While establishing the equivalence of many of  these conditions, he asked for a possible extension of his results to not necessarily nested families of relations (in order  to tackle the third way suggested above). Fishburn (1973) extends Roberts' results to a larger setting, although remaining in  the same context of chains of relations associated to "probabilistic valued relations" (where p(x, y)+p(y, x) = 1, for every  x, y). As an answer to Roberts' question, we consider arbitrary families of relations and prove the equivalence of different  consistency conditions which, in the cases studied by Roberts and Fishburn, reduce to their conditions.  The basic concept in this paper is actually rather old. It first came out of the fundamental idea of linearity (in the sense  of linear order) underlying many measurement models. In the context of "opinion measurements," it was somewhat informally  captured by "Guttman's scales" (Stouffer et al., 1950). Ducamp and Falmagne (1969) more precisely defined "bi-quasi series" as  the relations from one set into another (the subjects and the items, say) inducing a complete preorder on each of the two  sets, that is relations R for which there exist two real valued mappings f and g such that  xRy-f(x)>g(y).  Well-known characterizations of such relations will be recalled, e.g., the existence of a "step-type" tableau for R. In the  purely mathematical context of relations algebra, Riguet (1951) called "Ferrers' relations" the relations R which are given on  a single set and admit some step-type tableau. We have adopted here the term "biorder" introduced by Doignon et al. (1984) to  name such relations.  In fact, many particular cases of biorders have been independently developed. In preference modelling, for example, one knows  the importance of semiorders (Luce, 1956) and of interval orders (Fishburn, 1970) for representing nontransitive indifference.  But let us remark that the notion of interval order and several of its properties were already formulated by Wiener (1914) in  his study of chronological succession of events. Special kinds of biorders also appeared in various theoretical or applied  contexts, e.g., in connection with codings and dimensions of relations, "natural" partial orders, extrapolation theory,  significative differences of sample  BIORDER FAMILIES 437  means, subjective probability,.... [For more details about those independent fields, see Monjardet (1978), and Monjardet and  Jacquet-Lagreze (1978).]  Section II of our paper first lists several definitions of a biorder. Some are useful for applications, as the numerical and  matricial representations, others for their easy testability, as the conditions on the two associated "traces" [in the sense  of Luce (1956, 1958)]. Two particular types of biorders are pointed out: the interval orders and their dual relations, here  called interval duorders. Considering then a family of biorders, we formulate various conditions leading to the new concepts  of left- (or right-)homogeneous families of biorders, of interval orders, and of interval duorders.  A particular case of biorder appears when the two traces coincide. As it is known, such a coherent biorder can be  characterized by numerical, matricial, and relational conditions (see Sect. III). The important notion of a semiorder occurs  when irreflexivity is required, while a reflexive coherent biorder is here called a semiduorder. We introduce in Section III  homogeneous families of coherent biorders, semiorders, and semiduorders.  The two previous parts are concerned with not necessarily nested families of relations. Section IV is devoted to chains of  relations and to valued relations, the structures usually studied in the relevant literature. We first make explicit the  strong connections between the two concepts, showing that each property of one easily transfers to the other. We then present  equivalent conditions on biordered valued relations, that is nested families of biorders. Relying on the results of Sections  II and III, we characterize left- (right-)linear biordered, interval, coherently biordered, and semiordered valued relations.  In the last paragraph ipsodual valued relations provide the natural generalization of probabilistic valued relations.  Section V summarizes connections between our results and the existing work on the subject, and also mentions fields of  applications. Some open problems are presented in the conclusion.  II. APPLICATIONS IN BEHAVIORAL SCIENCES  Practical applications of the mathematical theory developed in this paper can be found essentially in psychological studies on  preference or discrimination and in (individual or collective) decision-making methods.  As explained in our introduction, a valued relation v arises when v(x, y) records the proportion of times a given subject  judges stimulus x to be "greater" (a generic term) than stimulus y. When the binary choices are forced, in the sense that  indifference and incomparability of stimuli are excluded, the resulting valued relation v is ipsodual, since v(x, y) + v(y, x)  = 1. The existing theory proposes several models formalizing the concept of probabilistic consistency. A simple one focuses on  a measure of response strength f (x) such that for all x, y in X:  v(x, Y) > v(Y, X) -f W >f (y).  This "weak utility model" as well as other models of the same kind are clearly reviewed in Roberts' book (1979). VI. CONCLUSIONS  The results we have presented contribute to the theory of linear structures. In fact interval orders, semiorders, and their  generalizations to families all constitute variations on the basic idea of linearity that underlies measurement models: one  tries to represent the data using some embedding in the real line. We have provided  478 DOIGNON ET AL.  various "observable properties" that characterize models admitting such embeddings. Moreover, some of these properties are  very easy to test, and directly lead to evident algorithms. We also hope that our approach using general families of biorders  and the associated traces will shed some light on previous results (mostly confined to particular cases).  Let us now mention problems that we did not touch upon. We completely left aside the symmetric structures, as exemplified by  interval and indifference graphs [for these, see Roberts (1979) and Golumbic (1980)]. Cozzens and Roberts (1982), after  presenting various motivations, study families consisting of two indifference graphs. For the use of symmetric step-type  tableaus, we refer to Mirkin and Rodin (1984).  It is clear that our linear models can only approximate real data. Hence we formulate the following questions: give a precise  definition of a "best approximation," and design an algorithm for computing it. The literature abounds on the case in which  the data are to be approximated by a complete preorder. The more difficult problem of approximation by a biordered valued  relation has been considered only in the particular case of a semiordered valued relation by Jacquet-Lagreze (1978) and  Ouldbraham (1983). In view of explaining nonlinearity, multidimensional models are sometimes introduced. Those relying on  complete orders or biorders as one-dimensional structures have been broadly developed (see Doignon et al., 1984, and the  references there). It remains to extend the dimension concept to families of relations.  Finally, we just mention that valued relations are used in methods where a collective preference must be inferred from  individual preferences [here v(x, y) is the number of individuals preferring x to y]. In this context, biordered valued  relations are valued relations whose trace preorders can have strong properties, and so are candidates for the collective  preference.
