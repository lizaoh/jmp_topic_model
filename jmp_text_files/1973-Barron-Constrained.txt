MACKENZIE  School of Business, University of Kansas, Lawrence, Kansas 66044  A decision model is formulated for individual choices between gambles whose probabilities and payoffs are explicitly stated.  The decision situation is characterized as RN = (X, P, Z, T, a) where X is a payoff matrix, P is the objective probability  distribution over the states of nature, Z is a class of objective functions, T is tolerable regret and 1 - a is the maximum  probability for accepting regrets greater than T. Given RN , the problem of selecting a strategy is the solution of a chance  constrained optimization problem. The solutions for choice situations having two alternatives and n states of nature are  developed in a series of theorems. Some implications for experimentation and possible applications are discussed.  INTRODUCTION  This paper is concerned with a representation of a process by which an individual chooses among alternative gambles whose  probabilities and payoffs are explicitly stated. The individual's decision problem is one having m disjoint alternatives A1 ,  A2 , ..., Am ; n disjoint states of nature, sI , s2 , ..., sn ; an m X n payoff matrix, X, whose entries are the (known)  payoffs to the individual for each of the mn pairs of alternatives and states of nature. An objective probability  distribution, P, over the n states of nature, is defined via a chance device, the properties of which are known to the  individual. Thus, state s; occurs with probability p; and the payoff, if s; occurs when alternative i is selected, is x2; .  The individual is assumed to have an objective function z where z is a member of a class of objective functions, Z. Under  these conditions, the individual's choice problem is a risky situation characterized by the triple, Ro = (X, P, z). The  situation considered in this paper assumes m, the number of alternatives, equals two.  There are three reasons for considering the case m = 2. First, this case is important in its own right. Potential applications  are cited in a later section. Becker and McClintock (1967) and Edwards (1954, 1961) have reviewed this and related choice problems. One problem is the  specification of z, the objective function. Examples of prescriptive models which can be interpreted as objective functions to  be maximized include the expectation models, EV, EU, SEV, SEU (Coombs, Bezembinder, and Goode, 1967). The latter three,  considered as descriptive models, have received a substantial amount of experimental attention. Experiments to determine which  objective function is consistent with subject behavior under specified conditions include those conducted by Coombs and Pruitt  (1960), Coombs et al. (1967), Slovic and Lichtenstein (1968), and Tversky (1967).  There are many studies producing not easily integrable results. In fact, as Becker and McClintock (1967) note, experimenters  tend to report group, rather than individual, probabilities and utilities. Different scientists choose different restrictions  on R. For example, Slovic (1969) considers only gambles having constant EV, while Coombs and Meyer (1969) consider only  gambles with EV = 0. The possibly conflicting findings are probably reasonable given different characterizations of the risk  situation, Ro , and different experimental procedures (e.g., some studies are with actual bets where a subject can win or lose  money and some are with "booklet" bets where the subject does not receive results). This paper does not attempt to present a  new review of the many results and theories.  Instead, we reformulate Ro = (X, P, z). We shall not disturb X and P, but we shall restrict the class Z and add several new  components. The elements of Z, the class of objective functions, will be represented formally as linear functions' of indices  associated with each alternative (i.e., indices computable in terms of the entries in the payoff matrix). The choice process, which we subsequently model as a chance constrained optimization problem, involves first, possible  exclusion of alternatives due to excessive regret, followed by selection of the (a) preferred alternative from the possibly  reduced set of alternatives. In this framework for a risky decision, the objective function to be optimized is a possibly  irrelevant aspect of the choice problem. It becomes relevant only when the (possibly) reduced set of alternatives contains at  least two alternatives yielding different objective function values.  Postulating constraints can lead to situations in which an alternative is infeasible. Thus, constraints can exclude certain  alternatives from further consideration. This allows observed "optimal" choice behaviors to differ from those predicted by an  unconstrained model. An example occurs during a choice experiment where subjects have to play their decisions and could lose  money. Barron (1970) reports some subjects did not choose an alternative with a much higher expected utility (utilities were  measures using 50-50 standard gambles and tested using 75-25 standard gambles) when the possibility of moderate loss existed.  It is hypothesized that the regrets associated with the rejected alternative exceeded the subject's tolerable regret.  At this point, it is appropriate to comment on the motivation for R,v and the consequences derivable in R . There is both  survey (Mao, 1970) and experimental (Barron, 1970) evidence that unconstrained optimization principles considered as decision  rules fail to describe observed choice behavior. These principles fail, in part, because stimulus data such as (X, P) are  subjected to multiple considerations involving possible gains, losses and their associated probabilities. Optimization  principles (under uncertainty) such as maximax, maximin (Wald), minimax regret (Savage), etc., fail to consider knowledge of  state probabilities, while others such as expectation models only indirectly consider possible loss or regret.  Becker and McClintock (1967) discuss modifications of expectation models, e.g., the weighted subjective expected utility  model, which make expectation models more descriptive. However, these modifications severely restrict predictive power.  Expectation models do not explicitly consider regret. It has been suggested elsewhere that regret and/or expected regret play  important roles in choice behavior (Suydam, 1965, and Myers, Suydam and Heuckeroth, 1966). Our choice model incorporates  regret by introducing chance constraints on tolerable regret. We believe that this modification incorporating both  optimization principles and constraints produces a descriptive decision model having interesting consequences.  Formal consequences of the R framework are developed in Theorems 1 and 2 of this paper. Some of these consequences have been  tested. Results confirm the existence of a choice process which can be modeled and interpreted in the framework of a chance  constrained optimization problem with a linear objective function. In this sense, R, RISKY DECISIONS 63  represents a possible next step in the development of descriptive choice models. As before, in restricting attention to a  particular class of objective functions, the particular constraint forms need not be considered definitive.  It should be noted that in this paper we present no theory of how a subject sets his tolerable regrets, T, or how he  determines the size of the maximum probability, 1 - a, of accepting alternatives having regrets greater than this tolerable  regret. It is likely that T and a are functionally related in some circumstances. It is also plausible to expect that the  specification of an objective function z E Z and values for T and a are affected by experience, subject personalities, group  norms, group structure, social pressures, and the specific context of the decision. These more theoretical issues are not met  in this paper. We are primarily concerned with the predictions of individual choice behavior which follow from our  formulation.2  The choice situation is assumed to be represented by the usual payoff matrix for decision making under risk (cf. Luce and  Raiffa, 1957). The mathematical development formally introduces chance constraints-constraints which depend both on the amount  of regret and the probability of its occurrence. The model is solved for two alternative situations in Theorems 1 and 2. These  theorems can then be used to interpret individual choices. DISCUSSION  We have represented choice (descriptively), given X and P, as a solution to a chance constrained optimization problem, rather  than as a problem of optimizing an objective function. In fact, for particular conditions on T and ca, Theorem 2 shows the  choice of objective function is irrelevant.  Our reformulation is based on the more general notion of a chance constrained optimization problem. This representation is  more general in the sense that if the chance constraint holds with probability one, the problem is a constrained optimization  problem. A typical special case for the assumed class, Z, of objective functions is a linear programming problem. If either  the chance constraint holds with probability zero, or if T exceeds maximum regret, the problem is unconstrained.  The representation is flexible in two different senses. First, neither the objective function nor the constraint form need be  considered definitive. We have chosen a specific Z and a specific constraint form in terms of regret. Second, the model  permits a variety of "optimal" choices. For specific values for T and a and a particular objective function exactly one  strategy is optimal (unless alternate optima in the sense of, say, linear programming exist). However, for different values of  T or a or for different objective functions, different strategies may be optimal. Thus, the additional parameters increase the  descriptive power of the model but require additional specifications (viz., T, a, z).  This raises the question of possible empirical falsification. The model gives an experimenter considerable degrees of freedom  to fit observed choices, ex post. How might the model be denied? The predictions of Theorems 1 and 2 could only be denied if  there were some independent means of measuring T and a for a given individual in a given situation.4 However, two other  possibilities exist. One involves the use of protocols and the other involves the case m > 2.  The term protocol generally refers to a record of the time sequence of events (Newell, 1966). In the context of  experimentation it includes the continuous verbal behavior of subjects instructed to "think aloud." Since the model assumes  the individual  4 If the chance constrained representation is assumed to be true, Theorem 1 becomes a means for measuring T and a when T <  min{rll, r, and knowledge of the objective function is sufficient to rank the alternatives.  70 BARRON AND MACKENZIE  (subject) ranks alternatives according to an objective function, possibly designates alternatives as infeasible, and selects a  preferred feasible alternative, protocols could confirm or deny existence of these processes.  The case m > 2 provides for the possibility of a subject selecting an alternative which is inferior to another feasible  alternative. However, because the choice "algorithm" is more complicated mathematically for the case m > 2, there is a greater  chance for subject error in "computing" the intuitive solution to the chance constrained model. Thus, due to computational  errors, nonoptimal choices might be observed. Furthermore, there are difficulties in designing such situations. The  experimenter must be able to demonstrate that the alternative is inferior over the entire class of objective functions and  that each associated superior alternative is feasible if the selected alternative is feasible.  SIGNIFICANCE OF RN  RN represents an alternative conceptualization for descriptive choice models for risky decisions. The resulting chance  constrained optimization model is descriptive, includes regret parameters, and incorporates a plausible psychological  evaluation process. From this representation testable (confirming) propositions are derived (theorems 1 and 2). (Unless T and  a can be measured independently, empirical tests for m = 2 could not deny the model.) RN allows optimal mixed strategies under  conditions other than distinct alternatives yielding equal optimal objective function values.  We would suggest that the chance constrained model captures the essence of decision making under risk, that it is  descriptively valid. The decision maker eliminates alternatives via regret constraints and chooses among those remaining on  the basis of his objective function. Risk is limited by a probabilistic notion. This idea pervades many of the verbal results  reported by Mao (1970). His study suggests that a chance constrained concept well represents risk as perceived by a sample of  financial executives. For a discussion see Barron and Joy (1972).  Finally, we would suggest the case m = 2 has useful applications. The decision problem to adopt or not adopt an innovation can  be modeled as a 2 X n payoff matrix (Barron, 1970). A firm's decision to invest in a particular project can be modeled  similarly, where the second pure strategy is a standard investment. A go-no-go decision is a further example.  SUMMARY  Constraints are commonplace in most real world decision phenomena. While it is true that Ro = (X, P, z) could be defined such  that some constraints are taken care of in the formulation of an objective function, interpersonal differences plus the need  RISKY DECISIONS 71  of experimenters to exercise some control over the stimuli presented to subjects, make this possibility of a priori properly  formulating Ro rather impractical. It would seem more reasonable, experimentally, to keep the payoff matrix and stated  probabilities as given and to consider the problem as a situation in RN rather than Ro. Since there are many possible risky  situation in the sense of problem (5), it may be reasonable to use RN to construct new experimental paradigms for  investigating behavior in risky situations. A theory of choice in risky situations could use a framework like problem (5) and  RN to investigate how the setting, subject personality and experience, the group structure, norms and social pressures affect  the choice of a z E Z and the values of T and a.  The normative problem of imposing desiderata on an objective function would be considered in terms of additional constraints  on RN and a formulation such as problem (5). Conversely, since objective functions can be written as constraints and  constraints as objective functions, various desiderata can be imposed many ways and could be varied according to the  situation. While a normative theory of decision making in risky situations is probably valuable, we would suggest that such a  theory be based on a less restricted class of situations than Ro .
