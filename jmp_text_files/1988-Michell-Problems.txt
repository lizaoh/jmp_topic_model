JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 32, 466-473 (1988)  Theoretical Note  Some Problems in Testing the Double Cancellation Condition in Conjoint Measurement  JOEL MICHELL  University of Sydney  Two problems involved in testing the conjoint measurement condition of double cancellation are analysed. 1. It is argued that  in the case of complete data matrices tests of double cancellation either confirm or falsify the hypothesis of additivity and  that, contrary to popular belief, so-called "no-tests" are really acceptances. 2. It is shown that with a weak order upon 3 x  3 matrices both independence and a single test of double cancellation (which is logically independent of independence) are  necessary and sufficient for additivity. Hence, if a weak order satisfies independence in such matrices only this single test  of double cancellation needs to be performed. C' 1988 Academic Press, Inc.  The theory of additive conjoint measurement was first presented by Luce and Tukey (1964) as a new method of fundamental  measurement applicable to psychology. Since then the importance of the theory has been widely recognized (Krantz, Luce,  Suppes, & Tversky, 1971; Roberts, 1979) and applications within psychology have been increasing. However, applications have  been hampered by two problems: there is no clear understanding of how best to evaluate data relative to the conditions  necessary for additive conjoint measuremnt; nor is there any clear understanding of how these necessary conditions relate to  one another. This paper, which has arisen out of attempts to apply conjoint measurement theory to the relatively simple case  of 3x3 data matrices, considers each of these problems in relation to the conditions of double cancellation and independence.  In recent years, the view that tests of double cancellation may involve a "no-test" category has become entrenched (e.g.,  Levelt, Riemersma, & Bunt, 1972; Perline, Wright, & Wainer, 1979; van der Ven, 1980; and Nygren, 1985). For example, some researchers  (e.g., Levelt et al., 1972; Perline et al., 1979; Westermann, 1985) assess the extent of their data's violation of additivity  via the ratio of the number of violations of double cancellation to what they take to be the total number of effective tests  (i.e., the so-called "no-tests" are excluded from the denominator). Without commenting upon the validity of this approach, it  will be apparent that in so doing the fit of their data to the additive hypothesis will be underestimated. This is because in  Table 1, 6 of the 13 acceptance patterns are liable to be incorrectly classified as "no-tests."  Consider the results represented by Perline et al. (1979) (that is, their Tables 4 and 6). Restricting attention to their  tests of "direction 2" (which they correctly identify as the crucial tests in the circumstances), when the reported number of  "no-tests" are correctly counted as acceptances, the proportion of rejections in Table 4 falls from 0.2366 to 0.1467 and in  their Table 6, from 0.1119 to 0.0624 (the total number of tests involved being 4704 and 10,010, respectively). Hence, they  substantially overestimated the proportion of rejections in both cases. This may very well have made a difference to their  evaluation of the additive hypothesis in relation to their data. If it is agreed that confirming a hypothesis is quite  different from not having tested it at all, then it will be recognized that the conclusion that "no-tests" are actually  confirmations of additivity makes a difference in applications of conjoint measurement theory.  2. TESTING DOUBLE CANCELLATION IN RELATION TO INDEPENDENCE  Given a weak order on 3 x 3 data matrices two conjoint measurement conditions may be tested: double cancellation and  independence. Independence is satisfied in a data matrix if and only if there exists some permutation of the rows and columns  such that the cells in each row are ordered from least to greatest from left to right and the cells within each column are  ordered from least to greatest from top to bottom. (A data matrix so arranged will be said to be in standard form.) The issues  to be addressed here are these. (a) If independence is satisfied, how many tests of double cancellation are sufficient to test  the additive hypothesis? And (b) if independence is violated, is there any need to test double cancellation and, if so, how  many tests need to be performed? These issues are in need of resolution because some confusion surrounds them in the  literature. For example, Krantz and Tversky (1971) imply that there is a single test of double cancellation in each 3 x 3  matrix, but Perline et al. (1979) performed two such tests in each 3 x 3 matrix within their data.  The issue of how many tests of double cancellation may be conducted within any 3 x 3 data matrix is further complicated by the  suggestion of Krantz et al. (1971) that there are versions of double cancellation other than that given at the beginning of  this paper. This version is the familiar one deriving from Luce and Tukey (1964) and will be referred to as Luce-Tukey double  cancellation.
