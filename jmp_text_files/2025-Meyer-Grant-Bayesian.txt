A common approach to analyzing response-time data in terms of this data-generating model relies on the Bayesian framework ( e.g .,  Steingroever et al., 2021 ). Conducting such an analysis involves integrating information from a  prior distribution  (also simply referred to as the prior) of the process’s drift-rate and threshold parameters and information provided by the newly acquired data, which is contextualized by its  likelihood  under the theorized data-generating model. This, in turn, gives rise to an updated distribution of the parameters known as the  posterior distribution  (also simply referred to as the posterior).

In order to obtain this distribution, a pragmatic strategy is to utilize  conjugate priors , so that the resulting posterior has the same parametric form as the prior (see,  e.g .,  Gelman et al., 2013 ). The fact that the inverse-Gaussian distribution is a member of the two-parameter exponential family entails that a corresponding conjugate prior must exist. More specifically, there must exist simple rules for transforming prior into posterior parameters in light of new data ( DeGroot, 1970; Raiffa & Schlaifer, 1961 ), which can be leveraged to considerably expedite the calculations that are necessary for practical application.
