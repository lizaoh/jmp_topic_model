Psychophysics studies the quantitative relationships between physical stimuli and their psychological perception. In a typical psychophysical experiment, a subject is instructed to answer either “yes” or “no”, to indicate whether a certain feature of the stimulus is perceptible. This experiment is modelled with a  psychometric function , which maps the intensity of the stimulus to the probability of a “yes” answer. The model is used to estimate certain parameters of the psychometric function, such as the location of the 50%  threshold  of perception, the intensity where the subject detects the stimulus half of the time. Typically the experimenter is interested in how these parameters change between different  conditions . In  adaptive  sequential estimation methods, the intensity of the next stimulus is adjusted based on the outcome of the previous trials, so as to yield more informative answers. See  Treutwein (1995)  for a review of the basic concepts.

In many psychophysical studies, the psychometric function of interest is multidimensional (cf.  Ashby & Townsend, 1986 ). For example, given a color space, such as the space of RGB intensity triples, consider the probability of seeing the (multidimensional) difference between two target colors. The origin of the stimulus-space corresponds to identical target colors and any nonzero vector corresponds to a difference in a certain direction in the color space. The probability of perceiving is at its minimum at the origin and increases over all lines extending from the origin. In this case, the threshold is a closed surface (or contour) in the multidimensional stimulus space.

Sometimes such multidimensional psychometric functions can be separated into multiple one-dimensional (1D) functions based on a previously validated model of perception, for example, by assuming independent effects on cone-specific pathways when studying chromatic adaptation. The 1D components can then be estimated separately using a 1D estimation procedure. However, such separable models do not work in more complex settings, such as those involving effects of cortical processing ( Webster & Wilson, 2000 ).

So far, adaptive estimation procedures have only been applied to 1D psychometric functions. Although many methods can estimate multiple parameters of the psychometric function ( Kontsevich & Tyler, 1999; Snoeren & Puts, 1997 ), only one parameter of the stimulus has been adapted within one condition. In this subsection, we formulate a different implementation of the  
                            Ψ 
                          procedure, with different trade-offs, based on a particle filter method ( Doucet, de Freitas, & Gordon, 2001 ) for estimating the continuous distributions. Instead of discretizing the prior distribution as a large table, we will use a Monte Carlo sample of constant size to approximate the prior distribution at each trial. Thus, there will be a  random  error in each approximated distribution, yielding slightly different trial placements on different runs for the same data. This is in contrast to the  deterministic  algorithms discussed earlier, whose discretization errors, and therefore the trial placements, are always the same for the same data.

However, we do not believe the randomness to be of much consequence as the error can be made arbitrarily small. It could even be argued that some randomness might be useful in preventing the subject from making useful predictions about the placement of the next trial (cf.  Kontsevich & Tyler, 1999 ).

In this subsection, we consider how to compute parameter estimates from the posterior distribution. It would be best to just use the whole posterior distribution for inference, but a single estimate is often required.

Since the error of an ellipse estimate is difficult to assess from the separate errors of multiple parameters, we additionally use the non-overlapping area between the estimated ellipse and the true ellipse as a single measure of the shape error of the estimate, see  Fig. 3 
                         . The arguably best ellipse estimate would be obtained by minimizing the expected shape error. However, it would be computationally expensive.

Kontsevich and Tyler (1999)  use mean estimates for the log-threshold and log-slope parameters. However, with ellipse thresholds, a mean estimate is not uniquely defined; it depends on the parameterization. Furthermore, the mean of an angle parameter is not well-defined.

Brown (1957)  has averaged color matching ellipses of different subjects by averaging the covariance matrices of the Gaussians corresponding to the ellipses ( Wyszecki & Stiles, 2000, 5.4.2, p. 318 ). This parameterization could be used for obtaining a mean estimate from the posterior distribution. However, Brown had to use additional weighting to reduce the effect of exceptionally large ellipses, which suggests that this is not the ideal parameterization. Applying the Brown averaging to the present model without the weighting resulted in a relatively large upwards bias in the estimate of the size parameter.

While the 2D- 
                         Ψ 
                       procedure does give a quick overview in practice and is theoretically valid, there is a possibility of some measurement bias due to the unorthodox approach of adapting multiple stimulus dimensions simultaneously.

The results reported in  Garner (1962)  indicate that when a stimulus is varied in multiple dimensions simultaneously, absolute judgements of the value of each component dimension become less accurate. This suggests that multidimensional stimuli may be generally more sensitive to interactions between the trials than 1D stimuli (a violation of Assumption 2). Thus, the thresholds obtained by the 2D- 
                         Ψ 
                       method may be less reliable or differ from those of the traditional methods, especially if the task depends on a stable state of sensory adaptation. However, varying a stimulus in multiple dimensions is not too different from the common practice of interleaving different conditions, and in that case, the results should be similar.

In a similar multidimensional extension of adaptive latent trait estimation ( Wang & Chen, 2004 ) similar issues are raised: radically different trials may cause anxiety or fatigue. However,  Wang and Chen (2004)  note that such a mixture may also break the monotony of a test and thereby increase motivation.

In practice, it may be advisable to run a few conditions with both the 2D- 
                         Ψ 
                       method and traditional methods to find out if the task is sensitive to the experimental procedure. If the results agree, the better efficiency of the 2D- 
                         Ψ 
                       method can be harnessed in a large number of conditions.

The time-critical parts of both algorithms were implemented in optimized C and compiled with gcc-3.3 on a 2 GHz Opteron processor. In the deterministic algorithm, FFTW library ( Frigo & Johnson, 2005 ) version 3.01 was used.

The deterministic (FFT) algorithm used 410 MB of memory for the precomputed tables and 80 MB for the prior/posterior distribution. Each additional condition to be run in parallel would take an extra 80 MB. The placement computation took about 780 ms and updating the prior about 90 ms at each trial. For a typical psychophysical experiment with 30 or so trials per condition, smaller sampling rates may be sufficient. These high sampling rates were used for assessing the convergence properties of the model on a long run.

In order to assess the robustness of the 2D- 
                            Ψ 
                          method, we repeated some of the simulations with simulated observers that violate certain assumptions of the model.

For data generated using an observer with a rectangular threshold, the estimated slope is biased downwards and the efficiency is slightly decreased, but otherwise the simulation results are very similar to those of an observer following the assumed ellipse model. The aspect and angle estimates have the obvious interpretation and the size estimate converges approximately to the value corresponding to an ellipse of the same area as the observer's threshold rectangle. Thus, the exact shape of the threshold contour is not a critical factor.

Simulations using an observer with linearly (in time) changing ellipse parameters suggest that the method can tolerate at least about 50% total change in the observer's threshold (as measured by the shape error measure, see  Fig. 3 ) during an experiment with 128 or less trials. In that case, the estimates of each parameter follow about the average of its realized values, and the slope is slightly biased downwards (which is true for the “averaged” ellipse). Thus, the 2D- 
                            Ψ 
                          method appears to be robust with respect to reasonable learning effects.

In both of the above cases, the overall threshold ellipse estimate is descriptive, but there is a clear indication that the slope estimate should not be trusted unless the model is known to be accurate. As the 2D- 
                            Ψ 
                          method remains relatively efficient under these assumption violations, more complex models can be fitted and compared after collecting the experimental data, although the trial placements may then be slightly sub-optimal for fitting all parameters.

If the exact shape of the threshold is interesting, the online model can be extended with additional shape parameters. If stronger systematic learning effects are expected, or if the change itself is interesting, the online model can be extended with linear or sub-linear trend parameters (the basic framework remains valid if time is considered as a stimulus dimension). The particle filter algorithm can easily accommodate these extra parameters.

We have proposed a psychometric model for 2D stimuli, based on parameterizing the threshold contour as an ellipse. While the model is essentially the simplest one that could possibly work, it is general enough to be widely applicable. If the threshold contour is assumed to be convex and symmetric about the origin, there is not much possible variation from an ellipse—any experiment showing the invalidity of the model would need to have a large number of trials.

The  
                         Ψ 
                       method applied to this model—the 2D- 
                         Ψ 
                       method—is able to quickly estimate the overall shape of the threshold contour, much faster than the traditional method of estimating the psychometric function on certain 1D lines independently. Simulations indicate that the 2D- 
                         Ψ 
                      -method is robust with respect to deviations from the assumed ellipse shape of the threshold contour as well as reasonable learning effects. A psychophysical experiment indicates that the method is able to yield consistent results in a real experimental setting.

While the 2D- 
                         Ψ 
                       method is mathematically valid, the nature of the multidimensional adaptation may make it inapplicable to certain tasks that are sensitive to interactions between the trials. If test runs indicate that the 2D- 
                         Ψ 
                       method does give significantly different results than traditional methods, it may be desirable to modify the procedure to work more like traditional methods. The experiment can be divided into batches of similar trials by optimizing the expected information gain restricted to a certain line (or area) in the stimulus space. The line (area) for the next batch can be chosen as the one that has the globally optimal trial. The first batches should be short (one trial) so as to quickly find the overall threshold shape. In later trials, the batch size should increase to maximize stability. This procedure would automatically choose efficient 1D lines in anisotropic conditions, yielding still better efficiency than any unsupervised traditional procedures.

To enable realtime experiments with the 2D- 
                         Ψ 
                       method, we have improved the original  
                         Ψ 
                       algorithm. While the original algorithm explicitly computes and evaluates the posterior distributions for each possible stimulus position, our FFT-convolution formulation computes, up to round-off errors, the exact same results in one pass, using time proportional to the logarithm of the number of discretized stimuli. Thus, the new algorithm is essentially as fast as possible with the discretized Bayesian model. Still, the computation and storage requirements of the discretization grow exponentially with the number of model dimensions.

Therefore, we have also presented a new particle filter implementation of the  
                         Ψ 
                       method that uses exact, continuous distributions at each step, but approximates them with a Monte Carlo sample for placement computation. This yields much better scaling with the number of dimensions, but introduces the Monte Carlo approximation error, the effect of which is more difficult to assess than that of the discretization error. However, the results do converge to the same exact results as with the deterministic algorithm as the accuracy parameters of both algorithms increase.

With the present model, both implementations work about equally well. However, the deterministic, discretized implementation is near its limit; adding more model dimensions would be difficult. Even with increasing computational resources, fast convolutions become more difficult to formulate for three and higher dimensional stimuli. On the other hand, with the particle filter implementation, it should be easy to further extend the model.

The logical next step would be to use three or more dimensions to parameterize the stimuli. For example, the color difference experiment could be extended to three-dimensional stimuli by including the lightness dimension. One could also experiment with non-ellipsoidal models or include trend variables to model any systematic learning effects.

In this appendix, we list some notes regarding the implementation of the 2D- 
                         Ψ 
                       algorithms.

As the precomputed tables and the prior distribution table of the deterministic 2D- 
                         Ψ 
                       algorithm are huge, they should be stored in a cache-friendly order, namely the size and angle dimensions last (i.e., with smallest strides) for efficient FFT. The computations in steps 1 and 2 of  Algorithm 3  should be interleaved into one iteration over the values of  
                         β 
                       for maximum locality. Thus, only two passes over the tables are required, first to compute the placement and second to update the prior distribution with the trial result.

It might be possible to start with lower sampling rates of the parameters and then resample with reduced ranges when a certain level of convergence is reached as in the MUEST method by  Snoeren and Puts (1997) . While resampling may be able to keep the effect of the discretization error from increasing over a long run, it cannot reduce the exponential growth of the computation time with the number of dimensions. Furthermore, if the FFT convolution is used, the range of the angle dimension cannot be reduced.
