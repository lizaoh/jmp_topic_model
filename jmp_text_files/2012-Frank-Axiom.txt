The adaptive behavior of living systems relies on their ability to coordinate bodily motions in the service of specific task goals. Coordination is very obvious in locomotory tasks, such as walking to work, and less obvious–although equally essential–in other tasks, such as maintaining upright posture while talking to a friend. The ease with which these everyday activities are executed does not readily reveal the complexity of the problem movement systems evolved to solve. Any simple coordinated movement pattern requires regulation of a very large number of components (e.g., vascular, neural, muscular, skeletal) that vary in structure, function and rate of operation. The implication is that at the base of virtually all behavior sits the fundamental problem, first envisioned by Bernstein, of compressing these many degrees of freedom into stable and yet flexible low dimensional patterns required for task performance ( Bernstein, 1967, 1996 ; see also  Turvey, 1977, 1990 ). Attempts to provide solutions to Bernstein’s problem have yielded neurally, computationally and dynamically inspired models of coordinated movements (e.g.,  Collyer & Rosenbaum, 1998 ;  Fuchs & Jirsa, 2008 ;  Swinnen & Duysens, 2004 ). Our focus here is on the dynamical perspective.

Rhythmic movements have been the primary targets for investigations of coordination within the dynamical perspective. The governing assumption has been that interlimb (e.g., right and left hands) and intersegmental (e.g., hand and forearm) rhythmic movements abide by simple, elegant rules, despite the neuromuscular complexity involved in their organization. Accordingly, the strategy has been to identify, and give formal expression to, the simple rules. The resultant formalisms incorporate, in broad strokes, the essential features of rhythmic behavior observed in the context of functional tasks, and reflect the general principles that render the many degrees of freedom supporting the behavior controllable.

A paradigmatic and much studied example is the model of the 1:1 rhythmic coordination that typifies locomotion by fins, wings, and limbs ( Haken et al., 1985 ). This model–referred to as the HKB model after its authors–comprises two levels of formalization. The higher level is a potential defining the stability properties of relative phase. The lower level is a system of coupled limit cycle oscillators defining the individual limb movements and their interactions.

A motion equation derived from the potential describes the emergence and dissolution of stable coordination patterns observed when a person attempts to (a) synchronize left and right limbs, or (b) synchronize a rhythmic limb movement with that of another person ( Schmidt, Carello, & Turvey, 1990 ) or with an oscillating environmental target ( Wimmers, Beek, & Van Wieringen, 1992 ). Research guided by the HKB model has identified constraints covering factors intrinsic to the task or the motor system (e.g., movement frequency and differences between the coordinating limbs) and factors traditionally viewed as logically separate from the organization of motor behavior (e.g., concurrent cognitive activity). Hence, the HKB model of 1:1 rhythmic coordination has contributed to advances in movement science in particular and in psychology more generally ( Kelso, 1995; Kelso & Engstrom, 2006 ).

A central prediction from the HKB model is that the pattern and stability of coordination are co-dependent. More precisely, changes in coordination stability are expected to accompany changes in coordination pattern induced by experimental manipulations that are mapped to the model parameters (e.g., manipulations of the magnitude of asymmetry between limbs). Even though research findings have by and large supported the prediction of pattern-stability co-dependence, in a few experimental settings the prediction has been seemingly violated. The main goal of the present study is to show how a principled generalization of the HKB model might accommodate the observed invariance in coordination stability over changes in coordination pattern. The demonstration is conducted at the higher level of the formalization—that of the potential and the motion equation derived from it. The empirical validity of the higher level is well established. The validity of the lower level, the formalization in terms of coupled oscillators, is less consensual (e.g.,  Beek, Pepper & Daffertshofer, 2002 ;  Kudo, Park, Kay, & Turvey, 2006 ). Issues exist with regard to the kind of oscillator most befitting the individual limb movements and with regard to the nature of the function that couples them. In view of these issues, we consider the (higher level) potential dynamics and study the impact of a new symmetry parameter. We will show that variations of this parameter affect the coordination pattern but do not affect coordination stability. Accordingly, we will suggest that certain experimental manipulations induce changes in this new symmetry parameter which in turn results in the experimentally observed violation of pattern-stability co-dependence.

As previously noted, the HKB model was developed to capture the essential features of 1:1 rhythmic coordination and its dynamical constraints. Experimentally, a window into the dynamics of 1:1 rhythmic coordination has been obtained by having participants move their right and left effectors (e.g., fingers, arms, hand-held pendulums) at a common tempo. Without training, a participant confronted with this task is facile with only two patterns: in-phase, in which the effectors move in the same direction, and anti-phase, in which effectors move in opposite directions. Symmetry dictates these two patterns ( Stewart & Golubitsky, 1992 ). They will occur for all cases (nonbiological and biological) of two identical oscillators (e.g., weakly coupled limit cycle oscillators) independent of the coupling. The stability of the patterns, however, rests with the nature of the coupling.

The HKB model is a model of the intrinsic dynamics of interlimb 1:1 coordination. It correctly reproduces the key experimentally observed phenomena of phase transitions, hysteresis, critical fluctuations, and critical slowing down. To accommodate more varied experimental settings and observations, extensions and elaborations are required. For the most part they constitute theoretical and explanatory developments with the HKB model at their core ( Fuchs & Jirsa, 2008 ).

Among the earliest elaborations was that demanded by the putatively simple linkage of interlimb rhythmic coordination to an environmental pacer, a metronome.  Schöner and Kelso (1988)  suggested an additive linear driving term attracting  
                            ϕ 
                          to the pacer’s phase but subsequent experimentation ( Fink, Foo, Jirsa, & Kelso, 2000; Kudo et al., 2006 ) has favored multiplicative parametric coupling ( Jirsa, Fink, Foo, & Kelso, 2000 ).

Among the latest elaborations is that in response to the potential dichotomy of rhythmic and non-rhythmic (discrete) movements (for overviews see  Huys, Studenka, Rheame, Zelaznik, & Jirsa, 2008  and  Sternad, 2008 ). The issue can be framed in several ways. One is whether sequential discrete movements of two limbs and parallel discrete movements of two limbs are both understandable as reflecting HKB dynamics—that is, whether both can be shown to abide the HKB coupling. The study of  excitator  dynamics (an excitatory system that exhibits threshold properties and returns, post threshold-crossing, to initial conditions after some delay) has provided a positive answer. The excitator can produce the serial and parallel dynamics and the HKB model can be interpreted as a specific realization of the excitator for special parameter settings ( Fuchs & Jirsa, 2008; Jirsa & Kelso, 2005 ).

Mathematically, the HKB coupling brings about the same phase convergence phenomena for cases of discrete–discrete and rhythmic–rhythmic interlimb coordination ( Jirsa & Kelso, 2005 ). Although the original formalization of the HKB coupling is contested, as noted above, there are suggestions that the coupling is invariant across different movement types. Most notable is the empirical finding that, when appropriately characterized, discrete movement of one hand affects continuous rhythmic movements of the other hand in a manner that conforms to the present  Fig. 1  (see Fig. 6 in  Calvin, Huys, & Jirsa, 2010 ).

As mentioned above, the HKB model has been used to describe the emergence of in-phase and anti-phase coordination patterns with external rhythmic stimuli ( Wimmers et al., 1992 ). In this context, field theoretical models have been developed ( Jirsa & Haken, 1996, 1997 ) to account for experimental findings consistent with the predictions of the HKB model, namely, anti-phase to in-phase phase transitions ( Kelso, 1995; Kelso et al., 1992, 1998 ) and critical fluctuations ( Wallenstein, Kelso, & Bressler, 1995 ). In particular, it was found that the generalized stochastic HKB models that will be presented in Section  3.4  as Eqs.  (72) and (73)  govern the phase dynamics of one of the two modes of spatially distributed brain activity that are crucial for the emergence of anti-phase to in-phase transitions ( Frank, Daffertshofer, Beek, & Haken, 1999 ). Likewise, coupled neural oscillator models yield dynamic equations for relative phase that are of the form of the HKB model or exhibit a similar structure. Using the so-called phase reduction method proposed by  Kuramoto (1984) , it can be shown that weakly coupled neural oscillators exhibit a relative phase dynamics subjected to a force composed of a series of sine and cosine terms. On the other hand, the studies highlight the need for empirical tests of the assumptions that phase reduction methods (e.g., weak coupling) and slowly varying amplitude methods hold for the cognitive processes under consideration. Moreover, it is not a priori clear what the coefficients are of the relevant phase dynamic laws of a given cognitive process. As noted in our introductory remarks, there are experimental settings in which the expected relation between attractor location and stability (≈0° more stable, ≈180° less stable) is violated. The demonstration by  Mitra, Amazeen, and Turvey (1997)  is the most poignant. It makes transparent the key theoretical conjecture and mathematical challenge of the present paper: empirical observations of dissociation between attractor location and attractor strength are chimera. They are manifestations of contrasting frames of reference (coordinate systems) rather than contraventions of the HKB model.

Two reference frames underlying interlimb coordination are revealed by experiments that compare interlimb 1:1 rhythmic coordination in para-coronal and para-sagittal planes performed at several movement speeds. One reference frame is linked to the tendency for homologous muscle groups (e.g., ulnar muscles of left and right forearms) to be activated simultaneously, an “egocentric” or muscle-related constraint. The other reference frame is linked to a tendency for limb segments to move in the same direction, an “allocentric” or space-related constraint ( Swinnen, Jardin, Meulenbroek, Dounskaia, & Hofkens-Van Den Brandt, 1997 ). For movements in para-sagittal planes, the two constraints work cooperatively. For movements in para-coronal planes, the two constraints work oppositely. In the latter case, in-phase (antiphase) contraction of homologous muscles of the contralateral limbs produces spatially antiphase (in-phase) coordination. The experiments that motivated and substantiated the HKB model were conducted under conditions in which the two constraints were cooperative (for summaries see  Kelso, 1995 ;  Amazeen, Amazeen, & Turvey, 1998 ).

Of particular significance to present purposes are the investigations of the dynamics of coordination in the context of cognitive activity. These investigations have been conducted (to date) in the common para-sagittal experimental setting depicted in  Fig. 2  (left panels) where the spatial and muscular constraints are cooperative. They reveal that concurrent cognitive activity affects the attractive stable states of 1:1 frequency locking of two limbs.

In respect to  retrieving  a list of previously encoded unrelated words, retrieval affects parallel rhythmic coordination (new attractor, more noise) but not vice versa.

Of special relevance is the finding that when bimanual rhythmic coordination is performed in parallel with the semantic memory task of retrieving members of a category, retrieval is impaired and the dynamics of coordination are altered ( Shockley & Turvey, 2006 ). This mutuality was observed in an experimental task that excluded commonly identified sources of specific inter-task interference (e.g., shared input modality, similar semantic domain) ( Bourke, 1997; Bourke, Duncan, & Nimmo-Smith, 1996 ). Mutual impedance in the absence of specific dimensions of interference favors explanation of dual tasking in terms of limitations in central processing capacity ( Kahneman, 1973 ). Questions about these limitations tend to dominate current theorizing ( Hazeltine, Ruthruff, & Remington, 2006 ). For example: Do they lead to serial processing? Are they dependent on the content of the tasks? Is the duration of resource competition what matters? Is capacity amplified by arousal?

Although frequently used to address the consequences of divided attention, cognitive models and neural models based on the capacity hypothesis are unable to address the intricate pattern of mutual influences observed by  Shockley and Turvey (2006)  between semantic retrieval and rhythmic coordination. In the view of  Shockley and Turvey (2006) , stability is a more promising candidate for the implied general factor influenced by parallel performance of both tasks. The seeming mix of monitoring and control processes constituting semantic retrieval ( Baddeley, 1998; Koriat, 2000 ) suggest that semantic retrieval, like coordination, could be framed in dynamical terms. The dual-task performance of semantic retrieval and coordination could, therefore, be interpreted as a coupling of the two dynamics, a coupling that might be expected to entail parameter changes in retrieval dynamics paralleling the identified parameter changes in coordination dynamics. On Shockley and Turvey’s hypothesis, the criterion for these dual changes would be enhanced stability of the coupled system (see  Neumann, 1987 ).

The foregoing findings attest to the generality of the HKB dynamics: it makes a conceptual link between two phenomena (coordination, cognition) that have traditionally been viewed as fundamentally different. At the same time the foregoing findings indicate the need to re-examine the model in light of circumstances in which attractor locus changes but attractor stability does not. As previewed in Section  1.4 , it is possible that violation of the dependence between attractor location and stability by cognitive activity is only apparent. More precisely, it might stem from the assumption that the coordinate system in which the target behavior is measured, namely, LCS, is identical with the coordinate system that captures the evolution of the perception–action system on the attractor, namely, ACS. It is conceivable, however, that certain task constraints (such as cognitive activity) induce a mismatch between these two coordinate systems, which manifests as a shift in the reference frame about which coordination is organized. If coordination is organized around a shifted frame of reference, namely ACS, the intrinsically stable patterns of coordination (i.e., in-phase and anti-phase) will be mapped in LCS to attractors shifted from

The aforementioned studies invite a theoretical re-examination of the deterministic ( Haken et al., 1985 ) and stochastic ( Schöner et al., 1986 ) models of interlimb coordination from the perspective of a possible non-identity between ACS and LCS. To this end, we will review the derivation of the Haken–Kelso–Bunz model taking an axiomatic perspective. We will modify one of the axioms. In doing so, we will decouple the behavioral coordinate system from the attractor coordinate system. This decoupling will involve a symmetry parameter that has not yet been discussed thoroughly in the literature. In addition, we will include in our consideration how the effects of manipulations of the newly introduced symmetry parameter relate to manipulations of the imperfection parameter  
                            δ 
                          previously presented. We will study the deterministic and stochastic models thus obtained in more detail and derive predictions for experimental work.

The colloquial definition of symmetry is “the operations you can do to some thing  
                         x 
                       such that, having done them,  
                         x 
                       is the same as it was before”. If one looks into a mirror while performing in-phase or anti-phase movements of the index fingers in the coronal plane, under idealized conditions (i.e., when performing perfect in- and anti-phase movements) the mirrored phase relation is the same as the performed phase relation despite the exchange of left and right index fingers. Consequently, the mirror-reflection transformation–a spatial operation–leaves the phase relation invariant. This motivates the symmetry axiom from a purely descriptive point of view.

In addition, the symmetry axiom can be motivated from a structural point of view. In the original work by  Haken et al. (1985) , reflectional symmetry was assumed to hold because bimanual coordination was studied in healthy adults in the context of coordination tasks that involved homologous muscle groups in the right and left sides of the body (right and left coronal plane). The symmetry axiom expresses an idealized view of perception–action systems that on the structural level there is left–right symmetry with respect to the coronal plane. From this structural left–right symmetry the symmetry axiom follows. More explicitly, we assume that the phase dynamics as described by the HKB model is brought about by coupled oscillatory subunits. These subunits are assumed to have similar (in the idealized case, identical) properties and the mutual interactions between the units are assumed to be similar (identical) as well. Starting with explicit coupled oscillator models and applying phase reduction methods or alternative methods one can show that for weakly coupled oscillators with identical intrinsic evolution equations and identical coupling functions, relative phase dynamics exhibits reflectional symmetry ( Kuramoto, 1984 ).

However, if the intrinsic evolution equations and/or the coupling functions differ slightly in nature, then the violation of the symmetry will be characterized by a parameter that is of the magnitude of that violation. That is, the magnitude of symmetry breaking will be characterized by a small parameter (which may be identified by the detuning parameter that we will define below). In fact, research subsequent to the original work by  Haken et al. (1985)  showed that the assumed symmetry can be broken by various circumstances as reviewed in  Park and Turvey (2008) .

Out of all possible models that satisfy this condition, the model with the smallest number of independent coefficients (or the smallest number of eigenfunctions) is chosen starting with the coefficients of the eigenfunctions of low order (parsimony principle). 
                       There are two motivations for introducing the parsimony principle. First, the potential function is assumed to be bounded. Boundedness implies that the interactions of the coupled oscillatory units do not exhibit singularities. In the absence of singularities, the potential function can be decomposed into a Fourier series and the Fourier coefficients decay to zero with increasing order. Such decay renders the coefficients related to lower order Fourier modes more important than the coefficients related to higher harmonic modes. Second, nonlinearities in complex systems composed of interacting subunits may reflect interactions between units that evolve on a relatively fast time scale and can be eliminated by appropriate techniques (see e.g.,  Haken, 2004 ). For example, in the context of the derivation of amplitude equations of pattern formatting, complex systems close to bifurcation points, a quadratic (cubic) nonlinearity reflects the interaction between two (three) appropriately defined spatially extended modes (prototype patterns). Higher nonlinearities arise in the context of an even larger number of interacting modes. It seems to be plausible to assume that interactions between fewer modes are more relevant than interactions between many modes.

Our generalization of the HKB model is motivated by experimental work as discussed in the preceding section that challenged the original HKB model but can be explained in the context of our generalized framework. As already mentioned in the introduction, various modeling approaches have been developed for understanding rhythmic coordinated movements both on the behavioral level and the neural and cognitive level. Deterministic approaches such as a field theoretical model for neural brain activity, oscillator models for continuous rhythmic activity, and dynamic models for discrete and continuous repetitive movements have been proposed (for references see the introduction). Stochastic models that account for fluctuating forces acting on the relative phase dynamics ( Frank et al., 1999; Park et al., 2001; Schöner et al., 1986 ) have been proposed. In the context of developing probabilistic accounts for cognitive processes, we may also think of models that come with distributed parameters (e.g.,  Frank, Richardson, Lopresti-Goodman, & Turvey, 2009 ). How can we identify the model that most appropriately describes a given cognitive motor-related process? As mentioned in Section  1.3 , some of the mechanistic models will yield, on the behavioral level, rather similar evolution equations for the relative phase dynamics. Consequently, we will not be able to distinguish between them based on behavioral data. However, other types of data (e.g., brain activity recordings) might be useful for that purpose. Other models might predict different types of evolution equations for relative phase. For example, the original HKB model and the generalizations addressed in this study predict that the evolution of relative phase is subjected to forces that can be expressed in terms of sine and cosine functions. Other models have been examined that predict that the relevant forces correspond to exponential functions ( Brown et al., 2004 ). In this context, model predictions can be compared with behavioral experimental data in order to test which set of assumptions (set of Axioms as in our study) is consistent or inconsistent with experimental observations.
