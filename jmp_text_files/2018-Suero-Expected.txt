Note that both  Gourevitch & Galanter ’s (  1967 ) and  Miller ’s (  1996 ) procedures are appropriate for detection task data (yes–no). The variances of sensitivity indices with other experimental paradigms have different formulas, which have also been originally devised for single point estimates, and are suitable for samples of homogeneous observers. For example,  Bi (2002)  discusses the estimation of variance for same-different paradigms and offers a program to calculate the variance and tables with the components that allow obtaining an estimate within a range of values. Similarly,  Bi and Ennis (1998) ,  Bi, Ennis, and O’Mahony (1997)  and  Bi and O’Mahony (2013)  discuss the estimation of

Although the procedures described below are only intended for yes–no detection paradigms, its basis could be extended to cover estimations of the variance for heterogeneous individuals in those other paradigms.

However, in the homogeneous individuals situation there is a range of conditions in which the approximate method is almost as good as the exact method, but in the heterogeneous individuals situation this does not happens. In the latter situation the approximate method should be avoided as the estimates of the “true” variance with that method introduces considerable errors.

With the development of the approximation of  Gourevitch and Galanter (1967)  it reached a maximum of 29.3% and in the average it is around 10%. Sometimes the original formulas of  Gourevitch and Galanter (1967)  and  Miller (1996)  have been used to test hypotheses about  
                         δ 
                       or to construct confidence intervals, without acknowledging that one is not working with a single point or with a sample of individuals who do not necessarily share the parameters. If both components of the variance (between- and within-subjects) are not accounted for, then an underestimate of the variation and an overestimate of the precision of the population mean sensitivity can result  (Bi, 2005) . The assumption of homogeneity should be tested, which can be done by testing if the variance of the proportions of hits and false alarms is statistically compatible with that assumption. If there are cues that the heterogeneity assumption is more realistic, the formulas proposed here allow more accurate estimates of the variances of

Many efforts have been devoted to developing procedures for a variety of practical situations, but there are yet some waiting for a solution. We add to that body of research the procedures described here, developed from those of  Miller (1996)  and  Gourevitch and Galanter (1967) , for estimating the variance of
