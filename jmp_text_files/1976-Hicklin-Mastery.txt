JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 13, 79-88 (1976)  A Model for Mastery Learning Based on Dynamic Equilibrium Theory  WILLIAM 3AMEs HICKLIN  Department of Education, Washington State University, Pullman, Washington 99163  A theoretical model is developed based on dynamic equilibrium theory, which suggests a quantitative explanation for the basic  idea in mastery learning that individuals of different aptitude reacting in an ideal environment to the same total quantity of  material will ultimately reach the same mastery status, or gain equal increments of status starting from the same base, in  time spans inversely proportional to the aptitude. The approach incorporates two widely used mechanisms, one for growth in  learning and intelligence and one for loss through forgetting, into a single model that treats acquisition and loss as  opposing, but simultaneously occuring processes. Operational definitions for acquisition and loss rate constants that would  enable the model to account exactly for the basic assumption of mastery learning and at the same time yield a growth pattern  identical to that implicit in the Stanford Binet ratio IQ are identified. An analysis of the extent to which the requirements  of the model are confirmed by the available experimental evidence reveals that the basic idea of mastery learning is a useful  and convenient approximation, which applies with some validity to the growth of mental ability as well as to the growth in  school achievement.  Recent contributions of Bloom (1971), Block (1971), Caroll (1963), Bruner (1966), Glaser (1968), and Suppes (1966) have  stimulated a great deal of interest in the concept of learning for mastery. The basic idea in mastery learning is that in an  ideal environment individuals of different aptitude reacting to the same total quantity of material will ultimately reach the  same mastery status, or gain equal increments of status starting from the same base, in time spans that are inversely  proportional to the aptitude. This paper describes a quantitative theoretical model for this concept, examines the extent to  which the requirements of the model are confirmed by the experimental evidence, and attempts to show that the basic idea in  mastery learning is a convenient approximation that applies with some validity to the growth of mental ability as well as to  the growth of school achievement.  Dynamic equilibrium in intellectual growth (Hicklin et al., 1965) makes the assumption that the individual interacts with the  environment in such a manner as to experience, during time interval At, a gain of k1N1 units and a loss of k2N2 units. Environmentalism would require that the equilibrium constant remain invariant among individuals  of varying aptitude or IQ value, so that all would ultimately reach the same maximum status in an ideal environment. This is a  basic assumption of mastery learning. Nativism would require that the ultimate status attainable in an ideal environment would  depend on native ability (IQ). This model seems to account pretty well for data on the spread of grade equivalent scores which are noted in norms tables for  standardized tests. For example, on the Reading Test in the Iowa Test of Basic Skills, a spread of Zo in either direction  would encompass grade equivalent scores from 2.2 to 7.8 at the grade 5 level and from 4.2 to 11.8 at the grade 8 level. This  theoretical model and the calculations on which it is based also agree pretty well with the data obtained by the writer (1962)  in the 10-year longitudinal study on achievement at the University of Chicago Laboratory School. For example, a group of 75  students with a mean Stanford Binet IQ of 133 made average grade equivalent scores in reading on the Chicago Reading Tests of  7.0, 8.6, and 10.0, at grades 4.5, 5.5, and 6.5, respectively. Stanley and Hopkins (1972) report data  from the Iowa Test of Basic Skills Manual showing that the standard deviation for grade equivalent scores varies slightly with  the type of achievement involved but is (as we would anticipate) essentially a linear function fo the chronological age. For  spelling, at age 13 (Grade 8) the standard deviation in grade equivalent score units is given as 2.2; for vocabulary, 2.0; for  reading, 1.9; for language, 2.0; and for arithmetic, 1.7.  THE CASE III MODEL APPLIED TO MASTERY LEARNING  Either the Case III or the Case II model is compatible with Caroll's (1963) view that aptitude may be measured by the amount  of time required by the learner to attain mastery. If, for example, the three individuals with differing IQ's (132, 100, and  68) set out in the ideal environment to improve their reading grade equivalent scores from, say, from 3.0 to 6.0, the  calculated time spans required to do this are 2.27 years, 3.00 years, and 4.41 years, respectively-time spans which are  inversely proportional to the IQ's of the three individuals described. The Case III and the Case II models agree exactly with  this concept, and, as has been shown, either model provides a growth pattern known to be a familiar and convenient  approximation, the growth pattern implicit in the Stanford Binet ratio IQ.
