INTRODUCTION 

By Joel Michell 

This is a translation of Part II of Hoï¿½lder's paper. A translation of Part I (Michell 6 Ernst, 1996) was published earlier. Given the date of its original publication, Part II representsa contributiontomeasurementtheoryalmostequal in importance to a correct understanding of psychological measurement as that of Part I. If measurement is the assessment of the ratio of a magnitude of a quantity to a unit, as Hoï¿½lder, like all scientists or mathematicians from Euclid to the end of the nineteenth century, would have believed, then measurement theory contains three parts. First, the concept of magnitude of a quantity must be characterised in such a way that ratios of magnitudes, in something like Euclid's sense (Heath, 1908) can be defined. Hoï¿½lder achieved this in Part I of this paper with his axioms of quantity. Second, the relationship between ratios of magnitudes and real numbers must be explicated. Hoï¿½lder also achieved this in Part I with his central theorem. Third, ways in which quantity can be identified empirically must be explored, in particular, ways additional to extensive measurement. Hoï¿½lder pioneered a significant contribution to this enterprise in Part II. He showed how axioms for intervals within a straight line that contain no explicitly additive operation reduce to his axioms for quantity. As Krantz, Luce, Suppes, and Tversky (1971) make clear, Hoï¿½lder's axiomatisation is a special case of difference measurement and in their exposition they follow his treatment closely. 

What I have identified here as the third part of measurement theory proved to be a significant stumbling block in the development of quantitative psychology this century. As Brown and Thomson (1921, p. 12) noted, ``the real difference between mental measurement and physical measurement is simply that mental phenomena, being practically more difficult to handle, force to our notice the epistemological 

difficulties inherent in all measurement, whereas in physical measurement familiarity has bred contempt.'' Psychological attributes, being ``practically more difficult to handle,'' raise the issue of whether or not they are quantitative much more awkwardlythanis thecase inphysics.Thismeantthatpsychologists needed to explore indirect ways of testing for quantity, i.e., ways that do not rely upon explicitly additive operations. The single most important development in relation to this problem was the theory of conjoint measurement (Luce 6 Tukey, 1964). Prior to that, within the psychological literature, the study of difference systems made significant progress on this problem (e.g., see the discussion of difference systems in Suppes 6 Zinnes, 1963). However, this progress was not fast. 

In the English language literature on psychological measurement, the issue of ordering differences was first discussed by Russell (1903) without any recognition that it could provide a basis for quantification. Russell's remarks, however, stimulated Dawes Hicks (1913) which, in turn, prompted Brown and Thomson (1921) to include a discussion of the issue in their text. Without approaching it in the formal and rigorous way that Hoï¿½lder had done 20 years before, they could see that the ordering of differences was a possible route to quantification without grasping the much more fundamental point that it could provide a basis for testing the hypothesis that the attribute involved was quantitative. This failure proved crucial in the history of quantitative psychology. The British physicist and philosopher of science, N. R. Campbell, who did more than anyone to promote the thesis that there was no empirical evidence to support the hypothesis that psychological attributes are quantitative (see Ferguson et al., 1940), also saw that ordering differences in the manner suggested by Brown and Thomson was a ``theoretically possible'' method of measurement (Campbell, 1933). But, just like Brown and Thomson, he did not see that it also enables a test of the hypothesis that the relevant attribute is quantitative and, so, remained fixated upon the necessity of extensive measurement. It is at precisely this point that Hoï¿½lder's approach to the problem proves its commanding intellectual superiority. In identifying axioms sufficient for relations upon intervals (and, by

Interestingly, one of his axioms is not all that far removed from the key condition identified by Luce and Tukey (1964) for conjoint measurement, the double cancellation condition. If a, a$, b, b$, c, and c$ are points on a line such that a precedes b and b precedes c, a$ precedes b$, and b$, c$, then Hoï¿½lder's axiom ' amounts to the claim that if the ab interval equals the a$b$ interval and the bc interval equals the b$c$ interval then the ac interval must equal a$c$. If upper and lower points defining an interval are thought of as different attributes then it is as if intervals are an additive function of these two attributes and this axiom becomes simply an application of the Thomsen condition to this notional situation. As Krantz, Luce, Suppes, and Tversky (1971, p. 251) note, the Thomsen condition is ``a special case of double cancellation.'' Clearly, a knowledge of Hoï¿½lder's results could have advanced the standard of thinking within quantitative psychology in the first half of this century. 

Of course, its importance in this respect now is diminished by more recent work, especially that reported in the three volumes of the Foundations of Measurement (Krantz, et al., 1971; Suppes, Krantz, Luce 6 Tversky, 1989; and Luce, Krantz, Suppes 6 Tversky, 1990). Hoï¿½lder's paper remains an important resource for historians and philosophers interested in measurement, especially psychological measurement, but the kinds of mathematical results he obtained are now well known. However, my judgment in the Introduction to Michell and Ernst (1996) that there is nothing mathematically new in Hoï¿½lder's paper may be incorrect. Niedereï¿½e (personal communication) has suggestedthat some of Hoï¿½lder's methods of proof are still of interest to mathematicians. I leave it to those better qualified than myself to judge this assessment.
