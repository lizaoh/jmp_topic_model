In this paper we consider a notion, stochastic dominance, used in analysis of response times in Systems Factorial Technology (e.g.,  Townsend & Nozawa, 1995 ). We apply the notion to models primarily used in analysis of response accuracy, Multinomial Processing Trees. Both kinds of analyses find empirical evidence for experimental factors that selectively influence processes. We use this common finding of selective influence to link the two kinds of analyses. Speaking informally, a factor, e.g., brightness, selectively influences a process, e.g., perception, if the factor changes aspects of that process, leaving all else invariant. For a precise general formulation see  Dzhafarov (2003)  and  Dzhafarov and Kujala (2014)  and for a survey of applications, see  Schweickert, Fisher, and Sung (2012) .

Response time analysis through selective influence of factors has developed continually over the years since  Sternberg (1969)  pioneered it in a classic paper. For recent work see  Reimer, Strobach, and Schubert (2017)  and  Sung and Gordon (2018)  and for overviews see  Fific (2016)  and  Little, Altieri, Fific, and Yang (2017) . Response accuracy has played a minor role in this, primarily because in most experiments accuracy was deliberately kept near ceiling to simplify interpretation. On the other hand, starting with the earliest Multinomial Processing Tree (MPT) models in psychology, validity of an MPT model has been established by verifying that an experimental factor selectively influenced a parameter assigned to that factor by theory. For examples see  Batchelder (2017)  and  Jacoby, Begg, and Toth (1997) . Response time has played a minor role in this.

Most mental processes are unobservable, so evidence that an experimental factor selectively influences a process is based on observable consequences, for example, on the factor’s effect on the response time of the entire system to a stimulus. Initially, effects on means and variances were examined. Later, more stringent tests were developed based on response time cumulative distribution functions.

Sternberg (1973)  noted a testable consequence of a factor selectively influencing an unobservable process in such a way that the duration of the process at one level of the factor stochastically dominates the duration at another level. If the processes required for a task are in series, so response time is the sum of their durations, then the observable response time at one level of the factor stochastically dominates the observable response time at the other level of the factor. Further work showed that the processes required for a task need not be in series; a factor producing stochastic dominance of a process duration also produces stochastic dominance of response time if the processes are in parallel ( Townsend & Ashby, 1983 ) or in a critical path network ( Schweickert, Giorgini, & Dzhafarov, 2000 ). Although stochastic dominance is a rather strong relation ( Townsend & Ashby, 1983 ), a factor can easily produce it, by simply incrementing the duration of a process by a nonnegative random variable, an often reasonable assumption ( Schweickert & Townsend, 1989; Townsend & Schweickert, 1989 ). There is considerable evidence that a variety of experimental factors produce it for response times in a variety of cognitive tasks (e.g.,  Sternberg, 1973; Townsend & Ashby, 1983; Townsend & Nozawa, 1995 ).

A factor selectively influencing a process may change both its duration and its accuracy, but much investigation of response time CDFs does not consider the probability of a correct response. An exception is work on choice response time and on search. Sequential sampling models (see, e.g.,  Ratcliff, 1978; Smith, 2000; Vickers, 1979 ) predict both response time cumulative distribution functions and response probability. They account well for data, and parameters are validated by selective influence of factors on them. (See  Voss, Rothermund, & Voss, 2004 , for examples, and  Jones & Dzhafarov, 2014 , for a critique.) But the models focus on a particular mental process, the selection of one alternative from a set of possibilities. Other processes, such as perception and motor preparation are usually assumed to simply precede or follow the decision, all processes in series. Another exception, pertinent here, is work by  Townsend and Altieri (2012)  who considered the product of the correct response probability and the correct response time CDF. They incorporated this product in a measure of capacity, which has begun to be used in applications (e.g.,  Altieri, Townsend, & Wenger, 2014 ). This product arises in a natural way in Multinomial Processing Trees, as we will see.

For many years Multinomial Processing Trees have supplied a viable yet testable form of complex process organization. For reviews, see  Batchelder and Riefer (1999) ,  Erdfelder et al. (2009)  and  Hütter and Klauer (2016) . An example is in  Fig. 1 . A vertex in a Multinomial Processing Tree represents a mental process, such as memory retrieval or motor preparation. An arc descending from a vertex represents an outcome of the process, such as successful memory retrieval. A task begins with the process represented by the  source  vertex, a vertex preceded by no other vertex. (Some MPT models have more than one source, for example, a source for old items and a source for new items. Usually only one source is relevant in a particular circumstance, and the subtree associated with each source can be considered separately.) On a particular trial, a single outcome of the first process occurs, the arc representing the outcome is traversed, and the vertex at the end of the arc is reached. This vertex represents a further process, which is executed. Such steps continue until a vertex is reached that has no descending arcs. At such a  terminal vertex  a response is made. Responses fall into various classes, such as correct and incorrect. A particular terminal vertex is associated with only one class of response.

The probability a response in a particular class is made is the sum of the probabilities of the terminal vertices associated with the class.

A factor  selectively influences a vertex  in an MPT if it changes parameter values associated with arcs descending from that vertex, leaving all else invariant. One example is an MPT model of immediate serial recall by  Chechile (1977)  in which changing the phonological similarity of items changed a storage parameter, leaving all else invariant. Another example is a model of  Jacoby (1991)  underlying his Process Dissociation Procedure. In the model, participants recognize a word either by consciously recollecting it or by its high familiarity. These possibilities are represented in an MPT with probability  R  of successful recollection and, if that fails, with probability  F  of correct response based on familiarity. If a secondary task was carried out after items were studied, the secondary task decreased  R  but did not change  F  ( Jacoby, 1991 ). More examples of factor selective influence in MPTs are in  Schweickert et al. (2012) .

Results here are for the situation in which an experimental factor changes parameter values on arcs that descend from one and only one vertex. In some MPT models, vertices in several places in the tree represent the same process, occurring under different circumstances. For example, a participant may resort to guessing in several places in the tree. An experimental factor that changes the probability of a successful guess would then have effects at more than one vertex. This is contrary to our assumption of selective influence here. But note that sometimes the assumption that the same parameter occurs at several locations of a tree is made to reduce the number of parameters to be estimated, rather than for a substantive reason, so testing the selective influence of a factor is not useless. A paper by  Schweickert and Xi (2011)  considers factors having effects at multiple vertices and their effects on response probability. Work on effects on response time remains to be done.

Multinomial Processing Trees usually account for the probability of responses in various classes. Occasionally an additional measure is incorporated. In a decision tree, the additional measure is a gain or loss. In an MPT model of reaching, each arc produced an increment or decrement to a distance goal ( Rosenbaum, 1980 ). Although an additional measure could be any quantity, for ease of communication we usually call it time. Time was incorporated in MPT models by  Heck and Erdfelder (2016, 2017) ,  Hu (2001) ,  Klauer and Kellen (2018) ,  Link (1982) , and  Schweickert and Zheng (2018a, 2018b, in press) , all of whom considered selective influence of factors.

In  Hu ’s ( 2001 ) model when a vertex is reached, each arc descending from it has a probability of being selected and takes time to be selected. Hu assumed the probability of a path is the product of the probabilities of the arcs on it, and the time for a path is the sum of the times of the arcs on it. Hu derived equations for arbitrary MPTs for response probabilities and expected response times.

The MPT model of  Wollschläger and Diederich (2012)  is for preferential choice among  N  alternatives. For simplicity of explanation, suppose there are two alternatives to choose from  A  or  B . Processing begins at the source vertex. At the end of a time step of duration  h  one of four outcome occurs. The outcome is positive for  A , negative for  A , positive for  B  or negative for  B . When an outcome occurs, the end vertex of the outcome arc is reached. Processing begins at this end vertex, and at the end of another time step of duration  h , one of the same four possible outcomes occurs. As steps continue, positive and negative outcomes for each alternative are counted and a preference state is calculated from them. The steps are repeated until the preference state reaches a criterion specified by a stopping rule. The alternative favored by the preference state is then chosen. As in the model of  Hu (2001) , the probability of a path is the product of the probabilities of the arcs on the path and time for a path is the sum of the arc times; in this model, the time is a multiple of the step time  h . We have described a simple example, but various structures of the tree are possible. Depending on the stopping rule, the steps may continue indefinitely; that is, there may be no bound on the length of a path through the tree.

In recent work, the model of  Heck and Erdfelder (2016, 2017)  and  Heck, Erdfelder, and Kieslich (2018)  makes the usual assumption that the probability of a path is the product of the probabilities on the arcs on the path. Individual arcs do not have times associated with them, but when a terminal vertex is reached, a response time (or other measure) associated with it is produced. Response times are sorted into bins to estimate their density functions. The response time densities are decomposed, in a distribution free way, into a mixture of the times required to reach each terminal vertex.

In an application,  Heck and Erdfelder (2016)  compared several models for recognition memory. One, “Fast Detection”, assumes that a guess occurs after an unsuccessful detection attempt, and thus predicts that time required for detection is faster (stochastically dominates) time required for unsuccessful detection followed by a guess. To test for such stochastic dominance in their data they used an encompassing-prior Bayesian test developed by  Klugkist, Laudy, and Hoijtink (2005) . (For an evaluation of this test with simulated data, see  Heathcote, Brown, Wagenmakers, & Eidels, 2010 .) The test uses Bayes Factors to select a model from a set of models in which relevant arc times are constrained by stochastic dominance in some models and less constrained in others. In brief, large Bayes Factors favored stochastic dominance for detection faster than unsuccessful-detection-followed-by-guessing for targets. See their papers for further details.

Klauer and Kellen (2018)  also extended MPTs to include response times. In their model, each arc has a probability of being selected, given its starting vertex is reached. In addition, the time required to select an arc is a random variable. Some arcs represent outcomes of a decision, such as a detection or a guess. The time for such an arc is assumed to follow an exponential distribution. An arc preceding a terminal vertex is assumed to have a normal distribution, truncated to be nonnegative. Such an arc represents encoding together with response preparation (a time component sometimes called base time). As in the model of  Hu (2001) , the probability of a path is the product of the probabilities on the arcs of the path, and the time for a path is the sum of the times of the arcs on the path. The specific distribution assumptions make Klauer and Kellen’s model unlike the model of  Heck and Erdfelder (2016, 2017)  and  Heck et al. (2018) , in which the form of the response time distribution function (or other measure) is obtained from the data, through binning response times. The analysis of  Klauer and Kellen (2018)  is Bayesian and hierarchical, a general approach that could be used with alternative assumptions about the time distributions. Each participant may have different parameter values. A nice feature of their analysis is that after sets of parameter values are calculated for each participant, correlations between pairs of parameters over participants can be examined.

Klauer and Kellen (2018)  fit recognition data of experiments of  Arnold, Bröder, and Bayen (2015)  and of  Dube, Starns, Rotello, and Ratcliff (2012) . They tried two variants of the Two-High Threshold Multinomial Processing Tree model ( Snodgrass & Corwin, 1988 ). The main difference is in whether guessing precedes or follows detection (i.e., a memory retrieval attempt). Klauer and Keller point out that the two variants cannot be distinguished on the basis of response probabilities alone. But when response times were included in the analysis, the version less often used in the literature (guessing precedes memory retrieval attempt) was discriminated from the other and favored by the data. We note that recognition memory data were modeled by  Klauer and Kellen (2018)  and also by  Heck and Erdfelder (2016)  with MPTs that fit well, but somewhat differed in structure, a difference that remains to be resolved.

To use one of the approaches just described an investigator must know or assume a particular tree structure for an MPT model. The main advantage of our approach, Tree Inference, over others is that our results are true, under quite unrestrictive assumptions, for an MPT with arbitrary tree structure. Distribution free tests of whether two factors each selectively influence a vertex in an arbitrary MPT have been developed ( Schweickert & Chen, 2008; Schweickert & Zheng, 2018a ). So an investigator need not specify ahead of time the tree structure of an MPT model to be tested. A second advantage of our approach is that because the tests apply to an arbitrary MPT, if one of the tests fails the investigator knows, under quite unrestrictive assumptions, that there is no possible MPT in which the two factors selectively influence two vertices. The investigator can turn to other models, perhaps an MPT model in which the factors do not selectively influence vertices. A limitation of our approach is that it does not provide a method for analysis if factors do not selectively influence vertices, although a failed test may suggest directions. See the papers referred to for details.

This product was considered by  Townsend and Altieri (2012)  in another context. It arises again here in a natural way with Multinomial Processing Trees. As Townsend and Altieri point out, it is useful because it combines both response probability and time. Trying to combine  mean  correct response time and probability of a correct response into a single measure of performance is frustrating. A manipulation that improves both response time and accuracy makes one decrease and the other increase. A far more felicitous combination is the product of the  cumulative distribution function  of correct response time and the probability of a correct response. A manipulation that improves both response time and accuracy makes the pCDF increase.

Another use of weighted stochastic dominance is as an assumption made for a test for selective influence of factors ( Schweickert & Zheng, 2018a ) in the method of Tree Inference. Suppose an investigator manipulates two factors in an experiment and wants to determine whether the two factors selectively influence two different vertices in an MPT. There are only two ways the two vertices could be arranged in the MPT. There may be a path from one vertex to the other, and on this path is an arc whose parameters change when the level of one of the factors changes. Or there may be no such path. With the method of Tree Inference the investigator can test whether the two factors selectively influence two vertices in either of these arrangements. A nice feature of the method is that the investigator need not specify the structure of the MPT ahead of time, the method tests whether the factors selectively influence vertices in any possible MPT. Tests based on response probability were developed by  Schweickert and Chen (2008) . Inclusion of response time leads to further tests ( Schweickert & Zheng, 2018a ).

When response time is included, the test of the arrangement in which there is a path from one selectively influenced vertex to the other makes an assumption about weighted stochastic dominance. Results analogous to  Theorem 2  and  Corollary 3  were derived for processes in critical path networks by  Dzhafarov, Schweickert, and Sung (2004)  and  Schweickert et al. (2000) .

We have assumed so far that probabilities associated with arcs do not vary and that times associated with arcs are independent, but neither assumption is realistic. Arc probabilities may vary from trial to trial, and arc processing times may slow down or speed up together, depending on, say, how drowsy the subject is at the moment. We loosen our restrictions in two phases. The first is to allow dependencies via a common random variable, following a useful conception of selective influence by  Dzhafarov (2003) . The second is to allow different MPTs to be used on each trial.

We begin phase one with an example of how parameters may be dependent, in a simulated experiment in which a factor selectively influenced the source vertex of the MPT in  Fig. 1 . Later we show in theorems that results of the simulation are true in general. In the simulation, for each of the two levels of the factor, 1000 trials were run. The probabilities with which arcs were selected varied over trials. Further, the probabilities were not stochastically independent of the times to select them and the times were not independent of each other.

To test whether the heights of the two curves were significantly different, the height differences were collected into five bins and a bootstrap 95% confidence interval was formed for each bin. Parameter values in the simulation were correlated with one another. If two parameters are correlated, it is not obvious how changing the level of a factor could change values of one parameter without changing values of the other.  Dzhafarov (2003)  proposed a solution, a definition of selective influence in which statistical dependencies between parameter values occur because of functional dependences on a common random variable whose distribution does not change when the level of the factor changes. The definition of  Dzhafarov (2003)  follows, modified to apply to a Multinomial Processing Tree. The factor in the simulation selectively influenced the source vertex of the MPT in  Fig. 1  in accord with this definition.

It is straightforward to assume, as we did, that the times associated with different arcs are conditionally mutually independent, given a value  u  of the common random variable  U . But it is not possible to make the analogous assumption about the probabilities associated with arcs. Neither independence nor conditional independence is possible, because the sum of the probabilities associated with arcs descending from each vertex is 1. If one such probability increases another must decrease.

In the second phase of loosening our restrictions, we enlarge the possibilities for arc probabilities by noting that a subject may use different MPTs on different trials. Two of the MPTs used might have the same structure, but differ in the parameter values associated with their arcs. Or two MPTs might have quite different structures. We allow that on each trial an MPT is selected at random for use by the subject. As required, in each MPT the probabilities associated with all the arcs descending from the same vertex sum to 1.

Suppose for level 1 of the factor there is a finite set of MPTs, and every MPT has an index  z . For a given index  z , the MPT for level 2 of the factor is the same as that for level 1 of the factor, except that at one particular vertex parameters on arcs descending from that vertex have different values at level 2 than at level 1; that is, the factor selectively influences a single vertex in MPT z. Now suppose there is a random variable  Z  that takes on values from the set of indices, and the distribution of  Z  does not depend on the level of the factor. This assumption allows the structures and parameter values of the MPTs to vary from trial to trial.

                         φ

Multinomial Processing Trees, already widely used as models of psychological phenomena, have potential to become more useful if they account for response times. A nice consequence of extending MPTs to response times is that they then inherit a wealth of techniques developed for analyzing response times in other contexts. Here we consider the technique of manipulating a factor that selectively influences a process. This is a well established tool for validating MPT models that account for response probability, and also a well established tool in various response time analyses, including Systems Factorial Technology. Empirically, changing the level of a factor selectively influencing a process often produces a relation on response time distributions measured at various levels, stochastic dominance. We apply a modified version of the relation to response times generated by MPT models. The modification, which we call weighted stochastic dominance, is of interest in its own right because it combines both response time and response probability in a natural way. The relation can be easily checked graphically. At each level of a factor, say, brightness, the response time cumulative distribution function for a response of a certain class, say correct, is multiplied by the probability of a response in that class. If in a plot, the product for one level of the factor is never below that for another level, then the first level has weighted stochastic dominance over the second. Our simulation gives an example. We consider a factor that changes parameters at a single vertex of an MPT, producing weighted stochastic dominance for the arc probability and time of every arc descending from the vertex that leads to a response of class  
                         k 
                      . We show that the factor then produces weighted stochastic dominance for the probability and time of responses in class  
                         k 
                       produced by the entire MPT. Further, if the factor changes parameters in this way at a single vertex of every MPT of a mixture of MPTs, then the factor produces weighted stochastic dominance for the probability and time of responses in class  
                         k 
                       produced by the mixture of MPTs.
