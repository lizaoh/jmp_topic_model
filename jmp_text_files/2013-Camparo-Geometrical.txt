In this work, we develop a theory for the ordinal data of Likert scales, or more technically summated-rating scales ( Spector, 1992 ), using the language of abstract linear vector spaces. The motivation for this effort follows from the fact that while attitudes are often operationalized in terms of ordinal responses, the “traditional” means-analysis of the data does not always capture the full subtlety of subjects’ attitudes. Specifically, with Likert scales subjects are asked to respond (for example) strongly-disagree, disagree → strongly-agree to questionnaire items. Then, after summing responses from a number of questionnaire items a mean-response for the items’ factor is determined, and subjects’ attitudes are differentiated based on this mean response. (Obviously, to generate a mean value an ordinal-to-interval scale transformation must be performed, a topic we will return to below.) In point of fact, however, each subject’s set of responses generates a probability distribution on the ordinal scale, so that by concentrating solely on the subject’s mean response researchers only differentiate among subjects based on the lowest non-trivial moment of this probability distribution. Higher order moments will certainly contain useful and valid psychological/sociological information so long as they can be assessed in an intuitively meaningful manner ( Peabody, 1962 ). Here, we discuss how a vector-space approach to ordinal data can offer that more detailed view of subjects’ attitudes.

Of course, Item Response Theory (IRT) also goes beyond the simple analysis of means, and has received much attention as an alternative to the traditional method of Likert-scale analysis ( Andrich, 1978; Harwell & Gatti, 2001; Koch, 1983 ). In this regard, the vector-space methodology should be seen as an adjunct to IRT: whereas IRT focuses on differences among multiple questionnaire items, looking to grade those items in terms of “difficulty” and “discrimination” parameters, the vector-space approach views the items collectively in order to assess a subject’s location within a multi-dimensional attitude space.

In the present work, following an emerging trend in psychometrics and mathematical psychology ( Bruza, Busemeyer, & Gabora, 2009 ), we borrow from the quantum physicist’s mathematical toolbox. Specifically, in quantum physics the physical state of a particle (e.g., an atom) is described using the mathematics of linear vector spaces, and the physicist has developed sophisticated techniques for quantifying the probability that an atom (drawn from a sample) will be found in some particular atomic state. Though we will not digress into a discussion of quantum physics here, we note that at a purely formal mathematical level there is a one-to-one correspondence between categorical data and the physical states an atom can occupy. Consequently, the same mathematics that the physicist employs to describe the probability distribution of atoms among quantum states can be applied to describe the probability distribution of a subject’s responses to questionnaire items. We want to make it very clear, however, that we are  not  saying that subjects’ attitudes have some “mystic” quantum nature. Rather, we are simply saying that the mathematics of linear vector spaces, which physicists have developed and exploited to great benefit for their description of quantum particles,

In this section we want to forgo too rigorous mathematics, and concentrate instead on a conceptual discussion of the vector-space description of ordinal data and attitude measurements. In particular, we will imagine that we have a sample of subjects whose attitude regarding some psychological or sociological construct (e.g., affirmative action) is under consideration, and that we have a questionnaire that we are employing to access the subjects’ attitudes. Further, we will assume that we are dealing with a single attitudinal factor.

As illustrated in  Fig. 1 
                       for the specific case of a 3-point Likert scale, we represent any one of our subjects’ attitudes prior to responding to one particular questionnaire item as a point in an abstract space of attitudes; the number of dimensions in this abstract space equals the number of points on the Likert scale, which is easy to draw and conceptualize for dimensionality,  
                         N 
                      , equal to 3. (For brevity, we will refer to this abstract mathematical space as an  attitude space .) More specifically, our subject’s  “a priori”  attitude can be represented by a directed line connecting the origin of the attitude space coordinate system to the subject’s attitude point; we will call this directed line the subject’s  attitude-state vector  or  attitude state . We say that this is an  a priori  attitude, since we consider it as the attitude the subject “brings” to the questionnaire item (constrained by the item’s response categories), as opposed to the attitude state that is eventually recorded (i.e., the subject’s actual  response  to the questionnaire item); we will call the subject’s actual response the subject’s  a posteriori  attitude state.

The distinction between  a priori  and  a posteriori  attitude states is necessary, because we cannot ignore the effect of the questionnaire item on the subject and what is eventually recorded. Though the subject’s  a priori  attitude-state vector may not necessarily align with any particular attitude-space axis (for example, the subject’s attitude may be a superposition of the agree and neutral categories), if we ask the subject to pick only one categorical response, then the questionnaire item will have forced the subject’s “response-attitude” to collapse to a single attitudinal category. Using the geometrical language that we associate with  Fig. 1 , we would say that the subject’s response to the particular questionnaire item (i.e., the subject’s  a posteriori  attitude state) corresponds to the projection of the subject’s  a priori  attitude-state vector along one of the coordinate axes.

Pursuing this projection idea further, let us for the moment consider a subject whose  a prior  attitude-state is the same from item to time, and which corresponds to a superposition of the neutral and agree categories. Further, let us imagine that we have instructed the subject to pick only one response category for each questionnaire item. As noted above, in the vector-space description we see the act of measurement (i.e., responding to the item) as projecting the subject’s nuanced attitude state onto different axes. Obviously, as the subject responds to different items we expect different projections of the  a priori  attitude-state vector to manifest themselves: sometimes the  a posteriori  attitude-state will correspond to the neutral axis and sometimes to the agree axis. Nevertheless, if the subject’s  a priori  attitude-state vector aligns most closely with the positive-attitude axis of “agree”, then we expect the subject to respond most often with that positive attitude response. Consequently, we interpret the magnitude of the attitude-state vector’s projection onto any one of the coordinate axes as a measure of the probability that the subject will respond with that attitudinal category to a questionnaire item.

Though it may appear that this talk of vectors and “projections onto axes” obfuscates what is in reality a simple measurement process. In point of fact just the opposite is true: the measurement of attitudes using the ordinal data of Likert scales is anything but simple, and the vector space approach highlights the complications. As is well appreciated among researchers, subjects’ attitudes are often nuanced, conforming more closely to a continuum than a set of discrete attitudinal categories (e.g., disagree, agree, neutral). In the vector-space approach, the fact that subjects’ attitudes are constrained to a quantized scale is built into the mathematical structure of the analysis in a natural way: a subject’s  measured  attitude (obtained from a subject’s full set of responses to questionnaire items) exists as a statistical distribution of points in a finite-dimensional attitude space, whose dimensionality reflects the attitude-measurement scale’s degree of quantization (i.e., discretization). Additionally, as researchers know well, the response to a questioned item always forces the subject to make a choice. This is clearly manifested in the vector-space approach, since we describe the measurement process as projecting a subject’s  a priori  attitude-state vector onto the attitude space’s coordinate axes, yielding the subject’s  a posteriori  attitude-state vector.

Consequently, if at times the notation appears outside the norm of standard linear algebra texts, and perhaps a bit cumbersome, we hope the reader will remember that highlighting the ordinal-data/quantum-physics connection is our motivation.

The density matrix, however, is much more than a tool for computing a mean value of ordered data; it can be employed to quantify subjects’ responses in new and novel ways. Recently,  Camparo and Camparo (2013)  discussed how the density matrix can be decomposed into a set of orthogonal “state multipoles”, and that using this decomposition an individual’s Likert-scale responses can be assessed in terms of their symmetry about the central category. As an illustration of the psychological/sociological utility of their approach, Camparo and Camparo were able to show that African–Americans display a level of polarization in their endorsement of segregation as a cause of prejudice that is not reflected by European–Americans. We should note, however, that Camparo and Camparo’s work was limited to  diagonal  density matrices, and therefore did not place the density matrix in its broader context nor consider its wider applicability to psychometrics.

So far, we have defined the density matrix in terms of a subject’s set of  a priori  attitude-state vectors (i.e., the attitude states that the subject “brings” to the questionnaire items). However, we have gone to great pains to distinguish these  a priori  attitude-state vectors from what actually gets measured (i.e., the  a posteriori  attitude-state vectors). Specifically, in our geometrical language each questionnaire item projects a subject’s  a priori  attitude-state vector onto the attitude-space coordinate axes, and it is these projections that we measure. In physics, this projection process is known as “the collapse of the wavefunction”, and we will take advantage of physicists’ thinking about this process to connect our psychological  a prior  and  a posteriori  attitude-state vectors.

The collapse of the wavefunction represents what physicists call “the measurement problem” in quantum physics ( Wigner, 1963 ), and it is still unresolved ( Baggott, 1995; Cushing, 1998 ). To illustrate the nature of this problem with regard to attitude measurements, consider a subject who is in a mixed  a priori  attitude-state composed of the strongly-agree and agree basis states. If we ask the subject to mark only one response per questionnaire item, by what mechanism does the subject choose “agree” (for example) as opposed to “strongly-agree” for that particular item; by what process does the subject’s  a priori  attitude state actually “collapse” to just agree at the moment he or she responds to the item? Though one might answer this question by postulating the existence of a latent factor that affects the subject’s thinking, it could very well be that there is no latent factor.

In recent years, it has become apparent that “quantum-probability” methods of analysis hold a distinct advantage over more classical statistical approaches for addressing thorny problems in psychology and psychometrics. For example,  Pothos and Busemeyer (2009)  have shown that violations of “rational” decision theory (e.g., the “sure thing” principle and the prisoner’s dilemma) arise quite naturally in quantum-probability models. Along similar lines,  Busemeyer, Pothos, Franco, and Trueblood (2011)  have shown that by modeling cognitive processes in terms that  mimic  quantum-probability rules, one should expect errors in probability judgments (e.g., the probability that “Linda is a feminist” will be judged more likely than the probability that “Linda is a feminist or a bank teller”). In other work,  Wang and Busemeyer (submitted for publication)  have found that order-effects in survey research are easily explained when quantum-probability models are employed, and that these models allow researchers to make new predictions regarding order-effects.

Before closing, it is worth considering how the vector-space approach to Likert scaling sheds light on the infamous ordinal-to-interval scale transformation problem. This is a thorny issue that has engendered considerable debate in the psychometrics community for over half a century, and is still unresolved ( Gardner, 1975; Knapp, 1990; Lord, 1953; Scholten & Borsboom, 2009; Spector, 1980; Stevens, 1968 ). Briefly, in the traditional view of Likert scaling one thinks of the ordered responses (e.g., strongly-disagree, disagree, …) as discrete attitude markers along a continuum of attitudes. While critics argue that there is no reason to expect the psychological distance between (for example) strongly-disagree and disagree to equal the psychological distance between agree and strongly-agree, proponents counter that this often has little consequence for generating valid statistical conclusions ( Spector, 1980 ).

For the most conservative critics, the ordinal-to-interval transformation is not simply a problem of psychological uncertainty. Instead, for these critics the ordinal-to-interval transformation renders the results of parametric statistical procedures  invalid  for Likert scale data:  it is mathematically illegitimate to assign numerical values to ordered categories . For example, one will read that “re-coded nominal and ordinal data ( e.g., very bad, bad, good, very good ) are not numeric and should not be analyzed as numeric values” ( Jakobsson, 2004 , p. 437), and that “descriptive statistical measures such as mean and SD ( standard deviation ), which require addition of observations, are invalid whenever data are on ordinal scales” ( Forrest & Andersen, 1986 , p. 538). The rationale for this viewpoint derives from  Stevens (1946) , who argued that scales of measurement restrict the allowed types of statistical analyses that can be employed with the data: “the appropriateness of a statistic is  invariance  under the transformations” associated with the mathematical group structure of the measurement scale (p. 678). Though some would argue that “Stevens’ rules should not be applied mindlessly when choosing a statistical test, but that each situation should be considered anew” ( Scholten & Borsboom, 2009 , p. 74), one nonetheless finds statements in the literature to the effect that “the average of ‘fair’ and ‘good’ is not ‘fair-and-a-half”’ ( Jamieson, 2004 , p. 1218). Thus the debate over ordinal-to-interval scale transformations continues.

As mentioned above, in the traditional view of Likert scaling the ordinal-to-interval scale transformation problem is viewed as a psychological  metric  problem: the ordered responses are viewed as markers along a “line”, with critics arguing that there is no valid method of assigning a distance scale to that line. (Saying this more technically, critics would argue that the “attitude-line” exists in a space without a metric, and therefore distances along the line have no meaning.) We will refer to this traditional view as the “metric approach” to Likert scaling. In the vector-space approach, the ordinal-to-interval scale transformation problem becomes a question of psychological  magnitude : how are we to assign a psychological “strength” to agree that can be compared to the psychological strength we assign to strongly-agree? The answer to that question depends on the scoring operator’s eigenvalues,

In one sense, then, the uncertainty in the relationship between strongly-disagree/disagree and strongly-agree/agree that arises in the metric approach to Likert scaling also arises in the vector-space approach: in the metric approach the researcher does not know how to assign psychological distances; in the vector-space approach the researcher does not know the mathematical form of

In the metric approach, it is mathematically  illegitimate  to discuss distances between ordered categories, because the space in which the attitude-line is embedded has no metric. In the vector-space approach, there is  no mathematical illegitimacy , there is simply a (mathematically  legitimate ) uncertainty in the form of an operator’s eigenvalues.

One immediate consequence of our vector-space approach to attitude measurements is that it allowed us (for the first time) to distinguish between mixed and coherence attitude states. Speaking somewhat loosely, a mixed attitude state corresponds to an uncorrelated mixture of potential categorical responses to a questionnaire item. For example, while a subject’s true  a priori  attitude towards item  
                         μ 
                       may correspond to a combination of disagree and agree categories, the possibility of responding disagree is uncorrelated with the possibility of responding agree. Alternatively, for a coherence attitude state every time a subject marks (for example) disagree to item  
                         μ 
                      , they will also want to mark agree to item  
                         μ 
                      . As discussed in the text, mixed attitude states manifest themselves in questionnaire data as single category responses to individual questionnaire items, while coherence attitude states manifest themselves as multi-category responses to individual questionnaire items.

Since a questionnaire is typically formed from a battery of (single-factor) items, the vector space approach to Likert-scaling leads to a battery of  a posteriori  response states for a subject. These are combined into a density matrix,  
                         ρ 
                      , which describes the statistical distribution of categorical responses. Here, we showed how the density matrix could be used to compute mean values for a population, and in future work we will discuss the use of the density matrix for parametric statistical analysis. Additionally, we showed how the density matrix could be exploited to calculate the information entropy of attitude-state vectors,  
                         
                            
                               S 
                            
                            
                               I 
                            
                         
                      , and how this parameter might be employed to investigate order-effects in the behavioral sciences.

Finally, we showed how the vector-space approach to Likert scaling provides a fresh perspective on the ordinal-to-interval scale transformation problem. In the vector-space approach, numerical values are assigned to ordinal data through a scoring operator, which essentially assigns an attitudinal  magnitude  to each ordered category. This is very different from a metric approach to Likert scaling, where the ordered categories are viewed as markers along a continuum for which there is no underlying “distance” scale.

What we have discussed in this paper only scratches the surface of what may be gained with a geometrical perspective on Likert scaling and attitude measurements. In future work, we hope to use the vector-space approach to demonstrate the existence of coherence attitude states in real populations, and we would eventually like to examine the possibility of a vector-space approach to categorical data in general. Regardless of the eventual outcome of these future plans, we believe that the vector-space approach to the ordinal data of Likert scaling and attitude measurements has much to commend it, and that researchers will find it advantageous in their investigations.
