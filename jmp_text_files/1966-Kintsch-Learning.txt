A group of experimental items was shifted from one retention interval to  the other after three or four presentations. The shift from short to long presentation interval retarded performance while  items shifted from a long to a short retention interval did not differ from control items. A Markov model was applied to the  data. The theoretical objectives were (a) to separate the roles of forgetting and learning, and (b) to determine whether the  learning process may be viewed as a simple conditioning process or whether the subject employs different recognition criteria  after varying retention intervals.  In a recognition learning experiment subjects respond with either "new" or "old" whenever an item is presented. The sequence  of responses which is thus generated for each item can be described adequately by a simple two-state Markov process (Kintsch  and Morris, 1965). Responses on the first presentation of an item are handled separately by assuming a constant false-alarm rate g. Such a  two-state model was first used by Bower (1961) to describe the acquisition of paired-associates. This  definition has proved to be sufficient for many purposes. It is, however, incomplete. It does not specify how a physical  stimulus and the corresponding pattern are related. At least two quite different explanations are possible here. The first  might be called a simple conditioning viewpoint: the physical stimulus determines a pattern uniquely, at least for a given  subject in a given experiment. Whenever the stimulus is presented under constant conditions the subject will respond to the  same pattern, which will eventually become conditioned to whatever response is being reinforced. An alternate possibility is  that subjects actively construct patterns, given the physical stimulus. A physical stimulus does not determine, by itself,  what pattern the subject will construct. Recognition learning, then, would be the construction of a pattern for each stimulus  item. The subject might construct poor patterns, i.e., patterns consisting of very few discriminative features of the  stimulus, or of irrelevant features. On the other hand, a subject might construct a very elaborate pattern which contains all  or most of the information in the stimulus. If the experimental task which the subject is asked to solve is a very easy one we  can expect the subject to construct at least some poor patterns. If the task is demanding, such patterns will be useless, and  on a later trial the subject will have to construct a better pattern.  An important parameter of recognition learning is the interval between the presentation of an item and its test. A number of  recent studies of short-term memory have shown that a correct response can almost always be given immediately after the  presentation of an item. If one or more items intervene between presentation and test the probability of a correct response  decreases sharply as a function of the number of intervening items. Both recall (e.g., Murdock, 1963a) and recognition  procedures (Shepard and Teghtsoonian, 1961) provide similar results in this respect. Murdock (1963b) has therefore suggested  that the role of item repetition in verbal learning might be to increase the resistance to forgetting between trials. The  Markov model of Kintsch and Morris distinguishes two levels of resistance to forgetting. Items are either in the learning  state, where no forgetting occurs, or in the initial state. The probability p of a correct identification in the initial state  is a function of the length of the retention interval. Right after a presentation even items which have not been learned  permanently will nevertheless be identified correctly, but as the retention interval increases, the recognition of unlearned  items becomes more and more unlikely.  Of particular interest as far as variations in the retention interval are concerned is the question whether or not the rate of  entry into the learning state is dependent upon such variations. The two interpretations of the model discussed above imply  different outcomes in this respect. According to the conditioning interpretation the probability of entry into the absorbing  state, i.e. of conditioning, should depend upon variables effective at the time when an item is presented. Such variables  might be intralist similarity, proactive interference or the complexity of the pattern to be conditioned.  414 KINTSCH  The length of the retention interval, on the other hand, is a performance variable, affecting the amount of forgetting which  occurs between the presentation and the test of an item. The crucial point here is that the complexity of the pattern which is  to be continioned is independent of the demands of the task and uniquely determined by the physical stimulus. Hence,  conditioning rates are not related to the retention interval. If learning consists in the construction of patterns of varying  complexity, what constitutes a learning state will depend upon the level of task difficulty. For a long retention interval  patterns must be sufficiently complex if forgetting is to be prevented. The learning rate will be lower in this case than if  the retention interval is very short. In the latter case it is much easier to construct suitable patterns, since almost any  feature of the stimulus will be sufficient for retention over a very brief interval. MATHEMATICAL FORMULATION  It is often useful to distinguish two substates of the initial state in Eq. 1, depending upon whether an unlearned item is  forgotten during the retention interval or not. If an item is forgotten it will be trated like a new item, which, however,  does not mean that it will always be called "new." There exists a small probability g that a new item will be classified as  old, corresponding to the false-alarm rate in detection experiments. If an (unlearned) item is retained in temporary memory  during the retention interval it will, of course, always be called "old." This temporary memory state will be called the  short-term memory state to distinguish it from the learning state or long-term memory state. The proportionality assumption of the present model implies a reduction in the number of parameters. Greeno and Steiner (1964)  have shown that an empirical test of the adequacy of the proportionality assumption is possible. Matrix Q, which has three  unobservable states, can be rewritten as a matrix with three identifiable states: successes which are followed by at least one  error, errors and terminal successes. The proportionality assumption is then equivalent to asserting that the probability of  an error after an error is the same as the probability of an error after a success for all trials before the last error. A  chi-square test can be employed to test this assertion. This test is equivalent to the test for independence of trials before  the last error (Suppes and Ginsberg, 1963). After having specified the formal properties of the models under consideration, it is now possible to clarify further the  difference between pattern construction and pattern conditioning. Note that this problem is quite independent from the  considerations which led to the three models Q(1), Q(2), and Q(3). Either interpretation can coexist with each model.  According to both interpretations recognition learning is seen as the storage of a pattern corresponding to a physical  stimulus in a semipermanent location. Patterns differ on a scale of complexity, which may be inferred because the learning  rate for some items is higher than for others. The term pattern is used here, instead of some other designation such as cue,  in order to draw attention to the discrete nature of the learning process. Recognition, implies that a subject matches a  stimulus input with a stored pattern and responds with "old." Suppose that the  418 KINTSCH  subject only responds with "old" when the stored pattern is of a certain complexity, say of a complexity greater than some  cut-off value on a complexity scale. Otherwise the subject will treat the stimulus as new. In that case, the subject might  still say "old," either because information about the stimulus is available in short-term memory, or simply because he makes a  false alarm response. The three models Q(1), Q(2), and Q(3) determine the details of this process, as well as the manner in  which a pattern can enter into long-term memory. The pattern conditioning versus construction problem concerns the existence  and value of the cut-off point on the complexity scale above which patterns are accepted as "old." For the conditioning  viewpoint this concept is superfluous, since the cut-off point is fixed for the conditions of any given experiment. The  cut-off point, and hence the learning rate, depends upon such objective factors as kind of learning material, experimental  conditions and capacity of the subject. Patterns, i.e., sufficiently complex patterns, are conditioned with a certain  probability to the "old" response.  According to the pattern construction hypothesis subjects construct and store patterns of varying complexity. If a subject is  asked whether he recognizes a particular stimulus he searches through his memory store for a matching pattern. If he finds one  he will respond positively only if the pattern is of a complexity greater than some critical value. This critical value  depends upon the subject's decision and may be altered by the subject in accordance with pay-off conditions, instructions, or  the retention interval. Therefore subjects will treat as learned items for which only poor patterns are available after a  short retention interval (weak criterion), while after a long retention interval (strong criterion) a pattern of the same  complexity would not be acceptable. Hence the learning rates depend upon the criterion of complexity adopted by the subject.  The learning rate may be interpreted as the probability that an adequate pattern, i.e., one of a complexity greater than some  critical value, will be constructed.  As indicated by the preceding discussion, the present paper is concerned with two theoretical objectives: first, the  distinction between pattern construction and pattern conditioning; and second, the separation of the roles of forgetting and  learning by means of the models derived above. The first experiment to be reported is primarily concerned with the latter  problem, while the shift-experiments were designed to answer the question whether patterns are constructed or conditioned.  However, it is not possible to keep the two problems neatly separated at all times. METHOD  Subjects. Undergraduate students from the University of Missouri served as subjects as part of a course requirement. Three  lists of numbers were given to three groups of 30 subjects each. A list of nonsense syllables was given to 26 subjects.  Learning materials and procedure. Four lists of stimulus material were prepared, three consisting of randomly selected  four-place numbers and one consisting of high association value nonsense syllables (CVC). Each list consisted of 20  experimental items which were repeated six times and 80 dummy items which were shown only once. For one half othe  experimental items successive presentations were separated by one other item (short-delay items). The other half of the  experimental items (long-delay items) were shown again after 10 intervening items. The subject's task was to recognize each  item as old or new. Ten additional items were included as a warm-up task and were disregarded in the analysis.  Items were typed on 3 x 5-inch index cards and presented manually from behind a screen for approximately 2 seconds.  Presentation rate was increased to 1 i seconds for the syllable material.  RESULTS  The decrease in the proportion of incorrect responses as a function of repetition was significantly slower for the long-delay  items than for the short-delay items. Melton (1963) observed similar results in his study of the effects of repetition upon  the recall of nine-digit numbers. Long-delay numbers were learned much more slowly than long-delay syllables, while little  difference was observed between the short-delay numbers and syllables.  The data have a number of special features which can not be accounted for by the present model without undue complications.  First of all, the false-alarm rate is not constant in experiments of this type but increases somewhat during the course of an  experimental session (Shepard and Teghtsoonian, 1961). Secondly, the amount of interference exerted by different intervening  items may not have been equal in all cases. Lists were constructed in such a way that the items which filled any given  presentation-test interval were partly dummies and partly other experimental items. The dummies were always new items while  some of the experimental items were already well practiced. Greeno and Hull (1965) have shown that items at various stages of  learning seem to have different interference effects. Since both of these factors will be overlooked in the present analysis,  the model can not be expected to provide more than an adequate approximation of the data. Another prediction made by the present class of models concerns the stationarity of the proportion of "new" responses on  trials before the last failure of recognition. All models make this prediction after the first trial, as can be seen  immediately when the models are rewritten in two-state form. Kintsch and Morris (1965) have found stationarity in a  recognition experiment where subjects learned a list of fixed length. Stationarity was not observed in the present experiment.  When items between the first trial and the last error were Vincentized a decrease in the proportion of errors from the first  to the second half of these trials was observed in seven of the eight cases. The failure to obtain stationarity can not be  considered crucial. The model fits many other aspects of the data, and, although its failure in the present instance might be  due to a serious shortcoming, it might also have quite an innocuous explanation. Consider, for instance, the possibility that  the probability of a correct response in the learning state was not 1 but slightly less. This would have very little effect  upon most statistics, but would be sufficient to destroy stationarity. Inspection of the data and observation of the subjects  indicate that something like this may have been encouraged by the present procedure of presenting stimulus material without  interruption. Occasionally subjects seemed to have lapses of attention. Sometimes subjects noticed this immediately and  corrected themselves. The occurrence of blocks in vigilance tasks of this sort has been well documented by Broadbent (1958).  Summarizing the present results, three conclusions seem particularly important. First, the class of models considered here  provides a good account of recognition data as measured by a chi-square test for goodness-of-fit. Secondly, the model  permitting learning on all trials or from state S only, but not the model which allows learning  RETENTION INTERVAL IN RECOGNITION LEARNING 423  in the initial state only, lead to parameter estimates which are consistent with the experimental procedures. Thirdly, an  ambiguity remains. DISCUSSION  Performance on items shifted from long delay to short delay is not significantly different from performance on short-delay  control items. When items are shifted from short to long delay, on the other hand, performance is inferior when compared with  performance on the long-delay control items. This finding, that less is learned with massed practice than with spaced  practice, is similar to results obtained by Peterson, Hillner, and Saltzman (1962) and Greeno (1964). In these studies  paired-associate learning was found to be superior under spaced than under massed conditions if  RETENTION INTERVAL IN RECOGNITION LEARNING 431  appropriate tests were employed. The present results are consistent with the hypothesis that subjects learn simpler patterns  under massed training conditions and therefore learn less. The notion that what the subject learns is dependent upon the  requirements of the particular learning task has also been utilized in the computer program which Simon and Feigenbaum (1964)  used to simulate the verbal learning process.  The observed asymmetry in the shift results precludes the possibility that any of the models considered here can account for  the results while retaining the equal-learningrate assumption. If the learning rates for short- and long-delay items are  allowed to be different the shift results can be predicted with fair accuracy. The minimum chi-square estimates of the  parameter values for the simple two-state model as well as the derived estimates for the more complex forms of the model are  presented in Table 4. The predictions of the three models are shown in Table 6 together with the observed proportion of errors  after the short-to-long shift. The predictions of the LS-2 model (Q(1)) are as good as, or better than, the predictions of the  other two models in all cases. The finding that the assumption of equal learning rates can not account for the data implies that subjects employ different  criteria when identifying stimuli after varying retention intervals. Little related theoretical work has been found in the  literature. In a study of word-recognition Stowe, Harris, and Hampton (1963) observed that subjects vary the number of  dimensions on which a discrimination must be made before correctly identifying the stimulus. In their study the criterion  depended upon variables such as constraint of sentence information. In order to emphasize the discreteness of the learning  process, pattern complexity is used as a criterion in the present study instead of number of dimensions as in Stowe et al.  (1963). In this respect the pattern construction hypothesis is quite similar to Restle's theory for selection of cues (1962).  Restle's theory corresponds to Q(3) together with the pattern construction hypothesis.  Altogether, the performance of the recognition model is quite satisfactory. It describes recognition learning data well with a  minimum of assumptions and theoretical superstructure. It relates in a simple and meaningful way different aspects of the  recognition process, such as false-alarm rate, short-term forgetting and learning rate proper. Its deficiencies, such as its  failure to account for the drift in the falsealarm rate, or the nonstationarity before the last error, could be corrected with  minor changes, but such modifications are hardly worthwhile. The model serves its purpose as a first-order approximation to  the data. However, some limitations of the present approach must be noted. The concern with the learning process in terms of  correct and incorrect responses neglects some important features of recognition learning. We can only infer that patterns are  "simpler" for the short-delay items because they have a higher learning rate and because of their poor transfer after a shift  in the delay condition. It would be interesting to know wherein the difference between "simple" and "complex" patterns exists.  REFERENCES  ATKINSON, R. C., AND CROTHERS, E. J.
