Subjects have only one chance to guess the solution; yet, one finds large groups of subjects announcing their  results too soon (i.e., when more than one answer is consistent), moreover each such subject announces the same premature  alternative. Also one sees groups of answers at a particular step occurring later than necessary, e.g., the information is  sufficient by the third step, but the majority of subjects wait to announce the answer at the sixth or even the eighth step.  Seemingly obvious correct answers are given by only very few of the subjects, and with no particular correlation with the IQ  (see Section 1.2), etc. In this paper, we are presenting a model of solving a simple logical problem involving the evaluation  of sequential information. The model was used for explaining or predicting results (no matter if correct or erroneous) of 40  variations of the task, and there was a high degree of agreement with the experimental data. The model enables us to  understand why and when incorrect as well as correct answers appear.  In this paper we present a model of the mental process going on during the solution of a task requiring the identification of  critical elements from a system when one encounters successive hints on their whereabouts. More concretely (and more  formally), let a system (set) R and its critical subset C with c elements be given. At the start, the subject knows just R and  the number c. After a sufficiently informative sequence of such hints, the answer can of course be deduced. In formally very simple cases,  like identifying letters A, B among A, B, C, D, E, F, after sequences like  "not in CD," "not in EF,"  or  "In AC," "not in CD," "in BD,"  the deduction seems to be so easy that one certainly does not expect any need for prompting to give the answer. Yet,  surprisingly enough, only a relatively low percentage of subjects gave a correct answer after such deductively adequate  sequences. Furthermore, in other such sequences there were large groups of identical incorrect answers given simultaneously at  several steps. These phenomena cannot be attributed to inability of deduction or false deductions. Rather, they suggest  another, nondeductive mental process which is naturally going on. The model we are presenting explains satisfactorily such  observed phenomena.  The utilization of positive and/or negative information in problem solving and concept forming has received much attention.  Bruner, Goodnow, and Austin (1956) found that for their subjects it was difficult to deal with negative information. People  preferred to be told which attributes are involved in the concept rather than which are not; the same holds true for  problem-solving tasks. Similar results were obtained in detailed studies by Anderson and Guthrie (1966), Detambel and Stolurow  (1956), Dodd and Bourne (1969), Donaldson (1959), Falmagne (1970), Freiburg and Tulving (1961), Fryatt and Tulving (1963),  Haygood and Bourne (1964), Haygood and Devine (1967), Hovland (1952), Hovland and Weiss (1953), Restle and Emerich (1966),  Schwartz (1971), and Whitfield (1951). Mathematical models for similar data were developed by Bower and Trabasso (1964) and  Cotton (1971), among others. It is known that, roughly speaking, a sequence of positive information leads to a better  performance than presenting negative instances, and a mixed series of positive and negative instances results in an  intermediate level of performance. For general discussion see Dominowski (1970), Falmagne (1975), and Radford and Burton  (1974).  To analyze, explain, and predict the outcome of a human problem-solving situation of the kind discussed, various strategies  are open to a theorist:  (a) Considering the normative aspect, i.e., to define the correct solution and investigate how many subjects (and/or, at what  time, with what effort, etc.) solved the problem correctly. This approach was rejected. The relatively small number of correct  solutions at proper steps was only a minor cause for this rejection. What was really decisive was the ubiquitous phenomenon of  large groups of coinciding incorrect answers, for which the normative approach can give no explanation.  (b) Classical information theory approach: After analyzing the outcomes of a few experiments, this was rejected for the time  being. One of the reasons (there are several,  222 BRICHACEK, KATETOV, AND PULTR  in fact) was the observation of phenomena for which neglecting the negative pieces of information for a large number of the  subjects gave a good explanation. The negative pieces of information, however, have in our tasks a considerably higher  informational value than the positive ones.  (c) Analysis of the pattern of often appearing solutions (correct or incorrect, premature or delayed) and an attempt to  formulate hypotheses based on deductive and other processes of thought leading to them. NX e can state, e.g., that immediately  repeated pieces of information often suggest incorrect hypotheses (e.g., after AC, BC, AD, BD considerably more subjects  suspected CD than AB), that negative information is often processed rather incompletely, etc. A certain form of taxonomy of  thinking operations determining the types of often appearing solutions may be evaluated. This strategy has some merits and may  give suggestions about subjective evaluation of information, and contribute to understanding some aspects of human cognition.  Some findings were published elsewhere (Brichacek & Zastavka, 1974). But, in this approach the interpretation of experimental  results is largely based on ad hoc intuitive insight into the thinking operations of the subjects. This is why it was not  followed any further.  (d) Model approach: We can try to develop a model of the situation based on a plausible (but not necessarily obvious)  assumption. If the results predicted are in reasonable agreement with the experimental data, the acceptability of the  assumption as a hypothesis about the underlying mental processes is supported.  This last approach is what we adopted in this paper. The paper is divided into four numbered sections. In Section 1, a  detailed description of the task and the organization of the experiments is given. In Section 2 we present the model and the  rules of prediction. In Section 3 we present the analysis and prediction based on the model in comparison with the data in a  few concrete cases. Also an overall assessement of the agreement of the predictions with the outcomes in all experimental  series is given. In Section 4 we formally extend the model and study its adequacy for solving general problems of the type  considered.  1. THE TASK AND THE ORGANIZATION OF EXPERIMENTS  1.1. The concrete form of the task previously mentioned involved a basic system R with six elements and critical subsets of  two elements. The subsets Mi (presented clues) also had two elements.  The subject was first given a list of six objects (indicated as capital Roman letters, in the description here we use always  A, B, C, D, E, F; in the real experiment six different letters were chosen at random) and informed that some two of them are  critical (in the description here, these are indicated by A, B). Then the subject is confronted stepwise with couples (clues)  IX, , Yl>, <Xz, Y2j,... of the letters and told for each couple whether a critical letter is present or not. Each subject was  given exactly one opportunity to state a solution; he or she was encouraged to do it after as few steps as possible.  SEEMINGLY IRRATIONAL SOLUTIONS 223  1.2. Subjects. Three hundred and two male students (aged 17 to 23) without memory defects from various faculties of Charles  University and from higher grades of technical schools participated in the experiment. They volunteered and received a small  wage for the participation. In a subgroup (N = 56) the general intelligence was measured by means of Amthauer's IST  (Intelligence Structure Test) in Czech version, in another group (N - 84) mean school results were registered. The correlation  between the number of correctly solved tasks and these parameters was nearly zero (+0.09 with the IQ, resp. +0.14 with the  school results). The experiments took place at a student hostel and it was announced in the classes that a preliminary  psychological research in problem solving was being organized and volunteers could participate.  1.3. Apparatus. A piece of equipment called the "Analyzer of Logical Operations" was used. It had a signal panel with 15  numbered pairs of double keys in three columns. The keys were marked by letters, and the sets of labeling letters used for  various sequences differed. In any sequence, there were always 6 letters, and all the 15 combinations occurred on the double  keys. If one presses down a double key which contains at least one of the critical letters, a signal light appears on the left  side of the double key (positive information), otherwise the signal light does not appear (negative information). (Convention:  In the sequel, speaking about a particular sequence of information keys we indicate it directly as the sequence-or the  relevant part of it-of the pieces of information obtained, say,  AB, CD, BE, CE, AF, CF, BC  the bars indicating the negative information.)  1.4. Procedure. First, the subject presses down the "Start" key, and the first number (of the given sequence) appears on the  upper part of the panel. Then, the subject presses the corresponding double key and obtains the first clue ( the signal light  either switches on or does not). Then, "Start" is pressed again and the number of the next double key to be pressed appears.  After each step the subject is given an opportunity to state a solution if ready. However, he is allowed only one guess and  after a guess the sequence is terminated with no feedback.  On the whole, 40 sequences were designed (see Sections 3.1 and 3.2). For any given subject, several sequences (6 to 9) were  used. The sequences were presented to the subjects in a systematically varied order; the first one was used as a training  task. The time required by a subject for each step, and the total time for the solution were recorded but they are not  analyzed here. The experimental session took 20 to 40 min. During the session no questions were answered, and the subjects  were not informed whether their solutions were correct or not.  2. SHAPE MODEL  2.1. The general concept. We assume that the sequence of pieces of information up to a given step is used by a subject to form  a hypothesis about the situation, expressed by  224 BkICHREK, KATETOV, AND PULTR  a varying degree of willingness to take a letter for critical. During the course of the experiment, the degrees change in two  ways:  (a) New information transforms the degree of plausibility of the letters in question,  (b) the degree of plausibility of the elements which are not mentioned is reduced.  The subject knows that exactly two letters are critical. This results in seeking a pattern that has high degrees of likelihood  at two letters, and low degrees of the four others. If, during the course of the experiment, such a state is reached, the  subject may report the solution thus suggested. The tendency to solve the problem as soon as possible expresses itself in a  readiness to report a solution from a pattern not yet conforming to the desired state (e.g., if there is still a relatively  high degree of plausibility at three places, he may ignore one of them).  2.2. According to the outlined general concept, we attach at the nth step to each letter X a value S,,(X) indicating the  degree of likelihood "condensed" so far. From the general standpoint, the values are intended to be in some partially ordered  algebra with operation enabling us to give formulas for the transformations occurring after each piece of information. In  analyzing the current experiments and predicting the outcomes, integer values are used. In the theoretical discussion of the  model (see Section 4), we use the reals. (The difference in the range is only a matter of convenience here; it amounts to  shrinking the scale in such a way that the adding of constant a in 2.4 below transforms to adding one.)  2.3. An intuitive outline of the model. Before formulating exactly the rules of transformation for S,,(X), let us discuss them  intuitively. First, we assume that the memory of previous information is weakened after new step. This memory loss results,  other things being equal, in the reduction of S.(X) with n. We assume that the memory extinguishes (linearly) to a small value  in the case of a prior positive information, but to zero in the case of prior negative ones.  Now, suppose the values SZ) are given. Let the next piece of information be negative XY (for the case of ignoring negative  information see 2.9 and 3.5 below). Then, the values at X and Y (S1(X) and S,+1(Y), respectively) fall down to the lowest  value on the next trial. If, instead, the next piece of information were positive XY, the situation is more complicated. If  the values at X and Y are nonnegative so far, the new impression simply adds strength to them. If one of the values is  negative, say X (it will follow that at most one can be such), the value in Y increases, but for X one of two things may  happen: The subject either ignores entirely the negative value, which results in a new positive value for X, or the  impossibility of X results in an even smaller value of S.,,(X).  Finally, according to the observation (well known also in other contexts) that initial information is better remembered, the  impression at the first positive piece of information is expressed by a larger number than for subsequent ones.  2.4. Before formulating the rules for prediction let us make a few  observations:  First, it should be noted that in the motivation of the model, and consequently in the resulting rules for development of  shapes, logical reasoning beyond the  226 BRICHACEK, KATETOV, AND PULTR  rudimentary logic entailed in the transformation was neglected. This is not to deny it a role in a part of the results, in  particular at the simplest tasks. The experiments seem to indicate that, however, it hardly ever prevails, and that its role  is of a significance at the very simplest tasks indeed. (Let us recall that the subjects were not allowed to use an aid for  calculating-including paper and pencil- and not encouraged to analyze their thoughts after every single step.)  If we deliberately ignored deductive calculation, some of the failures of the model can be related to possible additional  logical processes; thus, in addition to providing good predictions in many cases, the model provides a baseline around which  to discuss more detailed logical processes.  Also in formulating the prediction rules below we try to avoid explaining by shape (value of Sn) those solutions which are  more probably due to deduction. Further, we have to give the shape some time to crystalize. Thus, we start to consider the  shape not before the third step, and not until after the first occurrence of _a positive information step (e.g., the solution  AB at the third step of a sequence like CD, EF, AB,... is probably a deduction one step delayed and we do not require it to be  predicted by our model).  A shape sufficient to cause a subject to guess the critical set should have at least two large values. We observed, however, a  certain tendency of subjects in cases corresponding to three large values to forget about one of them and infer a critical set  (cf. 2.1). This is why the definition of the meaningful shapes below includes such "blunt" cases. Another way to view the model is as an inductive scheme for inferring a critical subset of a given set from partial  information. While the model has proved to be useful in explaining subjects' actual behavior, we have not yet presented a  mathematical discussion of its adequacy. It turns out that the model represents a correct, although not too fast, method of  induction in the sense that in the limit it detects the critical subset. In particular, unlike logical deduction, the model  can identify in the limit the critical set despite incorrect information utilization on a random subset of the trials.  We see that in circumstances similar to those in the task in question, the "shape reasoning" has decisive advantages against  the logical deduction, and hence it can be viewed as, in a sense, more rational. Namely, if the situation is such that a  subject obtains a lot of redundant information, the shape develops so that after sufficiently many steps, it stably indicates  the correct solution, while, in deductive reasoning a simple error leads to complete confusion. This advantage becomes perhaps  more pronounced after realizing that in many practical recognization tasks analogous to the experimental one, there is no  strong motivation to premature conclusion.  4.2. Before proceeding to the proof some comments on various general features of the model are in place now. (1) The storage  of information implied in the model is rather economical: at every step, the whole information obtained so far is "condensed"  to an indexed (by elements of B) set of real numbers. (2) The information concerning distinct elements of B is evaluated  independently. This seems to be fairly adequate if neither B nor C are structured and subjects make virtually no assumptions  about the form of C or, e.g., about incompatibility of the presence of some specified elements in C, etc. Observe, however,  that in the actual experiment there is an a priori restriction, namely, the size of C is known. Nevertheless, a restriction  (an a priori information) of this kind seems to influence only the decision rules (see Sections 2.7, 2.8, 2.10). The sole fact of the small percentage of correct solutions at the correct step would not be much of a motive for looking for  an elaborate explanation. If there were a wide variety of scattered solutions (indicating "loosing of the thread"), the  natural reaction would be to consider the problem as still too difficult without auxiliary means (such as pencil and paper).  What really called for explanation was the fact that the results in fact had been far from scattered: On the contrary, large  groups of the same solutions at the same steps actually prevailed.  It appears that a relatively simple model fits the empirical results (and, applied to a further series of experiments, gives  fairly good predictions).  The question naturally arises as to whether, or to what extent, the model reflects real thinking processes taking place during  the solution of the task-and, possibly, while solving other ones. It is certainly premature to draw definite conclusions from  one type of experiment only. On the other hand, we feel that facts indicate that there may be more in the model than just an  ad hoc explanation of the structure of the experiment outcomes. There is the relatively plausible motivation of the shape  concept; successive information processing, the "economical condensation" of the previous information and its use (see 4.19).  Second, the model is actually quite simple. Third, there is the fact that people are confronted with "recognization problems"  in everyday life and have to possess a stable mechanism for it-and the mechanism indicated by the model is stable. Fourth, the  prediction meets the outcome, in our opinion, satisfactority indeed (see 3.4). Then there are the late reports of solutions,  which are so often correct (see 3.8).  Further, the model seems to be supported by a few experiments we have not reported about in the text: These were variants of  the conditions of the main experiment, in which one of the pieces of information was false (and the subjects were told  beforehand that there will be a false piece of information in the sequence). The outcome was again close to the prediction by  the shape model, and quite parallel to that in the "dependable" case.  The results show for sure that the solving of the task does not prevalently progress by  SEEMINGLY IRRATIONAL SOLUTIONS 247  calculation using the rules of formal logic. The solution is irrational in the sense that the subject cannot always explicitly  give good reasons for his or her decision. This is necessarily so in the case of the steps where the information is still  incomplete (as, e.g., in 3.3.1, 3.3.2, 3.3.5). It is probably so also in many correct solutions; it has not been, however,  practically checked, for any verbalization of the reasoning would have changed the character of the experiment: a subject led  this way to analyze his or her reasoning might proceed much differently in solving further tasks and very probably would. One  the other hand, the "reasoning" indicated by the model is rational in the sense that it is (in case of redundant  information-which is the everyday life recognization case) more likely to provide the correct answer than calculation.  NN," e are not claiming that normative logical reasoning has no influence on the outcome. This influence is seen in comparing  the (intuitively estimated as) easier tasks with the others. One has a considerably larger-while still not prevailing-  percentage of the correct solutions at the correct step there.
