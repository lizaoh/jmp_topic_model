In the social sciences contextuality has always been the rule rather than the exception but since the seminal papers of  Bell (1966)  and  Kochen and Specker (1967)  contextuality has also become a central concept in the foundations of physics. Today one experiences a kind of back reaction from physics to sociology, psychology and behavioral sciences. Various formal techniques and conceptual tools previously used to tackle the problem of contextuality and nonlocality in quantum theory have begun to be applied in social sciences ( De Barros et al., 2017; Dzhafarov et al., 2016 ). In this paper I would like to contribute to this interaction by clarifying some conceptual connections between contextuality, signaling, and Bell’s inequalities in the framework of operational theories and ontological models ( Spekkens, 2005 ). This framework is general enough to accommodate empirical theories—be them physical or social—with well-defined measurements (questions) and probability distribution of outcomes (answers) and to tackle the question whether the theory admits an underlying noncontextual model. In the paper I define causal contextuality and compare it with contextuality-by-default (CbD-contextuality) defined by Dzhafarov, Kujala, and colleagues.

                         1 
                         See  Dzhafarov and Kujala (2014b, 2017a)  and also  Cervantes and Dzhafarov (2018), Dzhafarov (2018, 2019), Dzhafarov and Kujala (2014a, 2014b, 2014c, 2014d, 2016), Dzhafarov et al. (2020), Jones (2019)  and  Kujala and Dzhafarov (2016a, 2016b, 2019) . 
                       I will argue that the two concepts are different. First, causal contextuality is a property of the ontological models while CbD-contextuality is a feature of the operational theory. Second, signaling operational theories can be CbD-noncontextual but they admit only causally contextual ontological models. To highlight the difference between the two notions of contextuality, I construct a simple toy model

This paper is structured as follows. In Section  2 , the framework of operational theories and ontological models will be introduced and contextuality will be defined within this framework. In Section  3 , the concept of CbD-contextuality developed by Dzhafarov, Kujala and colleagues will be presented and analyzed. In Section  4 , a simple toy model will be constructed with a classical causal mechanism which renders the model contextual without making it CbD-contextual. In Section  5 , an alternative “behavioral” realization of the same toy model will be given and two similar experiments of  Atmanspacher and Filk (2019)  and  Cervantes and Dzhafarov (2018)  will be analyzed. I conclude in Section  6 .

Thus far everything was purely probabilistic. To provide a causal account for the above correlations and probabilistic independencies, one needs to invoke general metaphysical principles connecting causal and probabilistic descriptions. Two such principles are Reichenbach’s Common Cause Principle and the Causal Markov Condition. The Common Cause Principle states that if two events are correlated, then there is either a direct causal connection between them or a third event, a common cause, which brings about the correlation. The Causal Markov Condition is a generalization of the Common Cause Principle stating that any event is probabilistically independent from any of its causal non-descendent if conditioned on its causal parents.

Also note that the causal structure depicted in  Fig. 1  is not always applicable; the dotted line marks just this fact. For example, the Causal Markov Condition coordinating causal and probabilistic descriptions might be in conflict with other general principles, such as the special theory of relativity excluding direct causal connections between spatially separated events. The EPR-Bohm scenario is a paradigmatic example for such a conflict. Here one has a set of correlations (an operational theory) for which Bell’s inequalities rule out common cause-type, non-conspiratorial, noncontextual ontological models. By adopting a contextual model, however, one introduces correlations between measurement outcomes and other spatially separated measurements. But to causally account for these correlations in accordance with the Causal Markov Condition one needs to violate the special theory of relativity. Some (see  Atmanspacher & Filk, 2019 ) coin the term “pure contextuality” or “quantum contextuality” for this  acausal  type of contextuality (see also  Cavalcanti, 2018 ). Note, however, that apart from quantum mechanics, any other form of contextuality is backed by the causal structure depicted in  Fig. 1 .

Also note that the question concerning the causal background of contextuality is an ontological question. It is about the objective causal order of the world giving rise to certain correlations. This question is completely different from the question as to whether this causal connection can be exploited in order to, say, communicate between the distant measurement  loci . Since the ontic states cannot, by definition, be manipulated, any “communication” must be expressed purely in terms of measurement choices and measurement outcomes of an operational theory. Signaling is just such an expression.

With this terminology in hand, let us turn now to the discussion of a homonymous concept, CbD-contextuality.

In an influential series of papers, Ehtibar Dzhafarov, Janne Kujala, and colleagues made significant contribution to the clarification of the nature of contextuality ( Cervantes & Dzhafarov, 2018; Dzhafarov, 2018, 2019; Dzhafarov & Kujala, 2014a, 2014b, 2014c, 2014d, 2016; Dzhafarov et al., 2020; Jones, 2019; Kujala & Dzhafarov, 2016a, 2016b, 2019 ). They developed a theory, called Contextuality-by-Default (CbD) theory, which provides an elegant framework to address the problem of random variables in incompatible contexts. They defined contextuality in this framework and provided many important results concerning the relation of contextuality to joint distributions, non-signaling, 
                         5

                         Also called “consistent connectedness” and lack of “direct influence” by Dzhafarov; “marginal selectivity” in psychology; “non-communication” and “no-disturbance” in quantum information theory. 
                       hidden variable models, counterfactuals, etc. They applied the theory to analyze and also to set up real-world experiments to scrutinize the status of contextuality in sociology, psychology and behavioral studies.

In this section I will just concentrate on the concept of contextuality as defined by Dzhafarov, Kujala and colleagues. I will refer to this concept as CbD-contextuality to discern it from causal contextuality defined in  (8) – (9)  and portrayed in  Fig. 1 . There are two main differences between causal contextuality and CbD-contextuality. First, causal contextuality is a property of the ontological model while CbD-contextuality is a feature of the operational theory (just like signaling). Second, signaling operational theories can be CbD-noncontextual but they admit only causally contextual ontological models (see  Lemma 1  in  Appendix A ).

To give a precise definition of CbD-contextuality, I should use the framework of Dzhafarov, Kujala and colleagues based on random variables, connections and couplings. For my purpose, however, it suffices to reinterpret CbD-contextuality and apply it to the specific operational theory introduced in the previous Section.

In this paper I defined contextuality as the causal and (due to the Causal Markov Condition) probabilistic dependence of the outcome distribution of a measurement in a given ontic state on another simultaneously performed measurements. This notion, which I called causal contextuality, is different from CbD-contextuality as defined by Dzhafarov, Kujala and colleagues since the former is defined at the ontological level and the latter at the operational level. Causal contextuality mirrors more the ontological-causal structure of the system while CbD- contextuality tests whether our empirically available theory of the system admits models in which all causal contextuality come to the surface, so to say, and manifest themselves in signaling. In other words, causal contextuality is a property of the model itself while CbD-contextuality is the property of our theory of the system.

In the second part of the paper I highlighted this difference between the two concepts of contextuality by constructing a physical toy model which was causally contextual but gave rise to operational theories which were CbD-noncontextual and hence satisfied the  
                         CHSH inequalities generalized by 
                      Dzhafarov and colleagues. I also constructed a causally contextual “behavioral” toy model isomorphic in certain aspects with the physical one.

Now, systems in the CbD formalism correspond to operational theories and couplings to ontological models. A system is consistently connected iff the associated operational theory is non-signaling.Contextuality, however, is placed at a different level in the two approaches: Cbd-contextuality is a property of a system, while causal contextuality is a property of an ontological model. Consequently, operational theories associated to CbD-noncontextual systems can have causally contextual ontological models.
