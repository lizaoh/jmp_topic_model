WOLFGANG SC14WARZ  Freie Universitit Berlin, Germany  We prove a conjecture due to Falmagne (1985, p. 262) that models of probability summation do not necessarily imply a  superiority of the m-channel performance over the singlechannel performance when performance is defined as a dominance of the  corresponding ROC curves. A theorem on the exact conditions for the coincidence of the single-channel and the m-channel ROC  curves is derived, and the implications of this theorem for the interpretation of data bearing upon models of probability  summation are discussed in some detail with special reference to research on binocular summation. 1992 Academic Press, Inc.  1. INTRODUCTION: PROBLEM AND NOTATION  It is well known that the binocular threshold for contrast detection is lower than the threshold for either eye alone. Pirenne  (1943) was the first to suggest a purely probabilistic basis for this phenomenon: if the probability of detecting a test  stimulus with either eye is p, and the excitation of at least one channel is sufficient for a positive response, then the  probability of detecting the same stimulus with both eyes should equal 1 - (1 -p)2, at least if the two eyes are equally  sensitive and act independently.  A considerable amount of research (reviewed by Blake & Fox, 1973; Blake, Sloane, & Fox, 1981) was devoted to testing this  prediction. Under optimal conditions (which include the temporally contiguous stimulation of corresponding retinal areas) the  amount of binocular summation exceeds the prediction of Pirenne's model. However, even in these cases, the model is  indispensable to estimate the separate contributions from probability and physiological summation, as will be pointed out in  detail in Section 3. The considerable importance of Pirenne's model for binocular summation derives from the fact that  basically the same reasoning has been used in numerous other fields of vision research, usually reformulated in terms of  independent channels, which are thought to be specifically tuned for some component of the stimulus. Guth (1971), Braddick (1972), and, most precisely, Falmagne (1985, p. 262) noted that a true superiority of binocular  detection requires that the binocular ROC curve dominate the monocular ROC curve, i.e., one must consider not only the  detection, but also the corresponding false alarm probabilities. It seems to be widely accepted that Pirenne's model always  predicts superior binocular performance; for example, Braddick (1972) asserts in his discussion of Pirenne's model: "...if the  subject adjusted to give the same false alarm rate in binocular as monocular viewing, he would still show a higher hit rate in  the binocular case" (p. 1435). IMPLICATIONS FOR THE INTERPRETATION OF MONOCULAR VS  BINOCULAR DETECTION PROBABILITIES  It is generally recognized that in order to compare monocular and binocular detection probabilities one must distinguish  between probability and physiological (or "neural") summation. However, the latter effect is not directly observable in  psychophysical experiments, and so it has become fairly standard to interpret the excess (if any) of the binocular empirical  detection probability over the prediction of Pirenne's model as a manifestation of physiological summation. To cite Blake and  Fox (1973): "The general practice has been to use the expected binocular performance computed from probability considerations  as a baseline for evaluating empirical performance. If the performance exceeds that predicted by probability summation, the  data are interpreted as evidence for some form of neural summation" (p. 168). Conversely, the absence of such an excess under  certain experimental conditions such as stimulation of disparate retinal areas (Thorn & Boynton, 1974), nonsimultaneous  stimulus presentation to both eyes (Matin, 1962; Eriksen, Greenspon, Lappin, & Carlson, 1966), or significant differences in  spatial (Blake & Levinson, 1977) or temporal frequency (Blake & Rush, 1980) is usually interpreted as an absence of  physiological summation, i.e., the "pure chance level."  Our results demonstrate, first, that the latter interpretation is not tenable: physiological summation may well be present,  while the observed binocular detection probability does not exceed the level predicted by Pirenne's model. Assuming the  conditions of our corollary to hold, no increase of the binocular detection probability relative to the monocular detection  probability will be observed at all for a common false alarm rate. If in addition physiological summation is actually present,  it would be mistakenly interpreted as mere probability summation, at least as long as it does not exceed the level predicted  by Pirenne's model. Also, even if it exceeds this level, the amount of physiological summation would be consistently  underestimated.  A second related point which follows from our results is that under a Yes/No procedure, no firm conclusions whatsoever can be  drawn concerning binocular summation without concurrent measurements of the false alarm rates. This is even true if the  binocular detection probability exceeds the level of Pirenne's prediction, because such an excess can always be due to a  concurrent increase of the  276 WOLFGANG SCHWARZ  corresponding false alarm probability. In fact, our derivation shows that even if monocular and binocular presentations are  randomly interspersed, so that a common response criterion can be assumed, a separate analysis of monocular and binocular  false alarm rates is strictly necessary, because Pirenne's model predicts a binocular increase of both detection and false  alarm probabilities relative to monocular presentations (see Figs. 1, 2). In order to compare unequal monocular and binocular  detection probabilities corresponding to unequal false alarm rates, it is obviously necessary to have information about the  form of the ROC curves. Specifically, if the curves are of the type specified in our theorem, any binocular detection excess  for a common false alarm rate can be attributed to physiological summation. On the other hand, if the ROC curves are not of  the form y = 1- (1- x)' such an interpretation is not correct. Thus, which interpretation is correct depends critically on the  form of the ROC curve, which must therefore be known.  Third, let us point out that essentially the same interpretation problems remain under the two alternative forced choice  (2AFC) paradigm, which has often been used to study binocular summation. For monocular presentations, the usual assumption  (e.g., Falmagne, 1985, p. 243) is that the observer extracts an internal random representation Xn, XS from the two intervals  containing the stimuli n and s, respectively, and selects the interval associated with the larger of both. Thus, the monocular  probability of a correct response is PM(C) = P(X 5 >1 X. Under Pirenne's summation model, the corresponding assumption for  the binocular condition is that there are two independent internal representations X X;, and XS, XS from each eye for the n  and s stimuli, respectively, and that the observer compares the maxima of both (e.g., Falmagne, 1985, p. 264). Thus PB(c) =  P[max(X,, Xs) 3 max(X X;,)].  Now, it is easy to show (and follows directly from Green's (1964) so-called Area Theorem applied to Fig. 2) that PM(C)  =PB(c)=a/(1+a) under the conditions of our corollary, i.e., there is no binocular superiority due to probability summation at  all! Consequently, any increase of PB(c) relative to PM(c) must be attributed to some kind of physiological summation.  However, in order to judge whether the conditions of our corollary are actually met, the 2AFC data format is in no case  sufficient; rather, information concerning the form of the underlying ROC curve is strictly necessary. For example, from Fig.  1 and Green's Area Theorem, it is clear that there is a substantial excess of PB(c) over PM(c) under the normal distribution  model. Thus, while in a sense the 2AFC method is "criterion free," a clear disadvantage of this procedure is that the amount  of binocular superiority due to simple probability summation cannot be calculated without rather strong further assumptions.  By definition, physiological summation in turn can psychophysically only be inferred indirectly from the discrepance between  the observed binocular detection probability and the level predicted from probability summation effects. Thus, we must  conclude that even with the 2AFC method an estimate of the amount of physiological summation requires a consideration of our  results given in Section 2.
