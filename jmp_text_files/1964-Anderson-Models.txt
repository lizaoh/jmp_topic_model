JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY: 1, 121-142 (1964)  Linear Models for Responses Measured on a Continuous Scale'  NORMAN H. ANDERSON  University of California, Los Angeles, California  A first-order linear model for responses measured on a continuous scale is considered. Expressions are derived for stimulus dependencies, autocorrelations, and variances, thus giving a unified treatment of previous work. The stimulus dependencies are shown to remain essentially unchanged for certain cases of nonconstant parameters. The analysis of continuous contingent schedules is illustrated.  A higher-order linear model is also discussed briefly. It is shown that the stimulus dependencies can provide parameter-free tests of the whole class of models under consideration. Relations of these models to autoregressive models, and to discreteresponse, linear operator models are pointed out.  Earlier reports (Anderson and Hovland, 1957; Anderson, 1961) have considered some specific linear models for situations in which the response is considered as a continuous variable. The class of situations considered includes various learning and opinion change tasks in which the response changes in a systematic way over a sequence of trials. The present report gives some straightforward generalizations of the earlier formulations. The present formulation contains the three linear models previously discussed (Anderson, 1959, 1961) as special cases. Model I is obtained from Eq. 1 by setting a = 0, b = 1 - a, c = a. In these three models, incidentally, a + b + c = 1, so that they may be considered as weighted average models though with possibly negative weights.  Two linear models which may be considered as generalizations of Model I have been independently investigated by Rosenberg (1962, 1963). Suppes (1959, 1960) has considered a "one-element" model, as well as a linear model which, although of rather different structure from the present models, is in certain respects comparable to the opinion change model. The general model of Eq. 1 has been independently proposed by Rouanet and Rosenberg (1963), who have studied its general stochastic properties and have used it to classify and compare the various specific models that have been proposed. A possible use of the linear contingent schedule would be to decrease the change rate in the mean response curve. As Rouanet and Rosenberg (1963) have noted, the change rate is fairly high in most published experiments to which the continuous response learning models seem applicable, and consequently the mean learning curve yields relatively little information. With a linear contingent schedule, the change rate in Eq. 9 would be 1 - (a + b + cs) instead of 1 - (a + b). In some tasks in which a single correct response is used, it would be feasible to tell the subject only a certain proportion, p, of his actual error instead of the full actual error. This would be equivalent to choosing s = 1 - p in Eq. 28, which would reduce the change rate by the amount, c(1 - p). Suitable choice of p could then yield sufficient preasymptotic data to test the model against the mean response curve. In addition, variation of p could provide a means of estimating c through comparison of the change rates.  It would also be possible to let the parameter s in Eq. 28 assume more than one value. Thus, s could be chosen to be zero on some trials, and to be some fixed, nonzero value on other trials. To each choice, there would correspond a replica of Eq. 30, and the difference between the two would give an estimate of csE(p.n). The question of the convergence of Eq. 1 will not be considered in detail here, but it does require a brief comment. In particular, Eq. 1 will not converge if a + b > 1. In this case, certain asymptotic expressions such as those given by Eqs. 9 and 27 become meaningless. Mathematically, however, Eq. 1 would still represent a valid stochastic process which could be studied over a finite number of trials. Psychologically, it might be of some interest to attempt to induce divergence by experimental means as suggested above in the discussions of contingent schedules.  Experimentally, of course, the variance cannot become infinite, at least in the psychological tasks which are ordinarily employed. However, it is possible that the behavioral process of major interest could be divergent, but that the behavior would fail to diverge because of extraneous factors, such as end effects. If so, one would probably wish to reduce the influence of the extraneous factors as much as possible.  SOME EXTENSIONS  In this section, some extensions of Eq. 1 will be briefly considered. REMARKS  1. Primary attention has been given here to the stimulus dependencies. It is thought that these dependencies not only serve as natural descriptive statistics of the data, but also yield the most immediately useful tests of the model. Thus, as has been seen, they provide quite simple, parameter-free tests of the whole class of linear models studied here.  There are, however, two limitations in the use of those dependencies that have been considered here. If the behavior departs severely from the model, as happens in discrete response probability learning (Anderson, 1960, 1964), then the higher order dependencies tend to be difficult to interpret psychologically. As a consequence, although serving their function as tests of goodness of fit, they tend to be less useful descriptively than the various subsequence curves (Anderson and Whalen, 1960).  In addition, the stimulus dependencies are not adequate for all purposes since the parameters, a and b, combine to form a single effective parameter, a -f- b. Consequently, it is not possible to determine from such data the separate weights associated with the overt response and with the underlying mean. For this purpose, it will probably be necessary to make use of some sort of response dependency, such as the autocorrelations.  It should be realized, however, that response dependencies suffer from potentially serious drawbacks. Pooling data over subjects would introduce subject selection artifacts, of course, but even an analysis on an individual subject basis does not avoid all the difficulties. The problem at issue can be illustrated by considering response speed of a rat run one trial per day in a straight runway with a fixed reinforcement. Suppose that Eq. 1 holds with b = 0. Then at asymptote, the model predicts that the autocorrelations of the response series are all zero. However, environmental conditions or health may affect the rat's speed in such a way that it oscillates with' a mean period of several days. The observed autocorrelations will then be nonzero since they are sensitive to the presence of such extraneous, nonlearning factors. And of course, the problem would presumably be more serious if the trials were run at shorter intertrial intervals.  This example illustrates the general possibility that the deviations, xmay be correlated over successive trials because of extraneous influences. Such correlation will affect the response dependencies in general. Accordingly, such statistics should be  138 ANDERSON  treated with due care. A more formal examination of this question has been made in the discrete response case (Anderson, 1959), and it may be noted that, here as there, the stimulus dependencies are not subject to such perturbations, at least when a random reinforcement schedule is employed.  It will be noted, of course, that in certain tasks only a single reinforced response would normally be employed. Except for the mean response curve, then, stimulus dependency information would not be available. In such cases, it may be profitable to consider task variations that would yield such information.  2. A problem that arises in the evaluation of continuous response models concerns the scale of measurement employed. In certain tasks, for instance, either time or speed might reasonably be used as the dependent variable, and it is evident that the same model cannot hold for both. This exemplifies a general difficulty in the interpretation of goodness of fit tests for continuous response models. Deviations from prediction might mean that the model is basically incorrect, or only that the scale of measurement is not correct (or not theoretically appropriate).  A possible method for alleviating this ambiguity of interpretation has been given in another connection (Anderson, 1962). The basic idea is simple. Suppose that a given model is basically correct and would fit the data if they were measured on the correct scale. In many situations, one can be reasonably sure that the actual scale, if not itself correct, is at least ordinally related to the correct scale. If so, then there exists a monotone transformation of the actual scale that would yield data that satisfied the model. In practice, the monotone transformation could be approximated by a Maclaurin series, although the details depend on the experimental design and the descriptive statistics employed. Of course, if the model rather than the scale were at fault, then no such monotone transformation would exist. In principle, therefore, the ambiguity in the interpretation of goodness of fit tests for continuous response models is not insurmountable.  3. It is of interest to compare the continuous response models studied here with those employed in two other areas: classical time series analysis, and discrete-response learning models.  Time series analysis, especially in economics (e.g., Kendall, 1948; Tintner, 1952), makes frequent use of autoregressive models. These have certain similarities to the present linear models.
