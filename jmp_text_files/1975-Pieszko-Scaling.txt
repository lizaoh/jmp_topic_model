JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 12, 449-477 (1975)  Multidimensional Scaling in Riemannian Space*  HENRY PIESZKO  Department of Psychology, California State College, Dominguez Hills, California 90246  A metric which is a function of position is proposed for the analysis of the intrinsic geometry involved in preference or  similarity judgments. Variation in the distance function or metric is characteristic of the Riemannian spaces and may be  interpreted as curvature, stress or distortion in distance estimates and thus in the subjective perceptual space. It is  possible to find the coefficients in the distance function at selected points by fitting a least-squares Riemannian surface to  the Euclidean plane. The functional form of the distance can then be obtained by an application of the Laplace equation.  Several examples are worked out for the two-dimensional solution but extension to higher spaces appears to be quite feasible.  Of the several models which now exist for the analysis of individual differences in preference or similarity judgments (Horan,  1969; Coombs, 1964; Stouffer, 1950) the Tucker & Messick (1963) method especially, although criticized (Ross, 1966; Carrol &  Chang, 1970), has been extended and applied in several areas (Messick, 1960; Helm & Tucker, 1962). At this stage these  developments have been extensive in that facet of scaling theory which presupposes a matrix of interpoint distances  preliminary to a complete analysis requiring the construction of an appropriate space, either Euclidean (Abelson & Sermat,  1962; Messick & Abelson, 1956) or a non-Euclidean variant (Kruskal, 1964; Lingoes, 1965) which will support approximations of  the distances derived from dissimilarity judgments.  None of the methods, however, including those involving differentially weighted bases (Carrol & Chang, 1970) postulate a  metric broad enough to allow for variation not only in the individual viewpoint, for example, but more importantly in the  intrinsic and quite possibly non-Euclidean structuring of the space peculiar to an individual. Although the Minkowski metric  has been used (Torgerson, 1958) the results apparently have not been impressive possibly because a sound mathematical basis  has been lacking. The need or justification for a more specific or intrinsic geometry may not necessarily be obvious since as  is true in most cases where the vector product matrix (Torgerson, 1958) yields only positive roots a Euclidean solution can  always be constructed.  * This paper is based on a doctoral dissertation submitted to the University of Illinois in partial fulfillment of the  requirements for a Ph.D. degree. For example, Luneburg (1947, 1950) in synthesizing available experimental data concerning binocular vision (insofar as it  relates to the perception of physical objects) concluded, partially on the observation that in this form of perception form  and localization are uncorrelated (apparently as a result of the homogeneity and isotropy of the substratum), that the  appropriate metric is one which characterizes a Riemannian space of constant curvature. While this class of metrices includes  the Euclidean as a special case, the data that had been collected point to a hyperbolic metric. Ideally the goal in  multidimensional scaling may not be to fit a Euclidean space to a set of distances but rather to recover, if that is possible,  the space which best represents in an admittedly approximate way the actual processes involved in human judgment.  In the approach to be proposed it may be possible to obtain a perfect fit for the existing distances, to obtain alternate  estimates of the "additive constant," and to recover some of the information represented by the smallest roots discarded in  the usual scaling procedures (Torgerson, 1958) by introducing a metric or space in which these smallest roots are measures of  spatial distortion, curvature that may be involved in occasional intransitivities (Kendall, 1955) or irreducible interaction  between the dimensions underlying the expressed preferences. In this way uncorrelated variation is no longer treated as error  but is incorporated into the structure of the metric of the preference space.  The problem of obtaining stable individual measures remains but it can be argued that in measuring preferences or judged  similarities as against aptitudes or personality traits it may be precisely the instability which on occasion is of most  interest as for example in the distorting effects of select stimuli which elicit unusually intense emotional responses from  certain subjects. The existence of such "complexes" or seemingly inappropriate emotional responses to certain stimuli (and  stimuli associated with them) has been well documented (Ericksen, 1952; Ullman, 1962) and has stimulated the construction of  presumed personality dimensions specifically concerned with subject responses, overt or otherwise, to classes of stimuli  (Goodenough and Karp, 1961; Byrne, 1961).  Of interest therefore would be a geometrical model which reduces to the familiar Euclidean or Minkowski metric under certain  ideal conditions and perhaps only in small regions but which is flexible enough to represent intricate and fallible judgments  which are subjected to various stresses and distortion attributable it may be supposed to the interaction between subject and  stimulus, and since distortion in a particular region implies a deviation in the distance function, the model which is  proposed is thus based on a distance measure which is itself a function of position.  A variable distance function is characteristic of a geometry as proposed by Riemann (Weyl, 1922) based on studies of surfaces  embedded in Euclidean spaces and successfully applied to such seemingly diverse problems as the paths of planets, the slowing  down of clocks (Weyl, 1922), and the continuum mechanics of elastic materials (McConnell, 1947). As will be shown later, such  spaces are pointwise Euclidean and thus the Pythagorean theorem becomes applicable. In scaling theory the appropriate  application of the model will permit the investigation of the intrinsic perceptual geometry of the individual so that the  distances derived on the assumption of an embedding Euclidean space will remain invariant within the personal space but with  some gain not only in the reduction in the number of required dimensions but also in a hopefully more meaningful space which  may be lost if analyzed into Euclidean components.  The method as proposed has certain limitations the most prominent of which are (1) the fact that a finite model is based on  theoretical relationships which have been established (and are strictly accurate) only in the infinitesimal domain and (2) the  complete solution has been worked out only for the two dimensional case. The first limitation is a serious one which however  can be minimized since the final step of the proposed model involves a set of differential equations which in general do apply  in the infinitesimal domain. Our feeling is that the second limitation is perhaps temporary since extension to higher spaces  is in theory very much a possibility.  While more elegant solutions exist, the starting point for the present analysis is the standard Eckart-Young MDS procedure. It  may be noted that in one of the proposed minimizations the coordinates (as obtained from the Eckart-Young procedure)  themselves enter in as parameters and thus the final solution (which includes an estimate of the additive constant) reflects  the constraints of the underlying Riemannian space rather than the assumption of the initial Eckart-Young solution. As has been shown (Friedman, 1965) any Riemannian (Vn) manifold of n dimensions with analytic positive definite metric  may be isometrically embedded in a Euclidean (Em) space of m = 2n(n -{- 1) dimensions. By averaging the coordinates for the two points it is possible to  obtain the g as functions of the mean coordinates across all the interpoint distances. There is apparently no way of  allocating the g values to specific points unless the distances are extremely small so that any given neighborhood is very  systematically sampled. The practice of assigning the values to the midpoints is then one which seems to be least  objectionable and may not be crucial if the potentials method to be described later is followed.  The midpoints procedure will then fix the g at selected points on the surface but will not yield the functional relation  between the g and position. The problem of tying down the g to definite points is a problem whose solution would require some  hypothesis as to how the metric coefficients are distributed across a surface.  There appear to be two solutions to determine the function f. The first of these presupposes prior knowledge as to the general  form of the surface and the fit to the parameters is then achieved by means of the solution of the normal equations (Deming,  1964). The second method, while not extensively used in psychometrics, flows naturally from the identification of the metric  coefficients with some type of potential function. Much has been written concerning the potential function (e.g., Morse &  Feshbach, 1953) in what is called the classical theory of fields.  The first method would be applicable in those cases where both the stimuli and their effects on the subject are very well  known, as, for example, embedding a very critical or influencial stimulus in a series of indifferent ones so that a strong  source exists, approximating perhaps a normal distribution function. In such situations the parameters of the surface can be  determined, approximately, for the distances as supplied by the subject. Moreover, there is no need to restrict the method to  single sources since it should be possible to state the equations of a surface of multiple sources of "disturbances." However,  such knowledge is rarely given, and thus other methods must be considered.  The problem essentially is to extrapolate or interpolate from values of g at select points to arbitrary points on the surface.  A solution which makes this possible follows along the lines of physical theory (Morse and Feshbach, 1953) and identifies the  g values as representing a type of potential function. If this is granted then the solutions which are sought are those of the  equation of Laplace. In place of midpoint localization of the g several possibilities suggest themselves, one of which would require a measure of  "correlation" between a stimulus and the extent to which that particular stimulus was involved in the high-g stimulus pairs.
