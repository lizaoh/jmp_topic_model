Chi-square minimizing search procedures were used to obtain parameter values for each model, with significant discrepancies between obtained and predicted values appearing in every case. For a fixed probability of guessing correctly, the models ordered themselves in predictive efficiency as follows from best to worst: two-element with three free parameters, random-trial increment, two phase, two-element with two free parameters, linear, long-short, one-element, and no learning, the last three being substantially poorer than the others. The present investigation is an attempt to provide interconnections between those lines. Estes (1960, 1961) and Estes, Hopkins, and Crothers (1960) have emphasized the inadequacy of the stimulus sampling theory linear model to handle certain data, notably the relative infrequency of success preceded by failure in an RTI design (i.e., a design with one reinforcement or exposure trial of the stimulus and response item pairings followed by two test trials on which only the stimulus items are presented). No defender of the simple linear model in such situations seems to have appeared, but mathematical learning theorists such as Atkinson and Crothers (1964), Norman (1964a, 1964b), and Suppes and Ginsberg (1963) have noted evidence of paired-associate learning which does not conform to the simple all-or-none model (hereafter called the one-element model), leading them to other theories such as two-state models (Suppes and Ginsberg) or combinations of all-or-none and linear models (Norman). The first group of research workers have commonly employed fewer response options than stimulus items. Thus Atkinson and Crothers' Experiment la used a nine-item stimulus list and three responses, making the probability of a correct response by guessing roughly constant and equal to one-third, particularly if there is no restriction that exactly three of the stimuli must have each response as the correct answer.  The second group of investigators of the all-or-none learning problem are not mathematical learning theorists but tend to support, on a qualitative level at least, the incremental theory proposed by Hull (1943), as interpreted for verbal learning studies. This theory asserts that habit strength (or associative strength as it is more often called in the verbal learning literature) gradually increases with the number of exposure or reinforcement trials. Until habit strength for a particular item reaches a level called the threshold, the subject of the experiment will make correct answers only by chance. Above that level there will be some nonzero probability of a correct response. A sufficiently high value of habit strength will lead to 100 % or nearly 100 probability of correct responding, depending upon the specific assumptions made by the Hullian theorist. Postman (1963) and Wollen (1962) have let reasoning such as this lead them to the prediction (or a variation of it in Wollen's case) that increasing the number of exposure trials prior to testing should lead to an increased conditional probability F(C2 I I,) of a correct response on the second test given an error on the first test, in contrast to the prediction of the all-or-none model that this probability should be constant and at chance level, assuming a fixed chance level.  The present paper is directly concerned with Postman's work on this topic. Using two-digit numbers as stimuli and highly familiar one-syllable words as responses, his  444 COTTON ET AL.  Experiment I varied the number of exposure trials by employing separate groups with 1, 2, 4, or 6 such trials prior to five test trials without reinforcement. Though his prediction was confirmed, its theoretical importance is limited for two reasons: (a) no formal derivation of his prediction was made; and, (b) some response learning (Underwood and Schulz, 1960) is presumably occurring in Postman's experimental situation. This is because, although the responses are highly familiar words, the subjects must learn which highly familiar words are possible associates of the stimulus items (unless it be presumed that all such response learning occurred by the end of the first exposure trial). It seems most appropriate to think of all-or-none theory as applying only to a single learning event, such as the association of a stimulus and response, not response learning plus association.  We have attempted to meet the former objection by mathematizing the Underwood and Keppel (1962) exposition of Hullian theory for paired-associate learning, making additional assumptions as needed to permit explicit prediction of such statistics as P(C2 I Ii). These predictions are given later in this article. To meet the latter objection, we have repeated Postman's Experiment I but used the integers "11" through "30" as responses after telling the subjects that these were the only responses to be employed. The stimulus items employed were the letters "A" through "1l," omitting "I" and "O" because of their similarity to numerals.  Because the present study, like most performed by the second group of investigators mentioned, employs one-to-one pairing of stimuli and responses, it seems appropriate to consider the possibility of a shifting guessing probability. For example, with all 20 items unlearned the probability of correct response by guessing should be approximately .05, but with 18 items learned, at least in part, the probability may be as high as .50, assuming that the subject can remember which responses have not yet been tied to stimuli. For that reason the theoretical section of this paper will consider application of a variable guessing parameter to several models previously discussed by Atkinson and Crothers, as well as to the Hullian model mentioned above. For most models both a fixed guessing probability of .05 and a variable guessing probability will be employed in order to permit comparison of the two approaches. Since an RNT5 design is used here, rather than the anticipation designs reported by Atkinson and Crothers, theoretical equations based on their various models have been slightly modified for this report.  METHOD  Subjects and conditions. The subjects were 144 high school juniors (1962-63 school year) who attended the 1963 summer session at the University of California, Santa Barbara. They were randomly assigned in pairs to one of four conditions (1, 2, 4, or 6 reinforcements).  Apparatus. A Kodak "Carousel 550" slide (2 X 2 in.) projector with remote control was used to project the learning materials onto a translucent screen, 9 X 12 in. in size. The screen  RNT5 DESIGN 445  was mounted in a 16 x 19 in. opaque panel. Inter-item intervals were automatically controlled by a Hunter (Model 125) Two Cycle Timer, with a stopwatch used to time the intertrial intervals. Subjects were run in pairs, separated for the experiment by a 15 x 28 x 58 in. metal file cabinet, and seated 7.5 ft from the screen. The projector was placed 36 in. on the side of the screen away from the subjects. Learning materials appeared as 2.25 in. high black figures on the screen. Slides were manually prepared, employing Kodak 2 x 2 in. Ready-Mounts, 135 size, flat and unsealed. Numerals and letters of black Transfer Type Cello-Tak, 0.3125 in. high were mounted on the slides. Subjects indicated their responses by pressing the appropriate keys of adding machines. Slides used on the training (reinforcement) presentations contained both stimulus and response units (with the stimulus, a letter) appearing on the left and the correct response, two numerals, appearing on the right), while slides during test trials contained only the stimulus units.  Lists. The learning materials consisted of 20 capital letters, "A" through "V" inclusive, omitting "I" and "O," paired with 20 numeral sets, "11" through "30" inclusive. Pairing of letters and numeral sets was random except that neither "A" nor "V" was ever paired with "I1" or "30," and no letter was paired with any numeral set more than once. Adjacent letters were never paired with adjacent numbers (either backwards or forwards). No letter-number pair appeared in more than one of the six lists used within every group. The arrangements of the lists were randomly established, separately for every training trial and every subject. Onethird of the subjects in each group received each of three specific five-trial orderings of stimuli, differing arrangements occurring on each test trial.  Procedure. Subjects were briefly instructed how to record two-digit numbers on adding machines. Two recording trials were permitted, using "95" and "96." The practice tape was then destroyed and subjects wrote their names on the top of the fresh tape. It was explained that a series of 20 pairs of letters and two-digit numbers would be exposed for a few seconds each on the screen in front of them. The subject's task was outlined as attempting to remember as often as possible which number went with which letter. Subjects were informed that each number from "11" through "30" was to be associated with a single letter. Each training slide was exposed for 4.5 sec with 0.5 sec changing time between slides and 20 sec between list presentations. At the conclusion of the training presentations, 45 sec were used for an intertrial rest and to instruct subjects that only letters would appear in the remaining presentations. Subjects were told to record on their adding machines the number previously paired with each letter. Guessing was encouraged, and if no guess was made, a blank space was to be left on the tape. Five test trials were then presented, using the same time intervals as outlined above for training.  THEORY  A linear habit strength model with a threshold assumption. Application of the long-short (LS) model. Atkinson and Crothers (1964) have developed a long-short (or LS) model, so called because it assumes that an item can either be stored in long-term memory (L), in which case the individual makes no more errors on it, or in short-term memory (S) in which case the correct response will bd made so long as the item does not leave short-term memory for a "lower state." The two lower states are forgetting (F), in which the subject will be correct with probability only g but has a fairly large probability of shifting the item into either L or S, and the uncoded (U) state in which g still applies but the probability of shifting into L or S is normally smaller than from F. Since space is not available to present the predictions for each of the 32 response patterns listed in Table 1, we comment on the general effectiveness of the various theories in fitting those data. All theories except that of no learning successfully predict that the average probability of 5 correct responses on the test trials will increase with N and that the average probability of 0 correct responses will decrease with N. The phenomenon of the data that is most clearly predicted by the more successful models and not by the others is as follows: Of the possible numbers (K) of correct responses, 0 and 5 always rank first and second in mean frequency of occurrence per pattern within any N value in the data, though not necessarily in that order. (In order to make the following comparison, the parenthesized numbers in Table 1 would each have to be divided by the number of patterns having 5 correct, 4 correct, or whatever may be the value of K under consideration. Then the predicted mean frequency per pattern within a K value predicted for each theory would be compared with it.) Of the seven models yielding chi-square values less than 1500 as shown in Table 3, all show K -= 0 and 5 as ranking first and second and in the same order as do the data, for each N. The model with a chi-square of 1507 shows the proper ranking for all N values except N = 1. No other model shows the proper ranking for more than two N values. Since the next lowest chi-square is 13,824, this suggests that a very modest standard of performance is required of the better theories: they must simply predict that the patterns for 0 and 5 successes are the most frequent of the 32 possible, and they must properly predict the rank order of those two frequencies for each N value. Differential performance among the seven best models seems to reflect numerical closeness between observed and predicted values, not effectiveness of matching the rankings for all values of K. RNT5 DESIGN  461  In conclusion, Postman's finding that P(C2, ; I,) is an increasing function of N has been confirmed by an experiment in which response learning (as opposed to associative learning) is minimal. This finding and other aspects of the data strongly contradict predictions from the one-element theory with g = .05. While no model has been shown to meet rigorous standards of predictive accuracy, several are moderately successful. One of these, not previously investigated in detail, was evolved from suggestions by Underwood and Keppel. That model is somewhat anomalous in that it is a Hullian model with linear rather than exponential growth of habit strength as a function of trials. However, excellent mathematical work with a Hullian linear habit strength model has previously been done (Ono, 1958, 1959), and no intent exists to generalize the present findings to models other than the specific ones tested here. As presumed by Underwood and Keppel (1962), as well as by Postman (1963) and by Wollen (1962), at least one Hull type model has predictive power in this situation. Because an arbitrary distributional assumption was made in it, this linear habit strength model with threshold assumption may have even better predictive possibilities than are shown above. The more conventional assumption of an exponential growth of habit strength with it should also be considered. However, this model and its competitors will not be evaluated on the basis or predictive accuracy alone; the intuitive appeal (to specific research workers) of the assumptions themselves and of the procedures used to develop them may be equally influential.
