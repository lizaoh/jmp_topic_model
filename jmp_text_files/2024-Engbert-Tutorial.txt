During text reading, we generate 3 to 4 fast eye movements (saccades) per second to move words into the center of the visual field for high-acuity processing. Almost all word processing happens during fixations, where average durations range from about 150 to 300 ms ( Kliegl, Grabner, Rolfs, & Engbert, 2004; Rayner, 1998 ). The number of fixations in a typical text is comparable to the number of words, however, some words do not receive a fixation (word skipping), while others are targeted several times. A secondary fixation of the same word as the currently fixated word is denoted as a refixation. The eye’s scanpath is even more complicated, because some saccades shift the gaze against the reading direction to previously fixated words or regions of text. Such regressions represent about 5% to 10% of the saccades for a typical text. Observed sequences of fixations during reading are characterized by sequential dependencies. Experimentally, these sequential dependencies are investigated via complicated designs such as the boundary paradigm ( Risse & Seelig, 2019; Starr & Rayner, 2001 ). In mathematical modeling, eye-movement control can be explained by dynamical theories that make specific assumptions on the observed sequential dependencies ( Engbert, Longtin, & Kliegl, 2002; Reichle, Pollatsek, Fisher, & Rayner, 1998 ).

In this tutorial article, we provide an introduction to dynamical modeling of eye movements in reading as an example for the dynamical systems approach to cognition ( Beer, 2000; Engbert, 2021 ). Potential readers might have varying degrees of interest in dynamical models, eye-movement control, or in one of following topics: Reading models, likelihood computation, Bayesian inference, Markov Chain Monte-Carlo techniques, or individual differences. What is specific to dynamical models is that they can predict a future data point from a current data point (the currently observed state) via simulation. This property of dynamical models can be exploited for their statistical inference, when time-ordered data are available and the likelihood function can be computed sequentially.

The dynamical model to be investigated in detail in this article is the SWIFT model ( Engbert et al., 2002; Engbert, Nuthmann, Richter, & Kliegl, 2005 ) of eye-movement control in reading. This model has been developed with the aim to provide a theoretically coherent framework for all types of eye movements during reading. Since the model is based on temporally evolving word activations, it is conceptually similar to the dynamic field theory of movement preparation ( Amari, 1977; Erlhagen & Schöner, 2002 ). The SWIFT

A major modification of the model was published in 2005, where we included, among other aspects, the full complexity of word-length effects and saccadic errors ( Engbert et al., 2005 ). It is important to note that a number of other models have been developed in parallel over the last 20 years (e.g.,  Reichle et al., 1998; Reilly & Radach, 2006; Snell, van Leipsig, Grainger, & Meeter, 2018 ), which are all good candidates for implementing Bayesian parameter inference as proposed in this tutorial.

The next major step in the further development of the SWIFT model was related to statistical inference. First, in  2020 , we proposed an approximate likelihood function for the model ( Seelig et al., 2020 ). The application of a likelihood-based Bayesian framework to a challenging data set was achieved in 2021, where we demonstrated that likelihood-based inference of the model provides the possibility to estimate model parameters for individual readers ( Rabe et al., 2021 ). Thus, for the first time in eye-movement research in reading, we were able to study interindividual differences in an advanced computational model. This result has been discussed recently in the broader context of dynamical cognitive modeling ( Engbert et al., 2022 ).

Because of the remarkable advances in the field of eye-movement modeling over the last 25 years, current models are elaborated and, therefore, are difficult to teach, in particular with respect to statistical inference ( Schütt et al., 2017 ). Therefore, for teaching and training purposes in dynamical modeling, it seems useful to start with a simplified version of the SWIFT model to introduce researchers to the key concepts of both dynamical models and their statistical inferences.

This tutorial article is organized as follows. In Section  2 , we derive and explain the simplified version of the SWIFT model, where the key concepts are temporally-evolving word activations by a set of ordinary differential equations and stochastic target selection for saccade preparation. In Section  3 , examples for simulated eye trajectories are discussed. We refer the reader to an  R -based computer implementation for running their own numerical experiments. The likelihood function of the simplified model is discussed in Section  4 . The likelihood function can be decomposed into temporal and spatial components. We report numerical simulations to explore profile likelihoods as a parameter identifiability analysis. In Section  5 , we introduce Bayesian inference and carry out Monte-Carlo simulations to estimate posterior densities over the model parameter space. With individual parameter estimates for a larger text corpus, we run posterior predictive checks to reproduce interindividual differences in reading behavior in Section  6 . We close with a short summary and discussion section. All computations are available (see Data Availability Statement below) in an implemented version in the  R -Language for Statistical Computing ( R Core Team, 2018 ).

The motivation to work with a simplified version of the SWIFT model is twofold. First, our aim was to provide a mathematical model that can be studied numerically on a desktop or notebook computer. Second, the focus in this tutorial is on the key principles of the model, neglecting more subtle effects of spatially-distributed processing ( Snell & Grainger, 2019 ). The major limitations of the simplified SWIFT model arise from the fact that space is approximated by discrete word positions, so word length effects ( Kliegl et al., 2004 ) and the complicated processes of random and systematic saccadic errors are neglected ( Engbert & Krügel, 2010; Krügel & Engbert, 2014; McConkie, Kerr, Reddix, & Zola, 1988 ). As pointed out above, the simplified SWIFT model shares all the critical dynamical principles with the full model, however, one limitation is that within-word fixation positions cannot be accounted for in the simplified model, since words are located at discrete positions but have no spatial extension by assumption. When working with the model on your own data, there are two different directions where the simplified SWIFT model could be helpful. First, the model can be used to obtain results on the overall reading statistics without investing much computing time. Second, if the interest is not specifically related to spatial processing, where within-word fixation positions are important, then the simplified SWIFT model could be a platform for implementing other aspects of reading research (e.g., learning studies, effects of syntax or semantics).

The profile likelihoods under single-parameter variation are highly informative for the modeler during both model development and implementation of the computation of the likelihood function. First, it can happen that the mathematical form of how a certain model parameter is specified does not have a strong impact on the model’s behavior. Consequently, a flat profile likelihood will indicate that the parameter is not identifiable ( Raue et al., 2009 ), which suggest to rethink and either omit the parameter or reformulate its mathematical form or scaling. Second, finding a maximum at the wrong position in the profile likelihood indicates errors in the likelihood computation. Therefore, the likelihood function provides an important consistency check before continuing with parameter estimation using Monte Carlo methods, which we discuss in the next section.

Before we continue with applying the Bayesian inference scheme of the simplified SWIFT model to experimental data, we should note that the careful analyses of the profile likelihoods and the parameter recovery simulations are not exercises for merely didactic purpose in this tutorial, but are essential for any model inference. A more comprehensive framework of how to implement a principled Bayesian workflow for cognitive modeling has been proposed by  Schad et al. (2021) , where the reader will find additional considerations for proper computational modeling.

After passing all of the test of the inference framework carried out above, we apply the same numerical procedures to the experimental data, which were taken from a previously published study ( Risse & Seelig, 2019 ). In a reading experiment, 34 participants were required to read 114 sentences that consisted of 6 to 12 words (see  Appendix ). The purpose of the study by  Risse and Seelig (2019)  was to investigate preview effects in a version of the boundary paradigm ( Starr & Rayner, 2001 ). The computational modeling in this tutorial, however, aimed at normal reading behavior. Therefore, experimental data used here were taken from the control condition (normal reading). The investigation of preview effects is beyond the scope of this article (see  Seelig, Risse, & Engbert, 2021  for details).

The amount of data of the control condition of the study by  Risse and Seelig (2019)  is representative for a typical eye-tracking experiment in reading. After preprocessing, a total of 30,639 fixations were used in the simulations. Data obtained for the first half of the sentences (14,926 fixations) were used for parameter estimation, while the second half of the sentences were used for model evaluation (15,713 fixations) in Section  6 . We estimated posterior densities independently for each participant. Computational modeling has become a critical tool in cognitive science, which supports or even replaces verbal-conceptual representations of cognitive theories ( Busemeyer & Diederich, 2010; Engbert, 2021; Lewandowsky & Farrell, 2010 ). The advances in cognitive modeling have resulted in an impressive increase in the complexity of cognitive models. As a consequence, the field now needs more teaching and training in model development, analysis, and statistical inference within an open science framework ( Barton et al., 2022 ). The aim of the current tutorial was to provide an introductory example that discusses all of these aspects in a succinct and practical way to motivate researchers to enter the field of cognitive modeling.

We believe that the simplified SWIFT model represents an instructive example for dynamical models ( Beer, 2000; Engbert, 2021 ), which aim at process-oriented modeling frameworks. Dynamical models generate time-ordered simulation data with sequential dependencies, which need to be compared to time-dependent experimental data to exploit the full potential of the models’ internal behavior. Statistical inference of dynamical models against time-ordered data defines the field of data assimilation ( Asch et al., 2016; Reich & Cotter, 2015 ). We recently discussed the benefits of data assimilation for dynamical cognitive models in a recent article ( Engbert et al., 2022 ), in which we suggested that Bayesian dynamical models might be able to integrate process assumptions (the strength of dynamical modeling) with interindividual differences (the strength of statistical modeling).

In the current work, we introduced a simplified version of the SWIFT model ( Engbert et al., 2002, 2005 ) of eye guidance in reading, which is based on some of the critical concepts of the currently most advanced version ( Rabe et al., 2021 ). Next, we developed the likelihood function of the simplified model which can be decomposed into temporal and spatial components ( Seelig et al., 2020 ). In the simplified model version, temporal and spatial processes are completely decoupled. Numerical analyses demonstrated that the temporal likelihood can only be influenced by the variation of the mean saccade timer interval, while the spatial likelihood only depends on the parameters that control the rate of processing.

Finally, we introduced a coupling between temporal and spatial control processes, which yields a dependence of the temporal likelihood on the parameter controlling the processing rate. Thus, the numerical analysis of the likelihood function and, in particular, the temporal and spatial likelihood components provide insights into the model’s structure, which can be compared to experimental data.

The importance of the likelihood function for dynamical models can hardly be overestimated as a tool for efficient and robust statistical inference. Recent examples of Bayesian inference of dynamical models of eye-movement control ( Rabe et al., 2021; Schwetlick, Rothkegel, Trukenbrod, & Engbert, 2020 ) might support this view. We presented MCMC simulations to explore the posterior density over the model parameter space. We demonstrate that model parameters can be recovered from simulated data with known parameters.

Next, we estimated model parameters for individual readers, which indicates a remarkable reduction of the required amount of data ( Rabe et al., 2021 ) for reading models that typically need data sets averaging over tens of participants. Using posterior predictive checks, we show that the simplified SWIFT model can reproduce interindividual differences in reading behavior via model parameter variation.

The simplified version of the SWIFT is built on all the critical dynamical principles of the full model. The major advantage of the simplified SWIFT is that all of the computations necessary for statistical inference can be done in on a desktop or notebook computer. Beyond this tutorial, there might be an additional value of the simplified SWIFT model as a research tool. First, since the model can be fitted to data sets with moderate effort, results on estimated model parameters give a first impression of the potential effects in your experimental data. To give a concrete example, the interpretation of experiments on the development of reading skill is supported by parameter estimation of the processing span (parameter  
                         ν 
                      ): For increasing reading skill level, we expect an increasing processing span (which is numerically indicated by a decreasing value of  
                         ν 
                      ). After such an application of the simplified SWIFT model, one might be in a better position to decide about possible option to build models for more specific effects. Second, an important limitation of the simplified model is that words have no spatial extension by assumption. If the reader is interested in spatial processing, then within-word fixation positions are critical, but the simplified SWIFT model could be a platform for implementing such effects or one might want to work with alternative models, e.g., the latest versions of SWIFT ( Rabe et al., 2021 ), E-Z Reader ( Reich & Cotter, 2015 ), or OB-1 Reader ( Snell et al., 2018 ), all of which can handle within-word fixation positions.

We hope that the example of the SWIFT model discussed here will serve as a useful starting point for eye-movement modeling. Furthermore, we believe that some of the problems in this tutorial will also be informative on the general approach of dynamical cognitive modeling and Bayesian statistical inference.

Experimental data used in this study were published previously ( Risse & Seelig, 2019 ), where an eye-tracking experiment was carried out to investigate effects of parafoveal word difficulty on fixation durations. In the experiment, 34 participants, most of them students of psychology, University of Potsdam, were required to read 114 single sentences shown on a computer screen. The German sentences consisted of 6 to 12 words. Every sentence contained a gaze contingent invisible boundary before a specific target word. Data used in this study were limited to the control condition, so that no display change occurred in our experimental conditions. Word frequencies were taken from the dlexDB database ( Heister et al., 2011 ).

The experimental data were collected using an Eyelink 1000 Tower-Mount System (SR Research, Osgoode, Ontario, Canada) with a temporal resolution of 1000 Hz. Spatial resolution was preprocessed to letter accuracy, however, in the current study spatial information was restricted to the fixated word. Fixation durations smaller than 25 ms or larger than 1000 ms were discarded. Trials consisting of fewer than three fixations were also removed from the data-set. Additionally, re-readings signaled by regressions starting from the second last or last word of the sentence and all subsequent fixations were discarded. After preprocessing, 30,639 fixations from 3422 trials were included in the data-set for our numerical simulations.
