Fishburn,  Foundations of risk measurement, 1. Risk as probable loss. Mimeo., Bell Laboratories, 1981) focussed on risk as probable loss.  The second part considers measures of risk that include effects of gains on perceived risk. It adopts the position that  increased gains can reduce the risk of fixed probable losses without completely negating this risk. Moreover, every prospect  that has no chance of yielding a loss is presumed to have no risk. Several numerical measures of risk are axiomatized. The  more specialized are separable in gains and losses. Further specializations isolate the effects of loss and gain probabilities.    1. INTRODUCTION    the second of two papers in a study of axiomatic foundations of risk measurement. The first paper (Fishburn, 1981)--referred  to henceforth as F81-looked at risk as a function of the probability of loss and the distribution of losses, which corresponds  to "pure risk" in insurance (Kogan & Wallach, 1964; Williams, 1966). The present paper considers "speculative risk," which  involves prospects that have possible gains as well as losses. My purpose is to propose and axiomatize risk measures that  incorporate effects of gains on risk in the latter situation.  sures are motivated by studies which establish the salience of gains as well as losses on perceived risk (Slovic, 1967; Payne,  1975; Aschenbrenner, 1978; Coombs & Lehner, 1981), by observed differential preference attitudes toward gains and losses  (Fishburn & Kochenberger, 1979; Kahneman & Tversky, 1979; Payne, Laughhunn, & Crum, 1980; Laughhunn, Payne, & Crum, 1980;  Hershey, Kunreuther, & Schoemaker, 1981), and by the conventional notion that risk is a chance of something bad happening. The  measures incorporate the consensus of the first set of studies that risk increases as loss probability or amount increases,  and that greater gains and/or gain probabilities reduce perceived risk. As cautioned in F81, readers are advised not to  confuse the present conception of risk with other uses of this term in the literature of decision theory.    s for reprints should be sent to Dr. Peter C.
