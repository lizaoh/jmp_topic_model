Multisensory processing  generally refers to the ability to detect multiple signals from different sensory modalities. In many applications, multimodal stimulation increases the efficiency, measured by reaction times (RTs) of an information processing system compared to unimodal stimulation  (Colonius & Diederich, 2012; Miller, 1982; Raab, 1962) .

In a now classic study on multisensory processing,  Miller (1982)  conducted a redundant-target audiovisual detection task where participants had to simultaneously monitor auditory and visual modalities and detect the presence of an auditory target (i.e., a pure tone) or a visual target (e.g., a star presented on a computer screen). Mean RTs in the audiovisual condition (i.e., both auditory and visual targets were presented) were found to be faster than the mean RTs in the unisensory conditions (i.e., either an auditory or a visual target was presented). This well-known effect is termed the redundant-target effect (RTE) which has been a robust finding in the study of response time  (Diederich & Colonius, 1987; Gondan, Niederhaus, Rösler, & Röder, 2005; Hughes, Reuter-Lorenz, Nozawa, & Fendrich, 1994; Miller, 1982; Raab, 1962; Townsend & Nozawa, 1995)

Broadly speaking, the RTE has been explained by: (1) parallel models with separate channels for auditory and visual activation or information accrual  (Gielen, Schmidt, & Van Den Heuvel, 1983; Raab, 1962)  and (2) coactive models  (Diederich, 1995; Miller, 1982; Schwarz, 1989, 1994) . Separate-activation models assume that auditory and visual signals are processed simultaneously and accumulated into two separate accumulators ( Fig. 1(a) ). A detection decision is determined by the accumulator which reaches its decision criteria first. According to the separate-activation model, the RTE occurs due to statistical facilitation from audition and vision since the minimum time of detecting the redundant target is stochastically faster than either of the single targets (Raab, 1962)  .

Alternatively, the coactive model assumes that auditory and visual information originating from parallel channels are combined into a single accumulator (see  Fig. 1(b) ). When the summed activation reaches the decision criterion, a target is detected. According to the coactive model, RTE occurs due to the increased information accumulation rate (for instance, as dictated by a Poisson summation process; e.g.,  Townsend & Nozawa, 1995  and  Schwarz, 1989 ).

The first question that arises concerns how one might empirically distinguish parallel processing from coactive processing. One of the most general tests, historically, has been to use an inequality to determine whether the processing rate in the redundant-target condition differs from the bound predicted by a parallel processing model assuming independent channels.  Miller (1982)  introduced the test of the race-model inequality (RMI or Miller inequality) to distinguish between the coactive model and the separate-activation model. This assumption implies that the distribution of the processing time for a channel (i.e., audition) under unimodal stimulation should be identical to the distribution of the processing time for that channel under audiovisual stimulation. Unfortunately, this assumption does not necessarily hold in audiovisual detection.

Two ways in which the assumption of context invariance might fail are if the mean or the variance of one of the channels increases or decreases when presented together with the other channel compared to when presented alone. If the mean RT of a channel decreased or if the variances of a channel increased when presented as part of a redundant target, then this would allow a separate-activation model to violate the RMI  (Otto & Mamassian, 2016) .  Otto and Mamassian (2012)  found that data from an audiovisual redundant-target detection task was best fit by a model that allowed for increased noise in both channels when both targets were presented. Although this model was an independent parallel race model, the violation of context invariance allowed the redundant-target RTs to exceed the bound provided by the RMI. Second,  Mordkoff and Yantis (1991, 1993)  demonstrated that the RMI can be violated when the occurrence of a signal can predict the presence of another signal due to inter-stimulus contingencies (i.e., stimulus presentation effects).

Finally, even when context invariance holds, the RMI does not provide unambiguous evidence of processing architecture. Instead, the RMI provides an upper bound on the  capacity  of an independent parallel race model (  Townsend and Eidels, 2011 ; see also  Ashby and Townsend, 1986 ;  Townsend and Ashby, 1983 ). Hence, the RMI is an assessment of a system’s capacity and not the architecture of a processing system; rather, each of these attributes of a processing system is logically distinct from the other. For instance, a supercapacity but independent parallel system could still violate the RMI. Likewise, parallel models whose channels partially interact without completely pooling information into a single decision (as in a coactive model) may also violate the RMI depending on the degree of interaction ( Altieri and Yang, 2016; Eidels, Houpt, Altieri, Pei, and Townsend, 2011 ; see  Fig. 1(c) ). Finally,  Townsend and Nozawa (1997)  provided proofs that the antipodal (i.e., to a parallel system) serial exhaustive model could also violate the RMI (see also  Cheng, Moneer, Christie, & Little, 2017; Little, Eidels, Fific, & Wang, 2015 ).

In summary, in order to determine whether redundant-target audiovisual processing is best captured by a separate-activation model or by some form of pooling across channels requires some test other than the RMI.  Townsend and Nozawa (1995)  developed alternative measures to determine mental architecture, which we apply in the present paper and discuss in the following section.

Along with the development of the capacity coefficient inEq.  (5) ,  Townsend and Nozawa (1995)  developed a comprehensive framework for differentiating mental architectures. These measures, along with capacity and related developments for non-parametrically analyzing properties of information processingsystems, have been collectively termed  Systems Factorial Technology  (SFT; for recent reviews, see  Algom, Eidels, Hawkins, Jefferson, and Townsend, 2015 ;  Little, Altieri, Fific, and Yang, 2017 ).

Processing capacity and architecture are two closely related though logically distinct properties. Specifically, in redundant-target processing, architecture refers to how multiple signals from audition and vision are processed prior to a decision (serial/parallel/coactive) whereas processing capacity denotes the change in processing efficiency as a function of workload (i.e., the number of channels or modalities to be monitored, one or two or more;  Blaha and Houpt, 2015 ). As illustrated above, a violation of the RMI is directly related to processing capacity but only indirectly related to mental architecture. Our goal in the present paper is to tease apart whether observed violations of the RMI are due to a true facilitation between channels (including coactivity) or due to a violation of context invariance in a separate-activation model.

We note for clarification, that  Townsend and Nozawa (1995) , and subsequent research utilizing SFT, reserves the term  coactive  for models that demonstrate a complete pooling between channels.  Miller’s (1982)  use of the term coactive (and subsequent papers following in that tradition), however, applies to any model which violates the RMI and, hence, also includes, as a subclass, parallel models with a smaller degree of facilitation between channels.  Miller (2016)  additionally proposes that models which violate context invariance should also be considered a form of coactive model. The reason for this is that Miller’s use of coactivity is contrasted with the alternative  race  model metaphor, which encompasses all separate activation models with and without stochastic dependence (i.e., correlation) of finishing times. In  Miller (2016) , violations of context invariance are considered coactive because there must be something about the presence of a target in the other channel which causes the previously single target channel to alter its rate of processing. Hence, for Miller, “coactivation models are defined by the idea that both stimuli can influence the response on a single trial” ( Miller, 2016, p. 518 ). However, throughout we argue, as others have argued before (e.g.,  Ashby & Townsend, 1986; Townsend & Eidels, 2011; Townsend & Wenger, 2004a, b ) that violation of the RMI is not a test of model architecture but a test of workload capacity. Hence, we restrict our use of the term coactive to stricter definition referring to a complete facilitatory pooling of information across parallel channels. We use the term  parallel interactive  (with the interaction being either  facilitative  or  inhibitory ) to refer to cases with less than complete pooling between channels. Finally, we denote models with violations of context invariance as a separate category since, as we illustrate below, these violations can arise even in parallel models with independent channels without any stochastic dependencies. We feel these definitions provide the appropriate level of nuance for understanding the complete range of findings we observe in our experiments. In other words, our definition allows us to actually test the different consequences of violations of context invariance compared to co-activation or parallel interaction (see e.g.,  Townsend & Wenger, 2004a ). That these different models mimic each other under some tests (e.g., RMI) is not reason to conclude that they are the same.

Procedure  
                      
                         . 
                       On each trial, after a foreperiod which was randomly chosen from an exponential distribution with a mean of 300 ms, a test stimulus was presented for 150 ms. The manipulation of random foreperiod was to avoid anticipation of the presence of a target (see  Luce, 1986  [alp], pp. 51–56). Data analysis.  First, survivor functions were estimated using the empirically-derived RT distributions. We then tested the selective-influence assumption, which assumes that the salience manipulation of one channel only affects the processing speed of that channel and that this manipulation does not affect the processing speed of the other channel  (Dzhafarov, 1999; Kujala & Dzhafarov, 2008; Townsend & Thomas, 1994) . We conducted two levels of analyses (i.e., mean RT level and distribution level). First, a 2 (high/low salience of the auditory target)  
                         × 
                       2 (high/low salience of the visual target) analysis of variance (ANOVA) was conducted on the mean RTs of the redundant-target conditions. We expect significant main effects of the two factors. Second, we conducted Kolmogorov–Smirnov (K–S) tests in order to verify the selective-influence assumption at the distribution level. The ordering of the RT distributions was examined by using eight two-sample K–S tests (i.e., HH >HL, HH >LH, HL >LL, LH >LL, HH <HL, HH <LH, HL <LL, and LH <LL; see  (Houpt et al., 2014) ). If the selective-influence assumption holds, we expect that the former four tests are significant, and the latter four tests are not significant. See  Houpt and Townsend (2010)  for more details.

If the selective-influence assumption is verified, then the MIC and SIC were computed in order to infer the mental architecture and stopping rule. Two methods were adopted to confirm the value of MIC. First, a two-way ANOVA was conducted to examine the mean RTs of the redundant-target conditions. A significant interaction effect indicates that MIC is not 0, and that the two factors are non-additive, ruling out serial processing as a viable explanation. Second, the 95% confidence interval (CI) for MIC was simulated based on a nonparametric bootstrapping method  (Van Zandt, 2000) . If the 95% CI for MIC does not include 0, we infer that MIC is not equal to 0.

Next, we used the SFT R package  (Houpt et al., 2014)  to separately assess the positive-going deviations from SIC = 0 and the negative-going deviations from SIC = 0. Two one-sided K–S tests were conducted; one test tests whether the largest positive value of the SIC (D+) is significantly different from zero, and the other test tests whether the largest negative value of the SIC (D-) is significantly different from zero (Houpt–Townsend statistic,  (Houpt & Townsend, 2010) ). Because the null hypothesis for the Houpt–Townsend statistic is SIC(t) = 0 for all times  t , a conservative significance level would bias the test toward retaining the null hypothesis (i.e., serial self-terminating model). Discussion

In audiovisual discrimination, all the participants had similar patterns of results, suggesting that they adopted similar processing strategies. The participants had positive MIC and positive SICs for all times  t , and their capacity coefficient was greater than 1 at the faster time points with a violation of the RMI. These results suggested that all the participants adopted parallel processing and followed a self-terminating stopping rule with supercapacity processing. Although we did not observe a significant initial negative deflection in the SIC, visually there is some indication that some initial negativity might be present. Consequently, our failure to find a significant negative deflection may simply be due to the fact that we have finite data in this region of the SIC. This tempers our conclusions against coactivity somewhat. We likewise observed RTs that violated the RMI as many audiovisual studies have demonstrated.

In Experiment 2, we tested audiovisual signal recognition using a simultaneous matching task. Previous studies regarding the perceptual comparison process using multi-attribute stimuli suggest that it may involve a variety of processing mechanisms—thus making the task more complex than Experiment 1 (see  Farell, 1985  for a review). That is, multiple attributes can be compared holistically (e.g.,  Lockhead, 1972 ) or analytically (i.e. feature-by-feature) via a serial (e.g.,  Egeth, 1966 ), independent parallel (e.g.,  Taylor, 1976 ), or a facilitatory parallel mechanism  (Miller, 1986) . Nonetheless, no studies, to our knowledge, have considered the perceptual comparison process in audiovisual signal processing usingSFT.

If audiovisual perceptual matching is based on a bound audiovisual representation, we should expect to observe coactive processing with supercapacity. Alternatively, if the audiovisual matching can be achieved through a feature-by-feature, analytic comparison process, we predict that participants will adopt parallel processing or serial processing in audiovisual matching with corresponding unlimited or limited capacity, respectively.

Method

Participants.  Three students (S1, S4, and S5) from National Cheng Kung University volunteered to participate in the experiment (ages from 20 to 27). Each received NTD 150 per hour for participation. Each participant had normal or corrected-to-normal vision. Each signed a written informed consent prior to the experiment.

Stimuli, design, and procedure.  Participants were required to match two simultaneously presented Gabor patches (at the left side and right sides of the screen) and pure tones (from the left and right speakers). If the two auditory and visual stimuli were identical, participants had to emit a “same” response by clicking the left button of the mouse; otherwise, they had to emit a “different” response by clicking the right button of the mouse. The time course of the procedure was the same as that used in Experiment 1. One of the visual stimuli was the reference stimulus (i.e., a horizontal Gabor patch with 0.1 contrast; the reference stimulus was the same throughout the experiment). The other visual stimulus was manipulated to be the same as the reference stimulus or different from the reference stimulus with a small difference (low salience, 0.3 contrast) or a large difference (high salience, 0.5 contrast) from the reference stimulus. Likewise, one of the auditory stimuli was the reference stimulus (i.e., a 1200-Hz pure tone presented as 50 dB). The other auditory stimulus was manipulated to be the same as the reference stimulus or different from the reference stimulus with a small difference (low salience, 59 dB) or a large difference (high salience, 64 dB) in loudness from the reference stimulus. Therefore, there were four types of trials including both-different trials (i.e., the test stimulus and the reference stimulus were different in both auditory and visual dimensions), auditory-different trials (i.e., the test stimulus and the reference stimulus were different in the auditory dimension), visual-different trials (i.e., the test stimulus and the reference stimulus were different in the visual dimension), and both same trials (i.e., the test stimulus was identical to the reference stimulus). The four types of trials were equally probable within a block; for different trials, salience differences were equally probably and combined factorially across sources.

Discussion

In Experiment 2, we found individual differences in the perceptual comparison process in audiovisual simultaneous matching. S1 showed positive MIC and SICs for some times  t , suggesting the adoption of parallel self-terminating processing for audiovisual information in perceptual matching whereas S4 and S5, they had zero MICs and a near zero SIC for all times  t , suggesting that they adopted a serial self-terminating processing strategy (although there were some positive and negative deflections in the SIC). All the participants had  C(t)  violating RMI at the faster time points  t , suggesting that they had supercapacity processing.

Although we observed supercapacity with a violation of RMI for all the participants, we did not observe the S-shaped SIC; hence, similar to Experiment 1, we ruled out the coactive model. Our tentative conclusion is that the perceptual comparison on audiovisual signals does not rely on a bound representation; rather, the audiovisual signals are compared in an analytic feature-by-feature fashion, either in parallel or perhaps serially.

Interestingly, our results showed evidence for individual differences. The results of S1 can be well explained by a parallel interactive race model or by a separate-activation parallel model with a violation of context invariance. Results of S4 and S5 suggest that both adopted a serial self-terminating processing strategy with supercapacity—under the assumption of context invariance, a serial model will predict unlimited capacity. Consequently, this result may also reflect a violation of context invariance. We explore this possibility after presenting Experiment 3.

In Experiment 2, we investigated how signals are processed in an audiovisual perceptual matching task in which the perceptual comparison process is based on the current percept. Here, we examined how audiovisual signals are processed in an audiovisual delayed matching task, where participants retrieve audiovisual information from memory and compare it with the current percept.

Method

Participants.  Three students (S1, S4, and S5) who participated in Experiment 2 volunteered to participate in this experiment. Each received NTD 150 per hour for participation. Each participant signed a written informed consent form prior to the experiment.

Stimulus, design, and procedure.  The stimuli and procedure were the same as those used in Experiment 2 except for the followings: (1) the visual stimuli were presented at the center of the screen. (2) On each trial, a memory display and a test display were presented in sequence. Each display lasted for 150 ms with a 1000 ms ISI (inter-stimulus interval) in-between the two displays. Participants were asked to match the audiovisual signals between the two displays. In Experiment 3, on half of the trials, the reference stimulus was presented in the memory display and on the other half of trials, the reference stimulus was presented in the test display.

Results: Experiment 3

All the participants had ceiling-level accuracy in matching the auditory and visual stimuli. Across all the conditions, mean accuracy was .99 with a standard deviation of .02. Correct RTs were extracted for further analysis.  Table 2  shows the mean RTs of the both-different conditions and MIC for each participant.

In order to test whether context invariance was violated for the redundant audiovisual targets compared to the single targets, we follow  Otto and Mamassian (2012)  by adopting a simple recinormal model of RTs ( (Carpenter & Williams, 1995) ;  (Reddi, Asrress, & Carpenter, 2003) ) for the single targets. This allows for a straightforward investigation of the effects of correlation and violation of context invariance on capacity for the redundant targets. The recinormal model of RTs assumes that the reciprocal of the RT distribution, which can be loosely interpreted as the rate of rise (or drift rate; cf.  (Brown & Heathcote, 2008; Ratcliff, 1978; Smith & Ratcliff, 2004) ) of a linear deterministic growth model, is normally distributed. This model (instantiated more formally as the LATER model by  (Reddi, Asrress, & Carpenter, 2003) ) is not a complete model of RTs because it lacks a number of mechanisms necessary to account for correct and error RTs along with the joint probabilities of choice and RTs (e.g., the non-decision time mechanisms known to play a role in decision process; between-trial variability in starting point; e.g.,  (Brown & Heathcote, 2008; Luce, 1986; Ratcliff, 1978; Ratcliff & Rouder, 1998) . It is also far too flexible to be a viable process model of RT (see e.g.,  Jones & Dzhafarov, 2014 ). Nevertheless, the assumption that the reciprocal of RT is normally distributed has the convenient property that we can use the known distribution of the maximum of Gaussian distributions to model the minimum RT distribution expected under the separate-activation model.

Unequally-weighted Parallel Model.  It is possible that the observation of the serial SIC can be predicted by an unequal-weighted parallel self-terminating model. That is, audiovisual signals are processed in parallel but they are processed at different processing speeds due to the unequal attentional weights. If participants considered that visual signals are more reliable for their decisions as visual information may dominate decisions, they may pay more attention to the visual stimulus and process it at a higher processing speed, potentially resulting in a SIC function which is similar to the prediction of the serial self-terminating model. We tested this by fixing the mean processing time and variance in one channel and systematically slowing the mean processing time of the other channel. In order to preserve the correlated relationship between the increasing mean and variability of RT, we also increased the variability of the slowed channel  (Wagenmakers & Brown, 2007) . Rho in this simulation was set at -.4 to capture the negative correlation between channels (see  Otto & Mamassian, 2012 ). The results of this simulation are shown in  Fig. 14 . In line with our observed data, these results show that slowing one channel can indeed result in an SIC function which approaches 0 for all  t.  Likewise, the slowing of one channel can result in capacity which moves across  t  from supercapacity to unlimited capacity. Importantly, unlike the serial capacity simulation, the unequally-weighted parallel model capacity is above the Grice bound. Hence, these simulations provide existence proof that this model could provide an explanation for our observed data from Experiments 2 and 3. One caveat is that biasing one channel of the parallel model results in a separation in the survivor functions along the biased channel; that is, if the first channel is biased, then the HL survivor function is pushed toward the HH function while the LH function is pushed toward the LL function, which we do not observe in our data.

Audiovisual detection has been investigated for a few decades (e.g.,  Miller, 1982 ); however, audiovisual discrimination versus recognition has not received much attention until recently. Although studies have reported violations of the RMI for audiovisual discrimination  (Blurton et al., 2014; Gondan et al., 2010, 2005; Miller, 1982; Sinnett et al., 2008; Wada, 2010) , the question of whether a generalized (i.e., consistent) mental architecture arises across variable experimental designs remains unclear.

It may be, for example, that the human brain is hard-wired to process audiovisual signals in a specialized, modular way regardless of the type of stimuli presented or the task. If this is indeed the case, we should observe, say, parallel (interactive) architecture regardless of whether elementary detection or audiovisual speech identification is being examined. Accordingly, the SIC and capacity should show consistent profiles across experimental designs, stimulus types, and perhaps even individual participants. Alternatively, there may be substantial differences across tasks and contexts, which would suggest that audiovisual processing is highly context dependent. If this is indeed the case one must be cautious when drawing quantitative or qualitative conclusions when comparing data across different experimental designs.

To address this broad question, our study investigated the mental architecture of audiovisual signal processing under three general experimental designs common to the psychological literature: audiovisual discrimination (Experiment 1), audiovisual simultaneous matching (Experiment 2), and audiovisual delayed matching (Experiment 3).  Table 7  summarizes the results of all the three experiments.

In the audiovisual discrimination task (Experiment 1) where participants had to discriminate visual or auditory targets among audiovisual distracters, results showed that all the participants had positive MICs and SICs for most times  t  with a violation of the RMI. While these results are consistent with a facilitatory parallel interaction, we suggest that the results are also consistent with a separate-activation, parallel model in which the violation of the RMI is caused by a violation of context invariance. Our simulations, in which the variance of the channels in the audiovisual target was increased were able to produce this qualitative result (see  Fig. 12 ). In other words, audiovisual signals did not pool together to form a cohesive representation prior to decision-making, nor were they processed sequentially. The discrimination of an audiovisual target is carried out by separate auditory and visual accumulators, but when there are multiple channels operating simultaneously, the variance of those channels is increased.

Experiments 2 and 3 investigated the comparison processes used in audiovisual recognition. To our knowledge, no prior studies have investigated audiovisual comparison processes using SFT; the present work is thus the first to systematically examine how audiovisual signals are compared for decision-making and its system’s capacity. Experiment 2 used a simultaneous matching task requiring participants to make audiovisual comparisons between two percepts. A cursory examination of the SIC/MIC results showed evidence for individual differences in the mental architecture (see  Table 7 ). For S1, we observed a positive MIC and SIC for some times  t  together with a violation of the RMI; our simulation results suggest that this can be explained via parallel processing with a violation of context invariance (see  Fig. 12 ). On the other hand, the results of S4 and S5 showed a zero MIC and flat SIC for all times  t  together with a violation of the RMI. Their MIC and SIC results appeared to suggest that they adopted a serial self-terminating processing strategy with supercapacity (see  Table 1 ). When the same participants in Experiment 3 were asked to perform an audiovisual delayed matching task rather than a simultaneous matching task in Experiment 3, the SFT measurements showed the same pattern as Experiment 2. One notable difference, however, was a comparable decline in capacity from supercapacity to unlimited to limited-capacity seen in Experiment 3 (see  Table 7 ).

What do these results suggest? According to  Houpt and Townsend (2010, 2011) , coactive models (e.g.,  Schwarz, 1989, 1994; Townsend & Nozawa, 1995 ) predict a distinctive S-shaped form of SIC (see  Fig. 2  ). From the data in each of our three Experiments, the observed SIC did not strongly exhibit the characteristic form for the coactive models although in some cases there was suggestive but nonsignificant evidence of the initial SIC negativity predicted by the model. A tentative conclusion would be that coactive models were not supported by the data even though we observed the RMI was consistently violated in Experiments 1 and 2 as  Miller (1982)  reported. Hence, our data do not provide convincing evidence that audiovisual signals are pooled together into a single accumulator prior to decision-making (see  Fig. 1(b) ).

One obvious complication is that individual differences in the SIC were observed in Experiments 2 and 3: two participants were inferred to adopt a serial self-terminating processing strategy (flat SIC and 0 MIC) combined with supercapacity in audiovisual simultaneous matching and with unlimited-to-limited capacity in audiovisual delayed matching. This combined SIC and capacity data pattern seems rather interesting because standard serial models are assumed to possess limited capacity. These findings may have been observed because participants adjusted the rates of evidence accumulation in visual and auditory modalities differently  (Gondan et al., 2010; Gondan, Lange, Rösler, & Röder, 2004) . In  Gondan et al. (2010) , some participants increased the evidence build-up in visual module with the cost of decreasing evidence build-up in auditory module only whereas other participants adopted a serial self-terminating processing strategy. The authors argued that the response conflict raised by combinations of audiovisual targets and non-targets (i.e., auditory target and visual non-target or auditory non-target and visual target;  (Fournier & Eriksen, 1990; Grice, Canham, & Boroughs, 1984) ) resulted in the serial self-terminating strategies. The response conflict could have also occurred in the present context of audiovisual matching (Experiments 2 and 3); that would explain the observation of the serial self-terminating processing strategy. Nevertheless, our simulation analysis provided an existence proof that these serial self-terminating results were consistent with an unequal-weighted parallel model (see  Gondan et al., 2010  p. 1706, for related discussion).

Additionally, we suggest two possible mechanisms to account for the observation of parallel processing with supercapacity in audiovisual discrimination and recognition (all the participants in Experiment 1 and S1 in Experiments 2 and 3). First, auditory signals may enhance the accumulation of the visual signals at the pre-accumulator, perceptual processing stage and vice versa (see e.g.,  Bernstein, Rose, & Ashe, 1970; Nickerson, 1973; Todd, 1912 ). Second, identifying or recognizing a target may increase the arousal of the participants such that participants will prepare their response for another target (e.g.,  Nickerson, 1973 ).

Our preliminary conclusion, which should be examined using combined SFT and neuroimaging methodology in future research, is that auditory and visual recognition should be modeled via separate accumulators with different accumulation rates and different levels of variability between auditory and visual modalities. This makes sense, given that auditory and visual processing regions lie in separate but anatomically connected brain regions. The magnitudes of differential weighting across these regions vary due to the task contexts. For instance, delayed matching requires participants to retrieve audiovisual information from memory and compare it to current percept whereas discrimination and simultaneous matching do not require memory retrieval. Attention can further influence audiovisual signal processing (e.g.,  Altieri, Lentz, Townsend, & Wenger, 2016; Andersen, Tiippana, & Sams, 2004 ).  (Tiippana, Möttönen, & Schwartz, 2015) , for example, reported evidence suggesting that attention can influence ERPs and the early binding of auditory and visual speech signals.
