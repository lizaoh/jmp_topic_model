INTRODUCTION 

Suppose someone, a, believes that the majority of the world’s wilderness areas should be preserved, while b believes that most should he sacrificed for imniediate economic gain. Since a’s attitude is more proconscrvation than b’s, there is an obvious ordinal difference between them. However, is there more than an ordinal difference here? In particular, is there a quantitative difference, in some sense of that word? The view that attitudes can be measured on an interval scale (at feast) requires the existence of quantilative differences between attitudes. Phat is, the familiar attitude measurement techniques, such as Thurstone’s (1927) methods deriving from his law of comparative judgment, Likert’s (1932) method of summated ratings, and Ekman’s (1962) adaptation of Stevens’ method of magnitude estimation, are all based upon the presupposition that there are quantitative difference between attitudes. Despite the ubiquity of this presupposition, it is not often tested directly. However, as 1 show in this paper, Coombs’ (1964) theory of unidimensional unfolding enables a simple test to be made.

NECESSARY AND SUFFICIENT CONDITIONS FOR UNIDIMENSIONAL UNFOLDING 

Typically, unfolding theory is applied to preference orders (i.e., a set of transitive, asymmetric, and connected orderings of some finite set of stimuli). This is what Suppes and Zinnes (1963) cali a preferential system. They note that “there are no simple necessary and sufficient conditions to indicate when a given preferential system is a Coombs systern” (1963, p. 46), where by a “Coombs system” they mean one that unfolds unidimensionally. Since then three conditions have been identified by Michell (1990) and, more formally, by Doignon and Falmagne (1991). I call these conditions the folding condition, the single-path condition, and the cancellation condition. They are explained informally in this section and formal definitions are given in Appendix 1. 

(i) The Folding Condition 

These conditions can be illustrated by taking the case of six unidimensional stimuli ordered on the relevant dimension as A, B,C, D,E, and F. That is, in Coombs’ {1964) terminology, ABCDEF is the qualitative J scale. An individual's preference ordering of these six stimuli (an / scale, Coombs (1964)) could be, in principle, any of their 6! permutations. However, Coombs’ theory requires that each J scale must be related in a special way to the qualitative J scale. This special _ relation derives from the fact that each J scale “may be thought of as the J scale folded at the ideal point” (Coombs, 1964, p. 80). Here, by the “J scale,” Coombs means the quantitative J scale, that is, the stimuli as quantitatively located unidimensionally. Considering only the qualitative J scale, this fact means that each I scale must be some “folding” of the qualitative J scale around any one of the stimuli. For example, an J scale formed by folding around C will have D, E, and F in that order (ie., their order within the qualitative J scale) and A and B in reverse order (i¢., the opposite of their order within the qualitative J scale). These three inequalities are contradictory because they fail to satisfy the double cancellation condition of conjoint measurement theory (Luce and Tukey, 1964). As noted by Krantz, Luce, Suppes, and Tversky (1971), double cancellation is just one of a hierarchy of cancellation conditions that an ordering upon a product set must satisfy for an additive representation. A general schema of this condition was first given by Scott (1964, Theorem 3.1). It is a formula which for any finite » specifies the finite set of cancellation conditions necessary and sufficient for an additive representation. An order upon a set of interstimulus midpoints, will be unidimensionally realisable only if it satisfies the hierarchy of conditions given by Scott (1964, Theorem 3.1). Thus the third condition may be stated as follows. 

Cancellation condition: A set of I scales unfolds unidimensionally only if the order upon the interstimulus midpoints (determined by satisfaction of the single-path condition) satisfies the hierarchy of cancellation conditions given by Theorem 3.1 of Scott (1964). This is really a finite collection of conditions for any finite stimulus set, though a complete set of conditions cannot be finitely stated for aif finite stimulus sets (Scott and Suppes, 1958). 

Obviously, each of these conditions is necessary for unidimensional unfolding. That they are jointly sufficient follows because satisfaction of the cancellation condition ensures a unidimensional stimulus representation and the folding and single-path conditions ensure that the J scales are therein represented. 

The folding condition is purely ordinal in the sense that J scales satisfy the proposition in (2) if they satisfy the folding condition. On the other hand, satisfaction of the single-path and cancellation conditions is not entailed by (2) and, so, they must depend upon the proposition in (3). Hence, they possess quantitative content and depend upon the existence of attitudinal distances. In particular, double cancellation is often thought of as a test of additivity (Coombs, 1983). The smallest n that enables a test of double cancellation is 6, so this is what is required in order to test the hypothesis that attitudinal differences are quantitative.

CONSTRUCTING SETS OF UNIDIMENSIONAL STATEMENTS 

The tests to be conducted here require unidimensional sets of statements in which the order of the statements along the dimension is easily discernable. With such a set, the ordinal predictions {i.e., the folding condition) should be confirmed. Here Coombs’ theory has little surprise value. However, a priori there seems to be no reason to suppose that the quantitative predictions (ie¢., the single-path and cancellation conditions) will be confirmed unless attitudinal differences are quantitative. Hence, the fit of the data to the folding condition will provide a baseline relative to which the fit of the data to the quantitative predictions can be assessed. If the fit to the ordinal component of Coombs’ theory is satisfactory and the fit to the quantitative component is at least as good then the hypothesis that attitudinal differences are quantitative will be supported. 

A procedure for constructing unidimensional sets of statements is easily given. I assume that it is only the semantic attributes of such statements that are relevant in this context. Only statements of the form X is Y (where XY, the subject term, denotes the object of the attitude, and Y, the predicate term, expresses an attitude towards it) will be considered. Since a unidimensional set of statements will all be about the same object, they will differ from one another semantically only with respect to the predicate. Hence, attention may be restricted to predicates. 

One way to construct ordered sets of predicates is via a binary tree structure. One begins with a kernel concept (say, the moral worth of some policy) and makes an initial binary division into predicates which are (a) fairly general, (b) mutually contrary, and (c) ordered relative to the kernel concept (e.g., continuing the example of moral worth, good and bad). Each node may then be subjected to further binary divisions according to the same principle, an additional requirement

SUBJECTS AND METHOD OF DATA COLLECTION 

Even given the semantic structure of the predicates in these two sets of statements, detection of the relevant dimension requires well motivated, literate subjects of at least average intellectual competence. The subjects used were all third year undergraduate psychology students at the University of Sydney during the years 1989 and 1990. There were 174 of them, 121 female and 53 male. The data were collected as part of a class exercise in the introductory tutorial of a course on psychological measurement. They were informed that the data would be analysed in later tutorials to illustrate various methods of measurement. Each subject was given a test booklet consisting of the above tweive statements, together with others. Each set of six statements generates 15 distinct pairs. The statement pairs from both sets were presented together in a random order (both within and between pairs). Subjects were instructed to judge within each pair which statement they agreed with most, or disagreed with least (if they agreed with neither statement). Following these pair comparison judgments, the subjects were instructed to rank order each set of six statements (according to their strength of agreement with them) without consulting their prior judgments. After the judgments had been made the test booklets were handed in to the course tutor. 

The responses from one subject were discarded because she made just 10 of the 30 pair comparison judgments and another subject declined to respond to the nuclear war statements.

It is interesting to speculate upon what other factors there might be. Considering the cognitive and response processes involved, the following very general steps may be identified. 

(1) Subjects must understand the pair of statements and perceive their order along the dimension. 

(2) Subjects must then perceive the order relation between this pair of statements and their own attitude. 

(3) Subjects must then respond in a way that communicates that perception. 

Of course, it is always possible that a subject may inadvertently respond in a way that does not match his or her perceptions. However, if it is assumed that such factors operate independently of the stimulus pair involved, then errors due to this cause should be fairly equally distributed across all stimulus pairs. Now, the proportion of errors is quite unevenly distributed across pairs, slightly less than half the stimulus pairs being associated with no erroneous judgments, while on others the proportion of errors is high. This is consistent with the view that response execution is a negligible sourse of error and that Steps 1 and 2 are more promising candidates. 

With respect to Step 2, there are many ways in which other factors could intrude. One significant possibility is that some subjects might not have a fixed attitude of their own (De Soete, Carroll & De Sarbo, 1986). One way of estimating the extent to which this is the case is to consider each subject’s most preferred stimulus in relation to the one ranked first (in the rank order judgments). Of course, these are not independent because in ambiguous cases the rank order data was used to identify the most preferred stimulus. However, if in such cases (overall there were only six cases where more than one statement was maximally preferred by any one subject) the statement with the largest ordinal deviation from the one ranked first (amongst those with maximal preference scores) is temporarily regarded as the most preferred stimulus, then an estimate of the fluidity of subjects’ attitudes may be obtained. In relation to the homosexuality statements, the most preferred statement was the stimulus ranked first 76.2% of the time. For the nuclear war statements, it was ranked first 77.9% of the time. Thus, the attitudes of a significant number of subjects were fluid enough to produce a change in most preferred stimulus between the pair comparison and rank order tasks. This fluidity, then, may well have been a source of violations of the proposition (2}. 

If one considers those subjects whose most preferred statement changed (between pair comparison and rank order) and those whose most preferred statement did not, then the former on average made more unilateral errors than the latter. In relation to the homosexuality statements, those without change made on average 0.318 errors on unilateral judgments, while those whose most preferred statement changed made an average of 0.525 errors. The other possible source of violations of the proposition (2) is failure to complete Step1 adequately. There is some evidence to suggest that the order relations between some pairs of statements may have been difficult to discern. Most dramatically, 50 of the 64 unilateral errors made on the homosexuality statements were made with the BC pair. As is evident from Table 1, errors made on other homosexuality stimulus pairs were negligible. This may have been caused by difficulty in seeing which statement of the two is most favourable towards homosexuality. It is not hard to see the source of this difficulty. In attempting to avoid repetitious wording of statements, some of the relevant content of B and C was more implicit than explicit. According to the structural diagram in Fig.4, the relevant content of B is that homosexuality is (i) not immoral, (ii) not superior to heterosexuality, and (iii) that it should be universally encouraged. From the explicit content of B these contents follow, though not perhaps without further premises (which it was thought subjects would take as read in the context). Similarly, it was intended that C imply the same content with the exception of (itl), where the restriction to consenting adults was made explicit. The presumption that subjects would reliably make these inferences was obviously mistaken. Without making these inferences and relying solely upon the manifest content of the statements, some subjects were confused about the order. In retrospect it would have been wiser to signal common content by common wording and to have made B, for example, something like “I have no moral objections to homosexuality and would like to see it encouraged universally.” 

Likewise, in relation to the nuclear war statements, 18 of the 39 unilateral errors made occurred on the EF pair. One cannot read E as asserting a nuclear war to be any less likely than as asserted by F. However, if the “almost” in E is ignored then E and F have the same content and it becomes an arbitrary choice how one would respond. (The balance may be tipped in favour of E because “not” is underlined). But why should a subject ignore “almost”? One can only speculate that the negation in E caused confusion in the minds of some subjects. 

Another 8 of the unilateral errors were made on the AB pair and 8 on the BC pair. Strictly speaking, had the structural diagram in Fig. 3 been followed to the letter, B would have explicitly negated A, and C negated B, These features of B and C were deleted (making them less wordy) and it was thought that in the context subjects would read them as containing those negations. Some subjects may not have done this and so may have seen them in the wrong order. Obviously, there is a need to word statements more carefully than was done in this study if errors of these kinds are to be minimized.

Thus, the fact that the bilateral judgments’ conformity to the theory matches that on unilateral judgments suggests that on these judgments also the theory identifies the major cause operating. In the case of unilateral judgments the major cause operating is obviously the order of the statements along the dimension relating to the subject’s point of maximum preference. That order is explicit and, in the cases identified as unilateral judgments, the relation between that order and the subject’s point of maximum preference is quite objective. Hence, the cause identified in this case is an objective, identifiable feature of the situation. The proposition in (2) appears to give us a useful and reliable generalisation about human behaviour. Useful because the antecedent conditions can be identified and reliable because having identified these conditions predictions may be made with greater than 90% accuracy (in the sort of context studied here).

For the proposition in (3) to be a similarly useful generalisation about human behaviour, the antecedent stimulus properties must likewise be identified. It is not helpful to conclude that the cause of bilateral judgments is attitudinal distance unless the stimulus properties determining attitudinal distance are independently identifiable. If the distances between stimuli are only identifiable via application of unidimensional unfolding theory then precisely what is meant by such distances remains obscure. The meaning of such distances must reside in the semantic structure of the attitude statements. 

Of course, it may be the case that there really is no objective basis for attitudinal distances. The demand characteristics of the data collection situation may be such as to cause the subjects to believe that distances between attitudes are real and so each subject imposes his or her own subjective distances on the unidimensional structure. However, there is then no reason to assume that all {or most) subjects would impose the same metric on the stimulus set. If they did not then there would be no strong expectation for the dominant path through the proximity graph to satisfy double cancellation. There are 286 distinct descending paths through Fig. 1. There are 118 of these paths which fail double cancellation. The probability of a randomly chosen path not failing double cancellation is then 0.5874. Hence, the probability of a randomly chosen path not failing double cancellation on four independent trials (1989 and 1990 on both Nuclear War and Homosexuality data sets} is 0.119. This is not impossible. However, an explanation that located a cause for this result in the semantic structure of the statements would be more plausible. CONCLUSION 

This study shows that when carefully constructed sets of statements are used as stimuli, both the ordinal and quantitative components of Coombs’ theory of unidimensional unfolding provide convincing explanations of pair comparison preference judgments. This provides evidence for the hypothesis that attitudinal differences are quantitative, an hypothesis that is often presumed true in psychological research but one that is rarely tested. If there are quantitative differences between attitudes towards the same object then this quantitative character must have a source within the logical and semantic structure of the statements used. Speculations have been made here as to what this source might be. The significance of this research into the foundations of attitude measurment is that it shows that an assumption common to techniques as varied as Thurstone’s, Likert’s, and Ekman’s is not only testable via Coombs’ theory of unidimensional unfolding but may, in fact, be more than a pious hope.
