WALLSTEN2  Department of Psychology, University of North Carolina, Chapel Hill, North Carolina 27514  A theory of how opinions are formed and revised on the basis of probabilistic evidence is presented. The decision maker is viewed as a limited information processor who attends to the dimensions of a sample of equivocal information in a sequential fashion, beginning with the most salient dimension and continuing in decreasing order of salience. The contribution of each dimension level to the final opinion depends on the strength of association between that level and each of the two hypotheses under consideration. This theory is represented formally as an additive-difference model, various special cases of which correspond to other algebraic models in the literature. Two of the special cases were empirically investigated under varying levels of experience and payoffs, using the techniques of conjoint-measurement theory and ordinal data from individual subjects. The data provided reasonably good support for the models under all conditions investigated, and in addition showed interesting effects on salience and on processing of the independent variables.  1. In these, and virtually all decision situations, opinions are formed and revised on the basis of ambiguous, or probabilistic, evidence. This paper is concerned primarily with the information processing by which such opinion revision takes place.  The problem of how ambiguous information is processed has generated considerable interest in recent years and there exist at least five apparently different approaches to it. One is based on the linear regression model and has been reviewed by Slovic and Lichtenstein (1971) and by Dawes and Corrigan (1974). Another approach to the study of probabilistic inference is based on Bayesian statistics, reviews of which can be found in Edwards (1968), Rapoport and Wallsten (1972), and Slovic and Lichtenstein (1971). Yet another framework within which these problems have been viewed is that of Anderson's (e.g., 1971) theory of information integration. Examples of this work are by Shanteau (1970, 1972) and by Leon and Anderson (1974). A fourth direction from which probabilistic inference has been studied is that of conjointmeasurement (Krantz, Luce, Suppes, & Tversky, 1971). This approach was introduced by Wallsten (1972), and provides the framework for the present paper. Finally, Tversky and Kahneman (1974) have attempted to explain certain aspects of probabilistic inference with the use of descriptive heuristics rather than models.  Note that the first four methods each employ a formal model or composition rule (Krantz & Tversky, 1971) to understand human inference, whereas the last approach relies on a more informal and less precisely developed "processing" theory. The former techniques share the advantage of allowing well-specified comparisons between models and data. Correspondingly, however, it is often difficult to generalize results beyond particular experiments. The latter technique, on the other hand, provides compelling generalizations, but in the absence of a formally developed model, it is difficult to determine the circumstances under which the generalizations apply.  It is not uncommon to obtain the advantages of both approaches by specifying a general process theory in such a way that it can be represented by well-defined formal models in particular situations. Thus, a particular model applies to specific kinds of data, but the general theory provides the interpretation of its parameters, predictions as to how the parameters will vary as a function of experimental conditions, and the means for generalizing conclusions across tasks. This interplay between model and theory forms the basis for this report, although it should be made clear at the outset that the models which follow were obtained initially as natural generalizations of the one discussed by Wallsten (1972), and that the process theory was developed after using the models to analyze the present data. A PROCESSING THEORY  The theory to be outlined here borrows from Slovic (1972), and is similar in certain respects to Tversky and Kahneman (1973, 1974). These authors, as well as others, have suggested that since humans have limited cognitive abilities, they form simple situation-bound strategies for processing information.  We are concerned with a decision maker (DM) who must process ambiguous information in order to decide which of two alternatives, X or Y, is more likely correct.3 In general, the information can be considered as a complex stimulus with a finite number of dimensions, where the dimensions may occur simultaneously or sequentially in time. For example, in diagnosing whether a patient has tuberculous meningitis, one dimension might be his temperature, another the results of a Tine test, a third the results of a cerebrospinal fluid culture, etc.  The informational stimulus can be represented by its values on each of its L dimensions. It is reasonable to assume that each dimension value has an association with hypothesis X and an association with hypothesis Y, and that the stronger the association between a dimension value and an hypothesis, the more that value suggests the hypothesis is correct. Thus, the subjective diagnosticity of a dimension value is a function of the relative strengths of the two associations. The strength of an association, in turn, depends on two things. One is the subjective likelihood that the value of the dimension will occur if the corresponding hypothesis is true. In this sense, the two associations are the subjective correlates of the numerator and denominator of a likelihood ratio. But the strength of association is also assumed to depend on the salience of the corresponding hypothesis. That is, the DM may differentially attend to the two hypotheses, or indeed not attend carefully to either, and this will affect the association strengths.  It is further assumed that stimulus dimensions, like hypotheses, vary in saliency. The effect of a dimension on a DM's opinion is positively related to its salience, and often the least salient dimensions will have no effect at all. It is convenient to assume that the most salient dimension is processed first and results in an initial opinion favoring an hypothesis to a degree which depends on the dimension's value. This is similar in spirit to the "anchoring and adjustment" process discussed by Slovic (1972). Whereas Slovic speaks only of an adjustment process that is crude, we are suggesting more specifically that the opinion modification becomes smaller as saliency decreases, and that in addition, depending on when processing is terminated, less salient dimensions will not consistently have an effect on the final judgment.  In general, it can be assumed that the salience of a dimension is relative to the entire display and depends, among other things, on the degree to which the dimension is perceptually given or must be inferred (Slovic's "concreteness," 1972), the range of values of the dimension, and the DM's experience in the situation. Similarly, the salience of an hypothesis depends in part on the nature of the task, the prior probability of the hypothesis, the utility to the DM of that hypothesis being correct or incorrect, and the DM's past experience. In this context, the situation-bound strategies referred to earlier can now be specified as determining the order in which dimensions are processed, i.e., as determining dimension salience, and as determining the relative attention afforded each of the two hypotheses.  The DM's strategy must also determine at what point he or she will cease processing dimensions when confronted by a particular display. Various probabilistic and deterministic stopping rules are possible, but we will only note here that in the experiments which follow, the number of dimensions available for processing was quite small. The nature of the stopping rule remains to be specified in subsequent research.  In summary, when a DM processes equivocal information prior to deciding between two hypotheses, the information is considered dimension by dimension in decreasing order of saliency. The diagnosticity of each dimension depends on the relative strengths of the associations between the dimension value and each of the two hypotheses. The first dimension processed provides the anchor for the judgment, and subsequent dimensions cause successively smaller adjustments of the initial opinion. Processing continues either until all the dimensions have been considered or until a stopping criterion has been reached, following which the DM overtly responds.  3. ALGEBRAIC MODELS  Before proceeding further it is necessary to make a distinction between the processing of information in order to form an opinion and the subsequent choice of an action or response based on that opinion. The theory just described is concerned with the former of the two processes, whereas the factors which determine the latter undoubtedly are motivational in nature and depend on the DM's goals. The two processes will interact, of course, in that one decision may be to get more information. This distinc-  148 THOMAS S. WALLSTEN  tion is not original, and indeed it is central to signal detection theory where the factors which influence the likelihood ratio of an observation are thought to be different from those which determine the response criterion.  It is impossible to study information processing without paying some heed to the decision making aspects of a task, since any observable behavior will of necessity reflect both factors. In order to avoid commitment to any of the available decision theories, but yet still be able to formulate the information processing model so that it has empirical bite, we will make the following assumption. When a DM assesses information in order to choose between hypotheses X and Y, and then provides a probability or a confidence estimate in, say, X, his response will be monotonically related to his underlying certainty. Developing a processing model which requires ordinal rather than interval or stronger data properties has the additional advantage of generality. Thus, it will be the case that the model developed here subsumes as special cases most of the models referenced in the Introduction, which otherwise superficially appear rather different from each other.  The particular model used to formalize the general theory depends on the portion of the theory under consideration and on the experimental paradigm within which it will be studied. Our concern in this paper is with the joint questions, (1) How do hypothesis-dimension value associations combine to yield the diagnosticity of a dimension? (2) How do the diagnosticities of separate dimensions combine? (3) What are some of the determinants of relative salience ? We will not attempt to determine whether processing is serial or parallel, nor how processing is terminated. Thus, the model that is appropriate for our purposes is an algebraic composition rule, with the nature of the composition rule being relevant to the first two questions above and the values of the parameters under various conditions being relevant to the third question. The paradigm is similar to that employed in Wallsten (1972) and is in the "book bag and poker chip" tradition. That is, DMs observed a sample of binary data in order to decide which of two a priori equally likely binomial distributions was more likely the parent. The present theory can be applied to this task by considering each event to be an independent dimension of the sample. Thus, each dimension takes on one of two levels, and the binomial parameters nominally designate the hypothesis associations of the dimension levels. Although this identification may seem artificial, it does provide a means for obtaining generalizations which apply easily to other, more complex tasks. Furthermore, it also provides a natural way to separate the two determinants of the strength of hypothesis-dimension value associations, that of subjective likelihood and that of hypothesis salience. Finally, if hypothesis and dimension salience can be shown to operate in this paradigm, it is a safe bet that they operate in other more complex ones.  The difference function. THE EXPERIMENTS  Two separate experiments were performed. In the more extensive one (Experiment I), the subtractive and distributive models were investigated independently of each other, and relations between the two models were also evaluated. Due to insufficient data, only the distributive model was studied in Experiment II. Each of the models entails a number of independent necessary conditions (specified as axioms in conjointmeasurement theory) which are empirically testable. Thus in principle, it should be possible to determine not only whether a model fails, but how it fails. When a model is not rejected, it can be used with ordinal data to obtain scale values (Wallsten, 1974).  The strategy of the present research called for crossing the required factorial designs with other independent variables which may affect information processing, but about which the algebraic models are silent. The models were evaluated and scale values were derived at each level of the latter variables. In terms of the general theory, axiom checking provides insight into how dimensions and hypotheses are combined under the various conditions, whereas comparison of derived scale values indicates the effects of conditions on relative salience given that the dimensions and hypotheses are processed.  The two independent variables of experience and payoff were superimposed on the factorial designs required for the models. Considering payoffs first, a strictly proper scoring rule (Aczel & Pfanzagl, 1966; Stael von Holstein, 1970), the spherical rule, was used to pay subjects according to their responses, R(X, Y I D) or 1 - R(X, Y I D), where R(X, Y I D) can now be thought of concretely as the subject's probability estimate that hypothesis X rather than Y is correct given data D.  To understand the spherical rule, let r be the DM's estimate that his decision, X or Y, is correct, and s = 1 - r be his estimate that it is wrong. Denote the event that occurs, i.e., correct or incorrect decision, by z, and the probability estimate of that event by t, i.e., t = r or t = s according to whether z = "correct" or z = "incorrect." Then the score the subject receives is  S(z, r) = a + b[tl(r2 + s2)1/2j, (12)  where a>0andb>0.  Two groups of subjects were formed in each experiment, according to values assigned to the constants a and b. One group (PN for Positive-Negative) won money for correct decisions and lost money for incorrect decisions, whereas another group (PP) always won money, but won more for correct than for incorrect decisions.  It has been established previously that when paid according to strictly proper scoring rules, subjects' responses depend not only on the probabilities of the events, but also on the signs of the payoffs (Jensen & Peterson, 1973). There is no compelling reason to expect that manipulation of the sign of the payoff will affect the processing  156 THOMAS S. Thus, if the distinction made earlier between the two processes is a viable one, then the payoff manipulation can be expected to affect the DM's overt response without affecting his prior information processing. In other words, our prediction is that the ordinal properties of the confidence estimates, which are assumed to depend only on the processing of information, will be the same for subjects in both groups PN and PP, but that the magnitude of the confidence estimates, and the function relating estimates to model parameters (cf. Eq. (4)), will be different for subjects in the two groups.  Experience was considered an important independent variable, since it seemed likely that a subject would process information differently after considerable practice at a particular task than before. Indeed, studies employing experienced subjects generally have yielded different conclusions than have studies using naive subjects (Rapoport & Wallsten, 1972). Although experience was expected to affect the processing of information, there were no a priori grounds on which to predict the nature of the effect. The interesting question was simply whether the presumed change would express itself in the model, and be understandable within the general theory.  Experience was both a between and a within subject variable. Subjects in Experiment I were run considerably longer than those in Experiment II. Data from Experiment I were collapsed over the entire study for analysis and for comparison with Experiment II, but comparisons were also made between the initial and final sessions of Experiment I.  A. Method  Apparatus and procedure. A PDP-8 computer was programmed to run the experiment. Each subject sat in a small cubicle in front of a teletype upon which stimuli were presented and responses obtained. The computer ran three or four subjects independently and simultaneously, each in his own cubicle.  On every trial the subject observed events to decide which of two a priori equally likely binomial distributions, A or B, was the parent. The experimental design was quite similar to that in Wallsten (1972). A block of 16 decision problems was obtained by crossing the four binomial distributions in the set W = (0.6 - 0.4, 0.7 - 0.3, 0.8 - 0.2, 0.9 - 0.1) with the four in the set Z = (0.4 - 0.6, 0.3 - 0.7, 0.2 - 0.8, 0.1 - 0.9), where the first number in each pair is the probability of the event "1." Within each block of 16 problems, half the time distribution A came from W and distribution B came from Z, and half the time this was reversed. For each problem in a block of 16, the subject observed a fixed number of randomly drawn events in order to decide whether A or B was the parent. Depending on the block this was either 1, 2, or 3 events.  This arrangement provided the factorial designs needed to evaluate both the subtractive and the distributive models, as shown in Table 1. Consider the distributive model, Eq. (11), first, which applied when all events in the sample were identical and for which the factorial Xi x Y; x n was required. The present experiment provided a 4 x 4 x 3 design in which the levels of Xi, nominally designated by P(E I Xi), were 0.6, 0.7, 0.8, and 0.9, for i = 1,...,4, respectively, the levels of Y; , designated P(E I Y;), were 0.4, 0.3, 0.2, or 0.1, for j ~ 1,..., 4, respectively, and the number of events, n, was 1, 2, or 3. GENERAL DISCUSSION  The present analyses yielded a number of interesting results which can be encompassed within the general theory presented earlier. However, it is necessary to comment first on the discrepancy between Experiments I and II in validating the distributive model.  Looking at either stress or -r, it appears that given sufficiently stable data, the model equally well describes subjects at both experience levels. However, only half the subjects in Experiment II were relatively consistent in their responding. This may have been due to the smaller number of trials in Experiment 11, or it may reflect learning and set effects. Subjects in the two experiments were prepared for different numbers of sessions, and may have approached their tasks differently. It should be pointed out that with the exception of certain applied research (e.g., Schum, Goldstein, & Southard, 1966) virtually all studies in this area have employed group analyses on data collected from subjects with even less experience than those in Experiment II. At the very least, the present differences between the two groups of subjects suggest that conclusions  PROCESSING UNCERTAIN INFORMATION 181  from many previous studies are of limited generality, and that caution should be exercised in incorporating their results into any general theory.  With the exception of those subjects whose responding was rather unstable, the two specific models which were tested survived rather well the detailed individual subject analyses. Thus, the basic assumptions leading to the models are supported, and the processing theory which these assumptions represent is viable. Our purpose now is to use the obtained pattern of results to make inferences within the context of the theory about the determinants of the relative salience of hypotheses and of dimensions, and about how processing strategies are affected by experience and payoffs.  Recall that subjects in the present experiment were required first to give a binary response indicating which hypothesis was more likely correct. Then they were asked for a quantitative response reflecting their subjective uncertainty in the preceding decision. If this sequence of responding structured the processing of the information, then it is reasonable to distinguish the hypotheses for purposes of the quantitative response as the one stated to be more likely, X, and the one stated to be less likely, Y. Associations, therefore, were between dimension values of the information sample and the hypotheses which had already been designated by the subject as more or less likely correct.  Consider now the slope ratios S(N)/S(D) and S(Or)/S(Q obtained with the homogeneous data samples, and how they were affected by experience (Table 7). Recall that these ratios indicate the relative salience of hypotheses X and Y, correcting for differences in objective probabilities. In an earlier study (Wallsten, 1972), S(N)JS(D) was greater than 1 for each subject, indicating that X was always more salient than Y. However, in the present experiments many subjects found Y to be more salient (S(N)/S(D) < 1). Furthermore, the overwhelming effect of experience for virtually all subjects was to increase considerably the salience of Y (S(~&S(0z) < 1).  Although initially the present results seem counterintuitive and inconsistent with the previous data, the entire pattern can be understood by assuming that subjects dynamically seek out and adopt strategies which reduce their information processing load. One way to reduce processing is to focus more on one hypothesis than on another. Since in the present design the objective likelihood ratio varies more steeply as a function of Y than of X, and therefore more information is to be had from the former than the latter alternative, it is reasonable to expect experienced subjects to ease their processing load by relying more on Y than on X.  It can be supposed that in general the more likely hypothesis X is initially more salient, with subjects perhaps reasoning that their opinion need not depend on an alternative that is probably incorrect. Since subjects in the earlier experiment (Wallsten, 1972) made a small number of decisions and were paid independently of their responses, they had neither the time nor the incentive to learn a more efficient strategy for focusing attention, and therefore their slope ratios consistently were greater than 1. The present subjects, however, made a large number of decisions with monetary  182 THOMAS S. WALLSTEN  consequences. Therefore, some of the subjects quickly reduced their processing requirements by focusing on the less likely, but more informative hypothesis Y, and virtually all of them learned with experience to depend more strongly on Y when presented with homogeneous samples.  When faced with heterogeneous rather than homogeneous data samples, the pattern of results concerning hypothesis salience (Table 10) was similar, but not identical, to that just described. We may suppose that the presence of conflicting evidence in a sample results in a more difficult information processing task than otherwise, and therefore that subjects eased their cognitive load somewhat earlier in the experiment by attending to the more diagnostic hypothesis Y. Thus, for virtually all subjects hypothesis X was generally less salient than Y, and for most of the subjects the salience of X continued to decrease from the first to the second half of the study.  The axiom violations obtained in Experiment I are also consistent with the interpretation that X is generally less diagnostic and less salient than Y. Virtually all the violations involved the factors P(E I X) or P(D I X), whereas none expressly involved P(E I Y) or P(D I Y). Furthermore, the violations primarily occurred with subjects for whom the ratios S(N)IS(D) were less than 1.  We now turn to the manner in which redundant dimensions, or events, in a data sample were processed to arrive at a final judgment. The relative salience of redundant events was influenced both by experience and by context in interesting ways. Recall that the diagnosticity of two or three identical events relative to 1, i.e., 0a(2) or 0a(3), was greater in Experiment II than in Experiment 1. 0a(2) values obtained in Wallsten (1972) were intermediate between those obtained here in Experiments I and 11.  These results all can be understood by assuming (i) in a homogeneous data sample, redundant events are not very salient; (ii) the effect of payoffs is to increase processing, i.e., more dimensions are processed; and (iii) the effect of experience is opposite to that of payoffs and is to focus processing on fewer dimensions. The three assumptions appear quite reasonable and are consistent with the orderings of 0s(2) and 0a(3) observed in the three experiments. However, the last assumption also predicts that the scale values should be more extreme in the first half of Experiment I than in the second half, and that result was not observed. As indicated above, subjects in Experiment I knew how long they were to run, and it is possible that they were set to ease their cognitive task early in the experiment, long before the first half was completed. Therefore, they quickly learned to ignore redundant dimensions, and no differences appeared between the two halves of the experiment.  The effects of context on the salience of redundant events was dramatically demonstrated when the rank order of the responses given mixed data samples was predicted not by the scale value of the two events in a homogeneous sample, but'by treating two events as twice as diagnostic as one. In other words, redundant events in a data sample were relatively salient when the sample contained other conflicting evidence, but not when it did not. Thus, it appears that subjects were flexible in their processing  PROCESSING UNCERTAIN INFORMATION 183  strategies, altering them as a function of the nature of the information sample. The other independent variable studied in the present experiments was that of payoffs. As expected, this variable did not influence the manner in which information was combined, nor did it have an effect on the relative salience of the different dimensions or hypotheses. However, the effect on response magnitude was considerable, and is interpretable in terms of considerations of risk avoidance, rather than information processing. In another experiment we have established that payoffs uniquely associated with a particular hypothesis do influence the salience of that hypothesis (Delaney & Wallsten).'  6. CONCLUDING REMARKS  The foregoing developments are interesting on a number of grounds. The algebraic work demonstrates a unity among the various approaches to the study of equivocal information processing that was not apparent before. Most of the models advocated in the literature can be derived from the single more general additive-difference formulation presented here. The models differ from each other primarily in the assumptions made about the difference functions, the scale values, and the response function, as well as in the nature of the factorial design to which each model can be applied. Furthermore, the basic assumptions of the general model can be embedded within a much broader theoretical framework which provides a means for generalizing conclusions beyond the relatively narrow paradigm necessary for using and evaluating a particular model.  Another feature of the present work worth emphasizing is the total reliance on ordinal data in conjunction with the structure provided by axiomatic measurement theory. The latter provides theoretical justification for interpreting derived parameters as measures of psychological states or processes, and indicates the properties of the measures which can be relied upon in theorizing (i.e., specifies levels of uniqueness of the measures).  Although the processing theory and the subsequent discussion are somewhat post hoc, the basic framework does have prior roots. The concept of subjects using simple strategies to process various dimensions of information in order to arrive at a decision has been put forth before (e.g., Slovic & Lichtenstein, 1968; Payne, 1973). Furthermore, Slovic and Lichtenstein (1968) have shown that the relative importance, or salience, of dimensions can be manipulated experimentally. In addition, the concept of salience, as used here, corresponds quite closely to the interpretation normally afforded the derived coefficients in the regression models. First, in the present development, the manner in which the dimensions of the information sample and the alternative hypotheses contribute to the final judgment is specified explicitly via the models of that process.
