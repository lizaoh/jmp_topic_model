Multiple Parameter Estimation in an Adaptive Psychometric Method: MUEST, an Extension of the QUEST Method 

Peter R. Snoeren and Marco J. H. Puts 

Nijmegen Institute for Cognition and Information, University of Nijmegen, Nijmegen, The Netherlands E-mail:snoeren�nici.kun.nl 

Adaptive Methods 

An extension of the so-called QUEST method of Watson and Pelli (1979, 1983) is proposed. In contrast to the traditional QUEST method, the new method, the MUEST method, offers the possibility of simultaneously estimating more than one parameter of a parametric representation of the psychometric function. Knowledge about more parameters gives a higher guarantee of obtaining correct threshold values. The basic extension itself is analytically quite modest and can be explained in a few sentences. However, the involved additional techniques, like the placing of stimulus levels, require a more extensive description, which is presented here. ] 1997 Academic Press 

INTRODUCTION 

Psychometric Function 

Psychophysics examines quantitative links between externally controlled stimuli and internal mental experiences. For example, the relation between the perceived sweetness and the concentration of the sweetener in units of moles per liter is such a link. Obviously, the first is not a physical quantity (one can only provoke relative sensory responses of sweetness, not absolute ones). In a typical psychophysical � experiment, with a yes no design, the subject is forced to assess the presence of the sweetener for various concentrations. Assigning zero to a ``no'' and one to a ``yes'' and averaging over many repetitions leads to the so-called psychometric function. This function returns the fraction of correct responses in a discriminative task for all feasible stimulus levels. Of course, when the experimental design is chosen differently, another function will be obtained. For example, when a two-alternativeforced-choicedesign (2-AFC) is chosen, in which a control sample (tap water) and a test sample (sugar water) are used, and the query is to discriminate between the two, the psychometric function will be offset by chance level (at 1�2 instead of 0 in the yes�no design).

One practical objective of psychophysics can be formulated as follows: derive the psychometric function when only the correctness of a finite number of responses at different stimulus levels is given. The simplest method to measure the psychometric function is the method of constant stimuli, an experimental design in which the experimenter knows the stimulus levels in advance. However, in this method the previously defined stimulus set probably also contains uninteresting points, e.g., stimulus levels where the curve is flat and boring, and thus this method is an exhausting way of measuring. Less time consuming methods have been developed that use prior information to assign new stimulus levels, so-called adaptive methods. It is beyond the scope of this paper to discuss them all (see Treutwein, 1995, for a survey), but an exception is made for the QUEST method (Watson 6 Pelli, 1979, 1983) because this method forms the basis of the extension discussed here. 

One may ask, why another psychometric method? The answer is that still no ``ideal'' method exists for all applications. Often, dubious assumptions are made that cannot always be legitimized beforehand. Because the QUEST method is used as a base, many explicitly and implicitly incorporated assumptions are also taken from it. The innovation of the MUEST method rests in the fact that one restrictive assumption is relaxed. 

QUEST METHOD 

In this section a summary of the relevant steps in the derivation of the QUEST method is given. In addition, a section is devoted to a particular choice of the placement of stimulus levels.

Assumption 1. The psychometric function has the same shape under all conditions, when expressed as a function of log stimulus level. From condition to condition, it differs only in the position along the log stimulus-level axis.[1] 

Here, different conditions are discerned by factors that are kept under the control of the experimenter. In the example of the sweetener, a condition could be the chemical composition of the sweetener. The assumed shape constancy of the psychometric function can be seen as part of the a priori information available to the experimenter. A sampled psychometric function can be measured for a certain stimulus condition (with the method of constant stimuli, for instance) to measure the shape of the psychometric function, and used later in the adaptive method, in the same or other conditions, to measure the horizontal shift. This is not done in practice, instead an analytical form of the psychometric function with estimated shape parameters is used. In their paper, Watson 6 Pelli used the Weibull psychometric function (Weibull, 1951) for this purpose. Placement of the Stimulus Levels 

Although the experimenter is free to choose new stimulus levels at will, not all stimulus levels will narrow the posterior pdf by the same amount. For example, a stimulus at level x where 9(x) & 1 will usually result in a correct response and the variability of the corresponding shift parameter will be large. A reasonable choice of placement can be obtained by minimization of the sweat factor, a measure of efficiency defined by Taylor (1971). 

To find the best stimulus level to measure at (in the sense that the expected variance of parameters is minimal), one should know what the psychometric function looks like. However, in practice, the true psychometric function is not known (that is why we plodded away through all this trouble after all). The best alternative is using the latest parameter estimate for the psychometric function, i.e., the estimate following from the posterior pdf P(: | Mn , H).

MUEST METHOD 

The MUEST method differs from the QUEST method in the number of estimated parameters. In the QUEST method a single parameter is estimated, whereas in the MUEST method it is in principle possible to estimate as many parameters as wanted. One reason to use a multiple parameter estimation instead of a single parameter estimation is that Assumption 1 is not always satisfied. Departures from the restriction in Assumption 1 can be found within subjects and between conditions (e.g., Maloney, 1990; Stromeyer, 1992). Another reason is that the experimenter may not be able to set the correct shape parameters. For example, setting the slope parameter ; to the wrong value will result in a converged, though biased, estimate of the shift parameter : in the QUEST method (see Fig. 3A). Releasing ; in the MUEST method solves this problem (see Fig. 3C), though this release is not without a cost.

As an illustration of the MUEST method the parameters : and ; are used as the free parameters in this paper. This leaves the parameters # and $ as frozen parameters.

Placement of the Stimulus Levels 

Of course, arbitrary values can be assigned to the stimulus levels or the ideal sweat factor can be used. However, neither is a smart choice; the first, for the same reasons as mentioned before; the second, because the degrees of freedom do not match. The latter can be seen as follows. Suppose we know the function value y of the curve at one stimulus level x (say, at the ideal sweat factor), then solving y=9(x ; :, ;) is impossible for two free parameters : and ;. Infinitely many combinations : and ; exist for which the psychometric curve goes through the known point (x, y). This implies that when all weight of the posterior pdf is confined to a small region, i.e., new measurements are confined to a small region of stimulus levels too, not much useful information is gathered about the shape of the psychometric function. However, if the number of known points is larger than or equal to the number of free parameters then the set of equations can be solved. In the example (: and ; are free parameters) this means that at least two points on the psychometric function should be known. However, not all combinations of two stimulus levels are equally effective, because of the variability in the measurements. A criterion of effectiveness should be defined.

In QUEST we make a distinction between two stages: the placement of trials (the original QUEST method) and the estimation of parameters. Estimates of the shift parameter and the slope parameter are obtained by maximum likelihood estimation after the experiment has finished (Watson, 1979; Watson 6 Fitzhugh, 1990). DISCUSSION 

In our example, when the slope parameter ; is chosen wrongly, MUEST works better than standard QUEST in the sense that it does not generate biased estimations. The variability of the estimated shift parameter : is slightly higher on the benefit of a less biased estimation. On the other hand, when ; is chosen correctly in advance standard QUEST leads to a better result. This can be seen in Figs. 3 and 5. 

Reducing the bias in the QUEST method by maximum likelihood estimation of the shift parameter and the slope parameter after the experiment is finished introduces extra variability in the estimated shift parameter (Figs. 3A and 3B). When the objective is to find the slope parameter of the psychometric function it is best to use the MUEST method instead of the extented version of the QUEST method, as can be seen in Figs. 4 and 6. 

We chose the above described two-dimensional sweat factor, but this may not be the optimal choice. Even so, the MUEST proved that it can be a better alternative than the QUEST method particularly if the slope parameter is not known in advance.

using look-up tables as in the QUEST method is possible. Nowadays this is not a real hindrance, because even our humble PC's can handle an incredible number of FLOPS.[3] 

Many different implementations of QUEST and MUEST are possible regarding the prior pdf, stop-criteria, resampling of the pdf, etc. In this appendix the used implementation in the simulations is given step-by-step without going into the details of alternatives. The reported outcomes of the simulations are the result of 300 repetitions of 128 trials each. Because QUEST and MUEST are closely related most of the steps concern both methods. 

Step 1: Initialization. Before the experiment starts the following should be done: 

Prior pdf. The prior pdf P(:, ; | H) should be defined. This function can either be based on the experimenter's insight about reasonable values of P(:, ; | H) or on previous sessions. In the first case, a two-dimensional Gaussian distribution may be used. In the second case, the prior pdf P(:, ; | H) is set to the posterior pdf P(:, ; | Mn , H) of a previous session. In our simulations the prior pdf's were two-dimensional Gaussians. For both MUEST and QUEST the prior pdf in the :-direction was a Gaussian centered around a random deviate of a Gaussian with mean 0 dB and width 3 dB, and the width of the pdf in the :-direction was 12 dB. For MUEST, the prior pdf in the ;-direction was a Gaussian centered around either 1.5, 2.0, 2.5, 3.0, 3.5, 4.0, 4.5, 5.0, or 5.5 in separate conditions with width 2.5. For QUEST, the ;-values were fixed at either 1.5, 2.0, 2.5, 3.0, 3.5, 4.0, 4.5, 5.0 or 5.5.
