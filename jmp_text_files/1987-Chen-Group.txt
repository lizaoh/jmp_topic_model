CHEN  Rochester Institute of Technology  A structure of the normal subgroups of the plane similarity group was constructed. In this group model, the plane similarity  contains two normal subgroups: the direct similarity group and the dihedral group. While the latter preserves the size of a  form, the former preserves its sense. Both subgroups contain a cyclic group as their normal subgroup. An experiment in form  recognition using reaction time as the behavioral index was designed and conducted to test the theory of the group structure.  The experimental results agree with the group theoretical model. The images generated by a normal subgroup that preserves the  sense of a form require less time to identify than those induced by a normal subgroup that preserves its size. The reversal of  the sense of a form creates more instability in an image and provides less information than change of size of the form. The  normal subgroup that preserves the sense and the uprightness of a form maximizes symmetries and supplies the most information.  Therefore, it provides the best condition for recognition of the image of a form. % 1987 Academic Press, Inc.  INTRODUCTION  A transformation of the plane is a one-to-one map of the points of the plane onto themselves. An isometry is a transformation  of the plane that maps points A and B into points A' and B' such that the distance between A' and B' is the same as the  distance between A and B. Because of its distance preserving property, an isometry preserves the shape as well as the area of  the interior of a simple closed figure.  Let A', B', and C' be the respective images of three noncollinear points A, B, and C under a transformation. Draw a curve  through A, B, and C in that order and a curve through A', B', and C' in that order. If both curves are in the same direction,  clockwise or counterclockwise, then we say that the transformation preserves the sense of triangle ABC. For brevity, we call the sense of a triangle the sense. A transformation  that preserves the sense is called a direct transformation and one that reverses the sense is called an opposite  transformation. For examples, translations and rotations are direct isometries, and reflections and glide reflections are  opposite isometries.  A stretch (homothety) of ratio r about a fixed point O is the transformation that fixed O and otherwise sends point P to P'  such that P' is the unique point on ray OP and the distance between O and P' equals r times the distance between O and P,  where r is a positive real number. The fixed point O is called the center of the stretch. A stretch preserves the sense. When  r < 1, the distance between O and P' is actually shortened. When r = 1, the stretch is the identity transformation, i, that  maps every point into itself. Hence the term stretch also applies to a shrink or the identity transformation. A stretch  reflection is a nonidentity stretch about a center followed by a reflection in a mirror through the center. A stretch rotation  about a center is a stretch about a center followed by a nonidentity rotation about the center.  A similarity of ratio r, a positive real number, is a transformation that sends A to A' and B to B' such that the distance  between A' and B' is r times the distance between A and B. When r= 1, a similarity is also an isometry. As an isometry  preserves distances, a similarity preserves equal distances. Let a similarity with ratio r send the pair of points A and B to  A' and B', and the pair of points C and D to C' and D'. If the distance between A and B equals the distance between C and D,  then the distance A' and B' equals the distance between C' and D'. The reason is that the distance between A' and B' equals r  times the distance between A and B, the distance between C' and D' equals r times the distance between C and D, and the  distance between A and B equals the distance between C and D.  The Classification Theorem for Similarities on the Plane states that a nonidentity similarity is exactly one of the following:  isometry, stretch, stretch rotation, and stretch reflection (Martin, 1982). While direct isometrics, stretches, and stretch  rotations are direct similarities, opposite isometrics and stretch reflections are opposite similarities. Isometry, rotation  or reflection, and stretch are defined as prime similarities, and stretch rotation and stretch reflection are defined as  composite similarities. It is demonstrated that each nonisometric direct similarity is the product of a rotation and a stretch  having the same center, and any nonisometric opposite similarity is the product of a reflection and a stretch whose center  lies on the mirror. Furthermore, such products are commutative, i.e., rh = hr and fh = hf, where r, h, and f represent  rotation, stretch, and reflection, respectively (Dodge, 1972). The ratio of the areas enclosed by two similar polygons is the  ratio of the squares of any two corresponding sides. A similarity is also called a size transformation.  A set of transformations with composition as the binary operation forms a group or an abstract group, if it contains the  product of any two (closure property) and the inverse of each (inverse property). When the order of factors of the product is  immaterial, the group is_ an Abelian or a commutative group. A subset of a group is  FORM RECOGNITION UNDER PLANE SIMILARITY TRANSFORMATIONS 323  a subgroup, if the subset is a group under the binary operation of the group. Plane isometries form the isometric or Euclidean  group. For lack of closure property, neither the set of reflections nor the set of rotations is a group, but the set of plane  translations forms a subgroup of the plane isometric group. Furthermore, the translation group is Abelian, and so is the group  of rotations with the same center. Similarly, the set of stretches is not a group, but the stretches with the same center form  a group.  The subgroups of similarities that we used are normal or invariant subgroups. A subgroup N of a group G is a normal subgroup  of G, denoted by N a G, if gNg-' = N for every g in G where g-' is the inverse of g. The product gng-' is called the conjugate  of n by g for n in N. If H is a subgroup of G, g in G, then Hg= {hg: h E H}. The set Hg is called a right coset of H in G.  Similarly, gH= { gh: h E H} is called a left coset of H in G. A theorem states that the subgroup N of G is a normal subgroup  of G, if and only if Ng = gN for every g in G (Herstein, 1964). It follows from the equation that the subgroups of an Abelian  group are normal subgroups. For example, the translation group in the plane is a normal subgroup of the group of direct  isometries which, in turn, is a normal subgroup of the isometric group (Chen and Chen, 1982).  In this study, the isometries consisted of rotations with a common center and reflections whose mirrors all passed through the  common center. The rotations form a cyclic group C, and the rotations and reflections form a dihedral group D. The set of the  identity transformation is a trivial group, denoted by I. The plane similarities form the similarity group S which contains  the direct similarities as a subgroup S+ (Martin, 1982).  Due to the fact that the transformations we experience in our daily life involve changes in position, orientation, sense, and  size, many perceptual phenomena may be readily described or explained by similarity transformations. Since the combination of  two nonidentity transformations is another transformation and every transformation has an inverse, that is the transformation  to undo what it has done, it is natural for us to use groups to classify and organize a structure of transformations. The  interplay between concrete geometric transformations and abstract groups, according to Lyndon (1985), was mutually beneficial.  On the one hand, we structure and unify concrete transformations within the abstract theory of groups. On the other hand, we  frequently profit from presenting an abstract group in the concrete form of transformations in developing abstract group  theory. The first attempt to apply the concept of group to perception was made by Helmholtz in 1886 (Cassirer, 1944). Cassirer  resurrected the role of group in perception by suggesting the use of group structure and the concept of invariance as common  denominators of mathematical and psychological problems. Gibson (1950) classified the family of visual forms into mutually  exclusive transformation group categories. Based on Hubel and Wiesel's findings in the cat and monkey cortex, Hoffman (1966)  selected Lie groups of transformations under which the invariance of patterns serves as perceptual constancy. Caelli (1976)  reported that the Lie algebra model with its group structure and visual invariances has greater theoretical  324 CHEN AND CHEN  scope than any other language proposed for representing human pattern perception. One of the phenomena of perceptual organization, which we studied, was shape constancy. Shape constancy refers to the fact  that people tend to see figures as having the same "shape" if they differ only in position, orientation, size, and mirrorimage  reflection. Earlier shape recognition theories were based on invariant features and assumed that brain extracts information  about shape independently of qualities such as spatial location, mirror reflection, and angular orientation (Deutsch, 1962;  Milner, 1974). These qualities are not intrinsic to the shape itself, but depend on how it happens to be oriented and located  relative to the observer. Thus the invariant features theorists believed that shape constancy perception is mediated by the  detection of those geometric properties of a figure that do not change when the figure is transformed in some specific ways.  For example, when a figure is rotated through an angle, angle sizes do not change. Angle size is an invariant feature for all  figures under the Euclidean similarity group, as it does not change when figures are translated, rotated, reflected,  stretched, or a combination of these. Each transformation group is associated with its own set of invariant properties. The  set of transformations that underlies shape constancy, according to invariant features explanation, is the similarity group.  This type of explanation has long dominated psychological theories of shape perception (Selfridge and Neisser, 1963; Gibson,  1969).  Recently there is evidence showing that the invariant features position is inadequate for certain types of discriminations  based on spatial structure. This evidence includes Shepard and Metzler's experiment (1971) on mental rotation of  threedimensional objects. Also Cooper and Shepard (1973) have furnished an elegant demonstration of the ability of subjects to  mentally rotate the internal representations of single letters or digits. This was followed by a series of reaction time  experiments that explored mental rotation of two-dimensional or three-dimensional stimuli (Cooper, 1975; Corballis, et al.  1978; Hock and Tromley, 1978; Pylyshyn,  FORM RECOGNITION UNDER PLANE SIMILARITY TRANSFORMATIONS 325  1979; Shepard and Judd, 1976). Similar results have been reported for figures that differ in size (Bundesen and Larsen, 1975).  They found that the time taken to perceive two objects as having the same shape increased linearly with the objects' size  ratio, when distance and orientation were kept constant. This type of mental size transformation gained wide support (Bregman,  1977; Cooper and Shepard, 1978; Bundesen et al. 1981; Shepard, 1981).  Any given set of tranformations results in two subsets of figural properties: those that change over the transformations and  those that do not. These transformational properties seem to provide important cues for recognition. For example, faces are  hard to recognize upside down (Rock, 1973). Rock argued that an observer does not recognize a shape, until he or she has  assigned a "top" and a "bottom" to it. He suggested that, if the shape is rotated from its customary orientation, his or her  assignment is equivalent to an act of "mental rotation." Rock was a pioneer in studying how shape and orientation might be  recognized relative to a reference frame. He showed that novel shapes, when presented in one orientation and tested in  another, were hard to recognize. In other words subjects failed to have shape constancy. He further showed that environmental  and/or gravitational conditions determine the reference orientation. Retinal factor was not responsible for the reference  orientation, because, when observers changed their head orientation by 90between presentation and test, recognition was not  much disrupted. These results were provided by Rock as evidence that shape is perceived relative to an environmental frame of  reference in which gravity defines the reference orientation.  Rock's results were replicated by Wiser (1981), when she used figures that lacked a well-defined "intrinsic axis." But she  failed to replicate it, when using figures that possessed a good intrinsic axis. She further showed that, when the axis was  not aligned with the vertical in an intrinsically structured figure, subsequent recognition was fastest when the figure was  tested in its vertical orientation. Her interpretation was that the shape was stored as though it was upright, relative to its  own reference frame. Wiser's results are consistent with shape constancy. The shape of a figure is perceived relative to its  own intrinsic frame of reference when it has the type of structure that determines one, and relative to an environmental frame  when it does not.  Palmer (1983) also arrived at similar conclusions about how reference frames are established by studying the perceived  pointing direction of ambiguous equilateral triangles. He proposed a shape constancy theory, using a reference frame  hypothesis while focusing on properties that vary over the transformations of the group. He assumed that the effects of  transformations are neutralized by imposing an "intrinsic frame of reference," thereby achieving shape constancy. The  intrinsic frame corresponds optimally with the structure of the figure and is not arbitrarily imposed. He believed that when  intrinsic frames are appropriately chosen, they can compensate for structural changes induced by the similarity group:  translations, rotations, reflections, dilations (stretches), and their composites. Shape constancy would follow if the same  frame were always selected for the same figure, because all the properties would then be invariant with respect to the frame.  Palmer chose the  326 CHEN AND CHEN  similarity group of Euclidean geometry in his analysis of perceptual organization, because similarities are easier to compute  and, hence, preferred by the human visual system to other transformations. He also proposed the use of groups to describe cognition. GROUP THEORY  A guiding principle in modern mathematics is "Whenever you have to do with a structure-endowed entity try to determine its  group of automorphisms, the group of those element-wise transformations which leave all structural relations undisturbed"  (Weyl, 1952). In this section, we construct a group structure for plane similarities. A homomorphism of G into H is a function  f: G -~ H which preserves the group operation, i.e., for all a and b in G, f(ab)= f(a) f(b). The kernel off is the set of all  g in G such that f(g) is the identity element of H. A one-to-one and onto homomorphism of a group into itself is called an  automorphism (Rotman, 1968).  In mathematics, parity refers to the property of an integer being either even or odd. A plane isometry is the product of at  most three reflections and a translation or a rotation is the product of two reflections (Martin, 1982). Thus we say that a  translation or a rotation has even parity, whereas a reflection has odd parity. Similarly, a direct similarity which is a  product of a stretch and a rotation has even parity, whereas an opposite similarity which is a product of a stretch and a  reflection has odd parity. The parity of a product of two similarities is the sum of the parities of the two factors. The  composition of two opposite similarities does not preserve the parity, for an odd number plus an odd number is even. It  follows that opposite similarities do not form a group.  LEMMA. This  means that the total subjects' RT (means) of rotated forms in sizes of 1.6, 0.8, and 0.4 scales and rotated forms in size of  1.6 scale are not significantly different. It is obvious that the size effect is pronounced, when reflection is included with  rotation in the analysis of total subjects. This is shown in the significant difference (P<0.05) or RT of rotated and  reflected forms in sizes 1.6, 0.8, and 0.4 scales under similarity group S and RT of rotated and reflected forms in standard  size with 1.6 scale, the size used in training, under dihedral group D. This suggests that subjects mentally transform size to  match the form against the internal template in size with 1.6 scale. The result is consistent with previous studies (Bundesen  & Larsen, 1975; Bundesen et al. 1981). The longer reaction time required to identify the images under opposite  similarities may be explained in terms of the process of mirror-image generalization, involving homotopic transfer of memory  traces between the two halves of the brain such that registration of a pattern leaves memory traces for both the pattern  itself and its left-right mirror image (Achim and Corballis, 1977; Corballis and Beale, 1970, 1976). The subjects in our study  were exposed in the training session to the standard figures in the version we considered normal. Hence the traces under  opposite similarities that depended on interhemispheric transfer rather than on direct input were weaker than the normal ones. As an application, we use Fig. 5 to select reference frames for algebraic stability ordering in the reference frame theory of  Leyton (1986, June). We have three ways to factor S: reducing S to S+, then to C, and finally to I, directly reducing S to I,  and reducing S to D, then to C, and finally to I. If we insist on prime factorization, i.e., each removed similarity must be a  prime similarity, we eliminate the direct reduction from S to I, because in this reduction a composite similarity, the stretch  reflection, is removed. Then our selection of a route from S to I is between I-C-S+-S and I-C-D-S. Based on the significant  difference between the RT of direct similarity group S+ and the dihedral group D, D > S+ (P < 0.001) as shown in Table 1, we  prefer the route I-C-S+-S to I-C-D-S. On route I-C S'-S, we first factor out S+ by removing the set of reflections, then  factor out C by removing the stretch group, and finally factor out I by removing the rotation group. Thus the series I - C -  S+ - S demonstrates not only an algebraic stability ordering, but also the preference of I - C - S+ - S to I - C - D - S by  the human visual system.  In conclusion, the results of this experiment show that the sense of an image is more important than its size in recognition.  Since the reversal of the sense induces a more unstable state of an image than the change of size, recognition is  significantly faster, if the sense of an image is preserved. Also, the orientation that preserves the uprightness of a form  maximizes symmetries, supplies the most information and, therefore, is the best condition for recognition. It follows that the  upright orientation is commonly used for selecting the frames of reference.  REFERENCES  ACHIM, A, & CORBALLIS, M. C. (1977).
