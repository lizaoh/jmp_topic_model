JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY: 5, 377-391 (1968)  Maximum-Likelihood Estimation of Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve Parameters'  JOHN C. OGILVIE AND C. DOUGLAS CREELMAN  University of Toronto  Toronto, Canada  Conventional least-squares curve fitting procedures are inappropriate for Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) plots because both axes represent dependent variables and are both subject to error. A brief review of the problem of interpreting ROC plots is followed by a solution which uses maximum-likelihood estimation of the best fitting straight line derived from a theoretical model which assumes underlying logistic distributions. The logistic is used simply because it is more convenient mathematically than the normal distribution. The normal and logistic models are compared empirically. Two advantages of this estimation procedure are that standard errors for the estimates of the parameters are obtained and there is also a test for goodness-of-fit of the theoretical model. Solutions are given for both yes-no and rating procedures.  The Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) or isosensitivity curve is a means of analyzing and presenting psychophysical data in graphical form. It was first presented to psychologists by Tanner and Swets (1954) who drew the concept from the Theory of Signal Detectability (TSD) of Peterson, Birdsall, and Fox (1954). When observers respond to an observation which may or may not include a weak signal or one masked by noise, the probability of correct identification of the signal (hit rate) is strongly influenced by the observer's criterion, his willingness to affirm that the signal is present.  The ROC is a plot of the hit rate against the probability of a false alarm, an affirmative response when no signal is present. It is the locus of experimental points that are obtained with the identical signal, as the observer's criterion is changed. The ROC thus represents constant sensitivity under different instructions or motivation.  In the binary choice or yes-no detection method, the changes in criterion are induced by the experimenter in separate experimental sessions. An empirical ROC curve can be obtained from ratings, with considerably less experimental effort. CURVE FITTING PROCEDURES  An objective basis is needed for fitting curves to ROC plots. Both ordinate and abscissa are dependent variables, so the important aspect is not to predict one from the other, but to estimate parameters which give the best fitting curve through the points. If the proportions of hits and false alarms are plotted on normal-normal probability axes, the points lie approximately on a straight line. This is equivalent to transforming the proportions to normal deviates and plotting on rectangular axes. The observed linear relation is an obvious starting point for fitting theoretical ROC curves. Since both variables are subject to error, conventional least squares or procedures which minimize vertical distance from the empirical points to the fitted line are not appropriate. Such procedures do not provide standard errors of estimates, nor do they take into account the correlation between successive points on a rating-method ROC curve.  Linker, Moore, and Galanter (1964), Swets, Tanner, and Birdsall (1961), and Tanner and Swets (1954), for example, have been content with visual fits to the data. Where the purpose was to show that the data did not fit the more traditional thresholdtheory model, (i.e., that the ROC curve was concave downward rather than a straight line on linear axes) such imprecise measures were quite sufficient, since inspection of the data was sufficient; the results are convincing indeed. Egan, Greenberg, and Schulman (1961) have used an empirically derived exponential equation to fit ROC plots. A recent report by Sekuler (1965) presents a least-squares fit to ROC data, and the dangers inherent in the procedure are exemplified in the results. As further substantive questions about the nature of sensory systems come to be asked within the framework of TSD there will be increasing need for a rational procedure for finegrain analysis of the data, estimating the parameters of the curve and estimating their standard errors. Norman (1963), for instance, places considerable emphasis on the implications of the shape of his obtained ROC plots. ESTIMATION OF PARAMETERS  We here obtain estimates of the parameters of the ROC curve by the method of maximum likelihood. The data consist of m pairs of proportions of hits and false alarms, the two coordinates of each point on an ROC plot. When the data are plotted on double probability paper the model assumes they can be fitted by a straight line. The problem is to estimate the parameters of that line. The problem is complicated by the difficulty of writing a simple equation for the normal linear curve.  The parameters to be estimated are r (or 11 a), d' and the m values of c, where r and d' are assumed to be constant for the whole collection of points. The maximum likelihood solution is theoretically straightforward but computationally and algebraically tedious with the normal model shown in Eqs. 1 and 2.  The logistic distribution will be used instead of the normal in this derivation for practical rather than theoretical reasons. The logistic distribution is easier to handle mathematically and computationally. Also, the two distributions are quite similar in shape within the typical range of empirical proportions (Bush, 1963, p. 454). In the yes-no procedure each point in the ROC curve is independent, obtained in a different experimental session, under a different criterion. The cases of yes-no and rating ROC curves will be developed separately.  The essential aspects of the method of maximum likelihood and its application to Luce's choice model are given by Bush (1963). COMPUTATION  The likelihood equations and their solution look rather forbidding computationally, but are in fact quite straightforward to program for a computer. Programs were written in IBM 7094 FORTRAN IV for the yes-no procedure (MALPRO) and the rating method (EPCROC) and both have been used successfully with many sets of data. In general, convergence is obtained with three or four iterations and the time required is less than 0.1 sec.  There is a special problem with the rating method. Some observers refuse to use certain categories of response and such data lead to computations with a 0/0 division. This is easily circumvented by collapsing across null categories which is done automatically by the program.  388 OGILVIE AND CREELMAN  COMPARISON OF NORMAL AND LOGISTIC MODELS  Most applications of signal detection have used estimates of d' which assume underlying normal distributions. The largest value of d was omitted for the yes-no model.  The normal deviates ZSN and zN were converted to the probabilities which define the ROC curve. In the case of the yes-no model, the probabilities were converted directly to frequencies of response assuming a denominator of 1000. For the rating model these probabilities were converted first to probability of response in each category using (8), and then to frequencies assuming 1000 presentations each of signal and noise.  From these sets of data estimates of d* and r were obtained for both methods. In no case was the goodness-of-fit X2 significant. The observed and fitted ROC curve probabilities typically differed by less than .01.  It was found that the relation d = 0.61d* was an excellent approximation and virtually unaffected by changes in r or the set of criterion values. In fact, for both methods the linear relation accounted for more than 99 % of the total variation of d* across the different conditions.  The relationship for r from the two models is not quite so stable and depends both on the value of d and the set of criterion values. On the average, the same value emerges from normal and logistic models. The largest discrepancies occur when d* is large.
