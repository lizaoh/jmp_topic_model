LEFEBVRE  University of California at Irvine  Special representations of standard Boolean functions allow us to model the process of ethical choice, both as a behavioral  process and as a structural description of the subjective inner world of the individual, with its categories such as "good,"  "evil," "doubt," "compromise," "conflict," "suffering," etc. Two different ethical systems based on different rules for the  binary contraposition of "good" and "evil" are postulated. Each ethical system corresponds to its own evaluation of the  ethical status of various types of individuals. The hypothesis is proposed that in Western culture and in Soviet culture the  different ethical systems are normatively realized, and experimental evidence to support this hypothesis is given. Finally,  the axioms that are selected for the two systems are shown to follow from a few precisely stated intuitions that have  empirical content.  INTRODUCTION  A person's inner world does not lend itself easily to mathematical modeling. Although there are many examples of successful  modeling of physiological functions and of behavior, efficient modeling of man's inner world is difficult. This paper will  describe a type of modeling which provides a way to represent the structure of certain types of cognition and to relate this  structure to external behavior.  One of the most successful among the traditional approaches to mathematical modeling of cognition is the representation of a  phenomenon under study as a special automaton or as a system of automata. With the help of automata, some of the most  significant results in modeling behavior were obtained by M. L. Tsetlin (1924-1966). For example, Tsetlin (translated, 1973)  succeeded in demonstrating that many forms of group rational behavior may be modeled using systems of automata with a very  simple structure of memory and with primitive connections between stimuli coming from the environment and responses. Tsetlin's  work is a sophisticated application of the behaviorist approach. In the western world, the popularity of Markov  models--essentially probabilistic automata-provides another example of the value of automata in cognitive modeling.  1 I wish to express sincere gratitude for suggestions concerning the preparation of this paper to: William Batchelder, Anatol  Rapoport, Ronald Walter, Russell Ackoff, and Victorina Lefebvre. Such a model  can be represented as an automaton with its sets of inputs, outputs, and inner structures (internal states). Each inner  structure can be represented as a mathematical formula emboding a rule connecting inputs to output. But at the same time,  together with this functional rule, the syntax of the formula corresponds to a special description of the psychological makeup  of the corresponding individual. In this way, a connection is established between two levels of modeling reality. On the one  hand we have behavior that can be modeled in a stimulus-response scheme. On the other hand, we can read the state of the inner  world of an individual in the "terms" of this inner world such as "image of oneself," "image of one's enemy," "feeling  guilty," "suffering," "sacrifice," etc. By changing a formula we can change both the behavioral dispositions and the inner  structure. In this way we are able to connect the features of the inner world with the behavioral tendencies of a given  individual. This is the main technical idea of our investigation.  How are stimuli and responses interpreted in this model ? Stimuli are "suggestions" for action directed at an individual by  his social environment. These "directions" may be normatively evaluated as "good" or "bad" within the framework of the  morality of the culture to which the individual belongs. The ethical connotation of an individual's responses may be evaluated  equivalently. Thus, from an ethical viewpoint, the individual either agrees to accept the environmental direction or he  refuses to do so.  We might call an individual "better" or of a higher "ethical status" the less frequently he performs "bad" actions. If the  frequencies of "good" and "bad" environmental directives are held constant, analysis of the structure of the "inner world" of  the automaton allows us to establish the connection between its "psychological type" and its "ethical status."  The present author is convinced that the primary test of any theory is the demonstration of its ability to provide solutions  to quite difficult and important scientific or cultural problems. For the present theory such a problem is the comparison of  Western and Soviet ethical systems. Of course, no single ethical system governs the beliefs of each citizen in either country.  Instead, the concern here is with the characterization and comparison of normative ethical values such as depicted in  mainstream literature, educational practice, as characteristics of typical "culture heroes," and as dominant modes of choice  in standard situations involving conflicting values.  The author's motives in choosing this problem were threefold. First, the fact that we do not know enough about such events as  Stalin's terror, Hitler's terror, or more recent cultural tragedies. What deep layers of human consciousness went awry? What  are the symptoms of such a process, and what is its dynamic? We do not know this; moreover, we do not have clear questions  about what we would like to find. We are as helpless as we were centuries ago in dealing with the devastating epidemics of  plague or cholera.  Soviet society has experienced a drama of self-destruction. And it seems unlikely that it has recovered. We do not have any  guarantee that the self-destruction will not recur again. On the other hand, American society seems to be relatively immune to  such a process. However, such examples as the Guyana tragedy and vandalism during the New York blackout raise questions about  the degree of this immunity. Despite an almost inexhaustible variety of circumstances requiring some form of ethical judgment, normative  ethical principles in any culture seem to be both few in number and invariant over a wide class of situational variations.  These informal observations raise the challenge to attempt to characterize and differentiate Soviet and American ethical  systems in terms of a formal structural model.  Finally, if our assumption about the difference between Soviet and American societies is true, it is interesting to see if it  is possible to determine the nature of and reasons for this disparity in normative ethical systems by comparing the choice  behavior in ethical situations of Soviet and American subjects. Here, we find a paradoxical peculiarity that (as the author  will later show) is a key to understanding ethical differences between Soviet and American cultures. Informally, the paradox  takes the following form.  An "ideal," "positive" Soviet individual in the situation where there are "good ends" and "bad means" would compromise. He is  willing to use "bad means" to reach "good ends," and moreover, he would positively evaluate such compromise.  On the other hand, if a Soviet individual is in a situation of conflict with another individual, this conflict is  compromiseless. A conflict seems to be valuable in itself. To compromise with an adversary (even in order to achieve a goal)  means to degrade oneself in one's own eyes. Therefore, in weighing ends against means-compromise; in situations involving an  adversary, do not compromise.  An "ideal," "positive" American appears to react differently. He would evaluate a compromise negatively in a situation with  "good ends" and "bad means." However, in a situation of conflict with another individual an American would tend to compromise.  Here, then, is the paradox: a positive evaluation of an ethical compromise is associated with a refusal (or at lca;t  reluctance) to compromise in a conflict with another; a negative evaluation of an ethical compromise is associated with  willingness to compromise with others.  The present author has found that representation of fundamental concepts of "good" and "evil" in a formal model may be used  for construction of a new theory of ethical evaluation. The model described in the following sections enables us to explain  this paradoxical peculiarity.  The remainder of this paper is organized into seven sections. In Section I, some empirical data are provided that strongly  argue that there is a difference between the normative ethical structure in Soviet and American society. These data, along  with other data presented in Section VI and the informal observations mentioned earlier establish the fact of cultural  variation in ethical systems between the two societies.  In Section 11, the formal properties of a two-valued ("good" and "evil") system for ethical evaluation are presented and two  different ethical systems are axiomatized. Each system is axiomatized in standard Boolean algebra.  Sections III considers the case of two interacting individuals in a situation permitting ethical evaluation of each  individual. A formal system is constructed that allows one to describe such interactions in various categories or levels of  self-reflection within one of the individuals. Section IV connects a formula representing a particular individual's view of a dyad to a social environment that directs  ethically valued input at the interacting individuals. The formula can be viewed as an automaton that tranduces inputs into  outputs. The "ethical status" of the individual is related to the tendency of the automaton to output actions that are  evaluated as "good."  Section V explores the connection between ethical status and the semantics of different types of individuals in both ethical  systems. In addition, the idea of letting an individual choose the type of his relationship with his partner is introduced. In the  framework of the model, it is shown that an individual who tends to look "well" in his own eyes would prefer a compromise  relationship with his partner if he negatively evaluates the compromise of good and evil, and he would prefer a confrontation  with his partner if he positively evaluates the compromise between good and evil. The data provide direct  empirical support for the validity of the systems themselves as well as their application to explain the variations between  the Soviet and American cultures argued in Sections I and II. EVALUATION OF COMPROMISE AND CONFRONTATION BY SOVIETS AND AMERICANS  The hypothesis about differences between Soviet and American individuals came originally from personal observations by the  author and his friends and colleagues. There are no serious works demonstrating the difference if any between normative types  of these cultures. However, even superficial observation shows that in American culture a display of aggressiveness (with  corresponding gestures and mimics) is a sign of bad manners and lack of restraint; in Soviet culture this is evidence of  "having principles in the fight with life's defects" and is caused by objective conditions of upbringing. A smile or other  signs of benevolence toward a possible adversary is interpreted as a manifestation of weakness and cowardice. The  agressiveness and compromiselessness of a Soviet man are not connected with his personal peculiarities; they are normative  features that are necessary for adequate functioning in Soviet society.  These standards, similar to those of language, do not belong to or necessarily characterize any single individual, but rather,  they are predetermined by the national culture of the country where a person is born and raised. ETHICAL SYSTEMS AND BOOLEAN ALGEBRA  Underlying the model is the idea of using classical propositional calculus for the representation of the interaction of two  abstract individuals. The category of "good" corresponds to logical "1"; the category of "evil" corresponds to logical "0";  and various relations between good and evil correspond to logical disjunction, conjunction, and implication.  We cannot now make a formal definition of such primitive concepts as good, evil, confrontation, compromise. Any attempts to  define them by reduction to other concepts would only displace the problem. For example, the categories of "confrontation" and  "compromise" may be reduced to the categories of "conflict" and "union" or "incompatibility" and "compatibility." These  categories are as complicated as the initial ones; however, such reduction may be helpful as a means of clarifying the  connotation of the initial concepts.  In this work we will construct abstract individuals who have inner models (or reflections) of their interactions with another  individual and who have the ability to reflect upon or evaluate these interactions. Our abstract individual has a recursive  structure: models of individuals may also have models of their interactions with other individuals and may have the ability to  evaluate these interactions, and so on.  The core of the structure of an individual lies in the connection between the states of the individual and his processes of  awareness. For example, once the formal theory is constructed, we will be able to represent precisely such informal  observations as: an individual in a state of evil and being aware of this state transfers himself into a state of good. We  would like to consider this statement as a law and include it as an axiom in our theory. However, it is not a psychological  law, but rather a normative law of ethical  AN ALGEBRAIC MODEL OF ETHICAL COGNITION 89  evaluation. The "state of evil" cannot be observed as a physical phenomenon. But we may observe that "awareness of evil" by  "an evil person" is evaluated as good.  Thus, when we say, "This person has been aware that he was unfair," we might be observed to change our initial evaluation of  him from negative to positive.  In all advanced cultures we see the phenomenon of an individual's preoccupation with his own inner states and of the search  for evidence of evil and imperfection in himself. for example, oriental meditation, Christian repentance and modern group  therapy all serve in part this purpose. EMPIRICAL STUDIES OF THE ETHICAL SYSTEMS  A series of experiments was performed for the purpose of examining the predictive power of the theory. The main idea is as  follows: a subject is given a questionnaire about two conflicting people. In some cases, the relation of relative ethical  status is given in the text, in others a subject has to indicate the ethical priority (or equality) of the two people in the  experimental questionnaire. The subject has to rely on his ethical intuition and transform them into a decision about ethical  priority. When the subject has given an overall judgment about ethical priority, we ask him a programmed series of questions  and use the answers to construct (algorithmically) a unique dyadic structure designed to represent the interaction as seen by  the subject. The model predicts that the overall judgment of ethical priority will be consistent with relative ethical status  computed from the model provided the correct ethical variant (of the two possibilities) is used to interpret  Dissembler  110 VLADIMIR A. THE PROBLEMS OF SUBSTANTIATION OF THE INITIAL AXIOMS  The model described in this paper is constructed on axioms. First we formulated several fundamental statements, and then we  deduced the consequences derived from them. However, it is important to note that our axioms represent a logical, rather than  an empirical, basis.  It is necessary to make a distinction between logical substantiation of the model and its  480/2212-3  114 VLADIMIR A. LEFEBVRE  experimental and empirical verification. It is natural to choose those statements as axioms that permit the deduction of  consequences with the greatest logical simplicity. But on the other hand, some of these statements may turn out to have no  direct empirical content. In such a case we have to choose consequences which conveniently lend themselves to empirical  implication, to verify these implications, and then to prove that the given consequences logically lead one to the given set  of axioms. For example, the axioms of geometry, it might be noted, were chosen by Euclid for their functional significance in  constructing his theory, and not for their ease of verification. For example, Euclid's famous fifth postulate, on the  non-convergence of parallel lines, cannot be directly verified empirically, but it is necessary in the deductive scheme of his  theory.  Let us consider the axioms described in Section 11. Some of them can be justified empirically, but others are defined out of  convenience for analysis and development of the theory. Here an intriguing question arises: why were those particular  definitions chosen? We do have some informal empirical argument in favor of, for example, the fifth axiom, evil aware of evil  becomes good. Systems of religious thought have special procedures for searching out and recognizing evil in oneself and  others. Such recognition is a precondition for overcoming evil in one's own activity.  However, axioms 6, 7, and 8 are not readily grounded with empirical arguments. Also it may be possible to find an operation  more convenient for modeling the process of awareness than the formal implication  aldeYb--~- a.
