LEFEBVRE University of California, Irvine  AND  JACK ADAMS-WEBBER  Brock University  In this paper we analyze the results of two repertory grid experiments in which subjects categorized themselves and others on  bipolar constructs (Adams-Webber and Rodney, Canadian Journal of Behavioral Sciences, 1983, 15, No 1, 52-59). We attempt to  explain these results on the basis of the assumption that the subjects relied upon a special "algebraic processor" for  modeling the self and others, previously described by Lefebvre (Journal of Mathematical Psychology, 1980, 22, 83-120; Algebra  of conscience.A comparative analysis of western and soviet ethical systems, 1982, Reidel: Dordrecht; Journal of Mathematical  Psychology, 1985, 29, 289-310). Adams-Webber, 1984) experiment of Adams-Webber and Rodney (1983), thirty-eight Canadian undergraduate  subjects categorized themselves and eleven acquaintances dichotomously on twelve bipolar constructs three times in succession:  with basic instructions, while role-playing a negative mood (a subject is asked to imagine himself a failure), and while  role-playing a positive mood (a subject is asked to imagine himself very successful). The order of the mood roles was  counterbalanced across subjects. For each trial the following frequencies were calculated:  positive judgments positive judgments like-self judgments positive judgments positive judgments  about the self about others (assigning the same pole to the self and to others) about others when the self was evaluated  positively about others when the self was evaluated negatively. MODELING AN EXPERIMENT 319  2. A FORMAL CHARACTER  We represent a subject as a formal character; that is, as a special formula which has two different interpretations. (Formal  aspects of this model are described in detail in Lefebvre, 1977, 1982, 1985, 1986.) On the one hand, his formula represents  the subject together with the structure of this inner world; on the other hand, it represents a function describing the  subject's behavior. Thus, in a single representation we combined both the inner characteristics and behavior of the subject.  In order to construct such a representation, we use an alphabet of two letters with apostrophes (a' and b') and a symbol *.  Two different letters joined by an are called a pair. In the place of an apostrophe we can write a letter with an apostrophe,  or a pair, or nothing; this procedure is called a substitution. An arbitrary letter with an apostrophe after a finite number  of substitutions constitutes a formula which is called a character. Consider now a Boolean algebra: (G, 1, 0), where G is 11, 0}. The function c -> d = c + d (c E G, d E G) is called  implication; we write it as an exponent c -* d = d`. If a symbol * in a formula of character A, is substituted by + or -, the  resulting configuration may be considered a formula of Boolean algebra. Each letter in this formula is an independent Boolean  variable, and we can calculate the value of the formula; that is, the value of a character. Therefore, every structural  representation is connected with a certain process of calculation.  Let the Boolean values 1 and 0 stand for "positive" and "negative," respectively. When a character's root takes on the value 1  we interpret this as his intention to make a positive choice. When a character's root takes on the value 0 we interpret this  as his intention to make a negative choice. 1's and 0's on the second and third tiers are interpreted as positive and negative  intentions of the corresponding characters. Knowing the probabilities with which each letter takes on the value of 1, we can  calculate the probability of 1's in the function's output. We call this number a character's ethical status. A symbol * also  can be considered as a variable which takes on the values from the set { +, - } with equal probability. In this case, the  ethical status is the mean ethical status of all characters corresponding to various substitutions of *.  For calculating ethical statuses it is convenient to use y-algebra (Lefebvre, 1982, 1985). This will  give us a numerical expression from which we can calculate the ethical status of a given character.  3. MODELING THE EXPERIMENTS  Let us describe the experimental conditions schematically:  - a subject corresponds to a formal character  - poles of bipolar constructs correspond to the Boolean 1 (positive pole) and 0 (negative one)  MODELING AN EXPERIMENT 321  - a subject's intentions to choose a positive or a negative pole correspond to the root's taking on values 1 and 0,  respectively  - a subject's choice corresponds to the character's value: 1 means a choice of a positive pole, and 0 means a choice of a  negative pole  - the frequency with which the positive pole is chosen corresponds to a character's ethical status.  Suppose that each subject in each trial exhibits two phases of functioning. In the first phase a subject categorizes only  himself. The value x of character A, is interpreted as his evaluation of himself: x = 1 when a subject evaluates himself  positively, and x = 0 when he evaluates himself negatively. Therefore, A,'s "ethical status" is analogous to the probability  of a subject's positive evaluation of himself. We assume that the experimental instructions also influence a character's  cognizant images (a3 and a4), making them Boolean constants 1 and 0 (see Fig. 1).  In the second phase, a subject categorizes others using a value of x for cognizant images of himself. To evaluate an  acquaintance, a subject needs images of himself, of his partner, and of their relationship. Therefore, we represent him by the  character shown in Fig. 2.  The value y of character A, is interpreted as evaluation of an acquaintance: if y = 1 it is positive, and if y = 0 it is  negative. Therefore, A,'s "ethical status" is interpreted as the probability of a positive evaluation of an acquaintance.  A general scheme for this experiment is given in Fig. 3. The variables b3 and b4 appear only in the second phase of the experiment and also take  on the value of 1 with probability z.
