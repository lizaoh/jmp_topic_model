INTRODUCTION AND OVERVIEW  Signal detection theory is generally assumed to have a static decision process (e.g., see Green & Swets, 1966). The decision  process of signal detection theory is based upon statistical decision theory (e.g., see Swets, Tanner & Birdsall, 1961; Green,  1960; Green & Swets, 1966). Within this framework, the decision-maker in a signal-detection task is assumed to divide a  likelihood ratio continuum into two subsets and bases his decisions upon this partition. There are two major difficulties with  such an analysis.  1 This research was supported in part by a grant from the National Institute of Mental Health, MH19963. In summary, Dorfman and Biderman (1971) found that the Kac model was the poorest of the special models with respect to its fit  to the entire sequence of observations when the predictions were generated from the maximum likelihood estimates. Their  finding sharply contrasts with the asymptotic analysis of Thomas (1973), and the results from the general model which also  support approximate error correction. The major goal of this paper is to explore some possible explanations for this apparent  inconsistency.  Here is a brief survey of the paper. In Section 2, some investigations of the asymptotic data are presented. There are tests  of an important asymptotic theorem by Norman (1974) on probability matching for Kac's model. The theorem makes no  MODELS FOR A CONTINUUM OF SENSORY STATES 181  assumptions about the location of the initial cutpoint, the size of d = d11 = 421 , or the sensory distributions. The results  provide very good support for- Kac's model, although there were some small, but statistically reliable departures from  prediction. Computations of Thomas' p statistic are also reported (Thomas, 1973). Using the methods of Dorfman and Biderman  (1971), the special models were evaluated relative to the general model on the asymptotic data by likelihood-ratio tests. In  this case, the pure error-correction model was the best of the special models, but it was significantly inferior to the  general model. Sections 3 and 4 present results on the effect of removal of some inessential assumptions on the fit of the  models to the entire sequence of observations. In Section 3, there is a reanalysis of the data of Dorfman and Biderman (1971)  with initial cutoffs estimated rather than assumed at the intersection of the sensory density functions. With initial cutoffs  estimated, Model 1 was not surpassed by the other special models. Nevertheless, a problem was still observed. The initial  cutpoints were very strongly biased in the direction of the asymptotic expected cutoffs. No such correlation was observed for  the general model. Section 4 explores the possibility that the problem relates to the arbitrary sensory assumptions of Dorfman  and Biderman (1971). First, they assumed normal distributions. Luce's choice theory with beta learning models is a possible  alternative (see Luce, 1963, p. 123). Through transformation to logistic distributions, these models can be represented as  additive learning models (e.g., see Norman, 1972). Choice representations of the special and general learning models were fit  to the data. Conclusions from logistic distributions were identical with those from normal distributions. A second assumption  was that sensitivity is constant for all trials and a priori stimulus probabilities. This assumption was abandoned, and a  sensitivity parameter was estimated for each 50-trial block within each stimulus schedule. Parameter estimates and inferences  about models were essentially unaffected by removal of the constant-sensitivity restriction. The high correlations between the  initial cutoffs and the asymptotic expected cutoffs remained for the special models, and the general model showed no  initial-cutoff bias.  Section 5 presents Monte Carlo simulations which provide good justification for the use of maximum likelihood and the  likelihood-ratio statistic. Moreover, they show that all of the major perturbations in the fit of the pure error-correction  model are duplicated if one simply assumes a very small change in the cutoffs after a correct decision. Thomas' p statistic is  also evaluated through these Monte Carlo simulations. The paper concludes in Section 6 with the proposal that there may be  nonsystematic random drifts in the decision criterion after successes and errors.  2. ANALYSES OF ASYMPTOTIC DATA  This section presents some analyses of the asymptotic data of Dorfman and Biderman (1971). Recall that their experiment was a  two-interval forced-choice simple  182 DORFMAN, SASLOW AND SIMPSON  auditory detection task with noise continuously present throughout the experimental session. ANALYSES OF ENTIRE DATA  The results obtained on the data of trials 201-400 were quite discrepant from the findings over the entire sequence of  observations analyzed by Dorfman and Biderman (1971). They found that Model 1 was the worst of the special models and  substantially inferior to the evidently untenable Model 3, the change-when-correct model. But Model 1 is clearly superior to  Model 3 for the asymptotic data. One possible explanation for this puzzling contradiction relates to some of the inessential  assumptions made by Dorfman and Biderman (1971) in fitting the models to the data. One such assumption was that the initial  cutpoint was at the intersection of the sensory density functions for all a priori signal probabilities and subjects. Other  assumptions were that the sensory functions were Gaussian and that sensitivity was invariant over trials and signal a priori  probabilities. Because conclusions about the learning process based on maximum likelihood and the likelihood-ratio test might  not be robust with respect to such assumptions, these assumptions were abandoned in the following analyses of the entire  sequence of observations.  190 DORFMAN, SASLOW AND SIMPSON  3.1.
