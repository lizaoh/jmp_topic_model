The following problem, which I am afraid is not new, arose from a request of Jean-Claude Falmagne. It is one that A.A.J. Marley and I have cited in grant proposals, but so far the progress is limited.

Although much of the theory of fundamental measurement is formulated as ordered algebraic systems with one or more operations, the fact is that whenever we collect data in the laboratory to see if an axiom is satisfied, we observe inconsistencies in the respondents choices. Despite this being a very familiar phenomenon, the efforts to formulate a probabilistic version of the algebraic theory do not strike me, and others, as having been very successful. I explore this a bit.

One referee has offered a detailed non-parametric Bayesian approach, which I hope he or she will continue to work on and publish, and cites an unpublished technical report (2005) of the University of Illinois-Chicago “Probabilistic measurement theory: A Bayesian nonparametric approach” by G. Karabatsos. But statistical inference is not my concern here. Rather I am interested in what amounts to a formulation of probabilistic axioms analogous to the algebraic ones. To some extent that has been accomplished, e.g., the property of transitivity and of the Thomsen condition. What remains is probability analogues of linking properties such as  (1)  and  (2) . Put another way, the task is to generalize the algebraic models to probability ones that accommodate both structure and randomness in a single integrated package. This need has been recognized for decades. At least three approaches have been taken in the small literature that exists.

One assumes that the class of numerical representations is fine in the sense that each experimental observation represents what is algebraically appropriate for the particular representation that is sampled at that time (see, for example,  Heyer & Niederée, 1989 ). This presupposes that a probability distribution over representations exists that describes the respondent. No particular reason is given for supposing any specific form for the distribution or the parameter values when some form plausibly exists. So far as I can tell, no experiment has been proposed to test the adequacy of such a distribution assumption. This has always struck me as a formalism that can explain almost anything and so it ends up explaining nothing.

And  Falmagne (1980)  focuses on the structure of extensive measurement. What has not been successfully tackled are probabilistic versions of the distribution-like properties interlocking the two structures. (The algebraic version is well understood, see  Luce, Krantz, Suppes, & Tversky, 1990 , §19.6, §22.6.) One might hope for an axiomatization that leads not to a numerical order-preserving representation but rather to a random variable one and that leads to predictions—not assumptions—about the form of the probability distributions of these random variables. Moreover, the representation should be such that the algebraic model is the limiting case when the noise vanishes.
