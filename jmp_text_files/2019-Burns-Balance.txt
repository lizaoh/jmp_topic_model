Booming technological developments in mobile computing power and inexpensive sensors have catalyzed substantial interest in the field of augmented perception: using technology to provide additional perceptual information to a human user. This information can either replace capacities lost or damaged due to injury or disease, enhance existing perceptual capabilities (e.g. more accurate orientation information) or provide access to novel variables (e.g. radiation levels).

One application area undergoing rapid development is augmentation of balance perception to help reduce falls in various populations such as the elderly ( Martinelli, Coelho, & Teixeira, 2018 ), peripheral neuropathy patients ( Richardson, Thies, DeMott, & Miller, 2004 ), and even construction workers ( Jebelli, Ahn, & Stentz, 2016 ). There are various ways in which additional information can be provided to users, but one of the more popular modalities is through touch or haptics. One of the oldest known balance aids, a cane or walking stick, provides not just mechanical support, but also additional information regarding surface stability, the distance from hand to ground, and even postural sway. While the foundational work in this domain focused exclusively on “quiet” standing balance ( Jeka, 1997 ),  Oates, Hauck, Moraes, and Sibley (2017)  provide a scoping review of the more recent literature examining the perceptual augmentation effects of such balance aids on gait parameters during walking.

In order to offer practical improvement in everyday life, augmentation systems have to be mobile, making use of wearable sensors and dedicated microprocessors.  Ma, Wong, Lam, Wan, and Lee (2016)  provide a review of such biofeedback systems, which use inertial motion and/or force sensors to guide realtime user feedback. This feedback can be provided through vision, audition, or perhaps most commonly, vibrotactile feedback similar to the sensation of a cell phone on silent mode.

The literature presents a compelling argument that balance augmentation may improve quality of life for large segments of the population, but its real world efficacy remains to be validated. One important concern enunciated by  Lin et al. (2015)  is that the processing of additional sensory information places additional cognitive and attentional burdens on the user. This is especially relevant when considering that many of the people standing to benefit the most from this technology, such as the elderly, can also have limited executive functioning and attentional capabilities. It is theoretically possible that providing additional information could distract users from making use of other relevant cues such as optic flow and produce a performance  decrement .

The investigation of how multiple sources of perceptual information are combined to guide a response has a long history in cognitive psychology. Situations where two sources of information are presented simultaneously and are independently sufficient for dictating the response are called redundant conditions. Mean response times in redundant trials can be compared to those for single cue conditions to indicate benefits or costs of additional information ( Colonius & Diederich, 2018; Mateo, Simpson, Gilkey, Iyer, & Brungart, 2012 ), but such aggregate measures can reveal relatively little about the structure of mental processing involved.

The two simplest models for representing how this information is combined both assume that each source of information is processed independently in its own channel, with these two channels operating either one at a time in a serial model, or simultaneously in a parallel model. Although these have been called “the world’s simplest and most antipodal models”, issues of mimicry (especially at the level of mean response times) have made distinguishing between them using behavioral data a much more challenging task than was initially anticipated ( Townsend, Yang, & Burns, 2011 ).

One of the primary metrics used for discerning the operant model has been comparing response times in the redundant condition to trials with only one cue present. A simple serial model would predict that redundant trials should take as long as the single cue trials for whichever cue was processed first. In contrast, a simple parallel model assumes both channels proceed at the same time, and whichever finishes processing first drives the response.  Raab (1962)  showed that such a model would be expected to display faster mean response times in the redundant condition than when either source was presented in isolation due to statistical facilitation: with two channels you have double the chance of getting a faster than usual time.

In the development of Systems Factorial Technology (SFT), however,  Townsend and Nozawa (1995)  point out that these attempts to infer the architecture of the system (serial, parallel, or coactive) based on response time differences between redundant and single cue conditions require an assumption that the speed of processing for one source of information does not depend on whether or not the other source of information is also present (context independence). As  Luce (1986)  and  Miller (1982)  had previously argued, they proposed that due to resource limitations we might expect to see many cases where the processing of an individual channel slows down as more channels are simultaneously activated. They defined this potential change in individual channel processing speeds as a function of the number of active channels as workload capacity.

The capacity coefficient was developed to help quantify such violations in context independence ( Houpt & Townsend, 2012; Townsend & Eidels, 2011; Townsend & Nozawa, 1995 ). The difficulty with these approaches is the well known problem of model mimicry. As an example, Miller’s inequality is only capable of distinguishing parallel and coactive models under the assumption of unlimited-capacity, when workload does not effect processing speed. A coactive model where channels slow down with increased workload (limited-capacity) can still satisfy the inequality, while a parallel model where the channels speed up with workload (super-capacity) could violate the bound.

Similarly, the capacity coefficient cannot alone distinguish whether a particular pattern of performance in the redundant trials is due to a specific architecture, stopping rule, or to changes in channel processing speed that depend on workload: any could push performance above or below the UCIP baseline. Fortunately, SFT comprises additional tools capable of identifying architecture and stopping rule using salience manipulations on the redundant trials ( Townsend & Nozawa, 1995 ). Through the Mean Interaction Contrast (MIC) value and the Survivor Interaction Contrast (SIC) function, we can identify the mental architecture with regards to whether these two streams of information are processed in serial, parallel, or pooled coactively. Simultaneously, the stopping rule can also be determined: if both sources of information are processed before a response is made (exhaustive), or if one is enough (self-terminating). Although such a stopping rule is ostensibly governed by the experimental task, participants do not always conform to the task structure, as seen in mandatory exhaustive processing with faces ( Wenger & Townsend, 2006 ) or the recent evidence of sporadically “breaking” decision rules in information integration ( Bushmakin, Eidels, & Heathcote, 2017 ).

The inferences concerning mental processing that can be drawn using the SFT paradigm stand to provide valuable information about the feasibility and potential success of a balance augmentation system. If performance were to violate the Grice bound, for example, the augmentation system would only be making things worse! In order to utilize these tools for this experimental situation, however, several modifications must be made. Let us consider a system that uses Center of Pressure (CoP) measurements to drive vibrotactile feedback. Typically, CoP is measured continuously, and whenever it moves too far in one direction a vibration signal is administered corresponding to that direction to help guide the user back to center. We should remember that the vibratory feedback signal is only one source of information being used to guide these postural adjustments, as people are constantly making use of optic flow, foot pressure, and many other natural perceptual signals ( Horak, 2006 ).

Systems Factorial Technology draws inferences based on response times to cues presented in hundreds of discrete trials, and as such cannot be immediately applied to the above situation, where continuous small adjustments are being made. In order to apply this paradigm, discrete responses must be elicited by discrete stimuli. In this experiment, our stimuli were comprised of visual cues displayed on a computer monitor with additional haptic cues from a vibrotactile feedback belt. In accordance with the Double Factorial Paradigm design for SFT studies, each type of cue was either high salience, low salience, or absent, yielding eight stimulus combinations (there were no absent–absent trials).

While responses in SFT experiments are almost always issued by pressing buttons on a response pad or keyboard, this experiment used a novel lean-to-respond paradigm designed to better mimic the application of interest. Participants completed the experiment while standing on a Wii Balance Board, and issued a response by quickly shifting their weight in the direction of the cue, which could indicate one of four possible directions (forward-left, forward-right, back-left, or back-right). This setup allows for a direct examination of how vision and augmented haptic cues are combined in order to make postural adjustments. The Wii Balance Board has previously been shown to accurately and reliably measure Center of Pressure (CoP) within 1.5 mm ( Bartlett, Ting, & Bingham, 2014 ). One drawback to this form of response is that it takes longer and is more tiring that simply pressing a button. For that reason, experimental sessions were limited to 30 min and participants were asked to complete two sessions within one week.

This experiment began with extensive pilot testing to select salience values which produced similar response times between visual and vibratory cues and reliable RT differences between low and high salience stimuli. While equality across modalities is not explicitly required by SFT, salience manipulations in one dimension may be ineffective if the other dimension is too salient by comparison ( Eidels, Townsend, & Algom, 2010 ). The experimenters and some volunteers participated in initial runs using single cues (no redundant trials) where vibration voltages and visual cue transparency were adjusted manually between different runs until two-sample Kolmogorov–Smirnov tests indicated significant distributional differences between low and high salience stimuli of the same modality, and response times were roughly similar between the two modalities (no objective criteria were used to compare modalities). After deciding on the final stimulus values and receiving approval from the university Internal Review Board (case #0005), the experiment commenced.

After pilot testing, a separate nineteen participants were recruited via fliers posted around campus. The only participation criteria were that they be 18 or older and have no balance problems. All participants provided informed consent. Participants were asked to return for a second session within seven days, but only 11 did so. All participants were compensated with $5 for each 30 min session they completed.

For the vibrotactile feedback, we designed and built an adjustable belt with four vibration motors positioned around it on the left and right sides of the front and back of the body. This belt was connected via bluetooth and controlled by an Arduino micro-controller, which was synchronized with a computer connected to a CRT monitor with 1024 × 768 resolution and an 85 hz refresh rate. This monitor displayed experimental instructions and feedback, as well as displaying visual cues in the form of small red circles. These circles appeared in one of the four corners of a white square in the middle of a black background, with the top left corner signifying that participants should lean forward and to the left (they were not required to “hit” the circle with their CoP, just lean in that direction). Participants were initially positioned 66 cm from a CRT monitor such that the white square subtended approximately 8 degrees of visual angle, with the circles sized at 1 degree.

After baseline initialization, participants were shown a demo screen that displayed how their CoP moved when they shifted their weight. When they were ready to proceed, they were given 16 practice trials, two of each possible stimulus combination. They were instructed to respond as quickly as possible while maintaining high accuracy. If they had no further questions, the experimenter left the room and they proceeded with the first of six blocks of trials.

Each block had 60 trials: 20 redundant trials where both types of cues were presented, and 20 single cue trials with only a visual cue, and 20 trials with just a haptic cue. The trials were distributed in this way so when one cue is present, the other cue is equally likely to be present or absent. The trials were then randomly shuffled within each block. The direction indicated by the cue was determined randomly for each trial, and for redundant trials both cues always indicated the same direction.

On every trial, a fixation cross was displayed in the center of the screen so that participants knew the trial was beginning. This remained on the screen for a randomly generated time between 500 and 1000 ms to reduce time-locked anticipatory responses. After this time, a cue was presented either on screen, through the belt, or both. Cues remained present for 3 s or until a response was issued, which ever occurred first. If no response was detected within the 3 s, a “time out” was declared and the next trial began. Responses were determined by averaging the weight at each corner within a moving window containing the most recent 20 samples taken at 100 Hz (so 200 ms). A response was declared when the weight at one corner increased by 10% above its baseline value, and the response time was recorded as the elapsed time since the cue was turned on. In the chance that two corners both increased above the threshold at the same time, the one that increased more was deemed to indicate the response.

Responses were determined using a moving window in order to help eliminate false alarm responses, since the act of maintaining upright balance requires many instantaneous forces, and leaning in one direction often involves initially applying force in the opposite direction. While this method of calculating the response helped to ensure responses were intentional, it also means that response times may be inflated by a small constant, since a response is not logged until the 200 ms average clears the threshold.

After either a response or a time out, the system waited for 1250 ms before checking if the participant’s CoP had returned within the 4 inch central region of the board. If not, a message was displayed asking them to return to center, and once they had, the next trial began. Participants were allowed to get off the board to rest between blocks, and a new baseline calibration was computed at the start of every block.

Architecture and stopping rule were identified using SIC and MIC results. SIC functions were computed for each participant and the group as a whole, with Kolmogorov–Smirnov tests used to check if these functions have significant deviations above and below zero ( Houpt & Townsend, 2010 ). Serial-OR models predict no significant deviations while serial-AND models predict initially negative and then positive SICs. Parallel-OR models predict a positive SIC, while parallel-AND models predict a negative SIC. Coactive models predict similar SIC signatures as serial-AND models, but can be distinguished using the MIC, which is predicted to be equal to zero for serial models, negative for parallel-AND, and positive for parallel-OR and coactive models.

In order to further examine MIC values, the hierarchical Bayesian model developed by  Houpt and Fific (2017)  was also employed. This model estimates a group level categorical distribution for the probabilities that MIC values are less than, greater than, or equal to zero. From this Dirichlet distribution, priors are drawn for each of the participants, with this hierarchical structure being especially advantageous for situations like this one where the number of trials per participant is relatively small.

Some of this uncertainty can be ameliorated through further analysis of the MIC data employing the hierarchical Bayesian model designed by  Houpt and Fific (2017) . This model provides posterior estimates for the probability than a given participant’s MIC value was less than, equal to, or greater than zero. These probabilities are informed by group level parameters, and all of these parameters are based on reasonable uninformative priors. For this analysis, consistent with the experimental example from  Houpt and Fific (2017) , model fitting included four MCMC chains using 10,000 warm-up samples and 20,000 additional iterations per chain. Estimated probabilities for the sign of the MIC values are shown in  Table 2 . These values indicate strong support for a positive MIC at the group and individual levels, as predicted by parallel self-terminating models and coactive models.

This experiment was intended to help establish a foundation for augmented perception through vibratory feedback by testing the degree to which redundant haptic information improved performance over vision alone in a novel lean-to-respond postural task. The tools of Systems Factorial Technology have shown that while participants benefited from the redundant information to a lesser degree than would be expected under UCIP processing, they still benefited, responding faster to redundant cues than when visual cues were presented alone. This finding helps demonstrate the promise of multi-modal sensory augmentation devices.

More specifically, the evidence indicates the most likely model to be self-terminating parallel processing with limited-capacity and/or inhibition between channels. Results showing positive SIC signatures and positive MIC values reject serial models and an independent parallel model with exhaustive processing, though  Eidels et al. (2011)  showed that an exhaustive parallel model with inhibitory interactions could produce similar looking interaction contrast results. Coactive models can also produce similar data patterns, but are predicted to elicit capacity values greater than one. With the exception of the very fastest responses, capacity was significantly limited, arguing against a coactive model.

It is possible that there is some individual variability across participants, with four participants displaying SIC signatures and MIC values potentially consistent with serial processing. However, it is quite possible that these apparent differences are just due to limitations in statistical power as the number of trials within each participant was somewhat small. Because serial self-terminating processing predicts an SIC equal to zero, this model cannot be confirmed through null-hypothesis statistical tests. However, serial processing predicts strongly limited capacity that would violate the Grice bound, which was not seen for these participants. As pointed out by  Houpt and Fific (2017) , a hierarchical Bayesian model is better suited for drawing conclusions when the number of trials per participant is limited, ameliorating the concern of false alarm inflation when each participant’s data is analyzed separately. These results offer even stronger evidence that serial processing is unlikely, with all participants showing a greater than 70% chance of having a positive MIC.

The capacity coefficient data appears even more uniform across participants. With the exception of the fastest response times, all participants displayed workload capacity between the UCIP and Grice bounds, consistent with parallel processing with mild limitations in capacity or inhibition between channels.While the earliest responses indicate violations of the Miller bound for some participants and violations of the Grice bound for others, these data should be interpreted with caution. As an example, participant P19 shows values consistent with extremely limited-capacity around 800 ms, followed by a rapid rise to unlimited-capacity by around 900 ms. The capacity coefficient is undefined until responses have been observed in all three relevant conditions: redundant, vision alone, and tactile alone. For this participant this first occurs at 739 ms, and the next observed response for the redundant condition comes at 879 ms and is quickly followed by 6 more redundant responses by 950 ms. With the number of responses so low at the beginning, the ratio of integrated hazards is strongly impacted by small clusters of responses such as these. The capacity tests are weighted according to response density, however, alleviating some of these concerns.

On the other hand, participant P01 continued to show capacity coefficient values above the Miller bound for the whole first half of the response distribution, as can be seen in the gray line indicating the pdf of all responses in  Fig. 3 . This participant thus may be demonstrating at least some degree of coactivation, allowing for fast responses violating the race model inequality, but later responses indicate this facilitation may be balanced out by the effects of limited capacity. At the group level, the initial violation of the Miller bound is based on only 3% of total trials, with a full third of them coming from participant P16, suggesting this may not be an important feature in the data demanding explanation.

While the capacity coefficient is typically examined by pooling across the salience manipulations, more information can be gained by examining how relative performance differs with respect to salience combinations. For example,  Miller (2016)  argues that having super- or limited-capacity is a violation of context independence (since individual channels behave differently in single versus redundant trials), and should be considered as another form of cross channel coactivation. Following his conception, capacity effects could be modeled as facilitatory (super-capacity) or inhibitory (limited-capacity) interactions between channels. Indeed,  Eidels et al. (2011)  explored SFT predictions when channels are not independent and showed that facilitation produces C(t) values indicative of super-capacity and inhibition mimics limited-capacity. One potential difference here is that such interactive effects would depend on the salience of the channel, while workload capacity is typically presumed to depend only on the presence of information in a channel.

In this data, violations of the UCIP bound were only seen for the fastest responses where the tactile cue was high salience. This was likely because the low salience tactile cue was slow enough that it was unable to provide much redundant benefit before the visual cue was capable of responding on its own. As can be seen in  Fig. 1 , the first quartile for the low salience single target tactile cue occurs around the same time as the third quartile for single visual cues of high or low salience.

While such interactions between channels could be thought of as denoting a resource limitation, as workload capacity is frequently represented, the change in efficiency as a function of channel salience would demand a framework where the limitation depends not just on the number of channels that are active but also on the amount of information in each channel, and thus looks more like what  Miller (2016)  described. Because it is still uncommon to compare C(t) functions across salience manipulations, there is not yet a statistical test for determining if differences between these functions are significant, but one could be developed based on the existing UCIP test and such a test could prove useful for future investigations of these questions.

One challenge in implementing the SFT methodology in this domain was the differential speed of processing for visual and vibratory cues. In order for responses to the high salience visual cue to be (almost) as slow as responses to the maximal vibration, the stimuli had to be reduced to 8% opacity, with only 3% for low salience. Even at these values, vision ended up dominating vibration, as discussed in the computation of the Grice bound. The principle of inverse effectiveness from the literature on multi-sensory supports that redundancy across modalities looses its power to improve performance when the single channel information is too strong on its own, similar to a ceiling effect ( Mateo et al., 2012; Welch & Warren, 1986 ). Future experiments could benefit from systematically manipulating the stimulus onset asynchrony between the two signals rather than the salience, as described by  Gondan and Blurton (2015) .

The relative unfamiliarity of responding to vibratory cues is likely playing a key role here, and it is reasonable to hypothesize that further practice doing so may reduce the observed asymmetry between the modalities. It also remains an important question as to whether the results found here under cases of similar speed of processing will continue to hold in less constrained situations where the visual cues may be much stronger. That being said, the application of interest concerns people who are having difficulty perceiving their balance, and thus using low salience visual cues with healthy young participants may better approximate the population of interest.

Another challenge is the relatively slow and variable response times obtained by this novel lean-to-respond methodology. As mentioned in the procedure, responses were determined using a 200 ms moving window, necessitating a delay between when the participant shifted their weight and when the response was logged.  Townsend and Honey (2007)  have previously analyzed the impact of base time on the capacity coefficient, where base time is understood as the “non-cognitive” component of the response (e.g. motor execution) and operationalized as the component of measured response times that is independently and identically distributed across trials and conditions. Subsequent work will need to examine the efficacy of vibratory feedback when it is driven by realtime changes in posture rather than presenting predetermined cues. While this experiment compared such cues to specific visual stimuli, the perceptual information available to guide postural adjustments and maintain balance is much more complex and comes from many modalities ( Horak, 2006 ). Although studies have already shown that vibratory feedback can help reduce postural sway ( Ma et al., 2016 ), it remains to be seen how such information is integrated with existing perceptual channels. Potential individual differences may exist such that some people are more amenable to perceptual augmentation than others, and investigation of the factors driving these differences would be of great benefit to the field as it moves closer to making such devices available to the public.
