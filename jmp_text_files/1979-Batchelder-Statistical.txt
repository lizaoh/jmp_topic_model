BERSHAD  School of Engineering, University of California, Irvine, California 92717  This paper formalizes and provides static and dynamic estimators for a scaling model for rating chess players. The model was  suggested by the work of Arpad Elo, the inventor of the chess rating system in current use by both the United States and  international chess federations. The model can be viewed as a Thurstone Case V model that permits draws (ties). A related  model based on a linear approximation is also analyzed. In the chess application, possibly changing ability parameters are  estimated sequentially from sparse data structures that often involve many fewer than M(M - 1)/2 observations on the M players  to the rated. In contrast, psychological applications of paired-comparison scaling generally use models with no draw provision  to estimate static parameters from a systematically obtained data structure such as a replicated "round robin" involving all M  entities to be scaled. In the paper, both static and sequential estimators are provided and evaluated for a number of  different data structures. Sampling theory for the estimators is developed. The application of rating systems to track  temporally changing ability parameters may prove useful in many areas of psychology.  Despite the central status of paired-comparison methodology in quantitative psychology, the largest application of the  methodology is found in the chess playing community. The United States Chess Federation (USCF) and the International Chess  Federation (FIDE) each maintain current ability ratings for a number of tournament chess players. The USCF has over 40,000  rated players who range from beginners to international grandmasters. The FIDE maintains ratings on over 1,000 active  international players at the master level and beyond. The ratings are positive integers that vary after each tournament. They  are based on a statistical model of chess performance, and they are designed to measure current playing ability on an interval  scale. The inventor of the system in current use by both USCF and FIDE is Arpad Elo.' Paired-comparison methodology is widely studied and employed throughout the social and biological sciences. Davidson and  Farquhar (1976) present over 300 references and David (1963) is a good introduction to the subject. It has been noted for some  time that chess contests easily fit into a paired-comparison mold. If one has M chess players, a rating system may be viewed  as assigning scale values to the players based on the results of chess games-win, loss, or draw(tie). In this paper, we will  consistently use chess terminology;  RATING CHESS PLAYERS 41  however, it is important to note that our results apply to any of a variety of other situations that fit into  paired-comparison methodology. Chess tournament results provide data structures for estimating ratings in the same way that  choice experiments provide data structures for estimating object scale values.  One reason to study chess rating systems is that the practical demands placed on the systems have forced them to overcome a  number of methodological restrictions that limit the applicability of paired-comparison scaling in psychology. The next five  numbered paragraphs describe five such problems and indicate their relevance to more general methodology. The formal systems  that we present provide a solution to these problems.  l. The problem of ties. In chess, a draw is as important to consider as are the win and the loss. In tournament results, the  draw counts as half a win in determining a player's total score; thus, the model behind a rating scheme must explicitly  incorporate the draw possibility. In choice experiments, a pair of objects may yield no choice of preference without  artificially forcing a choice. The models typically used by psychologists do not incorporate probabilities of ties.  2. The problem of implicit estimators. In chess, it is necessary to maintain updated published ratings on the entire community  of tournament chess players. These ratings are used to pair players in large open tournaments, determine the invitations to  smaller round robin tournaments, and to determine title prerequisites for such categories as national and international  masters and grandmasters. Expense alone motivates one to develop analytic formula that directly calculate rating estimates  from the results of each player against his or her opponents in a tournament. Further, player strategy often depends on the  desire to earn title norms which, in turn, are tied to the rating system; consequently, it is important that the rating  formulae be comprehensible to players even without extensive mathematical backgrounds. Most parameter estimation schemes for  paired-comparison systems in psychology involve complicated, implicit equations for the estimated scale values. Further, the  scale value for an object depends on the results of choices not involving that object. While such schemes are quite adequate  for any single study with stable proportions Pi; , the nonanalyticity of the estimates prevents the scale values form being  incorporated usefully as parameters into more complicated psychological models involving a choice mechanism. The development  of simple closed form estimation methods would increase the range of applicability of the models in experimental and  theoretical psychology.  3. The problem of sparse data. It is commonplace in chess tournaments to determine ratings of M players based on N - M games,  where N is much smaller than M. An additional complication is that tournament performance determines pairings in real time and  these pairings may be suboptimal in an estimation sense. Even a round robin provides only one observation on each pair of  players. The rating system must be designed to perform well despite the sparse and unsystematic data structure provided by  tournament crosstables.  Most estimation schemes in psychology demand multiple observations on each pair of objects. There is a large literature on  incomplete designs (see David, 1963, p. 61-73);  42 BATCHELDER AND BERSHAD  however, these designs typically demand systematic sampling of a subset of the pairs of objects. Practical research demands  may preclude systematic data structures and it would greatly enhance the applicability of paired-comparison methodology if it  could be applicable to sparse and unsystematic data structures.  4. The newcomer problem. New players are always entering the chess community and they often improve rapidly. It is important  that newcomers be assigned accurate ratings as soon as possible by the rating system. In psychological applications, once data  from a set of M objects are collected and scaled, there is no efficient method specified for including a new object a+i in  the system. In most current estimation methods, one would have to get stable estimates of pi,, ,, , for 1 S i < M, and rescale  the entire system. It would be useful if estimation techniques were developed for incorporating new objects into the choice  set and then scaling them with a minimum of experimental comparisons.  5. The problem of dynamic changes. Chess players are constantly changing their ability. Thus a chess rating system must  involve a plan for sequentially revising ratings to reflect these changes. Most of the paired-comparison work in psychology  proceeds under the assumption that the scale values do not change with time. While it is possible, for example, in a  longitudinal study to scale separate paired-comparison systems on the same object set at each point in time and compare scale  values, it would be of value if an estimation scheme existed for sequential updating of scale values. Ideally such a scheme  would be responsive to dynamical changes in scale values.  II. FORMAL MODELS  History. The first theoretical paper on chess rating systems was by the mathematician Zermelo (1929). Later Good (1955)  published an axiomatic approach to constructing a chess rating system. Neither system was put into practice, however.  Statisticians have been aware of the formal similarity between a round robin chess tournament and a pairedcomparison matrix,  and they have worked extensively on the problem of constructing rational ways to rank players from round robin results (see  David, 1963).  Prior to Elo's work, chess ratings were obtained by a variety of methods, (see Harkness, 1956, pp. 331-60) none of which were  based on a formal model. Elo (1961, 1965a, 1965b, 1966, 1967, 1973) developed the first practical rating system based on  formal considerations. With the exception of Elo (1965a), most of his papers are either privately printed or consist of short  expository articles in the chess periodicals. The aim was to obtain a working system for chess rating; consequently, much of  the writing is at an intuitive level and a formal model is not presented.  Elo's rating system can be viewed as a system of approximations designed to render serviceable a modified version of  Thurstone's Case V model of Eq. (2). While the actual model is never stated by Elo or tied to Thurstone's model, we present it  for our development.  The Models. The first model is a simple extension of the Case V model of Eq. CONCLUSION  In this paper we have analyzed the static and dynamic estimation problems for two models for rating chess players motivated by  Elo's chess rating system. If one were interested in paired comparison scaling where underlying choice probabilities were  constrained away from 0 and 1-even as loose a constraint as pi; E (.1, .9)-one could use the uniform model for both static and  dynamical scaling. Even if the "true" model was quite different, such as the Thurstone model, fairly accurate scale values can  be obtained from sparse and unsystematic data structures. The explicit sampling theory for the uniform model's estimators  presented in this paper can be used to make decisions about how much data to collect.  A chess rating system, with its ability to track changes in underlying scale values, may prove to be a useful tool in  longitudinal studies in a variety of areas of psychology. For example, Elo (1965) presents a study comparing the top chess  grandmasters from 1850 to date. Cronological rating plots are obtained from published tournament crosstables. The individual  curves tend to be inverted U's with a more rapid upswing than downswing. The mean peak rating was 36 years. There would seem  to be numerous studies in the aging and development area that could utilize a rating system to track time varying ability  parameters.
