THOMAS  Department of Psychology, Stanford University, Stanford, California 94305  A general model for learning signal detection in a Yes-No task is considered in which (a) the decision criterion shifts upward  and downward by equal steps, and (b) the probability that the criterion shifts on a trial depends on the stimulus-response  pair on that trial. Conditions are given for this process to have a unique stationary distribution, and the behavior of the  process, as the step-size decreases to zero, is studied. The special model where criterion shifts may occur only on error  trials can account for the probability matching behavior and for the interaction between signal strength and signal  probability typically observed when the payoff matrix is symmetrical. Further, when the shift probability is assumed to be an  increasing function of the distance of the sensory input from the criterion, the stationary mean criterion values are less  extreme than the values that yield probability matching. It is shown that the model provides a way of incorporating payoff  matrix asymmetry. Finally, the standard error in the maximum likelihood parameter estimates is calculated and a statistic is  proposed for discriminating the error-correcting model from the more general model.  1. INTRODUCTION  It is a common finding that subjects in detection, recognition and discrimination tasks perform differently from the ideal  observer, that hypothetical subject whose decision-making behavior is optimal. One form of nonoptimal behavior which has  received some attention is that where subjects attempt to match their response probabilities to certain event (stimulus)  probabilities, rather than attempt to maximize their payoffs. Probability matching, as this behavior is labeled, was proposed  by Parks ( 1966) as an adequate description of performance in a yes/no recognition memory task, the data being analyzed within  the framework of "signal detection theory" (Tanner and Swets, 1954). Thus there are at least three questions, concerning the setting of criteria, that ought to be studied further: the  relationship of K in Eq. On the first of these questions, Thomas and Legge (1970)  suggested that K can be defined as the value of being correct when the signal is present relative to when it is absent, but  there is no compelling reason to accept this formulation. What seems to be missing is some understanding of the mechanisms  that can generate matching behavior, and it is to a discussion of such a mechanism that we now turn.  Kac (1962), in a seminal paper, proposed an error-correcting procedure in which the subject's decision criterion changes only  when an error is made. It was shown that in a yes/no detection task, if the sensory distributions, given signal plus noise and  noise alone, are symmetrical and if the signal probability is 1/2, the limiting criterion value is the optimal value (it is  also the value given by Eq. (1) with K = 1). A similar model has been considered by Larkin (1971) in which the sensory  distribution is discrete, and it was shown that the model was better able to account for his data than fixed-criterion models.  Some evidence against the error-correcting model comes from a study by Dorfman and Biderman (1971) in which they conclude that  the overall goodness of fit of Kac's model is substantially inferior to that of a similar model that permits learning after  correct responses. Dorfman, Saslow, and Biderman (1973) reconsidered this conclusion in the light of a reexamination of their  data. A theoretical analysis of Norman (1972) shows that the Kac model predicts probability matching, and we shall see that  matching occurs in the data of Dorfman and Biderman.  In this paper we will consider a general learning model and will derive results similar to those of Norman (1972), though our  methods will be different. By considering a special case of this model, we will show how the effects of payoff magnitude and  signal strength can be assessed. Finally, a statistic is proposed which discriminates between the error-correcting and general  models.  ERROR CORRECTING MODELS 243  2. CONCLUDING REMARKS  This paper has considered a mechanism by which a subject might "learn" signal detection. It is assumed that there is a  continuum of sensory states, and that the course of learning is the time course of a decision criterion, shifting by equal  steps along the sensory continuum. The stimulus feedback, that is given immediately after a response, is effective in causing  a criterion shift with a probability that depends on signal and response, the effect being to decrease (increase) the  probability of an incorrect (correct) response. These "conditioning" probabilities, given a stimulus, are assumed to be  greater for incorrect than for correct responses, and their magnitudes are assumed to be directly related to the magnitudes of  the experimenter-defined payoffs.  Our analysis of the general learning model has had two foci-the behavior of the process a long time after its start, and its  behavior as the step-size tends to zero-and many of the results are similar to those obtained by Norman (1972). In the present  model, the conditioning probabilities determine the interval within which the signal probability must lie of the process is to  have a stationary distribution. Only for the  ERROR CORRECTING MODELS 263  error-correcting model is this interval equal to (0, 1), implying that a stationary distribution exists for all values of  signal probability.  Error-correction has been proposed as a possible decision strategy in learning situations other than signal detection (see,  e.g., Restle, 1962), and, in any case, has much intuitive appeal. In the present analysis it has been shown that  error-correcting models can account for many gross features of detection data, e.g., probability matching, conservatism in  decision criteria, the interaction between decision criterion and signal strength, and the effect on criterion of increasing  payoff magnitude. At the "fine grain" level, parametric tests carried out by Dorfman et al., (1973) have yielded only partial  support for the deterministic-shift model (i.e., with the conditioning probabilities equal unity.) The probabilistic-shift  model has not been tested, either by maximum likelihood methods or by the "nonparametric" method proposed in the preceding  section. Thus there is still some question as to whether the model accounts adequately for trial by trial data.  Payoffs are assumed to affect behavior via the conditioning probabilities, and much more data needs to be collected in order  to elucidate this relationship. For example, it has been shown that, if the conditioning probability is a decreasing  proportion of payoff (as payoff increases), then the decision criteria would be conservative. This result would have more  "explanatory power" if this effect of varying payoff were established empirically.  A feature of the model which merits comment is the role played by the shape of the probability density function of sensation,  given a stimulus. Results concerning the stationarity and limiting behavior, as step-size tends to 0, of the process depend  only on strict monotonicity of the distribution function. However, some of the results on the gross aspects, e.g.,  conservatism, depend on symmetry and log-concavity of the density function, and one of the results depends, in addition, on  the slope of the density function. Most of these results, then, are valid for the "low-tailed" distributions such as the  normal, logistic and Laplace distributions, but not for "high-tailed" distributions such as the Cauchy distributions. Further,  log-concavity turns up in another surprising place; it is used by Dorfman (1973) to prove the positive definiteness of the  variancecovariance matrix of the maximum likelihood parameter estimates. This property of the density function ensures the  monotonicity of the likelihood ratio (assuming the pdfs, are the same for all stimuli except for location), and that of the  hazard function. For these reasons, the assumption of log-concavity will probably continue to be critical in the analysis of  detection models.  REFERENCES  BARTLETT, M. S. An introduction to stochastic processes.
