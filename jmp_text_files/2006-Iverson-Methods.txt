This is the first in a sequence of short papers that emphasize the use of analytic tools in psychophysical theory. While psychophysical theory has benefited greatly from the (mainly) algebraic methods of fundamental measurement ( Falmagne, 1985 ;  Krantz, Luce, Suppes, & Tversky, 1971 ), it is the methods of the ordinary calculus that take center stage in the present analysis.

Our emphasis on analytic methods has its origins in the very birth of psychophysical theory, with Fechner's use of a “Mathematical Auxiliary Principle” ( Fechner, 1889 ) to justify his famous assertion that sensory magnitude grows as the logarithm of physical intensity. In their critical review of Fechner's psychophysics,  Luce and Edwards (1958)  examined the auxiliary principle and found it wanting. They pointed out, correctly, that the basis for a psychophysical theory consistent with Fechner's ideas was to be found in Abel's equation—a functional equation, not a differential one. They went further to recast Fechner's program of psychophysics in terms of psychophysical discrimination. Doing so introduced a new variable to the discussion, an index of discriminability that subscripts Fechner's notion of just noticeable difference (jnd); in terms of the theory of signal detection discriminability is merely

The next major chapter in the saga of Fechner's psychophysical theory was written by  Krantz (1971) ; see also the earlier work of  Pfanzagl (1962) . Krantz and Pfanzagl realized that Fechner's auxiliary principle could be given a logical basis, but only in the limit of zero discriminability. The Krantz–Pfanzagl theorem ( Section 2.2 ) constructs a sensory scale as the limit of a sequence of integrals of jnds, and in doing so provides a synthesis of the work of Fechner and Luce and Edwards. The Krantz–Pfanzagl theorem is a jewel in the crown of psychophysical theory, ignored perhaps but untarnished.

The present work is derivative of Luce and Edwards, Pfanzagl and Krantz. It has as a third springboard a little paper of  Falmagne (1977)  in which he explored the ramifications of an empirically testable inequality on discrimination probabilities. Falmagne termed his inequality  Weber ’ s Inequality  though it is quite clear that Weber knew nothing of it. That inequality, and other inequalities of a similar sort, allows us to estimate the error incurred by approximating a sensory scale as an integral of jnds. Put other way, we examine the sequence of integrals that appears in the Krantz–Pfanzagl theorem, but refrain from actually taking the limit of zero discriminability. Of course the failure to take a limit incurs error.  Section 3  explores the necessary error analysis and provides upper and lower bounds governing the size of errors. In  Section 4  we illustrate our analysis by way of a collection of examples. In  Section 5  we mention a variant of Fechner's auxiliary principle, one which does not lead to internal inconsistencies.

We briefly examine the heart of Fechner's method of scale construction, and show that, even in the presence of Weber's Law, his “Mathematical Auxiliary Principle” can lead to a very poor approximation to the true sensory scale. The basic ingredients of the modern theory of Discrimination ( Falmagne, 1985 ;  Luce & Edwards, 1958 ) are reviewed in  Section 2.2  and the notation that we use throughout the paper is introduced. That notation allows  Krantz’ (1971)  Theorem to be stated very simply, and in  Section 2.3  we give a short proof of the theorem, and provide a converse.

As interpreted by  Luce and Edwards (1958) , Fechner's psychophysics is a special case of difference measurement; discrimination probabilities are determined by differences of psychophysical scale values. The way that a psychophysicist chooses to measure the physical intensities that appear as arguments of the psychophysical scale is perforce irrelevant to the psychophysical judgment. Consequently, if psychophysical data do in fact conform to the axioms of difference measurement ( Falmagne, 1985 ;  Krantz et al., 1971 ), they must exhibit structural properties that are independent of the way in which the physical stimuli are described. For example, in the algebraic theory of difference measurement the so-called  quadruple condition  plays a key role. In the present development we have instead stressed the fact that sensitivities form a local Abelian group under composition.

On the other hand, Psychophysical Laws, a prime example of which is Fechner's Logarithmic Law, depend for their expression on an explicit choice of physical scale.

We take up these and related matters in this concluding section.

Our development has involved stimuli labeled by a set  D  of real numbers, and called “physical intensities”. As we pointed out at the beginning of  Section 2.2 , the set  D  might represent the amplitudes of a set of pure tones of fixed frequency, the power of white (or colored) noise, or those same quantities measured on the logarithmic dB scale; or the contrast of sinusoidally modulated visual gratings, or the orientation of those gratings, etc. This raises the obvious question: to what extent does the above theory depend on the way in which stimuli are measured?
