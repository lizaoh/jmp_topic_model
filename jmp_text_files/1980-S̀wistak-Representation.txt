INTRODUCTION  The semiorder notion introducted by Luce (1956) has been motivated in psychology by the notion of threshold and just  noticeable difference (jnd) presented by the nontransitive indifference relation. A representation problem can be described in  terms of assigning to each measured object x a value (real number) f (x) and a jnd about f (x), which is an interval on the  real line, so that we are indifferent between x and y iff f (x) is within the jnd of y. Following Scott and Suppes (1958), by  a semiorder we will mean a relational system <A, P>, where P is a binary relation which for all x, y, z, w E A satisfies:  Al. P is irreflexive on A.  A2. (xPy & zPw) => (xPw or zPy).  A3. (xPy & yPz) => (xPw or wPz).  In this axiom system P is described as a directed "difference" relation. An indifference relation (I) can be defined as a  binary relation on A such that  xIy iff not (xPy) & not (yPx), for all x, y E A.  The main results in representing semiorders were obtained by Scott and Suppes (1958) (semiorders with finite set of objects)  and Fishburn (1970, 1973) (infinite set of objects). The forms of representations studied here come from the two papers of  Fishburn (a closed, a closed interval and an interval representation) and from the paper of Scott and Suppes (1958) (a  representation with a constant).  0022-2496/80/020124-12$02.00/0 Copyright Q 1980 by Academic Press, Inc. The principle behind the "paradox" is  the following: on the basis of the intuitively proper form of the representation function, we obtain number representations  which are intuitively inadequate to the real structure of the represented model. (This problem can give rise to several  questions concerning the basic concepts of the theory of measurement.)  The principle behind our "paradox" stems from the fact that one of the representation types (a closed representation) does not  preserve the naturally arising linear structure of the represented semiorder. The main results arise from the comparison of  the usually employed forms of representations and the condition of preserving the linear structure. A closed (and a closed  interval) representation form is satisfactory to allow for the elimination of the "paradox," i.e., it can be transformed into  the representation form preserving the linear structure of the represented semiorder (Theorem 1). Another form of  representation, an interval representation, has also been studied in a somewhat different manner (due to its interval form) in  the context of our "paradox." In this case the elimination of the "paradox" is impossible (Example 2). That is why we  introduce a new form of representation, an R- representation, which seems to possess a number of better properties. For this  representation we prove the representation theorem (Corollary 2).  An R-representation appears to be of the most general form for number representations which preserve the linear structure of  semiorders. All our theorems have been proved for semiorders of arbitrary cardinality. The results presented here can be  regarded as the final solution of some representation problems, namely, those dealing with representations preserving the  linear structure of semiorders.  Detailed descriptions of the concepts used in this section are given in Section 2, the main theorems are given in Section 3,  and their proofs appear in Section 4.  2.
