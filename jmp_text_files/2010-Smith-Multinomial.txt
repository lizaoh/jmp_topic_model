For many years researchers have been highlighting the potentially dire consequences of ignoring participant and/or item heterogeneity in the analysis of cognitive models (e.g.  Ashby, Maddox, & Lee, 1994 ;  Batchelder, 1975 ;  Clark, 1973 ,  Curren & Hintzman, 1995 ;  Estes, 1956  and  Hintzman, 1980, 1993 ). Recently there has been accelerated interest in developing formal approaches to incorporating participant and/or item heterogeneity directly into cognitive models. These approaches include finite mixture models ( De Carlo, 2002; Klauer, 2006; Lee & Webb, 2005 ), Bayesian hierarchical models (e.g.  Karabatsos & Batchelder, 2003 ;  Rouder & Lu, 2005  and  Rouder, Sun, Speckman, Lu, & Zhou, 2003 ), and Dirichlet process modeling ( Navarro, Griffiths, Steyvers, & Lee, 2006 ).  Smith and Batchelder (2008)  provide model-free methods for detecting such heterogeneity in categorical data. This paper develops statistical inference for a class of hierarchical multinomial processing tree (MPT) models called beta-MPT models.

Traditional inference for MPT models has been developed for aggregated data and mostly it has employed classical (frequentist) asymptotic methods based on maximizing the likelihood function ( Hu & Batchelder, 1994 ); however,  Chechile (1998)  has developed his own procedures that involve Bayesian principles. Recently there has been an increasing use of MPT models for psychological assessment (see  Batchelder & Riefer, 2007  and  Chechile, 2007 , for a review of many of these studies). In such cases, individual differences among participants are expected because of variation in genetic, demographic, and other life history factors ( Batchelder, 1998 ). This paper is concerned with specifying a family of hierarchical MPT models along with new tools for statistical inference for the case where there is heterogeneity within a group of participants, each of whom responds to the same set of

The new model family was proposed in  Batchelder and Riefer (1999, 2007)  and is dubbed the beta-MPT family. Models in this family assume that each participant’s parameters for an MPT model are drawn independently from a multivariate distribution consisting of independent, marginal beta distributions (see Section  3 ). Before the beta-MPT family was explicitly formalized, the assumption that participants’ parameters were drawn from beta distributions was used in simulation exercises to check on the robustness of MPT inference based on aggregated data in  Riefer and Batchelder (1991) , and later the assumption was introduced as a simulation option in the standard MPT inference software package that analyzes aggregated data in  Hu and Phillips (1999, GPT.EXE) . To use that option, one had to first do the standard aggregate analyses of the data. Then the maximum likelihood estimates (MLEs) of the parameters were incorporated into the beta-MPT option along with selected variances, and samples of data with parameter heterogeneity could be analyzed and compared with the initial analyses based on aggregate data. However, none of the previous articles that discussed beta-MPT models provided any statistical inference for data that are not aggregated over participants. The main purpose of this paper is to provide and test new algorithms to assist in doing inference for beta-MPT models.

In this paper, we consider inference for hierarchical beta-MPT models from both a classical and a Bayesian perspective. We choose to deal with both approaches to inference because the popularity of the classical approach. Since many papers have appeared using the classical likelihood inference, it is important to offer a related approach to deal with hierarchical versions of MPT models. In addition, we want to provide approaches to data analysis for MPT models that researchers uncomfortable with Bayesian inference can use. However we will argue that the Bayesian approach is the better one to use for beta-MPT models. Both approaches are based on the likelihood function for the model. In simple cases the likelihood function for beta-MPT models can be obtained analytically; however, for more complex cases, Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithms are constructed to assist both classical and Bayesian inference. Several examples based on data from clinical settings are analyzed to demonstrate the advantages of the hierarchical approach over aggregate analysis in the presence of individual participant differences.

The paper is divided into six main sections. After this introduction, the structure of MPT models with binary links is presented. Then the data structure for hierarchical MPT models is presented and several approaches to constructing hierarchical MPT models are considered. Section  3  discusses the structure of beta-MPT models. It presents the definition of the beta-MPT family, and discusses classical likelihood-based inference for the family. Section  4  introduces MCMC methods for dealing with beta-MPT models, and general issues for conducting inference using MCMC methods are discussed. Then two algorithms are provided that can handle inference for the entire beta-MPT family. The way that these algorithms can assist in point and confidence interval estimation, hypothesis testing, and goodness-of-fit is discussed. Then in Section  5  several data sets from clinical settings are analyzed with the new algorithms. Special attention is placed on checking the quality of the algorithms, estimating parameters, testing hypotheses, and checking the adequacy of a model. Section  6  provides concluding comments.

It is easy to establish that any MPT model is statistically equivalent to a suitably parameterized BMPT model ( Hu & Batchelder, 1994 ), so from a statistical standpoint the BMPT models are useful ones to extend in a way to deal with participant heterogeneity.

Klauer (2006)  develops an approach to estimating latent class MPT models by using the EM-Algorithm ( Dempster et al., 1977 ), and model adequacy is evaluated by fitting statistics based on the category means, variances, and covariances of the participant by category counts in the data structure  H . Latent class MPT models are already being used in some MPT modeling papers.

In general, any hyperdistribution can be approximated well by a suitable number of latent classes; however, because of the proliferation of the number of parameters, latent class models are generally used only in situations where a small number of classes suffice. Such models can be expected to work well in handling participant heterogeneity if there is reason to suspect that the group of participants is drawn from several distinct but homogeneous groups. Of course the latent class MPT model may improve parameter recovery and model fit relative to inference based on aggregated data even if the components of the best fitting model do not correspond to psychologically meaningful components ( Smith, 2008 ); however, in most applications of MPT models to experimental groups it seems to us psychologically reasonable to assume that the parameters vary in a smooth, unimodal way over participants. This assumption is in concert with the usual assumptions in psychometric test theory, where participant ability is thought to come from a unimodal, normal like distribution, e.g.  Lord and Novick (1968) . The purpose of this paper is to develop and provide inference for a class of hierarchical BMPT models that exhibit this property.

MPT parameters represent probabilities of particular cognitive events so they are parameterized on the (0, 1) scale. As a result, for the consideration of parameter interpretability it makes sense to consider a hierarchical level on this natural scale. The beta distribution (e.g.  Johnson, Kotz, & Balakrishnan, 1995 ) is a flexible distribution on the interval (0, 1) that might be expected to model dispersion of binary MPT parameters across subjects. Also it will be shown later in this section that the beta distribution has some nice statistical properties that help in the estimation and inference used as a hierarchical level to the MPT. Classical confidence intervals for MLEs are generally obtained by asymptotic methods and they are interpreted as random intervals with a certain probability of including the unknown model parameters that generated the data. In the case of MPT models that assume both participants and items are homogeneous, confidence intervals are usually obtained from methods presented in  Hu and Batchelder (1994) . It is of course possible to use simulation methods to construct probable intervals around MLEs by treating the MLEs as the true parameter in the simulation.

Bayesian confidence intervals (or credible intervals as they are sometimes denoted) treat the parameter of interest as a random quantity and the interval as fixed. As a result, a 95% credible interval reflects a range of values in the posterior distribution where the researcher has a 95% confidence (given the data and the prior) that the true parameter will be found. In contrast, the frequentist approach treats the intervals as random quantities and the parameters as fixed. That is to say, given a fixed parameter there is a 95% chance over repeated samples that a 95% confidence interval calculated from a sample will cover the true parameter. For further information on the matching of Bayesian and Frequentist confidence intervals see  Kass and Wasserman (1996, Section 3.7) .

Even if a researcher prefers the philosophy behind frequentist confidence intervals there is reason to prefer Bayesian confidence intervals in the present case. When estimating MPT models using frequentist methods, confidence intervals are based on asymptotic approximations that may not be adequate given finite samples. As discussed earlier, in the case of beta-MPT models, asymptotic methods are not of much use since the number of rows  
                            N 
                          (participants) in  H  is not large relative to the possible patterns of category counts for the  
                            M 
                          items. Even in standard applications of MPT models to aggregated data, it is possible for confidence intervals estimated from the inverse of the expected Fisher information matrix to stretch outside the (0, 1) range to which the MPT parameters are restricted. In fact in research articles that use the software in  Hu and Phillips (1999)  to analyze aggregated data, these useless intervals are sometimes reported. Bayesian confidence intervals based on MCMC samples, in contrast, do respect the parameter space. In addition, the calculation of the Bayesian intervals does not require the same asymptotic assumptions, and they do have a valid interpretation even in the cases where they do not match the ideal frequentist intervals. Furthermore, as shown in examples presented later, confidence intervals for MPT parameters based on aggregate data tend to be too narrow in the presence of individual differences. Taken together, we believe it is reasonable to recommend Bayesian confidence intervals for beta-MPT parameters as an improvement over current asymptotic frequentist methods based on aggregate data.

Convergence occurs when the draws of an MCMC chain are no longer influenced by the initial values of the chain. The convergence of an MCMC chain can be examined using the R statistic initially proposed by  Gelman and Rubin (1992)  then later refined by  Brooks and Gelman (1998) . In order to calculate the R statistic a researcher runs several chains (e.g. 4 to 6) for the same model and data set but with widely dispersed starting points. At its core the R statistic provides a comparison of the between and within chain variability of these chains. Values of the R statistic close to one indicate evidence for convergence. The R statistic can be assessed for each of the model parameters separately or though a multivariate versions of R statistic (e.g.,  Brooks & Gelman, 1998 ).

The derivation of R statistic along with the theoretical background is described in detail in  Brooks and Gelman (1998) . In practice, the R statistic can be easily computed using a number of freely available software packages including WinBUGS ( Spiegelhalter et al., 2003 ) and the CODA ( Plummer, Best, Cowles, & Vines, 2007 ) package in the R programming language (not related to the ‘R’ of the R statistic). Nevertheless, if the convergence properties of an algorithm are in doubt for a given data set researchers should consider other diagnostic measures in addition to the R statistic. For details on a number of other available diagnostic procedures see  Cowles and Carlin (1996) . A number of these methods are available as built in function in WinBUGS, CODA, and other MCMC packages.

Often the early draws of an MCMC chain demonstrate poor convergence properties. This phenomenon is common, unless the starting values of MCMC algorithm are carefully chosen to lie in a high probability area of the posterior. The set of early draws, known as the burn in period, is usually discarded before proceeding with parameter estimation and inference. Sometimes it is enough to remove a fixed set of MCMC draws. For example, with the data augmentation algorithm, the chains often showed good mixing behavior after the first 1000 draws. Alternatively, a conservative approach to assessing a burn in period is to run the MCMC chain until the R statistic for the second half of the chain is close to 1. At this point the first half of the chain is discarded as a burn in period ( Gelman & Rubin, 1992 ).

Along with testing for difference between parameters across conditions, researchers may be interested in assessing how well a given model fits the observed data. In the hierarchical case this question splits into two parts. The researcher may be interested in whether the base MPT model provides an adequate description of participant behavior. In addition, researchers may be interested in whether the hierarchical level (e.g. marginal beta distributions) provide an adequate description of the between participant variability. Unless the hierarchical level is correctly specified it is not possible to check the validity of base model within the hierarchical framework using standard statistical methods ( Dey, Gelfand, Swartz, & Vlachos, 1998 ). However, researchers can still validate parameters using experimental manipulations as has been common in the existing MPT literature (e.g.  Batchelder & Riefer, 1999 ;  Riefer et al., 2002 ;  Schweickert & Chen, 2008 ). By performing experimental validations under the framework of hierarchical models, researchers can ensure their results are not artifacts due to inappropriate aggregation of the data.

A number of tools are available for checking the validity of the hierarchical level. These methods include prior and posterior predictive assessment (e.g.,  Chaloner & Brant, 1988 ;  Dey et al., 1998 ;  Gelman, Meng, & Stern, 1996 ), the application of Bayes Factors (e.g.,  Albert & Chib, 1997 ), and methods based on model selection metrics such as DIC ( Spiegelhalter, Best, Carlin, & van der Linde, 2002 ). For details on how to compute Bayes Factors using MCMC analyses see  Carlin and Chib (1995)  and  Kass and Raftery (1995) . For more details on the general issue of model checking at the hierarchical level see  Agresti, Caffo, and Ohman-Strickland (2004)  along with the above references.

An advantage of hierarchical models is they provide an estimate of the population distributions for the parameters of interest. If researchers are interested in interpreting the results at the hierarchical level it useful to consider whether the independent beta hyperdistribution provides an adequate summary of the parameter variability. Moreover, while previous research on hierarchical models has found them to be relatively robust under misspecification, it is reasonable to expect that the closer the match between the hierarchical distribution and the true underlying variability, the better the resulting estimates and inference. It is generally accepted in the application of simple statistical models to data that the models are at best approximations, and that they cannot be expected to accurately represent all aspects of a complex data set. It is important to tune the model in such a way that it is a close approximation on aspects of the data that one is primarily interested in. The approach of posterior predictive model checking is very useful for this purpose because it allows a researcher to select those aspects of that data that are most important. If correlation between base model parameters is of primary concern, it is obvious that the hyperdistribution should reflect this possibility. Nevertheless, even though the beta-MPT fails to parameterize the possibility of a correlation it still allows one to observe a correlation through examination of the estimated base model parameters. Moreover, the beta-MPT will still provide a far better approximation of participant variability than the aggregate analysis, which entirely ignores both the variance and covariance of the underlying parameters across participants.

Bias resulting from participant variability is troubling for both the clinical and experimental settings. Two groups may differ only in terms of within group variance, but the bias of parameter estimation based on inappropriate aggregate analysis may lead the researcher to interpret this difference in terms of a mean difference in a parameter between groups. Hierarchical models provide a route to reduce the possibility of such errors.

While it is beyond the scope of this paper to consider the effects misspecification on the hierarchical level in great detail, exploratory analysis in the pair-clustering case indicated that a similar reduction in bias could be achieved even if the variability on the parameters was not beta distributed. The exception to this finding was the case that the simulating distribution involved multiple modes at the hierarchical level such as might happen when fitting latent class MPT model ( Klauer, 2006 ) to data when the hyperdistribution is unimodal. As a result, the beta-MPT model should be considered with caution if the underlying variability is thought to be multimodal.

Even in cases where bias is not present, aggregation can still lead to issues in the performance of valid inference in the presence of individual differences. In the case of MPT models it is common to compare two different groups of participants and ask whether they differ on a particular MPT model parameter (e.g.  Batchelder & Riefer, 1990 ). In most cases ( Batchelder & Riefer, 1999 ) these comparisons are made by applying an asymptotic likelihood ratio test on aggregate data between a model that applies the same set of parameters to both samples, and a model that allows the parameter of interest to differ across the two groups.

It is well known that the type I error rate of an asymptotic likelihood ratio test comparing two groups based on aggregated data can be significantly inflated in the presence of participant heterogeneity (e.g.  Rouder & Lu, 2005 ). Informally this will happen even if there is no noticeable bias in the point estimates of the parameters because participant heterogeneity can inflate confidence intervals for parameters over what the asymptotic approximation provides. Because of this the likelihood ratio test may declare a significant difference even though both parameters true confidence intervals have a great deal of overlap.

The beta-MPT is well suited for the basic case where participants’ parameters are thought to follow a continuous-unimodal distribution and items are thought to be roughly homogeneous. In this case the beta-MPT provides a straightforward hierarchical model on the natural MPT parameterization. This parameterization provides interpretable hyperparameters because they are the means and variances of the base model parameters viewed as probabilities of latent cognitive processes. In addition, the data augmentation version of the MCMC algorithm allows for estimation of the MPT parameters with good convergence properties across a wide range of situations. Nevertheless there are circumstances when more elaborate hierarchical models should be considered.

Researchers have a number of options when choosing a hierarchical level. One possibility is to assume that the hierarchical level consists of a finite number of point masses (e.g.,  Klauer, 2006 ). This leads to a finite mixture MPT model that is well suited for handling multimodal variability across participants. Finite mixture models have been used in the case of the general linear model framework as a non-parametric method for capturing continuous, unimodal variability (e.g.,  Agresti et al., 2004 ). However, it remains to be seen if finite mixture models are equally suited to mimic continuous-unimodal hyperdistributions in the case of BMPT models.

Outside of finite mixture models, researchers may apply multivariate normal distributions to the logit or probit transformations of the basic MPT parameters. For example,  Rouder, Lu, Morey, Sun, and Speckman (2008)  provides a hierarchical version of the process dissociation MPT model by applying a multivariate normal distribution to a probit transformation of the standard process dissociation parameters. The probit and logit transformations open up the possibility of using the tools from the linear model framework. However, MPT models themselves are non-linear and do not fall under the extended General Linear Model (GLM) framework. As a result, findings related to the application and estimation of hierarchical GLMs do not necessarily apply to MPT models. Moreover, the linear model tool set requires additional assumptions such as additivity on the probit (or logit) scale that may be difficult to test when applied to MPT parameters. Future research on these types of models would be useful in identifying any limits to their applicability.

One possible application of the probit or logit transformation is in cases where variability is expected or identified across both items and participants. The examination of both item and participant variability has been the standard situation in psychometric test theory for many years (e.g.  Lord & Novick, 1968 ). Following the psychometric literature, models may be augmented assuming that participants and items combine additively on the logistic scale (e.g.  Maier, 2001 ). It is also possible to assume the two effects are additive on the probit scale such as the case in  Rouder et al. (2007) . We have done considerable work looking at these possibilities for dealing with correlations in parameters and item heterogeneity, but a definitive report on this work will await another paper ( Batchelder & Smith, 2006; Smith & Batchelder, 2005 ).

Hierarchical models have a number of critical advantages over models based on aggregated data. They help both to correct for biased parameter estimates and to provide valid confidence intervals and hypothesis tests in the presence of individual differences. This makes the models ideal for clinical settings where within group differences are often large. In addition, these models allow for improved estimates of individual participant parameters and provide a straightforward mechanism to examine within group variability across clinical and experimental conditions. Moreover, in recent years the availability of user-friendly software such as WinBUGS has gone a long way towards bridging the gap between the difficulty in applying hierarchical and standard aggregate analysis.

Previous research has already highlighted the utility of applying MPT models within the clinical framework (e.g.  Riefer et al., 2002 ). Hierarchical MPT models such as the beta-MPT extend both the scope and robustness of MPT models within the clinical domain.
