We extend Regenwetter's (1996) results on the relationship between (1) random relations, i.e., a probability measure on m-ary relations, and (2) random utilities, i.e., families of random variables, to (3) random functions, i.e., a probability measure over a function space. In this third approach, we assume that each sampled respondent accesses an urn of (utility) functions over the choice alternatives and that his�her judgment�choice is governed by the currently sampled (utility) function. Although the three approaches usually involve completely different sample spaces, we show, under reasonable conditions, that if any one of the representations holds then so do each of the others. We also develop the results for valued m-ary relations and relational structures. Our theoretical findings are illustrated with probabilistic models of magnitude estimation, probabilistic extensive measurement, probabilistic metric spaces, and (binary) subjective expected utility. The theoretical results complement and reformulate closely related research, e.g., that of Heyer and Niedere�e (1989, 1992), Niedere�e and Heyer (1997), and Suck (1995, 1996). � 2001 Elsevier Science

1. INTRODUCTION 

This paper extends earlier results of Regenwetter (1996) and complements and reformulates closely related work of Heyer and Niedere�e (1989, 1992), Niedere�e and Heyer (1997), and Suck (1995, 1996) by discussing the relationship between three fundamental ways of mathematically representing judgments and preferences. To motivate these three approaches, suppose that each member of a group of people is presented with a finite set C of objects, persons, or questions. Each respondent is asked to perform some choice or judgment task on some subcollection of C. For simplicity we call the elements of C choice alternatives although our results apply to various empirical paradigms other than choice tasks.[1] The empirical data to be modeled consist of relative judgment�choice frequencies in the pool of observations. The purpose of probabilistic models is to explain the sampling probabilities that gave rise to the observed relative frequencies. Typically, in the course of a study, each respondent is asked to perform several judgments�choices. As we discuss at the end of this Introduction, the reader may with some care substitute sample of responses (of one respondent) for sample of respondents. 

The three approaches of this paper to modeling the preference, judgment, and choice behavior of a population are as follows. 

(1) The random relation approach assumes that each time we ask a member of the population to perform a judgment�choice, this person is in some hypothetical cognitive (or latent) state which takes the form of a relation R over C. This cognitive state may vary between respondents and also for a given person over choice opportunities. The relation R may be binary (R�C[2] ): for example, the ranking abc (a ``best'', c ``worst'') on [a, b, c] can be represented by the binary relation [(a, b), (a, c), (b, c)]. More generally, R may be some m-ary relation (R�C[m] ), where m is a positive integer. In this paper we present two key extensions of Regenwetter's (1996) random relation approach. First, we cover the cases where the individual's latent state is a relational structure (i.e., a k-tuple of relations) in the sense of representational measurement theory (Krantz, Luce, Suppes, 6 Tversky, 1971; Luce, Krantz, Suppes, 6 Tversky, 1990; Suppes, Krantz, Luce, 6 Tversky, 1989). Second, we cover the case where the individual's latent state is a so-called valued relation. We also show how these results relate to work by Heyer and Niedere�e (1989, 1992) and Niedere�e and Heyer (1997). Since Regenwetter (1996) reviewed the first two papers in detail, we will focus on Niedere�e and Heyer (1997). 

The overt judgement or choice behavior of a person is modeled as a function of the person's latent state. When we observe the choice or judgment behavior of a random sample of persons, these respondents are viewed as a sample from an urn of (valued) relations (or relational structures) over C. Each respondent's overt behavior is a function of his�her underlying state, this function being the same for all respondents and specified by the researcher's theory. For instance, in the case of binary choices induced by rankings, the sample of respondents is conceptualized as a sample of rankings. Each respondent is assumed to choose the alternative (in the

In the context of this theory, the goal of the researcher then is, given binary choice probabilities, to find a probability distribution on the collection of rankings that explains the binary choice probabilities. More generally, in the random relation approach, given a collection of relations, given a function translating relations into judgments�choices, and given a probability distribution on judgments or choices of a certain type, the modeler's task is to find a probability distribution (possibly within a specified class of distributions) on the relations that best explains the judgment�choice probabilities. A probability distribution over a set of relations will be called a random relation. 

(2) The random utility approach assumes that a real valued (utility) random variable is associated with each choice alternative. More precisely, there exists a family of jointly distributed real random variables, indexed by the choice alternatives. The values and (marginal) distribution of each random variable describe the distribution of utility values of that alternative over the population at large. A sample of respondents is now conceptualized as a sample from the underlying probability space on which the utility random variables are defined. The judgment or choice behavior of a sample of respondents is a function of the sample outcomes of utility random variables, i.e., of the sampled random utilities. The modeler's task in this case is to find a family of jointly distributed random variables (i.e., random utilities) which can best explain the choice or judgment probabilities. 

Heyer and Niedere�e (1989, 1992), Niedere�e and Heyer (1997), Regenwetter (1996), and Suck (1995) have studied the relationship between these two approaches in detail. Abstracting away from the particular form of the empirical paradigm, Regenwetter (1996) has shown that under certain reasonable conditions a probability distribution over a class of finite m-ary relations can be translated into a family of (utility) random variables and vice versa. The present paper briefly summarizes Regenwetter's key results and shows how his results can be generalized in a straightforward way from m-ary relations to relational structures and valued relations. We also show how our theoretical approach and the alternative route taken by Niedere�e and Heyer (1997) converge to essentially the same general representation theorem. The main contribution of our current work is to link the first two approaches to the following third conceptualization (again, at a level that is independent of the empirical paradigm). 

(3) The random function approach assumes that at any specific choice opportunity each member of the population has a (utility) function on the set of choice alternatives and behaves in terms of the values that this function assigns to the choice alternatives. This utility function may vary between individuals (and within a given individual across choice opportunities). A sample of respondents is now conceptualized as a sample from an urn of such utility functions. Given a rule for translating utility values of the alternatives into an overt judgment�choice, and given a collection of judgment�choice probabilities, the modeler's task here is to find a probability measure on a given space of utility functions such that it explains the judgment�choice probabilities. A probability measure on a space of functions will be called a random function.

Each of the above three approaches may be more natural in different contexts: (1) The random relation approach is the most natural probabilistic extension of deterministic relational structures��we simply study probability distributions over a collection of relational structures. In particular, in applied measurement situations involving measurement error, this framework allows us to ask questions such as: ``What is the probability that we observe relationships that hold under the `true' relational structure?'' or ``Which deterministic relational structure yields the `best' aggregate of the data?'' The random relation approach also leads naturally to certain parametric assumptions, such as the Mallows (1957) ,-model for linear orders. (For an application, see Falmagne 6 Regenwetter, 1996.) (2) One of the most important reasons for using the random utility approach is that it allows one to rely on a large and well-studied range of parametric forms for the distribution functions of the utility random variables. (3) Finally, the random functions approach can be viewed as very similar to either of the other two approaches��first, each m-ary relation (and each relational structure) can be associated with the collection of all real representations with respect to a given m-ary relation (relational structure) on the reals; second, for each given sample point, the joint outcome of a family of real-valued random variables at that sample point is a function from the set of objects into the reals. 

It is therefore natural to study each of the three concepts in its own right. In this paper we show that under certain reasonable conditions one can interchangeably assume (1) a probability distribution on a class of relations (or relational structures), (2) a family of jointly distributed utility random variables, or (3) a probability measure on a space of (utility) functions. In the same spirit as Regenwetter (1996), we show this in a way that is independent of the particular empirical paradigms that such a representation would be applied to. This is based on Regenwetter's distinction between characterization and representation problems in the context of probabilistic choice models (see Fig. 1). Characterization problems concern the search for conditions that are necessary and�or sufficient for a set of ``data'' (e.g., choice or rating probabilities) of a given format to be induced by random relations, random utilities, or (now) random functions. Representation problems concern the relationship between the (three) possible representational forms: random relations, random utilities, and (now) random functions. We chose this terminology because it parallels the tradition in the representational theory of measurement (Krantz et al., 1971) where a representation theorem typically provides a set of necessary and�or sufficient conditions for the existence of a real representation of a relational structure. Our representation theorems operate at the level of probability distributions over relational structures and their real (random) representations.In this context, the representational theory of measurement also informally uses the term ``characterization problem'' as the search for conditions under which a solution to some representational problem exists. We constrain the word ``characterization problem'' to the issue of characterizing properties of observables which guarantee the existence of a given model. 

We direct most of our attention to representation problems, while the existing literature tends to strongly emphasize characterization problems (see, e.g., Fishburn, 1992; Koppen, 1995; and Marley, 1990, for some reviews of relevant results). In this section we extend our results to marginal probabilities such as those in the motivating example of Section 2. Our final result is very similar to Niedere�e and Heyer's (1997) General Representation Theorem (Theorem 1, p. 167) but is extended to include random functions. We first introduce a few more concepts, essentially following Niedere�e and Heyer (1997). This requires some minor differences from their notation in order to remain compatible with Regenwetter (1996) and with the notation elsewhere in the present paper. We first need to introduce the concept of a formal statement. Formal statements are a very powerful and general tool to describe observations or theoretical relationships. Suppose a person is asked to make a choice between options a and b; the formal statement Z(a, b)=1 (where Z is a [0, 1]-valued binary relation) could be used to denote the person's preference of a over b. For a person who is asked to choose a subset from a given set of choice alternatives, the formal statement Z(a)=1 7 Z(b)=0 7 Z(c)=1 7 Z(d )=0 (where Z is a [0, 1]-valued unary relation) could be used to say that the person chose objects a and c out of the set [a, b, c, d] of available options. We will not talk about formal statements in their full generality, only in terms of relational structures and at a level that suffices for our purposes.

10. SUMMARY AND CONCLUSIONS 

Figure 2 summarizes the results, two of which we now restate. The remaining results can be surmised analogously from the diagram.

The arrow from the upper left to the upper right represents Theorem 3 (and Theorem 4): Any probability measure on any Borel-realizable nonempty finite collection of (more generally, finite valued) m-ary relations translates into a corresponding probability measure and a family of jointly distributed random variables on any measurable space which has at least as many disjoint events as there are atoms on the upper left. In order not to leave our results in a theoretical vacuum we presented two very detailed examples as well as a short example, each of which illustrated various aspects of our approach. For instance, in the first example, using a weight comparison paradigm, we proposed three alternative approaches towards probabilizing extensive measurement structures. We made this example more interesting by also assuming that there exists an underlying true extensive structure of weight, which we did not assume, however, to be observable. In one approach we assumed that the balance behaves in agreement with an extensive measurement structure, which, however, may not be the true structure and which may also not remain the same structure over trials and across balances. A second approach was to view the impact on the balance of each object as a random variable. In this model the pan balance's behavior was governed by the current joint outcome of the random variables. In the third approach, we viewed the balance as a mapping which assigns positive real numbers to the currently presented objects. Being a mechanical device, the balance is subject to fluctuations over time and over objects. Thus the current state of the balance is like a randomly drawn real valued mapping from an urn of mappings. We illustrated some of our theorems that show the equivalence of the three approaches (under reasonable assumptions). With regard to defining the true structure, there may be many ways to do this. For instance, we might like our measurements to be such that the true structure can be, in principle, recovered, say, through statistical estimation. In that case, the ``estimated true structure'' would be a statistic of some sort, such as an ``average'' or a ``modal'' structure. These issues extend far beyond our specific example of extensive structures and deserve a more thorough investigation in the future. Also, we do not claim that any of our approaches adequately ``models'' real balances; this would require much additional work. 

In conclusion, this paper significantly extends Regenwetter (1996) in three ways. One is the development of the random function approach, which has often been used implicitly in the random utility literature, but has not been made explicit. This is a significant extension of the existing literature on probabilistic choice models in general. As Fig. 2 summarizes, the random function approach can naturally be viewed either as very similar to the random relation approach (left side of the figure) or very similar to the random utility approach (right side of the figure). We believe that a useful feature of the random function approach is that it enables one to state probabilistic versions of some results from the (deterministic) representational theory of measurement in a particularly natural way. 

Our second extension of Regenwetter's results involves moving from m-ary relations to relational structures and finite valued relations. The third extension consists of the two illustrative examples which are worked out in quite some detail. They 

910 

REGENWETTER AND MARLEY 

show that the theoretical results are useful in model construction. But they also illustrate that concrete examples can be more tedious to construct than the (simple and abstract) mathematical formulations of the theorems may suggest. In addition, the examples raised and briefly illustrated characterization problems where the work of Niedere�e and Heyer (1997), Suck (1995), and others, can be applied fruitfully. 

Furthermore, we have reached Niedere�e and Heyer's (1997) General Representation Theorem (their Theorem 1, p. 167) via a different route, namely starting with results on equivalent representations and then applying these results to marginal probabilities. We have furthermore extended the result to include random function representations. As a result of our approach to the theorem, we have been lead to clearly note that the general representation theorem (as stated so far) only involves ``empirical quantities'' (empirical probabilities) that are marginals of the underlying probability distributions. It is of interest to think about whether or not there may be cases where the ``empirical quantities'' are not marginals of the underlying probability distributions, yet nonetheless are in some appropriate sense ``generated'' by those underlying probability distributions, and, if this case does occur, to think about its implications for (a generalization of) the general representation theorem. 

We mention once more that, while the existing literature tends to emphasize the characterization problem, our approach follows Regenwetter's (1996) strategy of focusing on representation problems as distinct from characterization problems. Section 5 also discusses the relationship between characterization problems, paralleling Niedere�e and Heyer's general representation theorem. (As indicated earlier, Niedere�e 6 Heyer's, 1997, general approach is applicable to both the characterization and representation problems.) We have made no attempts, on the other hand, toward generating solutions of the characterization problems themselves. 

Finally, we comment on extending our work to other applications. As indicated in Section 9, our techniques extend naturally to more realistic theories such as those concerned with joint receipt preference structures (Luce, 2000b). These structures extend the usually studied gambling structures by assuming the additional primitive concept of joint receipt, which essentially corresponds to the intuitive idea of receiving two or more valued items at the same time. The basic gambles in such preference structures are usually represented numerically by a rank-dependent utility model. This is a generalization of the subjective expected utility model in which the (preference) rank order of the components of each gamble plays a fundamental role. The operation of joint receipt of two gambles usually has enough properties that an extensive measurement structure holds. Although this extensive structure has an additive representation, the assumed properties of the joint receipt structure lead to a natural nonadditive representation of the operation (Luce, 2000b). Interestingly, Luce (2000a) is now reinterpreting this (deterministic) model in a way that makes it applicable to psychophysical situations of the kind that we used in the motivating example in Section 2 (see Footnote 2). 

However, even though one can extend our techniques to more complex (utility and other) structures, it is by no means routine to do so. The reasoning in developing a probabilistic extension of a standard deterministic structure can be complex. This is especially true of random utility representations. In fact, when one looks at the relevant random utility representations, say, of the subjective expected utility
