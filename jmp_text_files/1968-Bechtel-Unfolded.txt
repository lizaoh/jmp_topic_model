BECHTEL  Oregon Research Institute, Eugene, Oregon 97403  A stochastic folding model is developed as a natural parametric extension of Greenberg's (1965) Successive Cumulations Method for constructing an unfolded scale from paired comparison preference judgments. This extension provides least squares estimates of the unfolded scale values from the transforms of (;) pairwise proportions. The stochastic folding model is a special case of a response biased preference model involving the observation of 2(2) ordered pairwise proportions. A least squares analysis is also employed in estimating the parameters of this general model. Illustrative data bearing upon each of the two models are analyzed by means of the procedures developed in the paper.  An important problem in scaling has been that of obtaining a numerical representation of a set of stimuli from ordinal judgments in the absence of strong parametric assumptions. Metric uni- and multidimensional scales constructed from nonmetric observations have been sought primarily in the areas of preference behavior (Goode, see Coombs, 1964; Abelson and Tukey, 1959) and similarity judgment (Shepard, 1962x, 1962b; Kruskal, 1964; Lingoes, 1965x, 1965b; Torgerson, 1965; Guttman, in press). Although these procedures appear to produce maximal output from minimal input, Torgerson (1965) has emphasized the value of checking their solutions against those obtained from traditional methods embodying stronger assumptions.  When any strong scaling model is approximately correct, or proves robust to being incorrect, it may be quite useful in providing inferences to underlying perceptual or cognitive structure. A classic example in uniclimensional scaling is that of Case V of Thurstone's Law of Comparative Judgment, which utilizes parametric assumptions to process qualitative pairwise choices into an interval scale. This latent attribute has been called an unfolded scale by Coombs (1952, 1964) to suggest that an individual's preference ordering over a set of stimuli may be obtained by folding the latent scale at his point of maximal utility. Thus in treating preference data by Coombs' technique, the attempt is made to unfold each individual's distinct preference ordering to a single permutation (or unfolded scale) common to all individuals.  Recently there has been considerable interest in converting the unfolded ordered metric scale to a numerical one, and the several algorithms cited above are available for this purpose. The present paper, on the other hand, presents a direct method of constructing an unfolded interval scale. This method circumvents most of the unfolding procedure as well as the subsequent algorithm for a metric conversion. The procedure developed here depends upon (a) the relation, recently observed by Greenberg (1965), between pairwise preference probabilities and the midpoints of the stimuli on the unfolded scale, and (b) an additional distribution assumption about the random variable (over individuals) representing the various ideal points of maximal utility on this latent attribute. This extension of Greenberg's method employs rather strong assumptions in processing ordinal judgments into an interval scale. Like the Law of Comparative Judgment, it is to be contrasted with currently available algorithms which operate without such assumptions in producing a numerical representation.  The next section briefly reviews Coombs' Unfolding Theory along with Greenberg's observation relating preference probabilities and interstimulus midpoints. Subsequently, a stochastic folding model is presented which is a parameterization of Greenberg's scaling method. This model provides a least squares procedure for estimating the latent scale presumably generating the (2) pairwise preference proportions. Finally, a generalization of this folding model is given in the form of a response biased preference model requiring the observation of 2(2) = n(n - 1) ordered pairwise proportions. The scalling technique based upon each model is illustrated with an analysis of paired comparisons observed in an applied setting. As is clear from Fig. 1, the unfolding model rests upon the assumption that stimulus j is preferred to stimulus k if, and only if, j is closer (on the latent attribute) to the ideal point, or, equivalently, j is preferred to k if, and only if, the ideal point lies on i's side of the midpoint between these two stimuli. The conceptualization of the ideal point as a random variate (over individuals) leads to the relationship between this midpoint and the probability of j being preferred to k. Goodness-of-Fit. Once the LS estimates of the scale values have been obtained, w may be tested for goodness-of-fit to the data. The test othis model consists of a three-step procedure, which involves inspecting (a) the estimated scale values of the single stimuli, (b) the plot of the observed upon the predicted pairwise proportions, and (c) the degree of stochastic transitivity of triples of stimuli. The form of the folding model co resembles that of Thurstonian and signal detection models, which also postulate cutting points upon a decision axis, along with a probability distribution over this axis, to account for observable choice probabilities. The decision axis is interpreted here as an unfolded scale and each cutting point as an interstimulus midpoint upon this latent dimension. However, it should be emphasized that the substance of this folding model is anything but Thurstonian, since its decision axis is quite distinct from that of the Law of Comparative Judgment. When applied to pairwise preferences, the Thurstonian model is confined to the utility continuum; while the decision axis for the folding model is a latent attribute presumably generating this utility scale. Indeed the great value of Unfolding Theory lies in the dual construction of a preference-mediating scale cA and a preference scale IL from the same data  356 BECHTEL  usually summarized by N, alone. This dual construction is more incisive than any utility scaling model, such as Case V of the Law of Comparative Judgment, which quantifies the preference continuum without, in addition, addressing the latent attribute underlying preferential choice.  Assumption 1 of co (and Q)  The folded and unfolded numerical scales are constructed by the present procedures at the expense of rather strong assumptions. The normality requirement appears less critical than the first assumption of co (and 0), namely that of fixed, serially ordered stimulus scale values upon the latent continuum. These two aspects of Assumption 1 require further elaboration.  Fixed A; . Instead of postulating fixed stimulus scale values, Coombs (1958, 1964) has represented the n stimuli by n nondegenerate distributions upon the latent attribute A. The distribution g(x) of the ideal has usually been left unspecified, but in their Unilateral Law of Comparative Judgment, Coombs, Greenberg, and Zinnes (1961; cf. Coombs, 1964, Ch. 23) have employed Case V assumptions for the distributions of Al ,..., A, . In practice this random variation may be over individuals or over occasions within a single individual. Typical Coombsian analyses, as well as those given in the present paper, are applicable to both group and intra-individual data, although samples are more frequently drawn from populations of individuals than from populations of occasions. In either case, the random variation of A; would seem to be small relative to that of the ideal x. Certainly in group scaling situations individual ideals will range across the entire A scale, whereas only slight variation will exist among individual perceptions of stimulus j's location upon A; i.e., individual differences in motivation will greatly exceed those in perception. Hence, the degeneracy of the distribution of A; (j = 1,..., n), which is postulated by Assumption 1, appears to be an adequate approximation for unidimensionally mediated preferences.  Serially Ordered A; . In many applications Assumption 1 will be satisfied by a unique permutation of the stimuli which is known a priori. For example, in any unidimensional perception experiment this serial order will be given by the single physical scale involved. There will also exist a unique permutation of stimuli upon certain cognitive dimensions, such as those generating the illustrative data reported here. However, perhaps the most important applications of the present scaling techniques lie in those domains for which Assumption 1 cannot be met a priori. In these applications it is, therefore, necessary to search for that permutation which will provide the best fit in terms of the criteria already given. This search, of course, necessitates a procedure in which all permutations must be tested sequentially. If a particular ordering of the stimuli yields identically ordered LS estimates of the stimulus scale values, then a pairwise criterion of fit may be invoked to further test m or S2  SCALING FROM PREFERENTIAL PAIRED COMPARISONS 357  under this permutation. In the case of the folding model co, the satisfaction of this second criterion would point to the final, most stringent test involving the stochastic transitivity of triples. Thus, a given permutation is vulnerable to rejection at any stage of this testing process, which can be applied to all orderings of the stimuli. When the permutations have been exhausted, either the best fitting one is selected, or if none has an acceptable fit, the entire set is rejected.  Unlike the applications in the present paper, the use of this sequential procedure makes possible the discovery of preference-mediating attributes. This is at the expense, of course, of more extensive data analyses than are required for scaling and model testing under a single, known permutation. Nevertheless, whether the permutation imposed by Assumption 1 is known in advance or is obtained from the data, these parametric preference models provide estimation and goodness-of-fit procedures which are easily applied to large samples of individuals, occasions, or stimuli.
