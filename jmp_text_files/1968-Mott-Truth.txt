C. 20017  ABSTRACT  A logical model of concept attainment that generates all the simplest concepts satisfying arbitrary sets of positive and negative instances is described. The model can determine either completely or incompletely specified conjunctive or disjunctive concepts for problems with all-positive, all-negative, or mixed positive and negative instantiation. The model is based on a generalization of the logical problem of simplifying truth functions that consists of determining classes of simplest normal formulas equivalent to an incompletely specified truth function. Empirical results with simple problems showed consistent attainment of concepts to about the same extent regardless of the type of instantiation or number of alternative correct concepts. It is also pointed out that some previously suggested empirical "strategies" are useful only in restricted experimental situations. The chief limitation of the present model is its current restriction to instances with two-valued dimensions.  This paper describes a logical model, based on the logic of truth functions, that can be applied to the design and analysis of concept-attainment experiments. Conceptattainment experiments are illustrated by Smoke- Hovland-type problems; subjects, faced with positive and negative instances, are required to discover the concept exemplified by the instances. In terms of the distinction drawn in a recent summarizing article by Haygood and Bourne (1965) concept-attainment problems comparing different concepts come under the general heading of rule learning rather than the heading of concept identification, which is a term denoting a simpler type of problem. Examples of rule learning are the experiments described in Bruner, Goodnow, and Austin (1956). In designing experiments to study concept attainment of the rule-learning type three different approaches have been prominent. First, Hovland (1952) in his "communication analysis" model calculated the minimum number of positive instances and the approximate minimum number of negative instances necessary to unambiguously instantiate a concept, and these calculations were used to set up concept-attainment problems (Hovland and Weiss, 1953). Second, computer simulation models have been described in which positive and negative instances are dealt with in terms of an information-processing model in order to arrive at the correct concept (Hovland and Hunt, 1960; Hunt, 1962, Chap. 8). A third approach has been to take logical operators as a basis for concepts and compare relative difficulty in attaining the concepts so formed. This third approach is represented by Haygood and Bourne (1965) and Neisser and Weene (1962) who distinguish three different levels of concept complexity depending on the number of elements and operators necessary to describe a particular concept. These three approaches have been little concerned with the logical structure of problems since the most complex concepts that have been studied are at the level of exclusive disjunction.  Our approach to concept-attainment experiments emphasizes logical structure., A logical model is presented that can determine from any conceivable combination of positive and negative instances what concept or concepts are instantiated. Nlore specifically, algorithmic procedures are described so that complete solutions in terms of the operators for conjunction, disjunction, and negation can be obtained on the basis of the instances alone. The chief limitation of the model presented in this paper is that only two-valued dimensions2 can be handled; however, limitation to two-valued dimensions is not inherent in the formal model.  In comparison with other approaches the logical model can dispense with a considerable number of restrictions and limitations on the scope of concept-attainment problems. Previous work narrowed a subject's search for solutions to problems in several ways. The Hovland (1952) model was concerned only with conjunctive concepts and the subject was told in advance how many dimensions were relevant. Bruner et al. (1956) also completely specified beforehand the type of conjunctive or disjunctive solution required by their problems and always administered problems in which only one concept was to be attained. The Hovland and Hunt (1960) computersimulation model can handle only simple conjunctive concepts, but Hunt's (1962) "information-processing model" could also deal with two-dimensional disjunctive concepts. Neisser and Weene (1962) told their subjects that not more than two dimensions were relevant. Even in their least restricted condition Haygood and Bourne (1965) told their subjects that two dimensions were relevant. Of course, the use of a restriction-free model in designing conceptattainment problems would not bar giving subjects the hints that a particular experimental condition might require, as in experiments of the Haygood and Bourne type. Assessing the help given by such information would be of interest in its own right.  Our model can determine solutions for problems in which unlabeled instances occur in concept-attainment problems. This removes a limitation common to all previous concept-attainment approaches. Previous work has dealt only with problem models in which instances were required to be either positive or negative. However, consider a problem where a concept is exemplified in four two-valued dimensions consisting of two positive and three negative instances. What should the subject assume about the 11 unlabeled (truth-value unspecified) instances that can be formed using these dimensions? In the logical model the unlabeled instances are treated as "don't know" instances. Neither a complete mapping of all possible instances nor a convention that unlabeled instances are false is required. Thus, a more involved treatment of "incomplete instantiation" is possible in psychological experiments. Considering the way in which most concepts are attained in daily life, it would appear that incomplete instantiation is far commoner than the presentation of complete arrays of defined instances. Problems designed by using the logical model can therefore have wider relevance to general reasoning than has been customary. Logical models other than the one we propose can be used in working toward the solution of problems with incomplete, complete, all-positive, all-negative, and mixed instantiation, but solutions cannot be obtained by considering only the logical notation of concepts arbitrarily defined as "correct" (Haygood and Bourne, 1965; Neisser and Weene, 1962).  The present logical model is based on a generalization of a well-known problem in the truth-functional calculus. This is the so-called normal form problem or the problem of simplifying truth functions, studied recently by Quine (1952, 1955) and others (Chang and Mott, 1963; Gazale, 1957; Mott, 1960, 1961a, 1961b; Nelson, 1955; Petrick, 1959; Roth, 1958). The generalization in question consists of determining classes of simplest normal formulas equivalent to an incompletely specified truth function. A description of the model and a brief expository account of how the model  LOGIC IN CONCEPT ATTAINMENT 473  derives from the logic of truth functions is presented followed by a section in which suggested computational procedures for applying the model are exhibited.  The paper concludes with several illustrative examples and a discussion of some uses for the model. Empirical data are given with the simpler examples. Results point to the rather surprising conclusion that for relatively easy problems the complexity of the concept is the major determinant of concept difficulty irrespective of the type of instantiation or number of alternative correct concepts a problem possesses.  THE MODEL  As previously stated, the model provides the investigator with a logical analysis of problems involving 2-valued m-dimensional concepts. From the standpoint of the concepts to be attained only conjunctive and disjunctive types may be considered. If the subject is instructed to find the simplest concepts (conjunctive or disjunctive), it is not necessary for him to be told in advance the number of dimensions required to define the concept. The present model also imposes an additional restriction with respect to the "logical order" of concepts, in that the model is capable of providing an analysis only of experiments involving concepts of order two or less. (A classification of "logical order" will be given next.) With the exception of the listed restrictions the model is intended to provide the investigator with all the knowledge necessary to analyze from a logical standpoint the relationships existing among the positive anti negative instances and the concept to be attained.  In order to introduce the model we begin with the definition of concept which we define recursively in the following manner.  DEFINITION. A letter x, or its negation x, is called a dimension provided that when x = a then x = b and when x = b then x = a. a and b are said to be the values of the dimension x, or dimension-values. A 0-order concept is any dimension-value standing alone. A first-order disjunctive (conjunctive) concept is a fundamental sum (product) of one or more 0-order concepts. A second-order disjunctive (conjunctive) concept is a sum (product) of first-order conjunctive (disjunctive) concepts. (Note that ab is to be interpreted as "a and b" and a + b is to be interpreted as "a or b".)  Let (a, b) be the values odimension x, (c, d) the values of y, and (e, f) the values of z. Then c standing alone is an example of a 0-order concept, say, large object; ad is a first-order conjunctive concept, say, black small object; b -{- e is a first-order disjunctive concept, say, red object or circle; (ac + e) a second-order disjunctive concept, say, black large object or circle; and (b + e)c a second order conjunctive concept, say, a red or circular and large object. For purposes of exposition it will sometimes be convenient to characterize a concept in terms of dimensions and their negations rather than values of dimensions. Hence the above examples of concepts  474 MOTT, JR. AND ROSS  may also be characterized respectively in the following way: y, xy, x + z, xy + z, and (x + z) y.  Let us consider what an optimal, rational strategy might be for a subject confronted with the problem of forming a concept of fewest dimensions and least order which is consistent with all the information contained in a series of positive and negative instances. A straightforward strategy to follow in solving the problem would be, first, to form a list of concepts which satisfy all of the positive instances. Every concept included in this list, then, would be such that all of the positive instances are instances of the concept. A similar list would be formed for the set of negative instances. That is, included in a second list would be every concept that satisfies any of the negative instances. Since the second list contains only concepts such that the negative instances are instances of them, it follows that the concepts of the second list are all examples of what the concept being sought is not. Similarly, the concepts of the first list are all examples of what the concept being sought could be. Hence, deleting from the first list any concept which also appears on the second and then selecting a concept from the remainder of the first list having fewest dimensions and least order would yield the correct result. The correct result is a concept of fewest dimensions and least order which simultaneously satisfies all of the positive instances of the given series and none of the negative instances.  As an illustration of this rational strategy, consider a concept based on stimuli described by three dimensions with two values for each. The subject is told that the dimensions are size (large, small), color (black, red), and form (circle, triangle), and he is also told that "small red circle" is a positive exemplar of the concept and "small red triangle" and "large red circle" are negative exemplars. Based on the information contained in the positive instance, he first forms the list of elements that are possible examples of what the concept is. They are:  small objects, red objects, circles, small red objects, small circles, red circles, and small red circles.  Next, he culls from the negative instances a list of elements that are examples of what the concept is not. They are:  small objects, large objects, red objects, circles, triangles, small red objects, large red objects, small triangles, large circles, red triangles, red circles, small red triangles, and large red circles.  Deleting from the first list elements which appear also as items of the second list, leaves him with only "small circles" and "small red circles" to consider. Hence, assuming that "small red circle" is a positive instance and "small red triangle" and "large red circle" are negative instances, he discovers that the concept of fewest dimensions and least order satisfying the given description is the two dimensional first-order conjunctive concept "small circles."  An analysis of the logic underlying the above strategy discloses a close parallel with a method of solution in the logic of truth functions for finding simplest conjunctive  LOGIC IN CONCEPT ATTAINMENT 475  and disjunctive forms of incomplete truth functions. A truth function F containing n distinct letters is complete when the value of F is defined to be 0 or I for all 2n possible assignments of truth values (0,1) to the n letters. A truth function is incomplete when the value of F is undefined (is neither 0 nor 1) for at least one assignment of values to the letters of F. In concept-attainment experiments, as in real life, an exhaustive accounting of all positive and negative instances is not generally possible. For this reason, specification of a concept by means of incomplete positive and negative instantiation corresponds in a logical sense to specifying an incomplete truth function F, as shown by the following considerations: (i) Let the positive instances of a concept correspond to those assignments of values to the letters of F for which the value of F is defined to be 1. (ii) The negative instances of the concept correspond to those assignments of values to the letters of F for which the value of F is defined to be 0. (iii) The instances which are unspecified correspond to those assignments of values to the letters of F for which the value of F is undefined. It should be evident that on this basis the problem of finding conjunctive and disjunctive concepts of least order which satisfy given positive and negative instances is reducible in a logical sense to the problem of finding simplest conjunctive and disjunctive forms of an incomplete truth function. Consequently, the logical machinery that is available for solving the latter problem (see Mott 1961a, 1961b) provides a framework in terms of which an effective method of solution for the former may be posited.  In essence, the problem of finding simplest conjunctive and disjunctive forms of an incomplete truth function is a generalization of the problem of finding simplest forms of a complete truth function. The latter divides into two parts:  (i) Determination of all shortest sums or products of letters and negations of letters implying the function that imply no shorter sum or product of literals also implying it (technically called the prime implicants of the function).  (ii) Selection of smallest subsets of such formulas whose sums and products are equivalent to the function.  The problem of finding conjunctive and disjunctive concepts of least order satisfying a given set F of positive and negative instances may correspondingly be divided into two parts:  (i) Determination of what we here call the putative concepts of F.  (ii) Selection of smallest subsets of these concepts whose conjunctions and/or disjunctions satisfy the given instantiation F.  Determination of Putative Concepts  What we call a putative concept is best explained informally by reference to the optimal rational strategy discussed above. In that strategy recall that two lists of possible concepts were drawn: one list which enumerated all examples of what the  476 MOTT, JR. Now, by the putative concepts of a given set F of positive and negative instances we mean simply those products and sums of dimension values which go to make up items that would be found on the first list after removal of all items of the second list. In other words, the putative concepts of F are simply firstorder concepts such that (i) some or all (or possibly none3) of the given positive instances are instances of the concepts in question and (ii) all of the negative instances are instances of what the concepts are not. When selection is made of smallest subsets of putative concepts whose sums and/or products satisfy all positive instances and none of the negative, the result will indeed be the concept(s) of lowest order satisfying the given instantiation F. The Selection Process  The second part of the model requires selecting from the list of putative concepts those which completely satisfy the given instantiation. In the event that no one concept qualifies for selection, as was seen to be the case in the example above, it becomes necessary to seek combinations of concepts which do qualify. The task involved in the second part of the model then is to provide an effective way of selecting minimal subsets of putative concepts whose sums and/or products satisfy the instantiation.  Several logical schemes are available for accomplishing this task. One that appears especially suited to the present context involves construction of a so-called table of putative concepts. A table of putative concepts is a matrix for which each row refers to one of the putative concepts of F and each column to one of the given positive instances included in F. When a positive instance P; is an instance of a putative concept Ci , the corresponding entry aia is marked by an x; otherwise, the entry is left blank. The logical statement that A covers B has the meaning that when B is 1 then A is 1. Consequently, a putative concept always covers those positive instances (if any) which are indeed instances of it, and conversely, a given positive instance is always covered by those putative concepts of which it is an instance. In the context of this paper, to say that A covers B means that B subsumes A (see footnote 4).  A characteristic property of putative concept tables is that every column always contains at least one x, although not all rows necessarily contain an x. This accounts for the interest in putative concept tables. It can be readily demonstrated that the desired concepts of least order satisfying a given instantiation always correspond to subsets of putative concepts which meet the following conditions: (i) For each subset every positive instance is an instance of at least one putative concept of the subset; (ii) If a putative concept of the subset is excluded from the subset, then at least one positive instance fails to be an instance of any of the remaining putative concepts of the subset. Remarks on Duality  Implicit in the discussion has been the claim that the model is capable of yielding simplest conjunctive and disjunctive concepts from given instantiations. However, the procedures that have been introduced so far provide ways of finding only simplest first-order conjunctive and second-order disjunctive concepts. Are additional procedures then required to handle the case where the simplest concept being sought is either a first-order disjunctive or second-order conjunctive concept ? Fortunately, no. Due to the existence of a duality property in the logic of truth functions, the same procedures are applicable for these cases, through a set of transformations which we fully describe below. Essentially, the idea is to operate on the negation of F, applying the same procedures as before, and then convert the resulting concepts into their respective duals.  480 MOTT, JR. AND ROSS  In detail, the procedure for finding simplest first-order disjunctive and/or secondorder conjunctive concepts of a given instantiation F is as follows: (i) F, the negation of F, is first obtained by (a) assigning the value 0 to F for all assignments of values to its letters which correspond to the positive instances, (b) assigning the value 1 to F for all assignments of values to its letters which correspond to the negative instances, and (c) leaving as undefined the value of F for those assignments of values to its letters which correspond to unspecified instances. (ii) F-positive is next obtained by assigning the value 1 to all undefined values of F, and then converted into the sum of its putative concepts. (iii) A table of putative concepts for F is constructed, and all simplest first-order conjunctive and second-order disjunctive concepts of F are obtained. (iv) Finally, each simplest concept obtained in (iii) is transformed into its dual by replacing every letter in the concept by its negation, every conjunction sign by a disjunction sign, and vice versa. The results are all simplest first-order disjunctive and/or second-order conjunctive concepts of the given instantiation F.  When the above procedure, applied to F, is performed in conjunction with a similar procedure (with the omission of Step iv), applied to F, the result is all simplest conjunctive and disjunctive concepts satisfying the given instantiation. This conclusion held regardless of the number of alternative correct concepts in the same problem and regardless of whether any particular concept was instantiated by all-positive, all-negative, or mixed instantiation. In fact, the 0-order concepts were achieved with about the same degree of success from one problem to another with consistently higher percentages shown for the size dimension. Thus "large object" was attained by 60.3 % in Example 1 and 56.2 % in Example 2, while "small object" also was attained by 56.2% in Example 3. The other 0-order concepts were all attained by between 40 % and 50 % of the subjects.  The result that concept complexity, as represented by the first-order vs 0-order contrast, nullified the effectiveness of other variables was unexpected. We hasten to add, of course, that this result should not be extended to include more difficult problems. Nevertheless, we had presumed that even with simple problems all-negative instantiation would give subjects more difficulty than all-positive instantiation. This is despite the fact that the logical model suggests that all-positive and all-negative instantiation are equivalent in difficulty. Present results suggest that the commonly found difficulty with negative exemplars may largely occur when they have to be retained in memory and subsequently transformed, as in problems with successive instantiation.  An interesting point concerning a possible semantic bias can also be made. The first-order concepts were attained by from 6.8 to 16.4% of the subjects except for two first-order concepts in Example 3; scarcely anyone attained the concepts "large red object" and "small black object." As expressed in language, the word "object" must be supplied by the subject, apparently making such concepts as difficult as second-order concepts. This is not the case with a first-order concept such as, say, "black triangle." It could be claimed that this notion is inconsistent since it does not hold for 0-order concepts, e.g., "red object" is empirically no more difficult than "circle." But note that "red" can stand alone in English (subjects who wrote "red" alone were in fact scored as correct) whereas "large red" cannot. Evidence as to whether this difficulty is simply a semantic anomaly might be obtained by presenting problems nonverbally using drawings for instances and concepts. This has not yet been done. DISCUSSION  The major goal of this paper has been to show that a model, derived from a generalization of the normal form problem in logic, is relevant to much of the area of concept attainment or concept formation as it has traditionally been conceived in experimental psychology. The present model has the advantage that groupings of concept-attainment problems are possible in numerous ways. These include number of concepts, redundancy, type of instantiation, and the complexity of the concepts to be attained; while a Hovland-type model offers only grouping by the number of instances necessary for solution. Present results with simple problems found only complexity of the concept to-be-attained to be important. This finding has a definite claim to generality, since type of instantiation and number of correct concepts per problem were irrelevant. Additionally this finding illustrates that, regardless of empirical results, it is advantageous to specify the formal characteristics of problems.  Formal characteristics other than concept complexity may be found to be important in more complicated problems. The families of problems with equivalent formal attributes that can be generated by the model might be useful for test-retest measures, particularly in learning and transfer-of-training studies. Computer programming of the model is not essential for use of the model, but preliminary work has shown that it can be coded in either machine language or computer interpretive languages. Since a common logical framework can be used in designing empirical experiments and writing computer programs, no translation problem arises in modifying a program on the basis of subjects' results. This can be a decided advantage in attempting a computer-program simulation of concept attainment. The chief limitation of the model, that it is at present limited to two-valued dimensions, is not a severe restriction in terms of current practice and the degree of problem difficulty that can be dealt with by the model (see Examples 4 and 5). An example of a recent large-scale study with only two-valued dimensions is that of Glanzer, Huttenlocher, and Clark (1963).  In the study of Bruner et al. (1956) considerable attention was devoted to the distinction between scanning and focusing as strategies in concept attainment. The argument was made that focusing strategies were, in practice, generally more efficient than scanning strategies. A peculiarity of the Bruner et al. treatment was that their principal mode of characterizing problems was patterned after the Hovland (1952) model. The Hovland "communication analysis" model, postulating successive elimination of hypotheses, classified problems by the minimum number of successive positive or negative instances required to define a concept. Despite the emphasis Bruner et al. placed on focusing strategies, models of the Hovland type are primarily models of a scanning strategy.  LOGIC IN CONCEPT ATTAINMENT  493  By contrast, the present logical model includes a rational step-by-step representation of one kind of "focusing" procedure,' in that the logical solution proceeds by way of operating on dimensions which have one value for a selected positive instance and the opposite value for another. Since our model is both complete in its formal description of solutions and incorporates a type of focusing strategy, it offers the hopeful possibility that contact between problem model and psychological process can be made. Nevertheless, no claim is intended that the subject will or should proceed by the steps the model outlines, and further work is needed to understand why, as borne out by the present results, 0-order concepts are considerably easier than first-order concepts.  At a minimum, however, the model shows that more than focusing is required to obtain correct concepts. This is because focusing occurs only in the first stage of the model, determination of putative concepts, but not in the model's second stage, the selection or "covering" process. Therefore, unless the experimenter curtails permissible solutions, some additional procedure is required on the part of the subject who follows a focusing strategy if the complete set of irredundant concepts is to be attained. The concepts that have been required from subjects in previous concept-attainment experiments have been so simplified by the experimental instructions that no secondstage procedure has been necessary. (Unbiased empirical investigation of this second stage in focusing would additionally require that, unlike the procedure used to obtain the data reported here, subjects not be informed of the number of correct solutions.)  Bruner et al. also considered the probability that subjects would adhere by chance to certain empirical strategies. These probabilities were vanishingly small. This result was used to bolster the argument that the "ideal strategy" typology was quite successful despite the rather large deviations found in subjects' adherence to them. The point is that Bruner et al.'s description made no real contact with a model of the structure of the problems presented to subjects. From the standpoint of problem structure all that could be said was that subjects performed much better than chance, but took many more than the minimal number of instances necessary to attain the required concepts.  The lack of a formal model of the problem especially undercuts much of the Bruner et al. description of strategies to be used with disjunctive concepts. When it is suggested for the attainment of disjunctive concepts that, "In the end what helps most of all is the good luck of negative instances-both informationally and psychologically-and what hinders most of all is a series of positive instances" (Bruner et al. 1956, p. 177), this conclusion is rather special since it applies exclusively to problems where the subject is instructed to find only disjunctive concepts. With problems in which the subject does not know in advance whether he must find disjunctive or  ' The reader familiar with Bruner et al. (1956) will recognize that neither the Hovland model nor ours fits the paradigms for focusing and scanning strategies exactly, as each of these "ideal strategies" has several elaborated subvarieties.  494  MOTT, JR. A disjunctive solution reached solely by exposure to negative instances (an example is given by Bruner et al. 1956, p. 167) is feasible with restricted problems, but it is a logical impossibility for the more general case where the type of concept required is not specified in advance.  The Haygood and Bourne paper (1965), while strongly emphasizing a structural model of the task, also suggests the empirical strategy that more experienced subjects might operate with an "intuitive truth table." Again, solely on logical grounds, this notion runs into difficulties. Truth tables, intuitive or actual, could appropriately apply only to a small and highly restricted set of possible concept-attainment problems. This is because, as was suggested in the introduction and subsequently borne out by the examples, the use of ordinary truth tables is insufficient to obtain correct solutions unless (a) the subject is told the number of relevant dimensions, and (b) it is understood that no unlabeled instances can occur. These restrictions can be removed by using incomplete truth functions, but this produces a model of the structure of problems for the experimenter, not a strategy simple enough to be generally used by the subject. As the data showed, some of the concepts in unrestricted problems may, nevertheless, be attained quite easily, indicating that the strategies used by subjects definitely go beyond operations with ordinary truth tables.
