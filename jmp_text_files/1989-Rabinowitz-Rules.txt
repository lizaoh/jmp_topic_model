In the first experiment, the rules used by subjects were shown to change as a function of age and inter-response  interval. Background  Research relevant to people's intuitions about probability and chance can be found in at least three related areas.  Investigators in the first area have studied the understanding of causality and randomness in children of different ages.  Investigators in the second area, working mainly with adult subjects, have studied people's ability to comprehend and apply  various statistical concepts. The errors and biases that result from the use of heuristics have been emphasized in much of  this research. Finally, investigators in the third area, also working mainly with adults, have been concerned with sequential  dependencies that occur when subjects emit responses under minimal situational constraints. We briefly review research  Conceptions of randomness in children. Flavell (1963) summarized the Piaget and Inhelder (1975, English translation) treatise  on the development of probabilistic thinking. Briefly, children's conceptions of probability evolve through three stages. Up  to the age of 7 or 8, children have not mastered the basic conservation operations. According to Piaget and Inhelder, in order  to understand probabilistic concepts one must first learn to discriminate determined events from chance events. Because young  children do not have concrete operations, their comprehension of probability is minimal (see Hoemann & Ross (1982) for  supporting data and Brainerd (1981, 1983) for data that suggest that preschoolers have some understanding of probability).  From about 7 to 11 years of age children can use concrete operations, but they do not understand the concepts of combinations,  permutations, arrangements, and proportions (see Siegler (1981) for additional data on proportions). Therefore, their  understanding of probabilistic concepts is intuitive. Only in the period of formal operations, from about age 11, do children  develop a mathematical concept of probability.  One experiment reported by Piaget and Inhelder involved a spinner which could stop on any one of eight, equally likely colors.  Most children younger than 7 believed they could predict the color that would occur on the next spin. They based their  predictions on color preferences, on other egocentric beliefs, or on compensa-  RULES AND RANDOM GENERATION 229  tion (the idea that each color must have a turn). Children between 7 and 11 began to understand the random nature of the  outcomes and the fact that there would be a distribution of outcomes over a number of spins. Many children older than 11  appeared to understand that some outcomes might be partly random and partly determined and that the distribution of outcomes  might therefore be uneven. Piaget and Inhelder argued that (1) the preoperational child confuses chance events like the  spinner with determined events that can be controlled, (2) incorrect compensation (i.e., equalization of events over the short  run) is more likely to occur in younger subjects, and (3) correct compensation (i.e., equalization of events over the long  run) is more likely to occur in older subjects.  Statistical intuitions in adults. Studies of statistical intuitions (Kahneman & Tversky, 1982b) in adults have focused on  subjects' judgments about such statistical concepts as covariation (see Alloy & Tabachnik (1984) and Crocker (1981) for  extensive reviews of this literature), the conjunction rule (Morier & Borgida, 1984; Tversky & Kahneman, 1982, 1983),  regression to the mean (Kahneman & Tversky, 1973; Schaffner, 1985), sample bias (Hamill, Wilson, & Nisbett, 1980), the use of  base rates in making predictions (Bar-Hillel & Fischhoff, 1981), Bayesian inference processes (Fischhoff & Beyth-Marom, 1983),  and the law of large numbers (e.g., Bar-Hillel, 1980; Evans and Pollard, 1982; Kahneman and Tversky, 1972; Tversky & Kahneman,  1971). Studies in the latter area appear to be most directly relevant to judgments of randomness and chance. The results of  these studies indicate that people tend to underestimate the frequency and length of repetitions in random binomial series.  For example, Kahneman and Tversky (1972) found that subjects, in judging the likelihood of two series of births of boys (B)  and girls (G), thought that the series GBBGBG was more likely than the series BBBGGG. The authors argued that irregularity and  local representativeness are central to people's conceptions of randomness. People believe that a random sequence will be  irregular in both the order and distribution of outcomes and that even short samples of outcomes will possess all the  characteristics of the parent population.  A tendency to underestimate the length of repetitive series in random sequences may underlie many popular misconceptions about  probability. Feller (1968), in deriving probability equations for random-walk problems, referred repeatedly to the surprising  nature of the results. For example, the probability that there will be no return to the origin in the first 10 trials of a  random walk (approximately one quarter) is higher than most people would expect. A similar misconception may underlie the  familiar gambler's fallacy (cf. Ross & Levy, 1958). Pollatsek, Konold, Well, and Lima (1984) defined the gambler's fallacy as  "the belief that, in random sampling, the data that have already been sampled will influence the data that are yet to be  sampled" (p. 395). This definition seems to imply that people believe in an active balancing process but the authors found  little evidence for such a belief. Rather, the gambler's fallacy appeared to stem from the belief that the average of the data  yet to be sampled and the average of the aggregated data would both be the same as (i.e., representative of) the population  average.  230 RABINOWITZ ET AL.  An important consequence of misconceptions about randomness is that truly random sequences, because of their nonrandom  appearance, may be the basis for unwarranted beliefs while significant departures from randomness may go unnoticed. For  example, Gilovich, Vallone, and Tversky (1985) found that basketball players, coaches, and fans mistakenly believe that  players' successes and failures tend to go in "streaks" (i.e., that the probability of a successful shot is greater following  a hit than following a miss). Conversely, sequences with more alternations than would be expected by chance were viewed as  random by the majority of subjects.  Sequential dependencies. Conceptions of chance and departures from chance may underlie behavior when people emit sequential  responses under conditions of minimal constraints (Wagenaar, 1972). Rabinowitz (1970) reviewed the literature relevant to the  response-response sequential dependencies exhibited by humans in free association, learning, psychophysics, and  random-generation experiments involving more than two response alternatives. In these experiments, three types of sequential  dependencies characterized the protocols of most subjects. First, subjects rarely repeated responses on successive trials.  Second, subjects often responded in a series. On motor tasks, for example, they often gave successive responses to adjacent  loci; on verbal tasks, they frequently recited numbers in either a forward (e.g., 2, 3) or backward (e.g., 6, 5) sequence.  Third, in situations with k response alternatives, subjects tended to cycle, i.e., to make every possible response over k  successive trials. A cycle of responses is defined as an n-tuple of responses in which none of the symbols from the possible  response set is repeated. Although Rabinowitz (1970) was able to demonstrate formally that excessive cycles are not an  artifact of avoiding repetitions and/or series responding, he was unable to derive a set of equations which permitted the  independent estimation of a subject's tendency to cycle, avoid repetitions, and respond in a series. Thus, he failed to  eliminate the possibility that avoiding repetitions and/or series responding are artifacts of cycles. This deficiency is  remedied in the present paper.  The small number of studies in the sequential-dependency literature that deal with developmental changes partially support and  partially contradict the arguments of Piaget and Inhelder. On the one hand, consistent with their argument that younger  children should be more likely to use simple and predictable response patterns, children between 4 and 8 years old generate  more series responses than do older children and adults (Rabinowitz & Cantor, 1967; Rabinowitz, 1970). On the other hand, in  contrast to their position that younger children are more likely to engage in incorrect compensation, excessive use of cycles  seems to be characteristic of the multiple-choice responses of humans over 4 years of age (Rabinowitz, 1970).  The research cited above presents a relatively consistent picture of people's conceptions of randomness. The tendency of  subjects to avoid repetitions when generating sequential responses is consistent with the tendency for subjects to  underestimate the frequency and length of repetitions in random binomial series. Similarly, the fact that series responses are  less common in older children is  RULES AND RANDOM GENERATION 231  consistent with the irregularity that adults expect will characterize random sequences. Finally, the tendency to generate  responses in cycles seems consistent with the belief that small samples or short series will be representative of the larger  parent population.  Research Focus  Although serious inadequacies in people's random-generation behavior have been demonstrated it is not yet clear whether these  inadequacies reflect a remediable lack of skill (Neuringer, 1986), a fundamental limitation in memory capacity (Tune, 1964),  an inability to avoid being distracted by previous responses (Weiss, 1965), or an inability to recognize "true" randomness  (Wagenaar, 1970a). The present research addressed these issues in two experiments. In the first experiment, children and  adults were asked to generate random strings of numbers. In the second experiment, adults were asked either to generate random  strings of numbers or to free associate numbers to nonsense syllables. Half of the subjects in each instruction condition had  their prior responses available.  In his review of the random-generation literature, Wagenaar (1972) made three recommendations: that measures be developed to  assess higher-order nonrandomness in short sequences of responses, that theories of nonrandomness be stated mathematically,  and that decisive experiments be conducted to identify factors responsible for nonrandomness. The research we will describe  has been guided by these goals. Our emphasis throughout this paper will be on characterizing and accounting for the behavior  of individual subjects. Like most researchers in this area (e.g., Teraoka, 1963; Wagenaar, 1970b), we assume that people's  conceptions of randomness can be inferred from their performance on a random-generation task (but see Wiegersma (1982a, 1982b)  for an alternative view). To facilitate inferences about conceptions of randomness in individual subjects a mathematical model  of random-generation behavior was developed.  THE MATHEMATICAL MODEL  Bogartz (1969) and Rabinowitz and Jackson (1972) proposed rule-based theories to explain the response sequences generated by  preschool children in two-choice probability learning. The idea that response sequences that appear to be probabilistic might  be accounted for by a rule-based theory was used in the present paper. A mathematical model with four estimated parameters was  developed to describe the sequential dependencies in the data of individual subjects who were asked to generate random strings  using the numerals 1-6. In the model, it is assumed that the subject's conception of randomness is based on one or more of  four classes of sequential dependencies that he or she monitors while attempting to generate a random string of responses.  Three of these classes represent nonrandom sequential dependencies, which were selected on the basis of Rabinowitz'  232 RABINOWITZ ET AL.  (1970) review: repetition, series, and cycles. The fourth response class represents pseudorandom responses.'  The model is derived for finite response sets containing more than two elements. It is assumed that these elements form a  closed group (e.g., 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6) and that the subject treats the end points (e.g., 1 and 6) as adjacent elements.  Therefore, if a subject responded with the two end points on successive trials (1, 6 or 6, 1), he or she was considered to  have generated a series of two responses. For example, 6, 1 and 2, 3 are treated as forward series, while 1, 6 and 3, 2 are  treated as backward series. The assumption of adjacent end points was made after the response sequences generated by the first  graders were inspected. These sequences contained a large number of series responses which did not appear to be discontinuous  at the end points (i.e., following either a 1 or a 6; see Subject A in Fig. 1 for an illustrative protocol). As will be seen  below, the data are consistent with several statistics derived from the mathematical model which are closely related to the  adjacent end point assumption. The relevant statistics include all series statistics, cycles (particularly those dependent on  the cycle and avoid series rule), and the mean absolute distance between responses.  A subject may form one or more rules about each of the four classes of sequential dependencies. When the subject is required  to generate a random string, he or she is assumed to select rules from one or more of the four classes to form a working set,  with a maximum of one rule representing each class of sequential dependencies. Therefore, a subject's working set consists of  one to four rules. For example, the repetition class is assumed to contain repeat and avoid repetition rules. The subject's  working set might not contain either repetition-class rule, or could contain either the repeat rule or the avoid repetition  rule, but not both.  Each time the subject responds, it is assumed that he or she uses a single rule from the working set. The rules selected to  form the working set and the probability of sampling a particular rule from the working set are a function of the experimental  conditions and the subject's history.  There are four free parameters in the model. Each parameter represents the probability that the subject will select a  particular rule from the working set when attempting to generate a string of random responses with k response alternatives, k  > 2.2 Because we were interested in developing a model to describe the strings generated by subjects of different ages (and  presumably different knowledge systems), under a variety of experimental conditions, it was necessary to sample an assortment  of rules. It was expected that only a small subset of these rules would characterize the strings generated by the subjects at  a particular age. However, the rationale for the particular rules sampled in the cycle, series, and repetition classes, as  well as the rationale for including the random class, merits discussion. Repetition rules. Two repetition rules are represented by parameter c in the model. One rule dictates that the previous  response be repeated while the other rule dictates that the previous response not be repeated. In a k-response situation, a  subject using the "avoid repetition" rule would not repeat the prior response, but would choose randomly among the remaining k  - 1 response alternatives. Thus, use of the avoid repetition rule is associated with a high degree of uncertainty in a  sixresponse situation. Because none of the other rules in the model could account for the possibility that a subject might  repeat responses more often than would be expected by chance, the "repeat" rule was included in the repetition class. On the  basis of Rabinowitz' (1970) review of the sequential dependency literature, we did not expect that many subjects would use the  repeat rule.  Pseudorandom rule. A single rule, the "pseudorandom" rule, reflecting an understanding of randomness is represented by  parameter d in the model. Assuming a good fit between model and data, the magnitude of d is an estimate of the variability in  the data string that matches randomness. In this paper, sequential dependencies up to and including those of length 6 were  analyzed. The rule is labeled "pseudorandom" because it is possible that subjects might produce apparently random response  strings by using some complex, higher-order sequential rule (like those used to produce pseudorandom sequences with a  computer) or  RULES AND RANDOM GENERATION 237  by using a lower-order sequential rule that generated sequential dependencies not detected by the mathematical model. However,  both these possibilities seem remote. With four or more response alternatives available, it would be difficult for the subject  to monitor a complex rule, while the 15 sequential statistics calculated from the data should detect most sequential biases  involving chains of up to and including six responses. Furthermore, even if the subject were able to mimic randomness by using  higher-order rules, d might still be a useful estimate of the subject's understanding of randomness.  Proportion of Maximum Uncertainty  A metric is needed that describes the adequacy of the rule system used by each subject. The estimate of the parameter d is one  such metric in that it assesses the proportion of the series generated by a subject that matches a pseudorandom algorithm. An  example will illustrate, however, why d is of only limited usefulness. Suppose one subject (S,) used the series rule, "respond  in a single sequential order", on half the trials and the "pseudorandom" rule on the remaining trials (Parameter Set S, : a =  0, b =.5, c = 0, d=.5), while a second subject (SZ) used the "avoid repetition" rule on all trials (Parameter Set S,: a = b =  d = 0, c = 1). Using d as the metric would be misleading because, in a six-response situation, one's predictions of S,'s next  response would be correct on 7/12 of the trials, but predictions of SZ's next response would be correct on only 1/5 of the  trials. On the basis of d alone, one would conclude incorrectly that S,'s responses reflected greater randomness.  In order to understand how the predicted probabilities are calculated consider Parameter Set S, . This parameter set contains  two nonzero values. On a randomly determined half of the trials, the "single sequential order" rule will be selected and used  (parameter value for b =.5), while on the remaining trials the "pseudorandom" rule will be selected and used. Note that  parameter values are estimates of the proportion of trials on which a particular rule is assumed to be selected and used. If  one predicts that S, will use the "single sequential order" rule on all trials, one will predict S,'s responses correctly on  all trials in which the "single sequential order" rule is used and on 1/6 of the trials in which the "pseudorandom" rule is  used. In contrast, even though SZ never uses the "pseudorandom" rule and always uses the "avoid repetition" rule, the best one  can do is predict responses on 1/5 of the trials correctly.  Another statistic, the proportion of maximum uncertainty, was developed as an alternative to d. One way of thinking about  uncertainty is to consider the length of the n-tuple that one could predict knowing the rule or rules used by the subject.  Consistent use of the "avoid repetition" rule, for example, would yield n-tuples of any length that were poorly predicted. In  contrast, consistent use of the "respond in a single sequential order" rule would yield n-tuples of any length that were  perfectly predictable.  The proportion-of-maximum-uncertainty statistic was calculated by having the computer simulate each subject's behavior. After  generating five random responses,  238 RABINOWITZ ET AL.  the computer generated a string of 10,000 responses using each subject's best fitting parameter set to determine randomly the  rule selected and used for each response. Across 10,000 responses, the proportion of times a particular rule was sampled  closely approximated its associated parameter value. On each of the 10,000 responses, the computer calculated loge x, where x  was the number of alternatives sampled on each trial (e.g., one alternative if the computer selected the "respond in a single  sequential order" rule and six alternatives, the maximum possible, if the computer was using the "pseudorandom" rule). The  reason a simulation was necessary is that for some response rules, x changes as a function of the previous responses. For  example, following a sequence of five different responses a "cycle 6" rule yields only one alternative, whereas following a  repetition the "cycle 6" rule yields five alternatives. The log values were averaged across the 10,000 trials and then divided  by log, 6. This ratio will be referred to as the proportion of maximum uncertainty. The ratio is bounded by 0 and 1 and can be  interpreted as an estimate of how good a subject is at generating a random string. The measure also serves as a summary  statistic of the best fitting parameter values obtained for each subject. A value of 1 reflects perfect random generation. An  estimate of the standard deviation of this measure was obtained by doing 20 simulations, 10,000 trials per simulation, using  parameter values that represented a cross between a typical fifth grader and a typical adult subject (Parameter Set 20 in  Table 2 with the following estimated parameter values: cycle 5, a=.4; avoid series and cycle, b=.1; avoid repetition, c=.25;  pseudorandom, d=.25). The mean was .715 and the standard deviation was .003.  Fitting the Mathematical Model  Because there were 24 possible parameter sets determined by the cycle (3), series (4), repetition (2), and pseudorandom (1)  rules (see Table 2), a procedure had to be devised to select the best fitting parameter set. The general procedure was, first,  to estimate the optimal values of the parameters in each of the 24 sets independently; and, second, to compare the predictions  generated by each of these 24 sets of parameters to the actual data and choose the best fitting parameter set.  Estimating parameter values in each set. The values of the four parameters were estimated using a least-squares solution of  nine, linear "f" or fit equations (see Van deGeer, 1971, pp. 57-58). Whenever the fitting program encountered a parameter  value of less than zero, this parameter was set equal to zero and the remainder of the parameters were refitted. This was  necessary because the parameters represent probabilities of using the various generation rules. Because the computer program  involved the coding of many complex equations, a check of the accuracy of the program and the original algebra was conducted  prior to fitting the actual data. For this verification, another computer program was written to generate strings of digits  via each of the parameter sets in the model, using a pseudorandom number generator from the IBM Scientific Subroutine Package  (1969). Data sets were generated for each parameter set in which the parameters a,  RULES AND RANDOM GENERATION 239  b, c, and d where set equal to .25. These data sets were then fitted by the original program. The requirements were that (1)  the correct parameter set fit most accurately, (2) there be no bias in the fitted parameter values, and (3) the values derived  from the 16 check equations (see below) closely approximate the 16 statistics.  Choosing the best fitting parameter set. After each solution was obtained using the nine fit equations, the program estimated  the values of 16 check statistics, nine of which also were used in the fit routine. (The values derived from the check  equations were a function of only constants and the estimated parameter values.) Whenever the check equations were linear,  they were used in the fit routine. However, if a non-linear check equation was to be used in the fit routine, it was necessary  to substitute statistics for portions of the mathematical expression in order to put the equation into linear form.  The deviation of the actual value from the estimated value was calculated for each of the check statistics. The average  absolute deviation across the 16 check statistics was computed and used to select the best fitting parameter set.3 The program  was first used to fit the data for each subject; then the program was modified slightly to fit average statistics, group by  group. In addition to fitting the model, the frequency of the various response alternatives was counted and a chi-square value  calculated. This statistic provided an index of zero-order redundancy (i.e., whether or not the distribution of responses  generated by each subject was uniform) and the tendency to use a favorite number or numbers. The mathematical model is  inapplicable to the data of any subject whose response distribution is not uniform. This is because all equations were derived  assuming equiprobable responses. Without this assumption, the mathematics would have been intractable. Although this is a  strong assumption, it is unlikely to be violated by many of the subjects if local representativeness (Kahneman & Tversky,  1972) characterizes their attempts to generate random sequences.  Subjects were required to respond using the numerals 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6. The longest n-tuple (number of consecutive  responses) from which statistics were calculated for the fit equations was of length 4. Thus, the same fit equations could be  used for each of the cycle rules which involved n-tuples of lengths 4, 5, and 6. Furthermore, it was mathematically simple to  alter both the fit and check equations in relation to the two repetition rules. The major task, therefore, was to derive the  equations corresponding to each of the four series rules.  The same 16 check statistics were used for each of the parameter sets. EXPERIMENT 1  Experiment 1 was conducted in order to assess the range of applicability of the mathematical model and to explore the effects  of age and inter-response interval on random-generation behavior.  Age. Besides the Piaget and Inhelder (1975) treatise described earlier, Cohen and Hansel (1956) and Ross and Levy (1958) have  written extensively about the development of probabilistic concepts in children. However, to our knowledge there have been no  prior developmental studies of the ability to generate random numbers. Based on Rabinowitz' (1970) review of sequential  dependencies, two developmental predictions were of interest. First, younger children will be more likely to generate series  responses than will older children or adults. Second, extensive use of cycles will characterize subjects across the  developmental spectrum tested. The first prediction is consistent with the Piaget and Inhelder idea that young children use  simpler rules than older subjects. Piaget and Inhelder claimed that young children are more likely to engage in incorrect  compensation than are older children or adults; this claim is inconsistent with the second prediction.  Inter-response interval. Six experiments have been reported in which the effects of inter-response interval on random  generation were assessed. In two of these experiments (Baddeley, 1966, Experiment 1; Warren & Morin, 1965), randomness  increased with increasing inter-response intervals, in two experiments it decreased (Teraoka, 1963; Wagenaar, 1971), and in  the final two experiments randomness was unrelated to inter-response interval (Baddeley, 1966, Experiment 3; Wagenaar, 1970b).  On balance, it would appear that randomness is unrelated to inter-response interval. This conclusion can be questioned,  however, because the six experiments differed on a large number of methodological variables including number of subjects, the  statistical sophistication of subjects, the inter-response intervals, whether the task was self-paced or paced by the  experimenter, the number of response alternatives available, and the assessment of randomness. Any of these factors could  account for the different conclusions.  Rabinowitz (1970) noted that the proportion of series responses decreases and the proportion of repetitions increases with  increasing inter-response intervals. Although these conclusions were drawn from experiments in which subjects usually were  asked to free associate numbers to nonsense syllables, it might be expected, considering the generality of the sequential  dependencies across tasks, that these changes would be reflected in random-generation tasks. A decrease in series responses  and an increase in repetitions should both be associated with increased randomness but surprisingly, the random-generation  studies have not consistently found that randomness increases with increasing inter-response intervals. One possible  explanation is that the subjects substitute one algorithm or rule (e.g., "cycle and avoid series") for another (e.g., "avoid  repetition") with no resulting change in randomness. Therefore, the inter-response interval may have a marked effect on the  rules used to generate random strings, but relatively little effect on overall randomness. Discussion  First graders' misconceptions about randomness as reflected by the generation task are characterized by tendencies to cycle  and to use simple series rules. The latter tendency accounts for the lower proportions of maximum uncertainty in the strings  produced by first graders, compared to those in the strings produced by older subjects. Fifth graders' misconceptions are  characterized by tendencies to cycle and avoid repetitions, while college students tend to cycle. Because fifth graders avoid  repeating, college students produce better random series. However, the rules or algorithms used at each age level are fairly  adequate, as even the first graders' mean proportion of uncertainty exceeded .5.  The present findings suggest that, in their descriptions of the development of  480,'33,/3-3  258 RABINOWITZ ET AL.  probability concepts, Piaget and Infielder (1975) are correct in some details (complexity of rules) and wrong in others  (tendencies to compensate in the short run or cycle). Consistent with the developmental predictions based on Rabinowitz'  (1970) review, first graders generated more series responses than did fifth graders or college students and subjects at all  ages tended to cycle.  The effects of inter-response interval on the sequential dependencies in the random-generation task are consistent with those  reported in the literature (Rabinowitz, 1970). As the inter-response interval increased, repetition responses increased,  series responses decreased, and the maximum proportion of uncertainty remained the same.  The mathematical model is useful in explaining why inter-response interval was unrelated to randomness in the fifth-grade and  college groups. If the "repeat the previous response" rule and the "cycle 6" rule are eliminated from consideration,  consistent use of any of the series rules would produce response strings lower in uncertainty than any of the remaining rules.  The magnitude of parameter b, an estimate of the series rules, increased with increasing inter-response intervals in both the  fifth-grade and college groups. The increasing value of b balanced other parameter changes, yielding proportions of maximum  uncertainty that were independent of inter-response interval. The "cycle and avoid series" rule was the series rule that was  most likely to increase in usage with increasing inter-response intervals in the fifth-grade and college groups.  The proportion of first graders who violated the assumption that all the numbers would occur with equal frequency was  substantial at the 6- and 10-sec interresponse intervals (30 and 40%, respectively). The mathematical model cannot be applied  appropriately to the data of subjects who violate the equal-frequency assumption. Therefore, the model is of limited  usefulness in explaining why randomness was unrelated to inter-response interval for the first-grade population. Furthermore,  it appears that a substantial proportion of the changes in sequential dependencies that occurred as a function of  inter-response interval for the first graders can be attributed to the increasing proportion of subjects who violate the  equal-frequency rule.  As noted earlier, the changes in sequential dependencies that were obtained as a function of age and inter-response interval  are consistent with those that appear in the literature with other tasks. The parameter estimates obtained from the  mathematical model can be used to assess which of the sequential dependencies directly reflect rule usage and which are  by-products. For example, if a subject always used the "cycle 5" rule, then series responses would occur on 40% of the  2-tuples (compared with 33.3% expected by chance) and response repetitions would never occur (compared with 16.7% expected by  chance). All three sequential dependencies (avoidance of repetition, over-production of series, and over-production of cycles)  would appear in this subject's data but only the cycles would reflect rule usage. The avoidance of repetitions and  over-production of series would be accidental by-products of the cycle strategy.  With the exception of 5 of the first graders who violated the equal-frequency  RULES AND RANDOM GENERATION 259  assumption, all of the subjects had cycle-rule parameter estimates that were greater than or equal to .148. It would appear  that in the random-generation task, the cycle rule is characteristic of subjects within the age range sampled. The use of the  simple series rules is characteristic only of first graders. The parameter estimates of 22 of the 30 first graders reflected  adoption of one of the three simple series rules, b > .160. In contrast, most of the fifth graders and college students  adopted a cycleseries rule, "cycle and avoid series," that empirically at least, tended to compensate for the over-production  of series that would be associated with the other cycle rules. The exception to this generalization is the behavior of the  fifth graders in the 2-sec condition. Only 1 of these subjects used the "cycle and avoid series" rule. Series rules were  relatively unimportant for the children in this condition; the mean estimate of b was .088. The "avoid repetition" rule was  important to the fifth graders; 29 of the 30 children used this rule (c>.1) compared to 19 of 30 first graders and 17 of 30  college students. In summary, the cycle strategies are characteristic at all three age levels. First graders tend to use  simple series rules, while older subjects seem to avoid producing series. Fifth graders avoid repeating responses.  EXPERIMENT 2  Tune (1964) suggested that limitations in short-term memory restricted randomgeneration ability. If subjects could keep track  of all n-tuples of a certain length, then they could make sure that all n-tuples of this length occurred with equal frequency.  Weiss (1965) pointed out an obvious shortcoming of Tune's proposal. Consider the six-alternative situation used in the present  experiments. There are 36 possible 2-tuples and 216 possible 3-tuples; it is unlikely that a subject could keep track of even  the 2-tuples without a record of his prior responses. However, if the subject is permitted to record and read his prior  responses and his conception of randomness is as sophisticated as Tune suggests, then he should be able to generate good  approximations to a random series.  As an alternative to Tune's proposal, Weiss (1965) offered an attentiondistracting hypothesis. Weiss argued that in order to  generate a response at random, the subject must suppress his prior response and maintain a set for random generation. Although  Wagenaar (1972) suggested that it was impossible to reject Weiss' hypothesis, it appears that the suggestions made by Tune and  Weiss are testable. If monitoring and memory processes are important in random generation, then making the subject's prior  responses available should have an impact on performance. According to Tune, performance should improve when responses are  available but, according to Weiss, there should be a decrement in performance because it would be difficult to suppress prior  responses. Note that it is assumed that the subject maintains a "set to be random" whether or not prior responses are  available.  In the present model, we have assumed that a subject samples from up to four  260 RABINOWITZ ET AL.  rules and implements the selected rule on a particular trial. Within the context of the model, the presence or absence of  prior responses should be important only if the number of response alternatives exceeds the subject's digit span. For example,  the subject could not use the cycle k rule unless he could recall the prior k - 1 responses. As college students were required  to randomize six digits in Experiment 2, it was not expected that prior response availability would affect performance. This  was a particularly important test for the proposed model.  It also was of interest to determine whether or not the rules used by the subjects in attempting to generate random strings  can be used to describe the sequential dependencies that subjects generate in other multiple-choice tasks with minimal  situational constraints. Recall that in many multiple-choice tasks subjects tend to avoid repetitions, cycle, and respond in a  series. If, as we assume, people's conceptions of randomness can be inferred from their performance on a randomgeneration  task, then the mathematical model should either fail to fit or provide a different description of performance on other  multiple-choice tasks. To investigate this possibility, half the subjects in Experiment 2 were presented with a  freeassociation task.  Method  Subjects  The subjects were 60 female and 60 male college students enrolled in an introductory psychology course. The data of 30 of  these subjects were reported in Experiment 1.  Design and Procedure  In Experiment 2, instruction conditions (random-generation vs free-association), response availability (responses-unavailable  vs responses-available), inter-response interval (2, 6, and 10 sec) and sex were combined factorially with five subjects in  each of the 24 cells. The parameter sets were based upon two  cycle rules (cycle 4 and cycle 5), one series rules (cycle and avoid series), one repetition rule (avoid repetition), and the  pseudorandom rule (see Table 9).  Three analyses of variance were conducted. The dependent variables were the proportion of maximum uncertainty, the chi-square  statistic, and the average absolute deviation of the 16 check statistics (the group means for each of these dependent  variables appear in Table 8). The independent variables were response availability, inter-response interval, and sex. No  significant contrast effects involving response availability were obtained. Therefore, if the number of response alternatives  to be randomized is within the subject's digit span, deficiencies in random-generation ability do not appear to be related to  memory. This finding strongly supports our contention that subjects have definite ideas about randomness and these ideas can  be accounted for by a rule-based model.  GENERAL DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSIONS  In this research, the effects of age, inter-response interval, and response availability on random-generation behavior and two  measures of higher-order nonrandomness have been studied. The results are relevant to each of the research areas surveyed at  the outset of this paper. We conclude with a discussion of the main points of relevance and an assessment of the usefulness of  the mathematical model that has been developed.  Conceptions of Randomness in Children  Two findings point to the conclusion that conceptions of randomness improve with age. First, the variability of subjective  conceptions of randomness, based on the number of parameter sets needed to account for the data, decreased with age. Second,  the proportion of maximum uncertainty, a higher-order measure of nonrandomness, increased with age. These developmental  changes suggest that the number and complexity of the rules used change with age. Consistent with the sequential-dependency  literature and the Piaget and Inhelder (1975) analysis, the youngest children tested, first graders (7-year-olds), were more  likely to use simple  RULES AND RANDOM GENERATION 265  series rules in attempting to generate random strings than were fifth graders or adults. In turn, fifth graders were more  likely to avoid repeating their prior responses than were adults. Contrary to the Piaget and Inhelder analysis, but consistent  with the sequential-dependency literature, the major departure from randomness in subjects at all ages was the tendency to  cycle.  Although conceptions of randomness improved with age, even the 7-year-olds had a fairly sophisticated conception of  randomness; their mean proportion of maximum uncertainty was .564. Recall that Piaget and Inhelder (1975) argued that  7-year-old children would be entering the period of concrete operations and just beginning to develop an intuitive  understanding of probabilistic concepts. As noted earlier, Brainerd (1981, 1983) found evidence for elementary probabilistic  knowledge in 4- and 5-year-olds. More recently, Kuzmak and Gelman (1986) have presented evidence suggesting that children as  young as 4 have some understanding of random phenomena. These data in conjunction with our findings suggest that Piaget and  Inhelder underestimated the probabilistic knowledge of children in the 4- to 11-year-old age range. Based on our data, by age  7 children appear to have learned a considerable amount about randomness.  A developmental model of conceptions of randomness: An initial proposal. Although the conception of randomness improves with  age, a close scrutiny of the data reveals a variety of developmental trends. The variability of subjective conceptions of  randomness and the use of simple series rules decreased with age. The actual generation of cycles was fairly constant across  age groups. The fifth graders used the avoid repetition rule more often and the pseudorandom rule less often than did the  first graders or the college students. Finally, the proportion of maximum uncertainty increased with age. Thus, depending on  the variable analyzed, monotonic increasing, monotonic decreasing, flat, inverted U-shaped, and U-shaped developmental  This complex set of developmental changes merits further analysis. Strauss (1982) describes five types of developmental models  that have been proposed to handle U-shaped functions. Among these, the rule systems approach outlined by Richards and Siegler  (1982) is most compatible with the mathematical model presented in this paper and also has been used to explain a variety of  developmental curves. The Richards and Siegler model is adapted below in an attempt to account for developmental changes in  random-generation behavior.  The first dimension children attend to and form rules about is representativeness or cycles. For example, if a 36-month-old  child is asked to find a marble in a twochoice hide-and-seek task with 100% reinforcement, the child will continually repeat  choices. In contrast, a 48-month-old will alternate, while children above 54 months generate less predictable sequences  (Gratch, 1964; Jeffrey & Cohen, 1965). Interestingly, the age at which children begin to alternate is the youngest age at  which Kuzmak and Gelman (1986) found that children discriminate determined and random physical phenomena. It should be noted  that the age at which children use cycles depends on the information load in the task. For example, in a twochoice task in  which stimulus and response alternation was not confounded,  266 RABINOWITZ ET AL.  Rabinowitz and DeMyer (1971) found that 48-month-old children alternate stimuli and repeat responses. In a three-choice  probability learning task with partial reinforcement, Weir (1964) found that 3- and 5-year olds repeat responses, while 7- to  15-year-olds use cycles (left-middle-right or right-middle-left button sequences) in a rigid manner.  Beginning at about 5 years, children become sensitive to patterns in supposedly random sequences and begin to generate  two-choice sequences that are less stereotyped than repetition or alternation (Gratch, 1964). The data obtained in Experiment  1 suggest that in the six-choice random-generation tasks, the first-grade children attend to one type of simple pattern  (repetitions) and develop a rule to eliminate this pattern (the "avoid repetition" rule). Sensitivity to patterns continues to  consolidate and by fifth grade, children are more likely to use the "avoid repetition" rule and also implement a rule to  eliminate simple series patterns. The "cycle and avoid series" rule replaces the simple series rules in our six-choice  randomgeneration task. The "avoid repetition" rule is associated with the inverted U-shaped developmental curve noted above.  In attempting to eliminate a simple repetition pattern, almost all middle-age children and about half of the 7-year-olds make  an error (see Table 5) that is not characteristic of 3-year-olds in a two-choice situation (see Gratch, 1964). This type of  error is reminiscent of the over-generalization errors young children make with irregular verbs (e.g., goed instead of gone).  On the basis of the data collected in Experiment 1, older children eventually learn that random sequences do include some  repetitions. Therefore, the avoid repetition rule is eliminated. On the other hand, the cycle and avoid series rule developed  in middle childhood is maintained. We believe subjects continue to use this rule because it counteracts the over-production of  series responses associated with the other cycle rules.  The three-phase model outlined above explains the variety of developmental curves obtained in the data. Subjects older than 4  years attend to representativeness and use cycle and series rules to generate locally representative sequences. The percentage  of simple series rules decreases as children become more sophisticated at monitoring simple patterns. The inverted U-shaped  tendency to use the avoid repetition rule and the U-shaped tendency to use the pseudorandom rule reflect attention to simple  patterns followed by the elimination of the incorrect avoid repetition rule. The increase in the proportion of maximum  uncertainty and the decrease of between-subject variability in conceptions of randomness correlated with age reflect the  gradual elimination of rules associated with low uncertainty.  Statistical Intuitions in Adults  In Experiment 2, a number of hypotheses concerning sources of nonrandomness in strings generated by adult subjects were  investigated. The fact that availability of prior responses had little effect on the proportion-of-maximum-uncertainty measure  (see Budescu (1987) for comparable data in a two-choice random-generation task) suggests that neither the memory-capacity  (Tune, 1964) nor the attentiondistraction (Weiss, 1965) explanations can adequately account for the departures  RULES AND RANDOM GENERATION 26)7  from randomness. An explanation in terms of representativeness (Tversky & Kahneman, 1982) appears more promising. In  attempting to generate random numbers subjects may be primarily concerned that the sub-strings produced be similar in  essential characteristics to the population of all such numbers. If Tversky and Kahneman (1971) are correct about a "belief in  the law of small numbers," the concern about representativeness may be the same whether the sub-string the subject attends to  is relatively short because of memory limitations or potentially long because prior responses are available in written form.  "Local representativeness" is an accurate characterization of the sub-strings generated by children, as well as adults, in  both the experiments reported here.  Sequential Dependencies  The change of parameter values with inter-response interval was consistent with the sequential-dependency literature  (Rabinowitz, 1970) and with the idea that the adequacy of the random string generated is relatively unaffected by  inter-response interval. Series responses decreased and response repetitions increased with increasing inter-response  intervals. In general, repetitions occurred less frequently, while cycles and series responses occurred more frequently than  expected by chance, across age, inter-response intervals, and instruction conditions.  The importance of the subject's conception of randomness as a determinant of sequential dependencies was highlighted by the  relatively minor effect of response availability on the nature and adequacy of the strings generated. In contrast, response  availability had a greater effect on subjects who were instructed to free associate numbers to nonsense syllables.  Furthermore, there were a number of striking differences in the strings generated by free-association and random-generation  subjects. Free-association subjects were more likely to produce series responses and less likely to cycle than were  random-generation subjects. Many of the freeassociation subjects violated the assumption that each of the responses occurs  with equal frequency. Random-generation subjects were too successful at keeping response frequencies approximately equal,  presumably because of their excessive use of the cycle rules.  The Mathematical Model  Higher-order nonrandomness. The present research has taken an important step toward explicating the difficult notion of  higher-order nonrandomness (Wagenaar, 1972). The measures developed here may be usefully applied not just in future studies of  random generation but, with appropriate modification of the mathematical model, in any research in which subjects make  sequential responses under conditions of minimal constraints. In addition, the quantification of individual differences in the  understanding of randomness may prove helpful in developing educational programs aimed at countering many of the popular  statistical misconceptions referred to earlier in this paper. In statistics courses, for example, one of the most important  and sometimes difficult things the student must  268 RABINOWITZ ET AL.  learn is that data patterns that appear interesting and informative can often be produced by purely random factors. An  appreciation of the "patterns" to be expected in random sequences and the need for various control procedures in experimental  design (e.g., randomization by blocks) might be conveyed to students more effectively if their attention were first drawn to  their own biased conceptions of randomness (i.e., cycles or local representativeness).  Limitations of the model. A considerable amount of evidence has been obtained that is concordant with the proposed  mathematical model. The fit of the model to the data of individual subjects was excellent. In addition, the model was useful  in assessing which of the sequential dependencies were a direct product of the subject's behavior and which were artifacts of  the rules chosen. There do not appear to be any constraints in applying the model in situations involving from five to eight  alternatives. However, if there are fewer than five alternatives, the distinctiveness of the rule classes becomes blurred.  Consider the three-alternative situation. The "cycle 3" rule is logically equivalent to the "continue a series once initiated"  rule and the "avoid repetition" rule is equivalent to the "series dependent only on the previous response" rule. It is likely  that subjects, particularly relatively sophisticated ones, will avoid repeated use of a rule that generates an obvious  pattern. This constraint might force the subject to use a more complicated set of rules.  With nine or more alternatives, two different problems are introduced. The number of alternatives is large enough that  applying a cycle rule might tax the subject's memory. Furthermore, the subject would need to developed a sampling procedure  which assures that all elements are sampled with approximately equal frequency, presumably over the short run, and that there  is no obvious bias in the order in which the elements are sampled. For example, if the alphabet constituted the set of  elements, following the response a the subject should be just as likely to sample an element from the last third of the  alphabet as from either the first or middle third. The simultaneous attempt to apply a cycle and two sampling rules would  probably compete for the subject's attention, putting stress on the information processing abilities.  A number of questions concerning subjects' random-generation behavior remain to be answered. First, why do subjects sample  somewhat different rule sets as a function of inter-response interval? Although temporal stacking of rules (White, 1965) or  limitations of working memory (Baddeley, 1986) are appealing explanations, one would think that the information processing  demands in random generation would lead subjects to select the same rules within the span of interresponse intervals sampled  in this paper. Second, what criteria do the subjects adopt in deciding to switch from one rule to another? The probabilistic  sampling assumption is a workable one, but it leaves much to be desired. Finally, what criteria do the subjects employ in  using the "avoid repetition" and "pseudorandom" rules? Both these rules yield highly uncertain sequences and subjects must  develop some algorithm for applying them. In a sense we have returned to the original question. We now know the rules subjects  use in generating the "nonrandom"  RULES AND RANDOM GENERATION 269  portion of a string, but we still must learn how the subject generates the "truly random" portion.
