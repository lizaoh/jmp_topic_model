JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY: 7, 97-117 (1970)  An All-or-None Theory for Learning on Both the Paired-Associate and Concept Levels  WILLIAM H. BATCHELDER'  University of Illinois, Urbana, Illinois 61801  Received July 31, 1968  A theory is proposed for paired-associate learning tasks in which several items are interrelated to facilitate learning. The  theory is a synthesis of the all-or-none pairedassociate and concept models. It assumes that when an item is presented either  nothing is learned, or that item alone is learned, or the group of related items is learned. Thus the theory postulates both  instance learning and rule learning. From the general theory, a number of learning models are derived for various item  presentation schedules and levels of data analysis. In particular, the serial learning, study-test, anticipation, and  Bernoulli procedures are studied in conjunction with the individual item level and concept level error-success processes.  Only an unusual learning task can be considered to be composed of unrelated stimulus-response (S-R) pairs. This paper proposes  a theory for the learning of groups of related items where item relationships are designed to facilitate learning. Examples  would be a simple one-dimensional concept identification task or a paired-associate learning (P.A.L.) task where similar or  conceptually related stimuli are paired with the same response. The theory is called the all-or-none multi-level theory  because it embodies features of both the all-or-none paired-associate model (Bower, 1961) and the all-or-none concept model  (Restle, 1961; Bower and Trabasso, 1964). Thus the theory postulates that learning can take place both on the individual item  level and on the level of the group of related items.  Some preliminary remarks will help motivate the theory. Both the all-or-none P.A.L. and concept models share several  assumptions. In both cases, the models postulate that all learning is on a single level. In the case of the P.A.L. model,  individual items are learned independently of one another; and, in the case of the concept model, the entire list of related  items is learned together. In cases of extremely simple list learning, these models have proven quite adequate; however, when  lists are made longer, or  1 This work is based in part upon my Ph.D. dissertation at Stanford University (1966). One approach has been  to extend the models by adding more states and transition parameters (e.g., Atkinson and Crothers, 1964; Bower and Theios,  1964; Greeno, 1967). This approach has sometimes proven successful, notably in the development of P.A.L. memory models.  Another tack, the one taken here, is to explore the possibility that in complex lists a subject may learn on several different  levels in the same experiment. An example illustrating such learning comes from an experiment by Suppes and Ginsberg (1962) in  which five- and six-year-olds learned a six-stimulus, two-response P.A. list. Three stimuli were paired with the response "4"  and were each binary number equivalents of the number four with different Greek letters playing the role of 1 and 0. The other  three stimuli were corresponding binary number equivalents of five. Suppes and Ginsberg thought that learning might proceed by  the all-or-none model; however, they did not know whether the learning would be at the individual item level or at the  "property" level, i.e., single items or groups of three related items. They attempted to fit the all-or-none model to both  levels separately. The model fit the item level data better, although post-learning tests indicated that some property  learning had taken place. The theory proposed in this paper would be ideally suited for such a situation since it postulates  learning on both levels. The relative value of the parameters would yield a measure of how much of each kind of learning  occurred in the experiment.  Another illustration of the importance of multi-level learning comes from the second-language learning experiments of Crothers  and Suppes (1967). In several of these experiments subjects were required to learn the correct response to each item in a  large list. Linguistic interrelationships existed among the items at several levels in the same experiment. For example,  several items might share the same consonantvowel letters and a larger class would share the same consonant phoneme (Crothers  and Suppes, 1967, Ch. 2). In other experiments morphological interrelationships existed among items on several different  levels (Ch. 5). On occasion the authors attempted to apply the one-element or two-element models separately to each of several  levels with only minimal success. A model postulating that learning is possible on multiple levels seems ideally suited for  such experiments.  The theory presented in this paper is intended to apply only to the simplest experiments with facilitative stimulus  similarity. Several of the properties of the theory were presented in Batchelder (1966) as well as the direction that more  complicated multilevel theories might take. Before developing the theory formally, the basic ideas can be described  informally. Suppose, for example, that a subject is learning a list of S-R pairs including a pair of similar stimuli, S1 and  S,, assigned to the same response, R. The theory postulates two types of learning: instance learning and rule learning. Thus,  when S,-R is presented for study, the subject can learn this pair on an all-or-none basis (instance learning) or he can learn  to associate response R to both Sl and S2 on an all-or-none basis (rule learning). If an item that has previously been  instance learned  LEARNING THEORY 99  is presented, the theory postulates that rule learning is still possible with, however, a different probability than on a  trial when an unlearned item is presented.  When both instance learning and rule learning are possible, there is not a single "appropriate" level on which to analyze  data. For some purposes the error-success process on individual items might be interesting, for others the error-success  process on the group of related items might be selected, and a third possibility would be the trial-ordered correlations  between pairs of individual item protocols. The theory is formalized generally enough to permit application to any of these  three levels of data analysis. An additional feature of the theory is its ability to handle different item presentation  schedules. Following the axiomatization of the theory in the next section, particular models for various combinations of  presentation schedule and level of data analysis are presented for the two item system, S,-R S2 R. The paper concludes with a  discussion of the tractability of the theory for groups of more than two items.  Axioms FOR THE THEORY  The theory is axiomatized under the assumption that subjects are learning a group of M related stimulus-response pairs  (possibly embedded in a larger learning task). Further, it is assumed that the items are presented one at a time for a series  of anticipation trials, i.e., on any trial a stimulus appears for a subject controlled response, and the response is  immediately followed by feedback from the experimenter. No restrictions on the scheduling of these anticipation trials are  imposed.  Axiom 1. Each of the M items is represented as being in exactly one of three states on any trial: 1. an unlearned state U; 2.  an instance learned (paired-associate) state P; or 3. a rule learned (concept, common basis, etc.) state R. All items are  assumed to start the experiment in state U.  Axiom 2. A presented item can change its state, and the transition probabilities depend only on the current state of the  presented item. The transition matrix for a presented item is  state following presentation   R  P U  state of item R 1  0 0  on trial of P Ir c (1 - c) 0  presentation U   p (1 -r -p)  Axiom 3. If on any trial the presented item makes a transition to the rule state, R, the M - 1 unpresented items also make a  transition to R. Other than this possiblity, items not presented on a trial remain in their current states.  100 BATCHELDER  Axiom 4. If the presented item is in state R or P, a correct response is made with probability 1, and if the presented item is  in state U, a correct response is made with probability g.  The U state and P state of Axiom 1 are the familiar unlearned and learned states of the all-or-none P.A.L. model. Axioms 2 and  3, in addition to postulating the usual all-or-none process on U and P, superimpose a rule learning process governing all  related items. Thus, on any trial, there is a probability of learning all M items which depends on the current state of the  presented item.  In order to apply the theory to an experiment it is necessary to specify a schedule for the anticipation trials and a level of  data analysis. Once these boundary conditions are specified, a stochastic model for the appropriate error-success process is  deducible from the theory. Before presenting several such derivations, it is possible to develop some general properties of  the theory.
