Models of human pattern classification have been traditionally based on implicit pattern descriptions which involve lists of continuous attribute values or discrete features. Here we propose an alternative approach which makes explicit use of pattern structure in terms of components and their unary (part-specific) and binary (part-relational) properties. Such attributes ``evidence'' different classes of patterns and allow one to model processes of both perceptual learning and generalization to novel instances. An object in an evidence-based system is represented by a set of rules, where each rule provides a certain amount of class-specific evidence. The accumulated class evidence over all activated rules determines the classification probability. We have examined how well this concept reflects human performance by training observers to classify compound Gabor patterns and then testing them with segmented (grey-level-transformed) versions of the patterns in the original training set. If the observers were to construct rules to define each pattern class in terms of perceived parts and their relations, then it should be expected that classification performance would generalize to these new patterns. Results confirm this hypothesis and the specific feature extraction, learning, and rule generation model used to predict performance. ] 1997 Academic Press

question concerns the degree of abstraction at which properties are represented at different levels of perceptual analysis. Three general modes of analysis can be distinguished (Treisman, 1986): Objects may be seen to differ (1) in their values along continuous dimensions, (2) in their discrete features, or (3) in their component parts. 

Terms like dimensions and features have received various interpretations in cognitive science (Garner, 1978) and therefore require further qualification. A dimension can be defined as a continuous variable of mutually exclusive levels, out of which one will characterize any noncomposite stimulus to which the dimension is applied. Dimensions may refer to both local and global properties of the object, and they may form nested hierarchies. Accordingly, they have been classified into different taxonomies, such as analytic vs synthetic (Berglund, Berglund, 6 Lindvall, 1976; Julesz, 1971), unitary vs analyzable (Shepard, 1964), and integral vs separable (Garner, 1974; 1978). In some cases dimensions may only take discrete values, where this property may be intrinsic to the attribute (such as for the binary attribute open�closed) or due to specialization of the perceptual system (such as in color naming or speech perception). The term feature is often used for these categorical values, independent of the origin of the quantization. Both dimensions and features may be regarded as attributes of an object seen as an unstructured entity, or, in other words, an object may be characterized by a list of dimensional values or features (Gibson, 1969). 

Alternatively, objects and their backgrounds can be described as consisting of spatially localized parts or articulated components, as well as having characteristic properties. Division into parts differs from dimensional analysis in that the parts are neither mutually exclusive nor

obligatory characteristics of a stimulus. Given a complete object, any one of its parts can be removed without being replaced (although this may change the character of the object). The parts of an object are assembled in particular relationships. Note that while each part must have values on the dimensions that characterize the object, additional emergent properties of the whole may be created by the relationships between parts. Furthermore, analysis into parts is a recursive concept: Repeated division into components on varying spatial scales yields parts of parts, thus producing a hierarchical structural description of object knowledge�� a partonomy (Miller 6 Johnson-Laird, 1976; Tversky 6 Hemenway, 1984). 

Within the above scheme, perceptual classification has been predominantly modeled in terms of dimensions and feature lists. This involves a particular type of representation, where an object will be represented either as a point in a multidimensional space or as a set of feature values. Models of the former type have been devised as distance models where an attempt is made to relate, as in multidimensional scaling, interpoint distance to similarity and hence to classification probability. Examples are prototype models (e.g., Reed, 1972), exemplar-based models (e.g., Brooks, 1978; Estes, 1986; Medin 6 Schaffer, 1978; Nosofsky, 1986), and General Recognition Theory (Ashby, 1988; Ashby 6 Gott, 1988). Feature-based representations have been used in probabilistic models of feature summation, e.g., the Pandemonium system (Selfridge 6 Neisser, 1960), Bayesian approaches (e.g., Rentschler, Ju�ttner, 6 Caelli, 1994; Ju�ttner 6 Rentschler, 1996), and the fuzzy logic model of perception (Oden, 1979; Massaro 6 Oden, 1980; Cohen 6 Massaro, 1992; Crowther, Batchelder, 6 Hu, 1995). 

Structural models have been introduced in terms of pictorial grammars (Narasimhan, 1969) and applied to perceptual tasks such as the encoding of line patterns and em bedded figures (Reed, 1973). A more direct approach to human object recognition is Biederman's ``recognition by components'' (RBC) approach (Biederman, 1985; 1987), which suggests that an image is segmented at regions of sharp concavity into simple, convex volumetric units such as blocks, cones, wedges, and cylinders. These primitives are known as ``geons'' and provide an inventory of parts or properties used to represent and to identify objects. However, RBC has been applied to the issue of the 3D representation of specific objects, rather than to that of classification. Hence it is unclear how the processes of class formation, i.e. the abstraction from individual instances to class concepts during learning, and of generalization, i.e. the application of existing class concepts to novel signals, can be accommodated within this concept. 

The problem of constructing explicit structural representations which are capable of covering the aspects of both concept formation and generalization has a striking parallel 

in a related issue in the field of computer vision, namely, the problem of how to construct a model of a 3D object by using multiple 2D views. The analogy is established by interpreting a 3D model as a class concept for a particular object and the individual 2D views as class instances. Recent solutions to the latter problem have been focused on socalled evidence-based classification approaches. Here patterns are characterized in terms of their constituent parts, their unary (part) and binary (relational) attributes, and the way in which such attributes ``evidence'' different pattern classes (Jain 6 Hoffman, 1988; Caelli 6 Dreier, 1994). In the following, we will introduce this concept and its implementation as a model for human classification performance. We will illustrate its usefulness in analyzing the data of a classification and generalization experiment and finally relate evidence-based pattern classification to standard dimensional approaches in the cognitive classification literature. 

2. EVIDENCE-BASED PATTERN CLASSIFICATION 

The evidence-based systems (EBS) approach to pattern recognition is founded on the widely accepted notion in machine vision (e.g., Jain 6 Hoffman, 1988; Shapiro 6 Haralick, 1981) that complex patterns are best encoded in terms of parts and their relations. That is, rather than characterizing patterns exclusively by their global, morphological attributes, these descriptions involve enumeration of unary attributes (i.e., attributes extracted from the individual parts) and binary attributes (i.e., attributes derived from part relationships). In short, the characteristic feature of evidence-based systems (Caelli 6 Dreier, 1994) is that they use bounded regions of attribute spaces as probabilistic evidence rules for the occurrence of objects which have feature attributes represented within those regions. An object in an evidence-based system is represented in terms of a set of rules which are triggered by the occurrence of attributes. Once triggered, a rule provides a certain amount of evidence for each object class in the database, according to the likelihood of any given object of a class having feature attributes which fall within the bounded region defining that rule. The accumulated evidence over all triggered rules for a particular class yields a measure for the corresponding classification probability.

In addition, the algorithms for segmentation and clustering have to be specified. Note that rule generation and evidence estimation are involved only during the learning phase of the model where patterns with known class membership are needed to train the neural network via the backpropagation algorithm. Once the rule regions and the evidence weights have been established, the system can process novel patterns as well and act as a predictor for classification performance. This feature will be demonstrated in the next section. 

Evidence-based pattern classification differs from both traditional pattern classifiers based on discriminant analysis and purely connectionist network models in two major respects. First, rather than characterizing a given input image implicitly by a set of global, morphological attributes, the model provides an explicit, structural pattern description. This means that a pattern is decomposed into its constituent parts and characterized by both part-specific and part-relational attributes. Consequently, the representation of the pattern will involve a set of attribute vectors rather than a single vector characterizing its properties in a holistic sense. Attribute vectors belonging to patterns of different classes therefore cannot be expected to be separable by simple discriminant functions since two patterns may share similar parts or part relations. Rather, the distribution of attribute vectors is used to generate rules, the activation pattern of which is related to class evidence. 

Second, the accumulated attribute values are grouped into attribute space regions delimited by hyperrectangles which maximize evidence functions. Such regions are defined by conjunctions of feature bounds and may be efficiently computed and defined by Horn clauses, a restricted type of conjunctive proposition which is fundamental for logic programming (Lloyd, 1984). In contrast to implicit clustering methods, such rules allow for a symbolic description of images, which is the basis for further extensions toward scene analysis involving multiple objects. Hence there are defined representational constraints on the network used to determine the evidence weights, something which is not guaranteed in direct neural net implementations (see Lippmann, 1987). 

3. EVIDENCE-BASED MODELING OF BEHAVIORAL CLASSIFICATION DATA 

This EBS model for pattern learning and classification was used to analyze the data of an experiment where observers were first trained to classify a given set of training patterns (supervised learning). After successful completion (perfect classification) of this learning stage the subjects ability for generalization was evaluated by presenting them - different sets of test patterns, which were grey-level trans formed versions of the original learning set. The underlying idea was to investigate whether the observers would recognize different classes from image parts presented in isolation, and together, in ways consistent with what would be predicted from an analogously trained EBS system.

Procedure of supervised learning and classification. The experiment was divided into two sections, a learning stage 

and a test (or generalization) stage. The learning procedure consisted of a variable number of learning units employed for the initial learning set of 12 signals. Each learning unit ended with a test of how well the subject was able to classify the 12 patterns. Only one exposure per sample was used here. The learning procedure was repeated for each observer until he�she had reached an error-free classification (1000 correct). 

In the generalization stage subjects were tested with respect to their ability to correctly classify the grey-level transformed versions of the original learning signals. Each of the 15 generalization test sets was applied subsequently. For a given test set, each of its signals was displayed 40 times in random order for 200 ms and had to be classified by the observer. After each test set observers were retrained with the original learning patterns to criterion before switching to the next one. 

Subjects. Six paid observers participated in the experiments. The ages of the subjects ranged between 20 and 30 years; three were female and three were male. All had normal or corrected-to-normal visual acuity.

Evidence-based analysis of the experimental classification data. The data of the initial learning stage provided the basis of the parameter estimation of our four-stage evidencebased classification model (Fig. 1). As already outlined, the model was characterized by a set of system parameters [nP , nR , Au , Ab , wij] which refer to the average number of parts per image, the number of generated rules, the sets of unary and binary attributes, and the evidence weights, respectively. 

Concerning the segmentation of the input pattern we employed a region-analysis technique based on partitioning the image according to connected grey-level regions (Duda 6 Hart, 1973). It must here be remarked that this particular choice for the segmentation procedure was due to the fact that compound Gabor signals inherently do not allow for a 

unique identification of segmentation processes��various conceivable procedures, whether based on Laplacian operators, curvature, or simple image thresholding, would yield similar segmentation patterns (see Rentschler, Barth, Caelli, Zetzsche, 6 Ju�ttner, 1996). Hence our choice was pragmatic from a computational perspective, in order to provide a fast segmentation of the input image into a part structure which is compatible with introspective reports of the subjects.

Minimum entropy clustering aims at minimizing the joint occurrence of classes within clusters. It requires prior knowledge of the class membership since it involves the minimization of the entropy function H=&�i �j pij ln pij , where pij is the probability of class i occurring in cluster j and the probability is determined from the relative frequency of class samples within a given cluster solution. The algorithm endeavors to change the position and size of a fixed number of rectangles (clusters) to maximally separate the occurrences of class samples per cluster (for more details, see Caelli 6 Pennington, 1993). This involves an extensive relabeling of the samples in order to minimize the entropy function. Such combinatorial optimization problems can be solved, for instance, by simulated annealing methods (see Aarts 6 Korst, 1988). 

The rationale behind this two-step approach of parameter estimation was the following: The data of the initial learning stage, representing an observer-specific confusion error profile, were used to fix all those system variables, which could be assumed to be adjusted during the learning stage of the observers, i.e., the attributes and the evidence weights. The data from the generalization process could then be taken to track down those parameters on which generalization performance critically depended, namely the number of parts per image, the number of rules, and the clustering method applied for rule generation. Taken together, this two-stage estimation procedure provides a type of cross-validation method for parameter selection and model fitting. 

The basic idea of the modeling was to use the initial learning data of the experiment to estimate the parameters listed above and to test the system with the test signals from the generalization phase of the experiment. 2-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) was performed with blob type and clustering algorithm as factors. The ANOVA yielded significant main effects of blob type (F(14, 213)= 8.22, p<0.001) and clustering algorithm (F(2, 213)=62.89, p<0.001). There was no significant interaction between the two factors (F(28, 213)=0.48, p=0.98). 

For a further evaluation of the observed main effects, linear deviation contrasts were constructed. Significant contrast parameters would then indicate, depending on their sign, a relative decrease or increase in prediction performance for a particular factor level (i.e., a particular blob type or clustering method) with respect to the cell mean. This analysis yielded the following results: Concerning blob type, prediction errors were significantly lower for the blob transforms B1�10 (t(213)=&6.65, p<0.001) and B1�20 (t(213)=&6.23, p<0.001). Such a finding can be attributed to the fact that, according to Fig. 3B, these blob transformations produced signals that were still quite similar to the original learning patterns used to train both the human observers and the classification model. Hence, the superior prediction performance for these pattern sets is less surprising. 

With respect to clustering procedure, there was a significant decline in predictive power for the ME-algorithm (t(213)=11.21, t<0.001) whereas the contrast parameters of both the KM-algorithm and the LM-algorithm indicated a performance significantly above the cell mean (t(213)= &5.81, p<0.001). This is corroborated by the fact that, according to Table 2, rules generated by KM-or LMclustering led to individual RMS values in the order of 0.17. In contrast, ME-clustering turned out to perform much poorer, producing average prediction errors of about 0.3 for all subjects. The superiority of KM-and LM-clustering suggests that attribute contiguity seemed more important for rule generation than class contiguity. The relative equivalence of both clustering methods may be attributed to the fact that both methods inherently are distance related in the sense that they tend to produce locally compact clusters in attribute space. Since attribute vectors in the present case are relatively sparse (40�80 points per attribute space), the additional property of the KM-method of minimizing intraclass variance does not impose severe constraints on the segmentation process as would be the case for a densely filled attribute space. 

Furthermore, the number of parts that observers apply for their classification judgment appears to be relatively small, being in the order of 3-4 parts per image. Because of the specific type of stimuli used (compound Gabor gratings) and the grey-level based segmentation procedure this means that only the most salient parts of the signals (i.e., the brightest or darkest bars) were used by the observers. This result agrees with observers' reports according to which subjects indeed tend to rest their judgments on only the most distinct image components, which 

appears reasonable since the signals were displayed for only 200 ms. 

Table 2 also lists the sets of reconstructed unary and binary attributes estimated from the data of the learning stage. Here two points are important to note. First, the total number of attributes apparently employed by the observers is rather small, involving only two or three attributes for five out of six observers. Again this finding stands in agreement with reports subjects have made in the context of similar experiments involving the same type of stimuli. Second, and even more striking, there is a high degree of interindividual similarity between the particular attribute sets. All of the six reconstructed unary feature sets include the attribute position; four of them are additionally augmented with the attribute aspect ratio. The binary feature sets mainly consist of only one attribute, either contrast or distance. 

As mentioned earlier, the optimum attribute sets listed in Table 2 define the dimensions of the corresponding unary and binary attribute spaces. For observers JI and PE these attribute spaces are depicted in Figs. 6A and 6B, respectively. Note that in both cases the unary attribute space consists of the attributes position and aspect ratio. The binary attribute spaces in Figs. 6A and 6B have only one dimension, referring to the attributes distance (for observer JI) and contrast (for observer PE). They result, as already outlined, from the clustering of the feature�attribute vectors and the subsequent adjustment of hyper-rectangles, which, in the present case, are either two-dimensional (unary attribute space) or degenerated to one-dimensional intervals (binary attribute space). 4. DISCUSSION 

Evidence-based pattern classification is based on the notion of an explicit structural pattern description in terms of unary and binary predicates which activate evidence weighted classification rules. This approach to modeling claims that the ``internal'' representation of shape at one stage of processing may be captured by a symbolic form of evidence rules which contain generalization bounds. 

Given the relatively simple structure of compound Gabor patterns the question may arise as to whether implicit or image-based approaches could equally well account for the experimental data. The present experimental paradigm involves a form of conceptual extrapolation, namely from the class structure of the originally learned patterns to that of the transformed test patterns. Therefore, image-based models in general would have to rely 

implicitly on the degree of similarity as expressed by the pixelwise correlation between the original versions of the stimuli and their various grey-level transforms. As a consequence they would predict that the observed generalization performance correlates with pattern similarity. We tested this prediction in the following way: Out of the six observers, only two revealed a marginal performance�similarity correlation in the order of 0.3, whereas the correlations for the other four subjects were insignificant. In contrast, the correlations between observed and EBSpredicted classification performance typically yielded values above 0.8. The noncorrelation between pattern similarity and performance appears not to be a result of the particular representation since similar difficulties persist even for alternative representational concepts such as Laplacian pyramids or 2D curvature representations (Rentschler et al., 1996). 

In cognitive psychology, the difficulties of relating perceived similarity to physical stimulus properties have been pervasive and, as Shepard (1987) points out, eventually motivated the development of techniques such as multidimensional scaling (MDS). The underlying representational concept of MDS is a metric psychological space where patterns or objects are represented in a pointwise

manner such that similarity is monotonically related to distance. This representational assumption forms the common base for many current classification approaches such as prototype models (Reed, 1972), exemplar models (e.g., Medin 6 Chaffer, 1978; Nosofsky, 1986), and General Recognition Theory (Ashby, 1988), which differ mainly in issues of category representation and response generation. The intrinsic limitation of these approaches can be seen in that they are purely descriptive, i.e., they do not describe the processing of stimulus information since the mapping from physical space into psychological space remains algorithmically unspecified. As a consequence such models do not allow one, without further modeling assumptions, to predict generalization, i.e., classification behavior with respect to novel instances. 

In contrast, evidence-based approaches provide a linkage between physical and internal representations. Image segmentation, attribute extraction, and rule generation are defined entirely with respect to the physical image domain. The pattern of evidence regions depicted in Fig. 6 is completely determined by the specific choice of attributes and the specific choice of clustering algorithm. The cognitive, or observer-dependent, component of the model manifests in the set of evidence weights assigned to each region and in the way in which the corresponding rules are combined. 

As already noted evidence-based systems provide a definition of constrained generalization in that each rule is a bounded region in attribute spaces. This is also a distinctive feature between EBS and probabilistic or Bayesian classification approaches. For the former, attribute vectors lying outside all rule regions provide no evidence for either class alternative. In contrast, evidence in the Bayesian sense cannot be neutral: Lack of evidence for one class alternative automatically increases the evidence for the logical complement. 

Within the current EBS implemention, rule regions are delimited by sharp discontinuous boundaries in attribute space. This leads to strictly binary-valued rule activation vectors, depending on whether a given rule has been triggered by an input image or not. Alternatively, a ``fuzzy'' version of the model, in which rules are continuously activated, is conceivable. In analogy to current fuzzy-logic approaches to classification (see Oden, 1979; Massaro 6 Oden, 1980; Cohen and Massaro, 1992; Crowther et al., 1995), where a stimulus is compared on a feature-by-feature basis with prototypes representing each response category and the degree of each match is measured in fuzzy logic truth value, an object in a fuzzy version of EBS would be represented as a continuously valued activation vector of fuzzy rules. Whether such an approach would be more adequate for describing human classification behavior is the subject of ongoing research (Ju�ttner, Unzicker, 6 Rentschler, in preparation). 

The property of constrained generalization implies a particular interpretation of similarity within the EBS concept. Note that any given attribute predicate may vary in its precise position within a bounded region and still provide the same evidence for its pattern class. In this sense the EBS concept of similarity appears as a hybrid of Tversky's (1977) ``feature-based theory'' of similarity and the distance-related notion of similarity prevailing in psychometrics. With the latter it shares the property of spatial contiguity: An optimal segmentation of the attribute spaces into evidence regions was derived using clustering techniques that emphasize spatial proximity (Leader and K-means method), though not in some psychological space, but in physical attribute space. With the former it has in common that, given a set of evidence regions, the degree of similarity of two given patterns is determined by the degree of overlap in the sets of rules activated by both stimuli. Hence the notion of activated rules would formally replace the notion of attributes in Tversky's approach. Since the set of activated rules corresponds to the occurrence of certain attribute conjunctions in an input pattern, they can be regarded as meta-attributes in a symbolic pattern description. 

A final question which, to some extent, is connected to the relationship between feature predicates and rules, is that of what actually is learned by the observers during the initial learning stage of the experiment. Traditionally, with respect to the representation of acquired knowledge in the context of perceptual learning, there has been a distinction between instance learning and rule learning (for a review, see Shanks 6 John, 1994). Rule learning models have been proposed in the form of ``prototype concepts'' (Posner 6 Keele, 1968, - 1970; Reed, 1972) and assume that categories are repre sented by prototypes, the closest of which determines classification behavior. Instance learning, on the other hand, assumes that observers do not form classification rules but simply rely on fragmentary knowledge in the form of stored class instances, the similarity of which with respect to a new item determines the classification (Brooks, 1978; Medin 6 Schaffer, 1978; Nosofsky, 1986). Within the EBS approach rules are not assigned to global attributes of objects as such, but to attributes of parts of objects. Claimed to be learned is (1) an appropriate partitioning of the attribute spaces into evidence region and (2) the determination of evidence weights for the response alternatives, or classes. The evidence regions can be interpreted as some sort of fragmentary class knowledge, since they appear as entirely stimulus driven and deterministic related to the distribution of the physical attribute vectors. The evidence weights, then, provide the way in which the fragmentary knowledge is combined, thus forming classification rules. In this sense the classical dichotomy of rule-versusinstance-learning appears to be dissolved within the EBS concept.

In conclusion, our experiment has demonstrated that human learning and classification even of relatively simple patterns seem to involve mental representations of an explicit, structure-oriented characteristic. The evidencebased analysis of the experimental data allowed for a con sistent reconstruction concerning the underlying sets of unary and binary attributes, the number of extracted parts, and the potential for generalization with respect to various grey-level transformations. Formally, evidence-based pattern classification can be considered as an extension of classical psychometric approaches toward structural stimulus description in terms of physical stimulus attributes. In this sense EBS analysis provides a bottom-up solution to the problems of categorical judgements, similarity, and concept learning��features that are also essential for a functional understanding of human pattern recognition.
