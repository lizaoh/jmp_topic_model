A Thurstonian Analysis of Preference Change 

Ulf Böckenholt 

University of Illinois at Urbana–Champaign 

This paper presents a Thurstonian model for the analysis of preference change. Preferences are expressed in the form of rankings, possibly with ties. A vector-autoregression framework is used to investigate relationships between past and current rankings. It is shown that this approach yields a parsimonious and easily interpretable representation of individual preference differences in time-dependent ranking data. A detailed analysis of the 1992 National Election Study illustrates the proposed approach. © 2001 Elsevier Science (USA) 

Key Words: maximum likelihood estimation; ranking data; ties; parameterdriven process. 

1. INTRODUCTION 

Many decision problems are faced not once but repeatedly. For instance, the choice of a residential location, the selection of a travel mode for a trip to work, or the purchase of consumer brands within a product-class are recurrent choice situations. It is therefore not surprising that much of the recent work on understanding choice behavior has emphasized the investigation of intertemporal choices rather than the analysis of choices at a particular point in time. Repeated choices provide valuable information about decision making and the inherently dynamic nature of choice processes that is not available when modeling individual preferences in a cross-sectional study (Heckman, 1981; Loewenstein & Elster, 1992; Sen, 1993). 

Two general approaches, referred to as observation- and parameter-driven, may be distinguished for developing mechanisms that describe temporal dependencies among individual preferences (Cox, 1981). A choice model is observation-driven if the probability of observing the choice outcome r at time point t is of the form Pr (rt)=f(r[(t−1)] , et) and parameter-driven if it is of the form Pr (rt)=h(nt, ot) with parameter nt=g(n[(t−1)] , zt) . Here, superscripts denote past histories and et , ot , and zt are white noise. Thus, in observation-driven models, the probability of observing current choices is predicted by a function of past choice outcomes. In parameterdriven models, a stochastic process is specified that generates serial dependencies.

For example, under a parameter-driven approach, it may be postulated that an autoregressive process in the hypothesized random utilities explains a person’s switching behavior among choice items over time. In contrast, under an observationdriven approach, it may be conjectured that the occurrence of a particular choice makes a person more prone to express a similar preference in the near future. A prominent example of an observation-driven approach is the preference evolution model by Regenwetter, Falmagne, and Grofman (1999) who derive transition probabilities among different observable weak orders as a result of a stochastic learning process. Parameter-driven approaches have been recently applied for the investigation of latent ability changes in chess players and soccer teams over time (Batchelder, Bershad, & Simpson, 1992; Fahrmeir & Tutz, 1994). 

This article presents another example of a parameter-driven approach by introducing Thurstonian models for time-dependent preference data in the form of, possibly tied, rankings. Much recent work has focused on the estimation and application of Thurstonian ranking models (Chan & Bentler, 1998; Maydeu– Olivares, 1999; Yao & Böckenholt, 1999). A Thurstonian approach is attractive because it facilitates a straightforward formulation of both linear models for the means and structural equations for the covariance matrix of the preference judgments. As a result, Thurstonian models provide a parsimonious and easily interpretable representation of individual preference differences. We extend this class of random utility models to the case of time-dependent ranking data. Since Thurstonian models postulate that individual preference differences can be described by a multivariate normal distribution, the proposed extensions are based on the notion that the parameters characterizing the normal distribution vary over time. The resulting class of models is a special case of state-space models (Lüthkepol, 1993) which have proven useful in describing a wide range of physical processes. 

In observational studies discrete choices can provide only limited insights about the underlying cognitive processes that gave rise to the data. However, rankings or weak orders contain much information on how individuals differ in their evaluations of choice items. A Thurstonian approach is well suited to analyze these taste differences without making strong assumptions about their underlying determinants. Moreover, by modeling individual taste differences over time, the stability in rankings can be investigated systematically. In particular, reliability analyses of rankings, either for each choice item separately or for the complete choice set can be conducted. It is also straightforward to include covariates or other sources of information in a Thurstonian model to identify factors that systematically influence individual differences and, possibly, changes in preferences over time. 

The remainder of this article discusses the specification of time-dependent Thurstonian ranking models in the form of an autoregressive process defined on the individual utilities. A number of special cases are also presented. The following sections focus on estimation and model testing issues, as well as on an application to the 1992 Presidential Election Study panel data (Miller, Kinder, Rosenstone, & NES, 1993). Regenwetter et al. (1999) analyzed this data set previously by using their preference evolution model. It is shown that the autoregressive Thurstonian

Genz (1992) and deDoncker, Ciobanu, and Genz (1999) review integration methods for the computation of multivariate normal probabilities. In the reported application with T=2 and J=3 , the approach by Genz (1992) is used, which is highly accurate. Model parameters are estimated by a quasi-Newton method that approximates the inverse Hessian according to the Broyden–Fletcher–Goldfarb–Shanno update (see Gill, Murray, & Wright, 1981). The algorithm utilizes the partial numerical derivatives of the log-likelihood function with respect to all parameters and estimates the Hessian in the form of the cross-product of the Jacobian of the gradient. 

For larger T and J , however, a simulation-based algorithm is required for an effective estimation of the model parameters. Recent studies by Yao and Böckenholt (1999) have shown that the Gibbs sampler provides a computationally feasible method for evaluating the multivariate normal integral even when the dimensionality of S is large. An equally promising approach is the method of simulated moments (McFadden, 1989) which in an application reported by Keane (1997) worked successfully for a 180-dimensional integration problem. 

Large sample tests of fit are available based on the likelihood ratio (LR) test ( G[2] ) and Pearson’s goodness-of-fit test statistic ( P[2] ). Asymptotically, if a time-dependent ranking model provides an adequate description of the data, then both statistics follow a q[2] -distribution with ( H[T] −m−1) degrees of freedom, where m refers to the number of parameters to be estimated and H is the number of possible ranking patterns. The LR test is most useful when J [ 5 and T [ 3 . Otherwise, only a small subset of the possible ranking outcomes may be observed even when the sample size is large. In this case, it is doubtful that the test statistics will follow approximately a q[2] -distribution. However, useful information about a model fit may be obtained by inspecting standardized differences between the observed and fitted ranking probabilities for subsets (e.g., all triplets) of the items. Although these residuals are not independent, a careful inspection of their direction and size is useful in identifying systematic misfit. Nested ranking models can be compared by computing twice the difference between their log-likelihood values. This difference is asymptotically distributed as a q[2] -statistic with the degrees of freedom equal to the difference between the number of parameters in the unrestricted and restricted models.

6. DISCUSSION 

This paper showed that a vector-autoregressive model structure can be used to investigate the strength of the relation between past and current ranking judgments. By focusing on the underlying mean and covariance structure of the rankings a parsimonious and interpretable representation of the individual preference differences in the data was obtained. Equally important, the consideration of tied rankings by using a threshold parameter proved to be adequate in the marginal as well as in the joint analyses of the ranking data. Note also that the fit statistics obtained under the Thurstonian model are comparable to the ones obtained under Regenwetter et al. ’s (1999) stochastic preference evolution model. This result demonstrates that observation-driven and parameter-driven approaches can be complementary in nature and may yield similar results although their model structures appear to be rather different. However, it should be noted that the T=2 case does not provide a strong test of the preference evolution or the time-dependent ranking model. Additional research is needed that investigates the practical usefulness of both approaches and their similarities and differences for a larger number of time points. 

Three further methodological questions are currently being investigated. First, the notion that individual preference differences can be captured by a multivariate normal distribution is most appropriate when there is substantial agreement among the rankers about the similarity relationships among the choice items. This assumption is not always adequate and may prove to be rather limiting in some applications. For instance, without the self–reported partisanships of the voters the Thurstonian model would have been considerably less useful in the analysis of the election data. Instead, in this case it would have been more appropriate to represent the heterogeneous population of respondents by a finite mixture of subpopulations that differ in their perception of the choice items (Böckenholt, 1993). This approach may also be useful in improving on the fit of the ranking model for the 

> A THURSTONIAN ANALYSIS OF PREFERENCE CHANGE 313

Independents since this group consists of independent Democrats, independent Republicans, and independent Independents (Regenwetter et al. , 1999). Second, the proposed approach assumed that data were collected at equally spaced time intervals. However, especially in surveys it is unlikely that the judgments of all respondents are obtained at the same time point. For example, in the reported application the pre- and postelection data were each collected over a time period of about one month with the result that respondents were exposed to different events that were relevant to their judgments. Although the Thurstonian ranking model proved robust to the condition that observations were not collected simultaneously, a timecontinuous approach that allows for the possibility that time intervals between observations may vary from person to person may provide more powerful tests about changes in the underlying preference structure. Third, an important issue is the treatment of missing data, for example, as a result of attrition. Because missing data are unavoidable in longitudinal studies, the approach presented in this paper to consider only judges with complete responses may not be satisfactory in some applications. Specifically, if the mechanisms that lead to the missing data are an integral part of the choice behavior under study, they need to be explicitly taken into account in a data analysis.
