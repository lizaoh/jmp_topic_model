Many theories of preferential choice assume a latent valuation function drives a decision process that terminates upon meeting a decision rule. This surprisingly simple description encompasses many perspectives that cut across psychology, behavioural economics, marketing, and neuroscience. Take, for instance – in the spirit of Prof Tony Marley – random utility theory. The latent valuation function in random utility theory assumes the agent has a subjective value they place on each option in a set of available options. The  utility  of any of those options, at any moment in time, is a combination of its specific subjective value and unknown random factors. The decision rule in random utility theory compares the utility of all options, at the moment of choice, and selects the option with greatest utility.

A subset of models derived from this broader class of ‘latent valuation function’ theories are based on sequentially sampling decision-relevant information from the stimulus environment (for reviews, see  Forstmann, Ratcliff, & Wagenmakers, 2016; Ratcliff, Smith, Brown, & McKoon, 2016 ). The principle of sequential sampling theories is that pieces of decision-relevant information are  sampled  from the environment, where each piece of information may favour one or another response. This process is repeated  sequentially , over time, until we have collected, or accumulated, enough pieces of information, or evidence, to inform a choice. The theories naturally predict the choice (which option had more evidence?) and the choice time (how many samples were collected?). Viewed this way, sequential sampling theories such as random walk and diffusion models generalise static signal detection theory (which predicts choices) into the time domain (to predict the joint distribution of choices and choice times).

Sequential sampling theories are most often studied in the domain of ‘objective’ or veridical choice, where one response option is objectively more closely aligned with the target stimulus quality, such as detecting coherent motion or judging line lengths. Sequential sampling models have been studied in great detail for such ‘low-level’ decisions — for example, those pertaining to perceptual and memory-based representations  (e.g.,  Ratcliff & McKoon, 2008; Shadlen & Shohamy, 2016 ). This has advanced psychological theorising by decomposing observed choices and latencies into latent components of processing including the speed of information processing, cautiousness, and biases, among others. A critical aspect of the use of sequential sampling models is the application of these theories to choice  and  response time data. Many of these components of processing cannot be decomposed on the basis of choice data alone.

More recently, the principle of sequentially sampling decision-relevant information has been applied to ‘higher-level’ decisions – those pertaining to attitudinal and preferential options – often referred to as value-based decisions. One can think of this as generalising random utility theory (which predicts choices) into the time domain, which leads to a set of sequential sampling models known as ‘race’ models, where each response option is represented with a different node or ‘accumulator’ (to predict the joint distribution of choices and choice times, for

Generalising the theories to new domains of decision making has been fruitful in many ways. Theoretically, it permits testing the principle of sequential sampling as a foundational unit of the decision process — units that can be pieced together much like atoms making up a molecule. Such work aims to provide a deeper understanding of the decision processes involved in higher-level decisions, working toward theoretical consensus on the principles driving decisions under different conditions and contexts.

Sequential sampling models have been studied in ‘simple’ value-based or preferential decisions in considerable detail. ‘Simple’ in this context pertains to the stimulus structure: choices for value-based options that implicitly have multiple features or  attributes  but the stimulus structure does not specify values for those attributes. For example, a choice between crisps and a chocolate bar, where information about the options is communicated with images that do not explicitly describe features such as the price, weight, caloric content, and so on. In this context, the primary predictions of the models are borne out in data (e.g.,  Fisher, 2021 ;  Gluth, Kern, Kortmann, & Vitali, 2020 ;  Krajbich, Armel, & Rangel, 2010 ;  Krajbich & Rangel, 2011 ; and  Smith & Krajbich, 2018 ; for review, see  Clithero, 2018 ). This supports the hypothesis that sequential sampling may be a principle underlying foundational unit of the decision process.

Value-based decisions are also widely studied with  explicit  multi-attribute structures, where the available options have specified values for a set of product attributes (for reference sources, see  Louviere, Flynn, & Marley, 2015; Louviere, Hensher, & Swait, 2000 ). For instance, a choice between a chocolate bar that costs $2.20, weighs 50 g and contains 780kj of energy and a bag of crisps that cost $2.60, weighs 60 g and contains 1400kj of energy. Throughout the manuscript, we refer to the class of multi-attribute stimuli with explicitly specified attribute values as multi-attribute options with  defined features .

Many existing theories of choice between value-based options with defined features either have no role for choice times (e.g., the family of logit and probit models derived from random utility theory;  Train, 2009 ) or they have a role for choice times though they are rarely used to test model adequacy (e.g., sequential sampling models;  Otter, Johnson, Rieskamp, Allenby, Brazell, Diederich, Hutchinson, MacEachern, Ruan, & Townsend, 2008  — see below for some exceptions). Such sequential sampling models naturally accommodate options with defined features, and can generate predictions for observed patterns of choices marginalised over the predicted distribution of choice times. This permits a test of the models’ predictive scope – the capacity to explain observed choices for options with defined features. This has been a useful test because latency data are rarely collected in multi-attribute preferential choice contexts, and when they are collected they are less frequently analysed and modelled. When they have been collected, there are typically far fewer trials than are considered in conventional applications of sequential sampling models in ‘low-level’ decision contexts, or even simple value-based decisions, which reduces the information value of the choice times, at least to some extent.

Nevertheless, applying sequential sampling models to choice data (without response times) is a limited test of the theories. The models predict a joint distribution of choice outcomes and choice times, so a test of the models based on just one dimension of the joint distribution has limited capacity to discriminate between competing theories. It is also possible the class of models mispredicts important features of the observed choice times, which has the potential to falsify the class of models as a proposed psychological process for preferential decision making.

Exceptions exist, of course, and sequential sampling models have been evaluated against choice frequency  and  choice times for preferential options with defined features (e.g.,  Cataldo & Cohen, 2021; Cohen, Kang, & Leise, 2017; Evans, Holmes, & Trueblood, 2019; Hawkins, Marley, Heathcote, Flynn, Louviere, & Brown, 2014b; Jones, Hawkins, & Brown, 2015 ); we restrict our focus to consumer-like decisions and do not consider further other types of preferential options, such as those studied in paradigms of risky gambles or delay discounting. While the two outcome measures have been jointly used to constrain the theories (i.e., estimate parameters) in previous studies, models have tended to be evaluated against marginal distributions of choice outcomes and choice times (i.e., for measures of goodness of fit to data). For example,  Evans et al. (2019)  compared choice frequency and choice time predictions between four of the major cognitive process models of multi-attribute choice: multi-alternative decision field theory ( Roe, Busemeyer, & Townsend, 2001 ), the multi-attribute linear ballistic accumulator ( Trueblood, Brown, & Heathcote, 2014 ), the leaky competing accumulator ( Usher & McClelland, 2004 ), and the associative accumulation model ( Bhatia, 2013 ). They found that analysing the models against the joint distribution of choices and choice times led to different model preference compared to analyses that only assessed the models against the choice outcomes, such as in  Trueblood et al. (2014)  and  Turner, Schley, Muller, and Tsetsos (2018) .

It is rare to consider the  joint  distribution of choice frequency and choice times in multi-attribute choice for options with defined features – evaluating choice times as a function of choice outcomes, or vice versa. This is commonplace in low-level decisions, for instance, with visualisation including latency-probability and quantile-probability (QP) plots, or defective CDFs (e.g.,  Donkin & Brown, 2018; Ratcliff et al., 2016 ). When the joint distribution of multi-attribute preferential choice frequency and choice times are inspected in this way an interesting pattern emerges that, to our knowledge, has not previously been documented. The pattern is important as it places critical constraints on the capacity of sequential sampling models to explain the cognitive processes driving multi-attribute preferential choice.

Here, we study a pattern in multi-attribute choice frequency and choice time data, for options with defined features, that violates a qualitative trend observed in countless studies of choices and times in speeded low-level decisions  (for  reviews,  see  Forstmann et al., 2016; Ratcliff & McKoon, 2008; Ratcliff et al., 2016 ). In low-level decisions, we almost always observe a monotonically decreasing relationship between choice frequency and choice times, conditioned on a given threshold/boundary. That is, as a stimulus dimension becomes more discriminable (e.g., increasing brightness in a brightness discrimination task) it is increasingly likely to be chosen and those choices are made more rapidly. This trend is typically observed in simple value-based decisions, too (for review, see  Clithero, 2018 ). In the value-based context, option discriminability refers to the difference in value of the available options: there is lower discriminability when the set of available options are of similar value and higher discriminability when the set of options differ markedly in value.

Sequential sampling models predict this relationship with a change in the parameter corresponding to the rate of accumulation – the drift rate. As decision-relevant information is acquired more rapidly (greater drift rate), the decision process reaches threshold sooner (faster response) with increasing likelihood (greater choice probability). This relationship is conditioned on a given value of the threshold/boundary parameter – the standard assumption in the decision making literature when pre-stimulus information about the upcoming decision context is not available. We maintain this standard fixed-threshold assumption throughout.

We show that the negative monotonic relationship between choice frequency and choice time is violated for decisions between multi-attribute preferential stimuli with defined features – there is an invariance between the two outcome measures. This result places a critical constraint on the generalisability of the principle of sequential sampling from low-level to high-level decisions – at least for options with defined features.

To our knowledge, two previous results hint at this finding. First,  Cohen et al. (2017)  report a moderate-to-strong positive correlation between choice proportions observed (in people) and predicted (by the multi-attribute linear ballistic accumulator) in preferential and risky choice contexts, yet a very low correlation between observed and predicted choice times. Given the model predicts a negative monotonic relationship between choice frequency and choice times, and the correlation between observed data and model predictions differed markedly for choice frequency and times, one might conclude the data violate some feature of the predicted relationship. Second,  Cataldo and Cohen (2021)  found that of the multi-attribute context effects, faster responses tend to be associated with a stronger similarity effect while slower responses tend to be associated with stronger attraction and compromise effects. This empirical result demonstrates choice frequency can depend on choice time, marginalised across stimulus values. We address an orthogonal question: conditioned on stimulus value, what is the relationship between choice frequency and choice time?

In the remainder of the paper, we document experimental evidence for our claim, along with preliminary evidence from standard sequential sampling models. We start with an overview of the generic paradigm studied in much of the decision literature: the discrete choice experiment (DCE).

Many studies of multi-attribute preferential choice with defined features involve a Discrete Choice Experiment (DCE). A multi-attribute DCE presents a set of two or more options that vary across two or more features or  attributes , and asks the respondent to select their most preferred option. For instance, when choosing between candidate fast food options, would you prefer an expensive meal that arrives quickly or a cheaper meal with a longer wait time? By manipulating the  levels  of the attributes, like cheap vs expensive meal and fast vs slow delivery time, the DCE requires participants to make tradeoffs between the multiple attributes that make up an option. By selecting one option over another the respondent reveals information about the psychological weight they place on the attributes of the chosen option relative to the unchosen option/s. Through repeated choices between sets of options with different combinations of attributes and levels, the analyst learns increasingly precise information about the psychological weight a decision maker assigns to the component attributes.

Multi-attribute DCEs in the applied choice field are typically analysed with random utility models. Random utility models share many similarities with standard sequential sampling models: the existence of a latent valuation function, sampling some number of times from the valuation function on presentation of a stimulus, and selecting an option based on some function of the sampled values. Indeed, there is close correspondence between random utility and sequential sampling models formally (e.g., see  Colonius & Marley, 2015; Marley & Colonius, 1992 ) and empirically (e.g.,  Hawkins, Marley, Heathcote, Flynn, Louviere, & Brown, 2014a ). Given similarities in the standard analysis approaches applied across disciplines, one might expect similar relationships to hold in data — such as the joint distribution of choice outcomes and choice times.

Many sequential sampling models that explain the multi-attribute context effects also violate the choice frequency and choice time prediction we document here. This is because the violation concerns the dynamic accumulation process of sequential sampling models and not the composition of the latent valuation function that provides inputs to the sampling process; the latter has been the target for most cognitive theories of decisions that violate the context effects, not the former. This applies to major cognitive theories of the context effects including multi-alternative decision field theory ( Roe et al., 2001 ), the multi-attribute linear ballistic accumulator ( Trueblood et al., 2014 ), the leaky competing accumulator ( Usher & McClelland, 2004 ), and the associative accumulation model ( Bhatia, 2013 ); see formalisation in  Evans et al. (2019)  that demonstrates the common underlying structure.

The specific question we address here is whether value-based options in a multi-attribute DCE that are chosen more often are also chosen more quickly. One can address this question at two levels: choosing  options  (a collection of attribute levels – like a cheap meal with fast delivery) or component  attributes  of options (the attribute levels considered independently – like cheap meals, or fast delivery). At the level of options, one can map all unique combinations of attribute levels permitted in the experimental design, where some will naturally be more desirable — a cheap meal delivered quickly is better than a slow and expensive meal. We ask if the former is chosen more rapidly than the latter. At the level of attributes, we examine the relationship between the choice frequency for options with a specified attribute level  marginalised over all other attributes and options , and how quickly those options are chosen. To continue the earlier example, a negative monotonic relationship at the level of attributes would be that cheap fast food meals are chosen more often  and  more quickly than expensive fast food meals — independent of the speed of delivery. As another example, and to foreshadow the DCE studied in Experiment 1, we would expect phones that cost $250 are chosen more often  and  more quickly than phones that cost $500, which in turn would be chosen more often and more quickly than phones that cost $750 — independent of the values of the 4 other attributes that comprise the DCE stimulus. The latter is a powerful test of an effect because it does not rely on specific collections of attribute levels to emerge. We present evidence for our argument using a common multi-attribute DCE context across four experiments: preferences for mobile (cell) phones. In each experiment, participants were presented with 2 or 3 phones per choice set (trial), where each phone was described by different values for each of 4 or 5 attributes. Participants were asked to select their most preferred phone in each choice set. Across studies we assessed ‘free’ choice, the impact of time pressure, whether the attribute structure of the options was correlated or not, and whether the analysis focus was at the level of component attributes or options. Regardless of the study differences, the same pattern emerged in data: the standard relationship between choice frequency and choice time is violated for multi-attribute preferential choice.

Experiment 1 reports data previously published in Experiment 2 of  Hawkins et al. (2014b) . Twenty six participants selected their most preferred phone from each of 150 choice sets, presented one at a time.

The most striking feature in  Fig. 1  is that response times do not change as a function of choice proportion. This observation holds for measures of central tendency, such as the median (middle horizontal line), and for the full response time distributions, representing both fast and slow decisions (lower and upper horizontal lines, respectively). For all attributes, levels that were in options more frequently chosen were not chosen more rapidly; there was no relationship with the speed of the choice. All standard sequential sampling models predict that options chosen more frequently will be chosen more quickly, conditioned on a fixed threshold. This is shown in  Fig. 1  with posterior predictive data of an exemplar sequential sampling model – the Linear Ballistic Accumulator (LBA;  Brown & Heathcote, 2008 ) model – applied to the data. The model specification follows standard assumptions, originally described in  Hawkins et al. (2014b) ; complete modelling details are given in  Appendix A .

A core feature of the model’s predictions is the monotonically decreasing relationship between attribute-level choice proportions and attribute-level response times. This relationship holds across the entire response time distribution, for the fastest through to the slowest decisions. It also holds for all attributes, even attributes with relatively little impact (i.e., small range in choice proportion).

The model clearly misfits the data. It  under -predicts choice proportions and  over -predicts response times. By only varying drift rates across choice sets – that is, sensitivity to, or utility of, the attribute-level information – the model cannot capture the observed data. The model is capable of predicting more extreme attribute-level choice proportions (i.e., a larger range on the  
                            x 
                         -axes) such that it would more closely match the observed choice data. However, in doing so the model must predict an even larger range in response times between the least and most desirable levels of an attribute, thus misfitting the response time data to an even greater degree than it already does. The predictions in  Fig. 1  are those that maximise the posterior likelihood of the joint distribution of choices and response times; the ‘best’ the model can do, given its (standard) specification for multi-attribute preferential choices.

To ensure the model predictions were not idiosyncratic to the particular sequential sampling model we chose, we repeated the modelling analysis with a second sequential sampling model — the racing diffusion model (RDM;  Tillman, Van Zandt, & Logan, 2020 ); for complete modelling details see  Appendix A . All conclusions were identical (data not shown). Thus, the preceding discussion applies equally to both models, and demonstrates the qualitative pattern of the model predictions generalises to at least two standard sequential sampling models.

The conclusions thus far were based on visual inspection of group-averaged data. We aimed to confirm that trends in the observed and predicted data followed qualitatively different trends via statistical analysis. The aim of our analysis was to test whether a linear regression predicting response time as a function of choice proportion has a slope coefficient of 0. A coefficient of 0, or a coefficient that does not statistically differ to 0, is evidence for no relationship between choice frequency and choice times.

We aimed to test whether the findings of Experiment 1 generalised to a context where decision makers modulate their decision threshold — the evidence required to commit to a decision. It is well known that people adaptively set thresholds based on relevant information at the time of making a decision; for instance, environmental cues encouraging speedy or careful actions. And while we assume throughout that thresholds do not vary as a function of the target stimulus (i.e., the content of the DCE), it is important to confirm whether we still observe no relationship between choice frequency and choice times when pre-stimulus information (e.g., a cue) positions the decision maker along different parts of the speed-accuracy tradeoff function. That is, do we still observe no relationship conditioning on  different  thresholds?

Experiment 2 reports data previously published in Experiment 2b of  Cavallaro and Hawkins (2022) , where 45 participants selected their most preferred phone from each of 250 choice sets. The structure closely followed Experiment 1 with two primary exceptions. First, participants were cued to emphasise speedy or careful decisions. The emphasis was randomised across trials and implemented with a pre-trial cue to “be speedy” or “be careful”. Second, the stimulus was composed of 4 attributes each with 3 levels; see  Table 2 .

Fig. 3  displays the attribute-level choice proportion and response time data in the same format as  Fig. 1 , separately for speedy and careful decisions. We observed similar qualitative patterns to Experiment 1 for most attributes. When given instructions to be speedy, there was no relationship between choice frequency and choice time for most quantiles and attributes, with some suggestion of a negative-going relationship for the price and camera attributes for faster decisions (lower quantiles). When given instructions to be careful, there was a general tendency toward a negative relationship for faster decisions for the price and memory attributes, and the battery attribute though to a lesser extent. Overall, there was a greater range in decision behaviour in Experiment 2, over which the relationship between choice frequency and choice times was still mostly absent, although unlike Experiment 1 there were some indications the relationship may be present.

As in Experiment 1, we compared the observed data to predictions of the LBA model – see  Fig. 3  – and predictions of the RDM (data not shown); complete modelling details are given in  Appendix A . We focus on two main results. First, the two models misfit the Experiment 2 data in the same qualitative way; under-predicting choice proportions and over-predicting response times. This again demonstrates the modelling result in Experiment 1 is not idiosyncratic to a specific sequential sampling model.

Second, as reported in  Cavallaro and Hawkins (2022) , the only parameter difference that emerged between the speedy and careful conditions was in the decision thresholds – for both models – such that participants accumulated more decision-relevant information when instructed to make careful compared to speedy decisions; in the LBA, there was also a smaller range in the start-point distribution for the speedy condition than the careful condition. The model specification permitted separate utility coefficients (drift rate parameters) for each attribute and emphasis condition, though the estimated utility coefficients did not differ between the speedy and careful conditions. This suggests our finding of no relationship between attribute-level choice proportions and response times is not dependent on a particular response speed or ‘mode’ of decision making. Rather, it generalises across (at least some of the) speed-accuracy tradeoff settings that people may adopt when making multi-attribute preferential choices.

Experiments 1 and 2 used a standard principle of DCE design:  uncorrelated  attribute structure. The attribute structure is uncorrelated when, for a given option, the level of one attribute has no bearing on the levels of the other attributes. Experiments 1 and 2 imposed an uncorrelated attribute structure by randomly sampling the level of each attribute from the set of potential levels. Many DCEs, particularly in the applied choice literature, specify uncorrelated attribute structures with carefully balanced orthogonal designs such that the attribute levels composing each option are assigned in a manner that makes the options in a choice set maximally different. On the whole, uncorrelated attribute structures are highly efficient for statistical estimation, and hence identifying the attributes of greatest importance to consumers from minimal trial counts.

However, uncorrelated attribute structures introduce a level of artificiality to the preferential choice scenario relative to multi-attribute choices as they occur in the marketplace. Real-world preferential choice occurs in a context of  correlated  attribute structures, where knowing something about the level of one attribute generally provides some information about potential levels of other attributes. For instance, one generally expects a $1000 phone to contain better features (camera, memory, etc.) than a $100 phone. It is not only a price ‘heuristic’, however. Having some content knowledge also allows the decision maker to draw inferences about likely attribute correlations. For instance, a phone with a low screen resolution is unlikely to have a high resolution camera, because photos taken with the high resolution camera cannot be clearly viewed on a low resolution screen.

It is possible that the lack of association between choices and response times observed in Experiments 1 and 2 was due to the artificiality introduced by uncorrelated attribute structures. For instance, with information about attribute correlations unavailable, it is possible that participants drew upon different cognitive mechanisms to make decisions about the novel stimuli.

In Experiment 3, we tested whether the same (lack of) relationship between choice frequency and choice time was observed when multi-attribute preferential options were generated with correlated attribute structures. To ensure the correlation structure of our stimuli mirrored the magnitude and direction of the attribute correlations observed in the marketplace, we sampled our stimuli from all mobile phones for sale at a prominent consumer hifi store in Australia during Summer 2020. We extracted the product descriptions for all the phones and selected 5 attributes for our DCE that were routinely specified in the product description for almost all phones; price, camera, memory, screen size, and screen resolution. We extracted the levels for these 5 attributes for all available phones and removed duplicates from the database, which were predominantly phones that differed on attributes we did not investigate, like colour. The final database contained 101 unique phones. The Appendix provides evidence of the correlated attribute structure of the phone database and a complete listing of options in the database is available at  http://osf.io/kdyp5 .

Experiment 3 manipulated the attribute structure between subjects in two conditions. Both conditions made choices between sets of 3 phones. The  correlated  attributes condition had choice sets derived directly from the phone database, where each choice set was generated from a random sample, without replacement, from the set of 101 phones. This ensured that, across trials and on average, participants would experience the magnitude and direction of the attribute correlations as they appear in the marketplace. The  uncorrelated  attributes condition had choice sets indirectly derived from the phone database. Separately for each choice set and option, we randomly sampled a level for each attribute from the set of available attribute levels across the 101 phones in the database. This stimulus-generation procedure balanced the range of attribute levels participants experienced across both conditions, on average, though it removed the correlated attributes structure inherent in the phone database. We have demonstrated there is no relationship between how often people choose an item from a set of preferential options with defined features and how quickly they choose from the set. Our evidence for this invariance in multi-attribute preferential/ value-based judgements came from four experimental contexts that were unconstrained (Experiment 1), imposed time pressure (Experiment 2), and contained artificial and real-life compositions of options (Experiments 3 and 4). The invariance held when data were inspected at the ‘higher’ level of overall options (Experiment 4) and the ‘lower’ level of the component attributes of options (Experiments 1–3).

Our findings lend support to a critical constraint on the relationship between choice frequency and choice times: a lack of relationship. Such an invariance is powerful because the dominant theories that have choices and times within their predictive scope – sequential sampling models – all naturally predict a negative monotonic relationship. Our results therefore place new constraints on theories of multi-attribute choice.

Before considering implications, there are some limitations to our conclusion to bear in mind. First, choosing between multi-alternative and multi-attribute options may include longer or multiple components of processing that are unrelated to evidence accumulation. If this is the case, then the expected relationship between choice time and choice frequency would be attenuated. For instance, compared to perceptual decisions, value-based decisions may require more time searching for decision relevant information (e.g., attribute values) relative to evidence accumulation (for review, see  Mormann, Griffiths, Janiszewski, Russo, Aribarg, Ashby, Bagchi, Bhatia, Kovacheva, Meissner, et al., 2020 ). Second, our findings relate to value-based decisions with defined features – explicitly specified attributes and levels, such as a choice between one phone that cost $30/month with a 12MP camera and 32 GB memory capacity and another phone that cost $45/month with an 8MP camera and 64 GB memory capacity. This differs to research that has investigated value-based decisions for options that implicitly have multiple attributes but the stimulus structure does not specify values for those attributes — such as a choice between crisps and a chocolate bar, most often displayed as images without a feature description. There is considerable research demonstrating that value-based decisions  without  explicit attributes observe the expected negative monotonic relationship between choice time and choice frequency (e.g.,  Fisher, 2021 ;  Gluth et al., 2020 ;  Krajbich et al., 2010 ;  Krajbich & Rangel, 2011 ; and  Smith & Krajbich, 2018 ; for review, see  Clithero, 2018 ). We recommend that future research further investigates this difference between implicit and explicit attribute structures for value-based decisions.

Given the behavioural differences, we argue that our findings have implications for theories of multi-attribute preferential choice – for options with implicitly  and  explicitly defined features. This is because our findings for options with defined features must be reconciled with choice frequency and choice time effects observed for options without defined features. While the models we studied assumed independent utilities for each attribute, footnote 1 noted that the well-studied class of sequential sampling models that explain context effects all predict the same negative monotonic relationship. Therefore, incorporating relative attribute values – comparisons of attribute levels across options – is unlikely to provide a fruitful path forward with respect to explaining the invariance between choice frequency and choice times.

We suggest the first steps along this path might incorporate different psychological mechanisms into existing sequential sampling theories of multi-attribute choice, and empirically test them against data. The goal of such work will be to determine whether the new mechanisms for different stimulus setups are (a) psychologically reasonable and (b) sufficient to explain patterns in observed data.

As a starting point for such work, we suggest the lack of relationship between the two outcome measures could be incorporated into existing theories of multi-attribute preferential choice. For instance, with complex displays of defined features (e.g., features defined in rows of a table, and available options in columns), the decision process may involve a dynamic attention-switching mechanism that discounts information that is not currently attended. For instance,  Yang and Krajbich (2023)  investigated value-based decisions between two options each with two defined features, and found that attention switching (eye movements) led to discounted weights (lower utility) for the non-fixated attribute and non-fixated option. Discounted weights for non-attended information will attenuate the relationship between choice frequency and choice time.

Another perspective is that preference may dynamically accumulate until a quantity of information is accrued  or  a self-imposed (i.e., internal) deadline terminates the process — a deadline on the amount of time one is willing to commit to a decision. A strict interpretation of such ‘deadline’ models suggest all choices should have the same choice time, though modern approaches suggest a distribution of internal times could terminate the process (e.g.,  Bhatia & Mullett, 2016; Hawkins & Heathcote, 2021 ). Internal deadline models truncate decisions with slower-than-average preference accumulation, and thus attenuate the relationship between choice frequency and choice times. One possibility is that internal deadlines may be used more often for choices between options with defined features because the deliberation process is (or is more likely to be) more taxing to the cognitive system than choices between options with undefined features.

Alternatively, our results might hark back to earlier work generalising random utility theory into the time domain (e.g.,  Marley, 1989; Marley & Colonius, 1992 ). This work showed that classic random utility models applied to choice data can be generalised to ‘horse race’ random utility models applied to choice and response time data. The resulting generalisations were considered inadequate because they predicted distributions of choice times that were independent of the choice outcomes. This prediction was considered to be inconsistent with data showing choice time depends on choice frequency – data predominantly from ‘low-level’ decisions. Our work shows that the rejection of the class of ‘horse race’ random utility models may need to be re-considered, given choice frequency and choice times appear to be independent, or almost independent, for the types of ‘high-level’ multi-attribute preferential choices where random utility models are most often studied – options with defined features.

To take a broader perspective, in the domain of value-based decisions between options with defined features, choice data is by far the most common outcome measure used to test theories and generate predictions of marketplace behaviour. This is clearly the most natural approach when we have a goal of enhancing market share; that is, in applied contexts what people choose to purchase is ultimately the most important outcome. Nevertheless, to accurately predict choices we need good theories. We argue that a focus on choice outcomes is a ‘first step’ metric in identifying and developing good theories. For more accurate predictions we ought to incorporate more choice-relevant metrics into the choice theories. This is not to say that other metrics have not been investigated with an eye to improving prediction, including eye movements, neuroimaging (e.g., EEG, fMRI), confidence ratings, and indeed response times. Rather, our perspective is that these various outcome measures provide a lens on a single generative process. As such, we ought to consider latent cognitive processes as a single ‘model’ capable of generating a joint distribution over outcome measures, only some of which we observe (i.e., measure) on a given occasion — like the joint distribution of choices frequency and choice times we examined here.

The burnin stage used 100 particles and ran for 1000 iterations.
