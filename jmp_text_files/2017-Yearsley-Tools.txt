Recent years have seen a surge of interest in so-called quantum models of cognition and decision making ( Aerts, 2009; Busemeyer & Bruza, 2014; Khrennikov, 2010; Mogiliansky, Zamir, & Zwirn, 2009; Pothos & Busemeyer, 2013; Wang, Busemeyer, Atmanspacher, & Pothos, 2013; Yukalov & Sornette, 2011 ). These models are based on the mathematics of quantum probability theory (QT), but abstracted from the usual physical content. These models have arisen in part as a response to the empirical challenges faced by ‘rational’ decision-making models, such as those based on Bayesian probability theory (such examples are mostly associated with the famous Tversky–Kahneman research tradition. See e.g.   Chater, Tenenbaum, & Yuille, 2006 ,  Tversky & Kahneman, 1974 .). These quantum models posit that, at least in some circumstances, human behavior does not align well with classical probability theory or expected utility maximization. However unlike, for example, the fast and frugal heuristics programme (see, e.g.   Gardiner, 2011 ), quantum cognition aims not to do away with the idea of a formal structure underlying decision-making, but simply to replace the structure of classical probability theory with an alternative theory of probabilities. This new probability theory has features, such as context effects, interference effects and constructive judgments, which align well with psychological intuition about human decision-making. Initial research involving quantum models tended to focus mainly on explaining results previously seen as paradoxical from the point of view of classical probability theory, and there have been a number of successes in this area ( Aerts, Gabora, & Sozzo, 2013; Blutner, Pothos, & Bruza, 2013; Bruza, Kitto, Ramm, & Sitbon, 2015; Pothos et al., 2015; Pothos & Busemeyer, 2009, 2013; Pothos, Busemeyer, & Trueblood, 2013; Trueblood & Busemeyer, 2011; Wang et al., 2013; White, Pothos, & Busemeyer, 2014 ). More recently, the focus has switched to some extent to testing new predictions arising from quantum models, and designing better tests of quantum vs classical decision theories ( Atmanspacher & Filk, 2010; Wang, Solloway, Shiffrin, & Busemeyer, 2014; Yearsley & Pothos, 2014, 2016 ).

Although good progress has been made in understanding how to build simple cognitive models using QT there has been less work done on developing more sophisticated or realistic models. Simplified QT models can provide a good qualitative understanding of some cognitive processes, but if the ultimate goal is to pit QT models against the current best models of cognition and decision making then the sophistication of QT models will need to grow to match such models. This necessitates a move beyond toy models to something more realistic. In addition, many of the current QT models have obvious conceptual problems beyond their over-simplicity; one simple example is that typical models for the evolution of cognitive variables are unitary, which means they have no fixed points. Under such a model no amount of evidence presentation can ever lead to a fixed belief state.

The solution to these problems is to move to a slightly more sophisticated framework for QT modeling that can better represent realistic decision making. However there is a challenge here; although this more sophisticated framework is not technically or conceptually more demanding than that employed by current simple QT models, it  is  considerably harder to access. Although there are a wealth of introductory accounts of quantum physics that can be easily accessed by cognitive scientists (e.g.   Isham, 1995 ,  Plenio, 2002 ,  Peres, 1998 ) (and even a number specifically written for them,  Busemeyer & Bruza, 2014 ,  Yearsley & Busemeyer, 2015 ) it is much harder to find introductory accounts of some of the tools needed to build more realistic QT models.

Some general discussion of these issues may be found in  Asano et al. (2015)  and  Khrennikov and Basieva (2014) , while specific examples of applications of the material in this tutorial may be found in  Asano, Ohya, and Khrennikov (2011) ,  Asano, Ohya, Tanaka, Khrennikov, and Basieva (2011)  and  Asano, Basieva, Khrennikov, Ohya, and Tanaka (2012a,b) . Nevertheless we feel there is a need for an introductory tutorial to collect together some of the basic ideas and present them in a pedagogical way suitable for readers with limited exposure to quantum cognition. The purpose of this tutorial is to try and give such an introduction. The material presented consists mainly of advanced concepts and ideas from the physics literature, some of which have made it into more sophisticated quantum cognitive models, but some of which are yet to find a concrete application. The idea is to present these ideas in a way which makes them accessible to cognitive scientists interested in QT models. A key aim of this tutorial is also to make clear when and why these more sophisticated ideas need to be applied, so that researchers have a better understanding of the limits of the current simple QT models.

Since the potential scope of this tutorial is vast, the presentation style will be somewhat non-standard. Specifically we are aiming for a broad and shallow overview of several different topics, or a sort of ‘London Bus Tour’ of modern quantum mechanics. We will be satisfied if we can point out some of the major landmarks, and suggest how readers may wish to explore the issues in more depth for themselves, as and when they feel it necessary.

The most commonly encountered quantum models of cognitive processes are idealizations in a number of ways. The key one for the purpose of this tutorial is that they assume experimenters have perfect knowledge/control over the cognitive state of participants, the form and effect of measurements, and finally the details of any ‘evolution’ of the state.

In the real word (or even the real lab), things are rarely this simple. We want to show you some tools that can let you generalize the models you have come across so far to apply in more realistic situations. It turns out that doing this will also teach us some profound things about the meaning of the quantum approach to cognition, and how it differs from classical approaches. The theme of this tutorial is therefore ‘Noise’, specifically ‘Noise in the cognitive state’, ‘Noise in the measurements’ and finally ‘Noise in the evolution.’

In the section on ‘Noise in the cognitive state’ we will introduce the notion of a density operator, or density matrix, which is a more general type of state than the usual state vector. We will explain how they can be used to capture the idea of a state with both classical and quantum uncertainties, and we will show how the entropy of these states can be defined.

In the section on ‘Noise in the measurements’ we introduce a more general type of measurement scheme than the familiar one of projection operators, and we will explain how these may be used to model response errors. We will also see how these can be used to model situations where the response options are not easily thought of as orthogonal, for example when responding on a Likert scale. We will also examine to what extent existing results on order effects hold when using these more general measurements.

Finally in the section on ‘Noise in the evolution’ we introduce a more general type of quantum evolution than the usual Schrödinger equation, which is essentially a sort of a quantum generalization of a classical stochastic process. We will explain how these sorts of evolutions arise, and we will show how they can be used to model irreversible transitions. We will also discuss some ways in which quantum systems may become ‘classical’ under these sorts of evolutions.

Second, when we build quantum models of cognitive processes we usually focus on one or two relevant variables and ignore any other knowledge/beliefs decision makers possess. This presupposes a sort of process where we can augment a quantum model with extra variables to increase the scope of a model, or systematically ignore certain variables to decrease the scope, while leaving the basic structure of the model the same. For this reason it is not really consistent to work with models involving a small number of variables with pure knowledge states, although it can be a useful approximation in some cases.

We mentioned that density matrices can be employed to model inhomogeneous groups of participants. We mean this less in the sense of explaining the behavior of particular participants, and more in the sense of predicting the spread of results, rather than just average behavior. Most quantum models in the literature are rather simple constructions that are concerned with predicting a particular average behavior. (for example the conjunction fallacy.) An important future direction for research will be understanding the spread of participant behaviors, rather than just the average behavior. Density matrices allow us to do this in two ways;  
                            
                               • 
                               If we happen to know that some individual characteristic is important, and we know the distribution of this characteristic in our testing population, then we can make direct predictions about the average behavior and the spread of behaviors by encoding these differences as an initial density matrix, in a very similar way to our toy example of left and right handed participants. 
                            
                            
                               • 
                               Suppose instead we only think there might be some individual characteristic that is important, but we have no idea about its distribution in our testing population. Well then we can encode the differences in an initial density matrix again, but now leave the distribution of the characteristics as a free parameter, and try to fit this distribution from the data. In other words, if we think different groups of participants might show different behaviors, we can use a density matrix to perform a sort of mixed models analysis, and determine what distribution of individual differences best fits the data. 
                            
                          These ideas obviously represent important next steps for the QT approach, if our ambition is to produce ever more accurate models.

The introduction of states with classical as well as quantum uncertainty represents a very significant development in the quantum formalism. We can now go ahead and represent a much more general variety of knowledge states, which probably better reflect the kind of participants we might encounter in a realistic experiment.

However the introduction of these states also gives us a chance to discuss what we think is one of the most powerful a priori reasons for considering quantum models of cognition and decision. At the heart of this argument is the difference between classical and quantum uncertainties. Suppose we have a classical system such as a fair coin, where our best description consists of a probability distribution for the two possible outcomes, head or tails. The probabilistic description reflects the fact that we are uncertain about the outcome of a given coin toss. Classical probability distributions have an associated Shannon entropy, and so classically the uncertainty about the outcome of the coin toss (probabilistic description) is related to a lack of knowledge (entropy) about the state of the system. In other words, classically we have,

Suppose we want to build a classical cognitive model of a participant’s preferences; specifically, let us imagine we want a model of what type biscuit the author will choose to eat from the box he has in front of him, as a reward for finishing this subsection. To simplify matters, suppose there are only two types, milk or dark chocolate. The author professes to be indifferent between them currently, and experience (and the number of each kind left in the box) tells us he is equally likely to choose either variety. A classical description of the author’s cognitive state, which aimed to match his behavior, would have to be a probability distribution which gave equal weight to milk and dark chocolate biscuits.

There is something odd about this though. To be clear, such a description  would  give the correct statistics for choices. However recall our discussion above; this classical probability distribution would have an associated entropy, and should be interpretable in terms of a lack of knowledge about the state. But wait, what information exactly is it that we lack? In the example of a coin toss above, if we toss a coin and ask you to guess heads or tails, there is of course a ‘correct’ answer, a probabilistic description reflects your lack of knowledge about the true state of the coin. Going back to our cognitive model, the only  knowledge  you could be lacking here is the author’s  true  preference for his next biscuit type. In other words, every time you use a classical probability distribution to describe a system, you assume that there is a  true  state of the system, that you are ignorant of. This might be ok for coins, but it is far from obvious that this makes sense for decision makers. For example, according to a classical theory, the author really does have a definite preference for my next biscuit, he is just not telling himself what it is….

The final type of noise we will consider is noise in the evolution of the state. In a typical experiment we manipulate the cognitive state of a participant by presenting some kind of stimuli. In summary then, this Montevideo master equation describes a very useful sort of evolution, which corresponds to evolving with a stimuli whose exact effect on participants we do not know. This is also a good example of an evolution that would be somewhat unusual in physics, but is very natural in cognitive science, where the effect of a stimuli on a particular participant is hard to control. Amongst other things, this evolution has the effect of killing off the off-diagonal terms in the density matrix, and thus making our superposition states look more like classical mixtures.

The result we have just seen, that a particular type of noisy evolution appears to turn quantum superpositions into classical mixtures, turns out to be pretty general. This effect goes by the name of ‘decoherence’, and is an important part of the story of why we do not see quantum effects like interference in our everyday lives ( Zurek, 1991 ).

In the world of cognitive modeling, this effect is also likely to be significant. Quantum effects are generally not robust when states are allowed to interact with an environment, or when the evolution is otherwise not well controlled. This has two implications; first, it suggests that care might be needed to ensure cognitive states remain quantum during experimental manipulations. Failure to do so could mean no quantum effects are visible. Second, it suggests an explanation for why some cognitive variables do not show quantum effects, perhaps certain preferences/beliefs are just to hard to isolate, and the inevitable interaction between them and other thoughts quickly kills off any quantum behavior before it can be observed. This is a worthy subject for future research.

More generally, this shows how in some sense CP-maps allow one to move smoothly between pure quantum and pure classical Markov models. Evolution under open system dynamics may kill off quantum properties of a system, and the long time dynamics may be essentially classical.

Most of the variables we study in cognitive science are discrete, either by definition, as for a ‘yes’, ‘no’ question, or because having a finite number of response options for a variable such as preference strength is more practical than treating that variable as continuous. However there are some occasions where it might be more appropriate to work with truly continuous cognitive variables. First, it may be the case that we really have a variable that we think of as being continuous, for example preference strength. We could in principle allow a response via a slider or something else with a continuous set of response options, but even if we only allow participants a finite set of response options it may be more appropriate to treat the underlying variable as continuous. After all, it seems reasonable that changing the number of options on a Likert scale should not change the underlying variable being measured. Second, it is hard to construct simple quantum models of discrete variables when the variables can take a large number of values, simply because the Hamiltonian involves many parameters. An alternative is to start with a continuous variable and discretize, this can quickly lead to a sensible Hamiltonian for the discrete variable. A good recent example of this can be found in  Kvam, Pleskac, YU, and Busemeyer (2015) .

To cover these cases we need to develop a CP Map for the evolution of continuous variables that is both simple enough to be useful and is reasonably general. Thankfully this problem has already been considered in physics. The general setting is a quantum version of Brownian motion, in a high temperature limit. In physics this form of the master equation is difficult to motivate, although it has been considered in the literature on extremely fast neutron scattering processes (e.g.   Chatzidimitriou-Dreismann & Tietje, 2010 ). However in cognition it is less clear what sorts of couplings are ‘reasonable’, and so this sort of evolution might be viable in some cases.

In this section we have explored the idea of noisy evolutions in quantum cognition. The idea behind such evolutions is that interactions between the system under study and other cognitive variables can influence the evolution of the system we are interested in a profound way. We saw that the most general type of evolutions in quantum cognition are those that can be written in the form of a master equation of Lindblad form. We saw that these evolutions, also called CP-maps, may also be used to describe irreversible changes to the state, and the effect of presenting stimuli of unknown strength.

We also saw that we can use CP-maps to model irreversible evolutions, such as we might realistically come across in the lab. It is an open question whether the sort of models we have presented match data from real experiments, but the ideas seem promising.

Finally we noted that generally noisy evolutions have the effect of diminishing the ‘quantum-ness’ of a system. This might have implications for attempts to observe quantum effects in more complex cognitive variables. It might also explain why some such variables do not appear to show any quantum effects.

That concludes our whistle stop tour of some topics in modern quantum theory! Let us see if we can recap and draw out any major themes.

In the real world, or even the real lab, all cognitive processes involve some level of noise. We have discussed ways in which we can incorporate noise into quantum systems via the state, the measurements and the evolution. Along the way we also learned some practical lessons, such as how to compute the entropy of a state, how to model non-orthogonal measurements, and how to model evolution where we are unsure about the strength of a stimuli.

Overall we hope that the reader has learnt about some interesting tools and ideas that can be applied to modeling realistic cognitive systems using QT. We look forward to seeing these appear in future research.

Realistic experiments involve inhomogeneous groups of participants, experimental error and noise, and interactions between the variables we would like to study, and those we are less interested in. QT can be adapted to incorporate all of these things. The way is open therefore to a whole new level of modeling which aims to reproduce not just the average result, but also the statistical distribution of results for a given trial.

Having the tools to model realistic experiments also lets us think about taking QT out of the lab and into the real world. In particular having an idea about how the qualitative behavior of a quantum system differs when there is noise present should help us decide where QT might be applicable in the wild. The tools we have covered here can also be used to address other problems in quantum cognitive modeling, such as how to model non-orthogonal measurements.

Overall then, this tutorial will hopefully have some practical value for readers who wish to go on to use QT to model decision making. It should also prove useful to those simply reading research papers in quantum cognition in helping to decide whether modeling assumptions are reasonable or not.

We have not often mentioned this explicitly, but it is clear that most quantum features are robust against reasonable levels of noise. What we mean here is that the size of features such as interference effects or order effects are continuous in the parameter describing the strength of the noise. Therefore just the fact that there is  some  noise is not by itself enough to kill off all quantum behaviors.

However it is also very interesting to see how quantum features can break down when there is too much noise, and the description reverts to looking much like a classical one. This has important implications, both on a practical level, for the types of variables we can hope to see QT effects with, and on a more fundamental level, for our understanding of why some variables appear quantum, and some do not.

One thing we hope to have communicated in this tutorial is that exploring the effects of adding noise to a quantum system does more than simply teach us how to model careless participants. In some ways the real structure of QT is only revealed when we introduce density matrices, POVMs and CP-maps. One particularly important thing we learn is about the connection between classical and QT systems. Studying ideal QT systems might lead you to believe that they are diametrically opposed to classical ones, and the only overlap is for ideal cases such as sets of commuting operators. However once we learn about adding noise to a QT system, we can appreciate that classical systems are in some sense a special case of QT ones, and that it is even possible to dynamically transition between QT and classical dynamics. Of course, we have only scratched the surface of this subject, and we would caution against overgeneralizing from the simple cases presented here, but nevertheless we hope that through this tutorial you can begin to glimpse the possibility of a unified approach to decision making, that incorporates both the quantum and the classical. Now that would make an exciting research project….
