JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 22, 218-243 (1980)  Statistical Significance Tests for Binormal ROC Curves  CHARLES E. METZI AND HELEN B. KRONMAN  The University of Chicago  Statistical significance tests are derived and evaluated for measuring apparent differences between an obtained and an  expected binormal ROC curve, between two independent binormal ROC curves, and among groups of independent binormal ROC curves.  A binormal ROC curve is described by two parameters which represent the spread of the means and the ratio of the standard  deviations of the two underlying Gaussian decision variable distributions. To test the significance of apparent differences  between or among ROC curves, approximate Xa statistics for each of the three tests were constructed from maximum likelihood  estimates of the two parameters defining the binormal ROC curve. The performance of each test statistic was evaluated by  simulating five-category rating scale data with equal numbers of noise and signal-plus-noise trials (set at 50, 250, and 500)  for each of three typical ROC curves. For the significance test involving only one ROC curve, rating scale data were generated  from the chance diagonal of the ROC space also. Although test performance was found to be somewhat dependent on the number of  trials and on the location of the ROC curve in the ROC space, comparisons of the obtained and expected fractions of (falsely)  significant results at various a levels showed the proposed statistical significance tests to be reliable under practical  experimental conditions.  INTRODUCTION  Psychophysical investigations involving ROC analysis have lacked a generally useful method for comparing experimentally  different ROC curves. Over a decade ago, Gourevitch & Galanter (1967) proposed a statistical significance test for pairwise  comparisons of ROC curves based on the single parameter d' estimated from a single operating point. A few years later,  Marascuilo (1970) extended that test to compare a single ROC curve against chance and to perform multiple comparisons of three  or more ROC curves including an appropriate post-hoc procedure. An ROC curve that can be defined by the single parameter d' is  symmetric about the negative diagonal of the ROC space, implying that the variances of the underlying Gaussian noise (n) and  signal-plus-noise (s) distributions are equal. However, such symmetric ROC curves rarely are obtained experimentally,  especially in visual detection tasks. Thus a generally useful statistical significance test must allow the possibility of  underlying n and s distributions with unequal variances. The former can be described by the two parameters A and B, where A = (IL, - p,)/as and B = alas. This  model assumes that the observer uses a cutoff to test a sensory or decision variable that has arisen from one of the two  normal distributions. The resulting ROC curve graphs as a straight line with generally non-unit slope (equal to B) on normal  deviate axes and, therefore, does not exhibit monotonically decreasing slope on linear probability axes. Depending on the  sizes of A and B jointly (or the location of the curve in the ROC. space), the latter property often is not apparent. The  proper ROC curve model also can be described by the two parameters A and B. In this model, however, the likelihood ratio (or,  equivalently, the log likelihood ratio) obtained from knowledge of the underlying normal distributions is assumed to be used  as a decision variable. An ROC curve implied by this proper model exhibits monotonically decreasing slope on linear  probability axes and is concave-up, at least slightly, on normal deviate axes.  When binormal ROC curves must be described by two parameters, two approaches to statistical testing of apparent differences  are possible. In the first approach, a single index (such as de or the area beneath the ROC curve) is chosen to summarize each  twoparameter curve, and the significance of an apparent difference in this index is tested. However, because many distinct  binormal ROC curves can be described by any single summary index value, this approach does not address the question of whether  an apparent difference between two curves is significant. In the second approach, a test is constructed to determine the  significance of a difference between the pair of parameter estimates (A and B) that specifies one binormal ROC curve and the  pair that specifies another. Thus this second approach addresses the question of whether ROC curves-as opposed to incomplete  summary indices-are different.  In this paper we take the second approach and present statistical tests for the null hypotheses that a single set of rating  scale data arose from a postulated binormal ROC curve, or that two or more independent sets of rating scale data arose from  the same underlying curve. These tests are equally applicable, in principle, to the improper and proper binormal decision  models. The tests are evaluated here only for the improper ROC curve model, however, because this model is commonly employed  for the analysis of psychophysical detection performance data, and maximum likelihood parameter estimation procedures have  been developed already (Dorfman & Alf, 1968, 1969). Each of the statistical significance tests is based upon an assumption  that the joint probability density distributions of the maximum likelihood estimates of the ROC curve parameters, A and B, are  bivariate normally distributed. This assumption is justified by the fact that, under rather general conditions, maximum  likelihood estimates tend toward a multivariate normal distribution in the limit of large numbers of experimental trials (Rao,  1970). The performance of each significance test is evaluated in the perspective of results obtained from simulated rating  category data with the limited numbers of trials typically used in psychophysical and medical applications of ROC analysis.  480/22/3-5  220 METZ AND KRONMAN  The statistical tests described here for two or more measured ROC curves require that the rating scale data sets be  statistically independent. Thus these tests apply to psychophysical experiments in which independent sets of stimuli are used  and to medical imaging experiments (see Metz,1978; Swets,1979) in which clinical images for the various rating scale data sets  are made from different patients. The tests described here do not apply when different observers are presented with the same  stimuli, however, or in medical imaging experiments when two or more image sets are made from the same patients. To deal with  such situations, the present tests must be generalized to account for correlations between the estimates of the parameters of  one ROC curve and those of another.  Although the proposed tests can be used to evaluate the statistical significance of apparent differences between independent  binormal ROC curves, the ranking of curves found different may not be clearcut. This fact is important in establishing the validity  of the statistical significance tests proposed in this paper and is discussed more fully in a later section regarding  simulated data. If the null hypothesis is rejected,  then the investigator can use a post-hoc procedure to find out where the differences lie. With a post-hoc procedure, any one  of several null hypotheses can be used. EVALUATION OF THE SIGNIFICANCE TESTS  The statistical tests described above are derived on the basis of an assumption that the ROC curve parameter estimates A and B  are bivariate normally distributed. Because this assumption is known to be valid only in the limit of large numbers of  experimental trials, one must determine empirically the extent to which the test performs adequately for the numbers of trials  typically used in psychophysical and medical applications of ROC analysis. To perform this evaluation, a digital computer was  used to simulate sets of rating category data from an "improper" binormal decision model. The various statistical significance  tests were then applied to ROC curves estimated from these data, and observed performance of the tests was compared with ideal  performance. (1), (5), and (8) are valid. The  performance of each test depends on the number of trials and on the position of the ROC curve. In general, a number of trials  as small as 50 of each kind leads to less reliable significance test results. Even in this situation, however, performance of  the tests seems adequate for most applications. For a given number of trials, comparison of a pair of ROC curves is generally  more reliable than testing a single ROC curve against an assumed ROC curve.  If an ROC curve model were to require more than two parameters, Eqs. (1), (5), (8), and (10) could be extended easily from the  bivariate to the p-variate case. In such situations, expressions analogous to Eqs. (1), (5), and (8) would be used to compute  approximately X2 statistics with p degrees of freedom, and an expression analogous to Eq. (10) would provide an approximately  X2 statistic with p(k - 1) degrees of freedom.  Equations (5), (8), and (10) apply to ROC curves estimated from independent data sets. A need remains for statistical  significance tests that are applicable to correlated ROC curves. Such significance tests could be used in testing  intra-observer differences between experimental sessions involving the same set of stimuli and would be particularly important  to the clinician when evaluating two or more diagnostic modalities applied to a common set of patients.  If a statistically significant difference is found between two ROC curves, one may wish to characterize the difference and  perhaps to state which ROC curve is "better." Various approaches to these problems are possible. The approach that we consider  most consistent with the statistical tests described in this paper is based on a point of view that when two ROC curves are  compared and are found distinct because the null hypothesis is rejected, the maximum likelihood estimates of the individual  ROC curves provide the best available estimates of-and hence best characterize-the differences between the ROC curves. In  general, these differences will involve two dimensions, corresponding to the two parameters of a binormal ROC model.  If the ROC curves must be ranked, a single (perhaps composite) dimension must be chosen on which to order the curves. As a  first step, the ROC curves should be examined in the ROC space, preferably on normal deviate axes, which accentuate  differences between curves. If the ROC curves do not intersect in a region of the ROC space of practical concern (as might be  the case with curves 1 and 3 of Fig. 2, for example, if operating points at very small true positive and false positive  probabilities were of no practical importance), then the curves can be ranked meaningfully and unambiguously on the basis of  height in the relevant region of the ROC space. On the other hand, if the curves intersect in a region of the ROC space that  is of potential importance (such as curves 1 and 2 of Fig. 2), then an appropriate ranking of the curves is less clearcut. In  such situations one could select a subregion of the ROC space in which the curves do not intersect and rank the curves there  on the basis of height.  The statistical tests that we describe in this paper were developed specifically to compare ROC curves by avoiding the  necessity of summarizing each ROC curve by a single index, which cannot uniquely specify the curve. Thus when ROC curves are  found by one of  TESTS FOR ROC CURVES 243  these tests to be distinct, we recommend that the curves not be ranked on the basis of a single summary index such as de , the  area under the curve, or the true positive probability at a specified false positive probability. If a particular summary  index should be judged to provide an adequate basis for ranking performance in an applied detection task, then the tests  described here would be inappropriate, and the statistical significance of differences in the summary index should be  evaluated directly by an appropriate univariate test.
