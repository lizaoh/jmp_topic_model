MILLWARD  Brown University, Providence, Rhode Island 02912  Thirty subjects learned 120 two-dimensional, 2-valued concept-identification (CI) problems over 5 days. After each problem was learned, the solution was shifted by interchanging responses (reversal shift, RS) or by picking a new dimension (dimensional shift, DS). Two groups with complementary probabilities of the type of shift were run. The probabilities of an RS for one group on the 5 days were: 1, 0, .7, .7, .7. The shift was so introduced that the subject's first response indicated the type of shift expected. The subjects learned to predict the shift and showed a tendency to follow the probability othe shift but with a bias toward the RS prediction. Negative recency and alternation tendencies occurred, but seemed to adapt out. The subjects improved greatly in trials to solution for each CI problem. A generalization of the cue-selection model designed to handle initially correct concept selection did not adequately describe the statistic of the trial of the last error, but a restricted randomization procedure accounted for the major deviations. The error distributions were more accurately described. The parameter which measured the initial probability of selecting the correct concept on the first trial also indicated that subjects approached probability matching in their expectations of the type of shift.  This study is contrapuntal in form. The two themes are probability learning (PL) and concept identification (CI). A series of 120 simple two-dimensional, binary-valued CI problems were run. The response assignment of each problem was shifted after original learning by switching responses within the relevant dimension (a reversal shift, RS), or by switching to the other dimension (a dimensional shift, DS). For the last 72 problems, the choice of an RS or DS was made probabilistically. By introducing a forced-error trial to indicate the trial on which the response assignment was shifted, the subject's anticipation of the type of shift was measured. The response after the forced error can be interpreted as the subject's prediction about the solution to the second half of the problem.  1 This research was supported in part by grant GU-448 from the National Science Foundation and grant MH-11255-01 from the National Institutes of Mental Health. The relevant dimension is selected and then the response assignment is learned. The Kendlers use this interpretation to explain the difficulty that adults have with dimensional shifts (DS) in which the relevant dimension is changed after the original CI has been learned. The cue-selection model developed by Restle and by Bower and Trabasso also interprets CI in this way. Kendler and Woerner (1964) have shown that the response of noticing the relevant dimension, which might be called a perceptual mediating response, can be extinguished. The problem of interest in the study to be reported is whether the response of shifting the perceptual mediating response between the two dimensions can be probabilistically reinforced.  The following routine illustrates one approach to the above problem. Assume that the subject is initially required to assign responses to the stimuli according to the first column, where Dl is the relevant dimension. If a DS occurs, then either column 2 or column 3 may be chosen. The relevant dimension is then D2. The crucial aspect of the procedure used to test a subject's perceptual response is to (a) indicate clearly to him that the problem has a different solution, and (b) present him with a stimulus with alternative responses for the two possible shifts. His response can then be used to indicate his expectation concerning the new solution.  Consider the following example: Assume that the subject has learned to respond according to column 1, and then the experimenter changes the rule without telling  198 MILLWARD  the subject, so that column 4 is correct. Then stimulus Sl is presented. The subject will be wrong, so he knows by the instructions that the rule has changed. But he does not know whether the assignment is according to column 3 or column 4. Note that, at this point, column 2 is eliminated as a possible assignment, so there are only two assignments left, one RS and one DS. Stimulus S4 is not presented next, since it is the complement to S1 and has a response opposite to that of S1, regardless of which of the two assignments is in effect. Either S2 or S3 is presented, so the subject by his response may indicate whether or not he has shifted his perceptual response. If he has shifted his perceptual response and S2 is presented, then, since S2 has a different value from S 1 on dimension D2, he should make the response opposite to the correct response to S1. If S3 is presented, then, since S3 has the same value as S1 on dimension D2, he should make the same response as the correct response for S1. In a like manner, if he has not shifted his perceptual response, he should respond "2" and "1" for stimuli S2 and S3, respectively. Thus, the subject's response on test stimuli S2 or S3 indicates the state of his perceptual response.  The first time that such a shift is made, subjects will not necessarily indicate the state of their perceptual response because the introduction of such an error trial will no doubt be behaviorally disruptive. On the other hand, Kendler and Kendler (1962) have shown that adults do not change their perceptual response, which produces difficulty in learning a DS. However, with repeated problems, each with the same type of shift, subjects should "catch on" to the rule for shifting response assignments and make a corresponding perceptual shift. It seems reasonable to interpret a high proportion of correct responses on the test trials as indicative of the state of the perceptual response. One further point should be emphasized: Trials on the shift assignment must be continued until the subject solves the problem according to the new response assignment. The learning of the shift assignment constitutes the reinforcement for the shift response.  Of course, it is possible that subjects might follow some hypothesis isomorphic to a perceptual shift. For example, with an RS, the subject might simply have the hypothesis that "a 2 is a 1," or, with a DS, he might remember the error stimulus and simply learn that it and its complement are changed while the other two stimuli remain the same. Another possibility is that the subject would choose between "stay with color (form) dimension" or "switch from color (form) to form (color)." In this case, learning to stay or to switch might be learned independently for the two dimensions.  Of major interest in this experiment are the effects of randomly presenting DS and RS in a series of problems. Do subjects indicate perceptual shifts in proportion to their occurrence ? In their response sequences, do the stimulus dependencies observed in the ordinary PL experiment occur? There is no guarantee that the insertion of a CI problem between responses is sufficient to make the responses independent of previous stimuli.  REVERSAL AND DIMENSIONAL SHIFTS  199  METHOD  Subjects. The subjects were 30 seniors from Hope High School in Providence, R. 1. who were paid $8 apiece for their services. They were selected from the top half of their class.  Apparatus. The apparatus consisted of a 36 in. X 24 in. vertical panel with a sloping response panel on which were placed two event lights directly above two microswitch buttons. At eye level on the panel there was a one-way window for presentation of the stimulus. The experimenter randomly placed one of four stimulus cards in a light-tight box directly behind the one-way window. Then the experimenter pressed one of four buttons, depending on the stimulus number. This preprogrammed one of the two event lights, turned the light on in the box so that the stimulus was visible through the one-way glass, and set relays so that the subject's response would time the presentation of the event light. The subject's response turned on an indicator light (so that the experimenter could see what response was made) and started the timer which automatically turned off the stimulus, event, and indicator lights. To facilitate switching from one response assignment to another, the four buttons were duplicated four times, each row of four buttons corresponding to a particular response assignment (see Table 1). Thus, when the experimenter decided to switch assignments, it was necessary only to move to a different row of four buttons. This insured that there was no delay which might indicate to the subject that a shift had been made and no cues to indicate the nature of the shift made.  Stimulus material. Fifty different problems of four stimuli each were made by randomly selecting two out of eight shapes and two out of eight colors. The shapes were square, circle, triangle, dumbbell, cross, heart, arrow, and pentacle, and the tempera colors were red, orange, yellow, blue, green, violet, black, and brown.  Design. There were two groups of 15 subjects each. The two groups were identical except for the kind of shift on each problem, i.e., RS or DS. A complete problem consisted of learning an original assignment (Problem A) and a shift assignment (Problem B) with a given stimulus set. Each group had three sequences, one for every five subjects, in which the stimulus sets, the random order of the shifts for days when both shifts occurred, and the random order of the stimulus presentations within each problem were different. Twenty-four problems were run on each of five days (120 problems in all). On day 1, all shifts were the same, i.e., RS for the RS group and DS for the DS group. On day 2, all shifts were again the same but opposite from those on day 1. On days 3, 4, and 5, the RS group received RS shifts with probability .7, and the DS group received DS shifts with probability .7. Let 7T = the probability of a reversalshift problem. Then, the RS group had v = 1.0, 0.0, 0.7, 0.7, 0.7 for days 1-5, and the DS group had 1T = 0.0, 1.0, 0.3, 0.3, 0.3 for days 1-5. The reinforcement schedule was generated without restrictions.  Of the 50 different stimulus sets, 24 different ones were used on day 1, and 24 of the remaining 26 sets on day 2. For days 3 and 4, the random selection without replacement was repeated; for day 5, 24 sets were again selected randomly.  Procedure. The subjects were read the following instructions:  "You are to be a subject in a concept-learning experiment. Various colored shapes will be presented in the window in front of you. Each colored shape is assigned to one of the two buttons in front of you. There is a simple rule for pairing a button with a colored shape and you are to learn the rule. When a colored shape is presented, you are to press one of the buttons. Then the light over the correct button will come on. The colored shapes are organized into sets of four; each set of four stimuli is a problem. Only one colored shape will be presented on  200 MILLWARD  a trial, but the four stimuli will be repeated over and over until you learn the correct rule for assigning the buttons to the stimuli. For example, the shapes may be a house and a hat, and the colors may be red and blue. Thus, the four stimuli in a set would be: a red house, a blue house, a red hat, and a blue hat. The four colored shapes will be presented over and over again. However, at some time the rule will change. When it does, you will be wrong. You are then to search for the new rule.  "Now, there are two instructions which you must follow carefully. First, do not try to guess when the rule will change. It will change at different times, and if you guess you will make an error which will slow down the experiment. You are supposed to make the error because it tells you when the rule changes. Second, try to learn the new rule as soon as possible. In any case, try to make as few errors during the whole experiment as possible. The procedure for one problem, that is, learning the two rules, will be repeated for many problems where each problem is a different set of colored shapes. Each new set of colored shapes should be thought of as a completely new problem having no relation to the previous ones. Therefore, your first few responses in each new set will have to be guesses. You are to try to find the rule which determines the assignment of each colored shape to one of the two buttons as soon as you can. The basic procedure is: A colored shape will come on in the window. You are to press one of the two buttons. Then the light over the correct button will come on and a few seconds later another colored shape will be presented. Are there any questions ?"  Questions were answered by paraphrasing the instructions. On day 2, the same instructions were read again. On days 3, 4, and 5, the following instructions were read in addition to those above:  "Today the task becomes more difficult. Basically, as we just indicated, the task is the same but you will see that it is harder to get the second rule. Still, try to follow the instructions as before. We want to see if people can do as well under this more complicated task as in the simpler one you have already learned. Do your best."  The 24 problems of the day were then run without a break. The event light was on for one second and it took about one second to insert a new card and begin another trial. The subject was allowed all the time he wanted to respond. The initial assignment of a problem was run until the subject made no errors for five to ten trials. This variation was to ensure that the subject did not anticipate the trial when the assignment was changed. There was a tendency for this variable criterion to approach the minimum of five correct trials as the experiment continued and as the subjects learned quicker and did not anticipate the shift. The shift-learning task was also run until a criterion of five correct trials was reached. The four different stimuli were repeatedly randomized without replacement.  Finally, in an attempt to motivate subjects and maintain motivation throughout the experiment, subjects were given a bonus on each day in addition to their $1.25 per-hour pay. The bonus was randomly varied from subject to subject within these amounts: 10-20, 30-40, 10-20, 50-60, and 40-60 cents for days 1-5, respectively. The total pay for each subject, including bonus, always equalled $8. At the end of the experiment, subjects were asked to answer a set of questions designed to determine how well they had understood the problem.  PROBABILITY LEARNING RESULTS  ANTICIPATION OF THE SHIFT  A shift in the solution of the problem was indicated by a forced error. However, as problems continued, subjects might try to anticipate the trial of the shift. For the  REVERSAL AND DIMENSIONAL SHIFTS 201  first day, a fairly long and variable criterion was used to insure complete learning and to make anticipation of the shift very difficult. Of course, there is no way to tell whether failing to make an error on the forced-error trial was due to the fact that the problem was not learned or due to the subject's trying to anticipate the shift. However, the instructions to the subject not to anticipate the shift and the procedure of the first day seemed to be quite effective. In the first place, problems eventually were learned very rapidly (see the section on concept-identification learning), and second, the number of failures of errors on the forced-error trial was small. The average number of times for all 120 problems that subjects did not make an error on the forced-error trial for whatever reason was 3.27, with an SD of 2.66. There was no significant difference between the means of the RS and DS groups. A great many of the anticipations were made in the first few problems where presumably some subjects may not have learned problem A at the time of the shift. The largest number for any one subject was 10; 20 of the 30 subjects made 4 or fewer correct responses out of the 120 forced-error trials.  SUBJECTS' RESPONSES TO QUESTIONS AT THE END OF THE EXPERIMENT  In this experiment, an attempt was made to eliminate some of the obvious hypothesis behavior concerning sequences of events which exist in the simple PL task. When PL is continued over several days, there is the problem of the subjects' calculating the optimum strategy. Boredom and knowledge of the fact that the sequence is random are also considered important factors in PL. A set of questions was asked of each subject in order to ascertain as well as possible his conceptualization of what he had learned. The subjects wrote their answers and were given as much time as they desired.  The first question asked was whether they thought about the experiment when they went home. Eight said "no" and 22 said "yes." Most of the "yes" answers were concerned with the solution to the concept problem. A large number thought about the importance of the experiment!  The second question asked about their interest in the experiment and whether they became more or less interested as it went on. Only 3 subjects said they were not interested. Of the 27 who were interested, 7 said that they were less interested during the last few days, 5 were more interested, and 15 showed continuing interest.  The final two questions concerned their idea of what happened on each day and how they solved the problems. The answers were divided into four categories and coded "yes" for a statement indicating that they did understand that category, " ?" for an answer which may have indicated understanding but which was not perfectly clear, and "no" for an answer with no hint of understanding. Included in the "no" class are cases in which no answer was given pertinent to that category. The four categories were: (i) stating a rule concerning the solution of each problem, e.g.,  202 MILLWARD  attend to color (shape) only and learn which color (shape) corresponds to each button; (ii) stating reversal shift correctly, e.g., shift buttons for relevant dimension (color or shape), (iii) stating dimensional shift correctly, e.g., begin to attend to the other dimension (color or shape), and (iv) stating the fact that days 1 and 2 had consistent shift rules and days 3, 4, and 5 had mixed rules. For category (iv) to be coded "yes," the subject had to state categories (i), (ii), and (iii) correctly, but the other three categories were coded independently. The following four 3-tuples give the number of subjects saying "yes," "?," and "no" for the four categories: (20, 5, 5), (8, 1, 21), (7, 2, 21), (3, 5, 21). It is obvious that most subjects could verbalize how to solve each problem. Those who did it vaguely or did not do it at all probably had trouble expressing the idea. But few subjects verbalized the idea of reversal and dimensional shifts, and only 3 subjects noticed the mixing of these during the last 3 days.  These statistics are important in relation to the extent of learning that subjects showed in their prediction responses on the trial immediately after the forced-error trial. Any simple categorization of subjects' verbalizations is bound to be subjective; in making the assignments, I have tried to be lenient and to assume understanding in questionable cases. GENERALIZED CUE-SELECTION MODEL  The cue-selection model developed by Bower and Trabasso (1964) and Restle (1962) assumes that all subjects begin in the unconditioned state. This implies at least one error before learning. This assumption is justified on the basis of their data. In the present experiment, a large number of A problems were solved with no errors and a large number of B problems were solved with only the forced error. This is not surprising since, for A problems, only two dimensions are involved and initial cue selection may be correct. Similarly, better-than-chance selection of cues is possible with B problems because of the nature of the shifts. The cue-selection model is easily generalized to take into account correct initial cue selection and forced-error trials.  The cue-selection model and its more generalized version assume that stimuli are presented randomly on each trial. However, previous tests of the model involving more than two dimensions have used without-replacement randomization of the stimuli (Trabasso and Bower, 1966). In this experiment, a randomization scheme without replacement was used for A problems. This procedure requires special attention. In B problems, after the forced error, the complementary stimulus is not allowed; this also is not accounted for directly in the model. (More will be said later about these failures to satisfy all conditions of the model.) Because of the restricted randomization, the cue-selection model cannot be applied strictly as stated. Consider, for example, a subject with the wrong hypothesis. On trial 1, he guesses correctly or not and adjusts the response associated with the stimulus so that the response assignment is correct. An error on trial 1 does not imply resampling of cues since the first trial may only be a guess about response assignment and not about cues. On trial 2, the subject has a probability q of an error and p of a correct response. What values should these two parameters have ? Traditionally, they are assumed to be equal to .5 in a two-choice problem, but since one of the four stimuli has been used, these probabilities might be different. If the subject's hypothesis is related to the value on the irrelevant dimension, then the error probability is .67 because two of the three remaining stimuli will lead to errors. But we cannot be sure the subject has a hypothesis so simply related to the stimuli; therefore, to assume a probability of .67 is too restrictive. Furthermore, what happens if the subject is wrong on trial 2 ? To what extent his reselection of a hypothesis changes the probabilities on trials 3 and 4 (for the remaining two stimuli of the set of 4) is not known. In the light of the above difficulties, the best strategy is to estimate q as an approximation to the overall q value on all trials after the first trial. The main point relates to the controversy over whether a cognitive or a conditioning theory is appropriate for PL. Anderson (1964) and Restle (1961) have pointed out that, in the typical PL experiment, the previous sequences of events and responses are distinguishable and certainly act as stimuli. One interpretation of PL requires a theory which assumes that subjects have a set of "hypotheses" or response tendencies to specific aspects of the sequence of previous events. The frequently observed negative recency effect (Jarvik, 1951; Nicks, 1959) and alternation tendencies (Anderson, 1960) support such an interpretation. Another view treats these sequence effects as transitory and extrinsic to the automatic conditioning of the response, which is considered the appropriate theory (Estes, 1964). This point of view is supported by a number of studies. For example, Bogartz (1965) found that preschool children run in a PL situation did not show the negative recency effect or the response alternation typically found with older subjects. Many studies (e.g., Derks, 1962; Edwards, 1961) and in particular the Friedman, Burke, Cole, Keller, Millward, and Estes study (1964) have shown that these effects diminish with trials. The question seems to reduce to one of whether an "hypothesis-only" theory can handle all aspects of the results or whether "automatic conditioning" must be assumed as an underlying process. Assume that trials could be separated in time or presented in such a way that subjects did not "remember" the past sequence of reinforcing events. Then, the hypothesis-only theory would predict that PL would not occur while the automatic-conditioning theory would predict PL without such transitory effects as negative recency and response alternation. A primary purpose of this experiment was to separate trials in such a way that the memory for reinforcing events would be minimal. It was believed that one way to achieve this would be to require active responding on an unrelated task between prediction trials. In the present experiment, this unrelated task is a CI problem and its shift assignment. Of course, there was no guarantee that the procedure would be effective and, considering the results, one must conclude it was not. Presumably, the subjects had a memory trace for at least the past three or four shifts, even though their description of the experiment indicated that they had no clear, conscious strategy. The two effects which might indicate that the spacing was effective to some degree were (a) the rapid recovery from negative recency for the more frequently reinforced response and (b) the positive differences observed on day 5 for  REVERSAL AND DIMENSIONAL SHIFTS 221  the sequential statistics. Neither is pronounced enough to be definitive and could be due to the specifics of the design used.  The second point of interest about the PL task relates to extending the domain of PL. There are a number of studies which show that PL is a fairly ubiquitous phenomenon. The study by Binder and Feldman (1960) illustrates that subjects respond to ambiguous cues proportionally to their relative frequency of past reinforcement. Kanareff and Lanzetta (1960) studied the PL of an imitative response. Church, Millward, and Miller (1963) studied the PL of a subject's expectation that he would win or lose in a competitive reaction-time situation. Mandler and Cowan (1962) and Mandler, Cowan, and Gold (1964) found that PL occurred in a concept-identification task in which the relative frequency of the positive stimuli to the negative stimuli was 2 : 1. Before the concept was acquired, the subjects increased the proportion of positive: responses, reflecting the frequency of cases which were positive. In this study, the response of interest can be described as a verbal hypothesis, a mediated response, or a perceptual response. In particular, it is the subject's anticipation of an RS or DS shift in a CI task.  When the overall results are considered, it is indeed astounding how similar the results are to those of a typical PL situation. A probability-learning curve is obtained, negative recency is observed strongly at first and diminishing over trials, and an alternation tendency occurs. But there are also differences. Some of these may be due to the bias toward reversal shifts; others may not. The present experiment supports neither a cognitive nor a simple conditioning view of PL. In this respect it is like many other PL experiments.  Concept Identification. There have been few studies of CI with extended practice. The 120 problems and shift problems present a situation in which the possible increase in efficiency of learning could be investigated. An improvement in the rate of learning the CI problems took place, as one would expect. On day 5, after more than 100 problems, the initially selected hypothesis for each problem was nearly always between the two possible hypotheses for A problems, and showed a bias in favor of the more frequently correct shift for the B problems. The learning rate increased in a way which indicates that, when the current hypothesis was wrong, the alternative one was selected with a high probability.  Two serious procedural problems hindered the application of the cue-selection model. One was the restricted randomization. The second was the need to combine over subjects and problems for which the subjects had different learning rates. Thus, the poor description of the TLE statistic by the model could be due simply to the restricted randomization. The better fits for the NE statistic, which did not depend upon the q parameter, support this idea. And the poor description of both statistics on days 1 and 2 could be caused by the fact that problems with heterogeneous learning rates were combined.
