JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY: 1, ZS-S3 (1964)  Point Estimation in Learning Models  HELENA CHMURA KRAEMER'  Stanford University, Stanford, California  The problem of point estimation for several forms of the all-or-none learning models is examined. Possible estimation procedures leading to estimators satisfying small sample optimality criteria are presented, and asymptotic distribution theory for large sample estimators is described. Several approaches to the problem are presented, and, when possible, comparisons are made among the estimators obtained.  The tendency in the estimation of the parameters of learning models has been to use the most computationally simple, or the most intuitively appealing method available without particular consideration of the problem of seeking out estimators having optimal small sample properties or indeed of the investigation of the properties of the estimators being used. Maximum likelihood estimators, which, for the type of distributions encountered in such models, have desirable large sample properties, can often be improved upon for small sample size. The commonly used method of moments estimators are based on a choice of statistics which often seems arbitrary and is sometimes statistically unsound.  The purpose of the following work is to present, for a particular type of learning model already discussed in the literature, possible estimation procedures with particular consideration of small sample distribution theory for estimators satisfying small sample optimality criteria, and asymptotic distribution theory for "good" large sample estimators. For each of the models several estimation procedures will be discussed, and, when possible, comparisons will be made among the different estimators obtained.  This study concentrates on point estimators of the parameters; the author hopes to extend the present work to a study of interval estimation techniques at a later date. It may be noted that interval estimation procedures in many cases are implicit in the distribution theory presented here. THE ALL-OR-NONE MODEL  HYPOTHESES AND FORMS OF THE MODEL  The basic assumptions of the All-or-None models are two: (1) The subject, until he enters the conditioned state, in which he gives a correct response to the single stimulus element with probability one, has a certain probability of guessing the correct response; and (2) Following the reinforcement after each trial in the subject's unconditioned state, the subject has a given probability of entering the conditioned state.  There are several mathematical expressions of these assumptions. The model, which will be called the Simple Form, hypothesizes that the guessing probability in the unconditioned states is constant, and that the probability of entering the conditioned states after each trial in the unconditioned state is also constant.  Modification I of the All-or-None model retains the assumption of a constant probability of a correct response in the unconditioned state, but assumes that the probabilities of entering the conditioned state after a correct response and after an erroneous response are each constant but not necessarily equal.  Modification II hypothesizes two unconditioned states in each of which there is a constant guessing probability. From the first unconditioned state the subject may enter the second unconditioned state with a constant probability after each trial; from the second unconditioned state he may enter the conditioned state with fixed probability after each trial.  The experiments, on which estimation of parameters for these models is based, consist of observing s subjects each giving a sequence of responses which are classified as correct or incorrect. Thus the observation for subject j is a denumerable vector (x1; , x2; , ...) where xi; = 1 or 0 according as the jth subject's response on trial i is incorrect or correct.
