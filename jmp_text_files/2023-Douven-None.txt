Stopping rules in statistics are rules for determining when a scientist can or should stop collecting data and make an inference. These rules have been mostly discussed in the context of classical (frequentist) statistics, in particular in relation to null hypothesis significance testing (NHST). Yet recently, these rules have started to garner attention within Bayesian statistical circles as well. In the world of Bayesian statistics, the purpose of data collection is often to help concentrate the probability mass that, conditional on our current knowledge and in relation to our current purposes, is spread out too thinly across the relevant parameter space. 
                         1 
                      
                      
                         1 
                         The term “probability mass” is used loosely here, as it evokes a helpful image of some “stuff” (e.g., sand, or mud, or water; see  van Fraassen, 1989 , or  Politzer, 2016 ) being spread out in a space representing all relevant possibilities and that can be moved around in all sorts of ways. Strictly speaking, however, the term “probability mass” is reserved for discrete probability spaces; for continuous-valued parameters, the term “probability density” is used. 
                       A common suggestion is that data collection can cease when sufficient probability mass has condensed within a small enough region of that space.

The question of  how  concentrated probability should be before we can stop has, in the discussion so far, been understood in a purely quantitative way, focusing exclusively on the amount of probability to be found in a given region of parameter space. Taking our cue from the literature on acceptance rules in formal epistemology, we argue that qualitative aspects of a probability distribution may also carry important information as to whether stopping is warranted. As will be explained, a purely quantitative approach to stopping parallels a proposal in formal epistemology to the effect that a proposition is acceptable precisely if it is sufficiently probable. More recently, formal epistemologists have advanced acceptance rules that take a more structural look at probability assignments, by focusing on  relations  among probabilities rather than on the probability of a single proposition. We argue that, in Bayesian statistics, there can be benefits to similarly taking into account not simply how much probability is amassed in a region of interest but also  how  that probability is concentrated, in the sense of what the structure or shape of the distribution in the given region is. For instance, a sharp peak in a posterior distribution can sometimes provide a clear indication of the value of a parameter, even if, according to the quantitative proposal, it would be premature to stop. This paper works out the structural alternative and compares it with a purely quantitative stopping rule advanced in John Kruschke’s seminal book on Bayesian data analysis ( Kruschke, 2015 ). In addition, we discuss the Bayes factor approach to stopping, which has gained popularity in recent years but is not the central focus of this paper.

Kruschke compares his proposal with other stopping rules along two dimensions, viz., speed and accuracy. The former is important because we do not want to waste resources on experiments that are unlikely to have any further impact on our view, or delay implementing a policy or recommending a treatment if the experimental results clearly indicate that the policy/treatment will be beneficial; the latter (i.e., accuracy) is important because we do not want to stop too early, ending up with a mistaken view of the matter under study and consequently perhaps implementing policies or recommending treatments that are going to do more harm than good. The same dimensions are used in comparing our new proposal with the two alternatives mentioned above.

Section  2  describes present thinking on stopping in the Bayesian community, the main focus being on Kruschke’s contributions to this debate. Section  3  shows how applying to statistics some recent insights from the philosophical literature on rational acceptability suggests a structural Bayesian stopping rule that is interestingly different from the purely quantitative approach to stopping advocated by Kruschke. Using computer simulations, Section  4  compares this structural rule with Kruschke’s as well as with the Bayes factor approach in terms of speed and accuracy, finding that the three proposals make different speed–accuracy trade-offs. Because different contexts of use may call for different trade-offs, the conclusion of this paper will not be that one proposal can be declared the clear winner but rather that which rule is called for may depend on the context of use.

NHST has long been the dominant statistical practice in the empirical sciences. This methodology places the so-called null hypothesis front and center, where the null hypothesis is (roughly) the hypothesis that the intervention of interest (e.g., a medical treatment or the presentation of certain visual stimuli) has no effect, that whatever variability we may observe in the data is due to chance and so unrelated to the intervention. NHST allows us to reject this hypothesis only if, supposing the null hypothesis to hold, the probability of obtaining the data we have at hand, or data even “more extreme” than those, is less than some small value  
                         α 
                       (typically, 0.05 or 0.01). 
                         2 
                      
                      
                         2 
                         The meaning of “more extreme” depends on what the null hypothesis is. For example, if the null hypothesis states that a given statistic is not different from 0, then “more extreme” will mean any outcome that is further removed from 0 than the one we obtained.

In the context of this methodology, a lively debate has taken place about so-called stopping rules or criteria, which are intended to inform us about whether and, if so, when it is appropriate to stop gathering data and make an inference (in this case, either reject the null hypothesis or conclude that the data does not license its rejection). Statisticians agree that if NHST is our methodology of choice, then some such criterion is necessary. 
                         3 
                      
                      
                         3 
                         There has long been disagreement on what, in that case, the criterion should look like. Notable frequentist proposals include  Wald ’s ( 1947 ) Sequential Probability Ratio Test, which aims to minimize sample size by examining data points individually, updating the test statistic, and determining whether to continue sampling or stop based on pre-specified stopping boundaries related to Type I (false positive) and Type II (false negative) error rates;  Chernoff ’s ( 1959 ) proposal, which builds on information-theoretic concepts; and various decision-theoretic approaches (e.g.,  Ferguson, 1967; Shiryaev, 1978 ). In many fields, the frequentist consensus now leans toward calculating the stopping point using a power analysis ( Cohen, 1988 ) prior to conducting the experiment, preregistering the experiment with a clearly stated planned study size, and ceasing data collection once the required study size is reached. 
                       It is known (e.g.,  Anscombe, 1954 ) that if researchers can collect data indefinitely, they will be able to reject the null hypothesis almost surely (i.e., with probability 1, even if not out of logical necessity). This means, for example, that medical researchers would always be able to reject the hypothesis that their treatment has no effect on patients, even if it actually has none. Rejecting a true null hypothesis is known as a “Type I error”, and it is believed that, because of publication bias (journals’ favoring the acceptance of papers reporting statistically significant findings), such errors, commonly known as “false positives”, underlie many published results in the scientific literature (see, e.g.,  Ioannidis, 2005; Simmons, Nelson, & Simonsohn, 2011; Simonsohn, Nelson, & Simmons, 2014; Szucs & Ioannidis, 2017; Yu, Sprenger, Thomas, & Dougherty, 2014 ).

Over the past decade, the dominance of NHST has gradually given way to Bayesian statistics. While there are still computational challenges to overcome (running a Bayesian analysis can be prohibitively slow), proponents are hopeful that Bayesian statistics will become the standard methodology in the not-so-distant future because—they argue—in contrast to NHST, Bayesian statistics helps us to answer the question we are most fundamentally interested in, to wit, how probable the hypotheses under scrutiny are. 
                         4 
                      
                      
                         4 
                         According to Bayesians, their approach has many other virtues as well. See, for instance,  Bovens and Hartmann (2003), Lee and Wagenmakers (2013), Oaksford and Chater (2007) , and  Sprenger and Hartmann (2019) , though see  Douven (2022)  for critical discussion. 
                       Precisely because this statistics yields probabilities, it would seem that questions of when we can or should stop experimenting before we are possibly licensed to make an inference make no sense from a Bayesian perspective. As  Kruschke (2015, 2018)  argues, however, while Bayesians may like to avoid any black-and-white thinking, that is a luxury they cannot always afford. We are sometimes in a situation in which we have to decide one way or the other, or in which, rather than cite our probabilities, we are expected to assert categorically what we take to be the case. For example, a medical practitioner may need to decide which of two treatments to administer, even if she is not entirely certain which is more effective ( Kruschke, 2018 , p. 271). Or a government might need to make categorical claims to reach all its citizens, given that probabilistic information could be lost on a large part of the population.

From a Bayesian perspective, how do we determine when to stop collecting data? One suggestion is to apply decision theory: at every point in the data gathering process, recalculate whether continuing to sample has a greater expected utility than stopping, and act accordingly. But as  Kruschke (2015, p. 360)  notes, this is not always a realistic option, given that the information needed for an expected utility calculation can be difficult to obtain or even completely inaccessible in practice. Indeed, for many realistic decision problems, it can be exceedingly challenging to map out (let alone evaluate) all the intended and unintended consequences of each option in the choice set. Kruschke’s own proposal is to stop collecting data as soon as we are certain enough that the value of interest (e.g., the mean height of male adults in a population, the bias of a coin, the effect size of a new medical intervention, and so on) lies within a small enough region of the relevant parameter space, where “certain enough” and “small enough” are open to further specification. In his terminology, stopping is warranted as soon as, and no sooner than, the estimate or estimates of interest are sufficiently  precise .

This example is just to bring across the intuition that there are qualitative aspects of probability distributions—like perhaps their shape—that may matter to whether or not we can or should stop collecting data. But intuition is not to  decide  which stopping rule we are to use. How  are  we to choose such a rule? To answer this question, we must first say more about how we are to proceed after stopping.

Kruschke’s stopping rule does not in itself imply anything about what inferences can or are to be made at stopping time, if any can be made. He discusses a number of options, and the one that appears to do best in light of the outcomes of simulations he reports (to be discussed later) is (i) to accept a null hypothesis (or indeed any hypothesis of interest) if the 95 percent HDI (which is now smaller than  
                         ρ 
                      , given that, by assumption, the stopping point has been reached) lies in its entirety within the ROPE, which is the region we take to be compatible with the truth of the hypothesis; (ii) to reject the null hypothesis if the 95 percent HDI lies outside the ROPE; and (iii) to declare the matter undecided if the 95 percent HDI partially overlaps with the ROPE. So, for instance, the left panel of  Fig. 1  depicts a situation in which, according to Kruschke’s proposal, we are licensed to stop and infer the null.

Precision, whether understood in the purely quantitative manner of Kruschke or in the more qualitative manner to be put forward in this paper, does not guarantee  accuracy , in that a precise parameter estimate may be very far off from the parameter’s true value. For example, we may have obtained a precise yet entirely inaccurate estimate by updating on a slew of misleading data, which in turn might lead us to make an incorrect inference (e.g., reject a true null hypothesis). Nevertheless, aiming for precision, specified in a certain way, may still be the best thing we can do to increase the probability of making correct inferences and decrease the probability of making incorrect ones. Whether this is so may depend on how we define “precision”, which is why we should compare Kruschke’s proposal and the alternative to be presented with regard to their tendency to lead to correct inferences and avoid incorrect ones.

This should not be the only dimension of comparison, however. Often, we do not just want to make the right call, we want to make that call sooner rather than later. Why waste time and money on recruiting further participants if we can make the right call already on the basis of the data we have collected so far? Why postpone introducing a new treatment if the data already strongly support its efficacy? So, speed (or, more generally, minimization of study size) should be a dimension of comparison as well: in comparing different stopping rules, in particular also in comparing different stopping rules based on different conceptions of precision, we should attend not only to whether those rules lead reliably to correct inferences but also to how fast they do so (provided they do so to begin with).

However, speed and accuracy can come at each other’s expense. It has been shown that, time and financial resources permitting, gathering more data will, in most common situations, make probabilities converge to truth values almost surely ( Gaifman & Snir, 1982 ). The problem is that, in daily practice, time and financial resources tend to be all too limited. So, we should at least sometimes be prepared to trade off accuracy against speed.

In the remainder, we compare Kruschke’s proposal with an alternative that pays heed to structural features of a posterior distribution as well as with the previously mentioned Bayes factor approach. It will be shown that the proposals may lead to different speed–accuracy trade-offs so that, given that different contexts may call for different such trade-offs and thus for different stopping rules, there may be a place for all three proposals, albeit in different contexts.

Kruschke’s rule, stated in the previous section, takes as input probabilistic information and yields on that basis a categorical verdict (e.g., to stop experimenting and reject the null hypothesis). In that respect, it is akin to what philosophers have discussed under the heading of “acceptance rules”, which are bridge principles intended to connect our degrees of belief (or subjective probabilities) with our categorical (i.e., outright) beliefs.

The best-known such principle, commonly referred to as “Locke’s rule”, posits that it is rational for a person to accept a proposition if, and only if, their degree of belief in that proposition is above a certain threshold, typically taken close but not equal to 1. This rule is known to result in paradoxes when combined with seemingly plausible additional rationality principles ( Foley, 1992; Kaplan, 1981; Kyburg, 1961, 1990; Makinson, 1965; Moser & Tlumak, 1985 ), prompting philosophers to propose alternative rules.

One such alternative that has recently gained attention is the odds-ratio rule proposed by  Lin and Kelly (2012a, 2012b) . According to this rule, a proposition is acceptable if, and only if, it follows logically from the strongest acceptable proposition, which is defined as the set of all possibilities (or possible worlds, as philosophers often say) minus those with negligible probability relative to the most likely possibility. 
                         8 
                      
                      
                         8 
                         Another acceptance rule that has been discussed in the recent philosophical literature is  Leitgeb ’s ( 2014 ) Humean rule, but this has been shown to face serious difficulties; see  Douven and Rott (2018), Rott (2017) , and  Schurz (2019) .

We will not delve into how the odds-ratio rule addresses the issues associated with Locke’s rule. Instead, we highlight the former rule because it inspired the alternative to Kruschke’s stopping rule that we will present. To understand the connection, note that Locke’s rule, when expressed in terms of possibilities or possible worlds, only considers the possibilities that constitute a given proposition (e.g., for the proposition that snow is white, it considers all and only the possible worlds in which snow is in fact white) and asks whether, collectively, they represent a large enough part of the total probability mass. In contrast, the odds-ratio rule examines  relative  probabilities, defining the strongest acceptable proposition in terms of an elimination of those possibilities that are improbable  relative to the most probable world . Thus, when determining whether a given proposition is acceptable, the  entire  probability distribution must be taken into account, not just the probabilities attributed to the worlds constituting that proposition.

Accepting a proposition can be thought of as marking the end of inquiries into whether that proposition is true ( Harman, 1986 , pp. 47–49;  Huang, 2022; Nagel, 2010 ). In this light, Locke’s rule is conceptually close to Kruschke’s proposal: the former allows us to stop inquiring as soon as enough probability mass has accumulated for a specific hypothesis, while the latter permits stopping when enough probability mass is concentrated in a small enough region of parameter space (where “enough” can be contextually defined). To develop a Bayesian stopping rule that parallels the odds-ratio rule in a manner similar to how Kruschke’s rule parallels Locke’s, we must first consider that, given a continuous parameter space, there are nondenumerably many possibilities to take into account. For a probability distribution on these possibilities, we can identify the one or ones that have the highest density (the mode or modes) and we can compare the density of any other possibility with that of the mode(s). Consequently, the stopping rule most closely paralleling the odds-ratio rule for continuous spaces employs the “Relative Importance Interval” (RII), rather than the Highest Density Interval used in Kruschke’s proposal. In the following, we take Kruschke’s assessment of the frequentist stopping rule as well as of the HDI–ROPE without precision proposal for granted. Using the same coin tossing model that Kruschke used and also the same methodology of computer simulations, we compare his proposal with our new one. We do include stopping on the basis of Bayes factors in the comparison, given that many currently favor this stopping rule. For instance,  Rouder (2014)  used simulations to advocate for the use of optional stopping based on Bayes factors in the context of null hypothesis Bayesian testing.  Linde, Tendeiro, Selker, Wagenmaker, and van Ravenzwaaij (2021)  also argue for a Bayes factor approach, based on a comparison of a frequentist procedure, an HDI-based approach, and the Bayes factor interval null procedure aimed at examining each method’s classification performance in discerning statistical equivalence. (See also  van Ravenzwaaij & Wagenmakers, 2022; Rouder, Haaf, & Vandekerckhove, 2018; Schönbrodt & Wagenmakers, 2018; Tendeiro, Kiers, & van Ravenzwaaij, 2022; Wagenmakers, Wetzels, Borsboom, van der Maas, & Kievit, 2012; Wang & Gelfand, 2002 ; for discussion, see  de Heide & Grünwald, 2021 , and  Tendeiro & Kiers, 2019, 2021 .)

First, and just as Kruschke did, we compare the average behavior of the rules by running 1000 simulations of sequences of 1500 coin tosses for a fair coin and another 1000 such simulations for a coin with a bias of 0.65, the rest of the setup also being exactly the same as in Kruschke’s work, except that we only compare three criteria, the one based on Bayes factors, Kruschke’s proposal, and the one put forward in this paper, which is like Kruschke’s except that it relies on the RII instead of on the HDI. Then we generalize the simulations by picking biases randomly.

Indeed, the thresholds have different meanings for the three criteria. In Kruschke’s, the threshold indicates how much of the probability mass must be concentrated within the ROPE before we can stop, while in our criterion, it serves to determine when a possibility’s density is low enough compared to that of the mode that we can exclude it from the RII. This inevitably limits the value of the kind of direct comparisons made above and in Kruschke’s work. However, the results from the new simulations, reported in  Table 1 , provide a good impression of how the criteria differ in the way they trade off speed and accuracy against each other. Specifically, the table reports, for each of the three criteria and for the threshold values in the specified ranges, (i) the percentage of simulations in which the criterion led to the correct decision; (ii) the score, as measured by the squared distance between the LMS estimate at stopping time in a simulation and the bias selected in that same simulation; and (iii) the average number of updates needed until a hypothesis is accepted, with both (i) and (ii) operationalizing, in different ways, accuracy.

                            There could be still other factors contributing to the context-sensitivity of the question of which stopping rule to use. The conclusion we have reached aligns with a broader line of thinking about rational action and belief management, as advanced by, among others,  Elqayam (2011, 2012), Gigerenzer (2000, 2001) , and  Schurz and Hertwig (2019) . These authors argue that the age-old philosophical quest for universal, all-encompassing principles of rationality is misguided. Instead, they propose an ecological conception of rationality, which takes into account the resources available to an individual, as well as the constraints and complexities of their environment, when determining whether that person acted rationally (where the action can be purely cognitive). In line with this perspective, some argue that whether we should update our subjective probabilities via Bayes’ rule (as assumed in our simulations) or via some other probabilistic rule can only be determined within a specific context ( Douven, 2020a, 2022; Douven & Wenmackers, 2017 ). Similarly, it has been argued that assessing the accuracy of subjective probabilities (which we effectively did using the Brier score above) has no general answer but depends on factors such as what is at stake in a context ( Douven, 2020b, 2021, 2022 , Ch. 5). Our simulation results suggest that an ecological view of rationality naturally extends to the question of when it is rational to stop collecting data.

                            12 
                            While  Linde et al. ’s ( 2021 ) final recommendation is in favor of the Bayes factor approach, the simulation results they report are actually consistent with an ecological view on stopping. As mentioned in the previous section, these authors compare the Bayes factor approach to both a frequentist approach to stopping and an HDI-based approach. The comparison shows that the different methods exhibit varying strengths and weaknesses, depending on context-specific factors, such as the available sample size.

Kruschke’s Bayesian approach to stopping primarily focuses on the notion of precision, understood in terms of the Highest Density Interval (HDI). This stopping criterion is strictly quantitative, as it only considers the concentration of probability within a specific region of the parameter space. The present paper was driven by the intuition that, at times, the distribution of probability might provide insights on whether stopping is warranted.

We found inspiration for an explanation of precision that aligns with this intuition in a recent debate in formal epistemology concerning acceptance rules. While the simplest of these rules is purely quantitative, making the probability assigned to a proposition decisive for categorical belief, a recent alternative establishes a structural connection between categorical beliefs and subjective probabilities. In this paper, we proposed a structural alternative to Kruschke’s stopping rule, more akin to the older acceptance rule, by introducing a concept related to, but distinct from, the HDI: the Relative Importance Interval (RII). The RII also considers the shape of the density, emphasizing possibilities that achieve a sufficiently important level of density relative to the mode’s density.

Through computer simulations, we compared our proposal with Kruschke’s and the popular Bayes factor-based stopping approach. The simulation results suggest that if accuracy is the sole concern, our proposal might be preferable, assuming a high threshold value. However, accuracy is seldom the only concern in practice. Consequently, the more general conclusion from the simulations is that the choice between basing stopping on the Bayes factor criterion, Kruschke’s, or our proposal (and their respective thresholds) may depend on context, which typically dictates the relative importance of speed and accuracy. This calls for an ecological approach to stopping, rather than declaring the universal superiority of any specific stopping rule.
