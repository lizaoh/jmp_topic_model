A Neural Timing Model of Visual Threshold 

The Johns Hopkins University 

A theory of visual threshold-versus-intensity curves is presented. The theory is based on a model of spike generation by idealized visual neurons. The spike generation model relates the interarrival time statistics of the neural spikes to the statistics of weighted photon summation within the receptive field of the spiking neuron. The spike generation model is combined with the psychophysical assumption that the observer monitors a neuron which is ideally matched to the target in both scale and location. The resulting model specifies how the interarrival time distribution depends on the incremental flash intensity, area, and duration of the threshold stimulus and on the background luminance. A further psychophysical assumption is then added to the model: that the observer uses a neural timing model in order to discriminate test trials from blanks. On the basis of the complete model, a theory of threshold-versus-intensity functions is derived. The model observer exhibits threshold behavior that mimics many aspects of the threshold judgments of human observers. In particular, the theoretical threshold functions exhibit: (1) at low background levels, absolute thresholds which depend inversely on test area and duration; (2) at moderate background levels, de Vries�Rose behavior if and only if the test flash is sufficiently small and brief; and (3) in the limit of high background levels, asymptotic Weber behavior. The theory also accounts for changes in the shape of the threshold-versus-intensity curve as test flash area (or spatial frequency) and duration are altered. It is argued that empirical failures to find Weber behavior at high backgrounds under some stimulus conditions (high spatial frequency or small targets, brief targets) may be due to the occurrence of photoreceptor saturation at background levels that are below the range in which Weber behavior would be predicted by the (nonsaturating) model presented here. ] 1996 Academic Press, Inc.

The model neuron produces a random spike train with inverse Gaussian interarrival times. If the Gaussian increments are independent and identically distributed, the interarrival times form a renewal process (see Karlin 6 Taylor, 1975, Chapter 5 for a discussion of renewal processes). 

This idealization of spike generation by a real neuron is referred to in the theoretical neurobiology literature as a stochastic integrate-and-fire neuron (Glass 6 Mackey, 1988; Tuckwell, 1989). The model was originally introduced about 30 years ago (Gerstein 6 Mandelbrot, 1964). Throughout the 1960s and 1970s, a large modeling literature developed based on this model and elaborations of it. The goal of many of these papers was to increase the realism of the model (for example, by adding a neural membrane decay or a refractory period) and then to find analytic expressions for quantities of interest (such as the distribution of the first spiking time). In general, it was found that even minor improvements in the realism of the model considerably complicated the analysis of its behavior (see Tuckwell, 1989). For this reason��and because the simple version of the model just described in many cases models the distribution of spike interarrival times of an actual spiking neuron with reasonable accuracy��the simple integrateand-fire model is often assumed to be an adequate mathematical model of the statistical spiking behavior of real neurons (e.g., Glass 6 Mackey, 1988). For modeling purposes, the integrate-and-fire model of a neuron is often a reasonable compromise between physiological realism and mathematical tractability. 

In this paper, the integrate-and-fire model will not be used to fit neural data, but rather to investigate the theoretical implications of spike generation in early visual neurons for theories of visual threshold. To accomplish this goal, a theory of how the Gaussian random input to the integrate-and-fire model depends on the light stimulus must be constructed. The approach taken here is to assume that the Gaussian random input to the neuron is produced by weighting quantal photoreceptor events according to the value of the receptive field weighting function at the location of each event, then spatially summing over the receptive field. The photoreceptor events are assumed to be due to an additive combination of stereotypical photon absorption-triggered isomerizations and thermal isomerizations (Fein 6 Szuts, 1982; Lamb, 1990). 

The plan of this paper is to build a model of how the photoreceptor events lead to the generation of spikes in an integrate-and-fire neuron which pools the outputs of many photoreceptor units and then to ask what an observer who samples these spikes could know about the nature of the visual stimulus. I will specifically examine how this model observer's visual threshold behavior depends on various stimulus parameters: intensity, size, and duration. 

One of the basic assumptions of the theory is that the neural code which the observer accesses in order to make judgments about the nature of the visual stimulus is essentially statistical in nature. The statistical ``noise'' in the model is due both to random thermal noise in the neural elements which convey the message (noise in the sensory communication channel) and, more importantly, to the

Quantal Fluctuation Limitations on Threshold Visual Performance 

For an incandescent illumination source, the number of photons absorbed by the retina within a small area and a small time interval is a Poisson random variable (Saleh 6 Teich, 1991). The Poisson distribution is characterized by a single parameter which specifies both the mean and the variance of the distribution. In the limit as this parameter becomes large, the Poisson distribution approaches a Gaussian distribution with equal mean and variance. Therefore, the number of photons collected over a sufficiently large retinal area and temporal duration��or even within a small area and brief duration if the stimulus is sufficiently intense��will be approximately Gaussian-distributed. Furthermore, by the central limit theorem, the distribution of the means of a large number of photon counts of any size will be approximately Gaussian. 

Half a century ago, Rose (1942; 1948) and de Vries (1943) independently suggested that trial-to-trial fluctuations in the retinal photon count might be the limiting factor for incremental flash detection in visual threshold experiments. Rose and de Vries showed that the detection of a low contrast patch of light which is superimposed on a uniform field can be modeled as a statistical discrimination task in which an observer would ultimately be limited (in the absence of any internal noise) by the random statistical fluctuations in the number of photons absorbed by the eye. 

Rose and de Vries modeled the observer in a threshold experiment as a statistician who counts photons in order to attempt to discriminate trials in which a test flash is presented on a uniform background field from ``blank'' trials, in which only the background is presented. When the thresholds of spatial frequency gratings are measured, effects of spatial frequency closely parallel those of test flash area: high spatial frequency targets tend to produce de Vries�Rose-like t.v.i. curves, whereas long duration, low spatial frequency targets tend to produce Weber-like t.v.i. curves (van Nas 6 Bouman, 1967; van Nas, Koenderinck, Nas 6 Bouman, 1967; Kelly, 1972). 

In summary, the shapes of t.v.i. curves are very sensitive to changes in the spatial and temporal parameters of the test flash. To account for these effects alone is a considerable challenge for any model of visual threshold. 

Effects of varying the wavelengths of the target and background on the slope of the t.v.i. curve are complex (Sharpe et al., 1993) and will not be addressed by the theory presented here, which primarily addresses the effects of spatiotemporal pooling of quantal signals. Wavelength effects are generally thought to be due to the relative dominance of rod-mediated and cone-mediated detection. Evidence suggests that the two systems may not be independent, but probably interact in complex ways under some conditions (Blick 6 MacLeod, 1978; Buck 6 Makous, 1981; Buck, Peeples, 6 Makous, 1979; Sharpe et al., 1993). The distinction between rod and cone systems, and the nature of their interactions, will not be emphasized in the present model. A justification for ignoring this distinction in accounting for area and duration effects is given later in the paper. 

Barlow's Thermal Noise Account of Absolute Threshold 

The fact that the de Vries�Rose law is observed psychophysically under some stimulus conditions has been taken by many investigators to be strong evidence for a role of quantum statistics in visual coding under these same conditions. However, since the de Vries�Rose law holds only under a restricted set of stimulus conditions, it is clear that quantum fluctuations can be only part of the visual threshold story. I will therefore briefly discuss factors which are thought to limit visibility in other regions of the t.v.i. curve. 

At low background levels, it is generally believed that noise which is internal to the observer limits visibility. The influence of additive internal noise on the detection of brief flashes was considered in the 1950s by Barlow (1957, 1958). Barlow specifically assumed the dominant source of additive internal noise to be spontaneous thermal isomerizations at the level of the photoreceptors.

Visual Coding in the Weber Range 

The de Vries�Rose�Barlow (DRB) model successfully accounts for the slopes of t.v.i. curves in the absolute threshold and de Vries�Rose regions. As a realistic account of threshold judgments, however, the model has two major shortcomings. First, it is obvious that the observer in a psychophysical experiment does not literally count photons in order to make his or her perceptual judgments. Presumably, the observer's judgments are based on an all-or-none impulse code by which the information about the stimulus is transmitted to the brain. In order to understand how cortical signals could be influenced by photon fluctuations and spontaneous isomerizations in the photoreceptors, a theory which specifies how the photoreceptor output is translated into a neural spike code is required. 

A second shortcoming of the DRB model is that it fails to account for regions of the t.v.i. curve in which the slope is greater than[1] 2[. In particular, it fails to account for Weber or] approximate-Weber behavior at high background levels. It therefore also cannot account for the fact that Weber behavior is more likely to be observed with large (or low spatial frequency), long duration targets and de Vries�Rose behavior with small (or high-frequency), brief targets. 

The search for the mechanism underlying Weber's law has mainly focused on a possible role for deterministic physiological gain control in setting visual threshold, rather than the role of either photon or thermal fluctuations (for an excellent review of the physiological gain control literature to 1984, see Shapley 6 Enroth-Cugell, 1984). Changes in the observer's signal-to-noise ratio as a function of background level can obviously occur either as a result of changes in the noise level, changes in the signal level, or both. The DRB model accounts for the threshold increase in the square-root law region on the basis of changes in the noise level. Most models of Weber's law make the opposite assumption: that the effect of changing the background level 

is due to a change in the gain of the retina which modulates the level of the signal, rather than to a change in the noise level. 

A Weber-gain mechanism has the effect of multiplying the photon count difference signal 2I by a gain factor which is proportional to I[&1] B[. This background-modulated gain con-] trol thus results in a physiological output signal which is proportional to the Weber ratio 2I�IB. An implicit assumption of models which account for background dependence of visual threshold on the basis of such gain control is that the noise in the observer's criterion variable is independent of the background level within the Weber range of the t.v.i. curve.

Whether primate cones exhibit Weber adaptation is not yet known. 

The retina of Bufo marinus is an example of a neural pooling network in which the light adaptation properties of individual neural subunits are understood in some detail (Donner et al., 1990). The red rods of Bufo marinus undergo a Weber adaptation at a background intensity which induces about ten isomerizations per rod-second (see Fig. 3). As illustrated in Fig. 3, this rod adaptation appears to be reflected in the behavior of various retinal cells which receive input from the rods. The t.v.i. curves obtained from horizontal and ganglion cells make a transition to a Weber region at about the same background at which the rods begin to adapt. 

The toad ganglion cell t.v.i. curve bears a striking resemblance to the human psychophysical t.v.i. curve displayed in Fig. 2. Like the psychophysical curve, the ganglion cell threshold curve possesses absolute threshold, de Vries�Rose, and Weber segments. The similarity between the human 

psychophysical data and ganglion cell adaptation data in several species has led many investigators to suspect that the physiological processes underlying psychophysical threshold performance are retinal in origin. 

Donner, Copenhagen, and Reuter (1990) proposed a black box model of the retinal mechanisms responsible for light adaptation in the optic nerve signal of Bufo marinus. In their model, two separate adaptation mechanisms contribute to the threshold performance of the ganglion cells. An early Weber adaptation occurs in the rods at and above a background level of about ten isomerizations per rod-s. A second gain control mechanism, which depends on a pooled rod signal, begins to modulate the gain of the ganglion cells at a background level that is about three log units lower than the background level at which the rods begin to exhibit Weber adaptation. 

This second adaptation mechanism is also assumed to operate via multiplicative gain control; however, the gain of the second mechanism is assumed to be inversely proportional to the standard deviation of the statistical fluctuations of the pooled photoreceptor outputs, rather than to the average local light intensity. Donner, Copenhagen, and Reuter refer to this second adaptation mechanism as a ``noise gain'' box. The model implies that, at background levels which yield de Vries�Rose behavior, the noise gain mechanism operates directly on a quantal rod signal which

closely mirrors the individual photon absorptions and is not itself subject to a photoreceptor-level gain control. A schematic diagram of the Donner�Copenhagen�Reuter model is shown in Fig. 4. 

Rudd and Brown (1993; 1994; 1995; in press) have demonstrated that the simple spike generation model proposed here (i.e., the quantal absorption-summing integrate-and-fire neuron described above) can account for many aspects of light adaptation in retinal ganglion cells of the toad and cat, including the existence of absolute threshold and de Vries�Rose threshold curve segments. We have further shown, through Monte Carlo simulations, that our model produces a noise-gain control of the type envisioned by Donner, Copenhagen, and Reuter. Photon fluctuations influence the spiking rate of our model cell by reducing the gain of the optic nerve signal, rather than by increasing the spiking rate noise. This is also a property of cat ganglion cells (Barlow 6 Levick, 1969; Reich, SanchezVives, Mukherjee, 6 Kaplan, 1994). The integrate-and-fire neuron exhibits this noise-gain control despite the fact that no explicit gain control is built into our spike generation model. 

We have also demonstrated that the output of the integrate-and-fire neuron can account for all three segments of the ganglion cell t.v.i. curve, provided that a local Weber adaptation is assumed to occur at the level of the photoreceptors prior to the spatial pooling carried out by the ganglion cell. This account is essentially an instantiation of the two-stage retinal adaptation model of Donner, Copenhagen, and Reuter, in which our integrate-and-fire mechanism accounts for the noise-gain stage. 

In the present paper, an alternative account of Weber's law is given. No photoreceptor adaptation is assumed, and Weber's law is instead accounted for on the basis of a psychophysical neural timing hypothesis. The photoreceptors are treated as transmitters of stereotyped quantal signals which are pooled by integrate-and-fire neurons whose spike trains the observer monitors in order to make decisions regarding the nature of the stimulus pattern. Thus, the integrate-and-fire neurons serve the role of visual ``channels'' in the psychophysical threshold model. Since spatial pooling of both rod and cones signals is known to occur in the retina, the present model could potentially be used to model detection based on a pooling of either rod or cone signals��or both. The analysis presented here should thus be thought of as complementary to investigations of the contributions of specific photoreceptors to visual threshold. The main theoretical contribution of the paper is the demonstration that a shift from de Vries�Rose to Weber behavior at high background levels emerges naturally from the assumption of a neural timing model, without the need to assume the existence of an explicit Weber gain mechanism at any level of the visual system. Under the assumptions of the timing model, Weber's law results 

from a combination of photon noise-limited detection and stimulus intensity- and scale-modulated changes in the observer's integration time. These changes occur because the spiking rates of the integrate-and-fire units depend on incremental photon flux. 

Thus, a second viable theory of psychophysical t.v.i. curves��which is also based on the integrate-and fire spike generation mechanism��is presented here. Although a twostage retinal gain control model��such as the one that Donner et al. proposed to account for the threshold behavior of toad ganglion cells��is sufficient to account for the shapes of human t.v.i. curves, the present theory has the added advantage of simultaneously (although imperfectly) accounting for the observed intensity dependence of two common psychophysical measures of visual integration time: the critical duration below which Bloch's law holds (Raab, 1962; Stevens 6 Hall, 1966) and simple reaction time (Pieron, 1914; Vaughan, Costa, 6 Gilden, 1966; Mansfield, 1973; Burkhardt, Gottesman, 6 Keenan, 1987). Because the interpretation of these integration time measures in terms of the timing model involves some subtleties, and the goodness of fit between the predictions of the timing model and psychophysical evidence regarding visual integration time is apt to be clearer after the formal model has been presented, further discussion of evidence favoring the timing model over the two-stage gain control model is reserved for the Discussion. 

In summary, the role of photoreceptor function in determining psychophysical Weber behavior is controversial. By simulating a model of the toad retina, we have previously shown that an integrate-and-fire ganglion cell preceded by photoreceptors possessing a Weber gain mechanism is sufficient to account for the basic properties of t.v.i. curves. In the present paper, I am exploring an alternative account of Weber's law, which is not based on Weber-gain control in the photoreceptors and for which there is some independent evidence based on psychophysical measures of visual integration time. 

The mathematical formalism introduced here to characterize the stimulus transduction properties of the integrateand-fire ``channels'' which the psychophysical observer monitors can, in principle, be applied to any class of early visual neurons that elicit all-or-none impulses and possess linear receptive field weighting functions. Since visual neurons possessing linear receptive fields exist at several neural levels within the visual processing hierarchy, and I do not know where to place the ``observer'' in this hierarchical chain, I remain agnostic concerning the specific identity of the neurons which mediate visual detection.

2. SPIKE GENERATION MODEL 

Photon input 

Let I(x, y, t) be the intensity profile of the proximal stimulus, consisting of a delta function[1] at the location of each photon absorption. The units of I(x, y, t) are photons� area�time. Assume that the luminance source emits Poisson-distributed photon counts within each small solid angle and time interval. Some fraction of these Poisson emissions will be absorbed. The distribution of the absorbed photons will also be Poisson; so, I(x, y, t) is a Poisson random variable. 

We will restrict our discussion to the class of stimulus profiles I(x, y, t) that can be written in the form IB[1+C(t) f (x, y )]. Thus, the stimulus profile can be represented as a spatiotemporal modulation of a background field of uniform intensity IB by an arbitrary twodimensional spatial function f (x, y ) whose contrast is C(t). For example, we can represent a stimulus consisting of a circular disk of incremental intensity 2I, superimposed on a uniform field of intensity IB for duration D, by a diskshaped function f (x, y ) of contrast C=2I�IB , where 2I>0 in the interval [O, D] and 2I=0 at all other times. This representation of the input pattern will allow us to consider stimuli which are flashed or flickered, but not stimuli which are drifted. For present purposes (the analysis of the threshold detection of flashed spots and gratings), this stimulus characterization will be sufficiently general.

De Vries�Rose Behavior 

The theory developed in the last section leads to the conclusion that an observer who follows a timing rule will produce a t.v.i. curve with a slope of 1.0 at high backgrounds and a slope of zero at low backgrounds. In developing this theory, we explicitly assumed the stimulus to be present throughout the entire integration time. In this section, it will be shown that making the opposite assumption (i.e., that the flash duration is less than the time required to generate Nspikes) leads to the conclusion that the observer will follow the de Vries�Rose law when photon fluctuations dominate the thermal noise. 

To motivate this argument, first suppose that the target flashes are very long (effectively infinite in duration). The derivation of the distribution of times to receive N spikes which is presented in Appendix B is based on this assumption. The density function for this latency variable has a long tail. The test flash duration must therefore be long in comparison to the mean time to generate N spikes in order for the density function��and hence the theory developed in the last section��to be valid. The validity of the analysis essentially rests on the assumption that the flash duration is long enough that the N spikes will be received before the flash offset on almost 1000 of the trials. This long flash duration approximation is clearly only a limiting case. 

Now consider a real flash of finite duration. In principle, the flash can be made sufficiently short that, on most trials,

The rest of the spikes will be generated by noise (the uniform field) alone. The latency to produce the Nth spike is variable; so sometimes the N spikes will be received before the flash offset, even in the case of a very brief flash. The higher the background level corresponding to the beginning of the theoretical Weber range, the more likely it becomes that photoreceptor saturation will occur below this level in vivo. The idea that Weber's law is sometimes not observed in actual experiments because the photoreceptors saturate before the Weber range is reached is consistent with the observation that Weber behavior is less likely to occur with small, brief test stimuli than with large, long ones. As the size and duration of the test are increased in the model, the lower boundary of the Weber range is shifted to lower background levels. A modified version of the model that included a fixed photoreceptor saturation level would not exhibit Weber behavior unless the test area and duration were large enough to ``extend'' the Weber range to background levels below the saturation point. 4. SUMMARY AND DISCUSSION 

Summary 

Because the presentation of the model in Sections 2 and 3 is so densely mathematical, it may be useful to briefly summarize the main intuitions behind the operation of the model. The observer's task is to discriminate target trials from blanks. The location and onset time of the signal are known exactly. In order to make the discrimination, the observer attempts to estimate the signal strength within the region and approximate time interval in which the target might be presented. He monitors the single neural mechanism which would provide the highest signal-to-noise ratio for detecting the flash and estimates the signal strength by measuring the time that it takes to receive N spikes and dividing N by this time. 

The threshold-versus-intensity behavior of this model was analyzed. In the absolute threshold region of the t.v.i. curve, the observer's SNR is not affected by the level of the photon fluctuations, because these are swamped by additive photoreceptor noise. Therefore, the size of the increment which is required in order to produce a criterion SNR is independent of the background level in this region. 

At background levels which produce quantal fluctuation levels exceeding the thermal noise level, the SNR decreases as the background level in increased, all other things being 

equal. To keep d $ fixed, the incremental flash intensity must therefore be increased to compensate for the increase in the photon fluctuations from the background. When the flash duration is long (present throughout the entire time required to generate N spikes), the timing model predicts that threshold performance will follow Weber's law. 

For long flashes, the neural timing model does not predict the same behavior as the de Vries�Rose photon counting model for the following reason. As the incremental flash intensity is increased in order to maintain d $ as quantal fluctuations increase at higher backgrounds, the observer's N- spike statistical sample will be generated more quickly. A smaller percentage of the total flash will be processed during the (now shorter) integration time. Contrary to the assumptions of the de Vries�Rose model, the observer thus fails to count every quantum when the flash is long (in the Weber's law region) and he counts a progressively smaller percentage of the total quanta from the flash as the background level is progressively increased. Photons that are absorbed from the flash after the N spikes have been received do not contribute to the observer's photon sample of the stimulus and thus do not contribute to his contrast sensitivity. They may contribute to a second estimate of the flash luminance and thus to the contrast sensitivity of a second judgment, but the results of these two independent measurements are not statistically combined by the observer. 

The theoretical t.v.i. curve has a different shape when the flash is sufficiently brief. The de Vries�Rose law will be observed at moderate background levels when the flash offset occurs before the N spikes on which the observer bases his judgments are generated. As in the long duration case, for background levels above the thermal noise level, the increment must be increased in order to keep d $ fixed. Although this has the effect of increasing the rate of spike generation (as it does in the long flash duration case), the flash may nevertheless still disappear before the Nth spike is generated, provided that the flash is sufficiently brief to begin with. It is this situation that produces a de Vries�Rose t.v.i. curve segment. 

Over the region of background luminances in which an increase in the threshold flash intensity is not sufficient to cause the flash to exceed the time to generate N spikes, the observer will integrate all of the photons from the flash. The visual system will not throw away any photons in order to increase its temporal resolution, as it does when Weber's law holds. As a result, the observer's threshold judgments will follow the de Vries�Rose law. In summary, the de Vries�Rose law holds in the model whenever all of the photons from the flash contribute to the observer's judgments; Weber's law holds only when the flash is long enough to exceed the time to generate N spikes, and thus not all of the photons from the flash are integrated. 

Suppose that the flash is brief enough to produce a de Vries�Rose law threshold curve segment. As the background 

18 

MICHAEL E. RUDD 

level is further increased, the increment must also be further increased in order to maintain the observer's d $, until at some background level it will be increased sufficiently that all N spikes will be generated within the duration of the flash. At that background, the t.v.i. curve slope will increase from[1] 2[to 1.] 

Since the behavior of the model is stochastic, the transition from the de Vries�Rose region to the Weber region will be gradual rather than abrupt. However, the difference between gradual and abrupt is one of degree. The transitions in Fig. 5 appear to be fairly abrupt, but the parameters were chosen simply to illustrate the behavior of the model and have no physiological basis. Further work is required in order to simulate timing model t.v.i. curves on the basis of more realistic stimulus and neural parameters, and this will be necessary in order to make meaningful quantitative comparisons with psychophysical data. 

Experimentally, in the case of small, brief flashes a transition from de Vries�Rose to Weber behavior is sometimes not observed. I have argued that this may be due to the fact that, in the real visual system, the photoreceptors saturate before the Weber region is reached. According to the model, the Weber region will not emerge below very high background levels if the test flash is extremely small and brief; thus, it is more likely that saturation will occur at ``subWeber'' luminances when the test is small and brief. 

As is observed experimentally, small, brief flashes are more likely to produce de Vries�Rose behavior in the model than are large, long duration flashes. The reason for the duration effect should be clear from the foregoing summary. Test size also influences the t.v.i. slope because the spiking rate of the ideal neuron is based on flux rather than luminance per se, as is the case for cat retinal ganglion cells, for example (Shapley 6 Enroth-Cugell, 1973). When the flux is large��as it is with large flashes of high incremental threshold values (i.e., at high background levels)��spikes will be generated faster. Relative to the time that it takes to generate N spikes, a large flash of any given duration will thus be ``long'' compared to a small flash of the same duration. So, area and duration interactively affect the model's behavior. 

Discussion 

The following functional interpretation of the model might be given. The observer attempts to achieve a predetermined level of contrast sensitivity. For test flashes of sufficiently long duration, it has been shown that this can be accomplished if the observer follows a timing rule. Once this contrast sensitivity level is obtained, a new contrast measurement is begun. If a flash lasts longer than the minimal time required to achieve the designated contrast sensitivity level, the observer improves his or her temporal resolution by making a separate contrast measurement 

instead of taking a longer measurement, even though a longer measurement would further improve contrast sensitivity. 

In the de Vries�Rose region of the t.v.i. curve, the brief test flash duration does not permit the visual system to achieve the prescribed contrast sensitivity level. The contrast sensitivity of the visual system is therefore lower than the target value, and contrast sensitivity is not constant throughout the de Vries�Rose range. 

The model has a built-in speed�accuracy tradeoff scheme. Lengthening the duration of a threshold flash in the de Vries�Rose operating range results both in an increase in the observer's contrast sensitivity and in a shortening of his integration time (time to get N spikes). In the Weber's law region, further lengthening of the flash results in neither faster processing of the target, nor in a sensitivity increase. 

A serious problem with the model as it stands is that in order to predict Weber's law over a many log unit range of background luminances, the model requires that the visual integration time vary by the same number of log units. It seems improbable that the visual system could transmit spikes at a rate of more than one every few milliseconds (regardless of where in the brain this timing process is assumed to occur). On the basis of psychophysical studies, we might estimate that a ``long'' integration time could be as long as a second. Therefore, even if we assume that the observer's threshold judgments are based on a single spike (N=1), it is unlikely that the integration time of the system could vary by more than 2 or 3 log units. 

There is, however, quite a bit of psychophysical evidence which indicates that visual integration time decreases with increasing flash intensity, albeit at a slower rate than is predicted by the model. Two measures of integration time which clearly exhibit this intensity dependence are the critical duration for Bloch's law (Bloch, 1885; Breitmeyer, 1984) and simple reaction time (Pieron, 1914; Vaughan et al. 1966; Mansfield, 1973; Luce, 1986; Burkhardt et al., 1987). The tenability of the model rests largely on an evaluation of how closely its predictions match these integration time data. We will therefore examine the data concerning the intensity dependence of visual integration time carefully. We will consider the critical duration measure first. 

In Fig. 6 are plotted the data from two studies in which threshold was measured as a function of stimulus intensity and duration (Raab, 1962; Stevens 6 Hall, 1966). The visual integration times in the plots (indicated by the knees of the threshold functions) increase from about 10 ms at a flash luminance of 93 dB��which is equivalent to about 10[3] cd�m[2] (as determined from the scale provided in Stevens, 1975, p. 33)��to about 100 ms at a flash luminance of 60 dB (Stevens 6 Hall data) or about 200 ms at 50 dB (Raab data) (one or two log units, respectively, above photopic threshold).

Compressing both the numerator and the denominator of the signal-to-noise ratio by the same power law does not result in a change in the threshold law��only in the numerical value of d$. The behavior of this modified version of the model should definitely be explored, since the modified model would be very powerful, providing a simultaneous explanation of t.v.i. curves, Bloch's law, reaction time, and brightness judgments.

The model also makes predictions concerning the relationship between reaction time and the target duration and area (or spatial frequency). In the Weber region, RT should be independent of duration. In the de Vries�Rose region, it should be a decreasing function of duration. In both regions, reaction time should be inversely proportional to test area or to spatial frequency squared. A more detailed theoretical analysis of RT data, which is applicable in the Weber range, is given in Rudd (1988). 

Also contained within the timing model is an implicit theory of apparent contrast. In order to analyze the behavior of the model in the de Vries�Rose operating range, it was assumed that the observer's signal is based on a time-averaged measurement of the physical contrast, where the duration of the time averaging is given by the time to get the N spikes. In the de Vries�Rose range, the flash is assumed to be present for only a fraction of the integration time; therefore, the model predicts that��within this background range��a reduction in the flash duration should lead to a reduction in the apparent contrast of the increment. In the Weber region, on the other hand, the model asserts that the flash is present throughout the entire integration period; so increasing the duration of the flash at Weber backgrounds should not lead to an increase in its apparent contrast. These predictions are empirically testable. 

Although the signal will be integrated for only a fraction of the integration time when the model is operating in the de Vries�Rose range, the noise will be integrated throughout the entire time (because the background is always present). Since the photon counting model of de Vries and Rose 

assumed that the noise is only integrated while the signal is present, the d $ values predicted by the neural timing model should be smaller than those predicted by the photon counting model. For example, if the flash were present for only 100 of the time required to generate N spikes, the noise would be integrated for 10 times longer according to the neural timing model than it would be in the de Vries�Rose theory. To produce a given d $ the threshold intensity would need to be about three times higher than the de Vries�Rose model would predict. The discrepancy between the manner in which the two models generate the square-root law may help to explain the fact that actual increment thresholds exceed those predicted by the de Vries�Rose ideal observer model, even under conditions in which the square-root law holds empirically (Barlow, 1962, 1977; Pelli, 1990). 

If the neural mechanisms that the observer monitors possess receptive fields which extend beyond the region of the target (e.g., due to the presence of the inhibitory surrounds of retinal ganglion cells), they will also spatially integrate noise in a nonoptimal manner. This effect will also decrease the neural timing observer's SNR relative to that of an ideal observer. 

The assumption that the observer monitors only the one best mechanism for detecting the stimulus is, of course, an oversimplification. It is a first approximation to the true situation, at best. The value of making such an assumption (besides the fact that it happens to lead to a model which mimics many psychophysical results) is that it provides a starting point from which to derive more realistic models. A natural next step would be to consider threshold discrimination based on multiple parallel channels, each of which is an integrate-and-fire neuron. 

It is of interest to consider some of the properties that a multiple-channel model would possess that a single channel does not. In a multiple-channel model, spot stimuli of the type considered here would potentially excite many channels simultaneously. These excited channels would be tuned to different preferred orientations and spatial frequencies. In psychophysical studies, stimuli of different spatial frequencies often generate threshold curves with different slopes. This fact can be accounted for by the present model, which implicitly assumes that low-frequency stimuli are detected by large receptive fields with low-frequency tuning and high-frequency stimuli are detected by small receptive fields with high-frequency tuning (see also Rudd, 1988). Let us assume for purposes of exposition that the most sensitive channel is operating in the Weber range. A channel whose receptive field is not as well matched to the stimulus profile as the most sensitive channel will produce a lower average receptive field output per unit time. The nonoptimal unit will respond to the threshold flash by producing a lower spiking rate than the most sensitive unit, and, therefore, the flash might be turned off before the observer can integrate N spikes from the nonoptimal channel. This

channel would then be operating in the de Vries�Rose range. It follows that, if the observer's threshold judgments are based on some weighted combination of the outputs of these two channels, we should expect to observe t.v.i. functions with slopes between[1] 2[and 1 under these conditions. An] appropriate rule for combining the outputs of multiple channels might therefore account for the fact that threshold curves sometimes appear to follow power laws with exponents between 12[and 1 over a considerable range] (Sharpe et al., 1993). Of course, if the less sensitive channel can be neglected altogether (which is what we assume here), the slope will be exactly 1. Whether or not this speculation is borne out, the behavior of a timing model of visual threshold based on the simultaneous monitoring of parallel integrate-and-fire neurons needs to be explored. 

Finally, as was pointed out by one of the reviewers, the model��and in fact this entire paper��is fuzzy in distinguishing between rod and cone systems. As it presently stands, the model is mute concerning this distinction. Some of the assumptions of the model (e.g., lack of retinal gain control at stages preceding spatiotemporal summation by spiking neurons, quantal input closely tied to the photon absorptions) appear to be more consistent with the behavior of the rod system operating at low background levels than the cone system operating under the normal range of natural viewing conditions. However, the psychophysical evidence cited above for changes in visual integration time as a function of incremental flash intensity (Raab, 1962; Stevens 6 Hall, 1966) was obtained primarily under photopic viewing conditions; so, the model should not be ruled out as a theory of photopic threshold judgments. 

Much of the recent empirical research on the problem of visual threshold has focused on efforts to distinguish between the operating characteristics of the rod and cone systems. The results have led to many well-known ``rules of thumb'' concerning the shapes of t.v.i. curves obtained under scotopic and photopic conditions. For example, the de Vries�Rose law is more often obtained over a considerable range under scotopic viewing conditions than under photopic viewing conditions; and it is easier to obtain Weber's law under photopic viewing conditions than under scotopic viewing conditions. It is important, however, to keep in mind that the shapes of the t.v.i. curves obtained under these different conditions reflect the behavior of rod and cone systems and not necessarily the behavior of the rods and cones per se. 

The results of the theoretical investigations presented here suggest that the spatiotemporal characteristics of the test stimuli and receptive fields mediating detection��rather than the properties of the photoreceptors themselves��may be of primary importance in determining threshold behavior. As Barlow (1965, p. 540) pointed out, the dependence of t.v.i. slopes on spatial summation over substantial retinal area, as well as over tens of milliseconds, 

suggests that ``the transmission system, rather then the receptive system may be involved''. Although it has been assumed here that a channel of an ideal scale is always available for detecting any given stimulus, the range of receptive field sizes available to the visual system (or to the rod or cone system, in isolation) is in fact limited. If the ideal channel assumption is relaxed, the parameters of the random walk driving the spike generation process in the model will be influenced by the goodness of fit between the relative sizes of the test stimuli and the receptive field mediating its detection. 

The dimensions of receptive fields mediating foveal cone vision are smaller than those mediating rod vision. Therefore, a stimulus that is ``large'' compared to the characteristic receptive field dimensions of foveal cone-driven spiking neurons (ganglion cells, for example) may be ``small'' compared to the typical size of the receptive fields of peripheral rod-driven neurons. For this reason, a stimulus that is sufficiently large to produce Weber behavior under photopic viewing conditions may produce de Vries�Rose behavior under scotopic viewing conditions. 

These considerations suggest that, when comparing slopes obtained with scotopic and photopic stimuli, care should be taken if possible to ensure that the target scales in the studies being compared are equally well matched to the characteristic receptive field sizes of the spatial channels which are presumed to mediate detection. (This requires some speculation regarding the level of visual processing that is most relevant to threshold judgments.) Otherwise, an effect based on stimulus scale may be misinterpreted as a result of a gain control property of the photoreceptors mediating detection. 

It is unclear how the timing model is to be reconciled with the mounting evidence for Weber adaptation in the rods of humans and other primates (Tamura et al. 1991; Kraft et al. 1993). (So far, evidence for Weber adaptation in primate cones is lacking.) Of course, it may turn out that shift toward Weber behavior at high backgrounds that is observed psychophysically is a result of photoreceptor adaptation, as many psychophysicists and physiologists believe. One mechanism by which this could occur has been described by Rudd 6 Brown (1993; 1994; in press). Another possibility is that psychophysical Weber behavior is the result of photoreceptor adaptation at very high background levels, but that��under the appropriate stimulus conditions��intensity-dependent integration time changes extend the low end of the Weber range to background levels at which the photoreceptors themselves do not exhibit gain control.[3]

To understand the basis of the psychophysical Weber behavior, it is not sufficient to prove that human photoreceptors exhibit Weber's law; we also need to build and test theories of how gain control in the photoreceptors influences threshold judgments, which are presumably based on the spiking rates of cortical neurons.
