From these estimates it  was inferred that learning occurs on correct and incorrect trials; correct trials appeared more important in the R-E model  while incorrect trials assumed major importance for the W-S model, especially prior to overshooting.  The purpose of the present study was to compare the ability of two models to described behavior in the probability learning  task. All subjects received extended training on one of two levels of even probability (rr) and payoff. As a consequence,  overshooting of -x was expected (e.g., see Edwards, 1956, 1961; Myers, Fort, Katz, and Suydam, 1963). The models selected for  study, therefore, had to be able to predict overshooting as well as show promise of being able to describe choice data, e.g.,  marginal and first-order response-event conditional probabilities throughout learning and the second-order response-event  conditional probabilities and run curves in the last 100 trials.  1 Based on a doctoral dissertation submitted to the University of Massachusetts. Finite Markov Chain Model. The Weak-Strong (W-S) Conditioning Model (Myers and Atkinson, 1964) was derived from stimulus  sampling concepts to provide a framework within which the effect of incentive and motivational variables could be analyzed.  Briefly, this model assumes that on each of a series of trials one stimulus element is randomly sampled from an assumed  population of elements, and furthermore, that each element is either weakly or strongly conditioned to response Aj. If the  sampled element is in the weak state (W; , i = 1, 2) and the response is: (a) reinforced (correct), then with probability f.  that element shifts to the strong state (S; , i = 1, 2); (b) not reinforced, then with probability S that element shifts to  state W; , (j = 1, 2, i z"~: j). If the sampled element is in the strong state and the response is: (a) reinforced, then the  element remains in that state; (b) not reinforced, then the element shifts from state Si to state Wi with probability S.  Changes in response probability are assumed to be a function of transitioning of these elements among states of conditioning.  A more detailed presentation of this model and of several derivations are found in Myers and Atkinson (1964) and Heuckeroth  and Myers (1967).  METHOD  Procedure. Eight groups of 20 subjects representing all combinations of rr(.6 and .8), payoff (gains and losses of 0and 1,  and sex each made 600 binary predictions. Data were collected from two males and two females in each +r-payoff group using one  of 10 noncontingent rein-  420 HEUCKEROTH  forcing event sequences. As no sex effects were found in any analyses, this variable will not be further mentioned. Subjects  in the payoff groups were given several sheets with "1" and "-" columns to keep track of their correct and incorrect  predictions, respectively. Subjects in the nonpayoff groups made their predictions under the usual no-feedback condition. All  subjects received instructions that indicated the experiment was concerned with study of decision-making processes and that  their task was to make as many correct predictions as possible. Subjects in the payoff groups were told, in addition, they  would win or lose l c on each trial depending on the correctness of their predictions. All subjects were told that the event  sequence was strictly random and that attempting to find a pattern would probably hurt their scores.  Sequences. Ten sequences of 600 events were constructed for each level of ar so that the number of runs of each event was  approximately at its expected value (Nicks, 1959), and the proportion of El's did not deviate by more than 10o from a in  each 50-trial block.  Subjects. Subjects for this experiment were 80 male and 80 female University of Massachusetts undergraduates taking a course  in Introductory Psychology.  Apparatus. Each subject sat before a black panel (15.5 x 10 in.) upon which two 1-in. diameter green jewel lights were  mounted. These lights served as the reinforcing events. Directly below each light was a toggle switch with which the subject  indicated his event prediction. A buzzer sounded for 0.5 sec at the beginning of each trial signaled the subject to make his  prediction. After 3.5 sec, one of the event lights came on for 1 sec. After 3.5 sec, the buzzer signaled the beginning of the  next trial. Predictions of each subject were recorded by an Esterline Angus operations recorder. The event sequence was  controlled by a Western Union tape transmitter. One to four subjects participated in each experimental session as scheduling  permitted.  RESULTS  Empirical Results  Marginal Response Probabilities. The probability of predicting the more frequent event in each 50-trial block is presented in  Table 1 for all experimental groups. Analysis of variance (error mean square = 288.60) of the number of A1 predictions in each  50-trial block confirmed the usual findings, i.e., response probability varies directly with 7r and is higher when payoff is  introduced. Subsequent t tests of the number of A1 predictions in the last block of 50 trials indicated that performance of  each experimental group showed significant overshooting. Comparison of learning curves in the present data at comparable  points with those found in studies cited above revealed no consistent differences; however, later portions of the learning  curves reported here continue to show an upward drift.  Run Data. To evaluate the effects of previous events throughout the course of learning, run curves for successive 100-trial  blocks were plotted for each experimental group (Heuckeroth, 1965). Consistent with other authors' findings, the main features  of these curves indicate: (i) Negative recency is less salient at higher -rr levels (e.g., Anderson and Whalen, 1960; Edwards,  1961; Nicks, 1959); (ii) Negative recency  PROBABILITY LEARNING 421  decreases as the training session progresses (e.g., Anderson, 1960; Derks, 1963; Lindman and Edwards, 1961); (iii) Negative  recency is less salient when payoff has been introduced (e.g., Atkinson and Sommer, 1960; Derks, 1962).  Model Tests and Comparisons  In this section the ability of each model to describe the marginal, first- and secondorder response-event conditional  probabilities and run statistics is considered. All predicted statistics for the W-S model as well as the second-order  conditional probabilities for the R-E model were obtained with derived equations (Anderson, 1959, p.259; Heuckeroth and Myers,  1967; Myers and Atkinson, 1964). All remaining predicted statistics for the R-E model were obtained by analyzing 200 Monte  Carlo protocols generated for each experimental condition. The nonmonotonicity noted in predicted marginal and first-order  conditional probabilities (Table 1 and Fig. 1, respectively) and run statistics (Fig. 2) for this model is due to sampling  fluctuation is the synthetic data.  Observed and predicted marginal and first-order conditional probabilities were obtained for each 50-trial block; second-order  conditional probabilities and run statistics were obtained for the last 100-trial block. Parameter estimates used to compute  these predicted statistics were obtained by minimizing the squared differences between observed and predicted pooled  second-order joint probabilities in the last 50 and 100 trials, respectively (see Tables 3 and 4).  Marginal and First-Order Conditional Probabilities. Observed and predicted values for these statistics are presented in Table  1 and Fig. 1, respectively.  For the most part, marginal response probabilities predicted in early blocks are higher than observed, particularily for the  W-S model; as later blocks are approached, fit to this statistic generally improves. However, for the 0.6-1group, deviations  in fit increase for both models as later blocks are approached.  Fit of these models differs most noticeably in the rate of approach of predicted curves to their asymptotic values. Generally  (0.6-10 group excepted), W-S model predictions stabilize too rapidly; R-E model predictions approach their asymptotic values  somewhat more gradually.  Fit of both models to the first-order conditional probabilities (Fig. 1) generally shows that same trends noted above for  marginal response probabilities. Specifically, predicted values are higher than observed in early blocks, fit improves as  terminal blocks are approached, and W-S model predictions stabilize at their asymptotic values too rapidly. Furthermore, the early stabilization noted in these predicted statistics by the W-S model also has been reported by Heuckeroth  and Myers (1967) for the Suppes and Atkinson (1960) data. The pattern of discrepancies noted between observed and predicted statistics for the W-S model may imply that either the  model's axiom structure or the parameter estimation procedure used requires revision. Revision of the axiom structure might be  accomplished by requiring subjects to express weak or strong confidence in their choices (e.g., see Anderson and Whalen, 1960)  and then identify conditioning states in the model with levels of expressed confidence. An empirical basis for axiom revision  would come from examination of the first-order conditional expressions of confidence.  Early stabilization of marginal and first-order conditional learning curves predicted by the W-S model may mean that variables  with which parameters are identified, and consequently parameter estimates, are not invariant throughout learning. Some  evidence consistent with that possibility is provided in the next section. CONCLUSIONS  Available evidence does not permit a clear discrimination between the W-S and R-E models. For each model the initial learning  level in the predicted marginal and first-order conditional curves is generally higher than observed. While the W-S model  generally describes these asymptotic statistics best, the R-E model frequently is superior in the early blocks. Both models  describe the second-order conditional probabilities about equally well and predict the run curves equally poorly.  Parameter estimates for both models vary with rr and payoff as well as throughout learning. Estimates obtained for each model  imply that learning occurs on correct and incorrect trials; correct trials are more important according to the R-E model while  incorrect trials assume major importance in the W-S model. Furthermore, according to the W-S model, correct trials appear to  influence learning only after matching has occurred.  Future model development efforts might include variation in the parameter estimation procedure. Alternatively, the standard  choice situation might be modified so that an assumed isomorphism between responses and model states can be used to provide an  empirical basis for revision of model axioms.
