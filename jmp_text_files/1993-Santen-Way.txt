Exploring V-way Tables with Sums-of-Products Models 

JAN P. H. VAN SANTEN* 

AT & T Bell Laboratories, Murray Hill, New Jersey 07974 

Sums-of-products models can be characterized as a variant of the analysis of variance model, where interaction terms are assumed to be products of single-factor scales and where the assumption that main effects and interaction terms have zero sums is dropped. Because of the ordinal structure inherent in sums of products, these models are appropriate when data satisfy single-factor independence (the direction of the effects of a factor is independent of other factors) but have amplificatory violations of joint independence (the direction of the joint effects of a set of factors may depend on other factors, because a given factor amplifies the effects of some factors more than the effects of others). This paper describes data analysis methods for sums-of-products models that determine which main effect and interaction terms exist. The methods are exploratory in that no error theory is provided. A key concept is that of multi-facter residual with respect to a set of factors, computed by successively “taking out the means” of these factors. The methods involve analysis of multi-factor residuals in terms of their sums of squares and their N-way matrix ranks. © 1993 Academic Press, Inc.

Having defined this rather general class of models, two questions must be answered. First, how can these models be used to explore N-way tables? Second, what is their theoretical interest? 

Concerning the first question, ideally one would like to be able to find a unique sums-of-products model (that is, a collection of index sets and estimates of the factor scales h,,) that can be shown to fit a given data set better than any other sums-of-products model, and to make an inferential statistical statement about the quality of this fit. The present paper takes a first step towards this goal by presenting exploratory methods for finding which index sets are required to fit a given data set, but without inferential statistics. Once a set of index sets has been determined, the estimation of factor scales is straightforward and can be performed with many general purpose multivariate function minimizers. As will be seen, the key to these exploratory methods is the concept of a multi-factor residual with respect to a set offactors, computed by successively “taking out the means” of these factors. It will be shown that sums-of-products models make specific predictions about the sums of squares and N-way matrix ranks of multi-factor residuals. These predictions can then be used to infer from the data which index sets are required. 

As for the second question, two points can be made. First, sums-of-products models have features that make them appropriate for data with the following four characteristics. (1) The factors A, are at most on an ordinal scale. (2) The dependent measure is at least on an interval scale. (3) The data satisfy single-factor independence (the direction of the effects of a factor is independent of other factors [Krantz, Luce, Suppes & Tversky, 1971 ]). (4) There exist amplificatory violations ofjoint independence (the direction of the joint effects of a set of factors may depend on other factors, because a given factor amplifies the effects of some factors more than the effects of others).

A model can be viewed as a mapping of a parameter space into an observation space. For the model in Eq. (11), the parameter space has 12 dimensions (three parameters for A,,, two for fy), etc.). The observation space consists of all possible durations in 12 cells, and is thus 12-dimensional. One would be tempted to conclude that the good fit of this model is vacuous (in fact, should have been perfect), since the number of parameters is equal to the dimensionality of the observation space. However, the testability of a model is not simply a matter of comparing the dimensionalities of the parameter space and the observation space, because testability depends on the characteristics of the mapping defined by the model. It has been shown that the proper measure of the dimensionality of the parameter space is given by the maximal rank {over the paremeter space) of the Jacobian matrix of the model’s mapping (Bamber & van Santen, 1985). In this matrix, the rows correspond to the parameters and the columns to the dimensions of the observation space. The entries of the matrix are the partial derivatives of the cells with respect to the parameters. The rank of this matrix for the 12-parameter model is only 8, so that the good fit is not vacuous. 

In summary, this example illustrates that also with noisy data rank analysis provides guidance in model selection. The example also shows the importance of developing statistical tools for evaluating the size of eigenvalues.

8. CONCLUSIONS 

This paper has investigated properties of sums-of-product models—a class of models that are descriptive of data characterized by single-factor independence and amplificatory joint independence violations. Methods based on these properties can be used to infer from the data which product terms are required by analyzing multi-factorial residuals. 

When the data contain little noise and when scale matrix ranks are “large,” the methods are likely to produce the ultimate goal of uniquely determining the set of terms. But even when these conditions are not fulfilled, the methods are useful in restricting the class of viable models. This class can then be further explored with other methods, including brute-force model fitting.

The methods may also be useful when one has definite hypotheses about which terms exist. For example, one may hypothesize (H.J.M. Steeneken, personal communication, October 22, 1990) that there is an ordering of the factors, and that only product terms exist for pairs of adjacent factors, i.e.,

One can then inspect if the residuals ¢,,,;, 1, have systematically larger sums of squares than the residuals ¢;,;,,;,;, where j>1. Likewise, the methods provide informative tests of phonetic duration models. For example, van Santen and Olive (1990, using the terminology of the current paper) showed that the Klatt model (Klatt, 1973) contains the “wrong” index sets. 

The inferential-statistical aspects of sums-of-products models are not likely to be simple. For one thing, because these models are not a special case of the general linear hypothesis there is little that can be borrowed from the analysis of variance. The most realistic approach to statistical testing may lie in Monte-Carlo simulations. Currently, work is underway on a systematic approach to these simulations. 

Another issue to be addressed are missing data, or more generally, weighted data. The results discussed in this paper cannot easily be extended to weighted data. One solution to this problem is to smooth and fill-in the data, for example, by using the procedures developed by Dykstra (Dykstra & Robertson, 1982) that produce a matrix satisfying single-factor independence from a weighted-data matrix. The resulting matrix is an estimate of the complete, unweighted data, and can then be analyzed uing the methods described in this paper. 

Finally, I note some remarks on computation. Computation of multi-factor residuals and matrix rearrangements is trivial, as is the computation of eigenvalues. Fitting a particular sums-of-products model to the data can be performed with any standard minimization routine, or with a special purpose algorithm that iteratively solves the linear equations resulting from setting the partial derivatives with respect to the parameters equal to zero; while this algorithm converges slowly, it does not require explicit reparameterization often necessary for general-purpose routines that cannot handle superfluous parameters.
