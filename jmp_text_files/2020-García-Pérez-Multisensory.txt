Multisensory integration entails binding perceptual inputs from several sensory systems into a single experience of the physical event that they reflect. This ability allows us, for instance, to bind together the sound heard when an object falls on the floor and the visual perception of its impact. Arrival of signals at the sensory receptors must be sufficiently close in time for their binding to be possible, and they must be temporally separated from sensory input of the same type. Thus, in a fireworks show, binding the sound made by the explosion of a casing with its own pyrotechnic stars is impossible due to auditory delays and temporal proximity of consecutive blasts; in contrast, the absence of signal crowding still allows binding thunder and lightning during a storm despite the long auditory delay relative to the visual flash. Multisensory integration also manifests beyond the simple temporal binding of cross-modal sensory inputs, the clearest case of which is the McGurk effect in speech perception ( McGurk & MacDonald, 1976 ) whereby the perceived sound of a syllabic utterance differs according to the lip movements accompanying it without any temporal delay. Aspects of multisensory integration responsible for such qualitative characteristics of the perceived event are left aside in this paper, which focuses only on the measurement of temporal aspects of multisensory integration.

Humans also have a remarkable ability to detect temporal asynchrony and identify the temporal order of two sensory signals, which is sometimes an obstacle to multisensory integration. This ability is clearly appreciated upon watching continuous speech signals delivered via electronic media (e.g., online communication through the Internet), where offsets between visual and auditory components of the signal can often be identified as mismatches between the moment at which certain sounds are heard and the moment at which their articulations are seen. Because delivery of visual and auditory signals in exact synchrony is not always feasible, a seamless multisensory experience only occurs when their temporal offset does not exceed the allowance for subjective synchrony, often referred to as the  temporal window of integration  (TWI). Measurement of the TWI naturally requires assessing how perceptual judgments of simultaneity or temporal order vary as a function of the asynchrony of presentation of two stimuli. The focus of this paper is on the psychophysical methods used for this purpose and on the extent to which data thus collected can unequivocally characterize the TWI or other indices of timing ability and their determinants.

Interestingly, experimental manipulations can alter the location and width of the TWI. A first line of evidence to this effect comes from studies on  Titchener ’s ( 1908 , pp. 251–259)  law of prior entry  whereby attended signals are processed faster than non-attended signals (see, e.g.,  Donohue, Green, & Woldorff, 2015; Sharp, Melcher, & Hickey, 2018; Stone, 1926 ). A second line of evidence comes from studies on the  temporal recalibration  hypothesis ( Fujisaki, Shimojo, Kashino, & Nishida, 2004 ; see also  Navarra et al., 2005; Vroomen, Keetels, de Gelder, & Bertelson, 2004 ) whereby prolonged exposure to asynchronous signals produces an adaptation that shifts the TWI. Research on the mechanisms of prior entry or recalibration has been inconclusive (see, e.g.,  Spence & Parise, 2010; Yarrow, Minaei, & Arnold, 2015 ), mostly because observed changes in the psychometric function and the TWI are compatible with the operation of meaningfully distinct processes.  Fig. 1  provides a simplified illustration of this indeterminacy with psychometric functions arising from a model discussed later in this paper. Each column depicts a different scenario. In the left column, “normal” processing of visual and auditory signals with a “normal” decision criterion for perceptual simultaneity as might hold in a control condition yield the psychometric functions displayed in the bottom panel. In the center column, identical decision criteria are applied to faster visual signals due to sensory acceleration (e.g., prior entry), which produces a leftward shift of the observed psychometric functions. In the right column, “normal” sensory processing combines with a shifted criterion (e.g., recalibration) that produces a leftward shift identical to that in the center column.

The indeterminacy arises from the unidentifiability of the sensory and decisional determinants of the location of the psychometric function ( Schneider & Bavelier, 2003; Yarrow, Jahn, Durant, & Arnold, 2011 ). Yet, this is not a fault of the models used to fit and interpret data but, rather, an inherent limitation of the single-presentation method used to collect the data ( García-Pérez & Alcalá-Quintana, 2013 ). Luckily, this indeterminacy can be circumvented when data are collected with dual-presentation methods and treated appropriately (see  García-Pérez, 2014a; García-Pérez & Alcalá-Quintana, 2013, 2017, 2018, 2019; García-Pérez & Peli, 2014, 2019 ). However, dual-presentation methods have rarely been used in timing studies and, when they were, the rich information that such data can provide was generally lost by aggregating responses across the two arrangements of the dual presentation and by fitting arbitrary psychometric functions to the aggregate (for an exception discussed later, see  Yarrow, Martin, Di Costa, Solomon, & Arnold, 2016 ). Gaining full advantage of the information provided by dual-presentation data requires refraining from aggregating data across presentation arrangements, but it also demands model-based psychometric functions whose parameters capture the sensory, decisional, and response processes responsible for observed performance.

The main goal of this paper is to derive this type of psychometric function as a natural generalization of an earlier process model of performance in single-presentation timing tasks ( García-Pérez & Alcalá-Quintana, 2012a ), a model widely supported by empirical data (see  Chen, Lewis, Shore, & Maurer, 2017; Chen, Lewis, Shore, Spence, & Maurer, 2018; Chen, Shore, Lewis, & Maurer, 2016; Durnez & Van Damme, 2016; García-Pérez & Alcalá-Quintana, 2012a, 2012b, 2015a, 2015b, 2015c; Stanley, Chen, Lewis, Maurer, & Shore, 2019; Tünnermann & Scharlau, 2018 ). Complementary to this goal, we also present simulation studies that demonstrate the identifiability and accurate recovery of the model parameters and we validate the model both by analysis of available data in the literature and by analysis of new data collected in this study.

The organization of the paper is as follows. The next section provides a brief description of the two types of timing tasks (single-presentation and dual-presentation tasks) that are used to estimate the TWI and other indices of performance. A subsequent section presents the model for dual-presentation timing tasks, which captures sensory, decisional, and response processes analogous to those that govern performance in single-presentation tasks. Simulation results are then presented that demonstrate the identifiability and accurate recovery of model parameters and indices of performance derived from them. A further section presents the results of analyses that fit the model to available data from dual-presentation timing tasks, followed by another section in which new data are collected and analyzed to exploit the full potential of model-based psychometric functions. The paper ends with a discussion and commentary on issues that were mentioned in passing in the preceding sections.

Two groups of psychophysical tasks are used to assess observers’ ability to detect asynchrony and identify temporal order. The first group comprises single-presentation methods, which consist of the presentation of a single stimulus pair in each trial. The second group comprises multiple-presentation methods, which consist of the presentation of two or more stimulus pairs in each trial although here we will only deal with dual-presentation methods involving two pairs per trial. Methods within each group differ mostly as to (i) the question that observers are asked to respond to at the end of each trial and the response options that are allowed and (ii) the way in which responses are analyzed to extract the desired indices of performance. This paper focuses on dual-presentation tasks but the first group of tasks will also be discussed because performance in both groups must rely on the same sensory processes and on analogous decisional and response processes. The next two subsections describe the most common tasks in each group; a third subsection addresses how indices of timing ability are extracted from the data.

Suppose for illustration and without loss of generality that the pair consists of a brief audio signal and a brief video signal whose relative onsets (alternatively, offsets) vary across trials. Consider also that the onset of the video signal is regarded as the reference and the onset of the audio signal is the test presented with some  auditory delay ,

Presentation of a pair with a selected auditory delay (or, more generally,  stimulus onset asynchrony ; SOA) elicits a subjectively experienced delay  S  determined by the  psychophysical function , which maps physical delay (in chronometric units) onto subjective delay (in arbitrary units).  Fig. 2 a shows a hypothetical function assumed to be linear and not through the origin, as perceptual synchrony may not occur at physical synchrony. The psychophysical function describes how average subjective delay varies with physical delay, but subjective delay is actually a random variable.

Observers can be asked to report whether or not video and audio onsets were subjectively simultaneous (rendering the  binary synchrony judgment task , SJ2), whether video onset subjectively preceded or lagged audio onset (rendering the  binary temporal-order judgment task , TOJ), whether video preceded, audio preceded, or audio and video seemed simultaneous (rendering the  ternary synchrony judgment task , SJ3), or whether video preceded, audio preceded, audio and video seemed simultaneous, or they seemed asynchronous but their temporal order could not be discerned (rendering the  quaternary synchrony judgment task , SJ4). Responses are then tallied across repeated trials at selected auditory delays and plotted as a function of auditory delay to describe a psychometric function. In the SJ2 task, the psychometric function for “simultaneous” responses has an inverted-U shape (as shown in  Fig. 1 ); in the TOJ task, the psychometric function for “video precedes” responses is monotone increasing (also shown in  Fig. 1 ); in the SJ3 task, the psychometric functions for “audio precedes”, “video precedes”, and “simultaneous” responses are monotone decreasing, monotone increasing, and inverted-U-shaped, respectively; and in the SJ4 task, a fourth, bimodal psychometric function arises for “uncertain order” responses. The applicable psychometric function (or set of functions) is subsequently analyzed to extract performance measures. SPT tasks are overwhelmingly used in empirical research. For a further discussion of these four tasks, see  García-Pérez and Alcalá-Quintana (2018) .

Presentations occur in consecutive temporal intervals separated by a convenient temporal gap. Each presentation interval elicits a subjectively experienced delay  S  randomly drawn from the distribution that applies to the SOA delivered in that presentation. Besides the type of stimuli used to deliver SOAs, the magnitude of the standard SOA, and the range of test SOAs that are probed, variants of the 2PT task differ as to which question the observer is asked to respond to after each trial. This has consequences on how the observer’s responses are categorized, on the shape of the psychometric function thus obtained, on how the psychometric function encodes quantitative aspects of the underlying ability to judge synchrony or temporal order, and on how comparable are the results obtained across variants of the task. Two variants of the 2PT task have been used in the literature, which are discussed next.

To the authors’ knowledge, the 2PT task was first used by  Allan and Kristofferson (1974) . In their study, each trial presented two light–tone pairs. The onset times of light and tone were simultaneous in both pairs and it was their offset times that differed. The study included null and non-null standard SOAs and observers were asked to report whether the light terminated before the tone in the first or in the second interval. Presentation durations of light and tone were chosen so that the light was indeed extinguished before the tone in only one of the two intervals. These different ways in which observers’ responses on a 2PT task are categorized and their implications on the resultant form of the psychometric function must be adequately captured by a model of performance built on invariant sensory processes with decisional and response processes tailored to the response format chosen for the task.

In SPT tasks, the outcome measures of interest are typically the  point of subjective simultaneity  (PSS; defined as the SOA at which presentations appear perceptually synchronous), the  temporal window of integration  (TWI; defined as the range of SOAs within which perception of synchrony prevails over perception of asynchrony), and the  synchrony boundaries  (defined as the lower and upper boundaries of the TWI). All of these measures are computed from suitable percentage points on a psychometric function fitted to the data collected with the chosen SPT task.

                            Parametric or non-parametric adaptive methods are sometimes used to obtain direct estimates of the target percentage points needed to obtain these performance measures. We will leave aside this alternative approach mostly because these methods have been shown to provide wildly biased estimates (see  Alcalá-Quintana & García-Pérez, 2004, 2007; Faes et al., 2007; García-Pérez, 1998, 2001, 2002, 2011; García-Pérez & Alcalá-Quintana, 2007, 2009 ). It should nevertheless be noted that adaptive methods are still a suitable and efficient approach to collect data to which psychometric functions will be fitted ( García-Pérez, 2014b; García-Pérez & Alcalá-Quintana, 2005 ). 
                          Typically, the mathematical form of the psychometric function is chosen arbitrarily so as to provide a descriptive account of the data from which the required percentage points can be read off, but the parameters of such psychometric functions are uninterpretable in terms of the sensory, decisional, and response processes involved in making judgments and reporting responses. In addition, the mathematical form of these functions is not consistent across tasks, precisely because they do not relate to the processes that determine performance. Such outcome measures differ meaningfully across tasks for identical stimuli and conditions, suggesting that decisional processes inherent to each task affect the way in which the sensory processes of interest manifest ( Capstick, 2012; van Eijk, Kohlrausch, Juola, & van de Par, 2008, 2010; Love, Petrini, Cheng, & Pollick, 2013; Sanders, Chang, Hiss, Uchanski, & Hullar, 2011; Stevenson & Wallace, 2013; Vatakis, Navarra, Soto-Faraco, & Spence, 2008 ). A model-based approach was proposed to provide interpretable sensory parameters that have been shown to remain identical across tasks despite differences in observed performance ( García-Pérez & Alcalá-Quintana, 2012a, 2015a, 2015b ). For the extraction of performance measures from these model-based psychometric functions, see  Alcalá-Quintana and García-Pérez (2013) .

In the conventional analysis of data from 2PT tasks, analogous outcome measures are extracted from arbitrary psychometric functions fitted to the data (but see  Yarrow et al., 2016 ). The form of these functions is tailored to the way in which responses were scored so as to render monotone increasing, U-shaped, or inverted-U-shaped patterns, but these forms are again inconsistent across variants of the 2PT task and they are also inconsistent with the form of the functions used for SPT data if they are also collected (e.g.,  Capstick, 2012; Stevenson & Wallace, 2013 ). Yet, when the same stimuli and conditions are involved, performance on 2PT and SPT tasks must reflect common sensory aspects (distributions of arrival latencies for the stimuli of concern) and specific decisional aspects that come from how the task forces observers to handle perceived delays to give the requested response. When the same stimuli and conditions are involved, the distributions of arrival latencies must be invariant with the type of task (SPT or 2PT) and the variants thereof; only decisional and response components should vary across tasks and variants. A unified model of performance across all timing tasks is presented next, which extends an earlier model of performance in SPT tasks ( García-Pérez & Alcalá-Quintana, 2012a ).

The model presented next assumes common sensory components and different decisional and response components across tasks and variants and distinguishes unobservable processes from observed responses. The model assumes that, on presentation of two stimuli with some SOA (as in SPT tasks), observers use perceived delay to judge whether presentation was simultaneous or else which stimulus preceded the other. If two pairs of stimuli are presented (as in 2PT tasks), the observer feeds forward the two perceived delays to a mechanism that produces a response by application of a suitable decision rule.

Modeling 2PT tasks requires an obvious extension because observers perceive two subjective delays in each trial and they must report the output of their comparison. The implied decisional process varies in turn across variants of the 2PT task. For instance, in the variant used by  Allan and Kristofferson (1974) , where observers are requested to report the interval in which the light was terminated before the tone, observers must choose the interval in which the perceived auditory delay was positive (i.e., the interval that rendered a VF judgment). Since perceived delay can be positive or negative independently in each interval, the decision process requires extra rules to deal with situations in which perceived delay is positive or negative in both intervals. In contrast, in the variant of  Fouriezos et al. (2007) , where observers are requested to report the interval in which the presentation was more synchronous, observers must instead assess the unsigned magnitude of perceived delay in each interval and choose the interval in which this was smallest. The following development applies to this latter variant because of its widespread use.

We will focus here only on how these indices of performance are defined with respect to the TOJ task, where the PSS is taken to be the 50% point on the psychometric function and the TWI is the distance between the lower synchrony boundary (LSB) and the upper synchrony boundary (USB) respectively defined as, say, the 25% and the 75% points on the psychometric function (e.g.,  Kostaki & Vatakis, 2018; Sanders et al., 2011; Stevenson & Wallace, 2013; Tiippana & Salmela, 2018; Vatakis & Spence, 2006 ). The original justification for this practice comes from consideration that the observer’s performance as described by the psychometric function is only limited by sensory noise. In other words, observers are supposed to have infinite resolution that allows them to respond “audio first” or “video first” (under suitable designations) according exclusively to the difference in arrival latencies of the two sensory signals. However, such an ideal operation is unachievable by human observers, if only because they are unable to tell small differences in arrival latency as is immediately obvious under SJ2 tasks in which observers do not always give “asynchronous” responses. Then, empirically, the observed psychometric function for TOJ tasks is affected by decisional and response factors that make percentage points on it uninterpretable, as discussed earlier (see  Figs. 1 and 3 c).

The 2PT task with a ternary response format and segregation of data by presentation order allows separating out these influences and, hence, the putative operation of an idealized observer whose performance is only limited by sensory noise can be reconstructed from parameter estimates. In sum, use of a ternary response format with separate consideration of the two orders of presentation in a 2PT task permits accurate estimation of theoretically relevant parameters and prevents their confounding. In turn, this carries over to the accurate estimation of the mean and SD of arrival-latency differences that represent sensory limitations to faithful perception of synchrony and temporal order, and also to the accurate estimation of the sensory determinants of the PSS and TWI. Simulation results obtained with asynchronous standards were identical in these respects and their presentation is omitted. These results are in line with those of earlier studies that used other perspectives to investigate the ternary response format and the undesirable consequences of aggregating data across presentation orders (see, e.g.,  Alcalá-Quintana & García-Pérez, 2011, 2013; Bausenhart, Dyjas, & Ulrich, 2015; Dyjas, Bausenhart, & Ulrich, 2012; Dyjas & Ulrich, 2014; García-Pérez, 2014a; García-Pérez & Alcalá-Quintana, 2010a, 2010b, 2011a, 2011b, 2012a, 2017; Greenberg, 1965; Rammsayer & Ulrich, 2001; Ulrich & Vorberg, 2009; Vickers, 1975; Watson, Kellogg, Kawanishi, & Lucas, 1973 ).

Thus far we have only presented a model of performance in 2PT tasks that allows unconfounded estimation of timing parameters in studies of multisensory integration and other related topics. An important question now is whether this model offers a valid description of data gathered in empirical studies. The next two sections address this question, first for empirical data available in the literature and then for data collected in a study newly designed for this purpose.

This section analyzes available data sets under the model presented in this paper. None of the published studies we are aware of used the ternary response format and, then, the psychometric functions in Eqs.  (9) – (10)  will not be used in this section.

The first data set submitted to analysis is that of  Fouriezos et al. (2007) . We extracted data collected in their experiments 1 and 2 from their Figs. 4 and 5. Each experiment included several conditions and observers and, then, parameter estimates must be obtained independently in each case. In all conditions, a synchronous standard was paired in each trial with one of 13 test SOAs and the order of presentation of standard and test SOAs was randomized across the 50 trials administered per test SOA. Observers were asked to report which of the two intervals displayed a more synchronous presentation, with no option for a C response.

In terms of the model presented earlier, an observer would ask for a replay upon a C judgment that cannot be reported. A replay would produce independent draws from the distributions of arrival latencies, which might thus render “interval 1” or “interval 2” judgments. We will refrain from considering this three-chance sequential process for derivation of yet another type of model psychometric function to fit to the data. 
                       Responses were aggregated across presentation orders and the data were presented as number of errors at each test SOA, where an error represents selecting the interval that displayed the test SOA. (2007)  does not make clear whether the standard SOA was paired with each test SOA the same number of times under each presentation order.

Data and fitted psychometric functions are shown in  Fig. 12 . Fitted functions describe the data remarkably well for all observers in all conditions, even more so than the arbitrary Gaussians that were fitted in the original source and that cannot accommodate some of the features displayed by the data (e.g., relatively flat or much too peaked centers, asymmetries, etc.). Data and model curves concur in revealing systematic changes across the two or three conditions in each experiment, with a consistent pattern across observers. For instance, in experiment 1 ( Fig. 12 a) the “moving” condition (red curves) seems to produce a rightward shift compared to the “stationary” condition (black curves). These effects can obviously be measured via the PSS or the width of the TWI, but such measures are insufficient to figure out whether the changes are due to alteration of sensory processes or decision criteria. In contrast, parameter estimates from a model-based analysis provide answers to these questions, but we will not discuss this here because aggregation across presentation orders confounds the parameters of interest (see  Fig. 11 ).

Our unified model of performance in timing tasks proves capable of accounting for published data from isolated 2PT tasks involving either a single standard ( Fig. 12 ) or multiple standards ( Fig. 13 ), and also for published data from studies in which performance on SPT and 2PT tasks has been measured in within-subject conditions with the same stimuli ( Figs. 14 and 15 ). Yet, no published paper appears to have administered the 2PT task with the ternary response format. The next section describes the results of a study that collected such data for yet another test of model validity.

The only goal of this empirical study is to check out that the model accommodates the shape described by ternary 2PT data across presentation orders of standard and test SOAs. A convenient pair of stimuli served this purpose and the study did not include any manipulations to investigate other issues. This study thus departs from the context of multisensory integration but we chose our stimuli to illustrate another field of application, namely, investigation of differences in the speed of processing of different stimuli. There is a long-standing quest in vision science for the functional significance and perceptual correlates of anatomical and physiological differences between the magnocellular and parvocellular streams of the visual system. Studies in this area compare reaction times (e.g.,  Breitmeyer, 1975; Breitmeyer & Breier, 1994; Murray & Plainis, 2003 ), event-related potentials (e.g.,  Jeantet et al., 2019; Mihaylova, Stomonyakov, & Vassilev, 1999 ), simultaneity judgments (e.g.,  Takeshima & Gyoba, 2015 ), or temporal-order judgments ( Kanai, Dalmaijer, Sherman, Kawakita, & Paffen, 2017 ) with visual stimuli selectively processed through one or the other pathway. The current view is that the low-spatial-frequency (LSF) information channeled through the fast magnocellular system guides the subsequent integration of high-spatial-frequency (HSF) information channeled through the slow parvocellular system ( Petras, ten Oever, Jacobs, & Goffaux, 2019 ).

We thus conducted a study to collect ternary responses in a 2PT task in which one of the stimulus was an LSF sinusoid and the other was an HSF sinusoid. A proper study of differences in speed of processing would require a comparison of timing judgments for LSF–LSF, HSF–HSF, and LSF–HSF pairs. However, our only goal here is the preliminary one of testing the validity of the model by assessing its capability to provide a fitting account of ternary data, and this is the reason that we only collected data with LSF–HSF pairs. The study complied with the Declaration of Helsinki and its protocol was approved by the institutional ethics committee.

Eight observers with normal or corrected-to-normal vision participated in the study. All of them gave written informed consent prior to their participation and, except for the authors (observers #1 and #2), all were naïve as to the goals of the study.

Compared to single-presentation methods in which sensory and decisional aspects of performance are confounded, dual-presentation methods allow separating out these influences and assessing their respective roles in observed changes of performance across experimental manipulations ( García-Pérez & Alcalá-Quintana, 2013 ). This paper has presented a model of performance in 2PT tasks that is an extension of an earlier model of performance in SPT tasks. Simulations showed that model parameters are not confounded and can be estimated with reasonable accuracy from feasible numbers of trials in empirical studies. The model has also been validated in three forms. First, against published 2PT data collected with instructions to guess when uncertain and presented in aggregated form across orders of presentation of test and standard SOAs, with a single standard ( Fig. 12 ) or with several standards ( Fig. 13 ). Second, against data from within-subjects designs involving some SPT task and a 2PT task administered with instructions to guess and with ( Fig. 14 ) or without ( Fig. 15 ) aggregation of responses across presentation orders. Finally, against new data collected with the ternary response format and disaggregated by order of presentation of test and standard ( Fig. 16 ). The model fared remarkably well in all tests. Besides parameter estimates describing the sensory, decisional, and response processes responsible for observed performance, we also derived measures of the PSS and TWI under their conventional definition relative to a TOJ task. Because model parameters capture the operation of underlying processes, inferences can also be made about the distribution of arrival latencies and arrival-latency differences, thus providing estimates of differences in the speed of processing of the two stimuli used to deliver SOAs. The main goal of our empirical study was to validate the model by checking out if it fits ternary 2PT data, but it also had the secondary goal of illustrating how model parameters inform of differences in the speed of processing of the stimuli used to deliver SOAs. Admittedly, our choice of spatial frequencies for the patches does not ensure entirely separate processing of each patch through its nominal target stream. Then, our estimates cannot be definitely taken as measures of differential speed of processing in magnocellular and parvocellular pathways, but this does not weaken the conclusion that our LSF patch was processed faster than our HSF patch.

The use of 2PT tasks is certainly not limited to address research questions like that in our empirical illustration and they are also applicable in the context of studies of multisensory integration. Consider, for instance, the integration of visual and auditory signals into a single multisensory experience in audiovisual speech perception, a process generally investigated with SPT tasks (e.g.,  Magnotti, Ma, & Beauchamp, 2013 ; see also  García-Pérez & Alcalá-Quintana, 2015c ). In these cases, integration occurs if an eventual temporal offset between visual and auditory signals does not exceed the limits of simultaneity perception and these limits may vary across manipulations of the visual or auditory signals (as was actually done in the study just mentioned). In such cases, determining whether these manipulations affect sensory or decisional processes is crucial for an understanding of the mechanisms of audiovisual speech perception and demands the use of a task with which these influences can be separated out. The same holds for research on other issues, as discussed next.

Psychometric functions are often regarded as arbitrary but convenient mathematical expressions fitted to the data to extract percentage points that summarize observed performance via PSSs and TWIs. However, the parameters of such functions only accommodate the path of the data without links to the processes that generated them. This feature precludes identification of the processes that produced differences across experimental manipulations. The model-based psychometric functions presented here also describe the data but they have the distinctive characteristic that their parameters inform of the intervening sensory, decisional, and response processes. Thus, the model offers a suitable approach to investigating the sensory versus decisional determinants of changes in performance across experimental manipulations in studies of prior entry or temporal recalibration. The same holds for addressing the question of whether perceptual learning improves the operating characteristics of sensory processes or only reduce the TWI. 2PT tasks have indeed been used to assess the effects of training on the width of the TWI (e.g.,  Powers et al., 2012, 2009; Stevenson, Wilson, Powers, & Wallace, 2013 ), but data were collected in these studies with instructions to guess when uncertain and were aggregated across orders of presentation of test and standard, which are the conditions shown in  Fig. 11  to preclude identification of the cause (sensory or decisional) of the observed reduction.

A model-based approach such as that illustrated in this paper also presents itself as an alternative way to investigate issues that are conventionally addressed differently. Consider a comparison of timing perception across different pairs of sensory modalities, like audiovisual simultaneity (e.g.,  Chen et al., 2016 ), visuotactile simultaneity (e.g.,  Chen et al., 2018 ), or audiotactile simultaneity (e.g.,  Stanley et al., 2019 ). The three papers just mentioned addressed each question independently with SJ2 tasks. Each of these independent studies is thus affected by the problems of SPT tasks that were discussed above but these problems can easily be circumvented via a 2PT task in which the two intervals display, say, an audiotactile SOA and a visuotactile SOA with interwoven series of trials in one of which some audiotactile SOA is the standard whereas some visuotactile SOA is the standard in the other series.
