Cultural Consensus Theory (CCT) is an approach to information pooling (aggregation, data fusion) first presented in the mid-1980s ( Batchelder & Romney, 1986, 1988, 1989; Romney, Batchelder, & Weller, 1987; Romney, Weller, & Batchelder, 1986 ). Since its inception, CCT has become a popular methodology for measurement and inference in the social and behavioral sciences, especially in cultural anthropology (e.g.,  Romney & Batchelder, 1999 ). CCT is designed for situations in which a researcher has access to a group of informants (respondents, experts) who share knowledge or beliefs and agree to provide answers to a set of questions (items) about some domain of their shared knowledge. While the researcher is assumed able to pose relevant questions to the informants, the nature of their shared knowledge, and more specifically the consensus, or ‘culturally correct’ answers to the questions, are unknown  a priori  to the researcher. Thus the primary goal of CCT is to infer consensus patterns of cultural truth that represent the informants’ shared cultural knowledge. To achieve this goal, CCT consists of cognitive response models, each of which is designed to accommodate a particular testing format (e.g. true/false, multiple choice, ranking, probability estimates). Each CCT model specifies parameters for the consensus answer to each item, as well as for the response characteristics of each informant.

In the case of dichotomous true/false items, the standard CCT model is the General Condorcet Model (GCM), first published in the mid-1980s ( Batchelder & Romney, 1986, 1988; Romney et al., 1986 ), and it is described later in this paper. The GCM has become the most popular model used in applications of CCT to ethnographic studies in cultural anthropology, e.g.  Weller (2007) , and there are also the many articles that cite  Romney et al. (1986) . The popularity of the GCM is due in part to the availability of a software package for estimating a restricted case of the GCM developed by Steve Borgatti, which at the time of writing, is available in ANTHROPAC ( Borgatti, 1996 ) as well as in UCINET ( Borgatti, Everett, & Freeman, 2002 ). The original estimation method is based on an ad hoc blend of method-of-moments and Bayesian techniques developed in  Batchelder and Romney (1988) , and it applies only to a special case of the GCM. Later Bayesian estimation for a more general version of the GCM based on Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods is presented in  Karabatsos and Batchelder (2003) . One of the main goals of this paper is to provide new mathematical results as well as Bayesian hierarchical inference for the GCM, including a new posterior predictive check for the central assumption that the informants share the same basic cultural truths.

In general, questions appropriate for CCT are designed to tap knowledge or beliefs shared by the informants rather than their personal beliefs or opinions. For example, (1) “Is Indianapolis the capital of Indiana?” and (2) “Do Hoosiers like basketball?” (Hoosiers are folks living in Indiana) are appropriate CCT questions; but (3) “Do you like basketball?” is not appropriate because it invites an individual to express a personal preference. The GCM assumes that each question pertaining to the shared knowledge of the informants actually has a dichotomously-based ‘correct answer’, i.e. it is either true or false for the culture, as in two-valued logic. This dichotomous assumption about cultural truth is a strong and potentially limiting assumption in the GCM. For example, questions similar to (1) seem appropriate for two logical values, whereas questions like (2) may be better represented by a continuous representation of degrees of cultural truth, e.g. a value in [0, 1], as in fuzzy logic ( Zadeh, 1965 ).

The primary purpose of this paper is to introduce and compare a new CCT model for dichotomous items, the Latent Truth Model (LTM). The new model weakens the GCM assumption that cultural truth is two-valued by assuming that cultural truth is specified by parameters representing truth-values coded continuously in [0, 1]. The LTM shares some of the structure of a CCT model by  Batchelder, Strashny, and Romney (2010)  for cases where informant responses are in the interval [0, 1], as in judgments of the probability of events or the similarity between pairs of semantic terms. In this paper, we contrast the strong distinction of using CCT to infer truths as continuous values, as with the LTM, versus the traditionally-used, two-valued cultural truth specification in the GCM, first analytically, then with simulated data, and finally by applying both to published data.

The paper is divided into six sections. After the introductory section, Section  2  presents a generalized version of the GCM along with several new mathematical properties, and then Section  3  presents the new LTM model along with several of its mathematical properties as well. Section  4  compares the two models analytically, and discusses similarities and differences between the roles of the parameters specified in the two models. In this section, a statistic of the response data is provided that tends to distinguish the two models. Section  5  develops hierarchical Bayesian inference and posterior predictive checks for both models based on MCMC methods, and applies these developments to both simulated and published data. This paper is the first paper that presents hierarchical Bayesian inference for CCT models, and we hope that it sets the stage for others to adopt this approach. Finally, in Section  6  we discuss suggested conditions that need to be met for the appropriate use of each model.

In this section we present the assumptions for the GCM, and we show that it has the structure of a general signal detection model for dichotomous response data. In the case of the usual signal detection model, the hit and false alarm rates, as well as the ‘correct answers’, are manifest (observable); however for the GCM, these quantities are latent variables that play the role of parameters.

The first axiom expresses the strong assumption that cultural truth is two-valued, and that the truth-values of the items are specified as parameters in the model. The second axiom is typical of the assumptions made in item response theory (e.g.,  Embretson & Reise, 2000 ), as well as other models that also have parameters for respondents and items. Basically one would expect a lot of dependencies between the response random variables in  
                            
                               X 
                            
                          on the left-hand side of  (2)  because they share access to the same answer key; for example, if several informants each answer true to a particular item, it is a favorable bet that any other informant would also do so. The purpose of the conditional independence assumption is to specify that all the dependencies between the random variables are removed by conditioning on the model parameters. Finally,  Axiom 3G  specifies the marginal distributions in  (2)  in terms of hit and false alarm rates in the usual way that they are defined in signal detection models (e.g.,  Macmillan & Creelman, 2005 ). In particular, in terms of signal detection theory, the four limbs in  (3)  refer respectively to  hits, false alarms, misses , and  correct rejections .

The GCM, as stated in  Axioms 1G–3G , has the character of a general signal detection model that allows heterogeneity over informants in their hit and false alarm rates. As with most signal detection models, hit and false alarm rates are reparameterized into parameters that separately specify the ability to discriminate the two item types and response bias. There are a variety of such models including discrete-state models with thresholds as well as the popular TSD (theory of signal detection) model that assumes Gaussian distributions ( Macmillan & Creelman, 2005 ). In the original version of the GCM in  Batchelder and Romney (1986, 1988) , a discrete state, double-high threshold (DHT) model is imposed; however, in  Batchelder, Kumbasar, and Boyd (1997)  a TSD version and a single-high threshold version of the GCM are also developed. The version of the GCM in  Axioms 1G–3G  is a generalization of all earlier papers developing the GCM because it can accommodate any signal detection model that provides a specification for the hit and false alarm parameters. However, in this paper we focus on the DHT specification because it is the version that is most popular in applications. The primary motivation for the LTM comes from a desire to represent cultural truth in terms of degrees rather than in terms of two-valued logic, as in the GCM. Of course certain cultural truths are dichotomous by their nature, such as statements of fact concerning explicit laws, moral codes, or food taboos; however other statements seem better represented as a degree of cultural truth: such as the perceived severity of crimes, the importance of observing certain moral codes, or culturally characteristic food preferences. In these cases, it is desirable to have a CCT model for the case where true/false responses are required even though cultural truth is represented in degrees. Practitioners of CCT analysis as a measurement or inferential tool may then consider selecting either the GCM or LTM based on their assumption about truth in the data, or may apply both, for multiple interpretations. The conclusion will review the factors behind choosing each of these models in a given situation.

This distinction between dichotomous truth and degrees of truth has occurred in the study of concept identification and classification, in which the cognitive validity of continuous representations of truth in certain situations has already been established. For example, much of the earlier work in concept identification involved the acquisition of concepts where each instance either is or is not a member of the concept, which in turn is determined by a Boolean function defined over instance attributes ( Bruner, Goodnow, & Austin, 1956; Hunt, Marin, & Stone, 1966; Restle, 1962 ). Later work emphasized the property that instances of a natural category could have continuous strengths such as in Rosch’s fuzzy concept model ( Rosch, 1977 ), and in the categorization models that employ various probabilistic rules to determine the category membership of test instances (e.g.,  Kruschke, 2008; Nosofsky, 1992 ). It is our view that in some cases, aspects characterizing a culture have some of the properties of natural semantic categories as theorized by Rosch; for example, some aspects may be prototypical of a culture, and others less salient.

In the case of CCT, a natural approach to incorporating degrees of truth in a model for dichotomous responses is to adapt a model for continuous response data, such as the one described in  Batchelder et al. (2010) . This CCT model handles the case where informants indicate their response to an item in the interval [0, 1], and it is intended for cases where responses are probability judgments, strengths of belief, or confidence judgments. There are a number of similarities and differences in the properties of the GCM and LTM. Both models are latent variable models, in that an observable response variable depends probabilistically on latent (unobservable) variables. However, the models differ in the types of latent variables that characterize them. As pointed out in  Batchelder and Romney (1988) , the GCM is a latent-class model (e.g.,  Lazarsfeld & Henry, 1968 ). The model specifies two latent classes of items: items that are ‘true’ for the culture, and items that are ‘false’ for the culture. On the other hand, the LTM is a latent-trait model, where the underlying truth status (trait) of an item lies on a continuum rather than being in one of two classes. Although these models have markedly different characterizations of consensus truth, it is well known that models with these two contrasting types of latent variables can be difficult to distinguish statistically on dichotomous response data (e.g.,  de Menezes & Lasaosa, 2007 ). We will see in the next section, that when both models are applied to a given set of response data, the estimates of the truth parameters for both models tend to be positively correlated. Yet it will also be shown that the models behave quite differently and are clearly distinguishable under a variety of circumstances.

This section augments both models with hierarchical assumptions, and applies Bayesian inference on these augmented models (e.g.,  Bernardo & Smith, 2000; Gelman, Carlin, Stern, & Rubin, 2004 ). Hierarchical Bayesian inference is the subject of a recent special issue of the Journal of Mathematical Psychology, edited by Michael  Lee (2011) , and this approach to inference is becoming quite popular for cognitive models since its introduction to the area by  Rouder and Lu (2005) . The key to adding hierarchical assumptions to a cognitive model is to postulate that each class of subject or item parameters is a sample from a specificed hierarchical distribution family. Then to proceed with Bayesian inference of the hierarchical model, one specifies prior distributions for the parameters of these hierarchical distributions. Occasionally, one may specify a distribution family rather than a prior distribution for a parameter of one of the hierarchical distributions; however, in this case one must specify a prior distribution for the parameters of that distribution.

With these specifications, it is now possible to conduct Bayesian hierarchical inference for both models. At this time, there are several easily-obtained and well-documented software packages that can estimate many kinds of parametric models with MCMC methods, such as WinBUGS ( Lunn, Thomas, Best, & Spiegelhalter, 2000 ), and JAGS ( Plummer, 2011 ). The only challenging aspect of our models for these software packages is the fact that the likelihood function for the LTM in  (20)  requires estimated values of the cumulative distribution function of the beta. Fortunately the software package, JAGS, was able to accommodate this function. JAGS is a robust, open-source, and free software package designed for Bayesian analysis and inference. JAGS was utilized from the R interface by using R packages: rjags and R2jags ( Plummer, 2011 ). The JAGS model code for both the GCM and the LTM is in the  Appendix .

In previous applications of the restricted GCM, researchers used MINRES to factor row-by-row association matrices using a measure of response matching, rather than correlation as  Observation 1  uses. In order to check the single truth assumption, users developed a rule of thumb, based on the ratio of the first to second eigenvalues. The rule is to assume a single factor solution if the ratio is larger than 3.0 ( Weller, 2007 ). Unfortunately this is not a good rule; for example, if the competence level of the informants is low, a large amount of noise will exist in the response data,

Furthermore,  Hruschka, Kalim, Edmonds, and Sibley (2008)  have worked with a data set that satisfies the rule of thumb for a single cultural truth, but nevertheless there is converging evidence for two consensus answer patterns on a selected subset of the items. However, it is possible to develop a Bayesian posterior predictive model check of the single factor assumption of the GCM, based on the ratio of the first to second eigenvalues, which does not suffer from these problems.

To conduct a posterior predictive model check, there are four steps ( Gelman et al., 2004 ). First, one selects a statistic of the data that he or she is interested in. Second, for each of a series of samples of the parameters found in the MCMC run, one simulates a data set from the model. Third, for each simulated data set, the observation of the selected statistic is calculated, and these values are arrayed in a sampling distribution. Finally, the observation of the statistic from the actual data is calculated, and one sees if it falls inside a credible interval of the sampling distribution of the simulated values. Throughout our analyses of simulated and real data in Sections  5.2–5.4 , this posterior predictive check is used to assess the single truth assumption of the GCM.

Finally to differentiate the two models on response data, we have employed three approaches. The first is based on the VDI statistic in  (24) . When either model is applied to a data set, we use this statistic for a Bayesian posterior predictive model check. Considerable simulation work, some of which is discussed in Section  5.3 , shows that when data are generated by one of the models, the check is passed (the data value is within the 95% credible interval) when analyzed with the generating model, but the check is often failed when the other model is used to analyze the data. This is due to the fact reported in  Observation 4 , namely that GCM-generated data sets tend to deliver small values of this statistic because of its tendency to have homogeneous item column variances. On the other hand, LTM-generated data sets typically have large variations in their item column variances. The second statistic used to differentiate the models is a more traditional model-selection statistic known as the Deviance Information Criterion (DIC) ( Gelman et al., 2004; Spiegelhalter, Best, & Carlin, 1998 ), which is provided automatically as a measure of model fit in JAGS. For this statistic, the model with the lower value of DIC is preferred. Worthy of mention is that through repeated cross-fitting studies, it was found that the GCM and LTM may appear to be highly similar if one only makes comparisons with the correlations between their inferred parameter types, especially truth and bias parameters, with generating parameters. However, as shown in this section, the actual truth values between the models may suggest highly different interpretations of the data, or may be misleading when either model is misspecified or applied in unsupported conditions. A similar theme exists for the bias parameter of each model. Correlation tests between the models on their biases also showed a strong negative correlation (near −0.98) with each model’s posterior mean biases. Yet, the bias of each model acts very differently in that for the GCM, the bias parameter only affects a response when an informant fails to know the answer, whereas for the LTM, the bias parameter affects every response by working simultaneously in combination with the truth and competency parameters of each informant and item. Thus, the models are further distinguished in that latent truth position of each item on the LTM may affect an informant’s accuracy depending on its distance from his or her bias parameter, whereas the latent truths in the GCM have no effect on an informant’s accuracy. When comparing model correlations on the competency parameter, it has been found that the GCM has difficulty in recovering LTM-generating competencies but the LTM does a better job of recovering GCM-generating competencies. This is believed due to aforementioned differences in the model designs; or another possibility is that the LTM is more ‘flexible’ (more easily fits noise in data) than the GCM (e.g.,  Pitt, Kim, & Myung, 2003 ), as the property  (21)  exists only on the latent level and not necessarily on the response level, and so the bias threshold may allow for fitting more noise.

The main goal of these applications has been to examine informants’ responses to questions about their common culture and determine if there is a single cultural consensus behind these responses. If the model supports the hypothesis of a single consensus truth, the restricted GCM is then used to estimate the cultural competence and response bias characteristics of each informant, as well as the consensus truth of each item. One of the main goals of this paper is to provide new mathematical results and Bayesian hierarchical inference for a more general version of the GCM.

One aspect of the GCM that has raised concern in some quarters is the assumption that each item definitely has a consensus answer of either ‘true’ or ‘false’, as in a two-valued logic system (e.g.  Aunger, 2004 ). Indeed, as stated earlier, our view is that there are characteristic aspects of cultural knowledge that are much better represented by degrees of truth than in a two-valued system of logic. Consequently to handle this possibility, we developed a new CCT model, namely the LTM.

On the face of it, the GCM and the LTM differ greatly in how they regard cultural truth. In addition, they appear to be quite different in other assumptions they carry as well. Nevertheless, both models have three corresponding classes of parameters pertaining, respectively, to: (1) latent-cultural truth, (2) informant competence, and (3) response bias. Both analytically and with simulated data, the paper shows when both models analyze the same set of response data, there is a high correlation between the estimated truth and bias parameters, and a moderately high correlation between their competence parameters. These correlations, however, fail to reveal major differences between the two models. Of course there will be cases where both models pass the eigenvalue ratio and VDI posterior predictive checks, and the DIC does not indicate a decisive difference in the models, and in such cases, a researcher may want to apply both models to the data. The goal of CCT is to measure cultural knowledge, not to discover a single correct model for a set of response data. Indeed, following a well-repeated remark about statistical models attributed to the statistician, George E.P. Box: “… essentially all models are wrong, but some are useful” ( Box & Draper, 1987 , p. 424); it is our view that both models are wrong, and in some cases, both can be useful. The situation is similar to that in applying signal detection models to recognition memory studies (e.g.  Macmillan & Creelman, 2005 ), where there are a number of alternatives available for specifying hit and false alarm rates into more easily interpretable parameters. Rather than laboring to discover a single, correct way to specify these parameters, it may be useful to try several on the same data set to see what generalizations hold over the several models. In the case of the GCM and LTM, if both are applied to the same set of response data, it is important not just to report correlations between the posterior means of these parameters, but also to discuss the other aspects of the posterior distribution that may be informative.

One aspect of this paper that departs from all other CCT papers is that the models are formulated hierarchically, and analyzed with hierarchical Bayesian inference. Hierarchical Bayesian inference is becoming a popular practice in cognitive modeling because it offers many advantages over classical maximum likelihood methods. One of the reasons for this is that it provides entire posterior distributions of each parameter rather than a single point estimate which is obtained by maximizing the likelihood function, often after inappropriately aggregating data that violate the independent and identically distributed assumption. This paper uses JAGS to conduct MCMC inference for the hierarchical models. JAGS is a general purpose program that can conduct Bayesian MCMC analyses for a large variety of parametric models, without the need for the user to invest in detailed programming, or manual development of his or her own MCMC sampler. Based on the analysis of both simulated and real data, we conclude that the samplers JAGS employs, as well as BUGS due to its present similarity, using the model code provided, can be used to adequately conduct inference from both the GCM and the LTM. The output in the form of properties of the posterior distributions of the parameters, illustrated in the figures for the latent-truth parameters is particularly attractive because it provides means and credible regions of each model parameter.

In addition, this paper developed a new property of the GCM in  Observation 1 , namely that the informant-by-informant correlations over item responses should satisfy a one-factor structure. It was easy to implement this property in a posterior predictive check for the GCM, and we showed that the property was also a good approximate model check for the LTM. In our view, it is an essential step before reporting a data analysis with either the GCM or the LTM, to assess the validity of their assumptions in  Axioms 1G and 1L  of a single consensus truth.

Finally, both the GCM and the LTM have assumed that items are homogeneous in difficulty; however, there are likely to be situations where there is considerable heterogeneity in item difficulty because all aspects of shared cultural knowledge are not equally salient. As mentioned earlier,  Batchelder and Romney (1988)  proposed a way to augment the GCM in order to handle item heterogeneity and it was implemented in  Karabatsos and Batchelder (2003) . One advantage of the augmented GCM is that it can handle heterogeneity in the item column counts that lead to violations of the GCM with the VDI posterior predictive check, and in this way, mimic some of the properties of the LTM.
