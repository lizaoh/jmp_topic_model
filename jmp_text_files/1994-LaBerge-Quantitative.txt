Quantitative Models of Attention and Response Processes in Shape Identification Tasks 

Davip LABERGE 

University of California, Irvine 

Two classes of attention models are applied io the identification of an item in a cluttered field: a serial modcl, exemplified by a two-process fillter/gradcd-distribution model, and a parallel model, exemplified by a single-process zoom-lens model. The two attention models generate response-time distributions and response probabilities when the serial attention model is linked with a seria! counting model and when the parailel attention model is linked to a parallel counting model. Each response counter is assumed to accumulate its counts independently of other response counters. It is shown that the two compound models give rise to equivalent response-time equations. The serial counting model is then modified by allowing the response counters to interact during processing, When the predictions of the independent and interactive counter models of response cveciution are compared with data from a target/ lanker identification task, both models predict the obtained convergence of response times obtained from the Incompatible and Compatible flanker displays, but the interactive model appearcd to give a better account of response times obtained from the Neutral flanker displays. ©) 1994 Academic Press, Inc. 

The goal of this paper is to describe two quantitative models of attention that are formulated from current knowledge of brain architecture and circuitry, and then link the attention models to response time models having properties that are similar to the recruiting of ncural impulses to fire a cell in a neural circuit. It is hoped that the resulting compound models can provide useful ways to test propertics of illention and response pencration apainst experimental data. 

The plan of the paper is (1) to describe (wo quantitative models of the attention processes involved when subjects identify a target object in a cluttered field; (2) to describe how the attention and identification processes can be joined with responsecounter mechanisms; (3) to describe three response counter models; and (4) to briefly compare predictions made by the atlention and response time models with some relevant data. 

The mathematical concepts underlying both the attention models and the response-counter models are rooted in the stimulus sampling theory of Estes (Estes, 1950; Uistes & Straughan, 1954). In the present formulations, a stimulus display is conceptualized in terms of the stimulus features residing at specific locations. The time to evoke a response is represented by the successive registration of each type of (response connected) element at the alternative counters, and when a counter first reaches its particular criterion count, it emits an associated response. The formulation of response time within the context of the Estes stimulus sampling theory as the accumulation of a criterion number of stimulus elements was given in a paper (LaBerge, 1962) that named the process of response generation “recruitment” because it seemed to resemble the temporal summation, or recruitment, of neural impulses at synaptic receptor sites of a neuron leading to an axon firing response. The recruitment model provided equations for both simple and choice response times within a discrete time framework, and several subsequent variations of the recruitment (“accurmnulation” or counting) process were also based on the discrete time assumption {e.g., Audley & Pike, 1965; Pike, 1966; Vickers, 1970; Smith & Vickers, 1988). Models of the counting process have also been proposed within a continuous-time framework {e.g., McGill, 1963; Townsend & Ashby, 1983). If the random walk process is interpreted as the incrementing of the criterion value of a counter when another counter increments its own count (as assumed in the random walk model described later in the present paper), then one can list several random walk models that are related to the recruitment or counting mechanism (e.g., Stone, 1960; Laming, 1968; Link, 1975; Link & Heath, 1975). Reviews of these and other response time models can be found in Townsend & Ashby (1983) and Luce (1986). 

Two MODELS OF ATTENTION 

To set the stage for the description of the present general models of attention, it will be helpful first to indicate the type of task to which the present attention models directly apply. Subjects are shown a horizontal array of letters and are asked to identify the letter in the center of the array by pressing one of two buttons (see Fig. 1). They are also instructed to ignore the other letters in the display. Thus, in this task, subjects know in advance the exact location of the target item in the stimulus array. This type of task contrasts with search tasks in which the location of the target item{s) is not known to the subject in advance (e.g., Shiffrin and Schneider, 1977; Treisman and Gelade, 1980; Wolfe, Cave & Franzel, 1989). The selection of the center letter for identification is assumed to require that attention be focused at the area occupied by the center item. However, if attention is not constrained solely to the center location during the period when information is recruited to evoke a response, then information from adjacent items may contaminate the flow of information to the response mechanisms and delay a response (Eriksen & Eriksen, 1974; Estes, 1972; Kahneman & Chajezyk, 1983). By manipulating the type of flanking item around the center target, one can estimate from mean response times how widely attention is spread around the center target location. For both the parallel and serial models it is assumed that the spatial distribution of attention is initially established in the position-analyzer (PA) module, which contains a map of object locations. Information flow {increased activity} at a particular location in the PA map is produced from both sensory and higher-order sources. The sensory source of information arises from a stimulus display (e.g., SSHSS) and is represented in a module named the Feature Register (FR). From the FR module location information is directed upward toward the PA module, while feature information is directed from the FR downward toward the shape identification SI module. Information continues its flow from the PA module to the higher order processing (HOP) module concerned with spatial location, and information continues its flow from the SI module to response modules (as well as to HOP modules concerned with shapes and objects). 

In Figs. 4 and 5 the locations of the letters in a five-letter string are represented as a horizontal array of circles in the PA module. A point on the distribution curve may be interpreted as the amount of activity or “resources” at the corresponding spatial location. The contribution of the projection from the FR module to the PA map would be expected to be relatively flat, given the stimulus display SSHSS, except possibly for a slightly higher amount of activity at the center location owing to the difference in features between the H and the surrounding S’s (which we do not model here). The relatively high concentration of activity at the center of the PA distribution is assumed to be due to projections from “higher order processes” (HOP) where task instructions to select the center item for identification are represented and maintained in working memory. 

Although the instructed target location may be relatively precisely represented in some way in HOP, the information flow projected from HOP to PA is assumed to fluctuate from the center location into surrounding locations (and may also fluctuate in momentary amount of activity or rate of flow). Therefore, a selection by HOP of the center of a string of locations produces a graded distribution of activity in PA at surrounding locations as their distances from the center increase. The variability of this activity distribution is assumed to be controlled to a degree in the present tasks by the duration and item size of the display (first target) that immediately precedes the probe display (second target). For example, decreasing the duration and item size of the preceding display is presumed to increase the concentration (decrease the variability of fluctuation of the HOP—PA projection) of activity near the center location. While a current projection from HOP can momentarily change the location of its mode, it cannot appreciably affect the variance or spread of the activity distribution (unless, of course, the targeted object is larger than a letter, e.g., a five-letter word; see LaBerge, 1983). Also, it is conjectured that varying duration and/or size of Target 1 within a block would induce the subject to adopt the strategy of always narrowing attention to the extent necessary to

accurately identify the Target 1 display having the shortest duration and smallest size. This conjecture is based on laboratory observations that suggest that subjects can expand attention faster then they can narrow it. Also, it is assumed that the distribution of activity in the PA module represents the stored accumulation over many trials of projected inputs from the FR and HOP modules. This storage property of the PA activity distribution is assumed to account for the “range effect” in location-cueing tasks (e.g., LaBerge and Brown, 1986). 

In the parallel identification model, shown in Fig. 4, it is assumed that the distribution of activity in PA is copied to the filter module, and from the filter module the distribution of activity is projected onto the stream of feature information flowing from the FR module (to be described in the next paragraph) to the SI module. The operation of the Filter on the activity distribution projected to it from the PA module could result in an increase in the concentration of activity at the center focation, but for simplicity considerations in this model we assume that the Filter module does not change the shape of the distribution projected from the PA module. The effect of the projected activity from the Filter is a modulation of the flow of information from the FR module to the SI module. Figure4 shows an example of attentional modulation that allows the feature information at the center location to dominate the input to the SI module while at the same time allowing feature information from neighboring locations to enter this module. In this parallel attention model, the shape identifier is assumed to process all letter locations in parallel, but letters at or near the center location are more likely to be identified at any given moment. In many respects, the operation of the attention distribution on the information flowing from the FR module to the SI module resembles the operation of the “zoom-lens” model of attention (Eriksen & St. James, 1986; Eriksen & Webb, 1989), particularly with respect to the two assumptions that (a) the centrai area of activity (resource) concentration is variable and that (b) all items falling within the field of the lens may be processed toward identification simultaneously. 

The shape identifier projects to response counters the information corresponding to the type of identification event that has momentarily occurred. Owing to previous training with the task, there exists a learned association between the output of a shape identification and a particular response counter (see the response assignments in Fig.1). The information from a specific letter identification is represented in the model as an element, and the element projects to the response counter corresponding to the learned response. In Figs. 4 and 5, the outputs from the SI over time are represented by a chain of 1’s and 0’s or 2’s and 0’s. The 1’s represent elements arising from an identification of the letter H, and the 2’s represent elements arising from an identification of the letter S, while the 0’s represent noise elements. It is assumed that only the elements of Type1 enter Response Counter 1 and only elements of Type 2 enter Response Counter 2. The 0’s serve as time spacers between successive 1’s and 2’s. 

In the serial identification model, shown in Fig. 5, the ability of the Filter to conStrict (reduce the variability of) a distribution of input activity from the PA module is necessary. Logically, one could place the constriction operation in the PA module

or assume that HOP projects directly to the input area of the SI module. However, this constrictive activity has been observed in simulations of thalamic circuit models (LaBerge, Carter, and Brown, 1992) and since the connectivity of the Filter module to the other modules corresponds to the connectivity of the thalamus to other brain areas (see, e.g., LaBerge & Brown, 1989), we assume here that the constrictive mechanism is located in the Filter module. Furthermore, we assume that the mechanism can concentrate activity sufficiently to resolve the size of one letter of the present tasks (about 0.30 degrees). 

In addition to the ability to constrain the concentration of activity to the size of one letter, the Filter mechanism is assumed to be sensitive to momentary fluctuations of activity peaks in the PA module, which reflect the fluctuations of HOP inputs to the PA map and also fluctuations intrinsic to the PA module. Therefore, the location at which the Filter concentrates its output sometimes is at a location to the right or left of the center item. Hence the serial property of item identification in this model is controlied by (a) the sensitivity of the Filter to momentary shifts of the peak of the distribution in the PA map, and {b) the ability of the Filter to constrict the variability of an input distribution to resolve exactly one letter. These two properties have been demonstrated in network simulations of the thalamic circuit (LaBerge et al., 1992). For the parallel model, on the other hand, the Filter simply projects the PA activity distribution unchanged onto the stream of information entering the SI module, and therefore has no special computational role in that model. 

Since, in the serial model, the SI module is fed information from only one item location at a time, the SI module outputs only one response element at a time. Therefore the stream of output elements flowing to the counters will increment only one counter at a time. In Fig. 5, the string of response elements entering the counters shows no cases of simultaneous arrival times while for the parallel model shown in Fig. 4 there are two instances of simultaneous arrival times of countable elements. As indicated before, the 0’s represent noise elements that do not increment the counters and therefore can be regarded as units of time between the arrivals of. elements of Type i and 2 at the counters. 

The two types of attention models may be compared within the context of a typical experimental trial. According to the serial model, the attention distribution in the PA module is built up during the warning signal in preparation for the first target (see Fig. 2) and carries over into the presentation of the first target and the immediately succeeding second target. When each target appears, the momentary peak of the PA distribution induces the Filter to constrict the activity distribution at the location of that peak and then to project it to the stream of feature information entering the SI module. Thus, there are two attentional distributions established within a trial for the serial model: one (in the PA module) expressing the preparatory aspect of attention, and the other (produced by the Filter) expressing the selective aspect of attention. For a review of the literature over the past 100 years concerned with these two aspects of attention, see (LaBerge, 1990).

In the parallel model of attention the attention distribution that is built up during the warning signal in preparation for the first target traverses the Filter unchanged to modulate information entering the shape identification module. Thus, the same distribution that operates during the preparatory phase of a trial also operates during the selective phase of a trial. Hence, in the parallel model one distribution does all of the attentional processing, while in the serial model two different distributions do the attentional processing. Later in this paper both serial and parallel models will be linked with corresponding serial and parallel responsecounter models and their predictions will be compared.

THE COUNTING MECHANISMS FOR IDENTIFICATION AND RESPONSE PROCESSES 

The foregoing equations define the attention weights over locations (the “where”) of items of a display. The weighted locations in turn modulate the flow of feature information (the “what”) from the display that enters the shape identifier module. In order to represent the specific identification events that take place during the presentation of a display, we need to relate the population of available feature elements to the identification event, which we do by means of a counting mechanism. But first we describe the counting mechanism as it is used to generate response signals. 

The event of identifying an item generates an output to the response module. This output is represented as a set of “identity” elements, and the number of elements emitted from each identity event is assumed here to be one, so that the values of the criterion counts leading to a response may be kept small enough to produce the skewed form of the response time distributions typically observed in experiments. During a display, it is assumed that several identification events take place (serially or in parallel, depending upon the type of attention/identification model assumed), so that the response counters accept a sequence of identity elements until one counter reaches its criterion and signals a response. The response signal itself can be conceived as a set of elements that eventually produces overt muscle reactions as, for example, realized in a button press, 

DAVID LABERGE 

212 

The identification mechanism is similar to the response-determining mechanism in that it also operates on incoming elements to produce a single event, which itself subsequently projects a set of elements, or impulses, to the next information processing mechanism. For example, when a set of feature elements arising from a display become available to the shape identification module, the counters corresponding to each letter identity accumulate elements of its own type until its criterion count is reached and its identification event occurs. The serial and parallel attention/identification models differ in that the serial model assumes that an element from only one location in the display is available at a time to the item counters, leading to only one item being identified at a time; the parallel model assumes, on the other hand, that elements from all locations of the display are available to the counters, and consequently more than one item can be identified at a time. If more than one item can be identified at a time, then it would seem necessary to have duplicate identification counters at every location in the visual field. For example, when the display is HHHHH, the parallel model would seem to assume that an “H” counter exists at every letter location. The serial model, in contrast, requires only one “H” counter, which operates independently of the location from which feature elements arise. 

Since the present paper is mainly concerned with modeling the attention and response mechanisms, and not with modeling the intervening identification mechanism, we attempt to constrain the complexity of the combined model by assuming the simplest version of the class of counter models for the identification mechanism; that is, that the criterion count for identifying any item is one. BEHAVIORAL PREDICTIONS OF THE ATTENTION THEORY USING Two CLASSES OF COUNTER MODELS OF RESPONSE TIME 

We now describe two general classes of models in which counters operate on an incoming stream of elements to produce a given response. As shown in Figs. 4 and 5, both classes of response counter models under consideration here assume that each alternative response counter accumulates elements of its own type until one counter first obtains its criterion number of elements, at which time the counter 

QUANTITATIVE MODELS OF ATTENTION 

217 

emits a signal for an overt response to occur. The two classes of counter models are labeled here Independent Counter Models and Interactive Counter Models. For the Independent Counter Models, it is assumed that each response counter accumulates identification elements associated with its response type independently of the accumulation in the other response counter. For the Interactive Counter Models, it is assumed that the two counters are linked in such a way that an increment in one counter causes the criterion of the other counter to be increased by one unit. One might realize this kind of interaction by inserting a pair of reciprocal inhibitory connections between the two counters shown in Figs. 4 and 5; when one counter is incremented by one element, it inhibits (cancels) one of the accumulated counts in the other counter, which in effect increases the criterion of that counter by one. 

In our consideration of the class of independent counter models, we describe the case in which elements enter only one counter at a time, and also the case in which elements may enter both counters simultaneously. The first case is exemplified by the discrete serial recruitment model {LaBerge, 1962; Luce, 1986) and the second case is exemplified by the parallel Poisson model (Townsend and Ashby, 1983). It is shown, however, that the equations for response probability and response times for the parallel Poisson model are equivalent to those of the recruitment model. 

For each model described in the following sections, we describe equations for the probability of a response, for the distribution of response times given that response, and for the mean of the response time distribution. The demonstration that the serial and parallel attention models, coupled with their respective models of response time, generate equivalent predictions for the response time densities and response probabilities for this type of task provides another instance of the kind of difficulty encountered in testing models that differ with respect to these two modes of information processing. This issue has been extensively documented and analyzed by Townsend in several sources (e.g., Townsend, 1976, 1990; Townsend & Ashby, 1983). 

While the serial and parallel models are described here in the context of a nonsearch task, one could extend their application to search tasks if appropriate additional assumptions are made. One approach is to assume that the process leading to a “covert response” is repeated for each location scanned until an overt response is made. This seemingly simple assumption requires possibly complex subsidiary assumptions about the properties of the covert response and about the mechanism that drives the scanning process. If one assumes that the scanning process is controlled by higher order processes that exert their effects through the position-analyzer module to modulate inputs to the shape identifier module, then some signal must be sent from the SI module (or a response counter) to the HOP

Changes in display size may produce changes in this profile that complicate predictions of response times as a function of display size (see Townsend, 1990; Townsend & Ashby, 1983). 

A Serial Interactive Model of Choice Time 

The assumptions underlying the interactive model of choice time to be described here are in virtually all respects equivalent to the assumptions of the serial discrete recruitment model, except for the added assumption that when one counter is incremented by the arrival of the appropriate stimulus element, the other counter’s criterion is increased by one unit. One could generalize the model beyond this rather simple case by assuming that Response Counter 1 must be incremented by n, elements before the criterion of Response Counter 2 is increased by m, units, and that Response Counter 2 must be incremented by n, elements before the criterion of Response Counter! is increased by m, units. However, for the present, we restrict our derivation to the simpler case. One can represent the interaction property in Fig. 5 by inserting an inhibitory connection from the box representing Response Counter | to the box representing Response Counter 2 and vice versa. 

The process by which elements increment the two response counters and eventually elicit a response signal from one of the counters may be conceived as a random movement toward a criterion in which the step sizes are constant. It may also be viewed equivalently as a changing difference between the accumulated counts of the two counters; when the difference reaches a particular positive value, one response is emitted, or when the difference between counts reaches a particular negative value, the other response is emitted. As a random walk, the process of incrementing one or the other counter amounts to the movement of a marker along one dimension (starting at zero), one step at a time in either the positive or negative direction. The random movement of the marker continues until the marker first reaches an absorbing barrier A at integer a, or first reaches a barrier B at integer —d.

Our comparisons of the independent (recruitment or parallel Poisson) and interactive (random walk) counter models are both general and specific. We contrast the predictions of the two models with respect to response probability and response time measures of behavior, but we also derive contrasting predictions with respect to the present flanker task experiment. 

In the interest of economy for the present paper, our comparisons of these two classes of counter models are exhibited graphically instead of analytically. The graphic comparisons also provide rapid and intuitively direct comparisons that may enable profitable inferences concerning the relationships between these response models. CONCLUSION 

When either the independent or the interactive class of output models is coupled with the present attention model, it predicts the obtained monotonic convergence 

242 

DAVID LABERGE 

of response times to Incompatible and Compatible flanker displays as attention is progressively narrowed. 

Since the mathematical results indicate that the serial and parallel (independent) counter models generate identical (or limiting) equations for response time and response probability measures (and for covert identification events in the shape identifier module), the corresponding serial and parallel attention models (see Figs. 4 and 5) cannot be differentiated on the basis of these measures. 

When the Independent and Interactive Counter Models are compared with respect to their predictions of the pattern of the present data, the Interactive Model appears to give a closer account of the response time to the Neutral flanker display relative to the response times to the Incompatible and Compatible displays.
