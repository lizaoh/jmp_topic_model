For some decades many visual scientists have been inspired by the idea of representing human vision as a linear system. In this context the visual system is supposed to process an input (stimulus) pattern of light producing the output (neural) image

                       which is assumed to be represented somewhere in the brain. An obvious benefit of such a representation is that a linear system can be fully characterised in terms of its weighting (or modulation transfer) function.

In the 1950–1960s first attempts were made to identify the modulation transfer functions of the visual system in the temporal ( DeLange, 1952, 1958 ;  Kelly, 1961, 1964 ), as well as spatial ( Schade, 1956 ;  Campbell & Green, 1965 ;  Fry, 1969 ) domain (for review see e.g.,  Brown & Mueller, 1965 ;  Levi, 1970 ;  Kelly & Burbeck, 1984 ;  Watson, 1986 , Chapter 6). Then a more sophisticated, perturbation technique was employed by many researchers to identify (i) the visual spatial line spread function ( Kulikowski & King-Smith, 1973 ;  Rentschler & Fiorentini, 1974 ;  Rentschler & Hilz, 1976 ;  Hines, 1976 ;  Wilson, 1978 ); (ii) the visual spatial point spread function ( Blommaert & Roufs, 1981 ;  Blommaert, Heynen, & Roufs, 1987 ); and (iii) the visual temporal impulse response ( Roufs & Blommaert, 1981 ).

All these early results have been derived from data obtained in detection/discrimination experiments under the assumptions that the human visual system is linear and homogeneous (in space and/or time) and the human observer's performance in detection/discrimination experiments can be formally represented as a peak detector. More specifically, these early works have, sometimes implicitly, suggested the simplest model of detection/discrimination, which consists of a linear homogeneous system (i.e., a linear filter) followed by the maximum-of-output decision rule ( Fig. 1 ).  It is well known, however, that the visual system is neither linear nor homogeneous. Still, the linear system analysis can be extended so as to be applied to the visual system without an assumption that it is linear ( Logvinenko, 1990 ) or homogeneous ( Sirovich & Knight, 1986 ;  Stewart & Pinkham, 1994 ). However, assuming that human observers perform as a peak detector seriously undermines such an approach because it contradicts other assumptions on which the perturbation technique is based ( Watson, 1982 ). Also there is direct experimental evidence against it. For instance, it is well-established that probability summation takes place in detection/discrimination experiments ( Watson, 1979 ;  Graham, 1989 ). To take into account the effect of probability summation, the maximum-of-output decision rule should be replaced by the Minkowski norm ( Quick, 1974 ;  Rashbass, 1976 ).

Abandoning the maximum-of-output-decision-rule assumption rules out the perturbation technique in its original form. However, as will be shown in the present report, this technique can be generalised so as to be applied even when the decision rule is more complicated (e.g., the Minkowski norm). More specifically, we address here the following problem. An observer's performance is assumed to be described by the detection/discrimination model ( Fig. 2 )  comprising a linear filter, the output of which is nonlinearly transformed by a transducer function followed by a decision functional of quite a general form (including the Minkowski norm as a particular case). Our particular objective is to identify the characteristics of the filter (i.e., its weighting, or modulation transfer functions) without knowing the exact form of both the transducer function and decision functional. More specifically, a new identification technique presented here (called a method of quadratic approximation), allows one to derive the characteristics of the linear filter in  Fig. 2  provided that some a priori constraints are imposed on the transducer function and decision functional. These constraints are weak enough to be met by many detection/discrimination models available in visual (and perhaps, auditory) psychophysics.

One might argue, however, that applicability of this method will be quite restrictive in the spatial (and perhaps, temporal) domain because for the last few decades a general belief has grown that at early stages in the human visual system spatio-temporal patterns are processed by independent sub-systems selectively tuned to narrow spatial frequency bands, called spatial frequency channels (for review see  De Valois & De Valois, 1988 ;  Graham, 1989 ;  Campbell & Tegeder, 1991 ;  Wilson & Wilkinson, 1997 ;  Olzak & Thomas, 1999 ). These multiple channels undermine the very idea of considering the whole visual system as a unitary system. Alternatively, detection/discrimination is supposed to be performed by a number of independent channels each of which can be represented by the model in  Fig. 2 . (For this reason detection/discrimination models in  Figs. 1 and 2  are called single-channel.) A multi-channel model can be thought of as a series of single-channel models (i.e., detectors

It is clear that without a reliable method of identification for a single-channel model one can hardly expect much progress in the identification of multiple channels. Indeed, the fundamental questions of the properties of spatial channels in human vision still remain controversial. Whilst it is agreed that they are bandpass with respect to spatial frequency and orientation, there is no general consensus on their precise spatial frequency characteristics. Not much is known about their temporal properties. Even the number of these channels is not yet clear.

While the method of quadratic approximation is, generally, designed for identifying a single-channel model it can also be used to address some fundamental problems concerning multiple channels. In particular, this method can be readily used to evaluate the characteristics of the multiple channels provided they are independent (i.e., they interact only via probability summation).

The neural image of a stimulus is understood here as an internal representation of this stimulus formed by a set of analysers (cf.  Robson, 1980 ). A neural substratum of an analyser is widely believed to be a set of neurons of the same type. A mathematical representation for an analyser is a functional, that is, mapping the stimulus patterns onto the real numbers (written Re). Analysers are usually assumed to be represented by linear functionals ( Graham, 1989 ). While we will continue the tradition of considering analysers as linear mechanisms, we shall further assume that each analyser is followed by its own local non-linearity (a transducer function).

The analysers’ outputs constitute dimensions of the internal representation of the stimulation. We believe that discrimination and detection occur in the internal space spanned by these dimensions. For example, it seems natural to assume that two stimulus objects will look the same (i.e., match each other) when they are represented by the same point in this internal space, that is, when they have the same neural images. It is well known that two stimulus objects may match each other even when they are physically quite different. So, it is a fundamental task for vision science to describe and predict pattern matching.

Any two stimulus patterns which do not match each other, can be discriminated. While matching and discrimination are two sides of the same coin, it is discrimination rather than matching that has been the focus of psychophysics since Fechner's time (for review see e.g.,  Luce & Green, 1974 ;  Graham, 1989 ;  Thomas & Olzak, 1992 ;  Boynton, Demb, Glover, & Heeger, 1999 ). So the method of quadratic approximation is presented in terms of discrimination data.

It should be noticed that although the idea of multiple spatial channels in human vision was enunciated more than 30 years ago ( Campbell & Robson, 1968 ), there is still no unequivocal evidence for their existence in the human visual system. Indeed, it is widely believed that the fullest evidence for independent channels tuned to separate spatial frequency bands comes from psychophysical experiments which exhibit a spatial frequency selectivity. Such selectivity has been demonstrated by means of experimental techniques such as adaptation ( Blakemore & Campbell, 1969 ), simultaneous masking ( Stromeyer & Julesz, 1972 ), and subthreshold summation ( Sachs, Nachmias, & Robson, 1978 ;  Kulikowski & King-Smith, 1973 ;  Shapley & Tolhurst, 1970 ;  Quick, Mullins, & Reichert, 1978 ). In these early studies, spatial frequency selectivity was described in terms of “spatial frequency tuning curves” which are usually referred to as the frequency response of hypothetical channels.

However, there are serious doubts about such interpretation of the tuning curves, obtained in adaptation and masking experiments. First of all, the spatial frequency selectivity itself does not, generally, imply the existence of spatial frequency channels. For example, the classical experiments of  Campbell and Robson (1968)  and  Blakemore and Campbell (1969)  can be accounted for without postulating spatial frequency channels ( Legendy, 1975 ;  Wilson, 1975 ).

Second, selective adaptation has been found to be non-additive as it should be if the adaptation effect is a result of fatigue within (or inhibition between) linear channels. Such non-additivity emerges from experiments on adaptation to compound gratings ( Logvinenko & Chernakov, 1984 ). These have shown that an adaptation effect from a composition of two sine wave gratings is not equal to the sum of adaptation effects from each of these two individual gratings when adapted alone. In particular, it was found that the adaptation effect from compound gratings is smaller than the additive prediction, i.e. the sum of the adaptation effects from the individual components. The lack of additivity could not be accounted for by any local non-linearity following a linear analyser ( Logvinenko & Chernakov, 1984 ). Yet more pronounced non-additivity has been documented for spatial masking ( Perkins & Landy, 1991 ).

Thus, even if spatial frequency selectivity of masking and/or adaptation is indicative of the existence of multiple analysers, these psychophysical phenomena seem to be far too complicated for the analyser's characteristics simply to coincide with the corresponding tuning curves. Still, it does not, generally, exclude the possibility that the analyser's characteristics can be derived from, for example, selective adaptation data. However, such a derivation requires developing a proper method based on a comprehensive theory of selective adaptation. Stiles’ two-threshold technique ( Wyszecki & Stiles, 1982 ) was the first (though not entirely successful) attempt to create such a method. Since  Graham (1989)  explicitly formulated a list of assumptions which are necessary for applying the two-threshold technique, it has become clear that at least some of these assumptions are definitely not satisfied for spatial frequency selective adaptation ( Graham, 1989 , pp. 91–106).

As to the evidence for independent spatial frequency channels obtained in subthreshold-summation experiments, it has been known for a long time that detectability of one stimulus can be affected by the presence of another. It was realised that the subthreshold summation reflected a sort of integration of a neural activity (excitation as well as inhibition) within some individual neural units involved in detection performance. So there have been many early attempts to use subthreshold summation to reveal the spatio-temporal characteristics of these hypothetical neural units ( Fiorentini & Mazzantini, 1966 ;  Thomas, 1970 ). However, it was not until the early 1970s when a subthreshold-summation-based method to identify hypothetical multiple channels was put forward.

In conclusion recall that it has always been an important psychophysical problem to separate a sensitivity measure (such as  d ′ index in signal detection theory) from the effect of non-sensory factors such as response bias, decision rules, etc. The latter is taken into account in terms of, for example, parameter  β  in signal detection theory. The problem becomes even more complicated when the sensory space where the decision is made is multi-dimensional. In this report we consider the case when these subjective dimensions can be represented as the outputs of a number of analysers. It has been proven that the analysers can be identified independently of its following stages including decision making. In a sense it offers a solution to the old problem.
