One of the theoretical goals of cognitive neuroscience is to develop mathematical models that link behavioral and neural levels of description. In the area of speeded decision-making, progress towards this goal has received impetus from the discovery that models of choice response time (RT) developed in mathematical psychology to account for the speed and accuracy of behavioral decisions can also characterize the time course of the underlying neural processes.  Smith and Ratcliff (2004)  reviewed recent developments in this area. The discovery of this relationship invites the question of how the evidence accumulation processes hypothesized to underlie decision-making in behavioral models can be realized computationally in the neural ensemble. The purpose of this article is to propose a solution to one of the theoretical problems that arise in attempting to link behavioral and neural models. This is the problem of how the variance of encoded stimulus representations changes with the number of neurons recruited to represent a stimulus and how these changes are expressed in models of choice RT. Pragmatically, the problem is how to ensure that the properties of a neural model and a behavioral model can be made consistent with each other.

The most influential models of choice RT are the sequential-sampling models ( Laming, 1968; Link, 1992; Luce, 1986; Townsend & Ashby, 1983; Vickers, 1979 ). These models assume that decisions are made by accumulating successive samples of noisy evidence until a criterion quantity of evidence needed for a response is obtained. The best of these models successfully account for distributions of RT for correct responses and errors and the associated choice probabilities in a variety of cognitive tasks ( Ratcliff & McKoon, 2008; Ratcliff & Smith, 2004 ). Recently, a number of authors have reported what appears to be the neural signature of the evidence accumulation process in brain structures that are active in monkeys performing saccade-to-target decision tasks ( Hanes & Schall, 1996; Ratcliff, Cherian, & Segraves, 2003; Roitman & Shadlen, 2002 ). Structures in the oculomotor control system–such as the frontal eye fields, lateral interparietal area, and the superior colliculus–which are involved in the preparation and control of saccadic eye movements, show increasing, response-locked patterns of neural firing during the pre-response interval. These patterns of firing are consistent with the interpretation that they either implement an evidence accumulation process or read out the state of an accumulation process located elsewhere in the brain.

Consistent with this interpretation, several authors have shown that sequential-sampling decision models can be used to characterize the empirically-observed relationship between neural firing rates and the speed and accuracy of decision-making. Ratcliff and colleagues showed that the parameters of sequential-sampling models estimated from RT distributions and choice probabilities of monkeys performing saccade-to-target decision tasks predict the time course and relative firing rates in build-up cells in the superior colliculus ( Ratcliff et al., 2003, 2011; Ratcliff, Hasegawa, Hasegawa, Smith, & Segraves, 2007 ). Shadlen and colleagues showed a similar relationship for cells in lateral interparietal area in a global dot motion task ( Ditterich, 2006; Gold & Shadlen, 2001 ). Schall and colleagues showed that the firing rates of visual neurons in frontal eye fields can predict saccadic decision times when the visual spike trains are used as inputs to a sequential-sampling decision process ( Purcell et al., 2010; Purcell, Schall, Logan, & Palmeri, 2012 ). These demonstrations invite the development of models in which the properties of the evidence accumulation process are derived from the point-process statistics of the underlying neural firing rates.

To this end,  Smith (2010)  and  Smith and McKenzie (2011)  proposed a theoretical link between neural firing rates and evidence accumulation via the properties of the  Poisson shot noise process . The shot noise process, which was originally proposed as a model of electron absorption in vacuum tubes ( Campbell, 1909 ), provides an idealized model of the flux in the postsynaptic potential across cells in a neural population in response to a volley of action potentials that encode stimulus information. The latter are idealized as a (possibly time inhomogeneous) Poisson process. In the shot noise model, discriminative information is carried by the difference between an excitatory and inhibitory shot noise process, which Smith called an  excitatory–inhibitory shot noise pair .

The shot noise process represents the instantaneous state of the evidence available to the decision process at a given time during a trial. At high Poisson intensities, the shot noise process weakly converges to an Ornstein–Uhlenbeck (OU) velocity process. The time integral of the velocity process, which characterizes the accumulating evidence state, is an integrated OU process, or OU displacement process. The macro statistics of the OU displacement process converge to those of the Wiener, or Brownian motion, diffusion process, which is used to model evidence accumulation in  Ratcliff’s (1978)  model of decision-making. The diffusion model has provided a successful account of speeded two-choice decisions in a variety of experimental settings ( Ratcliff & McKoon, 2008; Ratcliff & Smith, 2004 ).  Smith and McKenzie (2011)  developed the shot noise model in a somewhat different way, in order to emphasize the relationship between evidence accumulation and the statistics of recurrent loops, as proposed by Wang and colleagues ( Wang, 2002; Wong & Wang, 2006 ).

The theoretical principles of the shot noise model can be contrasted with those of the recent neural accumulator model of  Zandbelt, Purcell, Palmeri, Logan, and Schall (2014) . In their model, every neuron in the population is treated as a separate evidence accumulator and the focus of their article was on how to obtain well-behaved RT properties from a large ensemble of weakly correlated accumulators. In the shot noise model, the ensemble statistics of individual neurons are aggregated across the population to form a single, diffusive accumulation process. The aim of the model was to provide a theoretical basis for diffusive evidence accumulation, motivated by the success of diffusion models in accounting for RT distributions and response accuracy from a wide variety of experimental paradigms ( Ratcliff & Smith, 2004 ). The neural accumulator model of Zandbelt et al. currently only predicts RT; it has not yet been extended to accuracy.

The OU process is used in the shot noise model as an intermediate step between the shot noise process and the Wiener process, which differs from the way in which it has been used in previous models of decision making. In the models of  Busemeyer and Townsend (1993) ,  Diederich (1995) ,  Smith (1995) ,  Smith and Ratcliff (2009) ,  Usher and McClelland (2001) , and others, an OU velocity process was used to model accumulating evidence. To obtain well-behaved RT distributions using the velocity process in this way, the OU decay parameter must be comparatively small; otherwise most of the mass of the OU stationary distribution will fall inside the decision criteria, leading to extremely skewed RT distributions ( Ratcliff & Smith, 2004 ). In the shot noise model, an OU velocity process with fast decay is instead used to model the instantaneous evidence state and the accumulating evidence is modeled as its time integral, which is an OU displacement process. The mean and variance of the OU velocity process become increasingly different from those of the Wiener process as the OU decay parameter is increased, whereas for the OU displacement process the converse is true: As the OU decay rate is increased, the convergence of the mean and variance of the displacement process to those of the Wiener process is increasingly rapid. The statistics of these various processes are discussed subsequently.

In diffusion models of decision-making, the predicted choice probabilities and distributions of RT are given by the first-passage probabilities and first-passage-time distributions of the evidence process through absorbing boundaries, which represent decision criteria. Unlike the Wiener process, there are few closed-form, first-passage-time results known for the integrated OU process.  Smith (2000)  investigated the first-passage-time properties of a single-barrier process using a rational approximation derived by  Hesse (1991)  and of a two-barrier process using Monte-Carlo simulation. For barriers of both kinds, he showed that families of RT distributions for an integrated OU process were well approximated by a Wiener diffusion model. This is as would be expected from the asymptotic equality of the means and variances of the evidence accumulation processes.

The main difference between the two families of distributions was that integrated OU process distributions with a zero value of the nondecision time (denoted  
                         
                            
                               T 
                            
                            
                               
                                  er 
                               
                            
                         
                       in Ratcliff’s model), were best fitted by a Wiener process model with positive nondecision times. The additional nondecision time reflected the fact that the mean and variance of the integrated OU process grow nonlinearly to their asymptotic values, whereas in the Wiener process they are linear for all values of time. The rate of approach to linearity of the mean and variance of the integrated OU process depends on the rate at which the OU velocity process approaches stationarity, which depends on the decay constant,  
                         α 
                      . In the shot noise decision model,  
                         α 
                       is assumed to represent fast processes at the cellular level, so the rate of approach to asymptote should be correspondingly fast. For the parameters used in Smith’s simulations, which were chosen to yield distributions like those found empirically, the nondecision times were estimated to be around 50–200 ms, which is well within the range of estimates of

Smith (2010)  concluded that evidence accumulation processes that can be described at the neural level by Poisson shot noise processes will be well characterized at the behavioral level by the Wiener diffusion process. This analysis provides a way to link cognitive and neural representations theoretically, and an explanation, at a conceptual level, of the success of decision models in which evidence accumulation is represented as a Wiener process (e.g.,  Ratcliff & Smith, 2004 ).

The assumption that discriminative information is carried by the difference between excitatory and inhibitory shot noise processes circumvents a problem that would otherwise arise when attempting to derive a choice RT model from assumptions of Poisson stimulus encoding, namely, the problem of variance scaling. The natural prediction of Poisson models is that the mean and variance of the encoded sensory representations will be equal to each other, because the number of Poisson distributed events in a stimulus sample from an interval  
                         
                            [ 
                            0 
                            , 
                            t 
                            ] 
                         
                       will have a mean and variance both equal to  
                         λ 
                         t 
                      , where  
                         λ 
                       is the Poisson rate. This scaling relationship is normally not a problem in Poisson signal detection models (e.g.,  Smith, 1998 ), in which the predictions depend only on  
                         μ 
                         / 
                         σ 
                      , the ratio of the mean to the standard deviation, and not on how they vary individually across conditions. In contrast, diffusion models need to assume a constant or near-constant diffusion coefficient in order to correctly predict empirical distributions of RT.

In diffusion process models, the diffusion coefficient sets the clock of the process, which determines how quickly evidence diffuses towards the absorbing boundaries. Large values of the diffusion coefficient mean that the evidence diffuses towards the boundaries rapidly. In most applications of the model to data, the observed families of RT distributions are well described by a model in which the diffusion coefficient is constant across experimental conditions. For this reason, the value of the diffusion coefficient is often set, a priori, to an arbitrary value and used as the fundamental scaling parameter of the model. In most applications, allowing the diffusion coefficient to vary with the drift rate, as implied by a Poisson scaling assumption, leads to predicted RT distributions unlike those found empirically. At low stimulus discriminabilities, the combination of a low drift rate and a small diffusion coefficient produces a large mean, extreme dispersion, and large shifts in the leading edge of the RT distribution, as indexed by its 0.1 quantile. The majority of choice RT distributions do not exhibit these features.

Exceptions to this characterization have been found when stimuli are embedded in dynamic noise ( Ratcliff & Smith, 2010 ) or are presented at low levels of contrast ( Gould, Wolfgang, & Smith, 2007; Smith & Ratcliff, 2009 ). Under these circumstances, reductions in stimulus discriminability result in shifts in the leading edge of the RT distributions, together with an increase in their mean and dispersion. One way to capture these shifts is to allow the diffusion coefficient to vary with the drift rate ( Donkin, Brown, & Heathcote, 2009; Smith, Ratcliff, & Sewell, 2014 ). However, the range of variation in the diffusion coefficient needed to capture such data is much less than is predicted by Poisson scaling, even under conditions of Poisson overdispersion.

However, it is difficult to see how, in a population in which only a fraction of the cells respond selectively to the stimulus, that a signal-to-noise ratio could be generated that is large enough to support the level of discriminative performance that is observed behaviorally.

The purpose of this article is to investigate conditions under which these conditions cannot, a priori, be satisfied, because the number of neurons assumed to represent a stimulus changes across experimental conditions. Its main purpose is to identify a scaling relationship that will satisfy the constant-diffusion requirement for stimuli of this type. The specific application to which these results apply is to models of the information, or resource, capacity limitations of visual short-term memory (VSTM), although the results are not restricted to this particular paradigm.

Fig. 3 
                       summarizes the results of a VSTM experiment reported by  Sewell, Lilburn, and Smith (2014)  that was designed to investigate the memory representations that support perceptual decision-making. Their study was motivated in part by earlier work by  Ratcliff and Rouder (2000)  and  Smith, Ratcliff, and Wolfgang (2004) , who showed that speeded decisions about brief, backwardly-masked stimuli are well described by a diffusion model with constant drift, in which there is negligible decay of the stimulus representation during the course of an experimental trial.  Smith and Ratcliff (2009)  argued that decisions about briefly-presented stimuli are based on durable stimulus representations in VSTM, which preserves stimuli in a post-iconic form for the second or so needed to identify them ( Coltheart, 1980; Phillips, 1974 ). Smith and Ratcliff proposed a mathematical model of VSTM formation, in which noise perturbed, time-dependent, VSTM traces provide the input to a diffusion process. Their model has successfully characterized the RT distributions and psychometric functions from tasks in which the quality of the information entering VSTM varies as a function of attention, spatial uncertainty, visual masks, and stimulus onset transients ( Gould et al., 2007; Sewell & Smith, 2012; Smith, Ellis, Sewell, & Wolfgang, 2010; Smith et al., 2004 ).

In  Sewell et al.’s (2014)  study, one, two, three, or four vertically or horizontally oriented Gabor patches were presented in Gaussian noise for 50, 100, 150, or 200 ms, and then backwardly masked by high-contrast checkerboards. Observers reported the orientation of a single Gabor patch at a location that was probed 50 ms after presentation of the mask. Sewell et al. limited their maximum display size to four items to avoid any item capacity limits that may be present because their aim was to characterize the information capacity limitations that arise when item capacity limits are not exceeded. Currently there is controversy about whether the capacity of VSTM is best conceived of as an item capacity limit, a resource capacity limit, or a combination of the two ( Donkin, Nosofsky, Gold, & Shiffrin, 2013; Rouder et al., 2008; van den Berg, Awh, & Ma, 2014 ). Converging behavioral and physiological evidence, reviewed by Sewell et al., suggests that the item capacity limit of VSTM, if it exists, is around four items ( Anderson, Vogel, & Awh, 2011; Cowan, 2001; Luck & Vogel, 1997; Todd & Marois, 2004; Xu & Chun, 2006 ), which was the maximum display size used in Sewell et al.’s study.

                         The critical condition for obtaining fixed-variance representations is the normalization condition (4). The normalization of Poisson shock amplitudes discussed in the previous section can be realized dynamically by systems of shunting differential equations. In shunting equations, unlike the more commonly used additive equations of linear systems theory, the input to the system or the system’s own internal state multiplicatively gates the change in its response. The Hodgkin–Huxley equations of neural conduction are equations of this type ( Tuckwell, 1988 ). The properties of shunting equations have been analyzed in detail by Grossberg and colleagues ( Grossberg, 1987a,b, 1988 ); a tutorial introduction focusing on normalization can be found in  Smith, Sewell, and Lilburn (2014) .  Smith and Sewell (2013)  used systems of shunting equations in their competitive interaction theory of attention and decision making to model the dynamics of attentional selection and VSTM trace formation.

The results in the previous sections follow straightforwardly from the properties of the shot noise decision model described by  Smith (2010) . Despite their simplicity, they are important theoretically because they allow us to show that models, which are not, a priori, theoretically and mathematically compatible with each other, can be made so by an appropriate scaling assumption. The sample-size model provides a parameter-free account of the VSTM representations that support two-choice decision-making. The Poisson neuron model provides an account of how the sample-size model might be realized neurally, based on the assumption that VSTM is represented by a fixed number of neurons that are shared equally among stimuli, and in which each neuron is idealized as an independent Poisson process. This model provides a simple account of the invariance of

Most behavioral data require a model in which the diffusion coefficient is either constant or varies over a fairly small range of values. Difference coding, by excitatory–inhibitory shot noise pairs, leads to a process in which the diffusion coefficient remains constant as discriminability is varied. This suffices as a model of single stimulus processing, but fails as a model of VSTM, in which a variable number of stimuli are represented in the memory system.

The normalization condition of Section   4  assumes that the amplitude of each Poisson event is reduced in proportion to the square root of the number of Poisson processes representing a perceptual object, such that the variance of the instantaneous representation remains constant. This is expressed in Condition 4, which can be viewed as a power-based normalization condition on stimulus coding. Section   5  showed how normalized coding can be realized computationally, via shunting inhibition within a neural population.  Carandini and Heeger (2012)  argued that normalization is pervasive throughout the nervous system and called it a “canonical neural computation”. Pragmatically, the benefit of assuming Condition 4 is that the sample-size model of VSTM capacity and a behaviorally consistent diffusion model of RT can be obtained from the same Poisson model of the stimulus representation.

Their competitive interaction model of VSTM assumed that trace-strength growth is controlled by the square of the trace-strength amplitude, or trace-strength power, which allowed them to derive the sample-size model from the normalization properties of shunting equations. Their model involves a nonlinear Riccati equation with similar dynamics to Eq.  (19) , in which the predicted VSTM growth follows a hyperbolic tangent function ( Smith, Sewell, & Lilburn, 2014 ). This contrasts with the linear shunting equation used in the integrated system model of  Smith and Ratcliff (2009) , in which VSTM growth is negative exponential. Our preliminary investigations suggest that a hyperbolic tangent model of VSTM growth may not fit RT data as well as an exponential model. The dilemma this creates is that a trace-strength power model of VSTM growth can realize the sample-size model predictions, but possibly at the expense of poorer RT predictions.

One way to resolve this dilemma is to assume, as proposed by  Bays (2014) , that VSTM normalization acts on the Poisson rates representing stimuli rather than on the VSTM traces themselves. This would simultaneously yield the predictions of the sample-size model, via the signal-to-noise properties of Poisson processes, and an exponential VSTM growth function, via the properties of linear shunting equations. To develop the model in this way, the variances of the stimulus representations and the diffusion coefficient of the approximating diffusion process need to remain constant as the number of neurons representing a stimulus changes. The population-size-dependent normalization condition proposed in this article provides a way to achieve the required invariance. In sum, then, population-size-dependent normalization provides a way to reconcile the RT model of  Smith and Ratcliff (2009)  with the multichannel attentional selection model of  Smith and Sewell (2013) , and with the experimental results of  Sewell et al. (2014) . It allows the sample-size model to be derived from a fixed neural capacity model of VSTM in a way that yields plausible RT distribution predictions and which is consistent with previous work modeling attentional selection in near-threshold decision tasks. Population-size-dependent normalization provides the link that allows these various strands of theory and model-building to be reconciled mathematically in a consistent way.
