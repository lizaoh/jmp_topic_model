This study uses a non-homogeneous, conditional point process with feedback to model human learning of unusual rhythms. It offers multiple points of difference to most other tapping studies: the complexity of the rhythms, the point-process analysis, and the fine temporal resolution of our models.

Many previous studies have used tapping experiments to shed light on perception of metre and rhythm. In  spontaneous tapping  experiments ( Essens & Povel, 1985; Fraisse, 1946, 1956 ), participants are asked to tap after an imprecise verbal description of a rhythm.  Continuation tapping  experiments require participants to continue tapping a pulse or rhythm after the cue has ceased.

Synchronisation tapping  refers to experiments in which participants try to tap along with an audible “cue”. In many of these  Aschersleben (2002), Herff, Herff, Milne, Johnson, Shih, and Krusienski (2020), Nozaradan, Peretz, and Mouraux (2012)  the cue sequence (that is, the target rhythm) is isochronous. A number of other synchronisation tapping experiments focus on participants’ adaptation to gradually changing rhythms, or perception and performance of the ratio between a long and short interonset time ( Goldberg, 2015; Polak, Jacoby, Fischinger, Goldberg, Holzapfel, & London, 2018; Repp, London, & Keller, 2005, 2011, 2012, 2013; Snyder, Hannon, Large, & Christiansen, 2006 ). Only a few, such as  Povel and Essens (1985), Repp et al. (2005)  and  Snyder et al. (2006) , to our knowledge, address challenging rhythms.

A variety of statistical methods have been applied to tapping data in the previous literature. Time-series analyses of tapping data are offered in  Launay, Dean, and Bailes (2013)  and  Pressing (1987) , with one observation per pulse, typically using a window around each pulse or cue to determine which taps to consider as simultaneous events. One focus has been the distribution (mainly mean and variance) of tap asynchrony ( Repp, 2005; Repp & Su, 2013 )—each tap sufficiently close to a cue is attributed to that cue, and the timing difference (asynchrony) is taken as an observed variable of interest. Asynchrony is difficult to define in a natural way for taps midway between pulses, or when multiple taps occur near one cue. Furthermore, for complex non-isochronous rhythms, some taps are not meaningfully associated with any actual cue because they result from an incorrect prediction of a cue’s presence. Another focus has been accuracy and bias in participants’ reproductions of the ratio between a long and short beat ( Repp et al., 2005, 2012, 2013 ). In each case, statistical estimates or models describe not the raw observations themselves, but higher-level, aggregated variables.

The synchronisation tapping experiment producing the data analysed here was also reported in  Dean, Bulger, and Milne (2021)  and  Milne, Dean, and Bulger (2021) . Informed consent was obtained from all participants, and the experiment was approved by the Western Sydney University Ethics Committee (H9869). It required participants to tap along to an audible cue sequence that was  not  isochronous, but a periodically repeating subset of an isochronous sequence of  pulses , which were also sounded, with a different tone. The experimental design was chosen to be challenging (most of the rhythms were difficult, and participants did not have long to learn them) to ensure that many errors would be made, since it is only in participants’ errors that we can gain insight into the learning process. Further detail appears in Section  2 . In  Milne et al. (2021) , Bayesian regression was used to model tapping accuracy as predicted by several measures of rhythmic shape, many of which were novel, in order to characterise the rhythmic qualities influencing tapping accuracy at the rhythm level and the pulse level. In  Dean et al. (2021) , a mixed-effects time series model focussed more specifically on participants’ improving performance of each rhythm over the time during which it was heard. The present study takes a finer-grained approach, agnostic to the underlying isochronous pulse; for instance, any tendency of the participants to tap in time with the pulse but not between pulses is revealed by the experimental data, rather than assumed  a priori .

Here we model participants’ tapped responses as a non-homogeneous, conditional point process ( Daley & Vere-Jones, 2003; Kass, Eden, & Brown, 2014 ). That is, we model a participant’s instantaneous probability of tapping as depending on recently heard cues (within the last rhythmic cycle) and the participant’s own recent taps. Conditional point processes have been used to model communication among songbirds ( Stowell, Gill, & Clayton, 2016 ), seismic activity ( Ogata, 1988 ), wildfires ( Peng, Schoenberg, & Woods, 2003 ) and neural spiking activity ( Truccolo, Eden, Fellows, Donoghue, & Brown, 2005 ), the similarity being events that occur at random, but with a varying propensity that is affected by exogenous variables  and  the time or location of other similar events. For example, when a neuron spikes, it cannot spike again until a short refractory period has passed, and its propensity to spike at any moment thus depends both on its incoming stimuli and its own recent spike history ( Kandel, Schwartz, Jessell, Siegelbaum, Hudspeth, & Mack, 2012 ). A similar circumstance prevails here since, after tapping when a cue is expected, a participant is unlikely to tap again at least until the next pulse.

Our key objectives were to determine the temporal changes in propensity to tap in relation to position in the rhythmic cycle, and to demonstrate learning capacities for different rhythms, simple and complex. We also wished to model a fine grained temporal dependence of the refractory tendency, taking account of all taps. In the process we could then define the distribution of taps within the pulse period around a cue. These objectives were framed in terms of the two dominant theories of sensorimotor synchronisation. The clock theory ( Povel, 1981; Povel & Essens, 1985 ) suggests that the listener to a repeating rhythm creates a hierarchical internal mental clock, aligned with which the position of cues can be expected. Because our rhythms included some that are complex, we provided a continuous isochronous pulse which we envisaged could replace or drive such a clock, reducing cognitive demand. The dynamic attending theory ( Jones & Boltz, 1989 ), in a related way, suggests that certain regularly (equally and unequally) spaced pulses form attentional foci ( beats ) that are expected to bear significant rhythmic events, aiding synchronisation with them. When learned expectations are strong and correct, dynamic attention can be “future oriented”, predicting the next event; in this situation, the theory is akin to predictive processing (see discussion). When expectations are weaker and less often correct, “analytic” heuristics may take over (for example, so as to tap every second or every third pulse). We demonstrate aspects of both kinds of dynamic attention in what follows. The theoretical background is discussed in more detail in  Dean et al. (2021) .

Ideally, the statistician’s role is merely to help the data set to tell its own story. Every assumption we make in pre-processing the data or analysing it goes untested in the analysis. A conditional point process is an attractive model for the data from this experiment; the model and its estimation are somewhat sophisticated, but make few presuppositions, and the data pre-processing is minimal. For instance, while  Dean et al. (2021)  and  Milne et al. (2021)  allocate each tap to the closest pulse (even if it is nearly as close to another pulse) and discard any additional taps within the window around each pulse, the point process analysis handles the timing of all taps in a more natural and continuous way. We shall find that this model does fit the observed data rather well (as assessed by the deviance ratio

We consider a point process model of participants’ tapping response as a fine-grained predictive behavioural model. We model a participant’s instantaneous probability of tapping as a function of the recent history of both cues and taps. We allow for the participant’s familiarity with the rhythmic pattern, and thus tendency to tap the rhythm correctly, to grow as each performance progresses. Following  Kass et al. (2014) , we conceptualise the process as a continuous-time, non-homogeneous, non-Markov point process ( Daley & Vere-Jones, 2003 ), but in practice model it in discrete time. For clarity, we expand here on the technical concepts and modelling decisions.

But our model should allow the possibility for a participant to learn a rhythmic pattern well, in which case, in a small window around a cue, we would expect to see a mean number of taps very close to 1, with a much smaller variance than 1 (as we see in  Fig. 1 ). We can also gauge fit quality via Q–Q plots of rescaled interarrival times. (“Interarrival times” and “intertap intervals” are synonymous; we use the former term for agreement with the point process literature.) Rescaling is a mathematical trick that converts an observed instance of a point process model into a Poisson process with constant intensity 1, by transforming the time axis. For a point process model, the  rescaled interarrival time  between two consecutive events is the integral of the intensity function (that is, the expected number of events) in the interval spanned between the two events. If the model  (1)  is a correct description of the observed process, then the rescaled interarrival times should form an independent sequence of exponentially distributed random variables with mean 1. A visual diagnostic of the distribution is given by a so-called Q–Q plot, plotting the empirical cumulative distribution versus the theoretical cumulative distribution. For this purpose, we further transform the rescaled interarrival times via the standard exponential CDF, to yield an ideally uniform distribution on the interval  
                            
                               [ 
                               0 
                               , 
                               1 
                               ) 
                            
                         . Thus a good fit will yield a nearly straight Q–Q plot. We also provide pointwise 95% confidence bands. For more details, see  Kass et al. (2014)  and  Brown, Barbieri, Ventura, Kass, and Frank (2001) .

Lastly, we will verify performance of the selected models by fitting simulated data. According to Box’s adage that “all models are wrong” ( Box, 1979 ), we of course do not expect this point process model to perfectly describe human behaviour; rather, we hope that it fairly depicts some important effects. However, we must ensure that it is self-consistent, and in particular, that if the truth  were  as simple as this model, that our methodology would accurately fit it, and recover the model’s parameters. Therefore, to verify the model-fitting process, we simulate 300 performances of 30 seconds each according to the model  (2) , using again the tresillo rhythm shown in  Fig. 2 . We use the same 6 ms discretisation as with most of the real data, and the same regularisation hyper-parameters.

The underdispersion indicates slightly fewer short and long interarrival times than the model predicts. To avoid the need for rescaling, suppose the target rhythm were metronomic, with pulses one second apart. We would expect the interarrival (inter-tap) times to mostly be close to 1. To describe that, we require a peak of intensity near each pulse. With no refractory effect, the number of taps at each pulse would be Poisson distributed, yielding a  mean  of 1, but the model would expect too many lower and higher tap counts at each pulse, implying too few low and high interarrival times. The resulting Q–Q plot would look somewhat like (a).

Since the participants have tapped along with regular, deterministic rhythms, we expect the observed tap sequence to reflect that regularity. This model tries to modify a Poisson process enough to accommodate that regularity, and the near straightness of (c) shows that it mostly succeeds.

The non-homogeneous, conditional point process described in  (2)  provides a faithful description of participants’ tapping in this experiment, as seen most easily in the Q–Q plots in  Figs. 5–8 . In many ways, and in spite of participants’ overall comparatively poor performance ( Dean et al., 2021 ), the results confirm our hypotheses, and are consistent with dynamic attending ( Jones & Boltz, 1989 ): thus we observe a general tendency for accuracy to improve over the course of each performance, faster learning of rhythms with lower and more conventional pulse counts (3, 4, 6 and 8), and a significant refractory (feedback inhibition) effect that decays within the duration of one pulse. On the other hand, the numerous examples of poor performance observed ( Dean et al., 2021; Milne et al., 2021 ) such as displayed in  Figs. 10 and 12 , are indications of failures of dynamic attending, or other forms of prediction. Both the complex feedback mechanism, and the distribution of taps around a pulse suggest considerable variability between individual events and between people in their tapping and tapping mechanisms, as we also describe in the companion papers ( Dean et al., 2021; Milne et al., 2021 ) and earlier work ( Launay et al., 2013 ). It is interesting to contrast these results with iterated-tapping studies ( Jacoby & McDermott, 2017 ) in which participants can adjust three-component rhythmic patterns until they reach a preferred position. These may be interpreted as indicating concentration of inter-arrival times around integer ratio values; while the emphasis of the present results might more naturally imply symmetrical concentration around any focal point for tapping. The two studies are addressing different objectives and do not directly speak to each other: however their contrasting implications are suggestive for further work.

The single-rhythm models are each based on a smaller sample than the period-specific models, and thus more variable in their quality even when not ill-conditioned. Most of the Q–Q curves in  Fig. 9  are close to straight, but several show a marked distortion, indicating a poorer model fit. While, overall, allowing a longer refractory effect yielded inferior fits according to the AIC, it may have provided the required modelling flexibility in some specific cases, such as the 6-pulse rhythm considered in Section  4.2 , by letting a participant’s tap probability at one pulse depend on whether previous pulses had been tapped.

In this study we have applied a novel technique to model participants’ learning of difficult rhythms. We tried to make as few assumptions as necessary, and be led by the data. An agnostic approach to model selection removed predictors that played no useful role for our rhythmic data but, if retained, could conceivably reveal other effects in different data. Two examples are multiple-cue effects and cue-tap interactions.

With a longer pulse, and especially with an interaction effect between recent cues and taps, we might have been able to model phase-correction techniques, that is, participants’ efforts to correct their sense of when the pulse occurs, in reaction to recently having tapped slightly early or late, though it is not clear that a point process model is best suited to that. Many previous studies have targeted phase-correction specifically, in both isochronic ( Aschersleben, 2002; Herff et al., 2020; Nozaradan et al., 2012 ) and nonisochronic settings ( Goldberg, 2015; Polak et al., 2018; Repp et al., 2005, 2011, 2012, 2013; Snyder et al., 2006 ), and their methods are better suited to studying such behaviour.

Each period-specific model in this study assumed a fixed duration for the rhythmic cycle, but did not directly assume the phase or frequency of the pulse train. It would be interesting to analyse similar data in which a fixed cycle duration was divided isochronously into various numbers of pulses, since then a single model could encompass all of the data. On the other hand, this sort of model could be applied to cycles of variable length by measuring time in fixed fractions of the cycle, rather than absolute units; this would also allow analysis of differing pulse rates. Since the submission of our paper, an interesting Bayesian point-process model of rhythmic entrainment has been published ( Cannon, 2021 ). This concerns generally successful performance or perception in relation to a repetitive rhythmic cycle, with clearcut beats (attentional foci), and regular or slightly irregular temporal subdivisions thereof. This model, like ours, relies on performer uncertainty, but it particularly focuses on probabilistic variation of continuously updating assessments of phase. The paper does not model empirical data, but evaluates a range of simulations. Its possible utility on data such as ours remains to be investigated, and our more complex rhythms with two interonset intervals arranged so as to obviate the induction of a regular beat might require multiple or hierarchical phases to be calculated. Our rhythms thus seem to present some additional problems. This modelling development will be of interest.

Another possible future direction would be the analysis of imperfectly cyclic cue sequences, to model participants’ ability to follow gradually changing rhythms. Lastly, an interesting extension of this work would be to include tap velocity data via a  marked  point process model.
