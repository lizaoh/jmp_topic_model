In (pure) mathematics, numbers are dimension-less objects. In the empirical world, by contrast, numbers come dressed in physical dimensions. The numbers in everyday life are written on classroom blackboards, appear on computer screens, or sounded by a teacher. In the absence of a physical/sensory dimension, no human would have ever perceived a number. Given that each number is a combination of semantic and physical attributes, one can ask: How do people perceive the  physical  dimension of the number? In particular, does numerical magnitude affect perception of the digit’s physical size? Many studies (see,  Dehaene, 1997 , for review) show that it does, which, in turn, led researchers to argue that the arithmetic/semantic dimension – numerical magnitude – is processed in an automatic fashion just whenever a numeral is presented for view for any purpose. Given the automatic processing of number, a number–physical size interaction is inevitable whenever different numbers in different physical sizes are processed (especially in tasks referring to physical size). In this study, we subjected the assumption of automatic number processing to new scrutiny, harnessing state-of-the-art tools of stochastic modeling. To anticipate our conclusions, we show that the architecture governing processing excludes the possibility of a number–size interaction. Consequently, we challenge the assumption of automatic processing of numerical magnitude.

When presented with a pair of numerals that differ in physical size, people select the  physically  larger member of the pair faster when this member is also numerically larger than when it is numerically smaller. Thus, the selection of the larger format is faster in the pair, 7-2, than in the pair, 2-7. The dimensional values match in the first pair (a congruent display), but conflict in the second pair (an incongruent display). The difference in performance between congruent and incongruent trials (favoring the former) defines the size congruity effect (SCE). One does not need to present pairs of numerals in order to observe the SCE. When presented with a single numeral, it takes people longer to decide that the physical format is small when the numeral is 9 than when the numeral is 2  (Algom, Dekel, & Pansky, 1996; Choplin & Logan, 2005; Fitousi, 2014) . The SCE has been obtained with numbers written in different notations and in different tasks (e.g.,  Besner & Coltheart, 1979; Fitousi, 2010; Ito & Hatta, 2003; Schwarz & Ischebeck, 2003 ), with natural and negative numbers  (Henik & Tzelgov, 1982; Pansky & Algom, 1999, 2002; Pinhas & Tzelgov, 2012; Pinhas, Tzelgov, & Guata-Yaakobi, 2010; Schwarz & Heinze, 1998; Szücs & Soltész, 2008; Tzelgov, Ganor-Stern, & Maymon-Schreiber, 2009; Tzelgov, Meyer, & Henik, 1992) , and with single- and double-digit numbers  (Fitousi & Algom, 2006; Fitousi, Shaki, & Algom, 2009; Ganor-Stern, Tzelgov, & Ellenbogen, 2007; Santens & Verguts, 2011) . Given its pervasiveness, the SCE is commonly taken to reflect on the mandatory processing of numerical magnitude. It is this view that we challenge in the current study.

The impressive evidence notwithstanding, pervasive biases in the standard experimental design call into question the obligatory nature of the SCE. Little stimulus alchemy suffices to eliminate the SCE or reverse the effect such that physical size intrudes on perception of numerical magnitude more than vice versa (the reverse-SCE;  Fitousi & Algom, 2006 ).  Algom et al. (1996)  identified two critical biases prevalent in published SCE studies. First, there is a glaring asymmetry in the number of stimuli used for the numerical and the physical dimensions. Typically, the numbers 1 to 9 (inclusive) are used for the former, but only two or three values (small, medium, large) are used for the latter. Virtually all pertinent research pitted a finely grained numerical dimension against a coarse physical dimension. This asymmetry itself can determine the observed interaction ( 
                         = 
                      SCE).  Melara and Mounts (1994)  have shown that the mere number of stimuli on an irrelevant dimension affects classification performance on the relevant dimension (see also  Melara & Algom, 2003; Sabri, Melara, & Algom, 2001 ).

For another bias, the relative salience or discriminability of values along the number and the size dimensions was not matched. Discriminability is matched if the values along the numerical dimension differ perceptually from one another to the same extent as do values of size along the physical dimension. This information is critical because the more discriminable dimension can disrupt performance on the less discriminable dimension (e.g.,  Algom et al., 1996; Fitousi & Algom, 2006; Melara & Mounts, 1994 ). Thus, irrelevant numerical value will disrupt performance with physical size ( 
                         = 
                      SCE) if the values of number differ psychologically from one another more than do the values of physical size from one another. Mismatched discriminability favoring numbers was present in virtually all studies of the SCE. However, when care was taken to match discriminability (and number of values along the two dimensions), the SCE collapsed. And, when physical size was purposely made more salient than numerical value, a reverse SCE emerged ( Algom et al., 1996; Fitousi, 2014; Fitousi & Algom, 2006; Fitousi et al., 2009; Pansky & Algom, 1999, 2002 ; see also,  Melara & Algom, 2003; Sabri et al., 2001 ). The malleability of the SCE casts doubt on the automatic nature of processing numerical magnitude. The question of the mandatory nature of the SCE is related to the fundamental contrast in cognitive science drawn by  Garner (1970, 1974)  between integral and separable dimensions ( Garner, 1970, 1974, 1976; Garner & Felfoldy, 1970 ; see  Algom & Fitousi, 2016 , for a review). Separable dimensions of an object do not intrude on one another in processing, whereas integral dimensions do — due to holistic perception of the object. The elimination of the SCE in the Algom studies is consistent with the view that numerical magnitude and physical size of numbers are separable dimensions, whereas the robust appearance of the SCE is consistent with the traditional view that number and size are integral dimensions.

All objects of perception are not experienced in the same way. Some show a character of totality, a  Gestalt  (cf.  Heidbreder, 1933 , p. 331), whereas other objects are experienced as separate features, which are simply grouped together in space and time. The objects in the first class are composed of integral dimensions, whereas the objects in the second class are composed of separable dimensions. Integral dimensions are processed in a unitary fashion, so that observers cannot attend exclusively to one dimension of the object without concurrently noticing the other dimension. It is the cross-talk between the constituent dimensions that yields the observed interaction in performance. Prototypical examples of integral dimensions are hue, brightness, and saturations of colors. According to the traditional view  (Dehaene, 1997) , numerical magnitude and physical size form another example of integral dimensions, expressed by the SCE.

Separable dimensions, by contrast, remain distinct in processing. Observers can successfully ignore a task-irrelevant dimension when attending to the target dimension. Prototypical examples of separable dimensions are color and shape of an object or size and angle of diameter of circles. Given independent processing with separable dimensions, performance with each does not yield interaction (facilitation, interference). The studies by Algom and his associates can be taken to show that numerical value and physical size are separable dimensions.

The assumption of automatic activation of numerical value is consistent with integral processing because magnitude is always activated and intrudes on physical size. However, the presence of an SCE may result from identifiable biases in the typical experimental design, not from truly integral processing. Consequently, the presence of the SCE does not, by itself, imply an interactive architecture. It is at this juncture that application of stochastic modeling is invited. The explication of the underlying architecture that sustains the SCE is vital for the understanding of its source and, and in general, for our understanding of number. We elected to deploy the tools of stochastic modeling subsumed under the umbrella of system factorial technology (SFT;  Townsend & Nozawa, 1995 ) in order to uncover the system underlying processing of the SCE in numerical cognition.

Four distinct yet interrelated features of performance are specified in the SFT: (1) Architecture (serial vs. parallel, and, if the latter, coactive or not); (2) Stopping rule (minimum time vs. exhaustive); (3) Capacity (limited vs. unlimited vs. super), and (4) Stochastic independence (independence vs. dependence). The SFT has been serving mathematical psychologists as a tool for theoretical resolution in a variety of simple visual detection or memory-search tasks (e.g.,  Fific, Little, & Nosofsky, 2010; Fific, Nosofsky, & Townsend, 2008; Fitousi, 2015; Townsend & Wenger, 2004 ). In this study, the task was not simple visual detection, but a decision with respect to numbers (see  Algom, Fitousi, & Eidels, 2017 ). The participants decided on each trial whether the number was larger or smaller numerically than a specified boundary or whether its physical size was larger or smaller than a criterial physical format.

We made use of the SFT version known as the  double factorial paradigm  (DFP;  Townsend & Nozawa, 1995 ; see also,  Algom, Eidels, Hawkins, Jefferson, & Townsend, 2015 ;  Eidels, Townsend, & Algom, 2010 ). We illustrate the paradigm as it applies to the current stimuli in  Fig. 1 . Our stimuli varied along numerical magnitude (large, small) and physical size (large, small) of the presented digits. The binary values of the two dimensions were combined to form a factorial design. The target was defined as a large stimulus on either dimension or both. A notable aspect of the DFP is that a second variable, salience, is also manipulated along each dimension. For the dimension of numerical magnitude, the target number could be salient, namely, noticeably larger than the boundary of 5 (i.e., 9) or less salient and closer to the boundary (6). This manipulation of numerical salience makes use of the well documented distance effect (  Fitousi & Algom, 2006 ;  Moyer and Landauer, 1967 ). For the dimension of physical size, the format could be salient, namely, noticeably larger than the boundary of 2 cm (6 cm) or less salient and closer to the boundary (4 cm). For trials including targets on both dimensions (hence double-target trials), salience creates a second factorial design (salience of the target magnitude: low, high; salience of the target size: low, high).

It is assumed that the two manipulations of salience  selectively  influence the respective processing of numerical magnitude and of physical size (see e.g.,  Sternberg, 1969; Townsend & Nozawa, 1995; Van Zandt, 2000 , for the critically important notion of selective influence). This means faster and more efficient processing of salient targets than less-salient targets on each dimension. Moreover, rendering numerical magnitude salient does not affect the processing of physical size and, conversely, presenting a salient physical size does not affect the processing of numerical magnitude. We test for selective influence via the survivor ordering condition, but note another approach developed by Dzhafarov and his colleagues (e.g.,  Dzhafarov, 2003; Dzhafarov, Schweickert, & Sung, 2004 ).

The Miller inequality is recast in the SFT as a statement about the capacity of the system. If the rate of processing a target is slowed down when presented along with another target, the system is of  limited capacity  (there is not a double-target advantage). When processing a target is the same in single and in joint presentations, the system is of  unlimited capacity . Notably, if the presence of two targets speeds up the ability of the system to process each target, the system is  supercapacity . Concerning the present study, the discrimination between number and size was trivially easy in which case, “the standard capacity coefficient...is...still useful” ( Little et al., 2015 , p. 29). The natural candidate architecture for generating the SCE is coactive processing. Automatic activation of numerical magnitude means that number information is always present along with physical size information (the relevant dimension in many studies) so that the two coactivate the response. Their interaction is expressed in the observed SCE. The coactivation can come in the form of coalescence into a common a-modal magnitude representation. Prominent theories assume the presence of such a representation, sustained by activity in the intra parietal sulcus (IPS; see e.g.,  Walsh, 2003 ; see also  Dehaene, 2007 ;  Piazza, Pinel, Bihan, & Dehaene, 2007 ). In this architecture, numerical magnitude and physical size coalesce into a single representation downstream. This architecture is consistent with the violation of the race model inequality and with supercapacity in processing (when magnitude and size correspond). In sum, the SCE is the behavioral manifestation of an underlying interactive architecture.

However, the absence of the SCE in many experiments casts doubt on the automaticity assumption. The approach advocated by Algom denies the assumption of automaticity in numerical processing  (Namdar, Ganel, & Algom, 2018; Pansky & Algom, 1999, 2002) . As an aside, Algom’s approach is also at variance with the theory of a common magnitude code (  Van Opstal & Verguts, 2013 ), a notion that is actually inconsistent with the main body of classic psychophysics ( Marks, 1974; Marks & Algom, 1998; Stevens, 1975 ). All of this means that an architecture entailing coactivation or other forms of interaction is not absolutely obligatory.

The next plausible candidate architecture is the benchmark model of minimum time parallel independent race with unlimited capacity. In this architecture, there does not exist a cross-talk between number and size. The number horse does not know the position, pace, indeed the very existence of the size horse. Inevitably, the SCE, when it is present, does not derive from an interaction in processing. It can rather reflect a redundancy gain due to statistical facilitation (  Raab, 1962 ; see  Eidels, 2012 ;  Eidels et al., 2010 , for a similar theory with respect to the Stroop effect). For an exhaustive stopping rule, the fact that the system must await the processing of the slower signal might be taken as a weak form of dependence itself.

The least plausible candidate architectures for generating the SCE are serial models, whether minimum time or exhaustive. How can an interaction, a redundancy gain, or the SCE ensue from such processing? To a first glimpse, they seem impossible. After all, one signal is processed only after the other signal has completed processing, so that there is no overlap between the two. On this background,  Townsend and Nozawa (1997)  have nevertheless proved that a redundancy gain is still possible under a  serial  exhaustive architecture.

Although an interactive architecture, in particular coactivation, is the model favored by the traditional view, it is problematic on two counts. First, the absence of the SCE in tightly controlled studies is inconsistent with the model. Second, the absence of an a-modal magnitude code detracts from the plausibility of coactivation.

Of the remaining models, both the benchmark parallel race model and some form of serial processing are possible. According to the former, the SCE, when it is present, derives from statistical facilitation in the face of redundant target. Consequently, this model also predicts relatively small amounts of SCEs.

Finally, we cannot exclude the possibility of a serial model. Following  Townsend and Nozawa (1997) , redundancy gain expressed as SCE is possible under serial processing. It is possible that one attribute is more salient or favorable to be processed first. Such an asymmetry leads quite naturally to serial processing.

To conclude, an SFT analysis, sustained by the DFP design, permits one to decide between the theoretical approaches suggested for an understanding of the phenomenon of the SCE.

Let us issue a methodological caveat with respect to the task used. In the typical SCE study, the observers respond to a single dimension of the digit – its physical size – while attempting to ignore the task-irrelevant dimension of numerical value. In the current SFT paradigm, by contrast, the task was that of divided attention: The observer attended to  both  dimensions in order to report the presence of at least one target. Note however that the current task invites an interactive architecture more strongly than does the typical selective attention task. The instructions calling on observers to attend to both dimensions virtually force an interaction — if there is one. Interactive processing is the natural result invited by the present task. Uncovering a non-interactive architecture would be all the more notable when viewed on the background of the environment created by the current task (cf.  Eidels et al., 2010; Fitousi, 2016a; Townsend & Wenger, 2014 ).

In the present study, we made use of the rich arsenal of the SFT toolkit in the quest of specifying the system underlying a numerical processing. We were particularly interested to see whether the processing of numerical magnitude and physical size is interactive. In two experiments, the participants decided, while timed, whether at least one target was present in the display. In Experiment 1, we used the common, spatially integrated version, in which number and size were features of the same digit. In Experiment 2, we used a spatially separated version  (Dormal & Pesenti, 2007; Fitousi & Algom, 2006) , in which physical size was varied via the length of a line drawn beneath the digit (of a constant size). Using a different stimulus preparation increases the generalizability of our results. If the SCE is based on the mandatory interaction of number and size, a coactive architecture with supercapacity is expected. If the SCE is an optional effect, a parallel or a serial architecture is possible. The latter architectures are incompatible with the view that the SCE is a mandatory effect and that the effect results from the automatic activation of number.

Consider fixed-order serial models, focusing on the target-distractor displays. When the channel containing the distractor is processed first, the inequalities remain unchanged so that double target displays are faster than single target plus distractor displays. However, if order is fixed such that the channel containing the single target is processed first, the redundant target effect is no longer present. This outcome is of great importance for understanding the variability in the literature with respect to the presence and magnitude of the SCE. As we recounted, the SCE is often present, but it is absent in a fair amount of cases. Our results show that when the SCE is present, the underlying architecture is serial minimum time of mixed-order or of a specific fixed-order. Order is fixed, so that the distractor is processed first in target-distractor displays. In contrast, in studies in which the SCE is absent, the underlying architecture is serial minimum time, but now the channel containing the single-target is processed first in target-distractor displays.

Can one predict the precise species of the serial minimum time model used and, consequently, the appearance of the SCE? We argue that prediction is possible given that processing is governed by the contextual factor of relative dimensional salience discussed earlier  (Fitousi & Algom, 2006; Melara & Algom, 2003) . The SCE is bound to appear when the task irrelevant dimension of numerical magnitude is more salient than the task relevant dimension of physical size. This was the case in the great majority of published SCE studies. Note that enhanced salience increases the probability for distractor number to be processed first. As we just showed, this order must yield a SCE. When number and size are equally salient, the SCE might or might not appear. This setup is consistent with a mixed-order architecture that sometimes favors the distractor and sometimes favors the target in priority. As Algom and his associates have shown, the SCE is not present in cases in which the to-be-ignored dimension, is less salient than the target dimension (see  Fig. 1 , again). This last setup favors priority for target processing in target-distractor displays, a fixed-order that does not yield the SCE.

Possible Violations of the Race-Model Inequality under Exhaustive Processing

Our data are consistent with a serial minimum-time model. The data also entailed appreciable amounts of redundant target advantage, but did not in general violate the race model inequality. On this background, it seems instructive to take a look at serial exhaustive models.  Townsend and Nozawa (1997)  have provided a proof that such models can violate the race model inequality. Again, two different features of the current situation should be kept in mind. First, we found self-terminating not exhaustive processing, and, second, the redundancy gains found, while appreciable, did not violate the race model inequality. With these points in mind, the development detailed in  Townsend and Nozawa (1997) , pp. 598–599) for an exhaustive stopping rule can be applied virtually unchanged here. Their proof enjoys generality (subject to the perfunctory change in a parameter due to the alternative stopping rule). Given the proof by  Townsend and Nozawa (1997) , it follows immediately that  with stochastically independent, serial minimum-time processing, and with context independence in force, there exist distributions with stochastically faster times to targets than to non-targets in which violations of the race-model inequality are possible . Note that our results are a bit more conservative as we did not even witness violations of the inequality.

In conclusion, the results show that the SCEs observed in our study do not derive from an interaction between number and size. Serial models exclude the possibility of such an interaction. Simultaneously, our results challenge the theory of automatic activation of numerical magnitude. Such activation mandates interaction in processing. Again however, interaction was absent in our results. We note that the authors of a recent study using other methods  (Sobel, Puri, Faulkenberry, & Dague, 2017)  have similarly reported independent processing of the dimensions of number–size stimuli. The results of the recent study by  Namdar et al. (2018)  cast serious doubt on the automaticity account of the SCE. Therefore, the present results show that the SCE is not obligatory. The contextual factors identified by Algom and Fitousi arguably determine the model and the priority of processing.
