MACKAY  Indiana University  A probabilistic multidimensional scaling model is proposed in which the coordinates of each stimulus may be correlated and  have distinct variances on each dimension. Maximum likelihood estimates of the parameters are obtained. The model is compared  to deterministic and isotropic models. Properties of the model are described. o 1989 Academic Press, Inc.  Thurstone's (1927) unidimensional theory of comparative judgment relates the proportion of times one stimulus is judged to  dominate another stimulus to the parameters of the probability density functions (PDFs) of the random variables which are  assumed to describe the individual stimuli. In this paper, a multidimensional, anisotropic version of the model is presented.  Anisotropic models have a number of desirable properties. Unlike isotropic distributions which are spherically symmetric and  characterized by their invariance under orthogonal transformations, anisotropic distributions allow for directional dominance  by permitting the stimuli to have unequal variances on different dimensions. The coordinates of each stimulus may also be  correlated. This property should be attractive to investigators in a variety of disciplines. (See Eaton (1981) and Ripley  (1981) for a further discussion of the properties of isotropic and anisotropic distributions.)  Along with other probabilistic models, anisotropic models have the ability to both accommodate error-laden data and to test  hypotheses. Hypotheses can be formulated with respect to coordinates or scale values, distances, and the variancecovariance  structure. The ability to test hypotheses about the structure of the variances may, in some cases, be of as much interest to  researchers as the ability to test hypotheses about the coordinates of the stimuli. In sensory perception one might, for  example, want to know if one dimension was perceived as homogeneously as a second. Similarly, a political scientist might have  an interest in knowing if the variability in the perceptions of subjects about two candidates on a particular axis were the  same. RELATION OF THE PROPOSED MODEL TO THURSTONE'S THEORY  OF COMPARATIVE JUDGMENT  Thurstone's theory assumes that stimuli may be represented along a psychological continuum by real-valued random variables.  When a subject is asked to express a dominance judgment about stimuli with respect to a particular attribute, the outcome is  determined by a comparison of the values of the random variables. If two stimuli are being compared and X; is the value of the  random variable associated with stimulus i, then the subject is said to judge S; as having more of the attribute of interest  than S; if X; > Xj.  Thurstone assumed that the random variables were normal random variables and that successive observations were independently  sampled. In this paper, the independence assumption is also made.  The probabilistic unidimensional model of Thurstone was extended by Hefner (1958) to a r-dimensional setting where for  stimulus Si, the coordinates xik, k = 1, ..., r, are assumed to be normally and independently distributed with means Pik, k =  1, ..., r, and variance 62. This model was later generalized by Zinnes and MacKay (1983) so that distinct variances Q; could  be estimated for each stimulus. In this paper, another extension is made so that distinct variances v k may be estimated on  each dimension and the coordinates of each stimulus may also be correlated.  2. PARAMETER ESTIMATION  The primary difficulty faced in implementing this anisotropic model for distance judgments is, of course, the inversion of the  characteristic function. Equation (11) in general cannot be explicitly evaluated. Several approaches to evaluating (11) have  been suggested in the literature on quadratic forms in normal variables. These approaches can be divided into two groups,  those that estimate the PDF exactly, or within a specified degree of precision, and those that approximate the PDF.  There are two major alternatives for estimating the density function exactly. The first seeks to numerically integrate (11). A  variety of numerical integration methods, differing primarily with respect to the path of integration (Grad and Solomon, 1955;  Imhof, 1961; Rice, 1980; Helstrom, 1983), have been proposed and some computer algorithms have also been published (Koerts and  Abrahamse, 1969; Davies, 1980). Unfortunately, the numerical integration approaches seem to have substantial difficulty when  the absolute values of the random variable are small and when there are few terms in the summation of (4), conditions which  may not be likely in some contexts but which are quite likely in a MDS context. In addition, some numerical integration  methods require the prior estimation of other terms, such as saddle points, which may necessitate a separate Monte Carlo  analysis. This is time consuming and prone to computational overflow and underflow problems for very small and very large  values of the random variable.  The second primary alternative to evaluating (11) exactly is to express the characteristic function (10) as an expansion of  the characteristic functions of other distributions whose PDFs are well known. By far the most popular approach is to express  (10) as a linear function of central chi-square PDFs. A good description of this method is in Johnson and Kotz (1970).  Algorithms for this task have been published by Sheil and O'Muircheartaigh (1977) and Farebrother (1984). In general, the  approach is to start with (9) and set  (P(t) = Y e;9(t, j)  (13)  where 9(t, j) is the characteristic function of a central chi-square distribution with r + 2j degrees of freedom. Advantages of this approach over some of the other possible expansions are first that the computations are  aided by being able to express the successive terms of the X2 distribution functions recursively and second that a bound for  error in truncating the series can also be readily computed so the estimation can be within any desired degree of precision to  the PDF. Given an estimate of the PDF, the estimates of the parameters are found by maximizing the log of the likelihood function which  is the sum of the logs of the PDFs over all observations. The only other details of parameter estimation that need to be  covered are two-the procedure for obtaining initial estimates and the method used for maximizing the likelihood function.  Initial estimates are found by the procedures discussed in Zinnes and MacKay (1983). Initial estimates of the variances are  thus for an isotropic process. The maximum likelihood estimation phase of the model quickly assigns separate variances for the  different dimensions and it was not thought necessary to obtain initial estimates with anisotropic properties.  This anisotropic model has been incorporated into the PROSCAL (MacKay and Zinnes, 1986) family of probabilistic scaling  programs. Like the other models in this package, the user has the choice of specifying different numerical optimization  algorithms for obtaining the maximum likelihood estimates. All of the examples cited later in this paper have employed a  stepwise search method (Chandler, 1969).  4. The Probability of Being Cassified as a Nearest Neighbor and the Difference in the Variances of the Dimensions  Theoretically, if one had a very large variance on one dimension and a very small variance on the other dimensions, a large  sample should provide some difference in the configurations obtained with an anisotropic and an isotropic model. With the real  data sets we have analyzed, configurations have differed more under Case III than Case V conditions.  Despite the similarity of their configurations, isotropic and anisotropic models are capable of estimating quite different  variances and of testing whether the likelihood of the anisotropic model is significantly higher than the more parsimonious  isotropic model. For some applications, the estimation of distinct variances and dimensional covariances may be of direct  interest.  In addition to estimating location and variance-covariance parameters, MDS models can be used to estimate the probability that  a particular stimulus will be judged to be the nearest neighbor of another stimulus. A significant advantage of probabilistic  MDS models is that one does not have to resort to ad hoc rules, such as assuming that the probability is proportional to the  distance, to do this (DeSarbo, DeSoete, and Eliashberg, 1987; Keon, 1983; Lehman, 1971). Instead, the properties of the  probabilistic model can be used to directly compute the probability of one stimulus being the nearest neighbor of another.  Determination of the probability that one point will be nearest another is used frequently in preference models to determine  the probability of first choice (Shocker and Srinivasan, 1979), but it can also be used with distance models. One application  of this would be if an advertiser wanted to see if an advertising campaign resulted in a particular product being perceived  more or less like another product.  200 DAVID B. The probability of one point's being a nearest neighbor to another point will  not, in general, be a monotonic function of the distance separating them. (See Ennis and Mullen (1986) for a similar  conclusion involving the triangular method of difference testing.) If the maximum variance for two points does lie along the  line connecting them, then there is a finite value of that variance which will maximize the probability of the points being  nearest neighbors.  6. AN EMPIRICAL EVALUATION  In this section, results from a study employing subjects' unidimensional estimates of the locations of 30 cities in the United  States are used to establish a hypothesis about the nature of distance judgment variances. A second set of data on distance  judgments among 15 United States cities is then analyzed with isotropic and anisotropic models to determine if the  expectations derived from the first set of data are realized.  As part of an earlier study (MacKay and Zinnes, 1981) on probabilistic MDS, 13 subjects were asked to estimate the coordinates  of the 30 largest cities in the United States. For each city, the subjects estimated how many miles east or west and north or  south the city was from the town where the questionnaire was administered. To increase the comparability of this study with  the subsequent one, only judgments pertaining to the 15 largest cities were evaluated.  For each city, the variance across subjects in the east-west and north-south dimension was calculated. The average variance  for the 15 cities along the east-west dimension was 11.7E5; along the north-south dimension the average variance was NE5. The  variances were significantly different (p<0.001). The covariance was 3.OE5.  In the second study (MacKay, 1983), 19 subjects were asked to judge the interpoint distances among all pairs of the 15 largest  cities in the United States. For the probabilistic MDS analysis, the log likelihood of the isotropic model was -14,747 and the  log likelihood of the anisotropic model was -14,500. The models are thus significantly different (df = 2, p < 0.001). Location  and variance estimates of the anisotropic analysis were transformed to maximum congruence with the physical coordinates of the  cities. The final transformed variances on the east-west and north-south dimensions were 9.1E4 and 1.2E4. The estimated  covariance was 3.OE4. Both studies, one involving coordinate estimation and the other involving distance estimation, thus lead  to the same conclusion, namely, that there is a difference in the variance of subjects' judgments on east-west and north-south  axes and, furthermore, that the variation on the east-west axis is greater. In both studies, the covariance was also positive  and between the two variances in magnitude. The overall level of variation using coordinate judgments was higher using than  when distance judgments.  8. DISCUSSION  All of the examples in this paper have involved two-dimensional analyses.
