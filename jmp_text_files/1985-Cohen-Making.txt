INTRODUCTION  Decision making under uncertainty is a term which covers a wide range of states of information concerning relevant events:  In one extreme case-risk-every event has an objective probability. In the opposite extreme case-complete ignorance-the  decision maker possesses no information whatsoever on the events. Unlimited numbers of intermediate cases are conceivable.  In the Bayesian theory-a fairly widely accepted normative model of behavior under uncertainty-all these cases are formally  reduced to a single one, namely risk: every event has a-possibly subjective-probability. This is a powerful result; however,  for both theoretical and experimental reasons, the Bayesian model has not received universal acceptance.  Please send requests for reprints to J. Y. The aim of this paper is to provide a partially affirmative answer to this question-a partial answer, since we only consider a  particular case of uncertainty which we call mixed uncertainty. Precisely, we assume that the decision maker is under complete  ignorance with respect to a certain subfamily of events, whereas conditionally on the realization of any elementary event of  this family he is in the situation of risk with respect to another subfamily of events (events of both families having an  effect on the outcomes of his decisions).  Mixed uncertainty is typically encountered in classic parametric statistics, where it is generally assumed that no prior  probability measure (nor any other information) concerning the parameter space s known, but that, conditionally on any 0 E  O, the sample distribution is completely determined. Interactive methods of assessment already exist for the utility function u [see, e.g., Schlaifer (1971) or Keeney & Raiffa  (1976, Chap. 4)]; outcomes being then expressed in utility units, the assessment of value function v can be made; more  precisely, indifference curves (iso-value curves) can be constructed and a value attached to each of them; the available  techniques of construction are the standard ones for two-attribute value functions (see, e.g., Keeney & Raiffa, 1976, Chap.  3); however, in questions posed by the analyst to the decision maker, the outcomes must be expressed in their original (say,  monetary) units; thus pair (m, M) is described as the possibility of winning an unknown amount of money which could range from  4.2.3.
