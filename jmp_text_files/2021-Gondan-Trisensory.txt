The  redundant signals task  is one of the most basic experimental setups in research on multisensory processes. In this task, participants respond in the same way to stimuli of two sources which are presented either alone (single signals) or in combination (redundant signals), for example, auditory ( 
                         A 
                      ) and visual stimuli ( 
                         V

                            A 
                            V 
                         
                      ) are faster than to the single signals—this effect is called the redundant signals effect. Several models have been suggested to explain the effect, including separate activation models (often denoted as context-invariant “race models”, see below) and coactivation models ( Miller, 1982 ).

Separate activation models  assume that processing of the redundant signals occurs in separate channels in a race-like fashion, with the faster of the two channels determining the response. The slower channel is canceled out, which, on average, yields a speedup of responses to redundant signals effect ( Raab, 1962 ). A critical additional assumption is context invariance, stating that the channel-specific processing times in the single-stimulus conditions have the same distribution as in the redundant stimuli (e.g.,  Luce, 1986 , p. 129;  Townsend & Wenger, 2004 ). The difficulties in localizing the source(s) of trisensory interactions have already been acknowledged by  Diederich (1992, 1995) , and articles on trimodal redundancy gains generally draw careful conclusions about the mechanisms that underlie potential violations of the selected upper bound. Nevertheless, it is fair to state that current practice is diverse ( Table 1 ), and the choice of the specific inequality is not always theoretically justified.

The manuscript is organized as follows: We first derive  Miller ’s ( 1982 ) original inequality for two redundant signals. The purpose of this exercise is to familiarize the reader with the notation and its key ingredients, such as context invariance and the class of distinct-signal coactivation models. We then introduce some basic lemmas for the union of several events; these lemmas will be useful in the later derivation of the upper bounds for three sensory channels. It will be shown that for responses to trimodal stimuli, several upper bounds can be derived for the redundancy gain, depending on whether zero (Ineq.  (2) ), one (Ineq.  (3) ), two (Ineq.  (4) ) or three distinct signals for pairwise coactivations are permitted (Ineq.  (5) ). Inequality  (5)  is “new” in the sense that it has not formerly been derived. We then discuss the limitations and the generality of the approach, and we outline statistical procedures for testing the different inequalities in data from individual participants or groups thereof.

In the literature on redundant signals tasks, there is no universally accepted definition of race models. Some authors use a general definition that allows for specific deviations of context invariance (e.g., interactive race model,  Mordkoff & Yantis, 1991 ; parallel decision models,  Otto & Mamassian, 2012 ). In contrast,  Miller  ( 2016 ) uses a more restrictive definition of race models that includes context invariance, which was originally coined “separate activation model” ( Miller, 1982 ). We adopted Miller’s restrictive notion of context-invariant race models mainly due to practical reasons—none of the above conclusions work without the context invariance assumption. Indirectly, this pragmatic decision bears on theoretical grounds; as Miller puts it convincingly, the “class of race-like models without the assumption of context independence is so broad that it can never be falsified.” (p. 516). It should be kept in mind that any observed violations of Inequalities  (1) – (5)  only rule out the respective context-invariant race model  (M0)  to M5. Such violations might occur because the race model mechanism is wrong, or because (some of) the processing times vary across contexts and engage in an interactive race, or any combination thereof. Generally speaking, violation of a prediction of Model X does not prove that Model Y holds, and specifically (e.g.,  Otto & Mamassian, 2017 , p. 10), violation of Inequalities  (1) – (5)  of this article does not “prove” the validity of any specific coactivation model.

The list of alternatives to context-invariant race models is quite long (e.g.,  Miller, 1982 , Appendix;  Miller, 1986 , Ineq.  (3) ;  Diederich, 1995; Mordkoff & Yantis, 1991; Schwarz, 1989, 1994; Ulrich & Miller, 1997 , Table 1;  Otto & Mamassian, 2012 ), and each model or class of models is, in principle, a promising candidate to describe the mechanism of bi- and trimodal integration in the derivations above. Moreover, in a brain with billions of neurons and connections it is probably futile to search for a uniform mechanism of multisensory integration that describes the redundancy gain across different tasks and environments. In our derivation above, we chose the class of distinct-signals models ( Ulrich & Miller, 1997 )—this choice was again driven by feasibility: without the additional racer, the derivations above would fail. This extreme example illustrates the need for a distinct coactivation signal in the derivations of Inequalities  (3) – (5) : The distinct signal guarantees that coactive processing of bi- and trimodal stimuli can only become faster, but never slower than the race of the constituents ( Colonius et al., 2017 ). In the redundant signals task, this assumption is plausible since participants are asked to make the same response to all the different signals. With more demanding tasks (e.g., conflicting information,  Fournier & Eriksen, 1990 ), it might not be tenable anymore. In other words, if the distinct signal is dropped by allowing, for example, inhibitory crosstalk between the channels (e.g.,  Mordkoff & Yantis, 1991 ; or the diffusion difference model for temporal order judgments,  Schwarz, 2006 ), the whole derivation breaks down.
