A Measurement of the Certainty Effect

Universita�t zu Kiel 

In order to measure the certainty effect, the present paper tries to develop an axiomatic model of decision under risk which can accommodate the certainty effect and is, apart from this property, equivalent to expected utility. This leads to a new concept denoted by certainty preference which implies that a higher utility index is assigned to a consequence in the case of certainty than to the same consequence in the presence of risk. Now, a notion of comparative certainty preference can be obtained which will be characterized in terms of properties of the derived utility representation and allows for a generalization of the Arrow�Pratt measure of absolute risk aversion. � 1998 Academic Press 

Key Words: certainty effect; nonexpected utility; risk aversion.

The purpose of the present paper is a measurement of the certainty effect as explained later. Therefore, we develop an axiomatic model of decision under risk which (i) is consistent with the certainty effect and (ii) is equivalent to expected utility in all other choice problems. In the next section we sketch the quantitative evaluation system and show that the model is purely descriptive due to the implied violations of first-order stochastic dominance. A set of axioms and a representation theorem are presented in Section 3. Section 4 explores some behavioral implications of the derived preference functional. It is shown that, in order to satisfy condition (i), a new concept has to be integrated into the model which is labelled certainty preference and leads to a natural separation of the notions of risk aversion and decreasing marginal utility of wealth. In order to measure the certainty effect, a notion of comparative certainty preference is proposed which can be characterized in terms of properties of the derived utility representation and allows for a generalization of the Arrow�Pratt measure of absolute risk aversion. Section 5 provides some remarks on the related literature and, finally, Section 6 contains concluding observations. Most proofs are relegated to the Appendix.

It seems natural to call the functions u( } ) and v( } ) utility and value function, respectively. There exists another approach in the literature which, apart from the risky utility function u(} ), additionally employs a value function v( } ) defined on the set of certain consequences.[2] In this approach, it is argued that u( } ) reflects both strength of preference for certain consequences and risk attitude. In order to separate these concepts, u( } ) is compared with the cardinal value function v( } ), which measures preference intensities under certainty. A decision maker is then defined to display intrinsic or relative risk aversion if u( } ) is a concave transformation of v( } ). Note that this approach does fully agree with expected utility theory because it is not based on a violation of the independence axiom; i.e., u( }) represents preferences on the whole set P as in expected utility theory. Therefore, this approach is distinct from expected utility with certainty preference despite the similarity of the functions employed. 

However, the idea that the utility function u( } ), in contrast to expected utility theory, does not represent preferences for certain consequences has already been proposed in the literature. One example is the concept of healthy-years equivalents introduced by Mehrez and Gafni (1989) in the literature on health economics. In response to some criticism of their approach, Mehrez and Gafni (1993) argue that risky situations are evaluated in their model by the expected utility with respect to the function u( } ), while the value function v( }) is maximized in riskless situations. However, the choice between one riskfree and one risky lottery is, according to Mehrez and Gafni (1993), a risky situation and, therefore also the riskfree lottery is evaluated by u(} ) in this case, which leads to violations of transitivity as pointed out by Johannesson, Pliskin, and Weinstein (1993) and others. Wakker (1996) argued that the theory of healthy-years equivalent is, therefore, based on a misunderstanding about the difference between risky versus riskless utility and, hence, a misunderstanding about the distinction between 2 and P�. In contrast, expected utility with certainty preference is consistent with transitivity, since here riskfree lotteries are always evaluated by v( } ). 

Also Bernard (1974, 1986) argues in favor of the distinction between u( } ) and v( } ). However, it may be argued that it is not Bernard's main intention to focus this issue rigorously because his approach also proposes deviations from expected utility theory on the set P� and, as Bernard (1986, p. 173) admits, his theory ``lacks formal proof.'' One major criticism on Bernard's model are the implied violations of firstorder stochastic dominance. 4.2. Risk Aversion and Comparative Certainty Preference 

Recall that in expected utility theory a preference relation is said to display weak local risk aversion if $E( p) � p, where E( p) is the expected monetary value of the lottery p # P�.[6] From the work of Pratt (1964) it is well known that this condition is equivalent to the condition that the utility function is concave on X. This equivalence has been criticized by Yaari (1987, p. 95) as follows: ``at the level of fundamental principles, risk aversion and diminishing marginal utility of wealth ... are horses of different colors. The former expresses an attitude (sic!) towards risk (increased uncertainty hurts) while the latter expresses an attitude towards wealth (the loss of a sheep hurts more when the agent is poor than when the agent is rich).'' In order to separate the concepts of risk aversion and diminishing marginal utility of wealth, Yaari (1987) has proposed a utility function which is linear on X and has integrated the possibility of risk aversion by transforming cumulative probabilities. More recently, Wakker (1994) and Chateauneuf and Cohen (1994) showed that by transforming cumulative probabilities a separation of marginal utility and risk aversion can be at least partly obtained even if utility is not assumed to be linear on X. 

The following analysis shows that a separation of both concepts can be achieved also without probability transformation. As in other nonexpected utility theories, the notion of risk aversion has to be defined as an aversion against mean preserving spreads in risk.[7] Interestingly, the property of Certainty Preference is implied by risk version within the present framework.

5. REMARKS ON THE RELATED LITERATURE 

Even though expected utility with certainty preference seems to be very close to expected utility, it has to be classified as a variant of nonexpected utility theory since its rests on a weakened version of the independence axiom. Nonexpected utility theory is usually divided into three classes of models:[10] utility theories with the betweeness property, rank-dependent utility, and local expected utility analysis. The present model can be assigned to none of these classes. This is because, in contrast to expected utility with certainty preference, all those theories satisfy Continuity and imply violations of Independence on the set P� if they are not equivalent to expected utility.[11]

6. CONCLUSION 

Since the bulk of observed violations of the independence axiom is due to the certainty effect we developed a model which can accommodate the certainty effect and yet retains as much of expected utility as possible. The main problems of this approach are the necessary discontinuities and the resulting violations of dominance. Further research could identify significant functional relations between u(} ) and v(} ). Additionally, it seems interesting to investigate empirically how the degree of certainty preference varies with changing individual wealth.
