A fundamental question in neuroscience is how the human brain makes sense of its environment. How does the brain manage to learn about, represent and recognize statistical invariances in the environment that guide its actions, ultimately ensuring our survival in a world that is in a continuous state of flux? The representation of invariant features of the environment is the subject matter of sensory neuroscience. In recent years, advances in computational modeling have changed the way sensory cortical responses to external stimuli are investigated. The objective of the present work is to provide an overview of these advances and to point out new directions in this exciting field of research. In the following, a number of concepts are introduced that are at the root of computational modeling in sensory neuroscience.

The scientific study of sensation and perception was initiated by  Wundt (1862) . However, it was not until the introduction of the notion of the  receptive field  (RF), defined as that portion of (concrete or abstract) sensory space which elicits a neuronal response when stimulated ( Sherrington, 1906 ), that sensory neuroscience was put on a more quantitative footing. In visual neuroscience, the receptive field has become a key concept for understanding the response properties of neurons along the visual pathway ( Barlow, 1953; Hartline, 1940; Hubel & Wiesel, 1959; Kuffler, 1953 ). While the characterization of receptive field properties has predominantly focused on the visual modality ( Ringach, 2004 ), similar successes have been booked in the characterization of auditory, olfactory and somatosensory receptive fields ( Hsiao, 2008; Knudsen & Konishi, 1978; Wilson, 2001 ).

The use of receptive fields as a theoretical construct has been extraordinarily successful in sensory neuroscience. At the same time, accurate characterization of the receptive fields of individual neurons remains extremely difficult for a number of reasons. First, RF structure can be hard to characterize ( Carandini et al., 2005 ), particularly in downstream areas ( Gallant, Braun, & Van Essen, 1993; Okazawa, Tajima, & Komatsu, 2015 ). Second, RF properties are modulated by non-classical receptive field effects such as surround suppression ( Fitzpatrick, 2000; Joo, Boynton, & Murray, 2012; Smith & Muckli, 2010; Zipser, Lamme, & Schiller, 1996 ). Third, they are modulated by top-down processes such as task demand ( Emadi & Esteky, 2014; McKee, Riesenhuber, Miller, & Freedman, 2014 ) and attention ( Çukur, Nishimoto, Huth, & Gallant, 2013; David, Hayden, Mazer, & Gallant, 2008 ). These confounding factors make testing of RF properties in controlled experimental settings difficult.

A receptive field characterizes what stimulus properties a single neuron responds to. However, if we consider non-invasive measurement techniques used to probe human brain function then we can only measure the aggregate activity of a pool of neurons. For example, functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) measures the blood oxygenation-level dependent (BOLD) response across thousands of volume elements (voxels) in the brain. Each voxel captures the hemodynamic response induced by a neuronal population consisting of about 5.5 million neurons ( Logothetis, 2008 ). As another example, electroencephalography (EEG) and magnetoencephalography (MEG) measure the electrical and magnetic fields, respectively, that are generated by the (weighted) responses of multiple populations of neurons dispersed across the brain volume.

For a population of neurons, the term  population receptive field  (pRF) was introduced to refer to those stimulus properties that drive their pooled response ( Victor, Purpura, Katz, & Mao, 1994 ). In case of fMRI, which will be the main focus of this review, the pRF is defined at the level of single voxels and also referred to as the voxel receptive field ( Kriegeskorte & Kreiman, 2011 ). One might question why one embarks on population receptive field modeling in the first place given that, in contrast to analyzing single-neuron responses, it does not lead to a direct understanding of neural coding. An important motivation is that understanding how population responses are organized at a macroscopic level may provide insight into the functional organization of neural representations in the human brain. This, in turn, may guide hypothesis formation on the computational principles by which such representations emerge. Research suggests that population responses reflect computational principles operating at a lower scale ( Kamitani & Tong, 2005 ). However, whether or not this can be explained by large-scale biases that exist at a macroscopic level has been vigorously debated (e.g.  Carlson, 2014 ;  Cichy & Pantazis, 2015 ;  Freeman, Brouwer, Heeger, & Merriam, 2011 ;  Kriegeskorte, Cusack, & Bandettini, 2010 ;  Op de Beeck, 2010 ;  Swisher et al., 2010 ).

Summarizing, encoding models can be thought of as providing a common framework that allows investigating (highly nonlinear) response properties of different cortical areas as induced by arbitrary sensory inputs, where responses are measured indirectly via signal-altering neural recording methods. The present paper provides an overview of this new area of research and explores how encoding models may be used to provide novel insights into how neural responses are affected by bottom-up sensory evidence as well as top-down executive processes in naturalistic paradigms. Section   2  describes how encoding models can be estimated and inverted using experimental data. Section   3  addresses related approaches. In Section   4 , the construction and use of different feature spaces are reviewed. Section   5  describes how encoding models may be used to inform about the nature of neural representations. Section   6  provides an outlook on future applications of encoding models in sensory neuroscience. We end with some general conclusions in Section   7 .

In contrast to more controlled paradigms in cognitive neuroscience ( Rust & Movshon, 2005 ), encoding models typically seek to characterize how arbitrary sensory input affects observed responses. This input often takes the form of high-dimensional naturalistic stimuli ( Creutzfeldt & Nothdurft, 1978; Einhäuser & König, 2010; Felsen & Dan, 2005; Gallant, Connor, & Van Essen, 1998; Hasson, Malach, & Heeger, 2010; Kayser, Körding, & König, 2004 ). Naturalistic stimuli feature characteristic statistics that are usually not contained in highly controlled stimuli (e.g. bars and gratings). To learn how this high-dimensional stimulus space maps onto neural responses, a sufficient number of stimulus–response pairs needs to be collected, resulting in many hours of scanning time per subject. For this reason, studies that employ encoding models tend to report results for a handful of individuals, as is common in animal studies, where data collection is laborious as well, albeit for other reasons.

An alternative to the use of a predetermined feature space which embodies our assumptions about stimulus–response relationships is to use a more objective approach, where the feature space is learnt directly from data. The underlying assumption here is that the brain is optimized to extract statistical regularities from the environment ( Blakemore & Cooper, 1970; Marr, 1970; Purves, Wojtach, & Lotto, 2011; Rao & Ballard, 1999; Simoncelli & Olshausen, 2001 ). This suggests that a statistical model that learns invariances from massive datasets may constitute an optimal feature space. In  Güçlü and van Gerven (2014)  a sparse coding (SC) model was used which learns simple- and complex-cell responses in an unsupervised manner by presenting the model with a large number of natural image patches ( Hyvärinen & Hoyer, 2001 ). Interestingly, it was shown that responses predicted by the SC model significantly outperformed those of the GWP model ( Kay et al., 2008 ). This implies that the GWP model remains wanting when it comes to explaining responses in early visual cortex. See  Fig. 3 (C) for an example of a Gabor wavelet and a sparse coding filter, showing both differences and similarities. An important upshot of this work is that it is possible to validate computational models of learning and inference in sensory cortex using non-invasively recorded neural data.

While the GWP model and related models such as the SC model capture low-level stimulus features that inform about responses in early visual cortex, they are unable to capture responses to high-level stimulus features that inform about semantic representations in downstream association areas.

One way to circumvent this problem is to manually construct a feature space, as in the GWP model for early visual cortex, but now geared towards the direct representation of semantic information. This approach was used, for example, in a seminal study by  Mitchell et al. (2008) , where the feature space for written nouns was modeled in terms of their association strengths with different verbs. It was shown that this model could predict responses to nouns that were not previously seen by the encoding model by using the mapping from nouns to verbs in conjunction with the mapping from verbs to fMRI responses.

In  Naselaris et al. (2009) , a low-level structural encoding model, predicting responses in early visual cortex, was combined with a semantic encoding model, predicting responses to stimulus category in downstream areas. The integration of both sources of information together with the use of an empirical prior led to high-quality reconstructions.

Huth, Nishimoto, Vu, and Gallant (2012)  annotated thousands of movie frames with hundreds of object and action categories to create a feature space with which to examine the representation of semantic knowledge across the human brain. Principal component analysis of the regression coefficients that map the feature space to voxel responses revealed a number of dimensions that had an intuitive semantic interpretation in terms of animacy, social categories and man-made versus natural stimuli. Recently, a similar approach has been used by the same authors to map semantic information across the cortical sheet using fMRI data obtained while subjects were listening to autobiographical stories ( Huth, de Heer, Griffiths, & Gallant, 2016 ).

A limitation of the manual annotation of stimuli in terms of semantic properties is that it is laborious and may introduce annotator bias. More importantly, it does not inform about  how  these semantic representations come about in actual brains. In a sense, human-defined feature spaces can be viewed as computational oracles that do not provide an explicit account of neural processing of sensory stimuli. Ideally, we would like to model how low-, mid- and high-level stimulus features emerge by committing to a learning procedure. These features can subsequently be used to probe representations across the cortical sheet. One way to tackle this problem is to use hierarchical models consisting of multiple layers of elementary units that are trained to process increasingly complex features of their environment.

Recently, hierarchical models of this sort, known as deep neural networks (DNNs), have been used to predict neural responses in downstream cortical areas. Researchers have begun to use DNNs to predict neural responses to complex naturalistic stimuli, yielding state-of-the-art results ( Kriegeskorte, 2015 ).  Khaligh-Razavi and Kriegeskorte (2014)  used DNNs in conjunction with RSA to explain responses in IT cortex. Their results were consistent with those of  Yamins et al. (2014)  who have shown that responses in Monkey IT are best predicted by hierarchical models that are explicitly optimized for object categorization.

Güçlü and van Gerven (2015, in press)  have subsequently shown that DNN layers higher up in the hierarchy better encode the responses of brain regions as one moves downstream along both the ventral and dorsal visual pathways (see  Fig. 3 (D)). Based on MEG data,  Cichy, Khosla, Pantazis, Torralba, and Oliva (2016)  used an RSA approach to show that this gradient is not only reflected in space, with deeper DNN layers corresponding best with downstream brain regions, but also in time, with deeper DNN layers being mostly expressed at later points in time.

While deep neural networks have mainly been applied in the context of visual perception, they can equally well be applied to auditory data in order to understand population responses in auditory cortex. See  Güçlü, Thielen, Hanke, and van Gerven (2016)  and  Kell et al. (2015)  for initial work in this direction.

Along the same lines, neural networks can be made suitable for processing of linguistic input using word embeddings learned from large linguistic corpora based on the skip-gram model ( Mikolov, Sutskever, Chen, Corrado, & Dean, 2013 ). See  Güçlü and van Gerven (2016)  and  Nishida, Huth, Gallant and Nishimoto (2015)  for neuroscientific applications of this model.

Finally,  Horikawa and Kamitani (2016)  have recently demonstrated that DNNs even allow the decoding of imagined object categories from fMRI measurements.

The previous section reviewed state-of-the-art approaches in the development of increasingly sophisticated feature spaces. Here, we explore how encoding models are used to provide novel insights in sensory neuroscience.

Encoding models allow for the characterization of how particular stimulus properties are mapped across (sub)cortical areas, providing a macroscopic view of the neural organization of sensory representations at the level of neuronal populations ( DeSimone, Viviano, & Schneider, 2015; Sanger, 2003; Wandell et al., 2015 ). This can, for example, be achieved by identifying which stimulus features drive a voxel

That is, given the large number of features, significance testing on individual model weights as in an SPM analysis not only becomes cumbersome, but also problematic due to multicollinearity ( Monti, 2011 ).

To resolve this issue, different approaches have been used to project the data onto a lower-dimensional space. For example,  Huth et al. (2012)  used a principal components analysis on model weights to reveal how semantic information is represented across cortex.  Güçlü and van Gerven (2015)  instead characterized stimulus features in terms of their Kolmogorov complexity in order to show how increasingly complex stimulus properties are encoded across the ventral stream.

Alternatively, one may resort to the use of what we refer to as  simulated neurophysiology , where encoding models are used post hoc to predict how neural populations respond to basic stimuli that are more easily characterizable ( Güçlü & van Gerven, 2014; Kay et al., 2008; Kay, Winawer, Rokem et al., 2013; Nishimoto et al., 2011 ). For example,  Güçlü and van Gerven (2014)  used point stimuli and sine wave gratings as input to an encoding model to estimate RF location, orientation and spatial frequency tuning of individual voxels.

Yet another approach is to use each feature individually to predict BOLD responses and to analyze the resulting response patterns. For example, cluster analysis of response patterns in area LO hinted at subsets of voxels that were sensitive to different groups of stimulus features ( Güçlü & van Gerven, 2015 ). This more detailed mapping of representations across cortex is particularly important for unraveling the elusive characteristics of mid- to high-level neural representations ( Carandini et al., 2005; Orban, 2008; Peirce, 2015 ).

An important utility of the encoding approach lies in testing different computational models against each other. Basically, each estimated encoding model implements a particular theory concerning how sensory information is represented in the brain. By comparing encoding models in terms of how well they explain observed data, we may select among competing theories. This makes it possible to test whether proposed mechanisms underlying the emergence of neural representations indeed lead to more precise predictions of neural responses to sensory input.

For example, the observation that sparse coding models outperform Gabor wavelet models ( Güçlü & van Gerven, 2014 ) lends support to the hypothesis that sparse coding could be a driving principle behind the organization of receptive fields in striate cortex ( Olshausen & Field, 1996 ). Likewise, recent observations that hierarchical models which are optimized for object categorization provide state-of-the-art predictions across different areas of the ventral stream ( Güçlü & van Gerven, 2015; Khaligh-Razavi & Kriegeskorte, 2014; Yamins et al., 2014 ) suggest that the ventral stream is explicitly optimized for object categorization.

At the same time, one should be careful in drawing inferences of this sort. The stimulus features that emerge by explicitly optimizing for object categorization may also emerge via the optimization of other objective functions, like the one used in deep reinforcement learning ( Mnih et al., 2015 ). That is, while maximizing a particular objective function can be sufficient for explaining neural responses, it might not be necessary. In other words, one cannot directly infer function from receptive field properties ( Churchland & Sejnowski, 1990 ).

So far, encoding models have been described in terms of feedforward processing of sensory information. However, the brain is not a passive input device. Top-down changes in attention ( Çukur et al., 2013 ), expectation ( Summerfield & de Lange, 2014 ) and task demand ( Fritz, Shamma, Elhilali, & Klein, 2003; McKee et al., 2014 ) induce population receptive field reconfigurations ( Gilbert & Li, 2013 ), which can be probed using encoding models. For example, attention has been shown to increase pRF gain, eccentricity, and size in visual cortex ( Kay, Weiner, & Grill-Spector, 2015; Klein, Harvey, & Dumoulin, 2014 ).  Brouwer and Heeger (2013)  used a pRF model to show that the representation of color in downstream visual areas changed to a more categorical representation in a color naming task compared to a diverted attention task.

Changes in population responses may also be induced by (reversible) learning effects ( Rainer, Lee, & Logothetis, 2004 ), developmental change ( Johnson & Munakata, 2005 ) or neurodegeneration ( Brewer & Barton, 2014 ). Encoding models can in principle be used to probe these fleeting or long-lasting changes in the organization of low-, mid- and high-level representations across the cortical surface, for example, in case of neurodegeneration, by comparing the predictions of neural response patterns in patients and controls.

Also within the general population, variability in population responses across cortex is to be expected. These differences can be explored by developing encoding models tailored to individual subjects. This constitutes a shift in research focus from finding commonalities between subjects using group-level analyses, towards understanding the detailed functional organization of neural representations in individual subjects as driven by more abstract key computational principles ( Stelzer, Lohmann, Mueller, Buschmann, & Turner, 2014 ).

Whereas encoding makes explicit our assumptions about how stimuli are represented in the brain, decoding allows us to peer into the human mind by reconstructing stimuli from multivariate response patterns ( Tong & Pratte, 2012 ). This allows one to address, for example, what stimulus information is present within particular regions of interest. It also informs about those stimulus properties that are not accurately captured by the used encoding models, as this would reveal itself in terms of degraded stimulus reconstructions.

Decoding also affords the reconstruction of percepts that are not a direct consequence of external bottom-up input but rather of top-down internal drive. One may think here of representations induced by imagery ( Horikawa & Kamitani, 2016; Naselaris, Olman, Stansbury, Ugurbil, & Gallant, 2015; Parkkonen, Andersson, Hämäläinen, & Hari, 2008; Stokes, Thompson, Cusack, & Duncan, 2010; Thirion et al., 2006; Vetter, Smith, & Muckli, 2014 ), selective attention ( Kamitani & Tong, 2005; Niazi et al., 2014 ), perceptual filling-in ( Chong, Familiar, & Shim, 2015 ), visual illusions ( Corney & Lotto, 2007; Kok & de Lange, 2014 ), memory processing ( Harrison & Tong, 2009; Polanía, Paulus, & Nitsche, 2012; Serences, Ester, Vogel, & Awh, 2009 ) and dreaming ( Horikawa, Tamaki, Miyawaki, & Kamitani, 2013 ). Decoded representations may also be directly related to overt behavior ( Haynes et al., 2007; Williams, Dang, & Kanwisher, 2007 ). Finally, if internal representations can be directly decoded from human brain activity, then this may spur the development of new kinds of brain–computer interfaces that are driven by changes in internally induced mental states ( van Gerven et al., 2009 ).

In the previous section, possible applications of encoding models have been sketched. Here, a number of research directions are pointed out that are expected to further advance computational modeling in sensory neuroscience.

So far, encoding models have mainly been applied to fMRI data collected using relatively standard acquisition protocols with repetition times on the order of 1 s and voxel sizes of about 2 millimeter isotropic. In the near future we can expect to see more examples of encoding models applied to high spatial and temporal resolution fMRI data acquired under novel protocols at ultra-high field strengths ( Barth & Poser, 2011 ). At some point, such studies may allow probing of representational content at the level of individual cortical columns ( Yacoub, Harel, & Ugurbil, 2008 ) as well as cortical layers ( Kok, Bains, van Mourik, Norris, & de Lange, 2016; Muckli et al., 2015 ). Research will also be facilitated by recent initiatives that provide open-access high-resolution fMRI datasets that are tailored to encoding studies ( Hanke et al., 2014 ).

As described earlier on, encoding models can also be used in conjunction with other imaging modalities. More detailed insights concerning the spatio-temporal dynamics of neural representations can be gained using non-invasive EEG/MEG recording techniques ( Cichy et al., 2016; Clarke, Devereux, Randall, & Tyler, 2014; Pantazis, Oliva, & Cichy, 2014; Scholte, Ghebreab, Waldorp, Smeulders, & Lamme, 2009; van de Nieuwenhuijzen et al., 2013 ). The use of more invasive recording techniques ( Kobatake & Tanaka, 1994; Quian Quiroga, Reddy, Kreiman, Koch, & Fried, 2005; Yamins et al., 2014 ) may lead to a new understanding of the receptive fields of single neurons or small neuronal populations. In practice, the use of other modalities only requires the development of appropriate forward models that link feature spaces to observed responses.

Since encoding models require massive amounts of data in order to afford proper model estimation, it is important to develop techniques that make optimal use of the data available. One way to achieve this is to impose additional realistic constraints on the models (see also Section   6.2 ). Also, better removal of (physiological) noise ( Kay, Rokem, Winawer, Dougherty, & Wandell, 2013; Särkkä et al., 2012 ) and data-driven estimation of hemodynamic response functions that map neural activity to observed BOLD responses will improve sensitivity ( Pedregosa, Eickenberg, Ciuciu, Thirion, & Gramfort, 2015 ). Combining data across subjects via techniques such as hyperalignment ( Haxby et al., 2011 ) may also further improve sensitivity, as demonstrated in  Güçlü and van Gerven (in press) . The use of optimal experimental designs ( Lewi, Butera, & Paninski, 2009 ), where the optimal stimulation sequence is determined on the fly such as to maximize information gain, could also further improve results. To date, this approach remains underexplored as it would require near real-time estimation of model parameters and the use of closed-loop experimental setups.

At present, encoding models do not yet provide a particularly veridical mapping from stimuli to observed brain responses. In their current formulation, various simplifying assumptions are introduced either for reasons of computational efficiency or because the details underlying particular transformations remain elusive. In practice, this mapping is extraordinarily complex, involving intricate short- and long-range interactions between neuronal populations in space and time that can only be partially observed through acquired measurements. To improve the sensitivity of encoding models, it is imperative that more realistic assumptions about neural information processing are incorporated.

Already at the sensory epithelia, the incoming stimulus is transduced in complex ways ( Gollisch & Meister, 2010; Masland, 2012 ). Furthermore, processing of these stimuli is an active process where sensory input is selected such as to maximize reward or information gain ( Ahmad & Yu, 2013 ). Neural population activity itself is characterized by intricate dynamics and may not only encode representations of invariants in our environment but also their sensory uncertainty ( van Bergen et al., 2015 ). By modeling such processes, more realistic encoding models can be obtained (also see  Edelman, 2015 ).

Concerning the employed forward models it should be mentioned that encoding models can in principle use arbitrarily complex forward models, replacing standard linear regression methods. In neuroimaging, these models can provide more explicit implementations of the transformation from neural responses to the BOLD response, as induced by neurovascular coupling ( Aquino, Robinson, & Drysdale, 2014; Kay, Winawer, Rokem et al., 2013 ). In electrophysiology, these models can incorporate explicit mechanisms for spike generation ( Paninski, 2004 ).

When considering the state-of-the-art feature spaces afforded by deep artificial neural networks it is important to realize that while they replicate some of the response properties of neurons across the sensory stream, they make assumptions that are unrealistic from a neurobiological point of view, both regarding the learning procedure with which neural networks are trained as well as their assumed architecture ( Robinson & Rolls, 2015 ). For example, while DNNs trained in a supervised manner provide state-of-the-art performance on object categorization tasks, the employed learning scheme is far removed from learning in their biological counterparts ( Bliss & Lømo, 1973 ).

As a tentative example of a neural network architecture which incorporates top-down processing consider the work presented in  van Gerven, de Lange, and Heskes (2010) . Here, a deep belief network (DBN) rather than a deep feed-forward neural network was used to reconstruct perceived images from fMRI measurements. DBNs differ from their feed-forward counterparts by having recurrent connections between network layers. Moreover, they are generative models in the sense that sensory inputs can be sampled from the model. This is reminiscent of predictive processing, in which top-down predictions are thought to explain away bottom-up inputs ( Clark, 2016; Rao & Ballard, 1999 ); a view which was already present in the writings of  Thurstone (1923) . Indeed, results show that, by sampling from the DBN after conditioning its internal state on observed brain data, accurate reconstructions of perceived handwritten digits could be obtained.

Lateral and feedback connections also support the inclusion of long-term temporal dependencies. These dependencies are essential since they afford modeling of complex spatio-temporal patterns in the sensory input and can account for adaptation effects at the neuronal level. One way to incorporate population dynamics in an encoding setting is through the use recurrent neural networks that have persistent states ( Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997 ). This approach was followed in  Güçlü and van Gerven (2016)  to model how low-level and high-level representations of dynamically changing inputs influence the BOLD response.

Given the rapid development of neural network theory, both in the engineering and computational neuroscience communities ( Yuste, 2015 ), it is to be expected that increasingly sophisticated neural network architectures will become available. When one considers such an architecture as a putative explanation of neural information processing then the encoding framework provides a means to test this hypothesis against neural data.

This review addressed the theoretical basis, applications and future directions of encoding models in sensory neuroscience. These encoding models strive to provide new insights into the functional mechanisms that mediate how representations of our environment are encoded in neural response patterns.

Encoding can be viewed as a model-based approach where internal latent states of computational models are fitted to neural and behavioral responses as subjects are engaged in that particular task ( Anderson et al., 2008; Ashby & Waldschmidt, 2008; Forstmann & Wagenmakers, 2015 ). The advantages of this approach are that (i) a good match between internal states and observed regional brain responses can be taken as evidence for a neural correlate of that state and (ii) different computational models can be tested against each other by comparing their fit to observed data. In the context of sensory neuroscience, concrete and abstract stimulus features play the role of these latent states. The comparison of encoding models that use different underlying feature representations provide the means to compare alternative theories about sensory processing in the human brain.

In summary, encoding models that are grounded in theory and veridically model the transformation from arbitrary sensory stimuli to measured neural response patterns provide the means to further our understanding of neural representations. Ultimately, this could even allow faithful decoding of internal representations in the absence of sensory stimulation.
