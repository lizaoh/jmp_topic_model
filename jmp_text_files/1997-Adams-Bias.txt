Bias-Independent Constructions from Biased Bisection and Adjacency Judgements 

Ernest W. Adams 

University of California, Berkeley 

This paper discusses the possibility of using a biased bisection and its inverse operations intrinsically, to ``correct for bias'' and thereby to produce ``corrected bisections'' and related constructions, like concatenations or ``sums'' of sensory intervals. General results concerning the scope and limits of ``corrected constructability'' are derived, showing among other things that corrected sums can be constructed but corrected bisections cannot. Related issues include: (1) what can be determined by constructive means, e.g., that a given element correctly bisects an interval is shown to be determinable by these means, although it cannot be constructed by them; (2) a multidimensional generalization is considered, in which elements are represented by points in n-dimensional space and, paradoxically, it seems that corrected bisections can be produced in those spaces; (3) unsystematic comparisons to famous construction problems of geometry are made. ] 1997 Academic Press 

1. INTRODUCTION 

Adams and Fagot (1975; henceforth referred to as AF) presented a model of biased bisection and adjacency judgements of intervals on a subjective continuum, Section 3 of which stated results on problems concerning the constructability, starting from these judgements, of unbiased bisections and adjacent equal intervals.[1] The present paper extends these results and comments on analogies between them and classical problems of geometrical constructability, e.g., those of trisecting the angle, duplicating the cube, and squaring the circle. 4. REMARKS ON CONSTRUCTIVE DETERMINABILITY 

Angle trisections cannot be produced by straightedge and compass methods, but these methods can be used to determine whether a given line trisects an angle. Similarly, 

unbiased bisections cannot be produced using biased bisection and adjacency operations, but these operations can be used to determine whether a given element is an unbiased bisector of an interval. In particular, y is an unbiased bisector of interval x, z if and only if xRy coincides with z. Thus, in both the geometric and psychometric cases the scope of constructive determinability exceeds that of constructability, and this section will comment on the former in the context of biased bisection and adjacency operations. 

Before entering on this subject, however, it should be noted that the scope of what can be determined by constructive means is very broad, and we will limit ourselves here to considering a small part of it. The operations to be allowed will be the trio of V, � , and � , and we will not consider what might be determined by some subset of them. Thus, we will set aside the question of what might be determined by the use of V alone, although, for instance, obviously it is possible to determine that an element z is an adjacent displacement of an interval x, y by this means.[13] Something that can be determined by the use of all three of V, � , and � is bias. For example, one way to determine whether a subject's basic operations have positive, negative, or 0 bias is by observing whether she judges x Vy to be greater than, less than, or equal to (x � y ) V (x � y ). But short of formalizing a language of statements or ``qualities,'' we cannot define the scope even of what can and what cannot be determined constructively, using the V, � , and � operations, and we will focus here just on an especially simple kind of determination, as follows.

Thus, we have a partial breakdown of the parallel between geometric determinability and psychometric determinability using biased bisection and adjacency operations, since, while duplicating the cube is geometrically impossible, it is possible to determine by geometric means that one cube is the duplicate of another. The psychometric impossibility of determining that one square is the duplicate of another is more akin to the geometric impossibility, not only of ``squaring the circle'' in the sense of constructing a square whose area is equal to that of a given circle, but it is even impossible to determine that the areas of a given square and circle are equal.

There is a resolution of this paradox, of course. That is to point out that finding intersections of segments is not among the basic biased bisection and adjacency operations, nor is producing ``segments,'' or ``intervals.'' The segment is part of the diagrammatic, geometrical representation, but it does not necessarily correspond to anything in the phenomena that the diagram represents. Thus, AF was primarily concerned with judgements of brightness, but a subject judging that the brightness of one light source is halfway between that of two other sources does not have in view a spectrum of sources of continuously varying brightness. Even allowing that segments and their intersections can be constructed, in addition to biased bisections and adjacencies, we still do not extend the range of the constructable beyond the ``linearly representable.'' For example, we now can construct certain arithmetic means (or centroids in the multidimensional case, as in the parallelogram in the second diagram) but we still cannot carry out essentially quadratically representable operations such as duplicating a square or squaring a rectangle. That is because the coordinates of segments' intersections are themselves linear functions of the coordinates of their endpoints. More generally, it is still not possible to determine constructively, that, say, the square of the length of one segment is twice that of another segment, even if the second segment is parallel to the first, and even though area is ``translation invariant.'' 

But, to conclude, we hope that this paper's little excursion into the subject of constructability, as distinct from the existence concept of formal logic that is presupposed in the 

models and theories of mathematical psychology, suggests that constructability, constructive determinability, and related ideas are interesting in their own right, and that there is some potential for further research into them in the psychometric domain.
