INTRODUCTION  People often exhibit inconsistency when confronted with a choice among several alternatives. When faced with repeated choices  of x and y, under ostensibly identical conditions, people often choose x in some instances and y in others. Furthermore, such  inconsistent choices have been observed even in the absence of systematic changes in tastes that might be due to sequential  effects or learning. To account for such inconsistencies, psychologists and economists have developed probabilistic choice  models in which the traditional concept of preference has been replaced by the notion of a choice probability. Instead of  specifying that x will be chosen over y or y will be chosen over x in a binary choice, a probabilistic choice model would  specify the probability with which x would be chosen over y.l  One type of probabilistic choice model is the random utility model, in which subjective utility values undergo random  fluctuations, and the alternative with the greatest utility at the instant of choice is selected. RISK ATTITUDE UNDER RANDOM UTILITY  When a DM's choice behavior under uncertainty is characterized by any probabilistic choice model, and in particular by the  random expected-utility model, the DM exhibits inconsistencies across multiple choice opportunities. Since the DM's choice  behavior is stochastic, any characterization of that behavior must embrace its stochastic nature. Specifically, such  characterizations of choice behavior as risk attitude and comparative risk aversion must take into account the stochastic  nature of choice behavior. The problem of specifying a criterion for comparative risk aversion is subtle in that the criterion  should reflect risk attitudes across all possible realizations of the random utility function, not just a particular  realization invoked on a particular occasion. Just because individual A chooses a risky gamble on a particular occasion while  individual B declines the gamble does not necessarily mean that B is more risk averse in general toward that gamble. If the  choice behavior patterns of A and B are characterized by random expected-utility models, then on other occasions A may decline  the risky gamble while B chooses the gamble. The problem then is to specify a criterion for risk attitude and comparative risk  aversion that takes into account choice behavior across the entire range of choice possibilities admitted by the random  expected-utility model.  This observation frames the problem of choosing a definition of comparative risk aversion under random utility, but it still  allows several alternative approaches. DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION  We have developed four alternative definitions of risk attitude and comparative risk aversion for individuals whose choice  behavior under uncertainty can be represented by a random expected-utility model. These definitions provide a vehicle for  conceptualizing what risk attitude means under random utility. Moreover, the concepts provide a means for empirically  assessing the risk attitudes and comparative levels of risk aversion reflected in choice data collected from individuals whose  choice behavior is subject to inconsistencies due to random utility. In this section of the paper we briefly consider how such  empirical assessments might be accomplished.  The following three definitions of risk aversion (presented in Sections 5.1, 5.2, and 5.3) all involve the distribution of a  DM's risk premia for a gamble z:  (i) risk aversion in certainty  (ii) risk aversion in probability  (iii) risk aversion in expectation.  Therefore, to empirically operationalize these definitions requires that the experimenter can elicit from the DM a  distribution of risk premia across repeated trials involving the gamble z. There are two issues here. First, we discuss a  common method for eliciting the DM's risk premium for the gamble z in a single experimental trial. Second, we discuss methods  for administering repeated trials with the same gamble z.  7.1. Elicitation of a Risk Premium  The DM's risk premium for gamble z is that amount 71 such that the DM is indifferent between facing the gamble z and receiving  the certain amount f - n (where z denotes E[z] ). The amount f - n is commonly referred to as the DM's "certainty equivalent"  for the gamble z. Thus, elicitation of the DM's certainty equivalent - 7z for the gamble z enables the experimenter to deduce  the risk premium 71, since the gamble's expectation (z) is known.  A method commonly used to elicit a DM's certainty equivalent for a gamble was developed and popularized by Grether and Plott  (1979). The DM is asked to state his or her certainty equivalent (denoted by CE) for a gamble z. After the CE is stated, the  experimenter randomly draws a number from a sequence of numbers that bounds all of the possible monetary outcomes from the  gamble z. Denote this randomly drawn number by R. If R exceeds CE, the DM receives the amount R. If  216 RONALD W. HILTON  R is less than CE, the gamble z is actually played by the DM, and the DM receives the outcome of the gamble. If R equals CE,  then a coin is flipped and, with equal probability, the DM either (i) receives R or (ii) plays the gamble z and receives the  outcome of the gamble. This method provides an economic incentive for the DM to correctly reveal his or her CE for the gamble  z. To see why, suppose the DM's true certainty equivalent for gamble z is 10, but the DM states a CE higher than 10 (say 15).  If R is between 10 and 15, the DM will have to play the gamble instead of receiving R. However, the DM would really prefer R,  because the DM's CE is 10, which is less than R. Suppose, on the other hand, the DM states a CE lower than 10 (say 5). If R is  between 5 and 10, the DM will receive R instead of playing the gamble. However, the DM would prefer to play the gamble,  because the DM's CE is 10, which is greater than R. Thus, the Grether-Plott elicitation method provides the correct incentive  for the DM to truthfully reveal his or her CE for the gamble z. From the DM's elicited CE for gamble z, the experimenter can  easily deduce the DM's risk premium 7z for the gamble.  It is not necessary for all of the trials to be actually played out as described above. To maintain the desired incentives,  only a portion of the trials need to be played out. For example, after the DM states his or her CE for a gamble, the  experimenter could toss a coin. If heads is tossed, the experimenter proceeds with drawing the number R and the DM either  plays the gamble or receives the amount R as described above. If tails is tossed, the trial ends and the DM receives nothing  on that trial.  7.2. Therefore, empirical  assessment of the DM's risk attitude (as defined in Sections 5.1, 5.2, and 5.3) requires that the DM's certainty equivalent  for z be elicited in repeated trials with the same gamble. Empirical Assessment of Risk Aversion in Selection  Risk aversion in selection, defined and discussed in Section 5.4, does not involve the concept of a risk premium. Instead,  this concept is based on a DM's binary choice between facing a gamble z and receiving its expectation 5. Empirical assessment  in this case does not require the elicitation of a certainty equivalent, but rather the elicitation of a DM's preference in  the binary choice. To provide the correct incentive for a DM to truly reveal his or her preference in a single trial with  gamble z, the DM actually plays the gamble z or receives the amount z, depending on the DM's stated preference. Alternatively,  only a portion of the DM's stated preferences need to be played out. For example, after the DM chooses between gamble z and  amount 5, the experimenter could toss a coin to determine whether the DM's choice will actually be played. If the DM chooses  the gamble, and the experimenter tosses a head, the DM plays the gamble and receives the outcome. If a tail is tossed, the DM  receives nothing on that trial.  To apply Definition 4a, it is necessary to determine the probability that the DM will choose the gamble in repeated trials  involving the same gamble. Such a probability can be estimated from the relative frequency of the binary choices in repeated  trials. As described earlier, such repeated administrations can be accomplished by (i) interleaving repeated trials with the  same gamble with other different gambles and (ii) masking the repeated administrations so the identical gambles are less  easily recognized as such.  After the probability of a DM selecting each gamble over its expectation is established, it is straightforward to apply the  definition of risk aversion in selection, which is stated and discussed in Section 5.4. For example, DMA is more risk averse  in selection than DM, toward gamble z if DMA selects amount f over gamble z in a higher proportion of the trials with gamble z  than does DM,.
