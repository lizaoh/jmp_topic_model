MYERS  University of Massachusetts, Amherst, Massachusetts  Expressions for the learning curve, preasymptotic first-order conditional statistics, and asymptotic variance have been  derived for the Weak-Strong model of choice behavior (Myers and Atkinson). Data published by Suppes and Atkinson were used to  test the model. The predicted statistics stabilized earlier than did the observed, and the predicted asymptotic variance was  slightly smaller than the observed variance.  A stochastic model to describe the effects of reward and punishment in choice situations has been developed by Atkinson (1962)  and Myers and Atkinson (1964). They assume a population of stimulus elements, each of which is conditioned to one and only one  response. It is further assumed that a single element is randomly sampled from the stimulus population on each trial, and that  the subject's response depends upon the state of conditioning of the sampled element. These assumptions are common to other  stimulus sampling models (Atkinson and Estes, 1963); however, the MyersAtkinson model differs from its predecessors since it  is assumed that an element may be at one of s stages of conditioning to a response. The special case which has received most  attention is the weak-strong (W-S) model which assumes that s is two; i.e., an element may be either weakly or strongly  conditioned to a response.  Myers and Atkinson showed that the W-S model gives a good account of marginal, first- and second-order conditional response  probabilities in sequential choice situations. Furthermore, the model is applicable to two or more choices, to contingent and  noncontingent reinforcement, and to a wide array of payoff matrices. Predictions of response time have also been derived from  the model. Further support for the model has since appeared; Calfee (1964), in an investigation of two-choice behavior in  rats, has obtained an excellent fit to the marginal and conditional statistics cited above, and has also shown that response  probabilities dependent upon preceding runs of events are adequately described.  1 This research was supported by funds from National Institutes of Health Grant MH03803-04 and National Science Foundation  Grant GS-386.  120  CHOICE BEHAVIOR AND REWARD STRUCTURE 121  A major limitation in the three reports on the VV-S model which have thus far appeared is that both derivational work and data  fitting have been carried out on the last, presumably asymptotic, block of trials. The preasymptotic derivations are  considerably more tedious and data on the earlier trials of choice studies are not always available. However, a complete test  of the model would seem to demand investigation of the learning curve. Furthermore, it is not always clear that curves have  stabilized at the last trial block, and we may be deceiving ourselves when we regard such data as asymptotic. In view of these  considerations, this paper presents expressions for marginal response probabilities, and for response probabilities  conditional on the response-event combination of the preceding trials, without the restriction that behavior has stabilized.  In addition, an expression for the variance of response proportions will be presented. Finally, some fits of the W-S model to  these statistics will be considered. Parameter estimates were obtained from  the conditional statistics in the last block of 60 trials. CONCLUDING REMARKS  The primary purpose of the present paper was to extend the testability of the weakstrong model by presenting methods for  predicting several measures which Atkinson (1962) and Myers and Atkinson (1964) had not previously considered. In addition, we  have provided a further, but limited, test of the model. The results are suggestive; they indicate that parameter estimates  taken at asymptote yield a rate of learning which is somewhat higher than it should be, and that asymptotic variability is  slightly underestimated. These are at best tentative hypotheses about the properties of the model. It is hoped that the  application to other data sets of the statistics presented in this and in previous papers will clarify the status of the model.
