JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 22, 70-81 (1980)  Matrix Derivatives and Its Applications in Statistics'  CHI SONG WONG  University of Windsor, Windsor, Ontario, Canada  The scalar values of differentials rather than symbolic derivatives are treated here. This approach simplifies the  differential calculus in statistics because, often, the linear representations of differentials in statistics (1) are  complicated, (2) depend on the bases used, (3) can be obtained by the scalar values on elements of a given basis, and (4) are  not necessary for many problems of finding maxima and minima. Among others, a product rule is obtained in a very general and  simple form. The results are applied to certain problems in multivariate statistical analysis that are useful to psychology  and other behavioral sciences.  1. INTRODUCTION  It says in Bentler and Lee (1978) that "although multivariate calculus has become a standard mathematical topic in recent  years (e.g., Chorlton, 1976; Curtis, 1972), the differential calculus of matrix functions is not yet a mature enough field to  have developed general matrix calculus rules that reduce to the standard pragmatic rules of scalar calculus when the matrices  involved are of order one. It is by no means an easy task to move from scalar calculus to matrix calculus." One reason for the  above difficulty is that various authors (e.g., Dwyer, 1967; Dwyer & MacPhail, 1948; MacRae, 1974; McDonald, 1976; McDonald &  Swaminathan, 1973, 1976; Neudecker 1967, 1968, 1969; Swaminathan, 1976, Tracy & Dwyer, 1969; Tracy & Singh 1975, 1976; Vetter,  1970) emphasized the linear representations of differentials. Often, instead of finding the scalar values of differentials,  the authors differentiated entries of the linear representations of differentials. These linear representations depend on the  bases used and as a result, the derivative is defined specifically for matrices.  In this paper, among others, we shall derive a very general and simple product rule and treat the model of Joreskog (1970).
