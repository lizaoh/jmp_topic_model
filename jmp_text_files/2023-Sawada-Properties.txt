Shapes of many natural and man-made objects in real scenes can be characterized by symmetry or they can be decomposed into parts that can be characterized by symmetry. The sensitivity of the human visual system to symmetry is critical for detecting and interacting with these objects.

( Mach, 1906,1959 , see also  Julesz, 1971; Kohler et al., 2016; Makin et al., 2013, 2012 ) pointed out that the visual system is sensitive to the following three types of 2D symmetry: mirror (or bilateral or reflectional), rotational (or cyclic or radial), and translational (or repetition). The same 3 types of symmetry can be found in 3D objects. However, the 2D retinal or camera images of 3D symmetrical objects are not themselves symmetrical except when the viewing direction is degenerate ( Sawada et al., 2011, 2014; Sawada & Zaidi, 2018 , see also  Sawada, 2020 ). It follows, that the sensitivity of the visual system to different types of 2D symmetry cannot be generalized to sensitivity to the various types of 3D symmetry.

Kanade ’s ( 1981 ) paper was the first attempt to analyze 2D images of patterns that are symmetrical in a 3D scene, but not in the camera image. The camera image is not symmetrical, but symmetry is not completely lost in the projection from a 3D scene to the 2D image. It is now conventional to call such images skew-symmetrical. Research conducted during the last 30 years has shown that the human visual system has mechanisms to handle skew-symmetrical images in ways that are computationally optimal, or nearly so.

It has been shown that the visual system is sensitive to 3D mirror-symmetry ( Sawada, 2010; Sawada & Farshchi, 2022 ) and that the 3D mirror-symmetry of an object is critical for perceiving the shape of the object and for recognizing the object from a 2D image ( Jayadevan et al., 2018; Li & Pizlo, 2011; Li et al., 2009, 2011 , see  Pizlo, 2019; Pizlo & de Barros, 2021; Pizlo et al., 2014  for a further discussion about a role of symmetry in the visual perception). The 3D mirror-symmetry of an object introduces  invariant  features in its 2D image that can be used by the visual system to detect the 3D symmetry in a 2D skew-symmetrical image ( Li et al., 2013; Sawada et al., 2015 ). 3D symmetry also works as an  a priori constrain t that is used to recover the 3D shape of the object from the 2D image ( Gordon, 1989; Hong et al., 2004; Li et al., 2009; Rothwell, 1995; Vetter & Poggio, 1994 ). Note that there is also a similar geometrical property that works with 3D rotational-symmetry ( Sawada et al., 2014; Sawada & Zaidi, 2018 , see also  Sawada, 2020 ) but the sensitivity of the visual system to 3D rotational-symmetry is, at best, very low ( Sawada & Farshchi, 2022; Sawada & Zaidi, 2018 ). It is possible that the human ability to detect 3D rotational symmetry is related to the fact that such symmetry can often be described (at least approximately) as 3D mirror-symmetry. This is true with objects such as flowers. The question as to whether the human visual system has a separate mechanism for detecting and using 3D rotational-symmetry as an invariant and a priori constraint, is still open. The present paper explores the mathematical and computational characteristics of the 3rd type of 3D symmetry, the translational-symmetry. More specifically, it is shown how a 3D mirror-symmetry can be used to recover 3D translationally-symmetrical objects. This connection between mirror-symmetry and translational-symmetry is important because we already know that the human visual system uses a mirror-symmetry constraint, and it does so very well. If translationally-symmetrical objects can be recovered by using a mirror-symmetry constraint, it is likely that translationally-symmetrical shapes are also perceived by human observers veridically. Our preliminary psychophysical results suggest that this is indeed the case ( Pizlo et al., 2014; Shi, 2012 ).

An object is with translational-symmetry when the object can be decomposed into components that are identical to one another and when the components align along a straight axis with equal steps. This type of translational-symmetry is discrete. Note that there should be an infinite number of components along an infinitely-long axis for the object to be exactly translationally-symmetrical. However, it can be often said that the object is with translational-symmetry even if the number of components is finite and the axis has a finite length. A more interesting version of translational-symmetry is continuous symmetry. Such symmetry exists when the object is a right-cylinder, which is produced by sweeping a planar closed curve along a straight axis that is normal to the plane of the curve. Note that in principle the cylinder should be infinitely long to be exactly translationally-symmetrical. However, it has been conventional to say that an object is translationally-symmetrical even if the cylinder is finitely long. The planar curve that was swept in the process of producing the cylinder shows at both ends of the cylinder as its end-sections. This cylinder is also 3D mirror-symmetrical about a plane that bisects the cylinder, is normal to the axis, and is parallel to the end-sections.

The generalized cone proposed by  Binford (1971)  can be regarded as a generalization of the continuous type of translational-symmetry. This generalized cone is produced by sweeping a planar cross-section along an axis so that the cross-section is always normal to a tangent of the axis at an intersection of the axis with the cross-section. The axis can be smoothly curved. The size of the cross-section of the generalized cone can change smoothly as a function of the position of the cross section relative to the axis. The orientation of the cross-section around the tangent of the axis at the intersection can also change smoothly. The resulting cone is composed of the two planar end-sections, the meridians connecting pairs of corresponding vertices of the cross-sections, and the surfaces connecting the end-sections and the meridians. The relationship between the cross-sections including the end-sections of the cone can be regarded as generalized-translational-symmetry.

Many complex and articulated 3D objects can be represented as composed of parts, where each part is a generalized cone ( Biederman, 1987; Binford, 1971; Marr, 1977; Pentland, 1986 ). It has been shown that observers can reliably recognize objects composed of generalized cones from contour drawings of the objects ( Biederman, 1987, 2001; Biederman & Gerhardstein, 1993; Stankiewicz, 2002 ). It was also shown that a 3D shape of a generalized cone can be veridically perceived from an image of the cone when the two end-sections of the cone are visible ( Pizlo et al., 2014; Shi, 2012 ).

                       If one wants to discuss the perception of the shape of a 3D generalized cone from a 2D contour-drawing of the cone, it is important to understand the geometrical properties of these contours in the drawing. In this study, we analyzed the geometrical properties of the contours that represented the end-sections of the generalized cone with the meridians around the end-sections in a drawing of the cone. Our analysis showed that there are model-based invariants of a generalized cone under a projection from the 3D scene to a 2D drawing of it. The end-sections and the axis of the generalized cone can be recovered from the 2D drawing when the axis is planar. We found that the 3D translational symmetry of generalized cones can be analyzed using tools designed for 3D mirror-symmetry. Based on the results of our analysis, we discussed the role the end-sections play in the perception of a generalized cone from a contour-drawing.

Two types of generalized cones are considered in this study. In the first type, the size of the cross-section of the generalized cone is constant along the axis of the cone. In the second type, the size changes smoothly along the axis. Each type of generalized cones was analyzed under both 2D orthographic and perspective projections.

In this study, for both types of generalized cones, we assume that their axes are smooth curves and the curvature at any point of the axis is small when compared with the size of the cross-section around the point. Without these restrictions, the surface of a generalized cone may have a self-intersection and its meridians may have singular points ( Fig. 1 ,  Hoffman, 1989 ).

From conditions (3) and (7), every cross-section of the cone is perpendicular to the plane of the axis. From conditions (4–7), every cross-section intersects with the meridians, and the tangents of the meridians at the intersections are parallel to the tangent of the axis and they are normal to the section. Under an orthographic projection, the projections of these tangents are a set of parallel lines and they are also tangents of curves that are orthographic projections of the axis and of the meridians. Under a perspective projection, the projections of the tangents are a set of lines converging at their vanishing point and they are also tangents of the curves that are perspective projections of the axis and the meridians. The line connecting the vanishing point to the projective center is parallel to the tangents of the axis and the meridians in the 3D scene.

The two end-sections of the cone are visible.

The meridians, that is, curves connecting corresponding pairs of feature points between the end-sections, are visible.

The axis of the cone is a planar curve.

The cross-section of the cone does not rotate around a tangent of the axis at an intersection of the axis with the cross-section.

The shape of the cross-section is constant along the axis.

The size of the cross-section changes smoothly along the axis. The center of scaling of the cross-section is its intersection with the axis.

A tangent of the axis at every point is normal to a cross-section of the cone at the point of the axis.

From conditions (3) and (7), every cross-section of the cone is perpendicular to the plane of the axis. A tangent of the axis at every point is normal to a cross-section of the cone at the point of the axis but the tangents of the meridians are not normal. The tangents of the meridians and of the axis at their intersections with the cross-section converge at a point in the scene. Under both orthographic and perspective projections, the projections of these tangents are also a set of converging lines and they are also tangents of curves that are projections of the axis and of the meridians.

Finally, we discuss a condition where only one or neither of the two end-sections of a generalized cone is visible ( Fig. 11 ).

First, consider that only one end-section of the cone is visible. If the size of the cross-section is locally constant around the visible end-section (the first 3 photos in  Fig. 11 ), the normal to the end-section can be determined from the projections of the meridians around the end-section under a perspective projection (see Section  2.1.2 ). The orientation of the end-section can be recovered uniquely from the normal and the shape of the end-section can be recovered by back-projecting the projection of the end-section. Under an orthographic projection, only the projection of the normal to the end-section can be recovered from the projections of the meridians (see Section  2.1.1 ). The projection of the normal represents the tilt of the end-section but the slant of the end-section cannot be determined. So, a one-parameter family of shapes of the end-section can be recovered from the image. If the size of the cross section around the end-section is not constant, the orientation of the end-section cannot be determined and a two-parameter family of shapes of the end-section can be recovered from the visual information alone under perspective or orthographic projections. If the shape of the end-section is given, the orientation of the end-section can be recovered from the image of the end-section ( Pizlo, 1994 ;  Minkov & Sawada, 2021 , see  Chen et al., 2013 ;  Chen et al., 2020  for studies using this approach in Computer vision). If neither the shape nor the orientation of the end-section is available, additional a priori constraints (e.g. maximizing 2D symmetry or 2D compactness of the end-section) are necessary. Once the orientation of the visible end-section is recovered, the orientation of the axis around the end-section can be estimated by assuming that the axis is normal to the end-section.

Note that there are also projections of the meridians of the cone and its occluding boundaries and that they can provide some additional information about the shape of the cone. For example, the projection of the cross-section can be recovered from the projection of the meridians but only if the size of the cross-section is constant and if the projection is orthographic (Appendix). This cross-section can be a substitute of the end-section to recover the cone’s shape (see Section  2.1.1  and Section  2.3.1 ).

When neither of the end-sections of the cone is visible, the only visual information available is the projections of the meridians and the occluding boundaries of the cone. A recovery is possible based on the projections of the meridians if the size of the cross-section is constant and if the projection is orthographic (Appendix). Otherwise, the recovery is impossible or it is at least difficult unless the shapes of the end-section and of the axis are given.

This study analyzed the geometrical properties of a generalized cone and its 2D image. Specifically, we analyzed the end-sections of the cone with the meridians around the end-sections. The generalized cone that was primarily considered in the study had an axis that is planar and a cross-section that does not rotate around a tangent of the axis of the cone at an intersection of the axis. The following two types of cones were discussed separately, namely, when the size of the cross-section is constant and when it changes along the axis. This analysis showed that the veridical shape of the generalized cone can be recovered uniquely from its 2D image except for the case that when the size of the cross-section changes under an orthographic projection. In this exceptional case, a one-parameter family of 3D shapes can be recovered from the image and this family includes the veridical shape. This ambiguity can be resolved if a projection of another cross-section of the cone is given. The analysis also showed that there are model-based invariants of the generalized cone under a projection from a 3D scene to a 2D image of the cone except for the case when the size of the cross-section changes under a perspective projection.

The generalized cone that had a non-planar axis was also considered in this study. The axis of the generalized cone cannot be recovered with the non-planar axis. The orientations and the shapes of the end-sections of this cone can be recovered from the 2D image of the cone uniquely under orthographic and perspective projections when the size of the cross-section of the cone is constant. The depth positions of the end-sections are two independent free parameters under the orthographic projection. The relative depth position between the end-sections can be recovered uniquely but the distance of the cone is a free parameter under the perspective projection. There is also a model-based invariant of the generalized cone under an orthographic projection from a 3D scene to a 2D image of the cone when the size of the cross-section is constant. When the size of the cross-section changes, a one-parameter family of shapes of the end-sections can be recovered from the image under the orthographic projection. Their sizes can be uniquely determined but their depth positions are two independent free parameters. Under the perspective projection, the shape and the orientations of the end-sections can be recovered uniquely but their depth positions are two independent free parameters.

A geometrical analysis in this study raised a question about how a generalized cone is perceived in the human visual system. Recall that the recovery of the generalized cone discussed in this study was based primarily on the pair of images of the end-sections. This approach to the recovery only works if both of the end-sections are partially- or fully-visible. Can the human visual system recover the shape of the cone using the method described in this paper? To address this question, the images of the end-sections should be controlled systematically and the perception of 3D shapes from their images should be tested in psychophysical experiments.

Note that the visual system probably has the ability to recover the 3D shape of the cone from visual information contained in only one end-section ( Fig. 11 ). An interesting example of this ability is shown in  Fig. 12 . The images of the two end-sections in the left and right halves of  Fig. 12  clearly violate the model-based invariant of a generalized cone. However, the two different shapes of the cones are perceived from these halves of the image and their shapes are merged together. This suggests that the two shapes of the cone around the end-sections are being recovered individually by the visual system and that these recovered shapes compose an impossible shape for the entire object, but, it does not mean that the visual system cannot recover the shape of a generalized cone by combining visual information contained in the images of two end-sections on the basis of their model-based invariant.

This study discussed recovering the axis of a generalized cone only when the axis is planar in a 3D scene (Section  2.1 . and 2.2.) by making use of an  a priori  constraint that the axis is normal to the cross-section of the cone (see  Chen et al., 2020, 2013  for studies using the same approach in Computer vision). Note that this study did not discuss the recovery of the axis when the axis was non-planar. The geometrical properties of the projections of the meridians and the occluding boundaries of the cone must be considered if one wants to recover the shape of the axis under general conditions.

This study analyzed the geometrical properties of a single generalized cone and its 2D image. It did not consider multiple generalized cones that compose complex 3D objects (see  Paschalidou et al., 2019; Tulsiani et al., 2017  for studies of this problem in Computer vision). Such an object is not a random combination of multiple generalized cones and their composition satisfies some  a priori  constraints, such as 3D mirror-symmetry ( Pizlo et al., 2014, 2010; Tulsiani et al., 2017 ). With this in place, the constraints satisfied by the composition of multiple generalized cones can introduce additional geometrical properties. For example, if there are two cones that are 3D mirror-symmetrical to one another, an image of one of these cones can be regarded as a virtual image of the other cone. Such an additional view of the cone should be helpful in recovering the shape of the cone.

The surface of a generalized cone can have a texture pattern and a luminance gradient of shading, but for simplicity, this study discussed only a contour drawing of the cone. Texture and shading on the cone’s surface can serve as depth cues that can affect the perceived shape of the cone ( Howard, 2012 ). The texture pattern should have an invariant component across the textured region of the surface. This invariant component can be regarded as one type of symmetry ( Pizlo, 2019; Pizlo & de Barros, 2021 ). Note that this symmetry of the texture pattern can interact with the shape of the cone if the pattern is anisotropic (see  Egan et al., 2011; Fleming et al., 2011; Li & Zaidi, 2000, 2004; Stevens, 1981; Stevens & Brookes, 1987; Todd et al., 2004; Todd & Thaler, 2010; Tse, 2002 ). The anisotropy of the pattern can help the visual system detect the images of the axis and of the cross-sections when the orientation of the anisotropy is along the axis of the cone, or when it is orthogonal to the axis. Note that the effect of the anisotropy of the pattern does not necessarily enhance the shape perception but it can suppress it (e.g.  Allen et al., 2012; Penacchio et al., 2018; Rowland, 2008 ).

This study assumed that the contours in the image of the generalized cone are given and it showed that the shape of the cone can be recovered by making use of some  a priori  constraints about the cone. But, detecting these contours from a retinal image of the cone and organizing them for the recovery of shape are also difficult tasks. These constraints can be also used for these difficult tasks. For example,  Chen et al. (2020)  used an  a priori  constraint in which the shapes of an object or of its parts are generalized cones with circular or rectangular end-sections for detecting and organizing the contours and for recovering the cones’ shapes (see  Michaux et al., 2016, 2017; Qian et al., 2019; Sala & Dickinson, 2015  for other studies in Computer vision that used similar approaches, but with different  a priori  constraints).

Analyses, in this study, were primarily concerned with geometrical properties of the end-sections of a generalized cone. The meridians of the cone received little attention. Note that the 3D shape of a generalized cone can be characterized by the shape of its cross-section, the shape of its axis, the relative position and orientation between its end-sections, and by scaling and rotating its cross-section along the axis. The shape of the cross-section and the relative position and orientation between the end-sections can be recovered from images of the end-sections. But, the projections of the meridians must be considered if one wants to recover the shape of the axis and the transformation of the cross-section along the axis. Further geometrical analyses of generalized cones and systematic studies that test human sensitivity to the geometrical properties of the cones are required to discuss the visual system’s ability to perceive generalized cones.

Consider finding a projection of a cross-section of a generalized cone from a set of curves that are projections of meridians of a generalized cone. As explained in the main text, it is assumed that the axis of the cone is planar and that the cross-section does not rotate around a tangent of the axis at an intersection of the axis with the cross-section, and that the shape of the cross-section is constant along the axis.

If the size of the cross-section is constant, a projection of a cross-section can be found at any point on a projection of a meridian under an orthographic projection. Note that every cross-section intersects with the meridians and that the tangents of the meridians are parallel to one another and they are normal to the section. The projections of these tangents are a set of parallel lines under an orthographic projection and they are also tangents of curves that are orthographic projections of the meridians. A set of points on the projections of the meridians that belong to a projection of a single cross-section can be found by using this parallelism (Figure A.1). Consider an arbitrary point on a curve of a projection of one of the meridians. A tangent of the curve at this arbitrary point is parallel to tangents of curves of projections of the other meridians at points that belong to a projection of the same cross-section as the arbitrary point belongs to. Note that the visual system is sensitive to the parallel configuration of contours ( Dvoeglazova et al., 2021; Feldman, 2007; Koffka, 1935; Koshmanova & Sawada, 2019; Leeuwenberg & van der Helm, 2013; Metzger, 2009; Wagemans, 1992; Witkin & Tenenbaum, 1983 ).

If the projection is perspective, it is much more difficult to find a projection of a cross-section based on the projections of the meridians. Note that the tangents of the meridians at every cross-section are parallel to one another and they are normal to the section. The projections of these tangents are a set of lines converging to their vanishing point. They are also tangents of curves that are perspective projections of the meridians. Now, consider a pair of given points that are on curves of projections of two meridians that belong to a projection of a single cross-section. The vanishing point for this cross-section is an intersection between two lines that are tangents of the two curves at the given points. A set of points on the projections of the other meridians that belong to the projection of the same cross-section can be found by using this vanishing point ( Fig. A.1 ). These points are tangent points between the curves and the lines that pass the vanishing point. However, this process of finding the projection of the cross-section depends entirely on the vanishing point determined from the given two points. If a pair of given points are on projections of two meridians but if they do  not  belong to a projection of a single cross-section, the process still can establish a set of points on the projections of the meridians but these points in the set do not belong to a projection of any common cross-section. Two lines that are tangents of the two curves at the given points intersect with one another. Note that there are lines that pass this intersection and that they are tangent to curves of projections of the other meridians. These tangent points, as well as the given points, belong to projections of different cross-sections. The visual system is also less sensitive to the converging configuration of contours than the parallel configuration of contours ( Dvoeglazova et al., 2021 ).

The difficulty posed when trying to find a point to which the tangents of curves converge is a problem both under an orthographic projection and also under a perspective projection if the size of the cross-section is variable along the axis. The tangents of the meridians at every cross-section are converging at a point and the projections of these tangents are a set of lines converging at a projection of the converging point. The projections of the tangents are also tangents of curves that are projections of the meridians. A projection of a cross-section can be found by making use of this converging point in the image if a pair of given points are on curves of projections of two meridians and if they belong to the projection of the cross-section. This process of finding the projection of the cross-section depends entirely on the converging point determined from the two given points. However, again, if a pair of given points are on projections of two meridians but they do “not” belong to a projection of a single cross-section, the process can still establish a set of points on the projections of the meridians but the points in this set do not belong to a projection of any common cross-section.
