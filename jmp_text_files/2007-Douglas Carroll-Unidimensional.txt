One latent variable model is said to approximate a second latent variable model if the two models make the same or nearly the same predictions about data. The approximation may be so accurate that it is virtually impossible to use data to tell whether simulated data have been generated by a model or an approximation of the model. Three-parameter logistic models with small “guessing” parameters provide a simple, though unimportant, example. A sequence of three-parameter logistic models for  n  items with item parameters  a i 
                   ,  b i 
                   ,  εc i 
                    ( i =1,…, n ) asymptotically approaches a perfect approximation of a two-parameter logistic model with parameters  a i 
                   ,  b i 
                    ( i =1,…, n ) as  ε  decreases to zero.

Holland and Rosenbaum (1986)  observe that the Common Causes Theorem of  Suppes and Zanotti (1981)  implies that every test model can be “perfectly” approximated by a unidimensional item response model. Here, “perfect approximation” of one latent trait model by another model means that the two models predict exactly the same item response data. Unfortunately, the models used in the proof of the Common Causes Theorem are unlikely to ever be applied to data because no one knows how to fit these models to item responses.

A recent theoretical result makes unidimensional model fit applicable to some large multidimensional data sets. According to the submodel theorem ( Levine, 2003 , Theorem 1), a multidimensional model with positive, continuous item response functions that are less than one is equivalent to various unidimensional submodels in the sense that both the multidimensional model and each unidimensional submodel predict the same pattern probabilities. This equivalence means that such multidimensional models can be perfectly approximated by unidimensional submodels.

The two most commonly used (multidimensional) item response models, the dominance and the ideal point model, satisfy the hypotheses of the submodel theorem. Consequently, it is possible to fit unidimensional item response submodels to the item responses from a two-dimensional test constructed with dominance items, ideal point items or a mix of both types of items.

The unidimensional submodels which are equivalent to a multidimensional model are necessarily nonparametric, even if the multidimensional model itself is parametric. Software has been developed which estimates nonparametric unidimensional models which are equivalent to the underlying multidimensional model. The estimation procedure is discussed in  Section 3 .

There are obvious advantages to fitting nonparametric unidimensional submodels rather than attempting to directly fit a multidimensional model to item responses. First, we are not limited by the facts that (a) nonparametric multidimensional item response theory is not very well developed and (b) appropriate software is available only for a narrow range of parametric multidimensional item response models.

Second, the most well-known parametric models (dominance and ideal point models) have implicit structural assumptions that often seem arbitrary and implausible. Unless these implicit assumptions are valid, any parametric multidimensional approximation is necessarily biased to some extent. Nonparametric models are more general and are thus better able to predict probabilities of item response patterns observed in actual data.

Estimation of unidimensional submodels of multidimensional models has been demonstrated for both simulation and actual data sets. In several unpublished informal studies and one published study ( Levine & Tsien, 1997 ), unidimensional models have been successfully fitted to simulation data sets generated from two-dimensional dominance and/or ideal models. item response function estimates have been obtained from actual personality inventory data which were almost certainly multidimensional ( Chernyshenko, Stark, Chan, Drasgow, & Williams, 2001 ;  Zickar, 1997 ). Goodness of fit was demonstrated in these actual data studies from the  χ 
                      2  statistic computed on single-item responses, pairs of item responses, and triples of item responses.

Nonmetric multidimensional scaling (MDS) was run on a manifold structure obtained from the unidimensional submodels (see  Section 2 ). Examination of the resulting configuration provided an answer to the first question, and reconstruction of a multidimensional model from the unidimensional submodels provided an answer to the second question.

Before attacking actual data with more general models, we must be able to reconstruct a known multidimensional model from unidimensional submodels obtained from simulated data. Data were simulated from two-dimensional dominance and ideal point item response functions, as discussed in  Section 5 .

The analyses in this paper were carried out in two stages. We first estimated several nonparametric unidimensional item response functions which are equivalent to the multidimensional item response functions for dominance items and ideal point items, by applying unidimensional estimation software on the simulated data. Goodness of fit of the unidimensional estimates is demonstrated in  Section 6 . We were able to obtain several estimates of unidimensional submodels by a selection technique described in  Sections 4 and 5 . We then applied nonmetric MDS on the unidimensional estimates (see  Section 7 ), and reconstructed the multidimensional model from the resulting configuration (see  Section 8 ).

The reconstruction technique requires the mathematical concept of a  test manifold , which is introduced in the next section.

The particular unidimensional model fitting program we currently use is  ForScore  ( Williams & Levine, 1994 ), which has previously fit large, complicated data sets very well. The theory behind  ForScore  was developed by  Levine (1984, 1989a, 1989b)  as  Maximum Likelihood Formula Score  ( MFS ) theory. A discussion of the theory is provided in the above citations, which were originally published as technical reports but are currently placed at the website  http://www.ed.uiuc.edu/cwl-mdm . A manual on  MFS  theory and the  ForScore  estimation technique is forthcoming. A brief but highly technical discussion of  MFS  is also in  Levine (2003) .

The manifold,  per se , along with its manifest distances, is generally of less interest to psychologists than the visualizable structures obtained by MDS. But some of the qualitative features that interest substantive psychological theorists (e.g., linear vs. nonlinear decision processes) are evident from such structures. Since the dimensionality of the manifold is  d ⪡ n , the manifold can be embedded into a space of much smaller dimensionality, which psychologists can examine in order to obtain psychologically meaningful interpretations of the dimensions.

Ideally, MDS would accept as input the  manifest distances  as defined above. But it is much easier to compute the length of the  line segment  connecting two points X

The chordal distance usually approximates the manifest distance well if the distance is short enough. But this approximation breaks down for widely separated points for very much the same reason that flat maps of planet earth can be linear with distances on the globe only over short ranges. The approximation of manifest distances by chordal distances also fails for manifolds that have folds like the bellows of an accordion or holes like a pretzel.

We have been fortunate in that the manifolds of the parametric models psychologists favor are simple enough to allow the successful use of MDS on chordal distances. Since for all but trivial models,

A primary objective of this paper was  reconstruction  of a multidimensional model from various unidimensional approximations. Reconstruction is not the same as estimation of multidimensional IRFs, or recovery of IRFs used in a simulation. We do not have the software for estimating item response probabilities as a function of 2 or more latent traits. The Submodel Theorem of  Levine (2003)  allows us to obtain estimates of various unidimensional approximations of the multidimensional item response model, using the unidimensional estimation software  ForScore . The ability of the unidimensional estimates to recover simulation data was established in chi square tests (see  Section 6 ).

Reconstruction requires the application of MDS software in order to associate each estimated item response probability (obtained by  ForScore  on some selection sample) with a pair of variables which are close to an affine transformation of the latent traits (unidentifiablity prevents us from estimating the latent traits directly). Graphs of estimated item response probabilities as a function of these variables obtained by MDS are able to distinguish the  cylindrical  dominance items from the  unimodal  ideal point items.

The application of MDS requires that the estimated IRFs be formed into a mathematical structure called a  manifold . An estimate of the manifold for a particular test can be obtained without knowing the latent space, and without assuming any parametric form of the IRFs. Unlike IRFs, the manifold is unaffected by transformations of the latent space. But the manifold cannot be visualized directly without a tool like MDS for creating an  isometric representation  with reduced dimensionality, since each manifold point is constructed from the IRFs of an  n -item test.

An MDS routine like KYST takes as input points on the input manifold, and outputs coordinates of each point on a reconfigured manifold embedded in a space of smaller dimensionality. The relationship between the input manifold and reconfigured manifold is such that distance properties are preserved. Distances computed between pairs of corresponding points in the two manifolds are matched in rank order as closely as possible.

In the present study, KYST succeeded in collapsing high-dimensional test manifold approximations into a sufficiently succinct representation to determine the nature and degree of multidimensionality. KYST consistently showed that the simulation data described in  Section 5  were 2-dimensional. The configuration { K 
                         3 (X*)} obtained in the  dominance  condition was nearly flat in  R 
                         3 . In the  ideal point  condition, { K 
                         3 (X*)} was the surface of an ellipsoid in  R 
                         3 . In the  combined  condition, { K 
                         3 (X*)} was the surface of a shallow bowl.

Unfortunately, these data did not provide much of a test of the efficacy of Shepard and Carroll's approach to curvature. KYST was able to find adequate data representations even when large distances were included. KYST's robustness may have been a consequence of (1)  ForScore  being able to place points close together and (2) selecting points more or less evenly spaced on the test manifolds. When there are many regularly spaced close points, the small distances greatly outnumber the large ones.

Since the dimensionality of the latent space equals the dimensionality of the test manifold, one can infer dimensionality by inspecting a configuration of an isometric representation of the manifold, like configuration { K 
                         3 (X*)} obtained by KYST. If the points are all on a 2-dimensional surface of a 3-dimensional structure, whether the surface is open like in the  dominance  or  combined  condition or closed like in the  ideal point  condition, then the dimensionality of the manifold, and thus the latent space, must be 2.

                            STRESS

But few of these indices have been applied to situations in which the manifold is very curved, or in which the IRFs deviated from a dominance model. It should be emphasized that we used KYST as a discovery tool, and not to compute indices like dimensionality with particular statistical properties and which can be compared to other indices.

These findings are consistent with thinking of dimensionality as a local topological concept rather than as a global concept. For example, if the configuration { K 
                         3 (X*)} lies on the boundary of a 3-dimensional sphere, then, for every point  K

                         3 (X*)} is contained on the boundary and in the interior of the sphere, then almost all neighborhoods of any point  K 
                         3 (X*) would contain points that would lie in the entire 3-dimensional neighborhood of  K 
                         3 (X*).

If KYST were run “locally” for X* in the  ideal point  condition, then  
                            STRESS1

                         2 (X*)} has a low  
                            STRESS1 
                          value whenever KYST is run on some neighborhood of all base points X*, then we can conclude that the dimensionality of the test manifold is 2.

The change in structure from a flat latent space to curved manifold may also affect the interpretation of dimensions. Two latent variables underlie each manifold point in the present study. These variables are independent and have a bivariate normal distribution. Points on the surface of the spherical configuration of a manifold constructed from IRFs for ideal point items, on the other hand, have attributes of latitude and longitude, which are not always apparent from the flat latent space.

Some research in vocational interests has attempted to provide interpretation of structures of occupations.  Tracey and Rounds (1996)  placed the occupations on the boundary of a sphere, with status provided as an additional substantive dimension to location on a circle conforming to the RIASEC model of  Holland (1985) .  Armstrong, Smith, Donnay, and Rounds (2004)  also represented occupations on the boundary of a sphere, but provided a more flexible interpretation of each of the dimensions.

It is unclear in the above studies whether the occupations are better represented in a 3-dimensional space (with points inside and outside of a sphere) or on the boundary of a sphere. If the latter is the case, then each point on the sphere corresponds to a pair of coordinates on a flat latent space. But it is the location on the MDS configuration which is psychologically more interesting. For example, latitude might represent prestige and longitude might represent one of the six types of vocational characteristics.

In contrast, ( Prediger (1982)  and  Prediger and Vansickle (1992)  attempted to represent the occupations in two dimensions. He interpreted the dimensions as Data/Ideas and People/Things. If only these two attributes accounted for all the variation in vocational interests, then the (2-dimensional) latent space and the isometric representation obtained from MDS are related by an affine transformation.

One might ask whether we actually need the discovery tool. In other words, are there tests which contain items satisfying both dominance and ideal point models? Although many studies have estimated IRFs satisfying an ideal point model (cf.,  Chernyshenko, et al., 2001 ;  Stark, et al., 2006 ), no known studies have analyzed both dominance and ideal point items. The present study implies that it is easy to identify tests containing both dominance and ideal point items—hemispheric-like configurations are obtained by running MDS on estimated manifold points.

In  Section 1 , it was pointed out that the dominance and ideal point parametric models have implicit structural assumptions that often seem arbitrary and implausible. More general models, parametric or otherwise, may better describe item response patterns obtained in actual data.

An example of an implicit structural assumption in the ideal point model, whatever its dimensionality, is the  radial symmetry  of IRFs. Equiprobability curves ( d =2) or equiprobability surfaces ( d >2) are implicitly assumed to be circles or spheres, as is shown in  Fig. 2 . Would not ellipses in various orientations (cf.  Carroll, 1972, 1980 ) better represent the anticipated anisotropy about ideal points for preferences and beliefs? The elliptical model reflects different weights in each dimension, and the orientation of the ellipse may reflect some trait which has the most influence in the distance from the ideal point.

An example of an implicit assumption in the dominance model, whatever its dimensionality, is  cylindricality  of IRFs. For every dominance item evaluated at  d -dimensional latent trait vectors, there is a ( d −1)-dimensional vector space of directions from each point in the model's latent space such that the directional derivative of the IRF is identically zero in each of these directions. It sometimes seems preferable, on the other hand, to allow what an item is measuring to smoothly change with increasing ability levels. For example, at very low levels of ability, performance on a wordy paragraph comprehension item most likely is measuring reading ability. Among better readers, the same item can be measuring individual differences in analytical reasoning to a greater extent than reading ability.

Further studies need to be carried out to determine whether MDS, applied locally or globally, can recover properties of manifolds constructed from more general item response models, and to discover the form of IRFs like those analyzed above. Of course, more general models may become necessary when data from actual tests are analyzed.

Although we can determine the dimensionality of the test and discover the shapes of IRFs, we cannot recover the simulation multidimensional IRFs directly. Our reconstruction approach obtains IRFs which are unique up to transformations which leave the manifold unchanged.

Some questions remain. In particular, which transformation of multidimensional IRFs is appropriate for computing examinee scores based on item response patterns? Knowledge of the exact simulation model would greatly assist in computing scores which are unbiased. Unfortunately, the current procedure cannot recover the exact IRFs like it could if the examinee's data were unidimensional.

More research needs to be conducted on multidimensional data in order to obtain multidimensional IRFs which are appropriate for scoring.
