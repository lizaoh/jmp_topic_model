Sequential decisions are pervasive and important in our daily lives. Examples include decisions about daily food consumption, exercise, medical testing, financial investing, and career path. All of these decisions are sequential in the sense that they occur repeatedly, and decisions made at each point in time affect the choices available and outcome probabilities for future decisions. To better understand sequential decisions, it is important to understand how people frame possible outcomes of each available alternative at any given point in time. In this study, we examined sequential decision making under risk involving high stakes in a naturalistic domain and constructed a choice model to compare decision frames for subsequent choices.

Differences between risky choice in the laboratory and in realistic situations has been observed for over half a century. The former involves hypothetical decisions or decisions with extremely low stakes.  Slovic, Lichtenstein, and Edwards (1965)  found that participants felt bored and unmotivated with imaginary choices or choices with very tiny payoffs and tended to use different strategies when facing bets for consequential amounts of money. Researchers began to study risky choice in naturalistic gambling and game show contexts that involve substantial monetary outcomes. Naturalistic study of risky choice can be traced back to the early 1970s when Edwards and his students collected data from gamblers on the floor of the 4 Queens Casino in downtown Las Vegas (e.g.,  Lichtenstein & Slovic, 1973; Snapper, Edwards, & Peterson, 1972 ). More recent studies used archival data from various TV game shows, including  Card Sharks  (e.g.,  Gertner, 1993 ),  Jeopardy!  (e.g.,  Metrick, 1995 ),  Lingo  (e.g.,  Beetsma & Schotman, 2001 ),  Deal or No Deal  (DOND; e.g.,  Andersen, Harrison, Lau, & Rutström, 2008; Blavatskyy & Pogrebna, 2008; Bombardini & Trebbi, 2012; Brooks, Faff, Mulino, & Scheelings, 2009; Deck, Lee, & Reyes, 2008; Post, van den Assem, Baltussen, & Thaler, 2008; de Roos & Sarafidis, 2010 ),  Joker  (e.g.,  Matsen & Strøm, 2010 ), and  Golden Balls  (e.g.,  van den Assem, van Dolder, & Thaler, 2012 ) etc.

The current study utilizes archived data from the DOND game show. There are several desirable features of the DOND game that have made it a popular naturalistic study domain for economists for the past decade. The DOND game involves sequential binary choices between a sure-thing amount and a complex, risky alternative (continue game play) at each round of play. Players in the game are selected based on attributes unrelated to any decision making skill or knowledge. Since the DOND involves actual payouts to players in the tens or hundreds of thousands of rewards (up to $1 million), players are highly motivated to consider their choices at each round carefully and deliberately.

Previous DOND studies mainly aggregated data for all players across all rounds of the game, constructed different risky choice models, and evaluated risk attitudes implied by the estimated model parameters (e.g.,  Aissia, 2016; Andersen et al., 2008; Blavatskyy & Pogrebna, 2010a; Bombardini & Trebbi, 2012; Botti, Conte, Di Cagno, & D’Ippoliti, 2008; Deck et al., 2008; Mulino, Scheelings, Brooks, & Faff, 2009; Post et al., 2008; de Roos & Sarafidis, 2010 ). Some of the DOND choice modeling studies also compared different decision models (e.g.,  Botti et al., 2008; de Roos & Sarafidis, 2010 ). Yet other studies were designed to detect framing effects (e.g.,  Baltussen, Post, & van den Assem, 2008; Brooks et al., 2009 ) or an endowment effect  (Blavatskyy & Pogrebna, 2010b)  in the DOND game. One DOND study found that advice from the studio audience had little or no impact on the players’ decisions  (Pogrebna, 2008) . Another DOND study reported that players’ attitude toward risk is not influenced by the likelihood of winning a large amount  (Blavatskyy & Pogrebna, 2008) .

We constructed different choice models to account for risky choice behavior in the DOND game. Moreover, compared to previous DOND studies, our study analyzed data aggregated at each round rather than data aggregated across multiple rounds. The by-round analysis enabled us to explore whether choice behavior changed over time. By comparing models based on different decision frames for how players evaluated possible outcomes of the subsequent choices at each round, we were also able to discover how players framed choices at each round in the DOND game.

In the following sections, we briefly describe the DOND game show as well as the archived data used in this study. We report summary descriptive statistics to characterize the game context and observed choices in the game. We then motivate and describe the choice models used under different assumptions of decision frame for subsequent choices and outcomes. Following, we report results from maximum likelihood estimation of model parameters, including a comparison of competing model frames. The final section includes a discussion of our findings and concluding remarks.

The DOND game show originated in the Netherlands in November 2000 and rapidly grew in popularity to eventually air in about 80 countries around the world. The show continues to air in several countries (as of June, 2018), including Algeria, Greece, Macedonia, Moldova and the Netherlands. The original game has morphed into different versions for different audiences, varying the number of cases, amounts of money at stake, number of rounds, and number of cases opened in each round. All versions of the game begin with several sealed cases (over 20) and a single identified player. Each case contains a number indicating a different amount of money sealed inside, which varies from nearly nothing (e.g., $0.01) to as much as $1,000,000. The player first chooses one case that is identified as the “player’s case”, which is removed as an option for opening during the game. Each game involves up to nine rounds, in which the player is required to select a specified number of cases to reveal reward values in each round. The player then faces a choice between either accepting a sure monetary amount offered by a banker (“deal”) or continuing the game with the remaining unopened cases (“no deal”). All players are paid the actual amount of the banker’s offer when they choose “deal”; in the event that the player never chooses to deal through the final round, the player is paid the amount in the (unopened) case selected at the beginning of the game.

This study utilized archival data from the UK and two US versions of the DOND game, including the US primetime (USP) and the US syndicated (USS) versions.

The “banker” is a fictional character who phones in the sure-thing offer to the host for each round of the game. The banker is in fact an imaginary character, cast as a villain whose objective is to pay out as little money to players as possible. The sure thing offers are generated in an unknown manner. To better understand the choice behavior of players, we began by examining the (banker’s) sure-thing offers.

Post et al. (2008)  reported that the banker knows the amount in the unopened cases in the Italian, French, and Spanish versions of the DOND game. We tested whether this was true for the UK, USP, and USS versions of the DOND game. We examined the bankers’ offers at the last round of each version of the game, when there were only two cases unrevealed in the game. If the player rejects the banker’s offer in the final round (“no deal”), the player awarded the amount in the player’s case, selected at the beginning of the game. If the banker knows the amounts in the two unopened cases, one would expect the banker to make a better offer when the player’s case contains the larger of the two remaining reward values. By comparing the amount sealed in the player’s case and that sealed in the other unopened case, all games that included a final round were partitioned into two groups, depending on whether the player’s case contained the larger or smaller reward amount. For each version of the game, the banker’s offer in the final round was compared between the two groups, and there were no significant differences among the UK, USP, or USS versions. Based on this analysis, we conclude that the person or algorithm generating the banker’s offers in the UK, USP, and USS versions of the game did not know the values for the unopened cases.

In sum, the banker tended to make better offers (defined as higher banker’s ratios) as the game progressed. One explanation is that the game becomes more entertaining in later rounds, and the producers of the show want to avoid players dealing in the early rounds. Bankers in all three versions of the game were found to have the same information as the players; both were blind to the specific amount in each of the unopened cases.

In total, about 73% of players in the UK version and about 85% of players in both US versions eventually accepted a banker’s offer (i.e., a “deal”). The majority of all deals are made in the two rounds just prior to the final round. No one accepted the banker’s offers in the first two rounds and very few players accepted the banker’s offer in the third round. The median amounts of final winnings were £12,235 (about $17,728 in 2009 January) in the UK version, $83,000 in the USP version, and $19,000 in the USS version, which are substantially larger incentives than the typical reward payments in laboratory experiments.

Nearly all previous studies modeling DOND player choices aggregated data from multiple rounds for all players to estimate parameters in the constructed choice models. This approach implicitly assumes that the choice model is stationary across rounds of the DOND game. By constructing models at each round of the game, we are able to assess whether players’ adherence to any particular model changes as the game progresses. Previous preference modeling studies incorporated a stochastic framework modeling preferences (e.g.,  Falmagne, Regenwetter, & Grofman, 1997; Harless & Camerer, 1994; Hey & Orme, 1994; Loomes & Sugden, 1995; Regenwetter et al., 2014; Regenwetter & Marley, 2001 ). Similarly, DOND choice modeling studies have combined different stochastic frameworks with decision theories to model risky choice in various versions of the DOND game.  Table 5  summarizes previous relevant DOND studies.

For the present study, we chose the Softmax action selection rule  (Sutton & Barto, 1998)  as the stochastic framework and combined it with a utility function. It is a reinforcement learning model and consistent with  Luce’s Choice Axiom (1959) . It helps map utility values into action probabilities. Though Luce’s choice model was considered identical to the FHO  (Blavatskyy & Pogrebna, 2010a) , the Softmax action selection rule utilized in the present study would be categorized as a random function approach that utilizes “a probability measure on a space of functions”  (Regenwetter & Marley, 2001) .  Blavatskyy and Pogrebna (2010a)  chose stochastic choice models that allow affine transformations for utility functions, which is inconsistent with the Luce choice model. Since the present study analyzed DOND game data by round, our stochastic choice model was not required to capture choice behavior changing over time. Instead, the parameter estimates from by-round analysis are compared to assess changes in players’ choice behavior over time. The Softmax action selection rule allows assessment of deviation from EU maximization.

In several previous DOND choice modeling studies, player choices are compared to a normative model that requires consideration of all possible payoffs until the end of the game and employed backward induction to evaluate the “no deal” option (fully forward-looking, (F-FL; e.g.,  Andersen et al., 2008; Deck et al., 2008 ). Some studies treated players as forward-looking, but more myopic in that they were assumed to consider possible outcomes only one round ahead to evaluate the “no deal” option (e.g.,  Blavatskyy & Pogrebna, 2010a; Post et al., 2008 ). Others considered players to be static rather than forward-looking, treating the reward amounts in the remaining unopened cases as the possible outcomes of the “no deal” option. Compared to the normative F-FL decision frame for evaluating the “no deal” option, both myopic evaluation strategies are more likely to be descriptive of how players are evaluating the “no deal” option and deciding whether to accept the sure-thing offer.

Busemeyer and Pleskac (2009)  concluded that players usually violated dynamic consistency and hence are unlikely to follow backward induction in making dynamic decisions in strategic games.  Binmore, McCarthy, Ponti, Samuelson, and Shaked (2002)  and  Johnson, Camerer, Sen, and Rymon (2002)  questioned the rationality of performing backward induction calculations in sequential bargaining games (as cited in  Post et al., 2008 ). The F-FL decision frame for evaluating the “no deal” option also implies EU maximization and constant risk attitude over time.  Post et al. (2008)  pointed out that their “video material indeed suggests that contestants generally look only one round ahead” in the Dutch, German, and USP versions of the DOND game. Therefore, in the present study, we propose more descriptive models in which the decision frame for evaluating the “no deal” option at each round of the DOND game was either completely static (no look ahead) or forward-looking looking only one round ahead.

For the decision frame of looking only one round ahead, previous DOND choice modeling studies assumed that players were able to follow certain rules, such as forming a distribution for the banker’s offers (e.g.,  Andersen et al., 2008 ), or performing an intuitive subjective regression (e.g.,  Blavatskyy & Pogrebna, 2010a; Deck et al., 2008; de Roos & Sarafidis, 2010 ) to forecast the banker’s future offers in subsequent rounds. The present study proposes a heuristic way to account for how players in DOND games evaluate possible outcomes of the “no deal” option inspired by  Simon’s (1955, 1956, 1991)  concept of bounded rationality. Generally speaking, boundedly rational players have limited access to information and constrained computational capacities. An expanding literature on the use of heuristics in judgment and decision making over the past few decades suggests that people tended to replace complex algorithms with simple ones (e.g.,  Tversky & Kahneman, 1974 ), and use partial information sets (e.g.,  Gigerenzer & Gaissmaier, 2011 ) in order to reduce necessary effort (e.g.,  Shah & Oppenheimer, 2008 ). Considering the limited time allowed for players to decide whether to deal in each round of the DOND game, we assume that players in the DOND game do not try to forecast the banker’s subsequent offers in coming rounds as part of the process of choosing whether to deal or not. Instead, they treat the expected values of possible combinations of remaining cases in the coming round as possible outcomes for the “no deal” option. To distinguish these two decision frames, we named the former focusing on the future banker’s offers as the deep forward-looking (D-FL) decision frame and the latter, ignoring the banker’s subsequent offers, as the superficially forward-looking (S-FL) decision frame. Just as previous DOND choice modeling studies assumed (e.g.,  Blavatskyy & Pogrebna, 2010a; Deck et al., 2008; de Roos & Sarafidis, 2010 ), in the D-FL decision frame model, players are assumed to look one round ahead and make subjective predictions of the banker’s offers in subsequent rounds based on the values of the remaining unopened cases. Some prior studies estimated regression functions for the banker’s offer for each round (e.g.,  Blavatskyy & Pogrebna, 2010a; de Roos & Sarafidis, 2010 ), while others estimated a single regression function across all rounds (e.g.,  Deck et al., 2008 ) of the DOND game. We adopted the latter strategy as it seems more plausible for players to assume the banker uses a consistent algorithm for computing sure-thing offers across all rounds of the game.

We found that the proposed S-FL decision frame outperformed both the static and the D-FL decision frames. Specifically, we found that players choose to deal or not at each round as though they treat the expected value of each possible combination of remaining amounts in the subsequent round as equally likely outcomes of the “no deal” option for the current round. Our findings suggest that players’ decisions are consistent with looking one round ahead rather than not looking ahead at all. If the main goal of heuristics in judgmentand decision making is to reduce efforts required for a task, as discussed by  Shah and Oppenheimer (2008) , our results suggest that players might adopt a somewhat forward looking but largely myopic heuristic in the sequential decision making context of DOND.

Based on the goodness of fit results at each round across all three DOND game versions, we found that when the number of remaining cases for the continuing game exceeds five (i.e., early rounds in all three game versions), the S-FL decision frame model always showed the best goodness of fit. When the number of remaining cases for the continuing game is four or less and players were asked to reveal only one case at each of the coming rounds (i.e., later rounds in both U.S. versions of the game), there are almost no differences in the goodness of fit results across all three decision frame models. This suggests that the degree of choice complexity at each round influenced the decision frame used to evaluate the “no deal” risky alternative. When the risky alternative is simple, the nearly constant goodness of fit among all three decision frame models in this study implies individual differences in how players frame and evaluate risky alternatives in sequential decision making.

Our results also demonstrate how risk attitude and adherence to EU maximization change over time as the DOND game progresses. As players started to accept the banker’s offers from round 3 in the DOND game, we might expect that the changes in both the risk attitude and the rationality over time were partly due to the non-random selection of players at later rounds, i.e., those who did not choose to deal in earlier rounds. This explanation implies that the changes in both parameters from one round to the next round should be larger following rounds in which larger numbers of players accepted the banker’s offer. Focusing on the numbers of players accepting the banker’s offers at both rounds 6 and 7 in the USP version of the game (See  Table 4 ), we find that the number of “deal” choices at round 7 is twice larger than the number at round 6. However, from the estimation results in  Table 7 , the changes in both parameters from round 6 to round 7 in the USP version is almost as negligible as the changes from round 7 to round 8. This pattern suggests that the non-random selection of players for later rounds is not the primary cause of changes in the risk attitude and the rationality parameters over rounds in all three versions of the DOND game. As the game progressed, perhaps players became caught up in the excitement of the audience, or became more determined to “beat the banker”, or just became greedier, resulting in less risk averse decisions. Increasing deviation from EU maximization over the course of the game suggests that players were more idiosyncratic in their choices later in the game, perhaps reflecting individual differences in risk attitudes that were manifested by model parameters that suggest nearly random responding in later rounds of the game.

The estimated values of the risk attitude parameter across all three DOND game versions in the S-FL decision frame model are all smaller than 0.5, indicating that players were quite risk averse across all rounds and all three versions of the game. This finding is consistent with  Holt and Laury’s (2002)  finding that people were extremely risk averse in the context of large stakes. The observed drift away from risk aversion across rounds in all three game versions is expected, given that the most risk averse players will generally accept the banker’s offer (“deal”) at earlier rounds, leaving less risk averse players to provide data at subsequent rounds. Interestingly, players in the UK version of the DOND game exhibited the greatest risk aversion, while those in the USP version were least risk averse.

The estimated values of the rationality parameter were smallest values for the S-FL decision frame model, supporting the finding that the S-FL decision frame model captured player’s choices better than either of the other two decision frame models. Compared across all three DOND game versions, the estimated values of the rationality parameter in the UK version were extremely small, which suggests that the UK players’ decisions were consistent with a rational choice model of maximizing EU. The estimated values of the rationality parameter for both US versions of the game suggest some consistency with an EU maximization model, but somewhat less consistent with maximizing EU compared to UK game players. This might be due to the format of the UK version, as it has the fewest number of rounds among the three game versions and players are required to reveal three or five cases in each round. The number of cases players are required to open in each round varies a great deal in both US versions of the DOND game. Estimated values of the rationality parameter increased over rounds for all three game versions, indicating that players are less predictable in later rounds. One explanation is that the players are more swayed by an emotional audience in later rounds of the game, producing less consistent choices. A related explanation is that players are given more time to consider their “deal” vs. “no deal” decision in later rounds, and this increased decision time interval results in less consistent decision making.

It is important to highlight limitations of the current study. Since we merely compared our proposed S-FL decision frame model with the static and D-FL decision frame models that had been widely used in the literature, it is possible that other decision frame models that were not tested may have been in a better fit. In this study, we applied a linear prediction function in each version of the game to predict the banker’s future offers for all rounds. It is also possible that a different prediction function, with different predictors or a different functional form, would have led to different results. Additionally, though the by-round analysis in this study relaxed the assumption of similar choice behavior over time compared to most previous DOND choice modeling studies, this study could not explicitly model individual differences in choice behavior across players.

The present study suggests that (DOND) players behave as though they look ahead in making sequential choices, but not too far ahead. In addition, (DOND) players’ choice behavior exhibits less risk aversion and less adherence to EU maximization as the game progresses. Since the proximal cues available to the player are the reward values of the remaining unopened cases, a natural heuristic for evaluating the option of continuing to play (“no deal”) is simply the EU of the remaining cases. It is remarkable that this (static) model was less consistent with the observed choices than one that allows the player to look one round ahead and consider the  
                         E 
                         V 
                       of all possible combinations of remaining cases at the next round. Our result that the model looking superficially one round ahead to future expected values (S-FL) outperformed a more optimal normative model looking one round ahead deeply with future sure-thing offers (D-FL) suggests that players are indeed bounded in their ability to consider available information.

Our results also have potential implications for other sequential decision problems outside of game show studios. Our results suggest that people are likely to look ahead to the very near term in making recurring decisions. Looking ahead to the near future may result in better decisions than not looking ahead at all; but lacking deep consideration of the outcomes for subsequent choices may result in substantial deviations from optimal strategies and overall reduced rewards. That players look forward at all suggests that they are aware that their immediate decision may impact both the range and quality of subsequent decision alternatives. The results of our analysis are consistent with both short-sided and impulsive decision making that constrains future opportunities at later points in time.
