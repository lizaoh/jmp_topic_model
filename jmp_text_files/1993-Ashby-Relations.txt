Relations between Prototype, Exemplar, and Decision Bound Models of Categorization 

I. GREGORY ASHBY AND W. ‘Topp MAbbox 

University of California at Santa Barbara 

Categorization models make assumptions about: (1) the representation of the stimulus and the contrasting categories; (2) the exact information that must be collected before a response can be made; and (3) response selection—how the subject selects a response after all the relevant information has been collected. Equivalence relations are derived between prototype, exemplar, and decision bound models with respect to these three assumptions. An exemplar model that assumes a deterministic response selection process is proposed that includes the generalized context model (GCM) as a special case. The general linear classifier of general recognition theory (GRT) is shown to contain the probabilistic weighted prototype model as a special case. The set of all percepts that are equivocal with respect to category membership define the equivocality contour. The GCM is shown to predict the same equivocality contour as the GRT optimal classifier under certain restriclive distributional conditions, Several more general exemplar models are proposed that can mimic the equivocality contour of the GRT optimal classifier under less restrictive conditions. © 1993 Academic Press, Inc. 

On each trial of a categorization task, a subject is shown one of n stimuli and is asked to assign it to one of + experimenter defined categories, where mm <n. This article explores the cquivalence relations between three types of categorization models. Prototype models assume that when asked to assign a stimulus to one of several categorics, the subject responds with the calegory possessing the most similar prototype (Reed, 1972). Exemplar models assume that subjects perform some sort of global match between the representation of the presented stimulus and the memory representation of every exemplar of each category and then choose a response on the basis of these similarity computations (Brooks, 1978; Estes, 1986; Hintzman, 1986; Medin & Schaffer, 1978; Nosofsky, 1986). Decision bound models, which are derived from general recognition theory (GRT; Ashby & Townsend, 1986), assume that the subject learns to assign responses to regions of the perceptual space. In spite of the apparently large differences between these models, they all make three kinds of assumptions: (1) representation assumptions, (2) retrieval assumptions, and (3) response selection assumptions. The representation assumptions describe the internal representation of the stimulus and the contrasting categories. The most popular versions of each model explicitly assume that the perceptuai effect of a particular stimulus presentation can be represented as a point in a multidimensional space, but the models differ on the issue of whether there is variability in the percepts associated with a single stimulus. The retrieval assumptions form the soul of the categorization theories. They describe the exact information that must be collected before a response can be made. For example, exemplar models assume that the subject computes the similarity of the stimulus representation to the memory representation of every exemplar of each category. The response selection assumptions describe how the subject selects a response after all the relevant information has been collected. The major point of contention between the current models is whether response selection is deterministic or probabilistic. A deterministic response selection process always chooses the same response given the same perceptual information, whereas a probabilistic process does not. 

This article examines each of these three assumptions in turn. This provides a common language from which to describe and formally compare the various models. The discussion begins with representation assumptions, proceeds with response selection assumptions, and finishes with retrieval assumptions. Our results focus primarily on experiments with two contrasting categories, although in many instances generalization to n-alternative experiments is straightforward. 

Nosofsky (1990) investigated the relations between a number of exemplar and likelihood models of categorization. Likelihood models are closely related, although not equivalent, to one particular class of decision bound models—namely, those that assume the subject maximizes overall categorization accuracy—and so many of the results that we describe later in this article may be seen as extensions of Nosofsky’s (1990) work.

RETRIEVAL ASSUMPTIONS 

Decision Bound Models 

General recognition theory (GRT) assumes that subjects attempt to solve the computational problem of maximizing categorization accuracy (Ashby, 1992; Ashby & Gott, 1988; Ashby & Lee, 1991; Ashby & Maddox, 1990, 1992). The optimal solution to this problem is to divide the stimulus space into response regions (e.g., Fisher, 1936; Fukunaga, 1990; Morrison, 1990). On each trial, the optimal classifier determines in which region the stimulus is located and then emits the associated response. The partition between two competing response regions is called the decision bound. GRT assumes that subjects attempt to respond optimally, but because of inherent limitations in their information processing capabilities, they fail in this goal. Five such limitations are identified: (a) perceptual noise, (b) selection of a suboptimal decision bound, (c) variability in the memory of this bound, (d) response bias, and (e) variability in the memory of the response criterion. 

In some cases the decision bound may be a line or a simple curve that has a straightforward verbal analogue. For example, consider squares that vary in size and brightness. The rule that large squares should be assigned to category A and small squares to category B is equivalent to using a linear decision bound that is parallel to the brightness dimension. In many cases, however, the decision bound will have no straightforward verbal analogue. This was the case, for example, with the quadratic bounds studied by Ashby and Maddox (1992). 

To construct its decision bound, the optimal classifier must have perfect knowledge of the structure of each relevant category. This information is unavailable to the subject, so he or she must adopt a suboptimal bound. Several versions of GRT can be formulated depending on the type of decision bound the subject chooses to select. The four versions considered in this article are: (a) the general quadratic classifier, (b)the general linear classifier, (c) the minimum distance classifier, and (d) the optimal classifier.

Lemma | and Proposition 4 indicate that there is a natural pairing of the normal distribution with Euclidean distance and the Gaussian similarity function and of the Laplace distribution with city-block distance and the exponential similarity function. Several facts suggest that this pairing is not arbitrary. First, note that as one moves away from the mean, the probability density function of the normal distribution decreases in accordance with the Gaussian similarity function, whereas the probability density function of the Laplace distribution decreases in accordance with the exponential similarity function. One consequence of this fact is that distant exemplars more strongly affect response probabilities in GRT models with Laplace distributions and in GCM models with exponential similarity functions. To see this, note that the Laplace distribution has higher tails than the normal. Thus, in GRT models, stimuli that are far from the decision bound more strongly influence the shape of the bound when the perceptual distributions are Laplace than when they are normal. Similarly, because the exponential similarity function has a higher tail than the Gaussian function, exemplars that are far from the presented stimulus will more strongly influence overall category similarity when the similarity function is exponential than when it is Gaussian. 

A second relevant fact is that the contours of equal likelihood for independent bivariate normal distributions and the contours of equal similarity for distance measure £3 are both eiliptical, with major and minor axes coincident with the dimensional axes. Analogously, the contours of equal likelihood for independent bivariate Laplace distributions and the contours of equal similarity for distance measure C'B3 are both diamond-shaped, with corners that intersect the dimensional axes, 

Lemma 1 and Proposition 4 show that the equivocality contour predicted by the GCM is as sensitive to category covariance structure as the equivocality contour of the stimulus invariant, uncorrelated GRT optimal classifier. Even so, the GCM and GRT models treat suboptimality differently. The GCM stresses the importance of selective attention, which indirectly affects the equivocality contour. The GRT models assume that the subject operates on the equivocality contour (ie., the decision bound) directly. If the optimal decision bound is linear and the subject is using a linear bound with a slope or intercept that is suboptimal, then the GRT general linear classifier and the GCM should both provide good fits to the resulting data because manipulating attention and response bias is essentially equivalent to changing the slope and intercept of the equivocality contour (i.e., see Proposition 2). However, if the optimal bound is quadratic and the subject uses a quadratic bound that is suboptimal, then because the effects of manipulating attention and response bias are limited, the GRT general quadratic classifier should provide better fits than the GCM. Of course, if the direct action of the subject is one of selective attention, then the extra power of the general quadratic classifier (in the form of extra free parameters) is wasted.
