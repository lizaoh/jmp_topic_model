Identification Model Based on the Maximum Information Entropy Principle 

Hisao Miyano 

Chiba University E-mail: miyano�cogsci.L.chiba-u.ac.jp 

A new theoretical approach to stimulus identification is proposed through a probabilistic multidimensional model based on the maximum information entropy principle. The approach enables us to derive the multidimensional scaling (MDS) choice model, without appealing to Luce's choice rule and without defining a similarity function. It also clarifies the relationship between the MDS choice model and the optimal version of the identification model based on Ashby's general recognition theory; it is shown theoretically that the identification model derived from the new approach includes these two models as special cases. Finally, as an application of our approach, a model of similarity judgment is proposed and compared with Ashby's extended similarity model. � 2001 Academic Press 

I. INTRODUCTION 

Models of stimulus identification have been developed and studied by many researchers as one of the most important and attractive problems in psychology, especially in the fields of mathematical psychology and psychometrics. This is perhaps because stimulus identification inevitably relates to other basic psychological concepts such as similarity, categorization, preference, and signal detection. In fact, Ashby and co-workers (e.g., Ashby 6 Townsend, 1986, Ashby 6 Perrin, 1988; Ashby, 1992; Perrin, 1992; Ashby 6 Maddox, 1994) proposed a unified framework for modeling psychological processes involving these concepts that is referred to as general recognition theory (GRT). 

There are at least three types of stimulus identification models, the similarity (biased) choice model (e.g., Luce, 1963), the MDS choice model (Shepard, 1958;

Nosofsky, 1991; Takane 6 Shibayama, 1992), and the probabilistic multidimensional choice models. The MDS choice model is a restricted version of the similarity choice model, in which each stimulus is represented as a point in some multidimensional space, and similarity is inversely related to interpoint distance. The probabilistic multidimensional choice models are the models based on GRT (Ashby 6 Lee, 1991; Ashby, 1992; Marley, 1992) and a probabilistic version of the MDS choice model developed by Ennis (1992). In this paper, the identification model based on GRT is referred to as the GRT identification model. 

We here address two issues related to the existing identification models. One deals with the assumptions of the MDS choice model, and the other with the relationship between the MDS choice model and the GRT identification model. As will be shown in the next section, the MDS choice model assumes that (1) identification performance can be modeled in terms of similarities among stimuli and response biases (Luce's similarity-choice rule; Luce, 1963), and (2) the similarity between a pair of stimuli is given by a negative exponential function of the distance between the perceptual representations of the stimuli, in some low-dimensional psychological space (Nosofsky, 1985; Shepard, 1987, 1988; Takane 6 Shibayama, 1992). Given that the MDS choice model often provides reasonably good fits to identification confusion data (e.g., Nosofsky, 1991; Takane 6 Shibayama, 1992), these assumptions may be valid, but it is not clear whether the MDS choice model really requires these two assumptions. 

The second issue was raised from the empirical studies designed to examine the validity of the identification models. Experimental results revealed that the GRT identification model outperformed both the similarity choice model and the MDS choice model (e.g., Ashby 6 Lee, 1991; Maddox 6 Ashby, 1996), while the latter two models often showed reasonably good performances. Also, Ashby and co-workers found that the GRT identification model is not a special case of the similarity choice model (and the MDS choice model) but that the two models do intersect, and that the performance of the similarity choice model depends on the forms of symmetry found in confusion matrices (Ashby, Lee, 6 Balakrisknan, 1992; Ashby, Maddox, 6 Lee, 1994). These findings raise the issue of whether and how the GRT and MDS choice models differ from each other. 

We address the above two issues by constructing an identification model based on the maximum information entropy principle (MIP). MIP has been effectively used to analyze complex systems by means of macroscopically observed quantities (e.g., Haken, 1988), or to estimate a probability distribution based on the moments of that distribution (Shore 6 Johnson, 1980; Myung, 1994; Myung 6 Shepard, 1996). Of particular interest to the present article, Myung used MIP to provide a maximum entropy interpretation of the decision bound (i.e., GRT) models and of the context model of categorization (Myung, 1994). The specification of moments, however, is not always required in the MIP approach, as has been shown in the field of neural computation (e.g., Rose, Gurewitz 6 Fox, 1992; Buhmann 6 Ku�hnel, 1993). In the following sections, we propose a new MIP approach that enables us to construct an identification model without assuming that the subject estimates moments, and that illustrates how our model can address the current issues.

In Section II, we describe a complete identification paradigm where each stimulus corresponds to a unique response, and we derive an identification model based on the maximum information entropy principle, referred to as the MIP identification model. We also discuss the relationship between our approach and Myung's MIP approach. Section III shows that the MDS choice model and the optimal version of the GRT identification model are special cases of the MIP identification model. It is also clarified that the difference between these two models can be explained by a parameter in the MIP identification model referred to as ``computational temperature.'' In the final section, as an application of the MIP approach, the MIP model of similarity judgment is proposed and compared with Ashby's extended GRT similarity model (Ashby 6 Perrin, 1988).

4. DISCUSSION 

In this paper, we have shown that if we employ the maximum information entropy principle, then the MDS choice model can be derived without assuming the Shepard�Luce choice rule or a negative exponential similarity function, and it has a simple relation to the optimal GRT identification model. 

The GRT model is very general and gives a unified account of a variety of phenomena. So we may expect that our approach based on MIP will also provide a unified account of many of these same phenomena. For example, the task of identification is closely related to that of similarity judgments, and Ashby 6 Perrin

(1988) showed that the GRT model provides an interesting interpretation of similarity judgments. We here illustrate that the MIP model also can provide a similarity model, and we discuss how it relates to the GRT similarity model. 

First, we assume that perceived similarity is proportional to the probability of confusion, since it is often assumed that the greater the similarity between a pair of stimuli, the more likely one will be confused with the other.
