Multiple-Event Forced-Choice Tasks in the Theory of Signal Detectability* 

Brian K. Scurfield 

Victoria University of Wellington, Wellington, New Zealand 

Receiver operating characteristic (ROC) analysis is generalized to unidimensional forced-choice tasks involving three or more events. It is shown that the performance of an observer in a unidimensional identification task with n independent events can be represented in n ! ROC spaces of dimension n. Each ROC space is associated with a unique pairing of the events and decisions. A hypersurface can be generated in each ROC space by manipulating the observer's decision criteria. Using information theory, a new measure of discriminability based on the hypervolumes under the hypersurfaces is defined. This measure, denoted D, is nonparametric and independent of the criteria. The value of D is shown to increase monotonically with n and to be equal to the channel capacity of an observer in an n-interval forcedchoice task. Procedures to compute ROC hypersurfaces from ratings and from ROC curves are given. ] 1996 Academic Press, Inc. 

MULTIPLE-EVENT FORCED-CHOICE TASKS IN THE THEORY OF SIGNAL DETECTABILITY 

In the theory of signal detectability (Egan, 1975; Green 6 Swets, 1966; Peterson, Birdsall, 6 Fox, 1954; Tanner 6 Swets, 1954; Van Meter 6 Middleton, 1954), receiver operating characteristic (ROC) analysis is a tool for evaluating how well an observer can discriminate between two events. It is important because changes in discriminability can be separated from changes in the observer's decisional bias and from changes in the prior probabilities of the events. A major limitation, however, is that conventional ROC analysis gives an incomplete picture of how well an observer can discriminate between more than two events. This limitation arises because conventional ROC analysis deals only with performance tradeoffs that have two degrees of freedom. For two-event discrimination tasks, the performance tradeoff usually examined is that between the probability of correctly identifying the ``signal'' event when it

In discrimination tasks with more than two events, there are multiple performance tradeoffs, each with more than two degrees of freedom. To apply conventional ROC analysis in these situations, it is necessary to focus on how well the observer can discriminate between each pair of events. Although this approach can yield useful information, the proper analysis of performance in multiple-event tasks requires a generalization of ROC analysis. 

The purpose of this article is to present the generalization of ROC analysis to n-event absolute identification tasks where the evidence can be modelled by a unidimensional random variable. Unidimensional models are considered here for three reasons. First, they have long been an important area of research (e.g., Adams 6 Messick, 1958; Braida 6 Durlach, 1972; Durlach 6 Braida, 1969; Kijewski, Swensson, 6 Judy, 1989; Kornbrot, 1978; Luce 6 Green, 1972; Thurstone, 1927; Torgerson, 1954, 1958), with a history dating back to the seminal works of Fechner (1860�1966). Second, they are simple but constitute a nontrivial problem in n-event ROC analysis. Third, they provide insights into how to generalize ROC analysis to multidimensional n-event tasks. Indeed, unidimensional n-event ROC analysis can be considered to be a special case of multidimensional n-event ROC analysis. 

The lack of an ROC analysis for unidimensional n-event tasks has meant that there are no satisfactory discriminability measures for these tasks. For example, consider the following five discriminability measures: total d $, average d $, Euclidean d$, the proportion correct, and the information transmitted.[1] Although the last two measures are seemingly obvious measures of discriminability, each is dependent on the observer's decisional bias and on the prior probabilities of the events. Consequently, each can in principle vary between its minimum value and its maximum value for the same discriminability. The other three measures are variations of the well-known statistic d $.

Suppose that the probability functions of the evidence associated with a set of n independent events are equal variance normal distributions with means +1 , +2 , ..., +n . Then total d $ is the standardized distance between +min and +max , average d $ is the average standardized distance between each pair of means, and Euclidean d $ is the length of the vector (d $ij)i< j where d$ij is the standardized distance between +i and +j . Total d $ is an inadequate discriminability measure because it is independent of the values of the means that lie between +min and +max. These values should influence discriminability because discriminability is determined by the relationship of all the probability functions to each other. Although average d $ depends on the value of each mean, it also is an inadequate discriminability measure because it implies that the discriminability of the set of events is no greater than total d $; that is, it implies that overall discriminability is no greater than the discriminability of the two events associated with +min and +max. On the contrary, the discriminability of a set of independent events should be no less than the discriminability of any subset of them because the observer's ability to discriminate among the set of events is at least equal to his or her ability to discriminate among the events of the subset. Euclidean d $ is a more satisfactory discriminability measure than either total d$ or average d $ because it depends on the value of each mean and it is always greater than or equal to total d $. However, like total d $ and average d $, Euclidean d $ has the serious disadvantage of being parametric. 

Measures of discriminability based on d $ also have the problem of being infinite in tasks where only a subset of the events is perfectly discriminable. Consider a discrimination task involving three events, denoted e1 , e2 , and e3 . Suppose that e1 is perfectly discriminable from e2 (i.e., d $12=�) and that e1 is partly discriminable or indiscriminable from e3 (i.e., d$13<�). In this task, the values of average d $, total d $, and Euclidean d $ are infinite because the value of d $12 is infinite. It can be inferred therefore that all three events are perfectly discriminable. This cannot be true, however, because e1 was assumed to be partly discriminable or indiscriminable from e3. 

In general, the discriminability of any finite set of events should be finite, even if the events are perfectly discriminable. This observation follows directly from the requirement that the discriminability of a set of events should be no less than the discriminability of any subset of them. In the above task, the requirement implies that the discriminability of all three events must be infinite if the discriminability of e1 and e2 is infinite. To avoid this problem, the discriminability of the events e1 and e2 should be finite, even though e1 and e2 are perfectly discriminable. Only in a task with an infinite number of perfectly discriminable events should it be expected that discriminability is infinite. 

Of the existing measures of discriminability for two-event tasks, the area under the ROC curve is perhaps the most 

satisfactory (Egan, 1975; Richardson, 1972; Swets, 1986, 1988). This measure has a number of generalizations in n-event ROC analysis. With the assistance of information theory, the generalized area measures can be used as the basis for a new measure of discriminability that is free of the problems of the existing n-event measures of discriminability. 

DEFINITIONS AND ASSUMPTIONS 

Definition of Tasks 

In the single-interval n-alternative forced-choice task, an observer attempts to discriminate among a set of n events, denoted [ei : i=1, ..., n]. The task consists of a number of trials, each of which contains three well-defined time intervals: an observation interval, a decision interval, and a payoff interval. During the observation interval, one event from the set of n events occurs. During the decision interval, the observer decides which event occurred and selects one decision from a set of n decisions, denoted [ri : i=1, ..., n]. Note that the decisions are indexed so that, for each i, ri indicates that the observer decided ei occurred during the observation interval. During the payoff interval, the observer incurs any costs and receives any payoffs resulting from his or her decision. For brevity, the single-interval n-alternative forced-choice task is herein referred to as the I1 An task. In the abbreviation, the subscript to the I refers to the number of observation intervals, and the subscript to the A refers to the number of decision alternatives. The fundamental detection task of the theory of signal detectability (see Egan, 1975) is equivalent to the I1 A2 task. To simplify the mathematics, this paper focuses on the I1 A3 task. The full generalization of ROC analysis to the I1 An task is briefly presented near the end of the paper. 

Stimuli 

The theory of signal detectability assumes that the observer detects a stimulus during the observation interval and that each event is associated with a distribution of stimuli. For example, consider a tonal discrimination task involving three events: a low tone, a medium tone, and a high tone. In this task, the low tone might be associated with frequencies ranging continuously from 100 to 700 Hz, themedium tone might be associatedwithfrequenciesranging continuously from 500 to 1100 Hz, and the high tone might be associated with frequencies ranging continuously from 900 to 1500 Hz. On each trial of the task, the observer detects a frequency ranging from 100 to 1500 Hz and on the basis of this frequency decides which event occurred. Note that because of the overlap in the frequencies, perfect discrimination among the events is not possible. Indeed, the greater the overlap of the stimulus distributions, the greater the confusability of the events.

Unidimensional Models 

As mentioned in the first section, a unidimensional model of discrimination is assumed in this paper. The model is similar to many models of discrimination that have been described in the literature (e.g., Braida 6 Durlach, 1972; Durlach 6 Braida, 1969; see also Macmillan 6 Creelman, 1991, Chap. 9) and has its antecedents in works such as Adams 6 Messick (1958) and Torgerson (1954, 1958). In the model, the observer is assumed to represent the stimulus associated with the observation interval as a numerical value x, which is a particular value of a univariate random variable X. The value x constitutes the evidence on which the observer's decision is based. Because X is a random variable, each event is represented on X by a conditional random variable. For the I1 A3 task these conditional random variables are denoted X1 , X2 , and X3 . As is usual in the theory of signal detectability, the conditional random variables are assumed to be independent and stationary.

DISCRIMINABILITY 

It is tempting to define the volume under the 123-ROC surface as a measure of the overall discriminability of the events in the I1 A3 task. Indeed, the common use of the area under the traditional ROC curve as a measure of discriminability sets a precedence for the use of V123 as a measure of discriminability. Nevertheless, V123 is an inappropriate measure of discriminability because the volume under each of the six ROC surfaces provides information about the discriminability of the events. Moreover, the area under the ROC curves associated with the 123-ROC surface are inappropriate measures of pairwise discriminability. To understand this point and to further understand why V123 is an inappropriate measure of overall discriminability, consider what properties any discriminability measure should have. 

Measures of discriminability are measures of the extent to which the observer can differentiate between the events. It was indicated in the first section that any measure of discriminability should be (1) nonparametric; (2) independent of the observer's criteria; (3) independent of the prior probabilities of the events; (4) finite, even when the events are perfectly discriminable; and (5) such that the discriminability of a set of events is no less than the discriminability 

of any subset of them (in the context of the I1 A3 task, overall discriminability should be no less than pairwise discriminability). In addition, the measure should (6) have a true zero so that indiscriminable events are not registered on the scale of measurement. Also, (7) ratios of the measure should be measures of relative discriminability. This property enables the discriminability of the events in one task to be compared, quantitatively, to the discriminability of the events in another task. Next, the measure should be (8) invariant with respect to the scaling of X and (9) invariant with respect to the labels assigned to the events and decisions. Property 8 states, in essence, that a discriminability measure should be invariant with respect to any strictly monotonic transformation of X. This property ensures that the discriminability measure is independent of the scale used by the observer to measure the evidence. Property 9 is reasonable because it is the correspondence between events and decisions, and not their identity, that determines how well the observer can differentiate between the events. 

Neither V123 nor A:(12) has all nine properties listed above. First, neither is invariant with respect to the labels assigned to the events and decisions. For example, the area under the 12-ROC curve changes from A12 to 1&A12 when the event labels are swapped (e1 for e2 and vice versa) or the decision labels are swapped (r1 for r2 and vice versa). Second, neither measure has a true zero. As a result, ratios of each measure are not measures of relative discriminability. For example, consider two 123-ROC surfaces with volumes greater than or equal to one-sixth. The ratio of the two volumes is at least one-sixth and at most six because the two ROC surface can represent performance ranging from chance, when V123=1�6, to perfect, when V123=1. This observation leads to the strange conclusion that when V123 is used as a discriminability measure then the discriminability of three events in one task can be no less than one-sixth and no more than six times the discriminability of three events in another task. Finally, Eq. (17) implies that V123 is less than or equal to each of A12 , A13 , and A23 . Therefore, as measured by V123 , the discriminability of three events is less than or equal to the discriminability of any pair of them, in violation of property 5.

DISCUSSION 

Originally, information theory was developed to deal with the problem of reliably communicating messages over a noisy channel (Shannon, 1949; Wiener, 1948). The statistics of information theory can be easily applied to multiple-event discrimination tasks because discrimination is fundamentally a communication process (Attneave, 1959). The results of such applications can be equivocal, however, because many of the statistics of information theory, including the information transmitted, the equivocation, and the ambiguity, depend on the observer's criteria and on the prior probabilities of the events. However, the combination of information theory with multiple-event ROC analysis yields a new information 

264 

BRIAN K. SCURFIELD 

theory measure of discriminability that is independent of the criteria and of the prior probabilities. 

The new measure (herein denoted D) is superior to average d $, total d $, and Euclidean d $. First, and most importantly, D is nonparametric. This means that D can be applied independently of any assumptions about the probability functions associated with each event. Second, unlike average d $, D implies that the discriminability of a set of events is no less than the discriminability of any subset of them. Third, unlike total d $, D takes full account of the form and location of the to the probability functions associated with each event. Finally, unlike Euclidean d $, D is always finite, regardless of whether or not the events, or some subset of them, are perfectly discriminable. 

The last property has the added advantage of enabling the discriminability relative to the best possible discriminability to be evaluated using the measure D$=D�log (n !). This type of evaluation is impossible with d $-like measures of discriminability because such measures have infinite values if the events are perfectly discriminable. By contrast, D is infinite only for a task with an infinite number of perfectly discriminable events. 

An important result of this paper is that D is equal to the channel capacity of an observer in an n-interval forcedchoice task. This relationship is an information theory analog of the well-known relationship equating the area under the ROC curve to the proportion of correct decisions in a two-interval forced-choice task. It should be realized, however, that D is a reasonable measure of an observer's ability to discriminate whether or not it agrees, in practice, with the actual channel capacity of the same observer in the n-interval task. The justification for D as a discriminability measure depends on its satisfying the nine properties listed earlier in the section on discriminability. The fact that D equals a theoretical measure of channel capacity is a bonus, but not required in practice. 

The model of discrimination presented in this paper was based on a unidimensional evidence variable. As Kijewski et al. (1989) pointed out, this assumption may be inappropriate for many kinds of identification tasks. For example, consider an observer whose decisions are based on likelihood ratios. For an n-event task, the likelihood ratio observer must deal with n&1 joint likelihood ratios (e.g., ei vs en for i=1, ..., n&1). Thus, the observer's decision is based on an n&1 dimensional vector rather than a unidimensional evidence variable. For tasks where n>2 there is no guarantee that the vector can be collapsed onto an equivalent unidimensional decision variable without degrading performance. If the log-likelihood ratios are linearly related to each other, however, then it is possible to define a criterion-based decision rule on a unidimensional axis that yields the same performance as the likelihood-ratio observer (Scurfield, 1996). An example of a set of distributions for which the log-likelihood ratios are linearly related 

is equal variance normal distributions with unequal means (Scurfield, 1996). 

Although the model of discrimination presented in this paper rests on the assumption of unidimensionality, the concept of an ROC surface does not. To plot an ROC surface it is only necessary to plot the relationship between P(r1 | e:(1)), P(r2 | e:(2)), and P(r 3 | e:(3)) as the prior probabilities or the costs and payoffs vary. No assumptions have to be made about the evidence to do this. For this reason, multiple-event ROC analysis should be useful in categorization and identification models based on multidimensional evidence (see Ashby, 1992, for an edited survey of these models). The generalization of multiple-event ROC analysis to multidimensional multiple-event tasks is the subject of a future article (Scurfield, 1996). 

As a final point, D can be recommended as a general nonparametric statistic for summarizing the extent to which any set of unidimensional probability functions are ordered with respect to each other. There is no requirement that these probability functions bear any relationship to the model of discrimination presented in this paper or to the theory of signal detectability. 

SUMMARY 

This paper presented a generalization of ROC analysis to n-event forced-choice tasks where the evidence can be represented by a unidimensional random variable. It was assumed that the observer makes a decision by comparing the evidence to a set of criteria and that the values of the criteria depend on the prior probabilities of the events and on the costs and payoffs associated with the decision outcomes. By manipulating the decision criteria, the probabilities of each decision conditional on each event can be manipulated. The variation of these probabilities with respect to the criteria can be represented as n ! ROC hypersurfaces. Together, the ROC hypersurfaces account for all the ways that the decisions can be paired with the events. The hypervolumes under the ROC hypersurfaces sum to one and can be interpreted as a set of probabilities. Using this fact, a global measure of discriminability can be defined by subtracting the Shannon entropy of the hypervolumes from the maximum entropy. The resultant measure is nonparametric and independent of the observer's criteria and of the prior probabilities of the events. In addition, it has unit, a true zero, and the ratio of two values of the measure is a measure of relative discriminability. Furthermore, the measure is equal to the channel capacity of an unbiased observer in a multiple-interval forced-choice task. This equality is an analog of the well-known theorem equating the area under the ROC curve to the proportion of correct decisions in a two-interval forced-choice task. Local measures of discriminability also can be computed from the ROC hypersurfaces. These local measures measure
