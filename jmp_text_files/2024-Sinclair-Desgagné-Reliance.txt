Such widespread reliance on algorithmic advice seems at odds with the judgment and decision-making literature which demonstrates human distrust of algorithmic output, sometimes referred to as “algorithm aversion”. 
                      
                         Logg et al. (2019)

In 2014, the media reported that Deep Knowledge Venture (DKV), a Hong Kong venture capital company, had brought a machine named Vital (‘Validating Investment Tool for Advancing Life Sciences’) into its boardroom. Under current Hong Kong and most countries’ corporate law, it is not possible to officially appoint an algorithm to a company’s board ( Möslein, 2018 ). Yet, Vital’s ability to spot hidden underlying trends in complex historical data is said to have been key in the approval of major investment decisions. Several firms have since followed DKV’s example, some even deploying what is known as ‘algorithmic management’, i.e. granting self-learning algorithms the responsibility for making decisions affecting labour ( Duggan et al., 2020  p. 119). According to Accenture, a business consulting firm, 85% of the surveyed executives now want to increase their investment in AI-related technologies.

Meanwhile, in laboratory experiments or field studies representative of the many empirical investigations led over the past decade,  Gogoll and Uhl (2018)  observed that most subjects prefer to delegate a calculation task affecting a third party to a human rather than a machine,  Dietvorst et al. (2015)  and  Prahl and Van Swol (2017)  that trust in algorithmic assistance is more sensitive to receiving an erroneous advice than is trust in human aid,  Yeomans et al. (2019)  that people refrain from using recommender systems in certain domains (in this case, picking jokes that people find funny), even when these systems outperform human advisors, and  Liu et al. (2023)  that drivers tend not to rely on algorithms if their peers do not or if they have relevant expertise. Similar behaviors have been reported to repeatedly occur in several occupations (e.g., auditing, consulting, forecasting, retail business, stock trading) and across various other groups of people (e.g., consumers and investors, physicians, and managers).

Such is the ongoing relationship between humans and algorithms. 
                         1 
                      
                      
                         1 
                         English dictionaries define an algorithm as a sequence of instructions to be followed in doing a certain task. All through this paper, I shall use the word algorithm more broadly, as a synonym for artificial intelligence, robots or autonomous machines, and will employ these terms interchangeably. This agrees with current practice across the popular press and the social sciences (see  Mahmud et al., 2022 , p. 20–23). 
                       On the one hand, people increasingly rely on algorithms in their daily activities, from managing their home to organizing their leisure time and holidays to driving their car to doing their shopping. Algorithmic trading (which includes high-frequency trading) now accounts for over 70% of trade on U.S. stock markets ( Parkes & Wellman, 2015 ). In health care, physicians often make use of artificial intelligence to establish their diagnostics and cures ( Cadario et al., 2021 ), while robots are employed to help children with autism ( Huijnen et al., 2017 ). In management, top executives at certain corporations are today using systems capable of analyzing the work of all their business units almost instantly at the touch of a button. All this demonstrates significant and pervasive ‘algorithm appreciation’ ( Logg et al., 2019; Sharan & Romano, 2020; Thurman et al., 2018 ). Yet, since  Meehl (1954) ’s seminal investigation of clinical versus statistical predictions in the context of medical decision-making, a wealth of rigorous empirical evidence has been published showing the tendency of humans to shy away from using algorithms, or to discount algorithmic advice, even when algorithms could enhance human performance. This behavior is now referred to as ‘algorithm aversion’ ( Dietvorst et al., 2015 ).

The fact that people’s attitude towards incoming technology can be ambivalent is nothing new, of course; it has been observed since at least the industrial revolution. The Luddites rebellion in 19

                               h 
                            
                         
                      -century England reminds us that resistance to novelty can be quite costly. What is unseen and challenging with the current situation, however, is that people display aversion to algorithms not only when the latter are substitutes for human workers so their jobs are at stake, but also in areas where algorithmic features are expected to  support  or  complement  human skills ( Miller, 2018; Wilson & Daugherty, 2018 ) so their use would likely enhance someone’s living or task performance. Private companies and public organizations thus worry about their own employees, client organizations and consumers displaying aversion to algorithms, as it can significantly affect their bottom line.

                         Considering the auditing industry, for instance,  Commerford et al. (2022, p. 172)  report that “(...) auditor susceptibility to algorithm aversion could prove costly for the profession and financial statements users.”Another (somewhat more dramatic) example occurs in aviation, where many people frequently have to entrust their lives to autopilots ( Logg et al., 2019 ). 
                       In response, policy makers, business executives, consultants and academics are accordingly putting extra effort to understand the determinants and causes of algorithm aversion/appreciation.

At this point, researchers have uncovered a number of factors influencing the human-algorithm relationship. 
                            3 
                         
                         
                            3 
                            This paper’s list of references is far from exhaustive. Thoughtful reviews and discussions of the flourishing literature on the subject include  Berger et al. (2021), Burton et al. (2020), Filiz et al. (2021), Germann and Merkle (2019), Goodrich and Schultz (2007), Hou and Jung (2021), Jussupow et al. (2020), Leyer and Schneider (2019), Logg (2016), Mahmud et al. (2022), Prahl and Van Swol (2017) , and  Schwienbacher (2020) . 
                          Following  Goodhue (1995) ’s initial analysis of user evaluations of information systems, the evidence can be classified as follows.

(i)  Some factors have to do with some characteristics of the task under consideration ( Castelo et al., 2019 ). For instance, machines are not yet accepted as autonomous moral agents ( Bigman & Gray, 2018; Gogoll & Uhl, 2018; Jago, 2019 ). In their empirical survey,  Ray et al. (2008)  find that people would like robots to watch over their house, mow the lawn or do the laundry, but they would strongly resist having a machine babysit their children. Productions conceived as ‘subjective’ – like jokes and artworks – or requiring a fair amount of ‘intuition’ tend to be preferably assigned to humans ( Burton et al., 2020; Yeomans et al., 2019 ). In retailing,  Kawaguchi (2022)  has found that a worker might rely less on machines with high sales volume but more on algorithms with high sales volatility. Meanwhile,  Agrawal et al. (2018a, 2018b)  report that the pleasure of watching a sporting event depends on there being humans competing; it is not really interesting if a machine that runs faster than a human is allowed to compete. Professionals in certain fields might also fear that the use of decision aids could hurt their reputation ( Kaplan, 2000 ). Finally, it is considered to be up to a human being to exercise leadership ( Parry et al., 2016 ).

(ii)  Another important set of factors relates to individual features. For instance, people unfamiliar with machines would rather seek human aid than algorithmic support ( Kramer et al., 2017 ). Some people might also resist using a device which operates in a manner they see as overly opaque ( Cadario et al., 2021 ) . Having experienced an algorithm’s failure would decrease as well someone’s trust in automation ( Dietvorst et al., 2015; Prahl & Van Swol, 2017 ), but augmenting human involvement in algorithmic outcomes might then restore it ( Dietvorst et al., 2018 ). There is, moreover, converging evidence that humans’ need for control has a biological explanation ( Leotti et al., 2010 ); managers might thus be hesitant to use AI as they fear losing control over their work ( Leyer & Schneider, 2019 ). Confidence in one’s own expertise/ability, whether it is low or high, is to also be taken into consideration for discounting or not an algorithm’s help ( Allen & Choudhury, 2022 ).  Dietvorst and Bharti (2020)  submit that people’s ‘diminishing sensitivity to forecasting errors’ can also entail a preference for human over algorithmic advice. Ultimately, since humans experience different emotions when receiving advice from another human versus a nonhuman, this can affect their use of algorithms ( Prahl & Van Swol, 2017 ).

(iii)  Other factors, finally, concern the design or the scope of an algorithm. In their literature review,  Jussupow et al. (2020)  report that people treat ‘advisory’ algorithms (like recommender systems or prediction machines), which mainly provide information, differently from ‘performative’ algorithms, which are capable of executing complicated tasks independently. Also, people may exhibit different preferences for rule-based systems versus machine-learning systems and performative AI, as they consider the latter to be black boxes ( Mahmud et al., 2022 ). Last, it seems that trust in anthropomorphized automation would be stronger than trust in traditional machines ( Mahmud et al., 2022; Sharan & Romano, 2020 )).

Thanks to this research, human attitude towards algorithms is now well-documented and articulated, while further empirical evidence keeps accumulating. As  Duan et al. (2019, p. 68)  point out, though: Overall, to have a systematic understanding of how and why AI based systems are used and affecting individual and organizational performance, an appropriate theoretical framework should be developed. A similar appraisal is expressed in other recent literature surveys (e.g.,  Jannach & Jugovac, 2019; Jussupow et al., 2020; Mahmud et al., 2022 ). Time seems thus ripe for doing theoretical work on human–AI relationships, in order to bring together the various empirical findings, elucidate their congruences and fundamental differences, spell out causalities, help distinguish noise in data, and draw meaning from contradictory facts.

While the observed postures towards algorithms are oftentimes attributed to human irrationality, 
                            4 
                         
                         
                            4 
                            Through experiments,  Hou and Jung (2021)  find, for instance, that ‘framing’ (of task type, human vs algorithm competence, etc.) is a key factor influencing algorithm acceptance/rejection. 
                          I believe, nevertheless, that fruitful theorizing should first build on rationality as a useful benchmark. This would foster unity and simplicity of treatment, the orderly development of finer explanations, and a more precise identification of human failures, their respective causes, and their possible remedies. A perhaps inspiring analogy might be made h ere with what expected utility theory – thanks to the empirical challenges it prompted and the substantial amendments the latter lead to – has done for the understanding of decision making under risk and uncertainty. This paper’s aim is accordingly to develop a theoretical account of the empirical evidence regarding algorithm aversion/appreciation, while departing as little as possible (for the time being) from rational decision making.

The upcoming Sections  2 and 3  will thus bring respectively into play two basic notions from the field of Decision Analysis: the  value of information  and t he  value of control . Both these notions are precisely defined and seem relevant to launch a first formal analysis of human-algorithm interaction. The former refers to the value of getting to know (at least partially) in advance what a random variable will be ( Hilton, 1981; Wilson, 2015 ), the latter stands for the value of changing some random variables into decision variables ( Howard, 1971, 1988; Matheson & Matheson, 2005 ).  Howard (1971)  applies a suggestive terminology to the ‘value of information’ and the ‘value of control’: getting to know what value a random variable will take is called ‘clairvoyance’, while changing random variables into decision variables is named ‘wizardry’ . An example illustrating and comparing the two notions can be found in  Appendix .

The value of information surely matters when considering to rely on ‘advisory’ algorithms (using  Jussupow et al., 2020 ’s terminology), since such devices essentially provide information and predictions. To clarify what might t hen be happening, Section  2  builds a first model which highlights the relative value of the information supplied by an algorithm whose advice can be mistaken. The impact of a person’s ambiguity aversion, subjective cost of using an algorithm, and asymmetric sensitivity to false negatives and false positives are spelled out in this context. Some remedies to algorithm aversion put forward in the literature – like favoring people’s exposure to algorithms or conveying machine improvements – are next examined, and some testable predictions are submitted.

The value of control, on the other hand, should matter when considering to delegate some key decisions or the execution of important tasks to a specific algorithm. 
                            5 
                         
                         
                            5 
                            Using again  Jussupow et al., 2020 ’s terminology, I will call such an algorithm ‘performative.’. 
                          Most people would then seek to exercise some control over the possible range of outcomes. Whether a rational subject will accept to delegate decision making to an algorithm, or rely instead on her own human capabilities, should depend on the expected value these distinct approaches respectively deliver. This assessment, however, would build on human cognition. Section  3  will posit that it might consequently derive from an imperfect understanding of algorithmic output, when the algorithm is actually a ‘black box’. Thanks to a first model of this situation, this section will characterize and illustrate the circumstances where a rational decision maker will be (un)favorable to using th e algorithm. Ways to then avert ex post regret or disappointment in this context will be discussed.

These developments will point to several research avenues, notably on the empirical side. Some are sketched in the concluding Section  4 .

People frequently and increasingly receive algorithmic advice before making decisions. Recommender systems help consumers to search more cheaply and effectively for the goods and services they want. Companies invest in devices capable to handle ‘big data,’ so they can track customer satisfaction, hunt for the best recruits, forecast market trends, and make judicious investments. All in all, the academic literature (e.g.,  Burton et al., 2020  and the popular press often refer to the use of advisory algorithms as ‘augmented decision-making’. Yet, many circumstances remain where algorithmic advice is ignored. Consider, for instance, the following thought experiment:  
                         You are contemplating driving to a dear friend’s home this afternoon, after learning he is recovering from a serious injury. You have never been to this area before and have only a rough idea of how long it takes to get there. You must make your way back in time to catch the last daily flight to an important business meeting. Before deciding, you may seek an advice on routing and travel time from a geographic information system, or you may rely on your own judgment.

While most people would welcome algorithmic support in this case, some might nonetheless do without it.  Agrawal et al. (2018a, p. 89)  observe, for instance, that “Users of apps like Waze do not always follow the directions”; they ascribe this to the fact that a machine often fails in capturing someone’s preferences. In a large-scale field study,  Liu et al. (2023)  find that drivers’ past experience and reflection of their peers’ behavior can also lead them to ignore algorithmic information.

To theorize on what might be going on here, one may want to look at the recent research stream on ‘information avoidance’. 
                         6 
                      
                      
                         6 
                         The more general literature on advice-taking could also come to mind (for a nice survey, see  Bonaccio & Dalal, 2006 ). Its basic findings are indeed similar to the algorithm aversion/appreciation literature’s ones: rather frequently, decision makers tend to discount their advisors’ views. Like the algorithm aversion/appreciation literature, however, it mostly lacks a formal theory. Moreover, the fact that the considered advisors are  humans  raises incentive issues which are out of the scope of this paper; these are notably theorized in the principal–agent literature stream dealing with ‘delegated expertise’ (see, e.g.,  Deimen & Szalay, 2019 ) or ‘delegated learning’ ( Escobar & Zhang, 2021 ). 
                       This stream’s broad perimeter, defined and covered in  Andries and Haddad (2020), Golman et al. (2017) , and  Golman et al. (2021) , encloses indeed the algorithm aversion phenomenon as the latter is described in contributions to the subject: first, the individual is aware that information is available; second, the individual still avoids the information even if it is free ( Golman et al., 2017  p. 97). But algorithm aversion seems to relate to different causes than the ones put forward in the information avoidance literature, namely hedonic factors ( Burton et al., 2020 ) – i.e., the desire to avoid bad news because it will make one feel bad –, lack of curiosity ( Golman et al., 2021 ), and strategic considerations ( Burton et al., 2020 ). In this section, I will consider instead the net value a rational decision maker would ascribe to algorithmic advice, when she displays a subjective cost of using an algorithm, is asymmetrically sensitive to false negatives and false positives, or is subject to ambiguity aversion.

As the power, scope and accuracy of advisory algorithms is growing, so is the capability of similar increasingly sophisticated machines to execute nontrivial tasks and render decisions which only humans could previously make. Well-publicized areas where these so-called ‘performative’ algorithms (in  Jussupow et al., 2020 ’s terminology) have recently been deployed include medical treatment and health care, recruitment and hiring, bail and prison sentences, trading and investment, access to credit, and transportation. To be sure, the escalating encroachment of algorithms on domains that used to be the preserve of human beings raises major occupational, organizational, governance, legal, and ethical issues.

To get some control over the outcome, you may hurry to identify and invite several people who, you believe, should appreciate your talk and thereafter boost your reputation. Alternatively, you are aware that an algorithm could select all attendees for you. To do so, it will draw from a large population, taking into account every relevant data there is on each individual’s personality, profession, consumption, political opinion, social network, influence, etc.

This story can be seen as one of choosing to delegate some task to a qualified expert (a machine, in this case). However, the literature on delegation has so far dealt mostly with human agency; it emphasizes asymmetric information and opportunism.

                         See, notably,  Alonso and Matouschek (2008), Deimen and Szalay (2019), Ottaviani and SØrensen (2006) , and the references therein. 
                       It is unlikely that a hired algorithm would display the latter and so be subject to strategic communication, moral hazard, or career concerns. The vast literature on human–machine relationship rather calls attention to the fact that human beings and algorithms often display cognitive discrepancies and divergent rationality. In her classical contribution,  Bainbridge (1983, p. 776)  points out that  
                         (...) if the decisions can be fully specified then a computer can make them more quickly,  taking into account more dimensions and using more accurately specified criteria than a human operator can . (...) One can therefore only expect the operator to monitor the computer’s decisions  at some meta-level , to decide whether the computer’s decisions are ‘acceptable’. [emphasis added]

More recently, in the specific context of algorithm aversion,  Burton et al. (2020, p. 225)  reiterate the necessity to cope with these differences:  
                         Algorithmic decision making inherently requires the integration of  two decision processes: that of the human decision maker and that of the algorithmic aid . (...) For this reason,  cognitive compatibility – the recognition and alignment of human and algorithmic decision processes  – is crucial for successful augmented decision making .  [emphasis added]

Back to our thought experiment, the rational decision maker (DM) would have limited control over outcomes compared to the algorithm. At the same time, there is no way she could know what precisely underlies the algorithm’s list of attendees; for this, she would have to run through, and grasp, all the data the algorithm has been using. As suggested in the first quote above, DM will only comprehend the algorithm’s choices through her own coarse lenses. She might consequently discard the algorithm’s selection because, say, it does not include enough acquaintances. Or she might go with it, but she will then deliver her talk based on some personal feeling about the assembled audience, which might not perfectly agree with reality and could lead to unwelcome results. The model to be now developed aims to capture this situation.

The above citations and thought experiment highlight two key features of the relationship between humans and performative algorithms.

                               First, the decision maker must have less control over outcomes than the algorithm. Were it not the case, she would simply do without the machine.

The above cases 3 and 4 point at two main reasons why people might develop aversion to algorithmic control: they may end up being satisfied with the results they achieved without using the algorithm (Case 3), or relying on the algorithm’s decisions may not live up to expectations (Case 4). This suggests that further exposure to AI could be a double-edged sword: it might well generate algorithm appreciation, as the proponents of proper AI training and support argue (see, e.g.,  Cao et al., 2021 , p. 12), but it might fuel algorithm aversion instead if Cases 3 or 4 materialize.

The growing presence of AI in people’s daily life and work has generated considerable research efforts to understand how humans might interact with algorithms. This paper brought decision-theoretic lenses to the subject. The aims were to (i) provide some rationales for the numerous and oftentimes contradictory empirical findings, (ii) formally discuss some proposed remedies to algorithm aversion, (iii) develop testable new propositions, and (iv) deliver additional prescriptions. To meet these goals, I exploited two well-defined and standard notions from Decision Analysis: the value of information and the value of control. Human interaction with advisory algorithms calls for investigating the value of the information they convey. Delegation of decision-making to an algorithm, on the other hand, can be addressed using the value of control.

With respect to aim (i), Section  2 ’s equations  (3) and (5)  captured the well-documented role that trust, previous experience, habits, privacy concerns, or overconfidence in human ability can play in algorithm aversion/appreciation. They also accounted for the observed asymmetric treatment of humans versus machines, and the low tolerance people often display towards algorithmic failure. Section  3 , furthermore, modeled how the widely acknowledged cognitive discrepancies between humans and algorithms can lead the former to retain control over certain tasks. With respect to the second objective (ii), Sections  2.2.1 and 2.2.2  respectively examined whether technological improvements and greater exposure to AI would overcome algorithm aversion, while Section  3.3  briefly discussed the impact of algorithmic transparency. Considering the third goal (iii), Section  2.2.1  related algorithm aversion to techno-skepticism and people’s attitude towards ambiguity, Section  2.2.2  identified key conditions for user exposure to be successful in improving human–AI relationship, and Section  3  formalized and illustrated settings in which a user might not regret having avoided to rely on an algorithm or might be disappointed after having delegated decision-making to a machine. As for aim (iv), finally, the discussion following  Proposition 1  considered the role of incentives and training, the end of Section  2  conveyed some remarks about making a user’s exposure to an algorithm fruitful in dealing with algorithm aversion, and Section  3.3  touched upon algorithmic nudges as a means to deal with human–AI divergent rationality.

In their survey of the empirical literature on algorithm aversion/appreciation,  Jussupow et al. (2020)  “(...) highlight that current literature is inconclusive because researchers on algorithm aversion often involuntarily use different algorithm and human agent characteristics in their experimental investigations”. A key benefit of using formal models is to precisely make such characteristics and their joint impacts explicit, so the subsequent experiments would avoid the latter shortcoming. To be empirically relevant, however, theoretical characteristics should be measurable, and the current exercise brought out at least two human features that might raise some challenges in this respect. One is ambiguity aversion, which might be rather new to experimenters in the actual context. I submit that the empirical research on technological adoption might actually provide the needed measurements (see, e.g.,  Barham et al., 2014 , among many others). Another challenging human attribute is unawareness, which was introduced in Section  3 . Support to deal with it might come this time from the empirical research on delegation under contractual incompleteness. In a recent empirical study, for instance,  Antinyan et al. (2023)  consider a situation similar to the above one, in which a principal with limited control must decide to delegate decision-making to a better-skilled agent after receiving a possibly vague message from the latter. Before it is applied here, though, the latter framework would have to be tried on non-opportunistic agents.

The general topic of technology acceptance and avoidance is a long-standing one in Information Technology (IT); various theories have accordingly been developed to explain the data and address the challenges (see  Cao et al., 2021  for a brief overview). This paper seeked to contribute a complementary approach to the subject, based on rational decision-making and focused on the specific case of AI. The introduced formalism delivered a rigorous grasp at certain phenomena and policies. But many empirically-relevant factors influencing human–AI relationships – psychological (self-esteem, extraversion), demographic (age, gender), technological (the growing variety of algorithmic features and applications), organizational, societal and cultural – still need to be accounted for. In a recent paper, moreover,  Chevrier et al. (2024)  report experimental evidence of algorithm ‘credulity’, rather than algorithm aversion/appreciation. All this certainly leaves room for further theoretical investigations, notably of relative human vs AI performance ( Peukert et al., 2023 ), organizational learning ( Balasubramanian et al., 2022 ), and especially context effects ( Davis-Stober et al., 2023 ). Building on the current approach, some of them might bring in the respective modeling perspectives and toolbox of behavioral economics and neuroeconomics. At the moment, time seems indeed ripe for additional theoretical work, given the wealth of existing empirical studies. One should strike the right balance, though; to be optimally fruitful, theoretical and empirical efforts should support each other. The end of Sections  2.1 and 2.2 , for instance, illustrates how theory might inform empirical inquiries. The black box issue, on the other hand, considering the developments initiated in Section  3 , appears to be a matter where progress in theory and modeling must rely on further empirical research.
