McGill University  Tversky (1972, Journal of Mathematical Psychology, 9, 341-367) showed that his elimination-by-aspects model is a random  utility model. He constructed the random utility representation by recursively defining a set of ranking probabilities on  various sets. In this paper, we demonstrate the converse of this result, namely, that if a random utility model has ranking  probabilities that satisfy Tversky's recursive formula, then the associated choice probabilities satisfy Tversky's  elimination-by-aspects model. We also illustrate structures of preference and aversion probabilities, each of which satisfy  the elimination-by-aspects model and which are compatible with a common random utility model. INTRODUCTION  Random utility models hold an important position in theoretical and experimental work in the areas of preference, ranking, and  choice (Colonius, 1984, is an excellent summary of much of the theoretical work). It is also often the case that a choice  model can be shown to be a random utility model, even when its initial motivation is rather different. Such was the case with  Luce's choice model (Luce, 1959), which was shown to be a random utility model by Block and Marschak (1960, p. 110), with an  explicit distributional representation being given by Holman and Marley (see Luce and Suppes, 1965, p. 338). Tversky (1972a)  introduced the elimination-byaspects (EBA) model, with Tversky (1972b) giving details of its theoretical motivation and of its  representation as a random utility model. Corbin and Marley (1974) showed that the transition probabilities of the EBA model  also satisfy a random utility model, with Marley (1981a) giving an explicit distributional representation of those  probabilities. Tversky constructed the random utility representation of the EBA model by recursively defining a set of ranking  probabilities on various sets (the recursive formulae involving the transition probabilities of the EBA model), and then  showing that these ranking probabilities satisfied the requirements of a random utility representation for the choice  probabilities of the relevant EBA model. The present paper has discussed random utility models with binary tree decomposable rank orders. The basic idea has been that,  at any stage, a person splits the current set into "acceptable" and "unacceptable" subsets, and proceeds to apply similar  decision procedures recursively to each subset until reaching singleton sets. We can generalize this idea-for instance, we can  assume that at each stage the person splits the current set into "acceptable," "indifferent," and "unacceptable" subsets, and  proceeds recursively on these subsets. Corbin and Marley (1974) showed that one can develop a hierarchy of such rules, such  that the complete hierarchy contains all random utility models.
