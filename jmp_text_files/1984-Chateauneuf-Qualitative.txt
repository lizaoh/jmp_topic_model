INTRODUCTION  Given a nontrivial weak order on an algebra of events which is compatible with the inclusion order, is monotonicity sufficient  to ensure the existence of an agreeing probability measure? This question was first asked by de Finetti (1937). Savage (1954)  answered the question affirmatively in the nonatomic case, and later Kraft et al. (1959) proved that in the general case the  answer was negative by constructing a counterexample on a finite algebra of events. Moreover, they proved that, in the finite  case, additivity (condition (C)) was in fact both necessary and sufficient; another proof is given by Scott (1964).  In the infinite case, condition (C) is not sufficient (see, e.g., Example 1 in Sect. 2.4). As shown by Narens (1974), (C) only  implies a weaker property-the existence of an almost agreeing probability measure. Actually (C) is not a necessary condition  for this last property. Necessary and sufficient conditions for it can be found in Wakker (1981), as Theorem 4, and related  results are given by Cohen (1980).  As far as the existence of an agreeing probability is concerned, several sets of sufficient conditions are known. They either  imply that there are no atoms as in Narens (1974) or that there are only atoms as in Luce (1967); necessary and sufficient  conditions are also known (Domotor, 1969), but are of an abstract nature. The aim of this paper is to demonstrate: first, that  an Archimedean condition which, like condition (C), can be expressed explicitly (and actually contains (C)) is 191  0022-2496/84 $3.00  Copyright C 1984 by Academic Press, Inc. For this second part, we have made extensive use of the results of Villegas (1964), who has shown that condition (M) greatly  facilitates the solution of the nonatomic case when, contrary to Savage (1954), a-additivity of the agreeing probability  measure is required (which makes (M) a necessary condition).
