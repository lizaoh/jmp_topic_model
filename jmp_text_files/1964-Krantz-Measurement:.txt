KRANTZ  University of Pennsylvania, Philadelphia, Pennsylvania  Axioms are given for an equivalence relation defined in a product set, such that a commutative group structure can be  introduced in the set of equivalence classes. Different group structures in the same product set, obtained by this  construction, are all naturally isomorphic. If the group is Archimedean ordered, an isomorphism into the ordered additive  reals leads to interval scale measurement.  Similar theorems result from axioms stated symmetrically for several interlocking equivalence relations on a single set. Group  structures are constructed on each set of equivalence classes, and a "global" group structure is obtained on the whole set,  modulo the intersection of the equivalence relations.  INTRODUCTION  The first measurement theories leading to ratio- or interval-scale measurement (Campbell, 1920; Holder, 1901) were based on an  empirical relational system that involves, in addition to an ordering of the objects, a "concatenation" operation of some  kind, for example, in measurement of weight, the juxtaposition of two objects in the same pan of a balance. Later, it was  shown that interval measurement can be obtained from an empirical relational system that involves only the ordering of  objects, provided that the structure of the object set is sufficiently enriched (Suppes and Winet, 1955). Luce and Tukey  (1963) have provided a very general scheme for interval measurement based on ordering, provided that the contributions of two  or more distinct factors are considered simultaneously. They call this conjoint measurement.  The basic idea of Luce and Tukey is easily sketched. Suppose one has an empirically defined weak ordering, <, over a set of  objects. Suppose that two factors contribute to the position of an object in the ordering. (Other relevant factors may be  considered as constant.) Section I of this paper presents the axiom system and the results of Luce and Tukey. Since they give an extensive discussion  of the meaning and plausibility of their axioms, such a discussion is not needed here. On the other hand, the proofs given  below differ markedly from theirs and have the interesting feature of separating the results based on the equivalence  relation, , defined from the weak ordering, from the results which properly involve the concept of ordering. Merely by  assuming an equivalence relation, together with the Luce-Tukey axioms specialized for it, one can introduce by definition a  "concatenation" operation in the object set. The resulting structure is shown to be a commutative group. The order relation is  then introduced, and the measurement theorems follow from standard theorems on ordered groups.  In Section II, the factors and the principal quantity being measured are treated symmetrically; one merely assumes that three  equivalence relations, on a given set, satisfy axioms that interrelate them in a symmetric way. One then obtains three  isomorphic commutative groups, and also an "over-all" group structure which is isomorphic to the direct sum of any two  "coordinate" groups. Introduction of order relations gives the same measurement theorems as in Section I, as well as an  additional generalization.  250 KRANTZ  Finally, Section III presents a generalization to n dimensions (n + I equivalence relations) of the development in Section II. Note added in proof. The resemblance of the Luce-Tukey theorem to the results of Debreu (1959) was pointed out by Luce and  Tukey (1964). Debreu's results are based on a theorem of Blaschke (Blaschke and Bol, 1938) concerning homeomorphisms of the  plane which map three systems of nonoverlapping curves into three systems of parallel straight lines. Recently, it was called  to my attention by J. Aczel (personal communication to R. D. Luce) that Blaschke's results have been reformulated in algebraic  terms by Aczel, Belousov, and Hossz(i (1960) and Aczel, Pickert, and Rado (1960); their theorem shows that if 6 binary  quasi-group operations on a set satisfy a bisymmetry condition interrelating them, they are all generated from a common group  operation defined in the set. Theorem 2.2 in the present paper is really a special case of their result, albeit arrived at  independently.
