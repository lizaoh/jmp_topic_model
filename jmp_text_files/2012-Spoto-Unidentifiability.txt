Our aim is to study two cases that, in our view, are important because of some properties that make them relevant and likely to occur in empirical applications. Among these properties, there are a certain kind of gradation and a clear interpretation in terms of skills. The unidentifiability is a consequence of linear dependencies between the error parameters of the model and the probabilities of the knowledge states. Among the possible ways to solve such identifiability issues, we focus on the one that consists of setting to zero certain error parameters. The implications of this approach are discussed. Before getting into the details of these knowledge structures, because of their close relationship with skill multi maps, some background concerning the skill-based approach in KST is needed.

The concept of a  skill  has been introduced in KST to allow the theory to go beyond a purely behavioral interpretation. The concept of a skill introduces a detailed cognitive interpretation of the solution process of items. The authors of the theory, referring to previous research ( Albert, Schrepp, & Held, 1994; Falmagne et al., 1990; Lukas & Albert, 1993; Marshall, 1981 ), identified the cognitive concept of skills as the description of the set of methods, strategies, procedures, etc., that a subject could follow to solve a specific set of items. In other words, it is supposed that for each item  
                         q

In this paper, after presenting an extension of the BLIM to skill multi maps, we show that in certain cases, these modifications yield BLIM parameterizations that are not identifiable. We also demonstrate the correspondence between these particular knowledge structures and a certain kind of skill multi maps. The second part of the paper presents an application of the introduced theorems and of their consequences on a skill multi map. We worked on clinical data derived from a questionnaire for Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD), as described by the DSM-IV-TR ( American Psychiatric Association APA, 2000 ): the Italian reduced form of the Maudsley Obsessional-Compulsive Questionnaire (MOCQ-R;  Hodgson & Rachman, 1977; Sanavio & Vidotto, 1985 ), included in the Cognitive Behavioural Assessment 2.0 (CBA 2.0;  Bertolotti, Zotti, Michielin, Vidotto, & Sanavio, 1990 ).

In this section, we aim to describe, through a practical example, the consequences of the theoretical results introduced in the previous sections. More specifically, we show that, although theoretically/qualitatively sound, a probabilistic knowledge structure model may be not statistically/quantitatively tractable. We propose a possible solution to this problem and explore its positive and negative aspects.

We refer to the construction of a knowledge structure, starting from a skill multi map representing the presence of diagnostic criteria (attributes) for the OCD, as defined by the DSM-IV-TR, in the set of items composing the sub-scale Cleaning of the MOCQ-R. The sub-scale includes eight items investigating the specification of the OCD related to the fear of contamination and the compulsive activity of cleaning. The application context is similar to those in  Spoto et al. (2010)  and  Spoto, Vidotto, Postal, and Pendoni (2008) . The empirical example is presented to illustrate the technical results only; no substantive predictions are made about obsessive-compulsive disorder.

In our application, we build a skill multi map associating to each item  
                         i 
                       a set of attributes. We then construct a matrix where each row represents a competency for an item and each column represents an attribute. We analytically assign to each item a subset of attributes (i.e., the clinical characteristics investigated by that item). Each single competency represents a minimally sufficient collection of attributes to positively answer to that specific item.

The special case of the BLIM presented in Section  2  is used as the reference model. An iterative MATLAB procedure ( Stefanutti & Robusto, 2009 ) applies the Expectation Maximization algorithm ( Dempster, Laird, & Rubin, 1977 ) for the estimation of the model parameters by maximum likelihood. Furthermore, the Chi-Square is computed as a goodness-of-fit statistic and is presented here along with error rate estimates and the estimated probability of each single attribute. Given the sparseness of the data matrix, we obtain an estimate of the Chi-Square p-value through parametric bootstrap.

This application allows several interesting methodological considerations for the model, the items, and the attributes. In fact, on the one hand, the general fit indexes could provide us with an overview of the quantitative properties of the hypothesized model; on the other hand, both the error parameters (i.e.,  
                         
                            
                               α 
                            
                            
                               q 
                            
                         
                       and  
                         
                            
                               β 
                            
                            
                               q 
                            
                         
                      ) and the information about each single attribute represent a unique tool to evaluate the validity and reliability of the items and of the structure. Moreover, the estimated probability of every single skill may be informative about the opportuneness of keeping it in the model. Given that the estimate of every single attribute probability lies in the interval  
                         
                            [ 
                            0 
                            , 
                            1 
                            ] 
                         
                      , a specific symptom with an estimated probability of, say, 0.99 (almost every subjects has this symptom) has no practical use; therefore, the attribute could be removed from the model. On the other hand, a small skill probability would be indicative of the presence of a quite rare symptom.

It is possible to see how the so-built structure is both forward- and backward-graded in some items. In fact, on the one hand, specific attributes are assigned to items 1, 2, 4, 6, and 7 (backward-graded), while on the other hand, a competency including only a specific attribute is defined for item 4 (forward-graded). From what was introduced and demonstrated in the previous sections, this kind of model happens to be not identifiable. The negative implication of this kind of solution is that the opportunity to estimate the actual “true” value of these parameters is no longer given. The skill multi map approach to clinical items allows for considerations on single skills’ probabilities. Given the aims of this paper, we limit our observations to the fact that they range from 0.32 to 0.89. This means that some clinical characteristics can be easily found in the general population, but that standing alone, they are not an indication of a psychological problem. On the other hand, some clinical elements seem to be less likely to occur in the normal population and thus could represent stronger evidence of potential problems.

We can now summarize the main results presented in this article and are applicatively displayed through the example.

In the paper, we provided a way to construct counterexamples underscoring unidentifiability of the BLIM and its link to the skills delineating the knowledge states. The theoretical results carried out in this paper shed light on potential limitations and suggest some recommendations in the application of the BLIM.
