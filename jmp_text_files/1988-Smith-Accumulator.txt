JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 32, 135-168 (1988)  The Accumulator Model of Two-Choice Discrimination  PHILIP L. SMITH AND DOUGLAS VICKERS  University of Adelaide  The accumulator model of two-choice discrimination conceives the decision process as a race between competing evidence totals  in discrete time and continuous state space. General expressions for the terminating probabilities for the model are derived,  and a tractable version considered in which the increment distributions are exponential. Expressions for response  probabilities, first passage time distributions, and a "balance of evidence" theory of response confidence are presented, and  the resulting model compared to that obtained under the more usual assumption of truncated normal increments. The exponential  model is Fitted to data from an experiment exhibiting both faster and slower mean error times across a range of  discriminability levels, and is shown to satisfactorily account for these when augmented with the assumption of negatively  correlated within-condition variation in discriminability and decision criterion. Cross-paradigmatic support for the fitted  model is provided by an estimate of sampling time of the order of 100 msec, in agreement with estimates obtained from a  technique employing brief stimulus exposures. 0 1988 Academic Press, Inc.  INTRODUCTION  During the last 25 years of psychological research, attempts by a number of investigators to develop a quantitative model of  two-choice discrimination have led them to propose some form of sequential sampling model, in which successive states of a  sensory process are summed to an evidence threshold. The attraction of sequential sampling models is that they provide a  description of the relationship between sampling time and performance accuracy, and hence are natural candidates for modeling  speed-accuracy tradeoff effects. The purpose of this paper is to provide a detailed investigation of one such model, the  so-called accumulator model of Vickers (1970). As part of this investigation, some consideration will be given to the  relationship between this and alternative formulations, and their various strengths and weaknesses, although no attempt will  be made to discriminate between alternatives in any systematic or exhaustive way. In the most general sense, any sequential sampling model may be thought of as consisting of three components. The first  comprises some form of sensory coding stage, which intervenes between the sensory receptors and the subsequent decision stage.  The output of this stage is represented as a stochastic process, whose statistical properties are a function of the stimulus  presented. From a theoretical viewpoint, the purpose of this stage is to provide a mathematically tractable representation of  the stimulus, reducing its qualitative features to analyzable quantitative ones. Typically, its characteristics are  incorporated into the assumptive structure of the model, and hence are not subject to direct empirical test.  The second and third components of such models are, respectively, a mechanism for evidence accumulation, and a stopping  criterion. Together, they represent the decision stage of the model. The former determines how successive samples of sensory  input are accumulated, and the latter, the point at which accumulation ceases and a response is emitted. Much of the debate in  the literature has focused on the form of the decision stage, where two main classes of model have received extensive  consideration. These are, respectively, random walk and counter models. In the remainder of this section, a general  introduction to these two classes of models is presented, prior to a detailed discussion of the accumulator model.  Random Walk Models  The term "random walk" is used to describe the stochastic properties of a random sum, which receives independent increments at  successive points in time. The magnitudes of the increments are described by a sequence of random variables, which assume both  positive and negative values, in accordance with some probability distribution. The sum continues to receive increments until  it attains a critical positive or negative value, at which point the process terminates.  Geometrically, the random walk may be characterized by the movement of a particle in two-dimensional Cartesian (t, y)  coordinates, where the horizontal (t) axis represents time, and the vertical (y) axis, known as the state space of the  process, represents the displacement of the particle from its starting position. Parallel to the t-axis are two "absorbing  barriers," located at y = KA and y = - KB, which specify the maximum positive and negative displacements required for the  process to terminate. In the simple random walk (e.g., Cox & Miller, 1965, Ch. 2), the process develops in discrete time (t =  0, 1, 2, ...) and discrete state space (y = - KB, ..., -1, 0, 1, ..., K,). Starting at the origin, the vertical displacement  of the particle is incremented or decremented by one unit at each step until it reaches (is "absorbed at") either of the two  barriers. The time taken for this to occur is termed the first passage time.  Applications of random walk theory to two-choice discrimination have tended to prefer a more general representation of the  process than the one just described, in which the assumption of discrete time sampling is retained, but the state-space  assumptions are relaxed, permitting the increments to be continuously distributed.  ACCUMULATOR MODEL 137  The theory of this process, for arbitrary increment distributions, was provided by Wald (1947) and forms the basis for the  classical Sequential Probability Ratio Test (SPRT) of mathematical statistics. The random walk is statistically optimal in the  case where the information accumulated is the log-likelihood ratio of the stimulus alternatives (Wald & Wolfowitz, 1948), and  it was in this form that the process was originally suggested as a model for two-choice discrimination by Stone (1960) and  developed in detail by Laming (1968).  The principal difficulty with the original SPRT formulation was that, when conditioned on the process terminating at a  particular barrier, it predicted that first passage times for correct and error responses would be equal. That is, if T;;  denotes the mean time to make response j to stimulus i, the SPRT model predicts reaction times of the form TAA = TB,9 and TBB  = TAB, a prediction which receives little support from the literature. Laming went on to show that with the additional  assumption of variability in the starting position of the walk, attributable to the subject sampling from the preexposure  field prior to stimulus onset, the SPRT model could predict the orderings TBA < TAA and TAB < TBB; that is, it predicts faster  mean times for errors. This is a frequently reported finding when stimulus discriminability is high and speed of responding is  emphasized, but when discriminability is low and the emphasis is on accuracy, the reverse relation often obtains (see Laming,  1979a; Luce, 1986, Ch. 6, for further discussion).  Subsequently, Link and Heath (1975) proposed an alternative random walk model, called Relative Judgment Theory (RJT), which  attempted to overcome the SPRT formulation's shortcomings. Link and Heath showed that the predicted equality of correct and  error response times is in fact critically dependent on the symmetry properties of the moment generating function (mgf) of the  distribution of increments to the walk. In RJT, a parameter determining the symmetry properties of this mgf is permitted to  vary, and in this way, the model can predict either faster errors or correct responses. That is, depending on the value of  this parameter, either of the orderings TAA < TBA and TBB < TAB, or TBA < TAA and TAB < TBB, is possible. Detailed discussions  of the relationship between the SPRT and RJT formulations, and of the conditions under which their predictions are equivalent,  may be found in Swensson and Green (1977), Townsend and Ashby (1983), and Luce (1986).  Link (1975, 1978) derived tests of the RJT model which depend on a demonstration of the linearity of particular functions  relating conditional mean response times and response probabilities. To a good approximation, the predicted linearity is  exhibited in a variety of empirical settings, and this, coupled with the model's flexibility in predicting reaction time  orderings, makes the RJT account an attractive one. At the same time, however, the model in its present state of development  provides no explicit rationale for the manner in which mgf asymmetry may vary across speed and accuracy emphasis conditions  within a particular experiment. Nor does it propose a family of distributions whose members exhibit the requisite mgf  variation under parametric transformation. Thus while this model is able to mathematically predict the variation in reaction  time orderings across changing  480/32,2-4  138 SMITH AND VICKERS  experimental conditions, it has not yet attempted to theoretically specify all of the psychological processes involved.  Another approach to generalizing the random walk model has been proposed by Ashby (1983), who developed a version of the SPRT  model which incorporates sensory bias into the likelihood ratio computation. Ashby reports that the resulting model possesses  considerable flexibility in its predictions of reaction time orderings, and, when fitted to data from the response time  deadline experiment of Green and Luce (1973), performed slightly better than its SPRT and RJT counterparts. However, as Luce  (1986, p. 351) points out, the numerical estimates of the sensory bias parameter obtained by Ashby were such as to suggest  that this form of bias actually played little or no part in determining the reaction time orderings in the data set.  An alternative application of random walk theory in psychological modeling is due to Ratcliff (1978). Rather than using the  discrete time, continuous state space formulation considered so far, Ratcliff developed a model employing the diffusion  process, which is a process evolving continuously in time, obtained as a limiting form of the simple random walk (see Cox &  Miller, 1965, Ch. 5). Ratcliff incorporated the diffusion process into a general theory of memory retrieval, in which the  degree of "resonance" between a probe and target item determines the degree of drift of the walk. This results in a flexible  model which has been successfully fitted to a variety of item-recognition and speed-accuracy paradigms (Ratcliff, 1978) and to  perceptual matching data (Ratcliff, 1981, 1985). While Ratcliff has tended to focus on somewhat different aspects of the data  to those considered here, the importance of his work in the present context is that it demonstrates the ability of sequential  sampling models to provide a unified account of a wider variety of psychological phenomena than has hitherto been envisaged.  Further discussion of this model may be found in Luce (1986, Ch. 11 ).  Counter Models'  The main alternative to random walk models in the literature is the class of counter models. Unlike random walks, in which the  evidence is accumulated as a single signed total, counter models assume that, prior to accumulation, some form of  classification scheme is imposed on the sensory input, whereby observations are tagged as favoring one or other response  alternative. Evidence for the two alternatives is accumulated in parallel, until one of the evidence totals reaches a  criterion value, and the associated response is emitted.  A degree of imprecision exists in this terminology, to the extent that the accumulator model is not formally a counting  process. However, since all the remaining members of this class of models are counting processes, this is a convenient generic  designation and should cause no confusion. An alternative would be to follow Luce (1986) and reserve the term "accumulator"  for the class, and designate the unit-increment model and the present model as the "simple accumulator" and the "strength  accumulator," respectively. We have not adopted this terminology because, on the one hand, it is somewhat more cumbersome, and  on the other, the term "accumulator" without qualification now has fairly widespread currency in the literature as the  designation for the present model. A more graphic designation for the class would be "parallel integrators."  ACCUMULATOR MODEL 139  Counter models may be characterized in the same manner as random walks, according to their state space and sampling time  assumptions. In the simplest of such models, the two counters receive unit increments in discrete time. This model has been  considered in detail by Audley and Pike (1965) and La Berge (1962). One of its disadvantages is that, as the number of  observations required for the process to terminate increases, so the sampling time distributions rapidly approach normality,  contrary to the positive skewness typical of empirical reaction time distributions (e.g., Ratcliff, 1978, 1979). Pike (1966,  1968, 1973) also considered a version of this model in which the intervals between increments are distributed exponentially,  rather than being fixed.  A model of this latter kind has been discussed recently by Townsend and Ashby (1983) in which the sensory input is represented  as the superposition of two Poisson processes, whose relative intensities represent the likelihood of one or other stimulus  being present. The numbers of arriving pulses favoring the two alternatives are summed in parallel until a critical count is  reached, in the manner described above. Townsend and Ashby show that with the natural requirement that the smaller Poisson  intensity occurs on the counter associated with the incorrect response, models of this class predict orderings of the form TAA  < TBA and TBB < T,qa; that is, for either response alternative, correct responses are always faster.  These predictions, while in accord with the low discriminability, accuracy-stress results outlined earlier, are inconsistent  with the results typically obtained when speed is stressed and discriminability is high. Hence if counter models are to  provide a viable general account of two-choice performance, they must be modified or extended in a manner consistent with  these findings. One approach is to assert that the errors in these conditions represent "fast guesses" (e.g., Ollman, 1966;  Yellott, 1967, 1971); that is, trials on which a response is initiated before the accumulation process is complete. However,  Pike (1973) has proposed a more satisfactory explanation, somewhat similar to Laming's suggested modification to the SPRT  random walk. Pike pointed out that in circumstances where there is trialby-trial variation in the decision criteria, a  reversal in the predicted ordering of correct and error times for counter models may occur. This is due to variation in the  proportion of errors produced at different criterion values, with the natural tendency for errors to be slower being offset by  the larger proportion of errors concentrated at lower criterion values. The literature on sequential effects provides ample  evidence for the existence of such trial-by-trial variation in model parameters (e.g., Luce, 1986, pp. 253-271), and under  these circumstances, the predictions of counter models are rendered qualitatively consistent with the empirical data. However,  to date, no counter model employing these assumptions has been formally fitted to a set of data. Also, as Townsend and Ashby  (1983) note, a further difficulty with this explanation is that there is no obvious reason why variation in decision criteria  should occur when discriminability is high but not when it is low. This issue will be returned to in a later section.  An alternative generalization on the class of counter models is that embodied in  140 SMITH AND VICKERS  the accumulator model of Vickers (1970). Like the random walk models of Laming (1968), Link and Heath (1975), and Ashby  (1983), the accumulator model retains the assumption of discrete time sampling, but permits the increments to the counters to  be continuously distributed. Specifically, the model assumes, in the manner of classical psychophysics, that any fixed sensory  magnitude is superimposed upon a randomly and momentarily varying background of neural activity, resulting in a distribution  of sensory effect which is normal in form (Thurstone, 1927). Samples drawn from this distribution are compared to a sensory  referent and the deviation between them computed. Deviations falling above the referent are added to one accumulator; those  falling below it are added to the other, with sampling continuing until one or other accumulator exceeds its associated  criterion value. The detailed predictions of this model will be considered in the following sections. The present section describes an alternative, more tractable version of the accumulator model, in which the increments to the  counters are exponentially distributed. The principal reasons for considering such a model are twofold. In the first instance,  the intuition from the Central Limit Theorem is that, when the mean of the distribution of step increments is small in  relation to the evidence criterion, the predictions of the two versions of the model should be quite similar. Under these  circumstances, even if the underlying distribution of sensory effect is thought to be normal, the exponential version may be  preferred as a convenient approximation because of its computational advantages. These, as will be shown subsequently, are  such as to permit investigation of certain problems for which the computational costs of the normal model are at present  prohibitively high. Second, such a model is intrinsically of interest, since when evaluating models like this, which rely on a  relatively complex set of assumptions, exponential forms may be used as convenient benchmarks against which to undertake  exploratory data analysis, and provide important qualitative insights, even when there is no immediate physical interpretation  for the assumption of exponential increments. Simulation Studies  The question arises as to whether, and to what extent, the exponential model can mimic the observed statistics of the normal  process when its parameters are estimated from empirical data. Obviously, this question may be examined in a variety of ways,  depending on which aspects of the model's behavior are selected for inclusion in the analysis. Here we considered two  possibilities. The first employed the technique of Smith and Vickers (1988), who showed that for single discriminability level  data from an expanded judgment task, two variants of the accumulator model could be successfully discriminated by fitting them  to the marginal sampling time distributions for individual subjects, and then using the resulting parameter estimates to  predict both the probability of a correct response and the difference between the mean sampling times for correct responses  and errors across the data set as a whole. The second comparison used the procedure more typically associated with the  analysis of two-choice reaction time data, in which the model was fitted to the conditional mean sampling times and response  probabilities across a number of different discriminability levels simultaneously.  Marginal Sampling Time Distributions  By way of a preliminary investigation, 500 simulated trials were generated for the fully symmetrical accumulator model (i.e.,  symmetrical with respect to both  ACCUMULATOR MODEL 153  stimulus parameters and response criteria), for each of the nine parameter combinations shown in Fig. 1. The marginal sampling  time distributions of the mimicking exponential model were fitted individually to each of the simulated frequency  distributions by minimizing the Pearson chi-square statistic, using the SIMPLEX algorithm of Nelder and Mead (1965 ).6 With a  two parameter fit (decision criterion and residual time), all nine distributions were fitted satisfactorily at the 0.10 level  or better, suggesting that, across a reasonably large range of parameters, the exponential model is capable of mimicking the  sampling time distributions produced by its normal counterpart. At the same time, however, the obtained fits showed a tendency  to underestimate criterion values (between 6 and 50%), to overestimate residual times (between 3 and 44% of the marginal mean  sampling time), and to underpredict the probability of a correct response (between 1 and 10%). EMPIRICAL TEST OF THE MODELS  The following experiment, conducted by Vickers and Willson (see Vickers, 1979, pp. 75-76), was designed as a test of the  accumulator model under conditions of systematically varying discriminability. The stimuli employed were such that, by  assumption, a large part of the encoding variability could be attributed to the distributional characteristics of the stimuli  themselves, and hence these could be used as an objective standard against which to compare the estimated parameters.  Method  Examples of the stimuli are shown in Fig. 2. Each stimulus consisted of 300 line segments of equal length, whose midpoints  were randomly distributed over a circular region of fixed diameter. In Condition 5, the ordering is TRL < TLR < TRR < TLL; that is, errors are faster than correct responses for both  response alternatives, a result consistent with the RJT formulation of the random walk, but not with any of the three counter  models. In Condition 4, the ordering is TLR < TRR < TLL < TRL. Here errors are faster than correct responses for one response  alternative, but for the faster of the two responses. (This is not only inconsistent with the counter models, but also with  RJT, which predicts that the ordering of correct responses and errors will be the same for both responses (Townsend & Ashby,  1983, p.308).) Here the orderings are consistent with all the counter models, as well as with RJT.  For the above reasons, in our evaluation of counter models, we chose to ask two distinct questions of the data. The first was:  in a context where the ordinal predictions of the models are satisfied, how well do they account quantitatively for the  observed statistics? The second was: if trial-by-trial variation in model parameters is permitted, can such models account for  the reversal in the ordering of correct responses and errors across discriminability levels? That is, can they provide an  alternative account of the reversal phenomenon to the mgf asymmetry explanation proposed by RJT? To answer the first question,  we restricted our attention to the data from Conditions 1 to 3. Three models were considered in this evaluation: the normal  and mimicking exponential models and, for comparison purposes, the parallel counter model. Townsend and Ashby describe a very  general form of the parallel counter model, with four stimulus parameters per discriminability condition. These are rate  parameters associated with correct and error responses, and may differ across the two counters. (That is, the rate of  accumulation for, say, a correct response, may differ depending on whether that response is associated with the total T,, or  TB.) Here we considered only the symmetrical case, where there are two stimulus parameters per discriminability condition, v,.  and v,., which describe the rates of accumulation for correct and error responses, respectively, these being the same for both  counters. ACCUMULATOR MODEL 161  MODELING CRITERION VARIABILITY  The one remaining problem is to provide an account of the reversal in reaction time orderings for the two easy levels of  discriminability. As stated earlier, Pike's (1973) criterion variability explanation can be taken as implying the existence of  a bivariate density function w(K,KB), which describes the distribution of criterion values over trials. Three main sources  of variability may be identified as contributing to the distribution of K,, and KB. The first is the differences in overall  mean criterion values between subjects. The second is the within-subjects drift in criterion values over time. The third is  the trial-by-trial variation in criterion resulting from the preceding sequence of stimulus presentations, and within it, the  distribution of correct responses and errors.  Under these circumstances, an obvious strategy for dealing with estimation problems might be to partition the data up  according to significant sources of variability, in such a way as to ensure homogeneity of each cell of the resulting data  matrix with respect to model parameters, and then carry out the estimation for each cell separately. Unfortunately, two  considerations militate against this scheme. The first is that, for even moderately large data sets, the number of partitions  required is likely to be large enough as to leave insufficient observations in each cell for reliable estimation. The second  is that the literature on sequential effects, while providing ample evidence for significant effects of this nature, provides  little guidance on how to partition the data to guarantee the absence of such effects. (For discussions of the problems  associated with this area, see Laming, 1979b; Luce, 1986, pp. 253-271.) A tractable version of this model employing exponential increments was considered in detail, and was shown to possess  considerable computational advantages over the original formulation, which employs truncated normal increments. Thus it provides an alternative explanation for this reversal effect to the variable  mgf asymmetry argument offered by RJT.
