In psychophysics, as in many quantitative fields of science, relative differences are more informative than absolute differences when estimating variations. Thus, although the  absolute  differences between 5 and 10 and between 100 and 105 are both equal to 5, it is more interesting to note the far wider  relative  difference in the first pair. A relative difference is the ratio of a plain–absolute–difference to a reference value and is often converted into a percentage to facilitate comparison. To express (for instance) a differential threshold or a change in performance, the choice of the reference value (i.e., the denominator underneath the absolute difference in the ratio) determines much of the result, and the divergence between estimates based on various references increases with higher variations. Consider for instance that the difference between 81 and 100 is smaller than between 100 and 120 relative to 100 (19% and 20%, respectively), but that a difference of 19 relative to 81 is larger than 20 relative to 100 (23.5% and 20% respectively).

The determination of psychophysical laws, which deal with changes in stimulus perception and in response performance, first requires a clear-cut, objective expression of the physics of stimuli and responses. This paper is restricted to measurements using Stevens’ so-called  scales of report  ( Stevens, 1957 ), expressed in units of the  Système International  (S.I.). These units can all be combined, e.g., distance in meters (m) combines with time in seconds (s) to express velocity in meters per second (m s −1 ). Direct measures expressed in such units are all positive; only differences, which are the topic of this paper, can be negative. Measures are null only in the absence of objects or phenomena, and this remains true for all units that are power combinations of S.I. units, in the form of

In psychophysics, the first and most important application in relative differences is the differential threshold. Sensitivity is usually tested based on the “just noticeable difference” (JND) between a standard stimulus and a set of stimuli close to it. Because the JND tends to vary proportionally to the standard stimulus, the Weber fraction, with the JND as the numerator and the standard value as the denominator ( Fechner, 1889 ), tends to be stable. A Weber fraction therefore describes a subject’s sensitivity better than a collection of JNDs.

Second, stimuli can also be matched in order to study larger contrasts between them. As with minimal differences, Fechner emphasized that the sensation of larger contrasts within pairs of stimuli of various values also tends to remain the same when their relative difference is constant. If the comparison is not subjective, neither stimulus within a pair should  a priori  be preferred over the other as standard, or chosen as reference in the relative difference calculation.

Third, in addition to comparing stimuli such as weight, colors, or duration, the experimenter is often interested in comparing performance, measured for example by distance to target, by duration of reaction delay, or by number of items completed. As for sensation scores, performance scores are better compared using relative differences than absolute differences.

Despite the extensive use of logarithmic scales by Fechner, Stevens, and their successors, none, to our knowledge, took advantage of the integral meaning of the Napierian logarithm, or logarithm to base e (i.e. the “natural” logarithm), to express a relative difference, perhaps because it was so tedious to calculate before personal computers became widespread. Another, more current impediment may be that, for very small differences, the deviation between estimates can be considered negligible, and for very large ones, the estimated percentage is not highly intuitive. For example, is the best estimate between (601−600)/600=0.1667% and (601−600)/601=0.1663% really that useful, when both are so close to Ln(601/600)=0.1665%? Many may fail to grasp the puzzling 69.3% relative difference between 50 and 100, or between 1 and 2, unless or until they learn it once and for all. Leaving such extreme instances aside, let us consider how common problems (such as the motor performance examples above) are solved by our method.

The difference between natural logarithms can be promoted as a standardized, advantageous expression of relative differences. Relative differences between physical data measured in S.I. units are central to psychophysics investigations, which essentially require the comparison of stimuli and performance. With a computer on hand, the natural logarithm difference can be easily obtained. It can readily be turned into a percentage we call the DNL%. It is additive, symmetrizable, and stable with regard to conversion of S.I. units. Logarithms in other bases have the same properties but yield values that are often abstruse to laymen, because they do not sit between the two most common expressions of the relative difference, a ratio of the absolute difference over either value at the denominator. Anyone can intuitively comprehend sensitivity through a Weber ratio, or performance through a discrepancy in reaction time, when they are expressed directly as DNL%.
