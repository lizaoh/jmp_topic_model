OGILVIE  Department of Psychology, University of Toronto, Toronto 181, Canada  Although Thurstone's basic model of comparative judgment has been generalized by several authors to accommodate more than two  categories of response in psychometric scaling, none has provided the psychophysical link from the subjective scale to the  physical magnitudes of the stimuli. Such a model, based on the assumption of a linear relationship, is presented for the  analysis of data from the method of constant stimuli with ratings of certainty. It yielded an excellent fit to empirical data  collected in a variety of psychophysical tasks where the number of observations was typical of threshold studies. A simulation  revealed that while the use of more than two categories results in greater precision in the estimation of the subject's  sensitivity, the gain in information for each category added beyond four categories is very small. More reliable estimates of  sensitivity were obtained from actual data when subjects were allowed four categories rather than two. The relationship of the  model's sensitivity parameter to the difference threshold of psychophysics is demonstrated.  Participants in the "interval ouncertainty" controversy in the 1920's debated the use of an equality or "don't know"  response category in addition to the usual responses of "greater than" and "less than" in discrimination tasks. In the absence  of an adequate model and procedure for parameter estimation, the controversy was resolved by simply abandoning the third  category of response (Thurstone, 1948). Yet there is surely more information to be obtained by allowing the subject to use  more than two categories, as many subjects would indeed prefer. This paper presents a model and estimation procedure for the  classical method of constant stimuli with ratings of response certainty, followed by tests of the model with empirical data.  Thurstone's (1927a, 1927b) "law of comparative judgment" provided a general model for the analysis of data from experiments of  the paired-comparison type, and in classical psychophysics it was basic to the two-category psychometric ogive for the method  of constant stimuli. In some early applications of the model the "interval of uncertainty," which resulted from the use of an  intermediate response category, was taken as a measure of the subject's discriminative abilities (Urban, 1910). Extensions of Thurstone's model have been proposed to accommodate the use of a "no difference" response category in  experiments designed to arrive at a subjective scale of a stimulus attribute. Both Glenn and David (1960) and Greenberg (1965)  assumed that when the subjective difference si; between stimuli i and j was not greater than an idiosyncratic threshold value,  say E, the subject reported a judgment of equality. Greenberg's model was concerned only with Thurstone's Case 5 and assumed a  normal distribution, while Glenn and David sought to avoid the problem of correlated estimates by the use of a sine function  which allowed an arcsin transformation in scaling the response proportions.  Bradley and Terry (1952) proposed an alternative solution for Thurstone's Case 5 by replacing the normal distribution by the  squared hyperbolic secant function, whose relation to the logistic function has been noted by Torgerson (1958, pp. 202203). As  in the above two extensions of Thurstone's model, Rao and Kupper (1967) assumed a threshold parameter, here referred to as E,  which permitted a generalization of the Bradley-Terry model to accommodate equality judgments. An alternative extension of the  Bradley-Terry model by Davidson (1970) involved the assumption that the probability of an equality judgment for a given  stimulus pair was proportional to the geometric mean of the probabilities of dominance for the members of the pair.  Torgerson's (1958) specification of the "law of categorical judgment" may also be considered an extension of Thurstone's model  of comparative judgment, specifically, from the two-category to the general k-category case; indeed, both date back to  Thurstone's (1927a, 1927b, 1931) early discussions of his model for a subjective metric. As with the model of comparative  judgment, solutions are available for the model of categorical judgment when certain simplifying assumptions are made. Besides  the solutions reviewed by Torgerson (1958, pp. 205-246), there exist minimum normit (Bock and Jones, 1968, pp. 225-239) and  maximum likelihood (Schonemann and Tucker, 1967) solutions, both of which allow goodness-of-fit tests, for the case assuming  constant correlation and equal category-boundary variances.  Thurstone (1959) maintained that he was chiefly interested in a subjective metric, but his basic judgmental model is the  conceptual framework which, when linked to a physical magnitude scale as in the classical psychometric function, can provide  the psychophysicist with a method of analysis for discrimination data. While all the above extensions of the Thurstonian model  to more than two categories successfully accomplished psychometric scaling with respect to a given stimulus attribute, none  tackled what might be called the psychophysical task of linking the psychological scale with the physical scale of stimulus  magnitudes. For the general case in which there are more than two response categories, what is required is a model which  322 OLSON AND OGILVIE  provides an estimate of the subject's sensitivity in relation to the physical scale but which is invariant over his choices of  category boundaries.  The requirement is clearly suggestive of the modern psychophysical approach known as the theory of signal detectability (TSD).  Basic to the development of TSD was the assumption that multiple presentations of each of two stimuli, noise and signal, give  rise to two distributions, usually assumed normal, ocentral neural effects, not at all unlike Thurstone's discriminal  processes. Maximum likelihood solutions to the TSD parameter-estimation problem where the variances of the two distributions  need not be equal have been provided for the logistic distribution by Ogilvie and Creelman (1968) and for the normal  distribution by Dorfman and Alf (1968, 1969).  The relationship between TSD and the basic Thurstonian model underlying the classical psychophysical methods has not gone  unnoticed, having received lucid expression by Treisman and Watts (1966). In particular, they developed a model which related  the TSD concepts of criterion values and sensitivity to the traditional psychophysical method of constant stimuli with two or  three response categories, as applied to absolute and difference thresholds. The attractive feature for the psychophysicist  was the fact that, as in TSD, the measure of the subject's sensitivity did not vary with the number of categories employed nor  with the subject's positioning of their boundaries. However, the Treisman-Watts model was not developed to the point where  estimates of the parameters could be computed from an explicitly stated mathematical model. A formally similar model, but from  a rather different point of view, has been presented by Wickelgren (1968).  The model presented below may be considered a further development of the application of TSD to the method of constant stimuli.  It rests on assumptions much like those of Thurstone's Case 5, with the additional assumption that the subjective scale is  linearly related to the physical scale. Its maximum likelihood solution is closely related to those of Ogilvie and Creelman  (1968) for TSD parameters and of Sch6nemann and Tucker (1967) for categorical-judgment parameters.  THE MODEL  In the method of constant stimuli with ratings of certainty, the subject is presented with m different pairs of stimuli, each  of which consists of the standard stimulus R and one of m comparison stimuli i, designated (R, i). The physical scale is  expressed in units appropriate to the attribute being judged and its origin is placed at the physical magnitude Yx of the  standard stimulus R. Similarly, the physical difference value yi = Yi - YR associated with each of the other m stimuli is  positive, negative, or zero according as its physical magnitude Yi is greater than, less than, or equal to that of R. The pair  (R, i) is presented to the subject ni times. On each trial, the  CONSTANT STIMULI WITH TWO OR MORE CATEGORIES OF RESPONSE 323  subject's task is to compare the stimuli i and R with respect to the given attribute and to indicate his comparative judgment  by specifying one of k ordered categories ranging from the unqualified assertion that i dominates R, through intermediate  categories where a degree of uncertainty is expressed, to the unqualified assertion that R dominates i.  Each presentation of the pair (R, i) with physical difference value yi is assumed to give rise to a central neural effect or  subjective difference si , which is positive or negative depending on whether i is perceived as dominating or being dominated  by R. The value of si is not a constant over repeated presentations of (R, i), but on each trial si may be regarded as a  sample, associated with the pair (R, i), from a distribution on a subjective or sensory axis. The mean of this distribution is  denoted by di , and its variance is assumed equal to unity for all i. As in Thurstone's Case 5, it is further assumed that the  si are independent, having zero correlation, or, what is equivalent, that their correlations are a constant.  The crucial assumption for the model is that the subjective or sensory axis can be related to the physical axis by a linear  transformation in the form  di = b(yi -yo),  where the scaling factor b is a sensitivity parameter for a particular subject on a particular discrimination task, and the  parameter yo is the point of subjective equality (PSE) on the physical difference scale. Clearly the assumptions of linearity  and equal variance contradict Weber's law, which implies that the variance of the distribution should increase systematically  with increases in stimulus magnitude. However, for the narrow range of stimulus values used in threshold studies, a linear  approximation is quite satisfactory. In fact, departures from linearity cannot be detected empirically in this range, as  Treisman and Watts (1966, p. 442) have noted. In Case A, in which none of the k response categories is labeled "no difference," there exists some criterion value, say c,, ,  which serves as the boundary between the least certain "greater than" category, which is category y, and the least certain  "less than" category, y + 1. It should be noted that the validity of whatever assumption is made has no  bearing on the accuracy of the sensitivity parameter b. As in previous extensions of Thurstone's model of comparative judgment  to accommodate a neutral category, the present model assumes c, = -cv+i when a neutral category is used, with the result that  c,+i need not be estimated directly in Case B. The criterion value cv is analogous to Glenn and David's (1960) threshold -r,  Greenberg's (1965) parameter k;; , and Rao and Kupper's (1967) parameter q, all of which were earlier denoted by E, the  threshold value above which the subject will venture an order-relation judgment.  The relationship between the model's parameters and classical psychophysical estimates is direct. A commonly used measure of  discrimination, derived from the psychometric function, is one-half the interquartile range (Woodworth and Schlosberg, 1954,  p. 212). In terms of the present model, this measure of the difference threshold in physical units is derived from Eq. 1 as  1.1 /b, depending only on the sensitivity parameter b. It is worth noting too that the choice of the normal or logistic  distribution for the model is irrelevant because the difference threshold is now in physical units.  The model may also be used in tests of absolute sensitivity, in which case the 50%-point absolute threshold is given by the  parameter yo . This parameter, in units of the physical scale, corresponds to the critical criterion value cin Case A and  lies between the criterion values c, and ci bounding the neutral category in Case B. The dependence of the absolute  threshold on the subject's choice of criterion values is not surprising in view of the close link between the present model  and TSD, in whose literature such problems are discussed (Green and Swets, 1966, pp. 117-123). Theoretically, the model sets no upper limit on the number of categories allowed, but one may well speculate about  the limit on the number which subjects are able to handle consistently (Miller, 1956). CONCLUSIONS  The model provided an excellent fit to empirical data collected in a variety of psychophysical tasks where the number of  observations was typical of threshold studies using two-category judgments. A simulation revealed that while the use of more  than two categories results in greater precision in the estimation of the subject's sensitivity for any given number of  observations, the gain in information for each category added beyond four categories is very small. Moreover, the information  gain is relatively insensitive to the exact positioning of category boundaries by the subject. The analysis of actual data in  a visual discrimination task supported the prediction that more reliable estimates of sensitivity could be obtained when  subjects used four categories of response rather than two.  By assuming a linear relationship between the subjective and physical scales, the model incorporates TSD's separation of  sensory and nonsensory factors into a traditional method of obtaining psychophysical estimates, with the result that TSD's d'  value for discriminability between any pair of stimuli is computable as is the psychophysicist's difference threshold,  unbiased by the subject's criterion selection.
