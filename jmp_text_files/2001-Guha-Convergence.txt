Convergence of the Integration Dynamics of the Construction�Integration Model 

Amal Guha and Jean-Pierre Rossi 

CNRS�Universite�Paris-Sud 

The connectionist network corresponding to the nonlinear integration dynamical system associated with Kintsch's construction�integration (CI) model is analysed with linear algebra tools. This addresses some theoretical questions raised and left unanswered by Rodenhausen (1992, Psychological Review 99, 547�549). A mathematical characterization for equilibrium points, which allows an a priori enumeration of all possible asymptotic states for the integration dynamical system, given a connectivity matrix, is given. The dynamics of convergence of the integration dynamical system is characterized in some detail as well. This provides a tool for understanding CI simulations and helps in particular to let us know to what extent the outcome will depend on the initial conditions. The criteria also provide a new mathematical analysis which allows for the explicit calculation of asymptotic states of the integration process without requiring computer simulation experiments. The new mathematical analysis should facilitate comparisons of the model's predictions with behavioural data. � 2001 Academic Press

2. INFORMAL QUALITATIVE OVERVIEW OF THE CONSTRUCTION�INTEGRATION (CI) MODEL 

For a comprehensive presentation of the construction�integration model and its various fields of application, the reader should refer to Kintsch (1988, 1998). We shall however briefly outline its main features. The model's aim was originally to model comprehension of written text. The representational units are concepts and propositions. The mental representation of the meaning of the text is a network whose nodes are concepts or propositions and whose links are the connection strengths between them. Comprehension consists in a sequence of construction and integration steps. Construction corresponds to the constitution of a network of propositions yielded by the text segment currently processed, and the integration step selects in this net, through the propagation of activation, the propositions which best capture the ``gist'' of the text. The integration dynamical system consists of a set of nodes whose interconnections are represented by a set of connection weights. Each node has a realvalued state called the ``activation level.'' The degree to which the activation of one node can influence another is represented by a coefficient (the connection weight) corresponding to the strength of the association between the two concepts or propositions. Thus, the connections among a given set of concepts or propositions (i.e., a ``proposition net'') may be represented by a connectivity matrix. 

Construction�integration goes as follows: the linguistic input is cut into segments corresponding to a few propositions; each segment is used to construct a proposition net (construction), containing possibly inferred propositions by combining the reader's world knowledge with the proposition net representing the reader's current understanding of the text (i.e., the current macrostructure). Then comes the integration step: Each proposition of the network constructed has a given initial activation

level (so there is an activation vector corresponding to the net), and integration consists in the spreading of activation according to the connectivity coefficients corresponding to each couple of propositions. These coefficients can be stored in a connectivity matrix, so each integration cycle can be seen as the multiplication of the activation vector by the connectivity matrix. For each integration cycle, this multiplication is followed by the replacement of all negative activations by zero, and then by a normalization step which ensures that the sum of all coefficients is 1. Thus, an activation vector can always be seen as a repartition of activation. Integration stops (hence succeeds) when the activation vector stabilizes (i.e., when the average change of activation is smaller than some threshold). Then the new macrostructure consists in only the most activated propositions (low-activation propositions are discarded), and a new construction�integration cycle can take place with the next segment of text as input. 

3. RELATION TO PREVIOUS THEORETICAL INVESTIGATION OF THE CI MODEL 

The first investigation of the mathematical aspects of the model was carried out by Rodenhausen (1991, 1992), who raised three questions about integration: When is the process well defined (i.e., when is it the case that activations cannot all become zero)? When do equilibrium points exist (i.e., activation vectors which would be stable for the integration process)? When does the integration process generate trajectories which actually converge towards these equilibrium points? Rodenhausen (1991, 1992) showed that if the sum of the connection weights leaving every node was strictly positive, then the integration dynamical system for the CI model was always well defined. Rodenhausen also showed (using topological arguments) that when the integration dynamical system for the CI model was well defined, there existed at least one ``equilibrium point'' (i.e., an activation pattern whose value is not modified by the integration dynamics of the CI model). Finally, Rodenhausen (1992) showed that when the connection weights entering a node added up to one and were always strictly positive (a fairly restrictive set of constraints on the connection matrix), then exactly one equilibrium point existed and all trajectories of the integration dynamical system converged to that equilibrium point. This latter constraint is very severe and is not satisfied in many important published applications of the CI model. 

Rodenhausen (1991) analyzed algebraically the equilibrium points of a dynamical process (``Model 1'') analogous to the CI model. There are some major differences between Model 1 and CI: Notably, nonlinearity breaks into the respective models differently. Despite these differences, in both cases equilibrium points are eigenvectors of matrices related to the connectivity matrix. However, Rodenhausen (1992) didn't elaborate a similar characterization for the CI integration dynamical system. An algebraic definition of equilibrium points for integration requires an extra condition compared to Model 1. Such a definition yields consequences about the dynamics of convergence (which Rodenhausen didn't explore) that provide elements for answering the third question (i.e., whether or not, for a given equilibrium point, there exists a trajectory converging towards it).

We address here with greater theoretical generality Rodenhausen's second and third questions about the model. In any case, the answers entirely depend on the properties of the connectivity matrix. Across the numerous versions and utilizations of the model, the construction of this matrix is highly variable, so the difficulty is that no hypothesis can be made about it in a general analysis of construction-integration. For example, the matrix need not be symmetrical (as in Rodenhausen's analysis), nor does it necessarily have only positive coefficients. This paper develops as follows. First, we give an algebraic characterization of equilibrium points, which provides a straightforward way of determining all existing equilibrium points for any given simulation. Then we describe the dynamics of integration, in the perspective of answering the question of whether a given equilibrium point may be an attractor. Then we state some consequences of our results in the terms of conditions on nonnegative matrices for the existence of attractors.

6. CONDITIONS FOR ATTRACTORS 

The aim of this section is to work towards a characterization of attractors, i.e., equilibrium points x� for which there exists an initial activation value (other than x�) such that the system converges towards x�. 

Theorem 2 (Rodenhausen, 1992, Theorem 4). Assume the initial state x belongs to the set K. Assume mij>0 for i, j=1, ..., n and �[n] j=1[m] ij[=1][ for i][=1, ...,][ n][.][ Then a] unique fixed point exists which is an attractor. 

We now use an algebraic analysis to identify attractors for a much more general class of connection matrices and initial states of the integration dynamical system of the CI model. 

First, we distinguish between ``smooth'' convergence and ``bouncy'' convergence. Then we prove that if convergence is smooth, the system converges necessarily towards a dominant eigenvector for some submatrix of M (we say that x is a dominant eigenvector of a matrix M if the corresponding eigenvalue has a maximal module among all eigenvalues of M). This implies that if convergence towards a nondominant eigenvector is possible, then it is necessarily bouncy. It is not known whether such nondominant eigenvectors may be attractors; but if this is the case, then the peculiarity of convergence towards them makes them different as to their psychological interpretation, from the other sort of equilibrium points.

In the smooth case, the integration process can be broken into two logical (and temporal steps): the first. step is the selection, through a kind of competition, due to negative coefficients of the matrix, of the winning propositions (those with nonzero coefficients). This step can be interpreted as a disambiguation step, for it is supposed to rule out incoherent interpretations that had previously been activated. The second step is convergence of activation repartition among the winners: the system eventually stabilizes to some activation vector. What Part (i) of Proposition 2 implies is that once the first step is over (when the winning propositions are selected) there is only one possible final activation structure for this subset of propositions: it is the activation structure that corresponds to the dominant eigenvector of MJ. 

It is not known whether convergence towards a nondominant equilibrium point is possible (i.e., convergence towards a vector which is not a dominant eigenvector of MJ , \J�[1, ..., n]). If some nondominant equilibrium point could be found (empirically or theoretically) to be attractors, it would be an important finding for CI theory. If we follow Kintsch (1998), who insisted on the psychological significance of the dynamics of the integration process, then the differences in the process of converging towards dominant or nondominant equilibrium points correspond to differences as to the psychological processes leading to the respective psychological states associated. Intuitively, a converging system such as that of Part (iii) of Proposition 2 involves two subsets of propositions, the subset corresponding to J[+] and the subset corresponding to J$. Integration carried out with the sole propositions of J[+] would converge towards a dominant eigenvector of MJ+ , say v1 . It is the continued interaction between the respective activities of propositions of J[+] and propositions of J$ (bouncy and converging to 0) that keeps the system from moving towards v1 .

7. CONCLUSIONS 

This paper presents, from a practical point of view, a method for determining what the possible outcomes of any given simulation are and for distinguishing between two families of outcomes among the theoretically possible ones. Such results should be of great interest to anyone undertaking simulation with CI, because it is a useful way to assess a priori the relevance and the quality of the parameter settings that were adopted by the experimenter, and they should as well inform better the interpretation of results. From a theoretical point of view, the criteria on the connectivity matrix that we provide also open a new perspective for the model since they address two important theoretical points. The first one is the conditions for convergence. Kintsch asserted (1988) that integration need not necessarily converge and that nonconvergence corresponds to cases which need extra processing; providing criteria on the (nonnegative) matrix for these cases, as we did, means that now we know which connectivity matrices stand (in the model) for cases requiring extra processing. The second legitimate concern is the impact of initial activation on the outcome of integration. Indeed, any psychological model includes at least some qualitative hypotheses about the correspondence between stimuli and responses, that is, between inputs and outputs of the model. An example of the technical relevance of this question is the paper of Otero 6 Kintsch (1992), who selectively increased initial activation values of some propositional nodes, according to their specific function in the text. Such a method is effective only when initial activation can actually influence the final outcome, and obviously this is the case only when there is more than one equilibrium point. If there is only one equilibrium point, the possible impact of initial activation will not be on what the final activation will be, but on the ``convergence rate'' of the integration dynamical system, which could be psychologically interpreted as ``comprehension time'' or ``comprehension difficulty.'' More generally, the existence of criteria on M corresponding to particular psychological situations (e.g., in the case of a single equilibrium point, only one possible interpretation of the text segment; in the case of a cyclic irreducible matrix, no convergence) is the start for a general validation of the model. Such correspondences between psychological situations and mathematical features of the connectivity matrix are an essential basis for the validation of any systematic construction of this matrix. These preliminary theoretical results, together with the recent development of Latent Semantic Analysis (Landauer 6 Dumais, 1997), open the perspective for the psychological validation of a method for establishing connectivity matrices, hopefully allowing us to avoid the uneasiness of constructing ``by hand'' a reasonable matrix for a given simulation. In addition to Latent Semantic Analysis, another means of constructing the connectivity matrix can be explored that has the advantage of relying on psychological data. The proof of Part (i) of Proposition 2 indicates clearly that if more than one equilibrium point have the same dominant eigenvalue, then the final outcome of smooth convergence depends on the initial activation vector. That is, output will vary according to which combination of dominant eigenvectors equals the initial activation. This is why matrices with multiple dominant fixed points are interesting ones for the model. Now, given a comprehension situation, it is possible to estimate, by empirical observation, the possible responses in terms of the propositions' activation repartitions (say v1 , ..., vl). Taking this as a basis, it is possible to construct a matrix M having as dominant eigenvalues v1 , ..., vl . The coefficients of M can in turn be tested against empirical data concerning the associative knowledge of subjects or be tested for relevance of their use, in other simulations. In other words, the CI model could be seen not only as a model for predicting activations, given a knowledge structure; it could also be seen as a model for predicting the structure of subjects' knowledge, with the final activations being parameters, which in turn can be more easily established by empirical observation.
