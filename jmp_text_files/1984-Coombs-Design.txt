Discussion. Summary.  INTRODUCTION  In a previous paper (Coombs & Lehner, 1981) it was found that symmetric changes in good and bad outcomes are not equal in  their effects on judged risk. As a consequence, moments of distributions are unacceptable as variables for measuring risk.  Variance, for example, is invariant under translation of a distribution and if the distribution is skewed the riskiness of a  gamble can be expected to reveal triple interactions, and so cannot be captured in a simple polynomial as defined in Krantz,  Luce, Suppes, & Tversky (1971).  An obvious prospect, then, is a model which separates the effects of good and bad outcomes, and here we propose testing a  theory which does that. The formal structure of the theory is that of the bilinear model (Kruskal, 1978), and we use an  experimental design which makes possible a conjoint measurement analysis of this model not previously feasible.  A bilinear model is one in which the independent variables can be partitioned into two subsets such that if either subset is  held constant, the dependent variable is a linear function of the variables in the other subset. These theories were deliberately chosen to illustrate a range over a wide variety of subject matters, but as formal models  they are closely related, and the methodology developed here for testing the bilinear model is applicable in principle to all.  The bilinear model has, of course, been given a lot of attention already. The most closely related development, perhaps, is  the extension by Anderson and Shanteau (1970) of functional measurement theory to the bilinear model. What is unique to the  present paper is the extension of conjoint measurement theory using only ordinal properties of the data which is made possible  by utilizing a particular experimental design.  An application to illustrate this development is in the context of a model for the measurement of risk, in which a risk  ordering is the dependent variable. Our bilinear model for the assessment of risk asserts that risk is an additive combination  of the effect of the bad aspects and the effect of the good aspects and that these two effects are each a consequence of their  respective outcomes and associated probabilities. A descriptive theory of perceived risk is concerned, among other things, with seeking common and  general relations that may exist between characteristics of the environment and the perception of risk.  Naturally, individual judgments of degree of risk in any particular instance may be uncertain and error-ridden, but controlled  replication may reveal consistent underlying relations obscured by the noise. Subjects  The subjects were 44 student volunteers from the University of Michigan paid subject pool.  6 COOMBS AND LEHNER  Procedure  Each subject participated in three experimental sessions on three separate, not necessarily consecutive, days. Data were  collected in each experimental session on seven stimulus matrices, the three shown in Table 1, 2, and 3, and four others, also  4 X 4 which contained the gambles used for the previous paper referred to above (Coombs & Lehner, 1981). On each day subjects  were asked to rank order each of the stacks of gambles with respect to risk by using the method of accumulation (Coombs &  Bowen, 1971). The order of the gambles in each stack and the order in which the stacks were presented to each subject were  randomized. Each subject was left to define risk in his/her own terms, our interest being in the descriptive theory of  perceived risk as a function of final outcomes and probabilities.  ANALYSIS AND RESULTS  Assessing Error in the Data  Implicit in the search for invariances embedded in noise is an error theory. There is, as yet, no adequate error theory for  conjoint measurement in general, and especially for the new tests introduced here. As a consequence, we developed ad hoc  procedures which provide a qualitative assessment of the results. We suggest some alternative procedures and report on their  use on different data bases, but these statistical issues are secondary to the main thrust of this paper, and so they are not  given the attention their importance warrants.  One of the procedures used to assess the quality of the data is the following. The three replications on each matrix provide a  measure of a subject's inconsistency and thereby a method for assessing the significance of violations of a condition being  tested. For each subject the rank order correlations (tau) between each pair of replications were calculated, and the subjects  were ranked from most to least consistent on the basis of the minimum (tau) between the three replications. The subjects were  then divided into two groups of 22 each, the more consistent, MC, and the less consistent, LC. The minimum tau which served as  the cutoff point between the MC and the LC groups for each of the seven matrices is reported in Table 4, where the stimulus  matrices 1, 2, and 3 used in this paper are numbered 5, 6, and 7, respectively. As may be seen, the consistency of the data  for the matrices used in this study is between the best and the worst of the four matrices used in the previous study (Coombs  & Lehner, 1981).  The philosophy underlying this method of assessing error and evaluating models calls for some explanation. We assume that a  subject's variability between trials reflects, in part, a subject's inconsistency within trials. If the number of violations  of a test condition is not directly related to random inconsistency, then it cannot be attributed to error, and the condition  being tested is significantly violated. The advantage of this method lies in the assessment of error independently of the  ANALYSIS OF THE BILINEAR MODEL  TABLE 4 Minimum Consistency of the MC Group  Matrix r  1 .82  2 .80  3 .70  4 .52  5 .73  6 .63  7 .62  goodness-of-fit of the model being tested. A model may be correct and not fit very well because it is incomplete in that it  does not include all the relevant variables; and it may be wrong and fit very well by virtue of being tested under limited  experimental manipulation, i.e., the behavior may be a function of variables which are varied over only a limited range. The  problem is to examine whether the dependent variable is behaving with respect to the interrelations among the independent  variables as the model requires. This approach is designed to be more sensitive than the gross goodness-of-fit measure. A  disadvantage of the method is that only some of its qualitative properties are known.'  The differences in quality of the data among these matrices, as reported in Table 4, have no bearing on the comparative  validity of any theories being tested, but reflect the comparative stability of the risk orderings for the seven matrices.  This comparative stability, itself, reflects the discriminability of the stimuli relative to the sharpness of the individual's  assessment of risk. The philosophy of the test is simple: reliability is determined independently of the model, and if those  individuals who provide the more reliable data violate a model more than those who provide less reliable data, then the model  is unacceptable. This procedure addresses the problem of assessing error independently of the model but does not avoid the ad  hoc property of statistical tests of significance. DISCUSSION  Cognitive considerations in information processing are the primary target of most contemporary experimental research in  decision making (Einhorn & Hogarth, 1981; Payne, 1982; Kahneman & Tversky, 1982). The stimuli frequently used in these  I This explanation was forcefully argued by Michael Aschenbrenner, personal communication.  32  COOMBS AND LEHNER  studies, and to which we will confine our attention in this discussion, are gambles or portfolios of gambles (consisting  usually of two gambles).  The tasks subjects are asked to perform are typically an assessment of risk or preference using as a response mode some  instance of the method of single stimuli such as a rating scale or absolute judgment, or using some instance of the method of  choice, like pair comparisons (see Coombs, 1964, Chap. 2 for their variety and some formal relations among them).  A characteristic of this research is that some standard of rational behavior exists, such as Bayes theorem or expected utility  principles, or other objective standards derived from first principles which are accepted a priori. The failure of subjects'  behavior to meet such standards is one of the forces driving this research for the past 10-15 years.  The experiments of Slovic (1967), Slovic and Lichtenstein (1968) and Anderson and Shanteau (1970) made extensive use of what  they called duplex gambles which were portfolios of gambles in which the component gambles were of a particular kind (see  below). The experiments of Lindman (1971) and Payne and Braunstein (1971), using portfolios of gambles that were constituted  differently but were formally equivalent in final outcomes and probabilities, demonstrated reversals of preference, an  inconsistency incompatible with conventional standards of rationality.  Most important of all have been the contributions of Kahneman and Tversky which have given coherence and direction to this  research with their demonstration of regularities in the departures from the a priori standards of rationality. They account  for these regularities in terms of heuristics and biases induced by the framing of the task. The existence of empirical  regularities which may be experimentally controlled and manipulated implies the existence of underlying laws and principles,  and in this sense the behavior is rational and rigorous theory can exist. The paper by Kahneman and Tversky (1981) and the  book by Kahneman, Slovic, and Tversky (1982) provide an introduction and review of this literature.  The dissection and testing of the cognitive processes in these judgment and choice tasks is aided by further enriching the  stimulus domain, which can be done in a systematic manner. A portfolio may, on the one hand, be presented in its most  elemental form as a set of final outcomes and probabilities. On the other hand, the gambles comprising a portfolio may  themselves be presented and constitute the experimentally independent variables that are manipulated. If the independent  variables are themselves gambles, then the tradeoffs being studied by the methodology developed here would be tradeoffs  between the contributions of gambles to a portfolio instead of tradeoffs between the elemental variables used in this  experiment.  This form of presentation, using gambles as the independent variables, implicitly introduces intermediate stages in the  processing of information in a manner which is under the control of the experimenter. There are, in some cases, as will be  seen, an infinite variety of alternative ways of phasing the process and this can be a powerful tool for both theoretical  psychology and applied decision making. The variety of distinct representations, all equivalent in final outcomes and  probabilities, permits  ANALYSIS OF THE BILINEAR MODEL 33  varying the salience of the elemental components and the distribution of attention and thereby inciting different heuristics  and biases in the search for regularities.  It is important to keep in mind, in the background, that tradeoffs remain, at the heart of all decision making, and that the  methods developed in this paper are directed at the fine-grain study of tradeoffs. The experiments reported here have been  based on tradeoffs between the elemental variables, outcomes, and probabilities, but the methodology applies equally well  whether the independent variables are the constituent gambles of a portfolio, or the task is assessment of risk or preference,  and/or the response mode uses a method of single stimuli or a method of choice.  We began the systematization of this stimulus domain using gambles as the independent variables in a portfolio, with the early  work on duplex gambles. A duplex gamble is a pair of gambles in which one consists of an amount to win at a specified  probability, otherwise nothing, and the other consists of an amount to lose at a specified probability, otherwise nothing. It  is understood by the subject that both gambles are to be played independently, and together constitute a single stimulus.  The data in the early experiments were analyzed by linear regression and analysis of variance using the values of probability  and amounts of money that had been displayed to the subject. Conclusions were drawn about the relative importance of outcomes  and probabilities on strength of preference and on riskiness, for example, that "`the probability of loss' was the most  important single determiner of perceived risk; and the `amount to lose' was far less important" (Slovic, 1967).  In terms of final outcomes and probabilities each duplex gamble, of course, corresponds exactly to a single gamble with three  or four possible outcomes. In the experiments by Slovic and by Slovic and Lichtenstein, 9 of the 27 duplex gambles used  corresponded to three-outcome gambles, and the remaining 18 corresponded to four-outcome gambles. As an illustration, an  example of a duplex gamble (taken from Slovic, 1967, Fig. 1) is shown at the top of Fig. 13. The heart of the issue is that, if the subject's assessments vary in any systematic manner over equivalent decompositions of  the same four-outcome gamble, then any model using only final outcomes and probabilities as variables can, at best, only  account for behavior in response to stimuli presented in the form of final outcomes and probabilities. All decompositions  disguise the final outcomes and probabilities, and this can only impede their usefulness for the purpose of assessing the  contributions of outcomes and probabilities to the evaluation of a gamble, whether it be its attractiveness or its riskiness. One of the consequences of this is that conclusions that one variable is more important than another may have little  generality beyond the levels used in the particular experiment." For example, in results reported earlier (Fig. 2), we saw  that the change in the amount to lose from -$10 to -$20 dominated the change in the probability of losing from .7 to .8, but  was dominated by the change from .1 to .2. There is no justification evident for stronger or  more general conclusions.  To summarize the discussion up to here, we have seen that duplex gambles, or any other decomposed form, misrepresent and, in  fact, may severely distort the actual outcomes and probabilities. Hence, their use in experiments to study models which have  final outcomes and probabilities as variables or to answer questions about the relative importance of outcomes and  probabilities in the assessment of gambles is, to say the least, not recommended. Furthermore, if the assessment of gambles is  considered to be a bilinear function of the outcomes and probabilities, or any of its special cases, then the behavior is a  response to a relation on ratios and questions about the relative importance of outcomes and probabilities are not generally  meaningful.  Most studies have been directed at modeling the assessment of gambles in terms of final outcomes and probabilities and not  with the study of behavior over equivalent decompositions (important exceptions are the papers by Aschenbrenner (1978) and  many of the studies by Kahneman & Tversky (e.g., 1979)). However, if individuals when presented with a decomposed portfolio  responded to it veridically in terms of final outcomes and probabilities, then the use of one form of display or another would  make no difference. If, however, behavior is not consistently compatible under all forms of display, then models of  assessment, whether risk or preference, must introduce other psychological concepts, such as attention and set, salience,  sequential processing effects, arithmetic biases, simplifying heuristics, to account for the failure of veridical behavior. One mode was in terms of final outcomes and probabilities, and the other mode was in terms of a wager in which  the player has to pay a stake y to play the gamble to win x + y with probability p. The stake presentation mode offers another approach to constructing a set of gambles equivalent to another set in terms of  final outcomes and probabilities, but predictably not equivalent in terms of preference. SUMMARY  The bilinear model, perhaps the most widely applied model in behavioral science, has not previously been open to study in  detail by conjoint measurement methods although Anderson and Shanteau (1970) have extended functional measurement to the  analysis of this model for a different experimental design, one in which the independent variables are duplex gambles.  The experimental design and the method of analysis developed here are ones in which the independent variables are not  themselves gambles, but are terms in a bilinear equation. The tests for this design are measurement-free in the sense that  only ordinal properties of the dependent variable are used and only ordinal conditions on the independent variables are tested.  Numerical scales more powerful than ordinal would be more sensitive, and lead to stronger conclusions, of course, but the  tests derived here reveal that ratio scales would be required for all the independent variables and, furthermore, their units  of measurement would be interdependent. Such requirements encourage the development of measurement-free tests.  Using a (2 X 2) X (2 X 2) experimental design, new tests are developed for the mutual independence and tradeoffs between  variables associated within the same term of the model, like an outcome and its associated probability, e.g., an amount to win  and the probability of winning; and between variables from different terms, called unassociated variables, e.g., the amount to  win and the probability of losing, or the amount to win and the amount to lose.  These tests may be used to detect the locus and cause of each violation of the model and, in the absence of violations, to  provide information about the shape of the functions which measure the contribution of each independent variable. Serious  40 COOMBSAND LEHNER  statistical problems emerge as with other conjoint measurement procedures, but ad hoc procedures and controls are devised  which offer some justification for the inferences drawn.  Findings include the following:  (1) Risk orderings are decomposable into contributions from good and bad components which are additive in their joint effect.  The good component (the joint effects of the probability of winning and the amount to win) plays a lesser role than the bad  component (the joint effect of the probability of losing and the amount to lose) in the assessment of risk when the  probabilities of winning and losing are matched (.1, .2, .4, .7, .8) and the amounts to win and lose are matched in absolute  value ($10 and $20).  (2) The pattern revealed by the decomposition of violations of independence between associated variables (like P and W and  like Q and L) was supported by the pattern of tradeoffs between changes in associated variables (like an increase in the  probability of losing versus an increase in the amount to lose). The results suggested the following conclusions:  (i) A change in the probability of losing had a more substantial effect than the corresponding change in the probability of  winning.  (ii) A change of 0.1 in probability had more effect at low probabilities (.1 to .2) than at high probabilities (.7 to .8).  (iii) A change in amount to lose had a more substantial effect than the corresponding change in amount to win relative to the  same values of the associated probabilities.  (3) Analysis of the tradeoffs between unassociated variables W & L, P & Q, P & L satisfied the bilinear model but those  between Q & W raise a serious question about the validity of the model for the assessment of risk. Two possible explanations  for the failure of the model were based on the inconsistency induced by small differences in the experimental variables and by  the use of alternative strategies for assessment of risk which are not compatible with the bilinear model. The latter  explanation is the more plausible insofar as the data of this experiment are concerned.  (4) An experimental procedure involving the presentation of a portfolio as a composite of gambles instead of a set of final  outcomes and probabilities is discussed. Two forms of composition, compound and contingent, are formulated and illustrated.  They are shown to distort the true final outcomes and probabilities and hence incite biases and heuristics and expose the  cognitive processes involved in decision making.  (5) Finally, the decision functions under the bilinear model using final outcomes and probabilities reveal that the behavior  must be a response to relations between ratios of the effects of the elemental variables and ratios of the effects of  differences between levels of the elemental variables.
