Psychological assessment has played an important role in clinical psychology for a long time; although the current assessment tools are more likely to be related to multiple-item test theory from classic psychometrics than tools that emerged from mathematical psychology. It is interesting to observe that the overlap between psychometrics and mathematical psychology is not particularly extensive because mathematical psychology has historically focused on theories with a linkage to research problems in experimental psychology while psychometrics has focused on general methods that play a central role in fields such as clinical psychology, educational psychology, and industrial-organizational psychology. Yet there has been some research in mathematical psychology that has potential utility for clinical psychology. For example, some researchers in mathematical psychology have developed models for the purpose of obtaining measures of important underlying cognitive processes. These models are not intended as general psychological theories, but instead are a detailed account as to how fundamental processes map onto available behavioral data. These models address a fundamental problem in cognitive psychology. The problem is the dependent variables available to experimental psychologists are entangled with a number of different cognitive processes (see  Chechile and Roder (1998)  and  Chechile (2007)  for a more detailed discussion of this point). As a result of the entanglement problem, changes in a dependent variable are not directly linked to a single psychological process. However, if a combination of dependent measures are modeled in terms of the underlying psychological processes, then it is possible to extract measures of the latent processes of interest. This model-based measurement approach is essential for an understanding of the cognitive changes that occur in experimental cognitive psychology. Model-based measurement can also be valuable for research with abnormal or clinical groups ( Batchelder, 1998; Batchelder & Riefer, 2007; Chechile, 2007; Chechile & Roder, 1998; Riefer, Knapp, Batchelder, Bamber, & Manifold, 2002 ). Multinomial processing tree (MPT) models are examples of model-based measurement, and MPT models can be particularly useful as a measurement tool because of the simplicity of the likelihood function and the ease by which the experimental task can be described in terms of the probabilities for various latent psychological processes.

In this paper a particular MPT model is used. The model was designed for obtaining separate measures for memory storage and retrieval, i.e., the 6P model from  Chechile (2004) . This model, like other MPT models, has a linkage to a specific experimental task. The task for the 6P model involves a random intermixing of recall test trials along with old and new recognition test trials. Furthermore the recognition test trials are followed by three-point confidence judgments where the subjects are instructed to use a “3” rating for a sure response, a “2” rating for an educated guess, and a “1” rating for a blind guess. This specialized task was first used by  Chechile and Meyer (1976) . Four MPT models for measuring storage and retrieval were advanced in the original  Chechile and Meyer (1976)  paper. However,  Chechile (2004)  criticized those older models and provided improved MPT models for this specialized experimental task; the 6P model being one of the improved models. The critical change was in the representation of memory processes for new recognition test trials. The change is not minor and can result in dramatically different measures for the underlying storage and retrieval processes. Consequently, there should be a reexamination of the studies that have used one of the earlier  Chechile and Meyer (1976)  models. Among those studies are two important studies that dealt with clinical problems. These studies used essentially the same experimental procedure, materials and design, and the studies were carefully conducted even in light of contemporary methodological standards. The first study is the  Gerrein and Chechile (1977)  study dealing with the effect of alcohol on memory. The second study is the  Larner (unpublished)  study that examined the nature of Korsakoff amnesia. The reanalyses of the data from these studies do in fact result in substantially different psychological accounts of the relative contribution of storage and retrieval processes than that originally reported. These applications of the 6P model are described in Sections  5 and 6 . Moreover, the new analyses also examine a number of new features that were not available at the time of the original reports.

Although there can be advances in clinical research methodology by utilizing contemporary methods and models from mathematical psychology, it is also the case that experimental and mathematical psychology can benefit from addressing research issues that have their origin in clinical psychology. For example, there is requirement in clinical practice to study specific individuals. This perspective leads naturally to the question as to what is the most accurate way to measure an individual’s storage and retrieval processes? It would seem that fitting a model to the data from an individual would be the obvious answer to this question. However, in Section  4  several novel findings indicate that there is a more accurate method for measuring the latent memory processes of an individual than that obtained by simply fitting the model on an individual basis. In Section  3  a related question is tackled, i.e., what is a more accurate method for averaging information in order to estimate the population mean for a latent process like the probability of sufficient storage? The two natural candidate solutions are: (1) fit the model to individuals separately and then average the individual estimates, and (2) pool the data and then fit the model to the group data. Monte Carlo simulations based on the 6P model demonstrate that the second method results in a more accurate estimate of the population parameter. In Section  4  another series of Monte Carlo simulations showed that a modified jackknife estimate based on a difference of pool estimates is a superior method for recovering the distribution of individual values. These questions and findings have potentially important implications for modeling beyond the application of the 6P model to clinical research problems, thus underscoring the mutual benefit for examining the application of mathematical models to clinical research problems.

Because the 6P model is central to this paper, it is reviewed and contrasted with a model from  Chechile and Meyer (1976)  in Section  1 . In Section  2  the issue of parameter estimation is addressed for the 6P model. In that section a relatively novel method for estimating the model parameters is described. The method is called population parameter mapping (PPM), and this method has several advantageous features ( Chechile, 1998a, 2004, 2007 ). Because the PPM method is not a standard method, it is contrasted with the maximum likelihood estimates (MLE) and traditional Bayesian estimates. Consequently, researchers with a general interest in the theory of parameter estimation should find the information in Section  2  valuable. However, readers who are not particularly interested in the technical details of parameter estimation can skip this section because the key findings of the clinical studies do not hinge upon the use of the PPM method of analysis.

Finally, in Section  7  general conclusions are drawn for the approach of applying MPT models for studying clinical populations. It is also stressed in that section that there is a broader medical application for this approach beyond that of clinical psychology.

Bayesian statistics is a mathematically rigorous system of statistical inference that results in point and interval estimates directly in terms of the properties of the posterior probability distribution (see  Bolstad (2004) ,  Box and Tiao (1973) ,  Congdon (2001) ,  Gill (2002) ,  Hartigan (1983) ,  Lad (1996) ,  Lee (1989) ,  Leonard and Hsu (1999) ,  Press (1989)  and  Raiffa and Schlaifer (1961) ). With the Bayesian approach, the unknown population parameters are represented with an initial probability distribution. This prior probability distribution is combined with the likelihood function to produce a posterior probability distribution. Inference is then a simple description of the posterior distribution within the usual framework of probability theory.

For some applications, the standard Bayesian method can result in computational difficulties. In fact computational challenges did historically limit the use of Bayesian statistics. However, statisticians discovered that Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods could be used to circumvent many of the computational problems (see  Gelfand and Smith (1990) ). Although the MCMC approach has greatly increased the class of problems that can be examined with Bayesian methods, this approach also has some limitations. The MCMC algorithm requires a convergence to an asymptotic state, but the rate of convergence is not known and is dependent on the model. However, for the 6P model the MCMC approach is not needed because an exact Bayesian solution is possible.

To obtain the posterior marginal distribution for any particular parameter requires the integration of the posterior distribution with respect to the other parameters. This problem is computationally challenging for two reasons. The first difficulty is connected to the fact that the powers of many of the terms in the numerator of Bayes theorem can easily result in computer “underflow” problems, i.e., too small a number for the machine to handle. These small values are not always due to trivial probability values because the multiplicative normalization constant is generally a very large number, so large a number that it can result in computer “overflow” problems. In the past this first problem is circumvented by ignoring the constant and computing kernel probability values in terms of logarithms (see  Chechile and Meyer (1976) ). Next a search is required to find the maximum kernel value so that the antilogarithms can be found relative to a rescaled maximum value. Finally the constant is found that normalizes the posterior probability distribution. There is also a second problem that contributes to the computational difficulties. This problem occurs if there multidimensional integrals that cannot be solved in closed form.  Chechile and Meyer (1976)  were able to circumvent this problem by finding closed form solutions for many of the integrals for the MPT models that they examined. In general, however, it is not always possible to find close form integrals to reduce the complexity of the numerical integration. This difficulty underscores the importance of the MCMC approach discussed earlier; however, as mentioned above, there are some costs associated with the MCMC procedure.

Fortunately we can use a well known property of a Bayesian analysis to circumvent the above two problems for the 6P model. In general a Bayesian analysis converts a prior distribution to a posterior distribution after some data are observed. The posterior distribution then becomes the prior distribution for the next set of data. In principle we can apply the Bayesian analysis separately for each observation in sequence. The final posterior distribution is the same regardless as to how the observations are processed, i.e., the same posterior distribution will result if we examine all the data as a single batch or if we examine the information one observation at a time. Moreover, with the approach of examining one observation at a time, the order for examining these observations does not matter. The issue of group differences versus individual differences within a group has been discussed by a number of investigators (e.g.,  Davidson and Costello (1969) ,  Neufeld (1977, chap. 2)  and  Neufeld et al. (2007) ). The issue is clearly important in clinical applications because there is an interest in detecting both group differences and detecting mixtures that might exist within the group, i.e., normal versus abnormal. Many of the statistical studies for categorical data about this issue have use the linear model (see  Agresti, Booth, Hobert, and Caffo (2000)  and  Agresti (2002) ). Yet many MPT models in psychology, like the 6P model, are nonlinear models. Consequently, it is important to study this issue of individual and group differences for the specific model of interest.

One possible approach is to use the 6P model on an individual basis and obtain point estimates for the model parameters for each subject. The estimates are then averaged to obtain an overall parameter estimate for the group. This approach has been used for a number of storage/retrieval studies with other MPT models. Most pertinent for the present paper, the  Gerrein and Chechile (1977)  study of alcohol-induced amnesia and the  Larner (unpublished)  study of the Korsakoff syndrome both used a model from  Chechile and Meyer (1976)  with the analysis done on an individual basis. Data from these two studies will be reexamined in this paper in terms of the 6P model.

Although the individual-subject level of analysis is reasonable and avoids the potential problem of pooling data across a heterogeneous group, it is still based on an unproven idea that the averaging of point estimates that have considerable uncertainty results in an accurate estimate for the group. There is an alternative method based on the pooling of the frequencies for each multinomial category across the group of subjects and then obtaining the model estimates for the group. In fact this alternative method for the analysis was used for the few studies that have used the 6P model, i.e.,  Chechile (2004, 2007) .

There are no general proofs available as to which method is best for applying MPT models. Moreover, this general and potentially important issue has not received much attention; although  Cohen, Sanborn, and Shiffrin (2008)  have explored the issue of grouping data versus averaging individual estimates in the context of model selection analyses.  Cohen et al. (2008)  found that the grouping procedure resulted in more accurate model selection decisions relative to the averaging method. However, the Cohen et al. study did not focus on the issue of the accuracy of parameter estimation and did not study the parameter recovery issue for a MPT model for a single experimental condition. Furthermore, this methodology question could yield different answers depending on the form of the model. Consequently, a series of Monte Carlo analyses were conducted to explore this issue for the 6P model.

At this point there is a caveat that should be acknowledged in regard to the issue of pooling versus modeling on an individual basis. There are applications where the investigator is trying to fit a function across a number of different experimental conditions. For some nonlinear functions it has been shown by  Estes (1956)  that the averaging of the data before fitting the model can result in distorting the function. For such applications it would seem that the analysis should be done on an individual basis. Yet the current application is not a case where a nonlinear function is being fit across a number of different independent variable conditions. The 6P model is a MPT measurement model for obtaining measures of the pertinent cognitive processes for each condition separately. Consequently, the problems that  Estes (1956)  addressed are not a concern with the 6P model.

It should also be acknowledged that there is another approach that is between the two considered in this section.  Klauer (2006)  and  Stahl and Klauer (2007)  have developed and implemented an approach called hierarchical multinomial models (HM)—an approach similar to finite mixture distributions (see  Titterington, Smith, and Makov (1985) .                          C 
                         = 
                         1

                         C 
                       is equal to the number of people in the group, then HM fits the model on an individual basis. Although this method can be useful for dealing with a heterogeneous group, in Section  4  an alternative procedure is presented that enables improved measurement for individuals without limiting the data to just the individual. Consequently, the investigator can examine each person without the need to specify ahead of time the number of subgroups.

In light of the general properties of the 6P model discussed above, there are several applications that can be made in the area of abnormal psychology. For example, both the Gerrein and  Chechile (1977)  study of the effects of alcohol on memory and the  Larner (unpublished)  study of Korsakoff amnesia used an analysis based on separate individual estimates that were averaged across the subjects in a group. Yet that method for examining the data is less accurate than the method of pooling the frequencies in the various multinomial categories prior to estimating the model parameters. Moreover, both of those studies used an older  Chechile and Meyer (1976)  model, which made a questionable assumption about the rate of recollective memory processes in foil recognition. The problem with the modeling of foil recognition resulted in the 6P model as an improved MPT model for the Chechile and Meyer task. Consequently, these two studies ought to be reexamined with the 6P model and with the frequency pooling method.

A great deal of the discussion of the effect of alcohol on memory has centered on the underlying information-processing mechanisms that are responsible for the memory loss. For example,  Goodwin, Powell, Bremer, Hoine, and Stern (1969)  and  Storm and Caird (1967)  argued for the role of a retrieval impairment because of the evidence for state-dependent learning. The design of state-dependent studies is one where the subject learns some information while intoxicated, later experiences retention difficulties when sober, but still later shows a memory improvement when intoxicated again. Although it is reasonable to infer from the phenomenon of state dependency that the reinstating of the original drug state can serve as a retrieval cue for some subjects, it does not result in a probability for retrieval, and it does not rule out an important role for storage in accounting for alcohol-induced amnesia. In fact  Lisman (1974)  and  Ryback (1970)  argued that in some cases subjects showed little evidence of remembering past events that occurred while intoxicated even when cued about those events and placed back in the same state of intoxication.  Birnbaum and Parker (1977)  also argued for a strong role for storage in order to account for the pattern of effects of alcohol on free and cued recall measures. However, these authors did not use model-based measurement in order to examine storage and retrieval.  Gerrein and Chechile (1977)  did use model-based measurement for the Chechile–Meyer task to measure the storage and retrieval parameters as a function of alcohol, and reported that alcohol did affect both storage and retrieval processes. However,  Gerrein and Chechile (1977)  measured storage and retrieval by means of Model 4 from  Chechile and Meyer (1976)  and did the analysis on an individual-subject basis. Consequently, the data from that study should be reexamined using the more recent 6P model, and the analysis should be done by pooling the frequencies in the various multinomial response categories before estimating the model parameters.

In the  Gerrein and Chechile (1977)  study there were two groups of subjects. One group received alcohol in an orange juice solution so as to produce a peak blood alcohol concentration of .095%, whereas the other group received an orange juice solution that only had an odor of alcohol but did not contain alcohol. The experiment was a double-blind design where neither the subject nor the experimenter knew the condition at the time of test. Also there were four within-subjects experimental conditions for all subjects. The four conditions resulted from a 2×2 factorial design where one factor was retention interval (either 4 s or 12 s) and the other factor was related to the intra-target organizational level (either High or Low). All the targets were word triads with the words selected from the  Battig and Montague (1969)  norms. In the high organization conditions the three words were from the same taxonomic category (e.g., GRAPE–CHERRY–WATERMELON) and in the low organization conditions the words were from different categories (e.g., GRAPE–TIN–DENIM). After the presentation of the target, the subjects were required to repeat a string of digits during the retention interval. At the time of test the participants were given either a recall, old recognition, or new recognition test. The foils for organized trials were items from the same category as the target. Half the foils were very similar (i.e., a rearrangement of target words) and half the foils were all different words.

By measuring the rate of target storage at the time of test and the rate of retrieval of the stored targets, a detailed description is achieved of the cognitive changes that occurred as a result of the independent variables. The three main independent variables that were manipulated in the  Gerrein and Chechile (1977)  experiment resulted in different patterns for the underlying storage and retrieval processes. The memory differences associated with the alcohol versus placebo manipulation as well as the memory differences associated with the length of the retention interval were linked to changes in storage alone. Both of those effects are reasonable. Retention interval has been studied in many other experiments, and the decrease in  
                            
                               
                                  θ 
                               
                               
                                  S 
                               
                            
                          with increasing retention interval has been a consistent finding (see  Chechile (2006) ). Moreover, the decrease in  
                            
                               
                                  θ 
                               
                               
                                  S 
                               
                            
                          with alcohol intoxication is also not a surprising outcome. Alcohol could impair the initial quality of the memory encoding, and a weak encoding is expected to undergo a more vigorous storage interference over the course of the retention interval. What is less apparent is the absence of detectable retrieval change for either the retention interval effect or the alcohol effect. Yet we should not over generalize from the findings of the reanalysis. For example, it would be incorrect to conclude that retrieval is never affected by alcohol. For the  Gerrein and Chechile (1977)  experiment, the learning and testing of the subjects was done in the same drug-level condition, i.e., the memory was tested shortly after studying a target. Under such test conditions there cannot be an effect of state-dependency. Finally for the target organization variable, the improvement in memory for organized targets was associated with both an increase in memory storage as well as an improvement in the retrieval rate. Again highly organized targets would be expect to be better encoded and thus less susceptible to storage interference. Retrieval also played an important role in accounting for the effects of target organization. The retrieval effect is reasonable because when there is a common category for the three words in the target triad, the memory of the category can serve as a valuable cue to guide memory search on the recall test.

The results of the reanalysis provides further support for the more recent 6P model from  Chechile (2004)  in comparison to the earlier  Chechile and Meyer (1976)  models. The key difference in the newer MPT model for the Chechile–Meyer task has to do with relaxing the assumption about the equivalence of storage as measured on old and new recognition test trials. The earlier  Chechile and Meyer (1976)  models incorrectly assumed that the rate of knowledge-based, correct and high confidence recognition are the same for old and new recognition test trials.  Chechile (2004)  critiqued this assumption and provided, in the 6P model, different parameters for these processes, i.e., the

Korsakoff (1889)  first described a case study of a patient with normal intelligence and with a normal memory for remote events, but the patient had a profound amnesia for learning new information.  Sack (1970)  provided a vivid case study of the “the lost mariner” who suffered from the Korsakoff syndrome. Sack’s patient believed he was 19 years old, but in fact he was an older man who had little recollection of the events that occurred to him over many decades of his life.

Korsakoff patients suffer from a profound amnesia linked to brain damage to the medial thalamus and the mammillary bodies of the hypothalamus ( Markowitsch, 2000 ).  Brion (1969)  also stressed that bilaterial damage existed in these brain regions for all 40 of the Korsakoff patients examined in that study. Yet  Brion (1969)  and  Whitty and Lewin (1960)  also reported finding damage to other brain regions with some Korsakoff patients. Korsakoff amnesia is typically associated with individuals who were chronic alcoholics; although the brain damage is now considered as a consequence of a severe vitamin B1 deficiency ( Thomson, 2000 ). Presumedly an alcoholic with poor eating habits is at high risk for incurring the vitamin-induced brain damage characteristic of the Korsakoff amnesic.

Although both retrograde amnesia (memory lost for past events) and anterograde amnesia (difficulty acquiring new information) have been associated with the Korsakoff syndrome, these two types of amnesia need not be unrelated because retrograde amnesia might be a consequence of an alcoholic life style with profound anterograde amnesia ( Squire & Cohen, 1982 ). There is no doubt about the anterograde amnesia exhibited by the Korsakoff patients ( Butters & Miliotis, 1985 ). Yet an information processing account for this impairment is not clear ( Larner, unpublished ). Some investigators (e.g.,  Warrington and Weiskrantz (1978) ) argued that the memory deficit is with retrieving stored traces. The case for the retrieval hypothesis is indirect and is primarily based on a demonstration that cuing helped Korsakoff patients. The demonstration of a cuing effect, however, does not rule out an important role for storage in accounting for the memory deficit. Also cuing might only help the Korsakoff patient by suggesting items for guessing. In fact other investigators (such as  Cermack (1977) ,  Reige (1977)  and  Talland (1965) ) argued for an important role of encoding and storage deficits with Korsakoff patients. However, even if the Korsakoff patients do not encode new information in an effective manner, it does not rule out an important role for a retrieval impairment. Without model-based measurement, it is difficult to account for the information processing mechanisms that underlie the anterograde amnesia. The unique contribution of the  Larner (unpublished)  experiment was that it used one of the  Chechile and Meyer (1976)  models to examine Korsakoff amnesia in terms of underlying storage and retrieval processes. The  Larner (unpublished)  study found a substantial role for retrieval in accounting for the short-term memory losses experienced by Korsakoff patients. However, the  Chechile and Meyer (1976)  models did not adequately model foil recognition as demonstrated with the present reanalysis of the  Gerrein and Chechile (1977)  study. Hence, it is important to reanalyze the Larner data as well as with the 6P model.

The  Larner (unpublished)  experiment followed the design of the  Gerrein and Chechile (1977)  study very closely except the key comparison was between a group of Korsakoff patients and a control group. Each group was studied using the same Chechile–Meyer task employed in the  Gerrein and Chechile (1977)  study. The same within-subject conditions for retention intervals were examined (i.e., 4 and 12 s) as well as the same conditions of high and low levels for target organization. The stimuli were also selected in the same way as in the Gerrein and Chechile study. All subjects were tested while sober.

The subjects in the  Larner (unpublished)  study were ten male Korsakoff patients selected from three psychiatric hospitals in Sydney, Australia and ten yoked controls. The Korsakoff patients were screened to assure that there were no other psychiatric difficulties such as psychosis and depression and were screened to exclude patients with evidence of dementia, aphasia, or other neurological deficients.  Larner (unpublished)  described in detail the evidence as to why the patients were correctly diagnosed as exhibiting the Korsakoff syndrome. He also carefully selected ten control subjects who were matched on age, gender, verbal IQ, educational level, and the  Congalton (1963)  occupational status level. The mean age of both groups was 53 years.

The reanalysis of the  Larner (unpublished)  data with the 6P model provides for a clearer picture of the information processing mechanisms involved with Korsakoff amnesia. A number of important points can be made from the storage and retrieval measures in the conditions studied by  Larner (unpublished) . For example, Korsakoff amnesia and normal forgetting share much in common. There are substantial storage losses that occur with long retention intervals with normal subjects ( Chechile, 2006 ); consequently, a storage loss is not a feature of only the amnesic patient. Both the Korsakoff patients and the Controls have some storage after a 12-s delay. Neither group was uniformly perfect, and neither group experienced total storage failure. Clearly there is a substantial difference in the rate for storage between the Korsakoff patients and the Controls, yet the differences between the two groups is better represented as a matter of degree rather than a contrast between people with a complete failure of a cognitive system versus a group with a completely perfect cognitive system. Perhaps after a much longer delay, the Korsakoff patients might have a near zero level for storage, but the Controls might also show a further decline in storage. Although there are massive differences in the storage retention rate between the Korsakoff patients and Controls, it still seems better to characterize the difference between the groups as a matter of the size of the storage loss.

With more interference and substantially longer retention intervals,  Chechile (2006)  showed that normal subjects (a) can have very low rates for target storage and (b) also have a nonzero storage asymptote that can vary as a function of the quality of the initial encoding.  Chechile (2006)  proposed a general two-trace hazard model for describing the dynamics of memory storage. It is outside the scope of the present paper to discuss in detail the two-trace hazard model. However, in  Chechile (2006)  there is an analysis of the data from the famous amnesic patient H.M., who had surgery to remove his hippocampus in both hemispheres (see  Hilts (1995) , for a detailed discussion of H.M.). Korsakoff patients have been characterized as exhibiting a somewhat similar pattern of amnesia as H.M. ( Squire, 1982 ). Nonetheless,  Chechile (2006)  demonstrated by means of a MPT model for the data published in  Freed, Corkin, and Cohen (1987)  that H.M. could be represented in terms of the two-trace hazard model. The  Freed et al. (1987)  study tested H.M. using a set of 1200 novel pictures that H.M. studied for 20 s each. H.M.’s memory of the pictures was above zero for some of the pictures even after a delay of 7 days. H.M.’s amnesia, like that of the Korsakoff patients studied by  Larner (unpublished) , was not total. It can be represented as an extreme case of normal forgetting. Normal subjects experience a decline in storage with increasing length of the retention interval, but some memories, in the two-trace hazard model, are able to survive over the life of the individual. Amnesic patients preserve much less, but there is no reason to assume that the storage loss is 100%. Also at short retention intervals, such as the 4-s retention interval in the Larner study, some Korsakoff patients have fairly good storage (see  Fig. 7 ).

It is also noteworthy to observe that the Korsakoff patients in the 12 s retention interval did not have a problem with retrieval. In fact the retrieval of the traces available at the 12-s retention interval is greater than the retrieval of the stored traces at the 4-s retention interval.  Warrington and Weiskrantz (1970)  hypothesized that Korsakoff patients are able to store new information but have difficulty retrieving that information.  Warrington and Weiskrantz (1974)  further proposed that the mechanism for the retrieval difficulty was an abnormally high level of interference from other information in memory. However, the Warrington and Weiskrantz studies did not use a methodology that measured storage and retrieval and their hypothesis was not uniformly accepted (see criticisms by  Butters and Cermack (1974) ). Also  Warrington and Weiskrantz (1978)  had difficulty supporting their interference hypothesis. Based on the present reanalysis of the  Larner (unpublished)  data, the Korsakoff memory deficit at the longer retention interval is due mainly to a storage problem rather than a retrieval difficulty. This result does not support the original  Warrington and Weiskrantz (1970, 1974)  hypothesis.

A number of investigators have reported encoding deficits with Korsakoff patients, i.e.,  Cermack (1977) ,  Cermack, Butters, and Gerrein (1973) ,  Reige (1977) , and  Talland (1965) . For example,  Cermack et al. (1973)  observed that when directed, the Korsakoff patient could encode targets on either an acoustic, associative, or semantic basis. However, without prompting the Korsakoff patients did not spontaneously use a semantic encoding. Also,  Talland (1965)  reported that when Korsakoff patients were presented with a card with ten familiar pictures from different categories that 16 of 22 Korsakoff patients inaccurately over generalized the pictures into a small number of categories. These reports of poor spontaneous encoding strategies could result in the pattern of storage and retrieval effects found in the present paper. Encoding quality in the  Chechile (2006)  two-trace hazard model influences the strength and richness of the initial memory traces. For lower quality encodings there is a greater subsequent storage interference effect that results in more of the traces becoming ultimately destroyed. Thus the pattern for storage and retrieval measures found in this paper is consistent with the hypothesis that Korsakoff patients generally use less effective encoding strategies.

In the clinical setting there is need for the extensive testing of patients. Certainly many measures of the patient’s health are recorded in clinical medicine. Similarly, psychological assessment is an important aspect of clinical psychology. Although many clinical assessment tools, such as the Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory (MMPI), emerged from multiple-item test theory and classic psychometrics, a case is made here for also using a different type of measurement tool for studying clinical populations, i.e., MPT models. For example, the  Chechile (2004)  6P model is a MPT model that has separate measures for storage and retrieval. Although the model was originally developed to assess issues in the area of memory research within the field of experimental psychology, the model can also be used to study clinical populations.

In this paper the 6P model was successfully used to examine alcohol-induced amnesia and Korsakoff amnesia. In both applications, the storage and retrieval measures provided for a detailed account as to how the clinical group and the control group differed. Also a new jackknife method was introduced that enabled the examination of model parameters for an individual, without the necessity of restricting the estimation to only the data obtained from the individual. Monte Carlo studies showed that this new method was more accurate than simply using the data obtained from the individual. Although this new procedure may eventually be used for nonclinical cognitive research, the clinical context stimulated the development of the method because of the necessity in clinical practice for obtaining precise measurements on the individual. Consequently, both mathematical psychology and clinical psychology can benefit by tackling clinical problems from the perspective of mathematical psychology.

Finally there is another medical/clinical context that can benefit from the use of model-based measurement with models like the 6P model. Drugs that are proposed for clinical use in medicine must undergo extensive testing to determine the effectiveness of the drug and to ascertain if there are negative side effects. In addition to the appropriate medical tests, a case can be made that information processing measures should also be examined. A model, such as the 6P model with specific parameters linked to storage and retrieval processes, has the potential for detecting the effect of a drug on basic memory processes.
