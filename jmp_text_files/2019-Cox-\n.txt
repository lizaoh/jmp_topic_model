In many subfields of psychology and cognitive science, it is of critical theoretical importance to decide whether or not a set of results can be accounted for in terms of a single underlying factor or process. As a toy example, consider that foot size and vocabulary size, which are strongly correlated, can be jointly explained in terms of a single underlying factor, namely, age. More pertinent to psychologists, researchers want to know whether memory retrieval is a result of separate familiarity and recollection processes ( Brainerd & Reyna, 2010; Parks & Yonelinas, 2007 ), whether categorization relies on separate rule-based and similarity-based systems ( Ashby & Maddox, 2005 ), whether judgments arise from a combination of rapid emotional reactions and slow deliberative reasoning ( Evans, 2003 ), to cite just a few examples.

The first – and most fundamental – step toward addressing these theoretical questions is to assess whether or not a single factor is sufficient to explain extant results. We begin from the position that this question is to be answered by making observations on two or more outcome variables, each of which is measured in several different conditions (which may be created by experimental manipulation or group assignment). Then the question becomes, is it possible to explain how this set of outcomes differs across conditions in terms of a single underlying factor, in the way that age can jointly explain differences in both foot size and vocabulary size? In the event that all the variables and conditions can be observed within an individual subject – for example, by measuring foot size and vocabulary size multiple times in the same individual as they age – directcomparisons (using, for example, information criteria like AIC or DIC, Bayes factors, or cross-validation) can be made between single-factor and multiple-factor models to determine which is the best account for each individual that account for the greater flexibility of the multiple-factor model. A more difficult situation arises when a fully within-subjects design is not possible, such as when comparing different demographic or clinical populations or assessing the effects of manipulations (such as pharmacological or training interventions) that cannot be performed independently. In the event of partially or entirely between-subjects observations, the relationships between different conditions and different outcome variables are  latent  and require additional statistical machinery to infer. Such machinery includes things like principal components analysis, factor analysis, and analysis of variance, but each of these techniques requires making strong assumptions, like linearity or orthogonality, about the nature of any latent relationships that are to be drawn out. One method that does not entail such strong assumptions is  state-trace analysis  ( Bamber, 1979 ).

A state-trace is a plot in which the observations of one outcome variable across conditions are plotted against the observations of another outcome variable measured in the same set of conditions—though not necessarily on the same subjects.  Fig. 1  shows some example state-trace plots each with two outcome variables.

                       The state-trace plot addresses the question of whether these measurements can be accounted for by a single underlying latent factor by illustrating how each condition jointly  effects  each outcome variable. If the two (or more) outcome variables are affected by the different conditions in either exactly the same way or exactly the opposite way, then for any condition that increases the value of one variable, it should also increase (or decrease) the value of the other, and vice versa. This kind of situation, depicted in  Fig. 1a , yields a  monotonic  state-trace. In this way, a monotonic state trace indicates that the effect of the observed conditions on the two outcome measures can be explained in terms of a single underlying factor, without requiring that we make any assumptions about the  form  (e.g., linear, logarithmic, etc.) of how that factor manifests across each outcome variable. If the state-trace is non-monotonic, examples of which are shown in  Fig. 1b , the two outcome measures are not affected by the manipulations in the same way, and cannot be explained by a single factor.

Although the qualitative distinction between monotonic and non-monotonic state-traces is conceptually clear, in practice, data are noisy, different numbers of measurements may be made in each condition, and data may only explore a small subregion of the space defined by the two outcome variables. For these reasons, statistics are needed to assess the degree to which the data used to produce a state trace plot actually provide evidence for or against monotonicity. For example, the state-traces in  Fig. 2 b and c were both generated from an underlying set of means that are monotonically related ( Fig. 2 a), but due to measurement variability,  Fig. 2 b appears monotonic while  Fig. 2 c does not. Recent work has investigated both frequentist ( Kalish, Dunn, Burdakov, & Sysoev, 2016 ) and Bayesian ( Davis-Stober, Morey, Gretton, & Heathcote, 2016; Prince, Brown, & Heathcote, 2012 ) methods for computing the degree to which a set of data yield a (non-)monotonic state-trace. As we discuss further below, however, extant frequentist methods have trouble incorporating information about different sources of uncertainty, and extant Bayesian methods are designed to deal primarily with binomial data. Although this can be done via maximum likelihood methods to obtain a point estimate, we advocate using Bayesian methods to obtain an entire distribution over the joint parameter space. The present application uses “Stan” for this purpose ( Carpenter et al., 2017 ), which estimates the joint posterior distribution by drawing a large number of representative  samples  of parameters from that distribution. Our method is based on the same mechanism behind Gaussian process regression (GPR;  Rasmussen & Williams, 2006 ), namely, the use of a kernel function to define the covariance between observations. As illustrated in  Fig. 4  (panels A and B), the kernel leads to an implicit distribution over functions that relate a set of predictor variables to an outcome variable. Our method differs from most applications of GPR in that the variables under consideration are not divided into “predictors” and “outcomes”, but instead are all treated as random variables. As such, our method is concerned with the joint distribution of functions that relate the different outcome measures, as shown in panel C of  Fig. 4 . Also as in GPR, a differentiable kernel leads to an implicit distribution over derivatives of those functions (panels D and E of  Fig. 4 ), of which our method is concerned with the joint distribution (panel F of  Fig. 4 ).

Although the problem of state-trace analysis is usually posed with reference to distinguishing between single-factor accounts and multiple-factor accounts, it is also possible to have a “zero-factor” account of a state-trace. This zero-factor account takes the form of a point and would result if, for example, there was no effect of any condition on any outcome measure. Obviously, experimentalists try to avoid this scenario, but it occurs often enough in both experimental and observational studies. A point state-trace should certainly not be considered to support a multiple-factor model.

Our statistical model is built around the assumption that the relationships between different outcome measures are  latent , manifesting in an inferred covariance structure, and are not observed directly. It is, therefore, important to know what relationships remain plausible in these “terra incognita”. One way to address this is to use prior knowledge to stake out particular points that any reasonable relationship may be hypothesized to include. In the case of this particular experiment, we might hypothesize that any relationship between RB and II should eventually converge at the point (1, 1), which corresponds to the claim that if performance is perfect in one task, it is also perfect in the other. Additionally, a reasonable hypothesis could have any relation pass through (0.25, 0.25), the point where both tasks are at chance-level performance.

These hypotheses can be incorporated in the analysis by simply adding hypothetical data to each participant that conforms to those hypotheses. Thus, for each participant we create two new hypothetical blocks of 80 trials, one in which they are correct on exactly 20 trials, corresponding to chance performance prior to learning, and another in which they are correct 100% of the time, reflecting an asymptotic level of performance (perhaps after many many blocks of learning). The constraint imposed by these hypothetical blocks is a “soft” one, in that they are still treated like potentially noisy data, but this ensures that the actually observed data (as opposed to the hypothetical data) still exerts a strong influence on the analysis. This example has showcased some technical aspects of our analysis, namely the use of a binomial likelihood, a mixed design, and an informed prior on monotonicity. We have also shown how hypothetical data can be used to encourage certain kinds of inferred relationships based on prior knowledge. Across this example, we have tried to emphasize not only how this analysis can yield conclusions from existing data, but how it can help drive the collection of new data that resolve ambiguities about the underlying relationships between outcome variables and help explore the space of possible outcomes.

Obtaining an estimate of the amount of evidence a given data set provides for or against monotonicity of its underlying data generating process is challenging. We have presented a model for estimating the statistic  
                         
                            
                               M 
                            
                            
                               ˆ 
                            
                         
                       which quantifies how much evidence a set of data provides for whether two (or more) outcome measures are monotonically related, and can therefore be explained in terms of a single underlying factor or dimension. This model uses a Gaussian process representation of the underlying relationship between the two outcome measures, with this process parameterized by a covariance kernel. The kernel allows for joint estimation of the means of each outcome measure in each experimental condition as well as their derivatives with respect to one another. In a series of simulations, we showed that  
                         
                            
                               M 
                            
                            
                               ˆ 
                            
                         
                       is capable of discriminating between situations where the state-trace is and is not monotonic, and is sensitive to the degree of uncertainty inherent in finite data. In our supplemental materials, we provide model code using the R ( R Core Team, 2017 ) interface to the Stan language ( Carpenter et al., 2017 ) so that researchers can compute  
                         
                            
                               M 
                            
                            
                               ˆ 
                            
                         
                       using data from between-subjects, within-subjects, and mixed designs.

The kernel-based method for computing  
                         
                            
                               M 
                            
                            
                               ˆ 
                            
                         
                       is widely applicable across different experimental designs and kinds of data and, using Stan ( Carpenter et al., 2017 ), can be done in a matter of minutes on modern personal computers, particularly if parallelization is available (all computations in this paper were done on a typical modern laptop with 8 cores). Our methods are also not restricted to only a particular type of data, such as real-valued or proportional outcomes. Finally, by estimating candidate functions that relate the two sets of outcome measures, our methods can help guide future research toward areas of the joint outcome space that are associated with greater uncertainty.

Having established the technical details of how  
                            
                               
                                  M 
                               
                               
                                  ˆ 
                               
                            
                          is computed and examined how it behaves across a wide range of simulations and an example, we take this time to situate  
                            
                               
                                  M 
                               
                               
                                  ˆ 
                               
                            
                          within the burgeoning fields of statistical methods for state-trace analysis.

Despite the apparent complexity of the analysis model, the underlying linear form of the regression model (Eqs.  (1) and (2) ) and the use of kernel-based Gaussian processes to model the latent covariance between outcome measures mean that posterior sampling can be done very efficiently, particularly if parallelization is available. This is in contrast to certain Bayesian ( Prince et al., 2012 ) and frequentist ( Kalish et al., 2016 ) methods which are often rather slow, owing to a need in these approaches to search and/or sample over a large space of possible latent orders (which grows factorially with the number of conditions). Sampling/search can be much more efficient in a continuous parameter space with a meaningful distance metric, as demonstrated both by our approach and by the efficient Laplace approximation developed by  Davis-Stober et al. (2016)  to obtain Bayes factors in state-trace analysis.

An important consequence of our model structure is that it is dependent on the scale of the data, in other words, it cares more about big differences between conditions than small differences. This is a contrast to the non-parametric, qualitative logic behind state-trace analysis, as described in the Introduction. However, some degree of scale dependence is unavoidable in any statistical setting for assessing goodness of fit—there has to be a way to determine how close something is to be considered “good”. This kind of basic scale dependence applies at the level of individual conditions, e.g., what are credible underlying parameters that could give rise to the observed data in that condition. Our model, like any regression model (e.g.,   Kalish et al., 2016 ), has an additional scale dependence in that the scale of differences  between  conditions also matters—a difference between conditions that violates monotonicity will not matter as much if those conditions have similar means than if they have distant means.

As described above, our model was developed to address problems in which the covariance between conditions is  latent  due to the fact that not all outcomes in all conditions are observed for every individual. This tactic is similar to other regression-based approaches to state-trace analysis ( Kalish et al., 2016 ), but is different from Bayesian approaches which focus on the level of individuals (but for alternative approaches to group-level analysis, see   Davis-Stober et al., 2016 ). As shown above, a focus on the group level can obscure important features of individuals, but of course there is danger inherent in any kind of averaging; even averaging across trials  within  an individual might obscure important features of their data (e.g., trial order or learning effects). As noted above, careful inspection of data prior to analysis seems to us the best way to avoid the pitfalls of averaging. And, of course, there is nothing precluding a researcher from applying our model separately to each individual.

Perhaps the greatest difficulty in comparing different statistical approaches to state-trace analysis is the fact that different methods yield very different kinds of outputs that are suitable for different kinds of inferences. Frequentist regression methods take a hypothesis-testing approach, where if the data are sufficiently unlikely under the best-fitting monotonic model, then monotonicity is rejected ( Kalish et al., 2016 ). This approach is, of course, subject to problems inherent in all null-hypothesis testing, especially the fact that such methods cannot  affirm  monotonicity, only fail to reject it; and that the hypothesis can be rejected for reasons other than the failure of monotonicity (e.g., measurement error). Although our exposition has focused on cases with just two outcome measures – as is typical of most extant applications of state-trace analysis – ourmodel is not restricted to just two outcome variables. As more outcome variables are included, however, two difficulties are encountered, one technical and the other conceptual. The technical difficulty arises from the fact that the prior probability of joint monotonicity plummets rapidly as more outcome variables are analyzed. Thus, it seems to us that situations with a large number of outcome variables all but require the imposition of partial order constraints to focus the analysis on subregions of the joint outcome space that are likely to be informative. The conceptual difficulty arises from trying to understand what it means for a large number of outcome variables to be jointly monotonic. If they are jointly monotonic, the underlying mapping between the latent factor and each outcome variable is likely to be complex; and if they are not jointly monotonic, it is still possible for  subsets  of outcome variables (e.g., individual pairs) to be jointly monotonic. In any event, these two difficulties regarding multiple outcome variables have more to do with interpreting rather than implementing the analysis and can only be surmounted by careful thought and expertise specific to the application domain.

Another potential realm of future work can focus on the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the estimated kernels that describe the covariance between outcome measures. Just as principal components analysis can yield evidence regarding the number of dimensions needed to reconstruct a data matrix ( Jolliffe, 2002 ), it may be possible to use the eigenvalue spectra of each kernel to decide how many latent dimensions are required to describe the covariance between a set of outcome measures, thus providing evidence not just about the adequacy of a single dimension (i.e., a monotonic state trace) but of  how many  dimensions (if greater than one) might be required. Unfortunately, analytic expressions of the eigenvalue spectrum of kernel functions are available only under highly restricted conditions, necessitating numeric approximations. Still, we expect this to be a fruitful avenue for future work, with the model presented here being an important first step along that route.

It may be that multiple causal factors are actually impinging upon the data, but either their effects are small or they are not strongly affected by experimental manipulations, such that recourse to multiple factors provides no explanatory value for a given set of data. On the other hand, a non-monotonic state trace does not indicate how many factors are needed to explain the data – although it is at least two – nor does it say what those factors might be. In all these cases, it remains incumbent upon researchers to use their own domain expertise to provide a theoretically-motivated explanation for the results of any state-trace analysis. The present work helps to make such analysis accessible and to provide statistical tools with which a researcher can properly express their confidence in the results of the analysis.
