Although the new representation based on conditional additivity is proposed in a tentative spirit, it has features that I hope  will interest others who are concerned with subjective probability judgment and measurement. For  example, the new theory tolerates cyclic patterns, such as A > B, B > C, C > A, and it allows dependent reversals such as A >  B and B U C > A U C when C r) (A U B) = 0. Third, there may be some interest in considering the new theory in a normative light. For some, its normative weakness will be  its failure to prescribe properties that are traditionally identified with rational judgment, such as transitivity. There is  also the ever-present danger of adopting unconventional behaviors as normative, even though they can be replicated  experimentally, due to the prevalence of framing effects that can arise in elicitation (Kahneman, Slovic, & Tversky, 1982).  Nevertheless, I hope that the proposal set forth below will stimulate further discussion on the normative side.  The central feature of the new representation that facilitates characterization of "unconventional" behaviors is  nonseparability of events A and B in the numerical function. The basic conventional theory represents A > B by P(A) - P(B) >  0, with separable additivity between A and B on the left-hand side of the inequality. The new theory represents A > B by p(A,  B) > 0, where p is a real valued function on the ordered pairs in V X .V. The nonseparability of A and B in p reflects the  possibility that comparative judgments between events may involve dependencies that cannot be captured by a separable  representation. Special cases in which p exhibits separability will be discussed later.  The precise nature of the new representation will be set forth in Definition 1 in Section 2, which then explores algebraic and  interpretive aspects of the representation. Plausible examples of judgments that are compatible with this representation but  not with conventional representations are discussed in Section 3. Section 4 presents axioms for (V, >) that are necessary and  sufficient for the conditionally additive representation, and compares these to conventional axioms. Section 5 then comments  on separable versions of p which presume that > and its symmetric complement - are transitive. The most plausible examples of violations of transitivity involve multidimensional events and a series of comparisons in which  one event is clearly more likely on all but one dimension than the other event. If the person judges the former event as more  probable in each comparison, it will be possible to construct cyclic > patterns.  For a specific example, let (x, y, z, w) denote the event that, during the year 1995 in a particular state in the Northeastern  United States,  at least x inches of rain will fall, unemployment will be at least y percent, the population will exceed z million people, the  state sales tax will be at least w percent.  Four potential events of this type are A=(10,11,10,7), B=(11,8, 11, 8),  C = (12, 9, 8, 9), D = (13, 10, 9, 6).  In each pair of the ABCDA cycle, namely (A, B), (B, C), (C, D), and (D, A), the first event is clearly more probable than the  second on three of the four dimensions.
