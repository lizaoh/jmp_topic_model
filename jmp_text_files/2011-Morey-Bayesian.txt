Human memory is often described as being composed of separate systems ( Atkinson & Shiffrin, 1968; James, 1890 ). One of these systems, called working memory (WM), is the memory system which allows for conscious storage and recall of information, within the span of several seconds ( Baddeley & Hitch, 1974; Cowan, 2001 ). The use of working memory is fundamental to many everyday tasks, including reading, driving, having conversations, and reasoning. As an example, consider that when you read you only read a few words at a time. As you read a paragraph, you must temporarily store words, word order, and ideas, and piece the words and ideas together in a coherent manner. Without the ability to temporarily store and manipulate information, reading would be impossible.

The storage component of working memory is often thought of as a buffer that allows for the rehearsal and manipulation of information from a variety of sources, including sensory stores or long-term memory. Working memory capacity is known to be extremely limited.  Miller (1956)  estimated the average capacity of human working memory as seven items; more recently  Cowan (2001)  has estimated average capacity at about four items. The limit of working memory capacity is a fundamental aspect of our conscious experience, which is governed by the fact that we apprehend only a small proportion of the information gathered by our senses. The ability to measure participants’ individual WM capacity is at the center of several important areas of research in psychology. For instance, WM capacity is correlated with reading comprehension ( Daneman & Carpenter, 1980 ) and measures of aptitude ( Cowan et al., 2005 ). WM capacity estimates are central to the research claiming that WM resources are modality general rather than separate ( Morey & Cowan, 2005 ). Measurement models of WM capacity have also been used to argue for discrete, all-or-none representation of items in WM ( Rouder et al., 2008b ). For each of these areas of research, the measurement of WM capacity is a critical part of the endeavor. In this paper, I will review current methods for estimating working memory capacity, and some difficulties with these methods. I will then present a hierarchical model which does not suffer from these difficulties, and show that the hierarchical model recovers parameters well in simulation. Following the simulations, I will apply the hierarchical models to the data set of  Rouder et al. (2008b) , showing that in a visual array change detection task, attention to the task is highly correlated with capacity, and different colors vary in the guessing and encoding biases they instantiate.

In the working memory literature, there are a number of conceptions of working memory. They largely fall into two classes: resource-based models ( Alvarez & Cavanagh, 2004; Bays & Husain, 2008 ) and item-based models ( Cowan, 2001; Luck & Vogel, 1997 ). The difference between the two viewpoints is whether they treat the item as the atomic unit of working memory capacity. In item-based models, each item takes up the same amount of space regardless of the number of features the item may have. Items can be thought of like physical objects that take up discrete slots in memory, and storage is either all-or-none; partial storage does not occur. In a resource-based model, properties of items, such as the number of features, are primary. Complex items may take up more storage in working memory than simple items, and partial storage (or a change in resolution of storage) may occur. In this paper, I will address the item-based models of  Cowan (2001)  and  Rouder et al. (2008b) . The item-based view is assumed throughout this manuscript.

The most common method of estimating WM capacity is by means of the change detection task. In a typical change detection task, a number of stimuli are presented for study. After a brief interval, the stimuli are presented again, either exactly the same as at study, or with one item different. The participant’s task is to detect the change and respond with either “same” or “different”. The visual array paradigm shown in  Fig. 1 
                      (left) is one such popular change detection task ( Luck & Vogel, 1997; Phillips, 1974; Wheeler & Treisman, 2002 ). The stimuli are colored squares, one of which may change color from the study to the test array. Because the entire array is displayed at test, we may call this design the  full-display  design.

Cowan (2001)  noted that in some experimental designs, Pashler’s formula for estimating working capacity is inappropriate. Consider the paradigm shown in  Fig. 1 (right). A study array is briefly presented, and after a retention interval a single item from the test array is presented. This item may either be the same color as, or a different color from, the item in the study array that occupied the same position. The participant responds either “same” or “different”. This paradigm is similar to the full-display paradigm, except that here only one item is shown at test, instead of the full array. This paradigm may be called the  single-cue  paradigm to distinguish it from the full-display paradigm.

However, both models suffer from distortions in the estimates of effects when some participants perform at ceiling or floor. For example,  Morey and Cowan (2005)  report that a concurrent verbal load had an effect on visual WM capacity estimates at set size 6 and 8, but not at set size 4. Performance at set size 4 was very close to ceiling, indicating that most participants had a capacity over 4. The set size 4 condition was thus not as sensitive to the verbal load manipulation. The average effect of the load is distorted by the lack of effect in set size 4. An ANOVA would report an interaction although the interaction occurs for a trivial reason, and only for some participants.

One solution is to avoid ceiling or floor performance altogether, but in practice this is difficult. It is often desirable to use a variety of set sizes, including small set sizes where many participants will be at ceiling ( Rouder et al., 2008b ). Even for larger set sizes participants are variable: for most set sizes used in working memory experiments, at least some participants will be at ceiling. In addition, manipulations which might reduce overall capacity, such as dual-task manipulations, force the worst performing participants near floor if the manipulations have even a moderate effect. The result is the distortion of the observed effect sizes.

The assumptions of ANOVA include the assumption that the errors for each observation are normal with equal variance. ANOVA is robust to moderate violations of this assumption, but the assumption can be violated in several ways in traditional analyses of capacity estimates. Observed hit and false alarm rates are distributed as binomial random variables. For larger sample sizes, these binomial distributions will be approximately normal. However, the Pashler formula, being a nonlinear combination binomials, will not in general yield approximately normal estimates.

In addition, neither formula guarantees normality for smaller sample sizes, or equality of variance. The variance of the estimates depends on several factors, including the sample size in a cell, the true hit rate, and the true false alarm rate. If these factors differ across cells, which they surely will, the assumptions of the ANOVA are violated, possibly leading to inflated Type I and Type II error rates.

In both models, if the observed false alarm rate exceeds the hit rate, the capacity estimate will be negative. Negative capacity estimates have no meaning, and it is not clear how to handle negative capacity estimates in practice. Setting negative capacity estimates to 0 is one option, but this will introduce another source of bias into the analysis. The capacity estimate for a participant in any set size is limited by the set size itself. Even if a participant has a capacity of 5, the maximum capacity estimate in a set size 4 condition will be 4. This is reasonable; if a participant scores perfectly in a set size 4 condition, it is unknown whether the participant has a capacity of exactly 4, or 20. However, suppose we impose a secondary memory load that reduces capacity for all participants  below ceiling  by 1 unit, and measure the perfect-scoring participant again. If we measure the previously perfect-performing participant’s capacity at 3.5 under the load, we can estimate their capacity in the no-load condition as 4.5, despite never actually observing it directly. In this way, we can make efficient use of information.

Due to the distortion caused by performance at ceiling, the manipulation affected our participant by half a unit instead of 1 unit. If we simply compute a capacity estimate for each participant in both conditions and subtract, the effect size will be distorted. However, if we remove the participant to mitigate the distortion, we are throwing away information. The typical practice of computing capacity estimates for all cells will either be subject to distortion or inefficient use of data.

Another way that the standard analysis uses information inefficiently is that each set size is treated separately. However, the model’s explicit assumption is that each participant has a single, fixed capacity in each condition. By computing a separate capacity estimate for each set size, many variable estimates of a single parameter are obtained, instead of a single estimate. The assumption of a single, fixed capacity was found by  Rouder et al. (2008b)  to fit visual array change detection data well; it therefore seems reasonable to use the assumption to reduce the variability of capacity estimates.

Rouder et al. used non-hierarchical techniques to fit the single-cue model to data from a visual array experiment and found that it fit change-detection data well: for 20/23 participants, the model with an added lapse parameter could not be rejected by a nested log-likelihood test against a fully-parameterized model. Specifically, they found that estimates of capacity were not sensitive to a manipulation of display-change probability, and estimates of guessing were not sensitive to a manipulation of set size. This led Rouder et al. to conclude that with the addition of a lapse parameter, item-based models capture the overall patterns in the data.

Given the success and widespread use of the item-based estimates of WM capacity, a hierarchical Bayesian model offering estimation using working memory capacity models would be a step forward for researchers. In hierarchical models, all participants in all conditions are modeled simultaneously, which uses all the information in the data efficiently. This solves several problems above, including extreme volatility caused by low sample sizes in cells and inefficient use of information across conditions and set sizes. A Bayesian approach would offer an alternative to frequentist approaches, yielding parameter posterior distributions and the possibility of Bayesian inferential techniques, which are becoming increasingly popular ( Wagenmakers, 2007 ). In the following section, I develop a hierarchical model of working memory capacity. Following its development, I show that it performs well in simulation, and then apply the model to the data of  Rouder et al. (2008b) .

Having demonstrated the weaknesses of the traditional approach using Pashler’s and Cowan’s formulas, I propose a solution which does not suffer from the problems outlined in the previous section. The hierarchical model correctly accounts for nonlinearities in the models; the hierarchical model does not return meaningless negative estimates of capacity; the model efficiently uses all the information available, including low set sizes; the model incorporates the correct binomial error model; and, the model includes a lapse parameter to account for misfit at low set sizes.

Having demonstrated the hierarchical model’s ability to estimate WM capacity in simulation, I will now show how the hierarchical model may be used to explore empirical data.

Rouder et al. (2008b)  presented data from a single-cue change detection task, arguing that the good fit of the item-based model with participant lapsing added provided evidence for the all-or-none, item-based encoding assumption that underlies  Pashler ’s ( 1988 ) and  Cowan ’s ( 2001 ) formulas. Rouder et al. ’s goal was not to provide a deep analysis of the data set, but there a number of interesting questions that may be explored using the data set. In this reanalysis, I will focus on the relationship of participants’ capacities to their lapse parameter and the effects of color on guessing and capacity.

Working memory includes more than simply the passive storage of information. Although theories differ, models of the WM system typically include both an executive component and a storage component ( Baddeley & Hitch, 1974 ;  Cowan, 1995 ;  Engle & Kane, 2004 ). The hierarchical models developed here include parameters that correspond roughly to separate executive processes and storage processes. The  
                         K 
                       parameter is strictly a storage parameter, indexing the amount of spare storage available for the representation of the  
                         N

                         Z

It seems reasonable to identify the lapsing parameter with the executive element of working memory, due the plausible relationship of lapsing with goal maintenance and focusing attention. One interesting question is whether participants’ capacities correlate with how often they lapse. The hierarchical model is a natural way to answer this question.

Rouder et al. (2008b)  asked 23 participants to perform 540 trials in a visual array task like described earlier in  Fig. 1 (right). The experiment was divided into 9 blocks of 60 trials each. The set size (2, 5 or 8 colored squares) was manipulated within each block. The proportion of change trials (30%, 50%, or 70%) was manipulated between blocks. On each trial the participants were instructed to respond with key presses corresponding to “same” or “change”, depending on whether they believed the display changed from study to test. Further details may be found in  Rouder et al. (2008b) .

After fitting both models, it may be desirable to select one of the two models according to model fit. One way of quantifying the relative fit of two models is by comparing penalized log-likelihood values. The deviance information criterion (DIC;  Spiegelhalter, Best, Carlin, & van der Linde, 2002 ) is a penalized-likelihood criterion appropriate for hierarchical models. The deviance (−2 times the log-likelihood) is used as a measure of fit — the lower the deviance, the better the model fit. The DIC is calculated by adding the number of effective parameters, estimated from the MCMC chain to the deviance, penalizing more flexible models. Models with higher parsimony and better model fits will have lower DICs, and thus be preferred.

The DIC for Model 2 is 9528, a substantial improvement over the DIC of 9695 for Model 1.  Fig. 10

On average, it appears that brighter colors lead to higher guessing rates.

A separate analysis confirms that black is the outlier; recoding the studied color as either “black” or “non-black” and refitting Model 2 yields a DIC of 9529, only 1 point higher than the DIC of Model 2. The near-equivalent DIC of Model 2 and the recoded black-vs-non-black model suggest that black is somehow different from the other colors.

The hierarchical model provides evidence for two claims: first, participants who lapse less also have higher working memory capacity; and second, there are substantial differences between squares of different colors in both guessing and their ability to be remembered.

The fact that the correlation appears to be fairly high is perhaps most consistent with models in which the executive and storage features of working memory arise from a common source, such as Cowan’s embedded processes model ( Cowan, 1999 ). The large correlation is less consistent (though not strictly inconsistent) with models in which the executive aspects of working memory arise from separate sources, such as Baddeley’s multicomponent model ( Baddeley & Hitch, 1974 ) or other models separating attention and storage (e.g.  Engle, Tuholski, Laughlin, & Conway, 1999 ).

If the correlation is to be taken as evidence for or against working memory theories, it is reasonable to ask whether the correlation is an artifact of the model. The lapse parameter and the capacity parameter both serve a similar function in the multinomial tree model; perhaps their correlation reflects the fact that it is difficult to tell the difference between capacity and lapsing. There are several reasons why this is likely not the source of the correlation. First, in simulation, the model was able to recover the values of both the population mean lapse parameter and the population mean capacity parameter, even though relatively uninformative priors were placed on both. Second, if the model had difficulties distinguishing the two parameters, one would expect a  negative  correlation between effects on  
                            Z 
                          and effects on  
                            K 
                         . For any given data, a lower attention parameter would require a higher capacity if the data are to be accounted for. A third reason why the correlation is probably not an artifact of the model is that not all effects on lapse and capacity show high positive correlations. In addition to the correlation between capacity and lapse parameters, the data also provided evidence that different items induce different guessing and are remembered at different rates. Regarding the guessing differences, it seems reasonable that different colors may be more salient against the gray background, and a change from gray to a brighter color may bias a participant to believe a change occurred. The capacity differences are potentially more interesting. Although the effects of color were described as being effects on “capacity”, they are actually effects on the probability of being encoded. In the item-based model that serves as the basis for the hierarchical model, capacity alone determines the probability that an item is encoded. If some items are preferentially encoded over others, capacity for those items will appear larger. All stimulus effects on capacity should be interpreted with this fact in mind. In the analysis of the Rouder et al. data, black squares appear to be encoded at a much higher rate than other stimuli. It may be that in the study array, black squares attract attention more readily, and thus obtain an advantage in encoding. Although this could explain the encoding advantage for the color black, it is difficult to see how the hypothesis explaining the guessing effects, and an “attention grabbing” hypothesis explaining the advantage of the color black, can be true simultaneously. If the onset of a bright color is more readily interpreted as a change, it should also be more attention grabbing. Further research, perhaps including eye-tracking, is needed to assess the evidence for these hypotheses.

In the foregoing sections, I have presented a hierarchical model of working memory capacity, shown that it performs well in simulation, and used it to analyze  Rouder et al. ’s ( 2008b ) empirical data. The model performs significantly better than the current methodology used to estimate working memory capacity, and enables the exploration of research questions, such as correlations between capacity and lapsing and the effects of color, that would otherwise be difficult.

The hierarchical model has a number of advantages over the competitors. First, the model is hierarchical. The hierarchical nature of the model allows for the efficient use of information across many conditions and participants. Even if there is a single observation in each cell, which is the case when the data is broken down finely, the model is able to estimate parameters where traditional techniques would yield biased, volatile, or meaningless capacity estimates. Second, the model is Bayesian. Bayesian approaches to modeling and inference have steadily gained popularity in recent decades, in part due to criticisms of more traditional frequentist methods ( Wagenmakers, 2007 ). The model described herein allows WM researchers who resonate with the critiques of traditional methods to abandon  
                         p 
                       values for inferences in the analysis of change detection tasks. Third, the model performs well in simulation. The simulations described above show that the hierarchical model is able to recover parameters well, without the bias inherent in traditional techniques. Finally, the model is easy to fit, even for researchers who are not specialists in hierarchical Bayesian methodology. A graphical user interface is provided at  http://drsmorey.org/research/rdmorey . The GUI program, called WOMMBAT (WOrking Memory Modeling using Bayesian Analysis Techniques) is built using the R statistical environment  R Development Core Team (2009)  to provide an SPSS-like interface for building models. One may also design experiments such to avoid the problem, by either avoiding near-ceiling performance or avoiding the full-display design altogether. Designing experiments with only high set sizes will avoid near ceiling performance, and has the benefit of making the model as a whole easily estimable. The disadvantage, however, is that the lapse parameter is no longer estimable. In this case, it is possible to set the lapse parameter to a value seen in other experiments, as was done in the simulations described previously. Avoiding the full-display design in favor of the single-cue design is also a viable alternative. If the assumptions of the item-based model of working memory hold, both designs will allow estimation of capacity. Unless the assumptions of the item-based model itself are in question, or there is no way of adapting a paradigm to the single cue design, using the single cue-design is the simpler choice. The model is more well-behaved with single-cue data and offers the ability to easily estimate the lapse parameter if low set sizes are included.

If the full-display design with participants near ceiling cannot be avoided, the alternative to the three approaches above is to run very long MCMC chains and to aggressively thin them until they converge. Because of the extreme discontinuities in the model, hybrid Monte Carlo is difficult to apply. The model fitting software available from the author’s website allows traditional random-walk Metropolis–Hastings ( Hastings, 1970 ) to be used instead, along with thinning options for long MCMC runs.

The model presented in this paper allows WM researchers to estimate working memory capacity in a principled, efficient manner. The method substantially outperforms current methods of estimating working memory in both efficiency and bias, and it is hoped that the availability of an easy-to-use graphical interface will spur the use of the hierarchical model. For working memory researchers who have had little or no exposure to hierarchical Bayesian methods, the hierarchical model presented here may serve as an introduction to the advantages of the hierarchical Bayesian approach.
