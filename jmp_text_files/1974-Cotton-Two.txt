COTTON  University of California, Santa Barbara, California 93106  This paper examines some mathematical implications of two process models of concept identification, the 1-element strategy  selection model with a local consistency assumption and Chumbley's hypothesis manipulation (HM) model. Under slightly  restrictive assumptions, each process model (Level 1) is shown to imply a stochastic model (Level 3), making predictions of  behavior in experimental situations in which the stimulus presented to a subject on any trial is randomly selected and  independent of that presented on any other trial. In addition, each model is shown to make predictions at an intermediate  level (Level 2) about performance on successive trials with specific stimulus sequences presented.  At Level 2, each model is shown to falsely predict zero probabilities for particular response patterns when stated stimulus  sequences are used. Fewer such problems arise with the HM model than with the 1-element model. Minimum squared error fits of  one set of experimental data show relatively good correspondence of predictions and observations when a Chumbley model with  different saliences for different hypotheses is employed.  This paper is intended to extend existing knowledge about the implications of two well-known concept identification models.  Each model includes a provision for memory of hypotheses confirmed or disconfirmed on an immediately previous trial (in  addition to eventual learning of the solution); the present article shows the degree to which predicted behavior depends upon  the similarity between the previous trial and the present trial. Respects in which these predictions are inaccurate are  indicated. In addition, predictions and observations averaged across stimuli are compared. Both models may be called  hypothesis models or strategy selection models, i.e., models in which a subject tries possible solutions to the concept  identification task, eventually settling on the correct one. If his  response is correct, he continues to use that hypothesis for the next trial; if his response is incorrect, then he resamples  (with replacement) from the pool of hypotheses." This may be termed a win-stay, lose-shift procedure because the subject never  changes a hypothesis which has led to success and always changes a hypothesis which has led to an error.  Gregg and Simon (1967) call the Restle model a process model, one among a family of such models. This first theme of the  present paper is that a closely related model (the local consistency model which Gregg and Simon name the P2 mode3), in  addition to being a process model (Level 1), implies a stochastic model (Level 3) under certain conditions, and implies an  intervening stimulus-specific stochastic model (Level 2). Later in this paper we develop a comparable theme for Chumbley's  Hypothesis Manipulation (HM) model.  The idea of levels of theory appears fairly frequently in psychology: the present specification of three levels is obviously  an expansion of the 2-level process versus stochastic model categorization developed by Gregg and Simon. Greeno and Bjork  (1973, p. 105) question whether stochastic models are different in kind from process models. The present distinction is a move  toward specification of a variety of smaller gradations among theories, with fundamental processes being operative at Level 1  and all other phenomena of the theory being derivable from Level 1. However, it must be emphasized that higher levels of  theory will be useful for certain purposes not immediately served by Level 1 analysis. Thus an investigator interested in  trial-bytrial probability of correct response as a function of specific stimulus sequences may want to begin theoretical  analysis at Level 2, where such phenomena are immediately discussed; and an investigator interested in trial by-trial  probability of correct response, averaged across a number of stimulus sequences, may wish to begin analysis at Level 3. The  reader may wish to make a mental note to himself that no new assumptions, other than equating some or all of the salience  values, about behavior are introduced as one moves from Level 1 to Level 2 or Level 3. Thus the multilevel theory discussed in  this paper is different in kind from Batchelder's (1970, 1971) multilevel theory, a theory which says that people either learn  by paired associate or concept processes and sometimes shift from the former to the latter. EMPIRICAL INADEQUACY OF THE 1-ELEMENT LOCAL CONSISTENCY MODEL  Testing the Level 3 Theory  Bower and Trabasso (1964) and later investigators have given support to many features of the Level 3 model represented in Eqs.  (13) and (14). The principal unsettled questions are whether the parameters p = I (probability of correct guessing) and c =  a,,,,, (probability of conditioning immediately following an error) are correct. Gregg and Simon (1967, Tables 1 and 2) have  compared data from six experiments by Bower and Trabasso (1964) to the predictions of four variants of the B and T model.  Estimated p values never deviated more that .023 from the theoretical value, .500. However, only three out of nine  subexperiments gave any indications of conformity to the 1-element local consistency model with equal saliences (the P2 model,  in Gregg and Simon's notation) with respect to predicted average errors per subject, X, and thus of the learning parameter, c,  estimated as 1 f X. Usually the P2 model predicted faster learning than occurred.  This raises severe questions about the adequacy of the equal salience model in question. One could argue that the c estimates  are distorted by memory effects other than those implied by local consistency, but to do so is simply to point to a new  deficiency in the model rather than to excuse existing ones. One could also claim that these estimates are affected by  confabulated irrelevant dimensions, or by conjunctive or other complex hypotheses. Such an assertion is more plausible, but  must cope with the fact that thorough pretraining and directions about acceptable hypotheses were given by Bower and Trabasso.  Existing data do not seem adequate to indicate whether the more general 1-element local consistency model with unequal  saliences is able to represent concept identification behavior under specified conditions. PROPERTIES OF CHUMBLEY'S HYPOTHESIS MANIPULATION MODEL  Development of Level 3 Theory from Chumbley's Assumptions  Chumbley (1969) has developed the hypothesis manipulation (HM) model as a quantification of a theory proposed by Levine  (1966). This model is the Restle (1962) all-strategies-at-once model with an added assumption that, with probability t, a  subject making an error will retrieve and retain only the hypotheses held on that trial which were not used but would have led  to a correct response; and, with probability 1 - t, he will return to the full set of possible strategies as in the Restle  model. Following a correct response, the subject keeps all old hypotheses which would have led him to that response but then  chooses the next response by a random process in which the probability of a correct response is the proportion of hypotheses  still held which dictate the correct response to the new stimulus. Once one response is chosen, all strategies (from the old  set) dictating that response constitute the new functional set of hypotheses until feedback requires a change. Levine  reported sizes of hypothesis sets inferred to be the reciprocals of values such as a probability of about. 133 of starting a  criterion run on Trial 1 of a four-dimensional problem and of about .200 of starting a criterion run on Trial 2. Since  orthogonal sets of stimuli from a four-dimensional task were used, we can reason as follows: the only way to start a criterion  run on Trial 1 is to start in State 8H, make a chance success on Trial 1 (Pr = z), automatically move to State 4H, make a  chance success on Trial 2 (Pr = 2), automatically move to State 2H, make a chance success on Trial 3 (Pr = 2), and  automatically move to State 1H. Multiplying the three probabilities yields a joint probability of .125 of starting a criterion  run on Trial 1, close to the empirical value of .133.  If the criterion run is to start on Trial 2, an error must have occurred on Trial 1; if a success had occurred without  starting a criterion run, a criterion run could not begin on Trial 2 either. We want to know the probability of beginning a  criterion run on Trial 2 conditional on having made an error on the previous trial (Levine, 1967, p. 429). Therefore, we need  not compute the probability of an error on Trial 1. Two sequences of events may yield what we desire. (1) Following the Trial  1 error, there is a probability of t that the subject retrieved the set of 4H's which would have led to a correct response,  with Pr = z that a chance success would occur on Trial 2, followed automatically by State 2H, with Pr = 2 that a chance  success would occur on Trial 3 followed automatically by State 1H. This yields a joint probability of t/4 for this sequence.  (2) Following the Trial 1 error there is a probability of 1 - t that the subject stays in State 8H, with Pr = I of a chance  success on Trial 2, followed automatically by a move to State 4H, with Pr = z of a chance success on Trial 3, followed  automatically by State 2H, with Pr = 2 of a chance success on Trial 4, followed automatically by State 1H. This sequence has a  joint probability of (1 - t)/8.  Adding probabilities from (1) and (2) yields (1 + t)/8 as the probability of beginning a criterion run on Trial 2, given that  it has not begun earlier. For t = .60, this probability is the .200 observed (approximately) by Levine. CONCLUDING REMARKS  We have shown that two process models (Level 1) each imply not only a stochastic model applicable to randomly selected stimuli  (Level 3 model) but also a model applicable to a specific stimulus sequence (Level 2 model). Neither the 1-element local  consistency model nor the Chumbley Hypothesis Manipulation (HM) model, with or without an equal salience assumption, is  completely consistent with Cotton's (1971) data obtained for use with Level 2 models, but the latter fits the data well except  for frequent failure to observe predicted zero relative frequencies. We state without elaboration that the data of Falmagne  (1970, Fig. 4), Kenoyer (1972), Levine (1969, Fig. 3), and Restle and Emmerich (1966, Expt III) may also be used to invalidate  these Level 2 models. The first Cotton (1971) sequence-specific model, though working somewhat better at Level 2 and implying  a conventional Level 3 model, is not an hypothesis model at Level 1.  Indow and Suzuki (1972, 1973) have shown that their data can be fitted fairly well by identifying certain subjects whose  introspected strategies conform to the BowerTrabasso model and others who conform to a model equivalent or nearly equivalent  to the HM model with t = 1. Note, however, that their Table 5 shows that the former group of subjects change the stated reason  for their response on 17.9 % or more of trials following a success, apparently contradicting the win-stay assumption used in  the closely related Po model. We emphasize that our calculations to date show that, though Level 3 predictions from the P2 model are frequently inaccurate,  Level 3 predictions and what might be called pseudo-Level 3 predictions from the HM model are more successful.
