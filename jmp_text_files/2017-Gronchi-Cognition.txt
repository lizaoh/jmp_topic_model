A long-standing tradition of psychological research has found a substantial disagreement between classic (Bayesian) probability theory and human judgments ( Kahneman & Frederick, 2005; Kahneman & Tversky, 2000; Tversky & Kahneman, 1974 ). Those observations, together with the strong order and context dependency of human decision making ( Hogarth & Einhorn, 1992; Schwarz & Sudman, 2012 ), have induced several researchers to propose that quantum probability theory could provide a better representation of human thinking when compared to classical models ( Busemeyer & Bruza, 2012; Pothos & Busemeyer, 2013; Wang, Busemeyer, Atmanspacher, & Pothos, 2013 ). Indeed, over the last decade the quantum cognition approach has shown its effectiveness in modeling psychological phenomena; for example, in explaining classical human probability judgment errors, such as conjunction and disjunction errors ( Busemeyer, Pothos, Franco, & Trueblood, 2011 ).

In the development of quantum theory, Bell’s theorem has played a fundamental role ( Wiseman, 2006 ). Using his famous inequalities, Bell demonstrated that quantum models can account for simultaneous correlations between variables that are stronger than any classical prediction. Here, we take advantage of such strong quantum correlations to model the probabilistic reasoning biases observable in human judgments. In the following, we explore the issue of Bell’s inequality violation in the context of human probabilistic reasoning. We developed two quantum models that are able to predict probabilistic judgments and the employment of representativeness heuristic in a Bell’s inequality-based task. In particular, we employed the Wigner–d’Espagnat version of Bell’s inequality ( d’Espagnat, 1979; Harrison, 1982; Stapp, 1979; Wigner, 1970 ) that is easily applicable in the domain of probabilistic judgments.

In 1964, Bell published his famous theorem ( Bell, 1964 ). According to Bell’s theorem, “no physical theory of local hidden variables can ever reproduce all of the predictions of quantum mechanics” ( Parker, 1994 , p. 542).

From an historical point of view, in the 1930s there was an intense philosophical debate over the implications of the Copenhagen interpretation ( Heisenberg, 1930 ) of quantum theory. Within this debate, Albert Einstein, Boris Podolsky and Nathan Rosen proposed that quantum theory was incomplete under the assumptions of locality and realism ( Einstein, Podolsky, & Rosen, 1935 ). In particular, they referred to quantum entanglement, the phenomenon in which pairs of particles must be described as a whole because they interact in such a way that the state of each particle cannot be described independent of the other. Interestingly, since the 1920s, it has become apparent that a single particle of an entangled pair possesses knowledge of what kind of measurement (and what outcome) has been taken out on the other particle even if there is no apparent means for such information to be communicated between them (independent of time and spatial constraints). However, Einstein, Podolsky and Rosen argued that an “element of reality” that is currently unmeasurable (a hidden variable) can account for the entanglement phenomenon. Thus, assuming locality (i.e., an object is influenced directly only by its immediate surroundings) and realism (i.e., what exists in the physical world is logically and conceptually independent by measurement),

In 1964, starting from the same two assumptions held by  Einstein et al. (1935)  (i.e., locality and realism), Bell derived his famous inequality. He demonstrated that the violation of such inequality entails that at least one of the two assumptions must be false ( Bell, 1964 ). This result has stimulated a lot of theoretical (and empirical) research. As a matter of fact, as inspired by the original paper, there are now many different versions of Bell’s inequality. Moreover, in the last 50 years, the complexity of the debate has increased along with many divergences in the interpretation of the problem’s terms.

The original inequality also required the perfect anti-correlation of outcomes. Due to the difficulty of having perfect anti-correlation in an actual experiment,  Clauser, Horne, Shimony, and Holt (1969)  generalized Bell’s results by introducing the CHSH inequality (which does not assume perfect anti-correlations). In subsequent decades, other inequalities related to Bell’s theorem have been proposed by  d’Espagnat (1979) ,  Leggett and Garg (1985) ,  Stapp (1979) , and  Wigner (1970)  among others. Within physics, despite a continuing debate, violations of Bell’s inequality have commonly been considered the ultimate proof of entanglement, and more generally, quantum theory. The violations of such inequalities were first convincingly observed in the experiment performed by  Aspect, Dalibard, and Roger (1982) . Since then, other experiments have confirmed this result, thus supporting quantum theory’s predictions ( Brida, Degiovanni, Genovese, Schettini, Polyakov, & Migdall, 2008; Franson, 1989; Genovese, 2005; Ou & Mandel, 1988 ).

Violations of Bell’s inequalities determine the necessity to appeal to quantum theory in order to formalize psychological phenomena. As far as we know, the first empirical tests of Bell’s inequalities within psychology were performed by  Conte, Khrennikov, Todarello, De Robertis, Federici and Zbilut (2008) . The authors developed a Bell’s inequality-based test employing ambiguous figures but they did not find an actual violation of the inequality. However, this pioneering work provided evidence of a strong link between quantum theory and human cognition. Afterwards,  Asano, Khrennikov, Ohya, Tanaka, and Yamato (2014)  and  Aerts and Sozzo (2014)  empirically proved Bell’s inequality violations in ambiguous figures and concepts combinations, respectively. For example, starting from a quantum mechanics-based theory for modeling and representing concepts combinations ( Aerts & Gabora, 2005a,b ),  Aerts and Sozzo (2014)  investigated how the context influences the typicality of a single exemplar and the applicability of a single property of a concept (the so-called Guppy Effect

Testing Bell’s inequality outside the domain of physics may imply some controversial issues typically associated with the absence of signaling between the measurements. In cognitive experiments, if each measurement (i.e., a judgment, in psychological terms) of the two variables is performed by different subjects that are far apart from each other (ruling out the possibility of any form of communication between them), then the no-signaling condition can be achieved. However, when each measurement of the inequality’s variables is performed by the same individual (as usually occurs in cognitive experiments), this requirement arises in terms of the possibility of signaling between concepts (or, more generally, mental representations). Given the intrinsic difficulty of assessing if mental representations “interact” in classical way or not, this issue is usually neglected by quantum cognition literature.

Yearsley and Pothos (2014)  proposed a new way to think about time, and how mental representations change with it, based on quantum theory. The authors discussed the implications of a non-classical time dependence in cognitive models. The basic idea is that observing a cognitive variable at a specific time will induce uncertainty about its value at other time points. This interpretation challenges the notion that cognitive variables always have well-defined values at all times (although these values can be noisy or not accessible).  Yearsley and Pothos (2014)  referred to  Atmanspacher and Filk (2010)  work which analyzed such time-dependence in the case of bistable perception. Their formal analysis is based on temporal Bell’s inequalities. Temporal Bell’s inequalities concern the correlations of a single system measured at different times, whereas spatial Bell’s inequalities probe entanglement between spatially-separated systems. Thus, in a temporal Bell inequality, we have multiple measurements of a certain physical quantity that are based on a combination of two-time correlation functions. In these terms,  Yearsley and Pothos (2014)  analyzed in these terms a prototypical task in probability reasoning research (the “Linda problem”, see the next section). The key observation is that the violation of the temporal Bell’s inequality entails the impossibility of having a joint probability distribution for the measured value across time points. If this is the case, such a property implies the possibility that two events can be incompatible. Incompatibility (together with entanglement and superposition) is a central concept in the quantum cognition approach and it is commonly represented by non-commutative operators. In the language of quantum theory, when two events are incompatible, the certainty of an event necessarily entails the complete uncertainty of the other. Exploiting this notion,  Busemeyer et al. (2011)  have explained a variety of observed probabilistic reasoning biases.

Recently, Dzhafarov and colleagues ( Dzhafarov & Kujala, 2014; Dzhafarov, Zhang, & Kujala, 2016; Kujala & Dzhafarov, 2014 ) investigated extensively the relevance of Bell’s inequality (in the CHSH form) to psychology and cognitive science. They developed a formal framework for measuring contextuality in systems with deterministic inputs and random outputs. Here, contextuality means that “these random variables cannot be sewn together into a single system of jointly distributed random variables if one assumes that all or some of them preserve their identity across different conditions” ( Kujala & Dzhafarov, 2014 , p. 2). Within quantum physics, it is unanimously recognized that the experimental settings based on Bell’s inequality allow the detection of contextuality ( Kujala & Dzhafarov, 2014 ). In order to determine if behavioral and social systems are characterized by contextuality in the quantum sense, Dzhafarov and colleagues ( Dzhafarov & Kujala, 2014; Dzhafarov et al., 2016; Kujala & Dzhafarov, 2014 ) explored the problem of selective influences (i.e., determining which factor influences only those variables that are expected to be influenced by the research design). The basic idea consists of subtracting the contributions due to a possible signaling among the variables involved in the experiment. To test this theory, they modified the CHSH-based Bell’s inequality by subtracting the marginals for signaling and thus providing a new upper bound in the inequality. Such an approach allows researchers to unequivocally demonstrate contextuality even if there are interactions among the variables. After controlling for selective influence in previous and new empirical data, they did not observe any contextual effect. The authors concluded that the well-known role of context in many psychological phenomena is not equivalent to the contextuality notion developed in quantum theory. Yet, this result does not imply that quantum formalism is not useful or applicable to psychological phenomena ( Dzhafarov et al., 2016 ).

The influential  Heuristics and biases  research program began as the study of the statistical-based intuition of people with expertise in this domain. It was observed that statisticians are overly confident in the repeatability of the results of small samples ( Tversky & Kahneman, 1971 ). In this pioneering research, the authors interpreted their results in terms of the philosophical tradition opposing intuition and thought. Such an idea, following the results obtained in subsequent decades ( Kahneman & Frederick, 2005; Kahneman & Tversky, 2000 ), has evolved to the current dual process theories of thought ( Evans & Stanovich, 2013 ). When reasoning about uncertain events, people can rely on inexpensive and fast heuristics strategies that usually give the correct response but in many cases they may lead to systematic biases of reasoning. In the following, we show that Bell’s inequality, in the Wigner–d’Espagnat form (Eq.  (2) ), can be violated not only in a quantum-mechanics experiment but also in a probabilistic judgment task. Such violation suggests that there can be correlations between cognitive representations that are greater than any classical expectation. Quantum probability theory can provide a useful framework to account for these correlations, either in terms of incompatible events or entanglement. Finally, we investigate how the subadditivity phenomenon may affect the observed results, discussing the unpackaging problem within the proposed model.

In a Wigner–d’Espagnat-based reasoning task, concepts combination is a critical issue. When an individual evaluates the probability of two combined concepts, such an operation, according to the quantum cognition approach ( Busemeyer & Bruza, 2012; Pothos & Busemeyer, 2013; Wang et al., 2013 ), is represented by two projective measurements that in principle can be commutative or not. In the latter case, the variables are incompatible thus inducing extra-correlation in concepts combination. This extra-correlation could lead to a violation of the Wigner–d’Espagnat inequality. Alternatively, if the variables are compatible, then the inequality can be still violated, assuming quantum entanglement between the concepts. Given the intrinsic difficulty of determining a priori the compatibility between concepts, in the following we present two quantum models that are able of describing the violations of the inequality. The first is based on two incompatible measurements of a quantum system, while the second is based on entanglement.

Here, we introduce an alternative quantum model able to describe the violation exploiting the entanglement between two quantum states. In this model, the Hilbert space presented before is duplicated for a number of times corresponding to the number of combined concepts (two, in our experiment). Differing from the original formalism ( Busemeyer et al., 2011 ), the decision process evolves through a sequence of local projections in each Hilbert subspace. This guarantees the commutativity between different projections. Then, the only initial states able to violate the inequality are the entangled compositions of the two Hilbert subspaces. Moreover, the degree of entanglement accounts for the degree of correlation between different events.

Overall, our results may suggest that when people evaluate events in a Wigner–d’Espagnat inequality-based task, correlations between mental representations (i.e., “being a male student” and “playing soccer”) that are higher than what is expected by a classical no-signaling probability theory are possible. Indeed, in order to observe the violations found in Experiment 1, it is necessary to assume a stronger form of correlation between concepts. Quantum theory may be a useful framework to represent such forms of correlation in a natural and parsimonious way. However, our study was limited by the fact that we did not control for possible signaling among variables, as suggested by  Dzhafarov et al. (2016) . In order to determine if quantum contextuality effects are present in the probabilistic reasoning domain, future works should develop analyses similar to those of Dzhafarov and colleagues ( Dzhafarov & Kujala, 2014; Dzhafarov et al., 2016; Kujala & Dzhafarov, 2014 ) for the Wigner–d’Espagnat inequality by testing it empirically and taking into account the marginals for signaling.

Assuming compatibility between mental representations, such strong correlation can be modeled in terms of entanglement ( Aerts & Sozzo, 2014 ). In line with the usual explanation of Linda-based tasks, the violation of the Wigner–d’Espagnat inequality has been achieved through the use of a representativeness criterion. Indeed, in the Italian context (the country in which the experiment was carried out), soccer is a sport commonly practiced by male primary/middle school student and therefore “a male student that plays soccer” is a very representative event. So, a possible interpretation of our results is that the similarity criterion, upon which the representativeness heuristic

In other terms, entanglement may represent a further formal approach to describe representativeness-related notions, such as prototypicality or goodness of example ( Mervis & Rosch, 1981; Osherson et al., 1990; Rips, 1975; Tenenbaum & Griffiths, 2001 ). Interestingly, in the Urn scenario, a similar association between concepts was achieved simply by providing unbalanced statistics about the objects contained in the urn (i.e., many green objects and many square-shaped objects). Although such dimensions were described independently from one other, it can be hypothesized that this kind of description could lead to the formation of a strong mental representation in which a “green square” is the most common and representative item in the urn. As a matter of fact, in the Urn scenario, the correlations between dimensions describing the items were high enough to violate the Wigner–d’Espagnat inequality.

The different results observed in Experiment 1 and in Experiment 2 can be explained to the fact that probabilistic judgments are strongly description-dependent ( Rottenstreich & Tversky, 1997; Sloman et al., 2004; Tversky & Koehler, 1994 ). Indeed, different descriptions of the same event (packed or unpacked) can elicit different subjective probabilities. Therefore, quantum cognition research, when aiming to verify if observations made in quantum physics hold in reasoning and thought, should explicitly investigate the role of different means of representing events.

Overall, our empirical and modeling results show the potential of the quantum framework in formalizing human probabilistic judgments. Further research is needed to better understand if Bell’s inequalities are actually violated in human thinking ( Dzhafarov & Kujala, 2014; Dzhafarov et al., 2016; Kujala & Dzhafarov, 2014 ).
