During a decision task, such as selecting an item to purchase among several available items, a person may invoke cognitive processes to identify a potential response choice. During such deliberation, a person would periodically monitor their progress and accordingly strategize further steps to optimize their subjective success likelihood. Such an ability to monitor and control one’s cognitive processes is called Metacognition. In other words, Metacognition is a dynamic model of the ongoing cognitive processes with the goal of optimizing success likelihood given the cognitive constraints ( Flavell, 1979; Nelson, 1990 ). To predict success likelihood, metacognitive processes construct a dynamic model utilizing ongoing cues such as consistency of evidence supporting a given response choice ( Ackerman, 2019 ). Then, utilizing success likelihood prediction from the dynamic model along with other personal and task-specific objectives (e.g., prioritize speed over accuracy), metacognitive processes indicate adequate cognitive resource allocation to successfully achieve current objectives ( Efklides, 2006; Koriat et al., 2006 ). Utilizing process models to study such a dynamic metacognitive process could also provide further insights into the underlying principles of how metacognition impacts response choice and response time.

Meta-reasoning processes are metacognitive processes associated with Reasoning tasks expected to take Longer Deliberation Time (RLDT) (e.g., see Box 2 in  Ackerman & Thompson, 2017  for a list of such reasoning tasks). Meta-reasoning has been widely studied in both Cognitive Psychology and Artificial Intelligence (AI). In the Cognitive Psychology field, Meta-reasoning studies examine the evolution patterns of metacognition within a trial and how it informs metacognitive control strategies ( Ackerman, 2014; Ackerman & Thompson, 2017; Dotan et al., 2018; Koriat et al., 2006; Metcalfe & Wiebe, 1987; Payne & Duggan, 2011; Rakefet Ackerman, 2017; Thompson & Johnson, 2014; Thompson et al., 2011 ). In the AI field, Meta-reasoning studies examine possible solutions to guide (multiple) agents to be rational about using available computational resources to (collectively) achieve a gain in the odds of success ( Cox et al., 2022; Cox & Raja, 2011; Griffiths et al., 2019; Herrmann, 2023; Richardson & Ball, 2024 ). In Cognitive Psychology, theoretical Meta-reasoning frameworks, such as Metacognitive Reasoning Theory ( Thompson, 2009; Thompson et al., 2011 ), Diminishing Criterion Model ( Ackerman, 2014 ), and Cognizant Confidence hypothesis ( Double & Birney, 2017 ) have been proposed to evaluate the impact of ongoing (or online) metacognition on response choice accuracy and time. However, further studies can be done to identify computational guidelines that may facilitate discussion, comparison, explanation, and evaluation of process models describing empirically observed Meta-reasoning phenomena. Such computational guidelines could also benefit AI reasoning models in evaluating the effectiveness of their reasoning strategy selection mechanism (e.g.,  Gao et al., 2024 ).

Given a mathematical state space, a random walk process transitions from a current state to a next state based on a probability function. The probability function is typically utilized to model a hypothesized underlying dynamic of an empirically observed process outcome. A decision-making random walk model utilizes the random process to calculate the likelihood of reaching specific states designated as response choices. The steps taken to reach a designated state are utilized to model response time given a response choice. Random walk models have been widely utilized to study computational underpinnings of decision processes, e.g., Decision Field Theory ( Busemeyer & Johnson, 2008 ), Markov Random-walk formulation of Drift Diffusion Model (MR-DDM) ( Busemeyer & Bruza, 2012; Busemeyer et al., 2006 ), Quantum Random-Walk Models (QRM) ( Busemeyer & Bruza, 2012; Busemeyer et al., 2006; Chen et al., 2022 ), and hybrid models combining diffusion and quantum dynamics ( Busemeyer, Zhang et al., 2020; Epping et al., 2023; Fuss & Navarro, 2013; Huang et al., 2025; Kvam et al., 2021 ).

Meta-reasoning studies usually instruct participants to respond based on their subjective decision-stopping criterion. Furthermore, an external (response triggered by an external cue, e.g., a beep) or an internal (response triggered by an internal cue, e.g., subjective confidence threshold) stopping procedure can be implemented in the decision random walk models (see Chapter 8 in  Busemeyer & Bruza, 2012 ). Hence, an internally controlled random walk model could be utilized to describe empirically observed Meta-reasoning phenomena. Also, random walk models have been utilized to track expected confidence state at intermediate time intervals ( Busemeyer & Bruza, 2012 ). A confidence trajectory could enable researchers to analyze the utility of subjective confidence evolution patterns as a decision-stopping criterion. For example, evaluate whether participants continue deliberation for a certain time period even with minimal improvement in confidence state, (e.g., The Diminishing Criteria Model proposed by  Ackerman, 2014  meta-reasoning study), or continue deliberation until a sudden spike in confidence is experienced (e.g., sudden spike in empirical warmth rating observed immediately before a response in an insight task  Metcalfe & Wiebe, 1987 )? Hence, these models could potentially enable meta-reasoning researchers to find deeper insights into the impact of metacognition/confidence evolution patterns on response accuracy, especially in studies where explicit intermediate judgments were not feasible or desirable.

MR-DDM suggests that as a stimulus is administered, a cognitive process begins to collect evidence for each of the provided response choices. The evidence is gathered to separate the signal (correct) from the noise (incorrect) response option. Then, a response is generated when a satisfactory amount of evidence is collected for a given response choice. MR-DDM has also been extended to account for post-decisional reports of confidence judgment, such as the two-stage Dynamic Signal Detection model ( Pleskac & Busemeyer, 2010; Yu et al., 2015 ), and the collapsing confidence boundary model ( Moran et al., 2015 ). Recently, MR-DDMs have also been utilized to model RLDT response choice accuracy and time ( Diederich & Oswald, 2016 ). Also,  Malaiya (2025)  PhD dissertation demonstrated the effectiveness of MR-DDM in estimating the expected confidence state in reasoning and planning trials with longer deliberation response times. Hence, MR-DDM can be further utilized to evaluate empirical Meta-reasoning phenomena in RLDT, especially in studies without explicit intermediate confidence judgments.

MR-DDM assumes that while evidence is being accumulated towards a choice, a participant is aware of the state of evidence, and the confidence judgments are readouts of the evidence status. In contrast, QRM suggests that a response (or state of evidence) is realized at the time when a participant decides to elicit a response. To model response choice at a given response time, a measurement operation is performed on the Quantum state of the random walk at the response time ( Busemeyer et al., 2006 ). To model intermediate responses, such as intermediate confidence judgments, a measurement operation is performed at every intermediate response time. The intermediate measurement mechanism of QRM has been successfully utilized to model the impact of intermediate response choices on a final response choice ( Busemeyer et al., 2019; Kvam et al., 2015 ). Also, QRM has been successful in modeling response choice accuracy, corresponding intermediate and post-decisional confidence judgments, and response times of fast two-choice decision tasks ( Busemeyer & Bruza, 2012; Busemeyer et al., 2006; Chen et al., 2022; Kvam et al., 2015; Pothos & Busemeyer, 2022 ). Also, QRM has been successful in modeling causal reasoning tasks ( Mistry et al., 2018; Trueblood & Busemeyer, 2012; Trueblood et al., 2017 ); (also see  Bruza et al., 2015; Pothos & Busemeyer, 2022  for a review). Also,  Malaiya (2025)  PhD dissertation demonstrated the effectiveness of QRM-estimated confidence states in explaining response patterns in conflict-based and high-difficulty reasoning trials. Hence, QRM could be further utilized to evaluate meta-reasoning phenomena in complex RLDT, especially studies where explicit intermediate confidence judgments were not recorded.

The current study reviews existing meta-reasoning studies that investigated characteristics of how confidence judgments evolve within and across a reasoning trial (see  Ackerman & Thompson, 2017; Double & Birney, 2019  for a review). Then, certain empirically observable Meta-reasoning phenomena associated with the evolution pattern of the intermediate confidence judgments are identified. Then, the current study proposes several computational features to theoretically aid the construction and evaluation of random walk models of Meta-reasoning processes.

Empirical Meta-reasoning phenomena of RLDT highlight the importance of item characteristics in metacognitive monitoring and controlling of reasoning processes. Prior work in Item Response Theory (IRT) ( Embretson & Reise, 2000 ) based models has demonstrated the relevance of modeling item properties in reasoning-based assessments ( Goldhammer et al., 2015, 2014; Scherer et al., 2015 ). Also, a Drift-Diffusion Model ( Ratcliff, 1978 ) has been extended utilizing IRT principles, named the Q-Diffusion model ( van der Maas et al., 2011 ). The Q-Diffusion model has successfully modeled reasoning assessments without explicitly constraining the model to describe Meta-reasoning phenomena ( van der Maas et al., 2011; Malaiya, 2024; Rijn & Ali, 2017 ). Therefore, this review also includes discussions on how one might develop potential extensions of the MR-DDM by incorporating item properties.

The reference studies specified for each phenomenon in  Table 1  do not constitute a comprehensive list but rather were chosen to represent typical studies supporting the relevant phenomena.

Reasoning can be modeled as a search over a mental solution space to find solution states with a high confidence state in solving the task at hand. The reasoning process may not always terminate with a high confidence state, and also may not converge to the same solution if restarted. Such a process collects evidence from multiple sources of information and combines such information to converge to a solution (see  Krawczyk, 2018 ). The Meta-reasoning framework proposed by  Thompson et al. (2011) , Metacognitive Reasoning theory, suggests that an initial Feeling of Rightness (FOR) for a potential response option serves as a cue to initiate a more deliberative reasoning process. FOR is a participant’s estimation of their confidence in the correctness of their potential response. Metacognitive Reasoning Theory is inspired by the dual process theories of decision-making that suggest that decisions can be made either by a Type I system (fast and intuitive response) or a Type II (more deliberative) system ( Evans, 2019; Neys, 2017 ).

In the conditional reasoning, probability judgment, and categorical syllogism tasks that  Thompson et al. (2011)  administered, participants were required to first respond to an item as quickly as they read it (Type I response). Then the participants were asked to report their FOR for the response, and then take a second attempt to respond when they felt appropriately confident (Type II response). The study intended to investigate whether FOR serves as a cue to invoke a longer deliberative reasoning process. The involvement of the Type II reasoning process was measured using rethinking time after FOR was reported, and the probability of answer change during the second attempt. In  Thompson et al. (2011) , the stimuli were manipulated for difficulty level to study the effect of response fluency on FOR. Response fluency represents the ease with which the first (intuitive) response appears to the participant. The probability judgment experiment also evaluated the effect of conflicting responses on the reported FOR for a given stimulus. In all these experiments, a weaker FOR was found to increase the re-thinking time and the probability of changing the first response ( Table 1 , phenomenon 1). Also, initial FORs for easier stimuli were faster and stronger than harder stimuli, suggesting response fluency serves as a cue for the FOR.

As discussed above,  Thompson et al. (2011)  found that a weaker initial FOR may indicate allocating further cognitive resources for a given stimulus to improve success likelihood.  Thompson and Johnson (2014)  further evaluated the Metacognitive Reasoning theory on conflict-based RLDT tasks. Participants were randomly assigned to one of 4 reasoning tasks containing both conflict and non-conflict-based items. The 4 reasoning tasks were (1) Syllogistic Reasoning, (2) Denominator Neglect, (3) Base Rate Neglect, and (4) Causal Reasoning. In general, FOR ratings were found to be lower in conflict-based items. To analyze whether a weaker FOR serves as a cue to invoke a more deliberate reasoning process, the mean of re-thinking time and probability of answer change was calculated for groups of participants having various degrees of difference between FORs of conflict vs non-conflict stimuli.  Thompson and Johnson (2014)  showed that as the difference between FORs for the conflict vs non-conflict stimulus increased, so did the re-thinking time and the probability of answer change. Hence, the studies suggest that initial confidence judgment influences the decision to further cognitive resource allocation ( Table 1 , phenomenon 2).

Ackerman (2014)  further extended the Metacognitive Reasoning theory to accommodate the continuous role metacognition has during an RLDT process, influencing the deliberation time of the reasoning processes until a response is elicited. The diminishing criterion model proposed by  Ackerman (2014)  modeled reasoners’ willingness to elicit a response choice with lower confidence as time proceeded.  Ackerman et al. (2020)  further discussed how certain metacognitive parameters, such as satisfaction level of potential responses, and cost of continuing the reasoning process, may play a role in deciding process termination criterion.

Ackerman (2014)  conducted experiments to examine the growth in confidence while a task was being performed. Participants were assessed using a compound remote associate (CRA) stimulus with either ample time or under time pressure to respond, and using math problems generally used in an educational context. The CRA task presented several words such as PINE, CRAB, and SAUCE. Then, a participant was instructed to respond with a word that would compound with all of the provided words (e.g., APPLE). The confidence scale was presented after the first 3 s of item administration and then in regular intervals of 15 s until a participant responded. The time pressure group in the CRA task got 16 mins for 30 items, and the ample time group got 30 mins for 30 items. For time pressure and ample time cases, the CRA experiment found that, as time elapsed, participants elicited responses with lower confidence states. Also, for the math task, participants were divided into free report (can choose not to provide any response) and forced report groups. Participants attempted 12 math items in 12 min. The results showed that as time increased, participants elicited responses with lower confidence. Hence, the study suggests that process termination criterion maximizes success likelihood, given ongoing personal experience, and assessment instructions ( Table 1 , phenomenon 3).

Also, the rate of progress in confidence rating between CRA and math problems was found to be different. The confidence in CRA rose slowly in the beginning and then rose suddenly just before eliciting the response. However, in the math problems, the confidence rose consistently throughout the deliberation time. Similar results were also observed by  Metcalfe and Wiebe (1987)  where the growth rate for the insight problem-solving task showed a sudden increase in confidence just before eliciting response vs the algebra (non-insight) task where confidence grew more incrementally. ( Table 1 , phenomenon 4).

Also, in both CRA and math problems, the initial confidence was lower than the final confidence, supporting  Thompson and Johnson (2014), Thompson et al. (2011)  conclusions that weaker confidence for the initial response serves as a cue to invoke more deliberate reasoning processes ( Table 1 , phenomenon 5).  Ackerman and Thompson (2017)  proposed the Meta-reasoning framework to combine Metacognitive Reasoning Theory ( Thompson et al., 2011 ) and Diminishing Criterion Model ( Ackerman, 2014 ) in a comprehensive theory that supports the idea that the impact of online metacognition begins the moment one experiences a stimulus till after a response has been made.

During a deliberation process within RLDT, a participant may repeatedly self-evaluate their current confidence for a given response option (see  Ackerman & Thompson, 2017 ). Do such repeated self-evaluations impact the possibility of a correct response? Reactivity to reporting a Metacognitive Judgment (MJ) suggests that a participant reporting MJ before, during, or after a task may impact their performance in current or subsequent tasks. MJ reports are shown to be reactive for various psychological phenomena such as learning strategy ( Metcalfe, 2009; Metcalfe & Finn, 2008; Metcalfe & Kornell, 2005 ), memory retention ( Dunlosky & Rawson, 2012; Witherby & Tauber, 2017 ), perceptual decision-making ( Busemeyer et al., 2019 ), and fluid-reasoning-based decision-making ( Callender et al., 2016; Double & Birney, 2017 ); also see  Busemeyer, Kvam, and Pleskac (also see 2020), Double and Birney (also see 2019), Double et al. (also see 2018, for a review) . For example,  Witherby and Tauber (2017)  evaluated the effect of Judgment of Learning (JOL) on retention of memory after a period of both 3-min and 2-days. JOL represents a report of confidence regarding the ability to recall a recently studied item on a future assessment ( Nelson & Dunlosky, 1991 ). The participants were instructed to study word pairs and, after completing the study, provide their JOL rating on a scale of 0%–100%. The cued-recall test performance of the JOL group of participants was found to be better than the no-JOL group for both 3-min and 2-day retention intervals.

To further analyze whether improving metacognitive awareness can further improve performance,  Callender et al. (2016)  conducted 2 studies based on exams conducted in an undergraduate course of Decision-Making. Given this was a Decision-Making course, the students received training on how to calibrate MJ with response accuracy. As part of this training, students provided both item response and RCJ on the multiple-choice questions and received feedback based on their correct or incorrect response accuracy and corresponding high or low RCJ. Also, the students received specific training on metacognitive biases such as over- and under-confidence effects (also known as Dunning-Kruger effect  Kruger & Dunning, 1999 ). Also, students were given clear instructions on how an MJ report closer to the actual scores will reward them with extra credits. Also, once a particular exam result was out, the instructor provided item-level feedback and asked students to retrospectively analyze their understanding of the performance.

Study 1 analyzed two exam results where students were asked to rate their exam-level performance on a 0-100 scale after completing an exam consisting of both dichotomous and constructed responses. The calibration score was calculated as the absolute difference between the exam score and the overall confidence rating. This calibration score reflected the metacognitive awareness of a student. The calibration score was calculated for both exams and the results showed an overall tendency of improved scores in the subsequent exam. This improvement can be attributed to the training received as part of the course curriculum. Further, to observe the effect of specific feedback after an exam targeted to improve MJ resolution, in Study 2,  Callender et al. (2016)  evaluated two exams where no specific feedback on the importance of reporting MJ on the overall exam was provided to one of the two groups of students. The curriculum and training on MJ before these exams were similar to Study 1. In Study 2 ( Callender et al., 2016 ), the calibration score of the second exam improved for students who received specific feedback after the first exam. This provides evidence that improving metacognitive awareness may help to improve performance.

To understand whether continued reporting of confidence within a trial helps to improve both final response accuracy and confidence,  Busemeyer et al. (2019)  conducted an experiment where participants completed sessions of dot motion tasks and provided multiple confidence judgments within each trial. After the first beep, the participants would report their confidence on a 0%(certainly left) to 100%(certainly right) scale. Then the stimulus will remain on screen till the second beep when participants may again report their confidence. The first session required confidence judgments at 0.5 and 1.5 secs, and the second session required confidence judgments at 1.5 and 2.5 secs. A third confidence rating was also collected but is not relevant to the current discussion. The study compared the difference between confidence reports at 1.5 s for both sessions. The results showed that during the second report, participants’ confidence reports improved and aligned more towards the correct motion direction for all levels of coherence (2%, 4%, 8%, 16%) used in the dot motion task ( Table 1 , Phenomenon 6).

Reactivity due to confidence reports can be both positive and negative. A positive (or negative) reactivity would suggest that eliciting a confidence judgment would increase (or decrease) the probability of getting a correct response.  Double and Birney (2017)  proposed the Cognizant Confidence hypothesis that suggested that participants with higher confidence will have a positive effect due to confidence reports, and participants with lower confidence will have a negative effect. To test the hypothesis,  Double and Birney (2017)  studied the effect of eliciting a post-decisional confidence rating on the response accuracy of the subsequent item. The study examined participants on the Ravens’ Advanced Progressive Matrices (RPM) task containing 20 items arranged in ascending order of difficulty. RPM tasks have been discussed to be suitable to evaluate the effect of metacognition on RLDT (see box 2 in  Ackerman and Thompson (2017) ). In the first experiment,  Double and Birney (2017)  randomly assigned 69 participants to both the confidence report group and the no report group. The confidence report group would provide their confidence rating after responding to each item, whereas the no report group will only provide item responses. Participants had to respond to 20 items within a time limit of 20 min. Participants reporting higher than median confidence judgments were found to have better response accuracy than both the lower confidence judgments and the no-report groups ( Table 1 , phenomenon 6 & 7).

In the second experiment,  Double and Birney (2017)  asked participants to provide their self-confidence in reasoning-based activities before administering the RPM task. The participants with higher self-confidence were found to have a positive effect on performance, and the participants with lower self-confidence were found to have a negative effect ( Table 1 , phenomenon 7). These results suggest that the nature of reactivity due to metacognitive awareness can be dependent on the metacognitive attributes of the participant.

A computational feature is defined as either a structural or functional characteristic of a model that may not be directly observable, or assumptions regarding how information or knowledge is represented in the model, or characteristics of a model’s observable behavioral dynamics. The current section proposes a set of computational features ( Table 2 ) that are intended to serve as guidelines to aid comparison and evaluation of random walk models of identified meta-reasoning phenomena ( Table 1 ).

Also, in the current study, confidence levels modeled by a random walk model will be described as a “confidence state”. The confidence state describes the latent confidence levels in a person’s mind that the person may convert to the confidence scale provided in a decision trial. For example, a two-choice decision trial may require a participant to provide confidence judgment on a 50%–100% scale. To define such a scale, a model may have to initiate 101 states (50 states for each response choice and 1 neutral state). However, to reduce computational requirements, a modeler may model only 11 states, i.e., 1 modeled state would describe 10 confidence levels and an additional 1 neutral state. Hence, confidence states and confidence levels may not always have the same scale.

As discussed in the Meta-reasoning Section  3.4 , Phenomena 4, and 5 ( Table 1 ) describe how the growth pattern of confidence judgment is influenced by assessment domain and item characteristics. For example,  Ackerman (2014)  showed that a participant’s confidence judgment growth rate may be linear or nonlinear depending on the administered task domain. Also,  Ackerman (2014)  showed that confidence would, on average, rise within a trial. Hence, a decision response model implementing computational feature 1 may have to constrain the confidence state growth pattern to a monotonically increasing growth pattern while ensuring that the final confidence state is higher than the initial confidence state. Also, the model may have to be flexible to learn different growth patterns of a monotonically increasing function so that the model may fit a wider variety of assessment domains.

As discussed in the Meta-reasoning Sections  3.2 and 3.3 , Phenomena 2, and 3 ( Table 1 ) describe the role of metacognition in monitoring and controlling cognitive resource allocation. Phenomena 2 emphasizes the importance of initial confidence and Phenomena 3 emphasizes the importance of ongoing personal experience. The confidence state growth rate may help to model ongoing personal experience and how a participant is prioritizing performance, given their personal success goals and assessment instructions. For example, a higher confidence growth rate may be required if a personal goal is to succeed in the assessment, where instruction is to prioritize accuracy over speed. Hence, Computational Feature 2 describes resource allocation as a confidence state growth rate and an initial confidence state function. In such a function, the resource allocation may be described as proportional to the confidence growth rate, e.g., a lower confidence termination criterion for smaller drift rates. Also, the resource allocation may be described as inversely proportional to the initial confidence state, e.g., a larger resource allocation for a lower initial confidence state.

As discussed earlier, Phenomena 6, and 7 ( Table 1 ) describe the positive and negative impact of repeated metacognitive monitoring on response accuracy. In other words, explicitly measuring the confidence state within a trial may result in selecting a different response than if no explicit measurement operation were performed. Also, Phenomena 1 describes the impact of initial confidence judgment on response choice selection. Hence, to accommodate Phenomena 1, 6, and 7, Computational Feature 3 requires the likelihood function of response accuracy as a function of the initial confidence state in a decision response choice, and the frequency of metacognitive monitoring within the trial.

Since the 19th century, decision response time has been used to understand the underlying cognitive processes of human fast decision-making tasks ( Donders, 1868; Jastrow, 1890 ). Response time has also been studied in several research fields, such as complex decision making ( Goldhammer & Entink, 2011; Goldhammer et al., 2015, 2014; Scherer et al., 2015 ), confidence judgments (see  Rahnev et al., 2020  for a review), and cognitive ability assessments (see  Boeck & Jeon, 2019; Kyllonen & Zu, 2016; Lee & Chen, 2011  for a review). The time that a set of cognitive processes may take to elicit a response is termed the decision time. The total response time observed during a trial is the sum of both the decision and the non-decision time. The non-decision time includes time for other activities, such as the time required for the initial encoding of the stimuli and the time required to register the response on the assessment device. A decision time can either be fast in the order of 100 ms or slow response in the order of tens of seconds, where longer deliberation is required, such as typically seen in problem-solving and reasoning tasks ( Ackerman, 2014; Goldhammer & Entink, 2011; Scherer et al., 2015; Thompson & Johnson, 2014; Thompson et al., 2011 ).

A random walk process is a stochastic process describing the probability of transitioning from a current state to the next state in a defined state space. In other words, a random walk process “walks” randomly within a defined state space, as time elapses. The transition probability may be uniform across states or calculated based on a latent factor assumed to be causing the randomness in the walk. Random walk processes can be broadly classified into Classical and Quantum Random Walk processes (see  Xia et al., 2020  for a review). A diffusion process is a type of classical random walk process where the random walk transitions from a current state to a neighboring state.  Fig. 1

Utilizing a random walk process, a random walk model can be constructed to calculate the likelihood of a random walk reaching a certain state in the mathematical space, given an initial state and time elapsed (also known as first passage time, see  Cox & Miller, 1965 ). For example, MR-DDM and QRM ( Busemeyer et al., 2006 ) utilize the probability distribution over state space (see  Fig. 1 ) at a given response time, to calculate the likelihood of a particular response choice elicited at that response time. In general, Drift-Diffusion models and Quantum random walk models have been widely successful in modeling two-choice decision response accuracy and times (see  Busemeyer, Kvam, & Pleskac, 2020; Ratcliff et al., 2016; Stafford et al., 2020; Wang & Busemeyer, 2021  for a review).

Typically, in such studies, the boundary (distance) between response choices is collapsed as a function of time elapsed. However, computational feature 2 suggests describing cognitive resource allocation as a function of the initial confidence state and confidence growth rate. Also, computational feature 1 suggests describing the confidence growth rate as a monotonically increasing function of the current state. Hence, to implement computational feature 2, the boundary parameter of MR-DDMs may be described as a collapsing function of the initial confidence state and state-dependent drift rate parameter.

In MR-DDMs, an initial bias probability distribution ( Fig. 2 ) is used to model a participant’s prior confidence. A higher initial confidence suggests closeness towards the upper boundary (or correct decision boundary) of MR-DDM. Hence, the overall initial confidence in the task can be modeled using the initial bias parameter.

However, a random walk begins from an initial state (or bias). The next step is taken considering the immediate previous step as a starting point, i.e., the second step is dependent on the state of the first step, and the third step is dependent on the state of the second step (see  Fig. 2 ). Hence, further studies would be helpful to evaluate the overall impact of initial bias on the final state as the number of steps (or response time) gets larger.

Also, Computational Feature 3 suggests that repeated self-evaluation (or metacognitive monitoring) of one’s progress should be incorporated into the likelihood function of the confidence state. MR-DDMs assume that a participant is always aware of their intermediate confidence state, and monitoring that confidence state does not change the likelihood of a response choice ( Busemeyer et al., 2019; Kvam et al., 2015 ). However, a multi-stage MR-DDM, proposed for complex decision-making ( Diederich & Oswald, 2016; Diederich & Trueblood, 2018 ), may be utilized to model a change in likelihood due to intermediate monitoring. A multi-stage MR-DDM slices response time into several segments and models each time-segment using independent model parameters: initial bias and drift rate. The final state of a given time-segment is considered as the initial state of the next time-segment. A piecewise DDM (dual-stage continuous state continuous time) has been utilized to model a change-of-mind study where participant may change their evidence accumulation direction (or drift rate) within a trial ( Holmes & Trueblood, 2018 ). Hence, a multi-stage MR-DDM may be suitable to accommodate Computational Feature 3.

Quantum Random-Walk Model (QRM) utilizes the Quantum superposition phenomenon to calculate the transition probability from the current state to the next state as time proceeds. The superposition phenomenon assumes that a random walk co-exists in all states at any given time point. This likelihood of co-existence is described using a probability amplitude. To locate a random walk, a measurement operation is performed, calculating a probability distribution over the states by utilizing the probability amplitude at the measurement time (discussed in more detail later).

QRM of a decision process suggests that, within a trial, a participant is unaware of their decision process state. Hence, a participant’s confidence state exists in a superposition between all confidence states. At the time of response, a participant measures the confidence state to get a probability distribution over the confidence states (see  Fig. 1 ). This probability distribution can then be utilized to model response accuracy and response time, post-decision confidence judgment, and the mean confidence state (see  Fig. 3 ) at any given time within a trial.

( Table 2 ), as discussed in the MR-DDM Section  5.1.2 , Computational Feature 3 describes the likelihood function of response accuracy as a function of the initial confidence state in the task and the frequency of metacognitive monitoring within a trial. QRM assumes that a participant is unaware of their confidence state within a trial unless a measurement is made (see Eq.  (6) ). Every measurement collapses the superpositioned confidence states to a probability distribution over the confidence states. If the random walk continues after a measurement, then the (collapsed) probability distribution over confidence states serves as an initial starting probability distribution for the continuing random walk.  Busemeyer et al. (2019), Kvam et al. (2015)  showed that such a property of QRM is suitable to model the interference effect of repeatedly reporting within-trial confidence judgments on final responses (also see  Busemeyer, Kvam, & Pleskac, 2020; Busemeyer et al., 2006 ). Also,  Fig. 3  shows that the transition pattern of confidence judgments is a function of an initial probability distribution. Hence, QRM is well-positioned to satisfy computational feature 3 requirements.

( Table 2 ), as discussed in the MR-DDM Section  5.1.2 , Computational Feature 4 requires the distribution of reasoning process duration as a distribution having positive real numbers with a large mean, ranging in order of tens of seconds, and a large variance. An interesting feature of QRM is that even after the peak confidence state is reached, QRM does not stay at the peak confidence state and fluctuates (like a wave) between high and low confidence states as the QRM proceeds (see  Fig. 1  and  Fig. 3 ). Hence, in QRM, a response time is a function of when self-evaluation is performed rather than reaching a peak confidence state. Hence, QRM may be able to fit high mean and variance response times. Response time models based on Item Response Theory (IRT) ( van der Linden, 2006; Linden, 2007 ) have shown that reasoning response times can be described as a function of participant- and item-specific properties ( Goldhammer & Entink, 2011; Goldhammer et al., 2015, 2014; Scherer et al., 2015 ). For example,  van der Linden (2006)  proposed that the log response time is a function of a person’s speed and an item’s time intensity parameters. The person speed parameter can be interpreted as the tendency of a person to spend time on an item, and the time-intensity parameter can be interpreted as the time an item demands. As discussed in the Computational Features Section  4 , item characteristics impact the within-trial dynamics of reasoning processes. Hence, the current section first briefly introduces Item Response Theory and the Continuous state, Continuous time Drift-Diffusion Model (CC-DDM) ( Ratcliff, 1978 ). Then, an extension of the CC-DDM incorporating item response theory, proposed by  van der Maas et al. (2011) , is further discussed. Then, the effectiveness of a CC-DDM Item Response Theory model in accounting for the computational features discussed in  Table 2  is further discussed.

The Psychometrics field of study has been extensively involved in explaining responses in ability-based tasks typically administered in educational settings. Such educational assessments may include simple or extensive reasoning-based tasks. The Item Response Theory (IRT) in Psychometrics has been widely used to explain response accuracy ( Embretson & Reise, 2000 ) and response time ( van der Linden, 2006 ) of a variety of ability-based tasks as a function of the participant-, and item-specific properties (see  van der Linden, 2016  for a review). In an RLDT where overall response time (and not per item) is fixed, the items placed towards the end of an assessment may induce more time pressure on the participants. This may result in participants being less response-cautious towards the end of an assessment. The items not placed towards the end may also induce time pressure. For example, a participant’s estimation of the time required to solve a particular item can be more than the average response time the participant assumed for solving each item. The above-average response time estimate for an item may be due to the difficulty of the item or maybe simply due to the total number of sub-steps required to solve the item (even when each step is easy). All of these factors may provide cues for metacognition to adjust resource allocation (see  Ackerman et al., 2020 ).

First, the current study reviewed the intermediate confidence growth patterns, process termination criterion, and impact of metacognitive monitoring on response choices empirically observed in Meta-reasoning studies conducted using RLDT such as Compound Remote Associate task ( Ackerman, 2014 ), and Raven’s Progressive Matrix task ( Double & Birney, 2017 ) ( Table 1 ).

Then, the current study proposed certain computational features ( Table 2 ) to evaluate the effectiveness of decision-making computational models in explaining the empirically observed Meta-reasoning phenomena ( Table 1 ). Computational Feature 1 described requirements for intermediate confidence growth patterns. Computational Feature (2) described requirements for decision process termination criterion. Computational Feature (3) described requirements to model the impact of metacognition on response choice. Computational Feature (4) described the requirement to model a high mean and variance response time distribution.

The current study evaluated a Markov Random-Walk formulation of the Drift-Diffusion Model (MR-DDM) and a Quantum Random-Walk Model (QRM) using the proposed computational features. Then, the current study proposed potential extensions of MR-DDM and QRM that may improve their effectiveness in implementing the proposed computational features. Also, the current study reviewed the existing Item Response Theory-based extension of CC-DDM and then recommended potential ways of extending the MR-DDM drift rate (or growth rate) and boundary parameters using item-specific parameters.

Following the  Diederich and Busemeyer (2003), van der Maas et al. (2011), Tuerlinckx and Boeck (2005)  Diffusion model studies, the current study proposed potential extensions to MR-DDM to implement the proposed computational features. The current study proposed to describe the MR-DDM drift rate as a function of the current state and item difficulty parameter to implement Computational Feature 1. Also, the current study proposed to describe the MR-DDM boundary threshold parameter as a function of the current state-specific drift rate and item-specific time pressure parameter to implement Computational Feature 2. Hence, further studies are required to examine how the drift rate and boundary threshold parameter of QRM may be extended to implement Computational Features 1 and 2 ( Table 2 ).

Computational Feature 3 ( Table 2 ), requires that explicit monitoring of confidence state evolution should alter the likelihood of future response choices. MR-DDM suggests that the state of the random walk is always known, and using the current state to elicit a response will not change the course of the random walk. However, QRM suggests that utilizing a current state to elicit a response requires explicitly measuring the current state likelihood. Every measurement operation further modifies the evolution of the random walk, impacting future response choice likelihood. Hence, QRM was found to have the potential to implement Computational Feature 3. Further studies may be required to examine the impact on response choice likelihood as the number of measurement operations performed increases due to long response times.

MR-DDM was found to have the potential to implement Computational Feature 4 ( Table 2 ) by increasing the number of states being modeled or by reducing the drift rate, thereby allowing MR-DDM to “walk” for longer response times. However, increasing the number of states will also increase processor/memory requirements and may reduce the performance of certain parameter estimation algorithms. Also, it is important to analyze the impact on the QRM wave-like evolution pattern given minor/major perturbations in the drift and diffusion parameters. A higher degree of parameter sensitivity may impact QRM’s ability to account for Computational Features 1 and 4 ( Table 2 ). For Computational Feature 1, further studies could systematically evaluate changes in evolution patterns as a function of major/minor perturbations in the drift and diffusion parameters. Computational Feature 4 evaluates how long a random walk can “walk” before converging to a probability distribution.  Fig. 3  demonstrates that the wave-like patterns in the “with monitoring” (or internally controlled) formulation of QRM converge to a central state (or 50% confidence) for the given drift and diffusion parameters. Further simulation studies could systematically evaluate how the rate of convergence changes for major and minor perturbations in the drift and diffusion parameters.

The current study focused on the diffusion and quantum decision processes. However, further studies could also evaluate the hybrid decision process models that combine diffusion and quantum dynamics. For example, an open systems model ( Busemeyer, Zhang et al., 2020; Epping et al., 2023; Fuss & Navarro, 2013 ) combines the diffusion and quantum model dynamics utilizing a weight parameter, and the Quantum Sequential Sampler model ( Huang et al., 2025 ) first evolves as a Quantum system and then utilizes the evolved quantum states to inform a diffusion process to evolve from subjective probabilities to empirical probabilities. Such hybrid models may provide an alternative to the discussed state-based solutions in explaining the monotonically increasing empirical confidence judgment evolution patterns.

To demonstrate how hybrid models may be evaluated utilizing proposed computational features, evaluation of the open system model ( Busemeyer, Zhang et al., 2020 ) is further discussed. The open system model may be better positioned to account for Computational Features 1, 3, and 4 ( Table 2 ) than MR-DDM and QRM separately. The open system model may account for Computational Feature 1 because it combines the diffusion upward trajectory with the quantum wave-like pattern (e.g.,  Kvam et al., 2021 ). Hence, the diffusion component of the model can describe the drift rate parameter as a monotonically increasing function of the current state, to achieve a monotonically increasing confidence growth rate for the entire open system model. Also, the quantum component of these models can account for the interference effect resulting from the initial response choice and repeated monitoring, thereby accounting for Computational Feature 3. Also, the open system model may account for Computational Feature 4, given that the quantum component of the random walk model is capable of modeling large mean and variance response times (Section  5.2.2  Computational Feature 4). Hence, further simulation studies could evaluate the dynamics of such hybrid models for various parameter settings to further establish these models for the proposed meta-reasoning computational features.

Thompson et al. (2011)  proposed a Metacognitive Reasoning Theory (MRT) that proposed a connection between metacognition and dual-process theories of reasoning (see  Evans, 2011, 2019  for a review). Certain dual-process theories of reasoning suggest that the reasoning process initiates as a Type-I intuitive process and then continues as a Type-II deliberate process while making a decision. MRT proposed that the metacognitive  Feeling of Rightness  during the Type-I process initiates the more deliberative Type-II reasoning process. The hybrid random walk models also incorporate two processes, Markov (classic) and Quantum, to model a decision process. Also, certain hybrid models (e.g.,  Epping et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2025 ) have characterized the Type-I process as a quantum-like process and the Type-II process as a classical process. Hence, meta-reasoning studies could further evaluate similarities and differences in intermediate confidence evolution patterns during Type-I and Type-II reasoning processes to guide further development of the hybrid models for describing meta-reasoning evolution patterns.

In a diffusion process, at every time step, a state may stay in its current position or transition to the immediate neighboring states. However, the jump process ( Shlesinger et al., 1993 ) has also been studied to explain cognitive processes ( Baronchelli & Radicchi, 2013; Voss et al., 2019 ). In a jump process, at every time step, a state may stay in the current position or jump to far states. The jump process-based Levy-flight model has been shown to have a better fit than the Wiener process model with a collapsing boundary for the number-letter classification task ( Voss et al., 2019 ). Hence, further studies may evaluate jump process models utilizing the computational features proposed in the current study.

Item Response Theory (IRT) models have been widely successful in modeling reasoning tasks ( Goldhammer & Entink, 2011; Goldhammer et al., 2015, 2014; Scherer et al., 2015 ). IRT-based extensions of Drift-Diffusion models have also successfully modeled reasoning tasks ( van der Maas et al., 2011; Rijn & Ali, 2017 ). However, IRT-based extensions of other random walk models, such as Jump process-based random walk models, Quantum random walk models, and Open system models, have not been widely studied. Hence, further studies may evaluate said random walk model’s improvement in describing Meta-reasoning phenomena after incorporating item-based parameters per IRT specifications.

Meta-reasoning features have also been increasingly studied in the Artificial Intelligence (AI) field to build systems that perform complex tasks, e.g., in large language models of reasoning ( Gao et al., 2024 ) and in autonomous robotic systems ( Herrmann, 2023 ). Further studies can be conducted to, first, model expected Meta-reasoning phenomena in a Meta-reasoning AI system and then utilize a confidence state random walk model to analyze the effectiveness of the AI system in implementing the phenomena. For example, Phenomena 2 and 3 ( Table 1 ) can be implemented in an AI agent such that further resource allocation is calculated based on the initial confidence in the ability to solve a problem and the ongoing success likelihood. Then a MR-DDM/QRM, implementing Computational Feature 2 ( Table 2 ), can be utilized to fit the AI agent’s response choice (and response time, if available). Then, a goodness-of-model fit method can be utilized to evaluate how well the MR-DDM/QRM fits the AI agent’s response choice (and time). The goodness-of-model fit can indicate how well the AI agent implemented human Meta-reasoning Phenomena 2 and 3.
