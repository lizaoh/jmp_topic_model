Humans have the remarkable ability to regulate their behavior according to a long-term goal rather than an immediately rewarding temptation. For example, while influenced by a goal to eat healthily, it is possible to resist a cookie and select a piece of fruit instead – even though consuming the cookie is, for many people, the more immediately rewarding option. Colloquially, we think of such behavior as exerting self-control.

More formally, self-control is often defined as a process by which an individual overcomes immediately-rewarding, pre-potent tendencies that are inconsistent with previously set goals, and proceeds instead with behavior that is less immediately rewarding but is consistent with previously set goals ( Friese & Hofmann, 1991 ;  Hayden, 2019 ;  Hoyle & Davisson, 2016 ,  2017 ;  Shenhav, 2017 ). This definition is not universally shared, but it is considered to be the closest to a consensus view available (see discussion in  Hayden, 2019 ).

The ability to exert self-control is profoundly consequential. It is thought to be a necessary ingredient for a well-functioning person, and for a well-functioning society ( Tangney et al., 2004 ). A lack of self-control is associated with poverty, poor health, and other undesirable states ( de Ridder et al., 2012 ). Unsurprisingly, self-control has attracted a great deal of interest from psychologists who have identified personality variables ( Hoyle & Davisson, 2016 ), situational factors ( Li et al., 2020 ), and learnable strategies ( Duckworth et al., 2016 ) that predict successful self-control decisions. However, much less is known about the mechanisms by which self-control-related decisions are implemented.

Researchers working on self-control have made important progress in specifying a high-level framework for how people implement self-control within the broader context of self-regulation of behavior ( Carver & Scheier, 1982 ,  2012 ). Their framework was based upon cybernetic principles ( Wiener, 1948 ), according to which information-processing structures achieve response stability by comparing current inputs to a goal or reference value, (potentially) observing a discrepancy between the two, and then making an adjustment to reduce any discrepancy.

Cybernetics was originally crafted as a theory of mechanical system regulation ( Richardson, 1991 ). The framework was formally specified in engineering and applied, with less quantitative specificity, to a wide range of phenomena in psychology including self-regulation and self-control ( Inzlicht et al., 2021 ). One reason for this widespread application was that the core cybernetic process seemed to capture widespread intuitions about how a non-homuncular system might exhibit control.

The cybernetic model is traditionally comprised of three components: (a) goal setting, (b) monitoring for a mismatch between the set goals and the current state, and (c) implementing behavior to reduce the extent of any mismatch ( Inzlicht et al., 2014 ). The implementing system is connected to the monitoring system via a feedback loop so that the output of the implementing system feeds the current state to the monitoring system until the mismatch between the set goals and the current state is less than some threshold. Notably, some elements of cybernetic feedback principles are prominent in many branches of cognitive science and psychology, including cognitive control ( Botvinick et al., 2001 ), emotion regulation ( Gross, 2015 ), motor control ( Thomas et al., 2019 ), and action selection ( Pezzulo & Cisek, 2016 ).

In the context of maintaining self-control, one instantiation of the cybernetic model may involve comparing a response option presently under consideration to a goal that is activated in memory. If the option is perceived to be discrepant from the goal, then a person could attempt to reduce the discrepancy by making a behavioral adjustment which may include the consideration of an alternative option. For example, in the context of selecting between a healthy and tasty food item, a person may first consider the response option of eating a cookie and compare it to the goal of eating healthy food. A discrepancy in this comparison might lead to the consideration of the alternative response option of eating fruit. Thus, the cybernetic account has the potential to specify a non-homuncular account of self-control.

If one accepts that self-control, like other cognitive phenomena, is a non-homuncular, emergent process arising from the interactions of neurons in the brain, then specifying an algorithmic process that showcases such emergence is an essential part of understanding its mechanism. Algorithmic models allow one to test the consequences of ideas ( McClelland, 2010 ) and establish constraints that have repeatedly proven to be generative at  Marr's (1982)  computational and implementational levels.

While the cybernetic account provides a high-level conceptual account of self-control, it is not well-specified enough to be used to simulate empirical phenomena related to self-control. In particular, it does not specify how a goal (e.g., eating healthy foods) that is relevant to the present scenario is represented and deployed. It does not specify how the options under consideration (e.g., apple or cookie) are represented in a way that can be compared with the representation of the goal. And importantly, it does not specify how pre-potent responses that are incongruent with the goal-state might be inhibited, and how non-prepotent responses might get amplified. Without such specifications, it is difficult to test whether the predictions of the cybernetic framework are consistent with observed empirical phenomena – which makes the framework neither provable nor disprovable.

For example, consider trials in which participants use the mouse to indicate a selection between an immediately rewarding tasty food item, and a goal-consistent healthy food item. In such contexts, mouse-tracking software offers an opportunity to identify the dynamic, online cognitive processes that underlie successful self-control decisions ( Stillman et al., 2017 ). Results indicate that participant mouse trajectories often show initial movement towards the tasty item, followed by a gradual curving towards the healthy item. While this broad pattern is consistent with the exertion of self-control, the cybernetic framework does not offer insights related to the underlying dynamics involved. In particular, it is consistent with a direct straight line towards the healthy item, or an initial move towards the tasty item followed by an abrupt midflight correction towards the healthy item, or the smooth trajectories curving towards the healthy item that are often seen in experimental trials.

A second example concerns temporal discounting in which people tend to accept smaller sums of money sooner compared to larger sums of money later. Temporal discounting is thought to be inextricably linked with self-control (e.g.,  Ainslie, 1975 ;  Ballard et al., 2017 ;  Frederick et al., 2002 ). Economic theories often posit an exponential discount curve in which the discount rate stays constant over time. Empirical observations, however, suggest a hyperbolic discount curve that features high discount rates in the near future and lower discount rates in the distant future. The cybernetic framework is agnostic to either discount curve which, once again, lessens its usefulness as a theory of self-control.

Relatedly, the cybernetic account for control is developed at the linguistic, not computational, level. While theories developed at the linguistic level have the advantage of having the rich descriptive power of natural language at their disposal, they are also hampered by ambiguities inherent in language ( Powers, 1991 ). Such frameworks can sometimes appeal to reasoning that is subject to interpretation or motivated cognition so that different modelers starting with the same inputs can arrive at different results ( Adner et al., 2009 ). On the other hand, a formal computational model is transparent in its initial assumptions and its allowed operations are fully specified so that different modelers starting with the same input will arrive at the same results. Such quantitative precision is helpful in testing ideas related to mechanism, and can also place important theoretical constraints related to the cognitive phenomenon in question. Next, we introduce such a computational model and use it to interrogate key empirical phenomena from self-control.

The Comparison with Goal States Model (CGSM) operates on options that lead to alternative courses of action. An option will often involve an item, such as an apple or a cookie. Options that are more similar and less dissimilar to a goal state become more likely to be selected – even if they are initially less preferred compared to other available options. Correspondingly, options that are less similar and more dissimilar to a goal state become less likely to be selected – even if they are initially more automatic or preferred compared to other available options.

As a mathematical function, the CGSM rests upon an expression that has the desirable property of amplifying preferences based on a parameter we attribute to an item's similarity with a goal. However, the expression specified in (2) does not enable us to simulate experiments related to reaction time or mouse-tracking that are commonplace in the study of self-control. Moreover, the mathematical formulation of the CGSM is not psychologically (or otherwise) motivated. It does not address questions such as how a cognitive system could detect similarities between items and goals; nor does it answer how a low initial preference for an item can be amplified due to its similarity with a goal. To make progress on these issues, we next conceptualize a neural network whose activations settle on the same values as the mathematical formulation of the CGSM specified in  Eq. (2) .

A fundamental first question concerns how one might implement an algorithm that features a comparison between an option (e.g., an object such as an apple or a cookie) and a goal state (e.g., healthy eating). In the context of a neural network-based computational model, comparisons require a mechanistically explicit way to compute similarity between representations. However, traditional models involving symbolic representations do not afford efficient or scalable ways to process similarity-based computations ( Elman, 1991 ). This is because symbolic representations use different symbols for different concepts – regardless of whether the concepts are similar or dissimilar. Thus, similarity information is not present in the representation of concepts.

An intuitive way around this difficulty is offered by distributed representations which have played a prominent role in the Parallel Distributed Processing (PDP) tradition ( Rumelhart et al., 1986 ) and in modern deep learning algorithms. Distributed representations entail a many-to-many relationship between a concept and a set of units in a neural network (or neurons in a brain) ( Plaut & McClelland, 2010 ). In a distributed representation, each concept is represented by many units, and each unit may participate in the representation of many concepts.

For example, a representation of the concept of an apple over five units might be 0.9, 0.8, −0.8, −1.0, 0.7. If activation levels vary between −1 and 1, this representation approximately follows the pattern H, H, L, L, H (where  H  = high,  L  = Low). The representation of the concept of a cookie might be very different from the representation of an apple; for illustrative purposes, let's assume it is −1, −0.7, 0.9, 0.7, −0.6 which approximately corresponds to a L, L, H, H, L pattern. Now, the representation of the concept of an orange – which is more similar to an apple than a cookie is more likely to follow the H, H, L, L, H pattern. For example, this representation might be 0.7, 0.9, −0.8, −0.9, 1.

Distributed representations reflect the average of the learned properties across contexts in which an item was encountered. For example, the distributed representation related to an apple may be based upon properties reflecting semantic knowledge of the apple such as ‘red’, ‘sweet’, ‘preservative free’, ‘rich in nutrients’, ‘high in fiber’, and ‘seedy core’, and may include contextual influences related to grocery stores and fruit trees. Many, but not all of these features may be reflected in the distributed representation of healthy items. A common set of features shared between concepts leads to similar distributed representations for those concepts.

Thus, the representation of the goal concept of eating healthy might be more similar to the representations of an apple and an orange than it is to the representation of a cookie. Distributed representations have such similarity-preserving properties because they are created within neural networks that capture knowledge about objects within connection weights between units. Weight updates related to similar properties tend to be similar and therefore lead to similar representations. Simulation 1 showcases how, with reasonable assumptions, the distributed representation of an apple is more similar to the distributed representation of a health goal than a distribution representation of a cookie.

The similarity-preserving property of distributed representation is crucial in modern deep learning. For example, in the field of Natural Language Processing (NLP), word embeddings ( Bengio et al., 2003 ) refer to numerical representations of words or phrases used to capture the semantic meaning and contextual relationships between words in a language. The CGSM is agnostic to how the distributed representations are generated as long as they retain similarities between entities in the world. Next, we will describe simulations that describe aspects of the CGSM and model empirical data. In Simulation 1, we shall show how the distributed representation of an item that shares many properties of a goal, such as an apple, is more similar to the distributed representation of a goal compared to the distributed representation of an item, such as a cookie that does not share many properties of the goal. Following that, we will, using the CGSM, simulate empirical choices related to healthy and tasty items as well as choices related to temporal discounting using the CGSM. Importantly, to our knowledge, this is the first demonstration of a computational model related to these phenomena in self-control. It is, therefore, not possible to compare fits with any prior computational model. However, it is possible to examine whether the CGSM faithfully captures important patterns of data that have been previously observed in these empirical phenomena.

Simulation 1 emphasizes an algorithm to create similarity-preserving distributed representations.

In the PDP literature and in modern machine learning distributed representations are typically created via the backpropagation algorithm ( Rumelhart et al., 1995 ). Backpropagation is acknowledged to be biologically implausible ( Crick, 1989 ;  Grossberg, 1987 ;  Lillicrap et al., 2020 ;  Roelfsema & Holtmaat, 2018 ). Alternatives to backpropagation are offered by a family of dimensionality-reduction algorithm based on the unsupervised, biologically-plausible Hebbian learning rule ( Cooper, 2005 ) and its extension to multiple neurons. These algorithms operate by extracting the Principal Components (PCs) inherent in an input stream. A biologically plausible version of such an algorithm was found by Földiák, who developed an algorithm ( Földiák, 1990 ;  Pehlevan & Chklovskii, 2019 ) that could find weights that spanned the space spanned by the PCs.

We do not claim that Földiák's algorithm is the only possible such learning algorithm, nor do we claim that this algorithm is, as described, actually present in biological brains. However, because this algorithm is biologically constrained and biologically informed, we do believe it affords generative opportunities in the computational modeling of many cognitive and affective phenomena. We have therefore explicated its mathematical underpinnings in the supplementary materials, and use it to generate the distributed representations in Simulation 1. We note that while the CGSM critically relies upon the occurrence of similarity-preserving distributed representations, it does not rely upon any particular algorithm to do so.

The prior simulation allowed us to examine the selection and reaction times for choices involving immediately-rewarding tasty food item versus a goal-congruent healthy food item. However, these dependent variables provided limited visibility into the internal dynamics of such choices. This issue is mitigated by mouse tracking experiments which are recognized to capture, in a non-self-report and unobtrusive manner, the real-time temporal profile of conflict during self-control contexts ( Freeman & Ambady, 2009 ;  McKinstry et al., 2008 ).  Stillman et al. (2017)  used mouse tracking to perform an in-depth, micro-examination of how cognitive processes unfold in real-time to enable food choice decisions with a self-control component.

In a typical experimental trial, the researchers plotted paths followed by a participant's mouse towards one of two food choices – a healthy item and a tasty item. Consistent with the finding that hand movement is tightly linked to underlying conflict-resolution processes ( Freeman et al., 2011 ), they found that even though individuals know that they should eat an apple instead of cake in order to be healthy, interference from the more-immediately rewarding often created an initial deviation towards the tasty item, which then gradually curved towards the healthy item.

Three important results emerged: First, participant mouse-tracks in self-control contexts revealed smooth trajectories that covered the spectrum of possible curvature amounts. This was in contrast to a more abrupt mid-flight correction towards a healthy food item that might be predicted by dual-process accounts of self-control that unfold in a sequential manner (e.g.,  Hofmann et al., 2009 ;  Shiv & Fedorikhin, 1999 ) in which one of the two processes may control mouse trajectories first – typically the more automatic, or System 1 process ( Kahneman, 2011 ) – and then yield control to the slower, more deliberative process, resulting in a mouse-track that initially veers towards the tempting item and then abruptly reverses towards the goal-congruent item.

Second, the researchers used an Area Under the Curve (AUC) metric to measure whether participant mouse-trajectories in trials involving self-control were different from trials in which healthy objects were pitted against inedible objects (e.g., a pencil). In these comparison (control) trials, participants were simply required to identify the healthy food item and move their mouse towards it. These comparison trials did not involve competition between two items and served as a baseline to measure trajectories in contexts not requiring control. The AUC measure for self-control trials was greater than the AUC measure for comparison trials.

Third, researchers found that individuals who are more successful at self-control show significantly less real-time conflict in only self-control-relevant choices. Thus, the mouse-track deviation towards the tasty items was generally greater for individuals with low self-control compared to individuals with high self-control. These results are illustrated in Figure-4.

Experimental psychologists and economists agree that people generally tend to accept smaller sums of money sooner compared to larger sums of money later. This pattern of behavior implicitly discounts future rewards. This general phenomenon is referred to as temporal discounting and has been applied to lapses in willpower, health outcomes, consumption choices over time, and personal finance decisions. Unsurprisingly, temporal discounting is thought to be inextricably linked with self-control (e.g.,  Ainslie, 1975 ;  Ballard et al., 2017 ;  Frederick et al., 2002 ).

The classical view of economics assumes that in temporal discounting people discount a future reward by a fixed percentage for each unit of time they must wait. If the discount rate is 5% per year, a person should equally prefer $1000 now and $1050 a year from now. Similarly, the same person should also equally prefer $1000 in one year and $1050 in two years. This view proposes that the increase in subjective rewards between two time periods only depends on the duration between. Mathematically, the subjective reward

A well-established literature has documented that people tend not to make choices consistent with a constant increase in subjective reward time periods. Despite its success, there are some significant explanatory gaps associated with the hyperbolic model. First, and most importantly, the model does not provide any mechanistic explanation of  why  temporal discounting tends to follow hyperbolic curves. There are no theoretical models in self-control that can be algorithmically linked to hyperbolic discounting, and it is unclear how the hyperbolic curve emerges. Second, there is considerable evidence that discounting is elevated when people are hungry, tired, emotionally aroused, or generally less able to attend to the goal of maximizing reward ( Giordano et al., 2002 ;  Li, 2008 ;  Van den Bergh et al., 2008 ;  Wang & Dvorak, 2010 ). Increased resources tend to change the shape of the hyperbolic curve closer so that it begins to approximate an exponential curve. Thus, decreased cognitive resources tend to make the discounting curve more hyperbolic and more ‘irrational’, whereas increased cognitive resources tend to make the discounting curve more exponential and therefore more consistent with ‘rational’ models of decision making. The mechanistic source of such effects remains unclear.

In the cybernetic loop of the CGSM, the maximum-reward-later represents the goal; options that are exercised later for greater reward are more similar to this goal than options that are exercised sooner for less reward. The Comparison with Goal States Model (CGSM), rooted in the PDP tradition and in cybernetic principles, consists of two elements: The first element consists of a biologically plausible, unsupervised network that creates distributed representations of goals and available options; the second element consists of a cybernetic loop that compares the distributed representation the goal to the distributed representation of each available option. The preference for options with similar features to the goal is amplified and the preference for options with dissimilar features is dampened – even if such dissimilar options were initially pre-potent. We next consider implications of the CGSM related to the nature of self-control, levers that can help instantiate and improve self-control, and its connection with cognitive-control.

The CGSM does not conceptualize self-control as a dual process framework:  Dual processes frameworks for self-control have been frequently proposed ( Chaiken S. & Trope, 1999 ;  Heatherton & Wagner, 2011 ;  Kahneman, 2011 ), but are explanatorily limited ( Hayden, 2019 ) and are sometimes inconsistent with empirical observations related to self-control ( Stillman et al., 2017 ). While the CGSM often features the influence of dual tendencies – immediately-attractive and goal-influenced – it is not a dual-process framework. Rather, operative tendencies interact and compete with each other within a single framework and result in emergent phenomena that recapitulate important empirical observations related to self-control. Relatedly, the CGSM does not rely on deductive logical inferences. This suggests that controlled action may be possible in non-human animals – even those that are generally thought to be incapable of such reasoning (see  Beran, 2021 , for gathering evidence consistent with this prediction).

The CGSM does not conceptualize self-control as another economic decision:  A generative and important view of self-control asks whether decisions featuring self-control are akin to any economic decision featuring a choice between competing options ( Berkman et al., 2017 ;  Neal et al., 2017 ). Economic decisions sometimes lead to the selection of less valued options, and such failures are often attributed to random noise. However, failures of self-control are overwhelmingly in the direction of succumbing to immediate temptation, and this pattern is not consistent with an economic decision between any two options ( Hayden, 2019 ).

The CGSM features diverse levers to instantiate and sustain self-control:  A lasting tradition in psychology has characterized self-control as the effortful inhibition of impulses. Consequently, according to this view, a person attempting to exert self-control requires must consciously recognize tempting impulses as being undesirable and then marshal cognitive and motivational resources to inhibit them.

Limitations and Future Directions:  There are two salient themes that may be appraised both as limitations as well as opportunities for further development of the CGSM: the relationship between cognitive control and self-control and the neural bases of control.

At least in principle, it is possible to simulate prototypical results in cognitive control via the CGSM. For example, trials in the Stroop task ( Stroop, 1935 ) may be conceptualized as a choice between a ‘name the word’ and ‘name the color’ option. In an incongruent trial (e.g., the word BLUE written in red ink), when asked to name the color, one might imagine a participant's goal representation to consist of memory traces related to each time she has named a color. In this case, the option representations might consist of the initially pre-potent ‘say blue’ option (due to a greater automaticity of word reading), and the goal-consistent but less-activated ‘say red’ option. The CGSM would, in time, amplify the ‘say red’ option although the reaction time for doing this would be, as is empirically observed to be the case, longer than in cases in which the participant was asked to name the word.

What causes us to limit the scope of the CGSM to self-control and remain agnostic to its explanatory potential in the domain of cognitive-control is because it is unclear to us that it is valid to assume that an experimenter's instruction can be instantiated as a goal similar to how the rejoinder to eat healthy might get instantiated as a goal. We can conceive of the possibility that a participant may have a goal of being accurate, or of fulfilling experimental instructions to the best of her ability, but the algorithmic process of whether and how such high-level goals might get reduced to actionable sub-goals (such as naming color) requires further development (or must be abandoned).

A second opportunity for future development of the CGSM consists of the neural bases of (cognitive- and self-) control. Consistent with the CGSM, evidence has pointed ( Cole et al., 2014 ;  Dixon et al., 2018 ;  Marek & Dosenbach, 2018 ) to the role of Fronto-Parietal Control Network (FPCN) that contains flexible hubs that regulate distributed systems according to current task goals. These flexible hubs are thought to implement control via feedback loops, similar to how feedback control is implemented in a thermostat (a prototype example of a device based on cybernetic principles) and other engineering and biological systems ( Cole et al., 2013 ). Importantly, such mechanisms were found to be common in the mammalian brain and, for example, played a prominent role in the motor system's control of muscles ( Diedrichsen et al., 2010 ). Such lines of evidence support the possibility that cognitive-control and self-control are implemented in the brain via cybernetic principles. However, considerable further work is required to meaningfully constrain the CGSM at  Marr's (1982)  implementation level of analysis.

Finally, we have discussed how using ideas related to distributed representations from the deep-learning/AI fields has been useful for computationally extending cybernetic principles that were previously specified at the descriptive level only. Conversely, cybernetic, goal-centric principles are largely missing from the deep-learning field ( McClelland, 2022 ). We invite consideration of the possibility that bridging this gap might enable a new wave of innovation in artificial intelligence.
