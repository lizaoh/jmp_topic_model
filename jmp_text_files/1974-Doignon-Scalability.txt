It is discussed in detail in Chapter 4 of the recent book of Krantz, Luce, Suppes, and  Tversky (1971), which contains, in particular, a presentation of several sets of sufficient conditions and a selected  bibliography. There is, however, a feature of all the axiomatic systems that have been proposed which makes them unsuitable  for applications in some cases of interest: They all assume that B, considered as a relation on A, is connected. In fact, in  many instances it is assumed that B = A X A (Debreu, 1958; Block & Marschak, 1960; Suppes & Zinnes, 1963; Kristof, 1967;  Pfanzagl, 1968). Why this may not be appropriate in some cases, is suggested by the two following empirical examples which  also provide the motivation of this work.  1. PSYCHOPHYSICS. Suppose that A is a sensory continuum, say, a set of visual, monochromatic stimuli, varying only in  intensity. Let P(a, b) be the probability that b is judged brighter than a by a subject. Fechner's Problem, as formulated by  Luce and Galanter (1963) and Falmagne (1971a, 1974) amounts to finding a function u, defined on the sensory continuum such that  P(a, b) < P(a', b') iff V n u(b) - u(a) < u(b') - u(a'). (2)  In principle, any pair (a, b) can be presented to the subject and the probability P(a, b) can be estimated. It cannot be  argued, however, that Fechner's Problem provides an instance of the difference measurement problem stated above in which B = A  X A. Indeed, whenever the brightness of a is sharply different from the brightness of b, P(a, b) = 0 or P(a, b) = 1. This  means that the "only if" part of the proposition (2) can be expected to hold at most for a specific subset of A X A containing  all the pairs of stimuli which are, in some sense, sufficiently close to each other.  2. PHYSICS. Our second example is suggested by Gay Lussac's Law for ideal gases, symbolized by the expression p(V, T) = RT/V,  in which p, T and V denote respectively the pressure, the absolute (Kelvin) temperature, the volume, and R is a constant.  Suppose that only the order properties of the function p are retained while observing the phenomenon. (Suppose, for example,  that only an ordinal scale is available to measure pressure.) Taking natural logarithm on both sides of the above equation, we  see that Gay Lussac's Law is an instance of the more general law, that there exists some function u such that  p(V, T) < p'(V', T') iff u(T) - u(V) < u(T') - u(V').  Notice that, in practice, not all the physical entities (V, T) will be obtainable. Conceivably, given a vessel of volume V,  and temperature T, the physical entity  DIFFERENCE MEASUREMENT 475  (V, T) will be obtainable if the resulting pressure p(V, T) does not exceed some upper bound. We might also expect that there  will be some lower bound to the pressure imposed by the practical conditions of the experiment.  This paper is a first step toward a systematic analysis of such cases from the point of view of difference measurement. This  clearly requires that the assumption B = A X A be relaxed. An obvious question is then: what reasonable assumptions can be  made on B ? Some reflection on the two examples above readily suggest the following "convexity" condition:  (C) If (a, b), (a", b") E B and u(b") - u(a") < u(b') - u(a') < u(b) - u(a), then, also (a', b') E B.  In our physical example, this condition means that a given entity (V, T) will be obtainable whenever its pressure is between  the pressures of two obtainable entities. An equally reasonable implication would result in our psychophysical example.  Condition (C) will play an important role in our discussion. This condition, however, although intuitively acceptable in the  case of our two examples, seems too strong for a comprehensive analysis. A basic, weaker "convexity" condition on B will also  be used, which essentially involves that C holds "locally," namely:  (C) If u(a) < u(a) < u(a"), u(b") < u(b) < u(b), and (a, b), (a", b") E B, then also (a', b') E B. REMARKS ON EMPIRICAL TESTING  Here, we consider the sets of axioms involved in Theorems 7-9 as substantive theories and discuss the possibility of empirical  testing. For concretness we suppose that the set A is the domain of some empirical scale (i.e., A is a real interval) and that  < is a weak order generated on B C A x A by some other empirical scale. In other words, we have a situation such as those  illustrated by our examples in Psychophysics and in Physics in Section I. Two of the four defining conditions of a structure  are thus automatically satisfied, namely, Definition 1 (i) and (ii). The convexity property (iii) is an important structural  (i.e., nonnecessary) condition. The monotonicity condition (iv) is necessary for realizability. We believe that, in most  cases, a mere inspection of the data, or even a "thought" experiment will be sufficient to decide whether these conditions are  fulfilled. The same remark applies to the positivity or nonnegativity of the structure.  Case 1. It is suspected, or known, that small differences are not defined. The relevant results are thus Theorems 7 and 8. By  hypothesis, we have a positive structure. The axioms remaining to be checked are: for Theorem 7: (AO), (AO'), (Al),  solvability, nondegenerateness, Archimedean; for Theorem 8: (A2), (A3).  This list suggests that the only axioms which will lead to extensive empirical testing are (A2) and (A3): This conclusion  derives from the following considerations. In Theorem 7, (AO'), solvability, nondegenerateness and the Archimedean property  must be investigated, but extensive data collection will probably be either unnecessary or practically difficult. As for (AO)  and (Al), they are implied, respectively by (A2) and (A3).  Case 2.- All small positive differences are defined. By arguments similar to those used in Case 1, one obtains that the  critical axiom to be tested is (AO).
