In a seminal paper  Doignon and Falmagne (1985)  suggested a set-theoretical framework for designing efficient procedures for knowledge assessment (see also  Müller, 1989 ). In this theory a  knowledge domain  is formalized by a nonempty set  Q  of problems or items. The set  Q  will be considered finite throughout this paper. A  knowledge state  is identified with the subset of items a person is capable of solving, and a set of possible knowledge states for a given domain  Q  is called a  knowledge structure  whenever it contains the emptyset ∅ and  Q . A knowledge structure is called a  knowledge space  if the set of states is closed for union. This notion is at the core of the theory of knowledge spaces that was developed and successfully applied over nearly two decades. A state-of-the art report is provided by  Doignon and Falmagne (1999) .

The querying of experts is one of the major sources of information for constructing a knowledge space. For some nonempty subsets  A ,  B  of the domain  Q  the expert is offered an assertion of the type 
                         “Failing all the items in  A  entails failing all the items in  B ”. 
                      The expert's task is to decide whether such an assertion can be accepted, or is to be rejected. The problem associated with such a querying procedure is that the number of possible assertions increases exponentially with the number of items in the domain. A reduction of the number of queries can be achieved by inferring an expert's responses from previously collected answers, which, however, requires to assume that decisions are completely reliable and consistent with a knowledge space. Various algorithmic procedures have been devised for implementing such an interactive questioning routine.  Müller (1989 ; see also  Dowling, 1993)  proposed a querying procedure based on a so-called implication relation, and  Koppen and Doignon (1990; see also Koppen, 1993)  suggested the QUERY-routine, which refers to the similar notion of an entail relation (see  Definition 1  below).  Düntsch and Gediga (1996)  also used entail relations to develop querying procedures for identifying knowledge structures that are more general than knowledge spaces. Practical applications, however, were not fully successful. The procedures turned out to be very sensitive to errors and inconsistencies in the expert's judgments ( Kambouri, Koppen, Villano, & Falmagne, 1994 ;  Schrepp & Held, 1995) . Simulation studies conducted by  Schrepp and Held (1995)  revealed that even small error rates in the expert's answers may induce large variations among the resulting knowledge spaces.  Cosyn and Thiéry (2000)  thus modified the QUERY-routine by introducing an error-handling mechanism. Their Pending Status QUERY (PS-QUERY) is designed to draw inferences on reliable judgments only.

The present paper is a follow-up on  Cosyn and Thiéry (2000) , where associating an entail relation to a set of given responses is captured by the notion of a closure on the set of all assertions.  Section 3  generalizes this approach by formalizing an inference mechanism as an extension of this closure to a set containing for each assertion  x  a positive instance  x 
                      +  and a negative instance  x 
                      − , representing acceptance and rejection, respectively. The extended closure has to meet several consistency constraints, one of which is closely related to consistent aggregations of information as treated by  Davey and Priestley (1990) . In  Section 4  it is shown that these constraints uniquely determine the form of the extended closure, which then actually corresponds to the inference mechanism of the QUERY and PS-QUERY routines. The implications of this result are discussed in  Section 5 .

Cosyn and Thiéry (2000)  based their consideration of the QUERY inference algorithm on the notion of a closure on the set of all (accepted) assertions. The PS-QUERY procedure was then developed as a generalization that retains some basic consistency properties of the QUERY procedure. The present paper takes a still more abstract point of view. As  Table 1  demonstrates, both accepted and rejected assertions play an essential role for drawing inferences. It thus seems to be straightforward to identify the inference algorithm with a closure on an extended set of assertions that not only comprises all accepted assertions, but also all rejected assertions. We then consider natural constraints on how the closure on this extended set is related to the closure of associating the least entail relation to any set of accepted assertions. We will investigate the restrictions that these constraints impose on the possible form of the closure on the extended set, and thereby characterize all possible inference algorithms.

The formalization of algorithmic procedures for querying experts in the context of knowledge space theory is based on the notion of an extension of a closure. The closure constituted by associating an entail relation to a subset of accepted assertions (cf.  Cosyn & Thiéry, 2000 ) is extended to a set containing for each assertion a positive and a negative instance, representing acceptance and rejection, respectively. Imposing a natural consistency constraint ( Definition 6 ) restricts the possible form of the extended closure ( Proposition 1 ), and leads to a concept that is closely linked to consistent aggregations of information ( Proposition 2 ) as formalized by  Davey and Priestley (1990) . For a noncontradictory querying algorithm that tries to recover an entail relation, however, still another consistency property is required. The extended closures that satisfy this additional constraint are exactly those which correspond to the inference mechanism the QUERY procedure rests on ( Proposition 3 ). This result explains why the PS-QUERY procedure, which was developed by  Cosyn and Thiéry (2000)  in order to incorporate error handling, had to retain this inference mechanism.

Application of the proposed framework is not limited to the querying of experts in the context of knowledge space theory. The presented results form a general basis for developing efficient algorithms that allow for consistent aggregation of information. Possible uses include procedures for discovering relationships among objects (or their attributes, respectively) within Formal Concept Analysis ( Wille, 1982 ;  Ganter & Wille, 1999 ), and Rough Set Data Analysis ( Pawlak, 1982 ;  Novotný & Novotný, 1992 ). In both approaches structures equivalent to entail relations are to be recovered (see  Düntsch and Gediga, 2001 , for details and further references). The assessment of individual semantic structures as suggested by  Heller (2000)  provides another field of application. In general, the framework may be applied to infer structures representing consistent aggregations of information whenever associating the corresponding structure to given partial information forms a closure.
