Waiting in queues has become an inevitable part of everyday life. People frequently find themselves waiting in queues at banks, supermarkets, fast-food chains, post offices and other governmental offices. When individuals have to wait, they experience costs associated with waiting. The traditional approach has been to focus on the opportunity cost of time wasted while waiting and to treat the waiting cost as economic in nature. In addition to economic costs, however, there are psychological costs involved in the process of waiting. Time in idleness and particularly waiting in idleness is perceived as longer than the real time ( Loehlin, 1959 ). Filled (occupied) time is reported to appear to pass more quickly than empty (un-occupied) time. There is evidence in the transportation field that people dislike out-of-vehicle waits three times more strongly than in-vehicle waits ( Ben-Akiva & Lerman, 1985 ). These findings imply psychological reactions to waiting, which have been linked to anxiety and stress ( Lazarus & Folkman, 1984 ).

Osuna (1985)  defines the ‘psychological cost of waiting’ as the psychological stress accumulated during the waiting period. He proposes that as individuals wait in idleness, they accumulate stress, where the rate of accumulation at a particular moment is represented by the intensity of stress the individual feels at that moment. As mentioned previously,  Osuna (1985)  and  Suck and Holling (1997)  model accumulated stress as a function of waiting time in a single-wait situation, such as waits individuals experience at a physician’s office or while being on hold during a telephone call. Essentially, they show that this stress increases during the waiting process and, thus, that the psychological cost of waiting (i.e. the accumulated stress) is a marginally increasing function of waiting time.

We focus on observable queues and model the impacts of service time distribution and queue length on the accumulated stress. In an observable queue, an individual waiting in a queue can monitor the number of people waiting in front of him or her. The service time is the time required to serve a given individual, and thus the total wait in a queue is the sum of all the service times of the people waiting in the queue ahead of our focal individual. That is, the total wait in a queue can be analyzed as the sum of consecutive waits in distinct states of the queue.

We extend  Osuna’s (1985)  single-wait model by representing the intensity of stress experienced by an individual at a certain time during the waiting process as a function of the total time he or she anticipates spending in the queue, which is the sum of the time already spent in the queue and the expected duration of the remaining wait. Similar to the assumption of a subjective probability distribution for the waiting time, as in Osuna’s work, we assume that the individual has a subjective probability distribution for the service time based on past experience with the service provider. We assume that the individual uses this information to estimate the total time he or she has to wait. The advantage of this augmentation is that we can model the impact of the dynamics of the queue progress on the individual’s accumulated stress.

In Section   3 , we provide a model of the stress accumulation process in an observable queue. We derive and present the TES of waiting in line for a general cost function in  Propositions 2 and 6 . We also derive TES in further detail for an exponential cost-of-time function, where the cost of time can be a constant, concave, linear or convex function of time. In line with  Suck and Holling (1997) , we focus on the expected stress and derive the wait in an observable queue, where the queue is characterized by a given length and a given service time distribution. We also examine an alternative version of the model.

As individuals wait in the queue to receive service, they reevaluate the probabilities associated with the total time they will have to wait but they do not update their beliefs about the priors of the service time distribution. Examples of such encounters include waiting for service in everyday coffee shops, regular banks, post offices or fast-food restaurants. However, for unfamiliar service encounters a Bayesian updating of the service time parameters would be required, which we do not consider in this article.

In the SA specification, as the wait in a state unfolds, the focal individual evaluates the current service time and infers implications for all the remaining service time completions. As a result, he or she feels optimistic for the rest of the remaining waits as the wait in the current state starts and gets pessimistic for the rest of the remaining waits if the wait in the current state exceeds the expected service time. Since the realizations of the service times comply with her priors, as assumed, she still does not need to update her beliefs about the performance of the service provider but she nevertheless feels the ups and downs of each wait as she progresses in the queue. We propose a model of the psychological cost of waiting in an observable queue based on  Osuna’s (1985)  work. The goal was to develop a way to compare the average “disutility” or “psychological cost of waiting” in a queue that could be attributed to any given set of queue characteristics.

Propositions 2 and 6  provide general extensions to the works of  Osuna (1985)  and  Suck and Holling (1997) , where the cost function

The results of the model, following from the dynamics of a waiting line, are not restricted to any specific cost or service time functions, and therefore the model results are useful for a wide variety of theoretical and experimental research. In addition, the evaluation of the model is a natural next step for future research.

The adaptation of the TES construct from a single wait in the literature to waiting in line enables us to observe the effects of several queue characteristics on the disutility of waiting. Assuming a linear cost-of-time function in line with the literature in operations research and economics, we obtain a measure of the psychological cost of waiting in observable queues.

An inherent assumption of our model of the psychological cost of waiting is that consumers do not update their beliefs about the service time distribution parameters while waiting in a queue. Thus, another natural next step for future research would be to relax this “no-update” assumption and allow for Bayesian dependence between stress intensities of consecutive states.
