Weaknesses and limitations of the  model are depicted and then a generalization of the model is presented along with a set of assumptions based on a hierarchial  stage-theoretic view of formal thought with which the generalization complied. The Boolean algebraic structure of  combinatorial thinking and the regular Boolean permutation group structure of hypothetico-deductive thinking are discussed.  The method of designating the formal transformations in the groups descriptive of formal thought using the  symmetric-difference operation is cited. The method of positive intersection generators is then employed to indicate the  primitive formal transformations proper to a level of formal thought. The two smallest proposed groups descriptive of formal  thought with their respective generators are displayed. Comparisons of the generalization with the Piagetian model are then  instituted indicating the greater scope, adequacy, and sufficiency of the generalization in the explanation of formal  operations. The usefulness of the generalization is indicated in various ways such as in the designation of the formal  transformation used by an individual in a hypotheticodeductive situation.  1. MODEL OF COMBINATORIAL THINKING  Jean Piaget (1957) contended that the system of formal operations has a lattice structure on which a group of four  transformations can be defined. An adolescent at the stage of formal operations can determine the set of all possible  combinations of a set of factors (either physical entities or propositions). That set of all possible combinations is a  lattice, for the set has two properties: a) the set is partially ordered with the ordering relation of inclusion; b) every  subset of two elements has a greatest lower bound (i.e., the greatest element included in both elements) and a least upper  bound (i.e., the smallest element which includes both elements). This four-group is an elementary Abelian group of order 4 with  two elements I and N being permutations and with R and C not being permutations. The identity transformation (I), the negation  transformation (N), the reciprocal transformation (R), and the correlative transformation (C) are the four transformations in  this four-group. However, it must be noted that this group-theoretic model is insufficient in explaining how  certain algebra elements which are operations can be transformed into other element operations in the same algebra. This "strong" view of reversibility is derived not from extensive empirical examinations of  reversibility behaviors but rather from a consideration of the reversibility indicated in the INRC group. The assumption of  formal cognitive transformations being involutive is compatible with the structure of the INRC group for the I, N, R, and C  transformations are each of order 2. This assumption is not derivable from Piagetian theory on formal operations but rather from a consideration of the principle  of parsimony as applied to the description problem for formal operations. A regular permutation group is the simplest group  with the fewest transformations that would comply with the first assumption stated and that would be sufficiently descriptive  of formal operations in being able to indicate the transformation that would relate any two particular propositions or n-ary  operations; thus, a regular permutation group defined on Fn would have a transformation mapping any operation in Fn into any  operation in Fn . It is entirely possible that a more elaborate and complex type of group provide sufficient description of  formal operations; however, the simplest type of group sufficiently descriptive of formal operationsregular permutation  groups-will be considered adequate in describing formal operational thought until this assumption is empirically disaffirmed  or is found to be inadequate in describing operations.  A regular permutation group is a transitive group whose order (number of transformations in the group) is equal to its degree  (number of elements being transformed) and whose every transformation transforms bijectively the elements in the set to be  transformed into the elements in the same set. SOME APPLICATIONS OF THE GENERALIZATION MODEL  To exemplify concretely the usefulness of this formal thought model generalization consider, first of all, the propositions  initially formed by an individual in understanding the law of centrifugal force as elaborated by Barbel Inhelder and Jean  Piaget (1958):  "If we let p stand for an increase in weight (and thus p its decrease) and let q stand for an increase in distance from the  center (and thus q a corresponding decrease if p, p, q, and q apply only to changes) and if we let r and Y stand for the  propositions asserting that one ball takes off before or after another and ro represent the statement that they take off  simultaneously, we see that the subject begins by assuming: p -* r and q --* r."  From this discussion let us assume that a subject exposed to the task which tests for understanding of the law of centrifugal  force considers a proposition of the form p -~ r first and then a proposition of the form q -~ r. With the proposed formal  thought model with its method of positive intersection generators the formal transformation that allows an individual to  consider a proposition of the form q r after considering a proposition of the form p --> r can be designated. Thus, the proposed formal thought model has much descrip-  552  BART  tive value in designating the types of formal transformations used by individuals in resolving complex relational problems.  Though other modes of explanation could be used to describe how an individual could generate a proposition q , r from  consideration ofa proposition p ~ r without recourse to a ~ transformation, those modes of explanation may be incompatible  with the concepts of reversibility and of cognitive operations forming systems. This method of designating formal  transformations used in problem-solving may prove to be quite valuable in such research as studies of cognitive styles in  formal thought using observational and interview techniques with professionals.  Another ramification of the generalization model is that qualitatively-distinct levels within the stage of formal operations  are indicated. Each level is marked by a 0,' defined on I',, for some value of n. The view of using 0, defined on I', for some  n to describe the level of cognitive functioning of an individual is not as complex as may appear; what is being contended is  that there are only 21, formal generators for some n for that individual which generate the elements in (15,z and which in  turn generate the elements in I'n with one element of I',, being given. Formal operational problems and situations may be  devised in such a manner as to demand for their resolution behaviors proper to a distinct level of the stage of formal  operations. Thus the measurement of advanced intelligence and higher cognitive development may be facilitated as measurement  procedures incorporating problems involving sets of bivalent factors are formed with the intent being the determination of the  level of formal thought for an individual.  If one realizes that alternative assumptions could be formulated, one recognizes that the generalization model cited is one of  various possible models for formal thought. However, this generalization model is simple, richly explanatory, and consistent  with many aspects of Piagetian theory. Thus, a crucial task for further research is the determination of the conditions under  which this generalization model for formal thought can be tested.
