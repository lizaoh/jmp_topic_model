JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY: 2, 190-195 (1965)  Nonstationarity in Paired-Associate Learning as Indicated by a Second Guess Procedure  JOHN R. BINFORD AND CHARLES GETTYS  University of Louisville, Louisville, Kentucky  Recent models for simple learning based on an all-or-none concept of the learning process have received extensive experimental  support. Analyses of the results of tests of such models have centered upon stationarity considerations, and analyses of  behavior prior to the last error have, under some circumstances, supported the stationarity implied by such models.  In the following study a second guess method was employed in which subjects were asked, whenever they made an erroneous  response, to make a second choice; under these conditions a simple all-or-none model implies stationarity. The results of the  study are not consistent with a simple all-or-none model and alternative models are suggested which may go further in  accounting for the results obtained.  Recognizing inadequacies in the linear model, several authors (Estes, 1959; Estes, Hopkins, & Crothers, 1960; Bower, 1961,  1962) have proposed a simple Markov model based upon an all-or-none concept of learning. In the linear model learning is  conceived as a process changing by small increments, but in all-or-none models the association of a response with a unitary  stimulus is assumed to occur entirely, or not at all, on a single trial. Applied to numerous sets of data the all-or-none  model has provided many predictions found to be in reasonable agreement with experimental results. Stationarity prior to the  last error, however, is implied by the model but has been found to obtain only when the number of responses from which a  choice is to be made is small; and several investigators, e.g., Suppes & Ginsberg (1963), have reported evidence of  nonstationarity.  The present study is concerned with evidences of nonstationarity arising when a somewhat different method than has been  previously reported, a "second guess method," is applied. The pattern model can be described in terms of two states-a  conditioned state and a guessing state. A conditioned state is one in which the subject has learned the correct response,  i.e., one in which the correct response is always given. A guessing state is one in which the subject chooses among  alternative responses at random and thus has a probability of 1f Nr (Nr = the number of responses) of being correct. Now if  the subject makes an error then he is not in a conditioned state; if required to produce a second response he will do so by  choosing at random from the  190  NONSTATIONARITY IN PAIRED-ASSOCIATE LEARNING 191  Nr - 1 responses, and the process should be stationary. An analysis of the data showing a higher relative frequency of correct  second guesses than 1/(N - 1), or an analysis revealing nonstationarity, would further indicate an inadequacy in a sample  pattern model.  METHOD  Forty-eight undergraduate students learned to associate single letters of the alphabet with common geometric figures, figures  being chosen on the assumption that stimulus learning would be at a minimum.  A 23 factorial design was employed, the factors being the size of the set of responses (Nr = 3 or Nr = 6), the methods of  learning (either paired-associate or "second guess"), and the number of experimenters (the authors). Either three or six  letters comprised a set of responses, the same letters being used for all subjects. For each subject a different random  assignment of response letters to stimulus figures was made, subject to the restriction that each response letter occurred  with equal frequency. Half the subjects thus learned to associate 3 letters, H, I, and J, with 12 geometric figures, one  letter to each of three figures; and half learned to associate 6 letters with 12 geometric figures, two figures per letter.  Half the subjects made one response per stimulus presentation-"normal learning" group; the remaining half were required to  make a second guess if their first response was incorrect.  At the beginning of the trial the subjects were handed a shuffled deck consisting of twelve 3 in. x 5 in. cards, on each of  which there was one of the figures. The subject preceded serially through the deck of cards; and after the subject had  responded in the prescribed fashion, i.e., either "normal learning" or "second guess," he was informed of the correct letter  for the particular figure. After the subject sorted serially through the deck, the deck was handed to the experimenter to be  shuffled and then returned to the subject for the next trial.  Learning was carried to a criterion of two consecutive trials on which all responses were correct, or to a total of eighteen  trials, whichever was the least.  RESULTS  Figure I shows the mean relative frequency of correct second guesses in blocks of three trials. For both Group III (3  responses) and Group IV (6 responses) the percentage of correct second guesses exceeds the chance levels predicted by a single  element pattern model. Predicted frequencies were compared with obtained frequencies and the resulting X2's, of 20.365 (d.f. =  5) and 120.746 (d.f. = 5) for, respectively, Group III and Group IV, are highly significant. An indication of nonstationarity,  i.e., for the relative frequencies to increase with trials, is also in evidence, particularly in the relative frequencies for  Group IV. X2's (Suppes & Atkinson, 1960) of 7.659 (0.25 > p > 0.10) and 46.712 (p < 0.001) for Groups III and IV,  respectively, indicate nonstationarity. DISCUSSION  This study was intended as a test of the single pattern model, the stringency of the test depending upon the second guess  method and upon the assumption that associative processes conceptualized in that model were the only important processes  operating in the experimental situation. The data of the first analysis reveal significant  194 BINFORD AND GETTYS  departures from the expected frequencies, the mean relative frequencies of correct second guesses (1) exceeding the predicted  frequencies and (2) showing a tendency to increase with trial. A prediction of the increase can be derived from the linear  model, but has not been derived in a sharp form, from various multipattern Markov models, and from Markov models in which  error elimination is a feature. Thus although the results do add further doubt as to the adequacy of the pattern model, they  do not-as here analyzed-say a great deal about the relative adequacy of the linear model and about various forms of Markov  Model. There is an indication of trend which is most notable for subjects learning the largest number of responses, but such a  result is a consequence of the multielement Markov Model, of the error elimination model, as well as of the linear model.  The results of the second analysis---the stationarity analysis of responses prior to the last error-came as something of a  surprise, particularly for the groups having the smaller numbers of responses (Groups I and III). A number of analyses have  indicated the process to be stationary, particularly when the response sets were small, but in this experiment Group I  exhibited a reliable trend and Group III did not. If the assumption of minimal stimulus learning was justified and if the  responses were "simple," then the pattern model is inappropriate and it is discredited by the results of the subjects of Group  I. The lack of trend in Group III, which is consistent with previous results, does indicate stationarity, but one is inclined  to suggest "other" factors: (a) if error elimination is a factor, one would expect clearest evidence of trend when there are a  substantial number of errors to be eliminated (in this experiment in Groups II and IV, as was the case), for small numbers of  responses the factor would be negligible; (b) recent studies of short term memory (Peterson, Peterson, & Miller 1961; Murdock,  1963; Reynolds, 1963) have revealed a rapid decrement in retention which is a function of activity following exposure of a  stimulus, rate of decrease being greatest when activity is greatest. It is reasonable to think that the subjects of Group I  followed a stricter schedule than those of Group II, who used the second guess procedure, the latter procedure, resulting in  longer trials and more intervening activity.
