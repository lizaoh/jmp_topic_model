GREENO  Indiana University, Bloomington, Indiana 47401  An analysis is given of a simple theory of paired-associate learning in which items may be retained for short intervals before  they are learned. Theoretical questions are discussed that involve parameters of the theory, and the problem of identifying  these parameters is analyzed. Analyses of several experiments lead to the conclusion that either there is no learning on  trials when the presented item is already in short-term memory, or an item usually is learned during the intervals between its  presentations. Results of one experiment that permits a choice between these two possibilities favor the assumption that  learning occurs mainly during the intervals between an item's presentations. Finally, some statistical methods are presented  for the general theory and for the special case that is supported best by the data.  In several recent discussions memory of individual items has been divided between two states. Different writers have given  various labels and interpretations to the states, but the analyses share an important common feature. An item in one of the  states will be remembered for only a short time, but an item in the other state will be remembered for quite a long time-long  enough to be called "learned" relative to the tests given in a specified experiment. Waugh and Norman (1965) distinguished  between two such states, calling them primary memory and secondary memory. Peterson (1966) distinguished between an activity  trace and a structural trace. Atkinson and Crothers (1964), Atkinson and Shiffrin (1965), and Bower (1964) distinguished  between short-term memory (sometimes called a buffer) and long-term memory. Bernbach (1965) and Friedman and Gelfand (1964)  postulated one memory state from which forgetting has a nonzero probability and another from which forgetting does not occur.  The present analysis investigates the roles of short-term and long-term memory in experiments where paired associates are  presented repeatedly. There is another possibility. Learning might be a kind of trial and error system with selection from a set of memory codes or  mnemonic devices. In this kind of system a study trial permits the subject to store a representation or code of the studied  item in memory. Using the idea of a selective process, the parameter d is the probability that the subject selects a code from the set when  the presented item is in State E or State G, and that the selection produces a code which is adequate for the experimental  criterion. The parameter c is the probability that the subject selects a code from the set when the presented item is in State  H and that the selection produces an adequate code. It would be natural to assume that the subject does not select a code when  he sees an item that he knows. This hypothesis is equivalent to the assumption c = 0. If the subject always selects when he  studies an item that he does not know and never selects when he sees an item that he knows, then d is the sampling probability  of the set of adequate codes. However, the value of a involves a psychological question of some interest. In buffer models cited above, and in the  consolidation hypothesis about learning and conditioning (e.g., McGaugh and Petrinovitch, 1966) it is assumed that important  events affecting learning occur after the time of reinforcement or presentation of information. This idea implies that a  should have a nonzero value. Also, if there is processing between presentations of an item, then the value of a should be  greater for longer interpresentation intervals, since these permit a longer time for the processing to occur. This  interpretation of the consolidation idea regarding verbal memory has been suggested by Landauer (personal communication) and  Peterson (1966).  To summarize: the value of a, and the relative values of c and d relate to interesting psychological hypotheses. According to  the consolidation or buffer-processing hypothesis, a should be nonzero. According to the reinforcement or contiguity  hypothesis, c should be greater than or equal to d. And according to the hypothesis that learning is a selective process which  operates by trial and error, c should be small or zero.  I. PROBLEM OF IDENTIFIABILITY  The questions considered in the introduction relate to the parameters of a welldefined theory. This suggests a direct method  for investigating the questions empirically: Run an experiment and estimate the parameters of Eq. 3a. Then if c turned out to  be small or zero, the idea of a selective process would be supported. If c were  434 GREENO  equal to or larger than d then the reinforcement or contiguity idea would be supported. And if a were substantially, larger  than zero, the cdnsoiidation-buffer idea would be supported.  Unfortunately, the direct test cannot be carried out under the usual experimental conditions. The reason is that the  parameters of Eq. 3 are not identifiable; in particular, we are unable to separately estimate the three learning parameters.  Two different notions of identifiability are in the literature. One idea is a standard topic in econometrics (see, e.g.,  Goldberger, 1964, pp. 306-318). General formulations of the problem have been given by Haavelmo (1944), Hurwicz (1950),  Koopmans and Reiers6l (1950) and Wald (1950). Important applications of the concepts include an extensive analysis by  Koopmans, Rubin and Leipnik (1950) of theories which specify systems of linear equations, and two papers by Fisher (1959,  1961) which extend the theory to some important nonlinear cases. Blackwell and Koopmans (1957) and Gilbert (1959) have  presented some results for ergodic Markov chains with stationary state probabilities. Barankin (1960) and Shimizu (1966) have  investigated the relationship between identifiable parameters and sufficient statistics. In psychology, the problem of  identification has been discussed in relation to multiple factor analysis (Reiers6l, 1950) and to latent class analysis  (Madansky, 1960; McHugh, 1956).  Using terminology specified by Hurwicz (1950), a theory stated in general terms with free parameters is called a model. When  each free parameter is given a numerical value the model specifies a distribution F of (possibly) unobservable variables. A  distribution G of observable variables is produced by an operation 9- performed on the theoretical distribution F. That is,  G=.l->F.  The specified distribution F, together with the specified operation is called a structure:  and the structure is said to generate G. The model consists of a class of structures. Since G is a distribution of observable  variables, it has parameters that can be estimated by whatever statistical methods are appropriate to the distributions  involved. This is the problem of estimation. Given estimates of the parameters of G, we wish to specify the particular  structure S which generated G. This is the problem of identification.  In the simplest case, for any observed distribution Gi permitted by the model, there is just one structure Si that can  generate G; . In this case, we say that the model is identifiable, otherwise it is nonidentifiable.2  2 In general, degrees of identification power can be distinguished, in which a finite number or a denumerable number of  structures could generate a given observed distribution G; . These cases do not arise for the present model, partly because  each parameter is a probability. In the case that we are considering, the model either is uniquely identifiable or the number  of structures which could generate a given Gi is nondenumerable. When a certain property is shared by all structures that can generate a distribution G; , we say that the model is  identifiable with respect to that property. In particular, the class of structures compatible with G; may all have the same  value of a certain parameter a. In that case, the model is uniquely identifying with respect to the value of a, and we say  that a is an identifiable parameter.  The problem of identification may be solved by introducing certain a priori assumptions called identifying restrictions. Let M  denote a model (recall that M is a class of structures). If we make a priori assumptions, say, about the parameters of the  model, we narrow the class of permissible structures to a subset of M. Let M; denote a submodel of M which arises from the  assumptions of M plus one or more a priori restrictions, O; . If there is just one structure S;; in M; that is compatible with  an observed distribution G; , then we say that the submodel M; is identifiable in the model M. Alternatively, we may say that  the model M is identifiable over the submodel M; . When this condition occurs, then the restrictions O; are called identifying  restrictions for M, or alternatively, that M is identifiable under the restrictions O; .  The Hurwicz-Koopmans notion of identifiability is concerned with the question of whether the probabilities of observable  events correspond to a single point in the parameter space. A similar but nonidentical idea was considered by Greeno and  Steiner (1964), but our analysis was concerned with whether a sequence of observations corresponds to a single sequence of  theoretical states. The notions are similar enough to be confused easily, so the situation would be clearer if we adopted  different names for the different concepts. CONCLUSIONS  The data of a paired-associate memorizing experiment may or may not agree with the model given as Eq. 3. If they do, then  there are just four parameters that can be estimated meaningfully, and in most applications only two parameters give  information about the important transition parameters a, c, d, and h. The analyses given here are relevant to the question of  how to interpret the values of the identifiable parameters. According to the interpretation given the results of White's  experiment, the important theoretical parameters are h, the probability of short-term retention between successive  presentations, and a, the probability of achieving long-term storage during the interval between presentations.  Previously, I thought that the effect of spacing supported an idea that pairedassociate learning is mainly a process of  differentiation (Greeno, 1964). The selectivecoding hypothesis sketched early in this paper is a more specific version of the  differentiation idea. The results presented here, and especially the results of White's experiment, support a different  interpretation. The effect of spacing now appears to be the result of temporal characteristics of the learning process (as was  expected, apparently, by almost everyone except me). However, the result does not contradict ideas like the selective-coding  hypothesis. The result says that processing occurs during the interval between presentations, and that this fact accounts for  the effect of spacing. But this does not bear on the nature of the processing that occurs. It may be that mechanical rehearsal  during the interval is what produces learning. Or it may be that a process like random selection among possible mnemonic codes  takes place. Or it may be that the processing of items in short-term memory consists of a kind of comparison and discovery of  relationships among the items, and that an important feature of paired-associate memorizing involves imposing some kind of  cognitive structure on the items to be learned. The point here is that the effect of spacing apparently does not tell us about  the characteristics of processing, and other lines of investigation will be needed to discover how (rather than when)  processing in short-term memory produces effective retention.  As was mentioned earlier, none of the experiments given here involved any response acquisition or formation of response pools.  However, an experiment has been reported now (Landauer and Rubin, 1966) in which the same kind of result was  PAIRED-ASSOCIATE LEARNING WITH SHORT-TERM RETENTION 463  found, and the responses in that study were short words that the subject did not have available at the start of the  experiment. Some results that made the restriction to associative learning seem plausible were Waugh's (1963, 1967) results  for free-recall learning, where two presentations of an item had an effect greater than a single presentation, but where the  interval between the two presentations had no effect. This raised the possibility that spacing had no effect when the task was  to learn some responses. However, Melton (1966) now has found an effect of spacing in a free-recall experiment like the ones  obtained in paired-associate experiments and discussed in this paper. Further investigation is needed to discover what makes a  spacing effect occur in some free-recall experiments and not in others. However, it now appears that independence between  retention and spacing is not a general property of tasks involving response learning.  Finally, a disclaimer needs to be made regarding completeness. The main facts involved in these analyses are (1) that two  presentations are more effective if they are spaced by a few seconds than if they are immediately successive, when retention  is tested several seconds or more after the presentations, and (2) that this difference breaks down in a particular way when  the conditions are compared between lists. I have dealt with these facts because they give leverage in distinguishing between  submodels of a general theory. However, the present analysis has ignored some other important temporal effects. First, the  data given in Fig. 2.1 suggest that the effectiveness of spaced trials reaches some maximum value and then declines. Young  (1966) has obtained data with the same property, and Young observed more points on the curve and took sufficient observations  to establish the effect reliably. I do not believe that any reasonable submodel of the theory considered in this ppaper will  permit retention to be a nonmonotonic function of the interval between presentations. Another effect that contradicts the  general model was obtained by Peterson et al. (1962), who gave tests after different intervals following two presentations.  When the test came very soon after the second presentation (2 or 4 sec), retention was better if the presentations had been  massed than if they had been spaced. I do not think that any reasonable submodel of Eq. 3 will permit this effect either.  These facts emphasize that Eq. 3 does not tell the whole story, and further investigation will be needed to determine what  kinds of processes occur that are not included in the model considered here.
