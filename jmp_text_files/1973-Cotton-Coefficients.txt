HARRIS  University of California, Santa Barbara, California 93106  In this article we show that a particular mathematical learning model, the BowerTrabasso (1964) concept identification model  taken together with an assumption of independence of replicate measurements, implies the existence of substantial and  statistically significant performance differences across individuals. The individual differences in turn imply a sizeable  reliability coefficient. These results contradict naive intuition, for this model (like many other mathematical models o learning) assumes that all individuals begin the experiment with identical parameter values for the process under study. Thus  at least one such model has the characteristic of implying the generation of individual performance differences among  Examination of data from an experiment by Cotton shows that the Hoyt reliability coefficient under classical test theory, a  lower bound for the (composite) reliability of total scores for a series of trials, increases with the number of trials  analyzed and exceeds the corresponding theoretical values implied by the Bower-Trabasso model. An experiment by Levine was  also analyzed because its use of blank trials between feedback trials permitted direct calculation of composite reliability  (or more properly stated composite consistency). For this experiment, the theoretical development just discussed (Case I) was  used together with Restle's hypothesis selection model specialized to include a local consistency assumption, the so-called P2  model of Gregg and Simon (Case II). Moderate conformity of empirical and theoretical reliabilities was found, with  discrepancies between observed and predicted values usually being smaller with Case II. However, the Hoyt reliability  coefficient is not a lower bound for composite reliability in Case II, because composite reliability is underestimated when  identical stimuli are not used for comparable trials.  Despite the Bower-Trabasso assumption of no initial differences, it seems reasonable to attribute the difference between  predicted and obtained reliabilities to preexisting individual differences. Implications of the tentative conclusion that  individual differences in concept identification performance are attributable to a combination of preexisting differences and  differences induced in a current task are discussed briefly. (A  distinguished recent exception to this generalization is Atkinson and Paulson's (1972) joining of a learning theory and a test  theory while developing an optimization procedure for teaching Swahili.) Most mathematical learning theories assume that, at  the beginning of any given learning experiment, the subjects are identical on all properties relevant to the experimental  task, i.e., have identical parameter values.  This is not to say that learning theorists generally believe that their subjects are functionally identical at the beginning  of experimentation. Denials of this proposition are common in the literature. Apparently this identity is usually assumed for  convenience, to simplify mathematical proofs about group performance, and to avoid the many complex calculations necessary if  different parameters are estimated for each subject. We take it as a given that most, if not all, mathematical learning  theories assuming identical organisms make prediction errors which could be reduced if individual differences parameters were  assumed and their values estimated.  The present paper is intended to show to what degree individual differences in behavior defined by classical test theory can  be expected in a learning experiment, even when no individual differences parameters are assumed in an associated learning  theory. Heretofore, most mathematical learning theory papers have used the variance of a statistic such as the trial of last  error as an index of individual differences in performance. The present analysis uses as its measures various reliability  coefficients, thus providing a closer tie between learning theory and test theory. We will show that it is possible to compute  the numerical value of one or more reliability coefficients implied by the Bower-Trabasso (1964) concept identification model  or by the closely related Restle (1962) model. Events and states in these concept models will be coordinated with observed  scores, true scores, and error scores from classical test theory. These models actually conform to strong true score theory  (Novick, 1966; Lord & Novick, 1968, Part 6) since the distribution of each kind of scores just mentioned is known subject to  specification of not more than two parameter values.  If indeed subjects do differ behaviorally more than implied by a model with constant parameter values from person to person,  then the obtained reliability coefficients should exceed the theoretical values deduced in this paper. Thus this study serves  both to show relations between specific learning theories and test theory and to test the assumption of no relevant individual  differences prior to training on the task.  In brief, the Bower-Trabasso model assumes that a subject receiving certain specified concept identification tasks will be in  one of three states on each trial: If in State C (conditioned state), he will make a correct response on the present  INDIVIDUAL DIFFERENCES 389  trial and will stay in State C for the next trial and thus, by implication, forever. If in State E (error state), he will make  an error on the present trial and may be in any one of the three states on the next trial. If in State S (chance success  state), he will make a correct response on the present trial but may be in either State E or State S on the next trial. The major reason for this decision  is that test theory is poorly developed for use with any other definition. Lord and Novick use the definition of Tk as the  expectation of Xk in most of their book; without this definition most of the standard reliability formulas do not estimate the  ratio of true score variance to observed score variance, as desired. Lord and Novick (1968, pp. 39-44) show that it is always  possible to convert a model using Platonic true scores into a model using classical true scores. Other options mentioned by  Lord and Novick (use of the mode or median of the propensity distribution) would require complete reworking of test theory.  Note that what Lord and Novick call a specific true score is a true score under a particular test condition, with the average  across conditions being a generic true score (Lord & Novick, 1968, p. 174). An observed score selected at random within a  condition is still an unbiased estimator of a specific true score for that condition; an observed score selected at random  from and within conditions is an unbiased estimator of a generic true score. Thus classical test theory applies to both  specific and true scores.  From a learning theory standpoint, we think of one other plausible true score definition: Let Tk = 0 if a subject is in State  C, Tk = 1 if a subject is in State E, and Tk = 1/2 if a subject is in State S. Because there would now be three possible true  scores, rather than two as in Eq. (6), this would give closer correspondence in one sense to the 3-state Bower and Trabasso  (1964) model. However, it is easy to show that -ff(Xk I State S) = 0, making Xk a biased estimate of Tk for that case.  Conversion of the definition of Tk to make Xk an unbiased estimator for each of the three states would simply force Xk = Tk,  making the test theory pointless. We will invoke a closely related model, a hypothesis selection model with local consistency,  since that model would average across random stimulus sequences to yield a special case of the Bower-Trabasso model (Cotton,  1972; Gregg & Simon, 1967, Model P2 ; Restle, 1962). It seems desirable to apply Cases I and II to Levine's data even though  neither is considered a perfect representation of the process under study. CONCLUDING REMARKS  We close with further comments on one point. Though extrapolation from one experimental context to another has obvious risks,  we note that intelligence score differences or achievement test differences from one individual to another may be due at least  in part to the same learning processes producing substantial reliability or consistency values with the two theories just  examined. We believe the assumption of no original differences (made for convenience by Bower and Trabasso and by Restle) to  be too strong. However, to the degree that empirical reliabilities exceeded our theoretical reliabilities, we can interpret  individual differences in the present context (at least) to be a composite of experimentally induced differences and  preexisting differences. Some preexisting differences, in turn, may prove to be environmentally induced-either reflecting  increased learning parameter values because of greater prior learning or reflecting greater opportunity for subsequent  learning because of excellence on early tasks. Future studies in the individual differences area may well be directed to  separation oexperimentally induced and preexisting  420 COTTON AND HARRIS  differences. It has often been observed that variability of performance of a group of individuals increases with practice.  This paper may have contributed to a scientific explanation of this phenomenon.
