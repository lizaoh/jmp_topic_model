Introduced in the seminal work of  Doignon and Falmagne (1997) , the notion of well-gradedness has proven to be useful in several theoretical applications: knowledge spaces ( Doignon & Falmagne, 1999 ), learning spaces ( Falmagne & Doignon, 2011 ), media ( Eppstein, Falmagne, & Ovchinnikov, 2008 ), stochastic theories for the evolution of preferences ( Falmagne, 1996, 1997 ), etc. Informally, a family

                       to another set

                         F

                       of alternatives, it is natural to study the well-gradedness of those families of order relations on  
                         X 
                       that may be representative of the preference structures of an economic agent (or a group of economic agents). This motivates the analysis of the well-gradedness of families of (i) total preorders, (ii) semiorders, and (iii) interval orders, since these types of binary relations are employed for the modelization of preferences in many applications.

Concerning (i), the classical way to describe an economic agent’s preference structure on a given set  
                         X 
                       of alternatives is indeed by means of a total preorder, i.e., a binary relation that is simultaneously transitive and complete. This approach to preference modeling has many advantages, the most important being that–under suitable separability conditions–a total preorder is representable by means of a utility function (see  Aleskerov, Bouyssou, & Monjardet, 2007 ;  Bridges & Mehta, 1995  for an extensive overview of the topic). However, the total preorder approach to preference modeling has some serious drawbacks, since the two main assumptions of transitivity and completeness are hardly satisfied in most real-world applications. That is why alternative modelizations of preference structures–using, for instance, (ii) semiorders, and (iii) interval orders–have naturally emerged over time.

Semiorders originally appeared in 1914 in the work of  Wiener (1914) , albeit under a different name and with a cumbersome notation. Nonetheless, the introduction of semiorders is usually attributed to  Luce (1956) , who formally defined them within the framework of preference theory. Luce’s seminal work was motivated by the necessity to deal with natural cases of binary preferences displaying an intransitive symmetric part. The importance of semiorders stems from the vast range of applications that require a more flexible modelization than the one a total preorder may provide, due to the fact that the agent’s preference structure is established up to a constant “threshold of discrimination/perception”. (See Chapter 2 of the monograph by  Pirlot & Vincke, 1997  for a vast account of these applications.) Among the several contributions to this very active field of research, let us mention the classical representation results of  Scott and Suppes (1958) , as well as the more recent papers  Beja and Gilboa (1992) ,  Candeal and Induráin (2010) ,  Giarlotta and Watson (2016)  and  Manders (1981) .

Successively to the introduction of semiorders,  Fishburn (1970)  identified a weaker notion of binary preference, called interval order. The idea of an interval order is to associate to each alternative an interval of evaluations (which may well vary along with alternatives) rather than a crisp number, with the purpose of taking into account more general phenomena of imprecision or indecisiveness. Since the original work of Fishburn, several contributions to this field of research have appeared: see the monograph by  Fishburn (1985) , as well as, among the many others,  Beja and Gilboa (1992) ,  Bosi, Candeal, Induráin, Olóriz, and Zudaire (2001)  and  Fishburn (1973) .

The importance of semiorders and interval orders in preference modeling motivates  per se  an accurate analysis of the properties that they internally satisfy “as a whole”. The well-gradedness of the families of interval orders and semiorders–proved by Doignon and Falmagne in  Doignon and Falmagne (1997) –goes exactly in this direction. In fact, the authors show that these families are internally well structured,

                       in the sense that they allow a smooth transition of states without changing the basic semantics of the preference representation. This is a fundamental feature in view of the development of stochastic theories describing the evolution of binary preferences through the random occurrence of quantum tokens of information ( Falmagne, 1996, 1997; Falmagne & Doignon, 1997 ).

The main result of this paper is motivated by the same rationale. In fact, we prove the well-gradedness of a special family of  bi-preferences , that is, a suitable collection of ordered pairs of nested binary relations on the same ground set. Bi-preferences are designed to describe preference structures that are more articulated than those usually represented by a single ordering relation. The special family of bi-preferences that we show to be well-graded is that of  normalized NaP-preferences .

Informally, a  NaP-preference  (necessary and possible preference) is a pair of nested binary relations such that the properties of transitivity and completeness are satisfied in the following combined form: the smaller relation (called the  necessary preference ) is transitive, the larger relation (called the  possible preference ) is complete, and the two relations are mutually consistent with respect to transitivity and completeness. Then  normalized  NaP-preferences are those having an antisymmetric necessary component (that is, the smaller relation is a partial order).

NaP-preferences have originally been introduced in applications to the field of multiple-criteria decision aid, within the framework of a methodology called  robust ordinal regression  ( Greco, Mousseau, & Słowiński, 2008 ). In this methodology, all information provided by an economic agent on a set of multi-dimensional alternatives (i.e., in the presence of several evaluation criteria) is used to build a family of additive value functions that are “compatible” with the modelization. Then, a NaP-preference structure naturally arises from this family of functions by using universal and existential quantification. More recently, NaP-preference structures have been analyzed from an axiomatic point of view ( Giarlotta, 2014, 2015; Giarlotta & Greco, 2013; Giarlotta & Watson, 2016 ), also in relation to interval orders, semiorders, and, more generally,

NaP-preferences on a set of alternatives can be arranged into a poset (partially ordered set), whose ordering relation is semantically connected to the informative content of their two components. Specifically, the two relations in a NaP-preference provide different types of information on the economic agent’s structure: “positive” for the necessary component, and “negative” for the possible one. Upon taking into account both types of information (and allowing no compensation between them), NaP-preferences are partially ordered according to their stability. From this perspective, the fact that the poset of (normalized) NaP-preferences is well-graded paves the way toward developing a theory of a smooth transition of states via the random introduction of quantum tokens of information, similarly to what has been done for semiorders ( Falmagne & Doignon, 1997 ). The purpose of this paper is to show that a stochastic theory of finite NaP-preferences would be well-founded, provided that we restrict our attention to normalized ones.

The paper is organized as follows. In Section   2  we recall basic notions related to preferences and NaP-preferences. In Section   3  we define well-gradedness for bi-preferences, and show that some families of NaP-preferences fail to be well-graded. In Section   4  we prove the main result of the paper, namely, normalized NaP-preferences form a well-graded family. Section   5  hints at further directions of research.

First we establish the basic terminology on preferences, and recall the notion of a well-graded family of binary relations. Successively, we introduce bi-preferences, focusing in particular on those satisfying suitable structural properties (necessary and possible preferences).

In this paper we have extended to a bi-preference setting the notion of a well-graded family of binary relations, introduced by  Doignon and Falmagne (1997) . We have proved that special types of bi-preferences, called normalized NaP-preferences, do form a well-graded family. Future research on the topic goes in three main directions.

First, we wish to fully determine the well-gradedness of classes of (mono-)preferences satisfying various types of  weak  
                      
                         
                            ( 
                            m 
                            , 
                            n 
                            ) 
                         
                      
                      -Ferrers properties  (in the sense of  Giarlotta & Watson, 2014 ), which generalize the classical Ferrers condition (i.e.,  
                         
                            ( 
                            2 
                            , 
                            2 
                            ) 
                         
                      -Ferrers) and semitransitivity (i.e.,  
                         
                            ( 
                            3 
                            , 
                            1 
                            ) 
                         
                      -Ferrers). This appears to be interesting in view of the fact that interval orders and semiorders are indeed well-graded ( Doignon & Falmagne, 1997 ).

Second, it appears of some interest to explore the well-gradedness of types of bi-preferences–and, in general, “multi-preferences”, defined as finite sequences of nested relations–in which the properties of mixed completeness  (MC)  and/or transitive coherence  (TC)  are suitably adapted. In fact, the related analysis can be undertaken for even more general (possibly infinite) relational structures, which are suitable metric spaces–whose metric is based on the symmetric difference of the relations–with the property that all pairs of elements are at finite distance.

Finally, and possibly more important, the property of well-gradedness of the poset of normalized NaP-preferences suggests the implementation of a probabilistic approach (e.g., Markov processes) on suitable bi-preference structures. The development of this topic should follow the same path– mutatis mutandis –employed for a dynamic analysis of knowledge spaces and learning spaces ( Doignon & Falmagne, 1999; Falmagne & Doignon, 1988, 2011 ), or for designing stochastic theories that describe the evolution of preference structures ( Falmagne, 1996, 1997; Falmagne & Doignon, 1997 ).
