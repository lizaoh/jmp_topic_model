Word stem and word fragment completion tasks are a set of verbal tasks and games that require a participant (or player) to determine one or more words that fit a particular letter pattern. Such tasks have been used for many decades to understand lexical memory access (cf.   Graf & Williams, 1987; Nelson, McEvoy, & Bajo, 1988 , for reviews), and they have emerged as a major paradigm used to study implicit learning ( Graf & Mandler, 1984; Roediger, Weldon, Stadler, & Riegler, 1992 ), as well as verbal fluency (e.g.   Benton & Hamsher, 1976; Ruff, Light, Parker, & Levin, 1997; Thurstone, 1938 ). Despite their use in many contexts, no mathematical or computational models exist that describe the cognitive, lexical, and memory processes through which humans actually perform the task (although  Bodner, Masson, & Caldwell, 2000  used a multinomial processing tree model to examine the joint role of episodic versus automatic influences in the task). Existing research has provided no mechanistic account of the processes involved, and thus no testable theory of how performance in these tasks relates to other verbal skills.

Past research on word stem completion has established that the ease with which a word stem is completed is correlated with a number of linguistic variables. One fairly comprehensive data set was provided by  Graf and Williams (1987) , who published stem completions for 40 word stems. Initial examination of their data showed strong positive correlations with verbal frequency and number of meanings, and a negative correlation with word length. In our initial investigation, our goal was to understand these basic results using a regression model and to determine whether these predictors were able to account for distinct aspects of the word stem completion.

Graf and Williams (1987)  collected a comprehensive set of data for unconstrained-length word stem completion. 100 participants each saw 40 3-letter word stems (with the three letters all appearing at the start of the word; e.g., STA___), and were asked to write six completions, taking roughly 1 min per stem. Distributions of both the first response and the remaining responses were reported across stems (we refer to these below as Response 1 and Response 2–6). Words that appeared rarely were grouped into an ‘other’ category for each stem. This lead to a data set with 1716 total response categories, and 1716−40=1676 unique word completions when the ‘other’ responses were excluded. One of the main conclusions of Graf and Williams was that the frequency of the completed word is a good predictor of the likelihood of completion, although not perfect. To develop a regression model of these factors, we transcribed all responses from the Appendix of the original paper.

This relationship is depicted in  Fig. 1

It should be noted that these correlations only include words that were given as a response in the experiment. Including all words that were in either corpus produced lower correlations, but including a larger database of words and word-like units that appeared in neither the corpora (and thus occur roughly less than 1 time in 1,000,000) nor in the response set would likely drive the correlation higher, as it could include thousands of potential answers that neither source contained data for, but are essentially 0 for each (cf.   Shaw, 1997 , for a discussion of these issues).

Graf and Williams showed that along with frequency, the length of the completed word is a strong predictor of stem completion probability, such that shorter words are more likely to be produced than longer words. However, word length is highly skewed in the lexicon, meaning there are more short words than long words, and shorter words tend to be more frequent in natural written language, and so these effects may be simply a result of the statistics of language. This final conclusion is most likely a consequence of the fact that the procedure allowed for only six responses. Any specific word from a stem with more unique completions will tend to be selected less often, simply because the six responses are spread over more possible completions.

These findings suggests that the stem completion process may be based on memory retrieval. That is, when a person reads, associations will sometimes form between letters or letter combinations within the word, and the word itself. Later, when a word-part is shown, memory is searched based on these associations. A memory retrieval hypothesis may contrast with a process more like the kind typically used by computer software for part–whole pattern matching, which involves fast exhaustive search over the entire lexicon, discarding any targets that do not match. The memory retrieval hypothesis is consistent with theories of naturalistic decision making in a number of domains, which suppose that cues in the environment activate past relevant memories that help us make decisions and solve problems ( Klein, 1989; Mueller, 2009 ). In the next section, we will describe a computational model of this hypothesis, and determine whether it can indeed provide a reasonable account for the data.

To date, few if any models have been developed to explain word stem completion ( Jacobs, Rokers, Rudra, & Liu, 2001  published the only relevant model we know of, but it was focused on a distinct different behavioral paradigm.) We began by assuming that the knowledge used to accomplish word stem completion takes the form of associations between parts of words (i.e., letters, letter clusters, syllables, etc.) and word wholes. We assume that when words are read, there will be occasions where one letter or a combination of letters is noticed, and incidental memory associations form between the part and the whole. We view this as part of the same process by which word spellings are learned, and although we consider only the part-to-whole associations in this model, these associative links are likely bi-directional and allow reciprocal activation and access. On its own, this assumption should explain the word frequency effect—words that appear in language more often should be more likely to have been associated with their parts. However, this may not by itself account for the observed relationship between word length and stem completion frequency, and so we will examine what happens when the length of a word is assumed to impact the probability of forming such an association.

We will examine two distinct models to determine whether the word-length effect is independent of the overall frequency effect (as concluded in the regression models). Model 1A makes no special assumptions about word length, whereas Model 1B assumes that shorter words are more likely to form the part–whole associations than longer words. This could be reasonable because particular elements from shorter words may be more likely to be noticed, and so individual associations between an individual part and its whole will be more likely to form. For example, a model that assumes exactly one part–whole association will be formed when a word is read will necessarily make the elements from shorter words more strongly associated to their corresponding word units. In a later series of models, we will also attempt to distinguish these from length-based effects that happen during retrieval.

The association-based word completion model has two main components—one related to how associations are learned, and a second related to how a participant uses those associations to produce a response. We will next discuss each in detail.

A number of mechanisms could be hypothesized about how associations are formed. For the present model, we followed the assumptions of  Estes’s (1950)  Stimulus Sampling Theory (SST). That foundational model of statistical learning made predictions about the learning function for a stimulus–response mapping. In our application of the model, we assume that a word part has a pool of elements that can be associated, and later re-associated, with words that the word part appears in (see the left side of  Fig. 1 ). The model begins with a large pool of unassociated elements, and when a word part/whole relationship is noticed, an element is selected and associated to a completed word. Thus, words that appear more frequently will have more elements associated to them, and word stems that appear more frequently will have more elements associated to words, and fewer of their element unassociated. (See  Fig. 2 
                         .)

Each word stem in our model starts with a pool of unassociated elements. The model then experiences a corpus of words whose distribution is taken from the Thorndike–Lorge corpus. The learning function in  Fig. 3 
                          is exactly that produced by Eq. (3)′ of  Estes (1950) , where a single element is sampled on each learning round. In the simulation, each time a word stem was experienced, an element was sampled at random, and it was associated with the word it appeared in. The number of elements is fairly arbitrary, but a rough estimate of 1000 is reasonable based on the following rationale: after about 2000 experiences with a word stem,  Fig. 3  shows that roughly 80% of the elements are associated. In the Kučera–Francis corpus (with about 1 million words), word stems in Graf and Williams’s experiment ranged from 246 (for GAR) to 6003 (for THI).  Nagy and Anderson (1984)  estimated that an average child reads 600,000 words per year (with the upper range less than 5,000,000), and so one might expect that an average college student has read on the order of 10 million words, which would mean 2500 experiences of GAR words and 60,000 experiences of THI words. If associations were formed on even 50% of the times a word was read (which might be an unreasonably high assumption), many elements would remain unassociated for low-frequency stems like GAR, but few would remain unassociated for high-frequency stems like THI.

Our initial hypothesis is that the act of word-stem completion relies primarily on a memory search process through the learned associations. This is akin to the memory search process hypothesized by  Raaijmakers and Shiffrin’s (1981)  Search of Associative Memory Simulation (SAMS), but uses part–whole associations instead of context–word and word–word associations as a memory probe. The model first identifies a word stem and its pool of elements. Then, an element is sampled, leading to a completed word, which is finally retrieved. We assume that these activities take some nominal amount of time that is equal regardless of the word or stem. This assumption could possibly be refined to incorporate time-related differences in retrieval based on learned strength, often referred to as the Fan effect ( Anderson, 1974 ). However, the model produces adequate fits to data without adding this additional complexity. Importantly, the memory probe timing parameter is a free parameter that controls how fast a word can be retrieved, and thus how many words can be produced in a 60 s period. Explorations using different timing parameters showed that assumptions about time could impact the goodness-of-fit of the models in minor ways (impacting RMSE more than scale-free statistics like

However, the main results were fairly robust to the choice of timing parameter.

Our initial model has no short-term memory, inhibition, or facilitation from previous responses. Memory search takes place completely without any influence by prior retrieval, and so does not incorporate either a mechanism that will inhibit prior retrievals, or one that can get blocked by a single high-frequency response. These types of mechanisms are important parts of many memory-retrieval models (e.g   Page & Norris, 1998 ), and introducing them might help to account for a number of phenomenon in verbal fluency and memory retrieval, such as the tip-of-the-tongue phenomenon (cf.   Brown, 1991; Brown & McNeill, 1966 ). We have not included these mechanisms because we deemed them unnecessary for the data we have examined, and it remains an open question whether such processes are necessary to account for other data. Instead, the model performs a repeated retrieve-and-check cycle, only responding when a previously unrecalled word has been retrieved.

Overall, this model provides a good account of the data and promising explanation of word-stem completion. By assuming that associations form between parts and wholes of words, and that the probability of those associations depends in part on the length of the word, we are able to produce model fits that are roughly equivalent to a regression model having 43 free parameters, but with a model that has just two free parameters that we were willing to adjust to improve the goodness-of-fit (and one of which we left at its initial value of 1.0 s/memory retrieval). To further test the assumptions of the model, we conducted a new experiment using a slightly different word stem completion method, which we will describe next.

In this experiment, we attempted to more closely reproduce the context of word-stem completion found in crossword puzzles, where a constrained-length word is sought, and the location of the letter cue need not be the first letter. As part of this study, participants also took part in a crossword puzzle solving task, in which they were given 25 min to solve a relatively easy puzzle. The data from the crossword task are not reported here, but a model of those data is described by  Mueller and Thanasuan (2013) .

Our 21 participants were recruited from the introductory psychology pool at Michigan Technological University, and received partial course credit for participating.

Participants were tested on 16 word stems, shown in  Table 3 
                            . Word stems were equally selected among 4- and 6- letter stems, front- and end-letters stems, and stems with relatively many or relatively few completions (according to the US-English dictionary provided by the libreoffice 3.5.4.2 software suite).

Before starting the experiment, all participants completed a demographic survey, which also included questions regarding their personal experience with crossword puzzles and other word games. Next, participants attempted to complete a moderately easy crossword puzzle in 25 min (none were able to complete the puzzle, although experts in other studies we have conducted were typically able to solve the puzzle in under five minutes). Finally, they completed sixteen word-stem completion trials using special-purpose software created using the Psychology Experiment Building Language Version 0.13 ( Mueller & Piper, 2013 ). On each trial, participants had 60 s to complete as many words as they could. The software only allowed letter strings that fit the template to be entered, but did not check a dictionary for correctness. Each completion was added to a text box on the screen to allow the participant to view their past completions, and duplicate completions were recorded but not placed in the text box.

The data logs saved a record of the timing of each completed word, which amounted to 1554 total responses across 21 participants. We first removed the duplicate responses made during any single trial, which amounted to only 12 responses, leaving 1542 total responses. Then, these responses were all checked against the libreoffice US-English dictionary described earlier. A small number of words were not found in this dictionary (common names, common foreign words, contractions, and some slang words) but were also accepted, leaving 1375 total responses. The timing of each of these responses was recorded, and each participant’s completion trajectory is shown in  Fig. 5 
                            , organized by each of 16 stems. Each row shows a distinct stem condition, and the left two columns shows stems with many completions (all greater than 128 distinct words), whereas the right shows stems with fewer completions (54–99 completions).

The maximum number of completions made by any participant in any 1 min session was 16 (for the stem B, which has at least 176 legitimate answers). Participants were rarely able to generate more than 10% of the possible answers in a 1 min trial. The completion trajectories show that for some stems, the trajectory was still positively sloped at the end of the time period, suggesting that more could have been generated if additional time were given. Other stems tended to flatten substantially prior to the 1 min time period, indicating that further search through memory would be unlikely to produce many additional answers.

It is important to recognize there is considerable variation among the stems within any given length and stem location conditions. For example, the L stem produced the second highest average number of completions, despite the given letter coming at the end of the word. The stem random effect has a standard deviation of 1.5 items, indicating that idiosyncratic aspects of a stem might matter more than word length, stem location, or number of completions. These effects may arise from aspects of the lexicon that are difficult to measure, but might be captured by our computational model that uses the lexicon to complete stems.

The present experiment differed from the one studied by Graf and Williams in two important aspects: first, the response methodology differed, in that we allowed as many responses as were possible in 60 s, whereas Graf and Williams limited responses to six; second, our stems had single-letter constraints (instead of 3-letter constraints), length constraints, and letter anchors at either the beginning or end of the word. To accommodate these differences, we made several minor changes to the model. We assumed that the same associative model is at work, but that these associations do not typically incorporate length. Consequently, we assume that the memory system cannot be searched for “4-letter words starting with B”, but rather just “words that start with B”. Thus, we employ a generate-and-check strategy, in which responses are generated, and then checked to determine whether they conform to the length constraint.

As with the earlier model, this model has a parameter to control the probability of forming an association based on word length, and an additional parameter controlling the likelihood of forming an association based on the serial position of the letter in the string. This association strength likely has a U-shaped function and a front-end bias, typical of many memory, attention, and reading processes (e.g., as described by   Estes, Allmeyer, & Reder, 1976 ). Because we only investigated two serial positions, the functional form of this attentive process remains a matter of speculation. To model this, we used a single free parameter that controls the probability of forming an association to an end-of-word item, while assuming the front-letter stems form on every trial in which the length-based process determines it should be encoded.

This time, because additional factors reduced the overall number of words participants produced, we reduced the cycle time parameter of 250 ms, and used a front/back parameter of 0.5 (i.e., end-stems were half as likely to form associates than front-stems), and length parameters of 0 and 0.2 respectively. The adjustment of the cycle-time parameter was necessary to maintain a roughly similar magnitude of responses, given the reduced likelihood of associations being formed. Model 2B showed a large improvement over Model 2A, but ultimately we felt that the fit to data was inadequate. One possible reason for this could because of response-based strategies not captured by Models 1A, 1B, 2A, or 2B. This is especially likely because of the overall difficulty of length-constrained stem completion—participants were typically able to generate only 5%–10% of the total legal responses, yet had frequently stopped making progress long before time elapsed. In these situations, a participant who happens to discover a legal response (or even a response that does not exactly fit) is likely to try another strategy: using a recalled word (whether valid or invalid) to help generate a new appropriate word stem, by manipulating letters so that they form new words. Participants reported using such strategies in informal questioning.

To model this process, we added a response manipulation strategy to models 2A and 2B. Randomly, on half of the memory probe retrieval attempts, the participant attempts ‘edit’ the word previously retrieved to find a new word that fits the pattern. The last word retrieved need not be one that fits the length pattern, but it may have a neighbor that does. Every word in the base lexicon within one letter of the previous word is identified (including words one letter longer and one letter shorter), and one of these is chosen at random as the new candidate response. In the model, the new candidate is then checked to determine if it is the correct length and fits the stem template. If so, it is given as a response. In many cases, especially for longer words with no immediate neighbors, this would result in a re-retrieving the word just retrieved (as it is a 0-distance neighbor of the previous response). But in other situations (especially shorter words with many immediate neighbors), this will produce a valid response. For example, for a stem such as B—, a recalled word “BATH” would activate words that are one-letter neighbors, such as “BASH” or “BATS” “BOTH” and “PATH”. One neighbor is chosen at random, and is then checked against the pattern, and will only count if it fits the pattern and has not been recalled before. For convenience, we assume that this path takes the same amount of time as a normal memory retrieval cycle.

There may be many other related manipulation strategies that prove useful. For example, no specific guidance is given in the model’s manipulation process, although it might be reasonable for someone who generates a five-letter word but needs a six-letter word to search for ways to add a single letter to the generated word so that it would work. Or, it might be reasonable to try to find more remote neighbors, to perform a syllable-based search, to edit particular letter positions selectively, to actively suppress previously-recalled words, or to engage in exhaustive search through the alphabet for particular letter positions. Nevertheless, the simple strategy we examined provided a sufficiently good improvement of the model that we did no further systematic investigation of response manipulation strategies.

The model we propose here provides a good account of word stem completion ability based on the contributions of learned memory associations and response manipulation strategies. This associative memory account, based on a SST mechanism first described by  Estes (1950) , provides a reasonable account of the major findings of unconstrained stem-completion data (cf.   Graf & Williams, 1987 ) (including frequency and word length effects), by assuming associations form incidentally between word parts and word wholes during reading. Our initial model was able to fit a large data set of word stem completions as well as a linear regression model fitted to the same data, suggesting it captures all of the systematic variance related to those factors.

We also tested this model on a new experiment in a slightly different paradigm, in which participants were asked to complete word stems with constrained length, and single-stem letters appearing either at the start or end of the word. The study manipulated three factors: word length (4 versus 6 letter words), number of legal responses (either more or less than around 100), and location of given letter (start or end of word). Our initial model was able to account for each of these factors, and parameters related to word length and stem location each accounted for reliable amounts of variance.

However, the association-only model is inadequate for a number of reasons. First, we found that because the associations did not incorporate length, a deliberate checking mechanism was needed to ensure that words of the correct length were produced. In this situation, we elected to incorporate a second process: adapting the retrieved solution to create a new workable solution. To adapt our simple model, we continued to assume that associations do not typically incorporate word length, but added a response manipulation strategy, in which previous retrievals are edited to form new words that are then used. This allows low-frequency words that are neighbors of high-frequency completions to be found. Thus, for the probe B, a successful retrieval of BATH might lead to BASH or BOTH or BATS, but for the probe B, a successful retrieval BEGGAR may be a dead end and require returning to an association-based strategy. This offers an alternate compelling explanation for the word-length effect, which does not require an encoding difference between short and long words.

The true role of length in stem completion remains somewhat difficult to determine however. In Graf and Williams study, a length-based effect was found even on the first retrieval, which was not entirely accounted for by frequency. Presumably, when no length-based constraints exist, the string manipulation strategy would be unnecessary for the first response, because the first retrieval is likely to work. This suggests that word–letter combinations in shorter words really are encoded more strongly. Alternatives not based on encoding may still exist, however. These might include other recall-based effects—participants may retrieve words without regard to length, but stop writing when a partially-complete word forms a word; participants may engage in alternate generation processes not described by either the association-process or the manipulation strategy, such as more traditional search through a phonological lexicon. Future studies and models may be necessary to understand these factors.

Our model represents the first comprehensive process model of word-stem completion that uses linguistically-relevant representations and corpora to predict behavior. Our model accounts for a number of factors that have previously been found to impact stem completion, which include: frequency ( Graf & Williams, 1987 ); rated familiarity ( Olofsson & Nyberg, 1995 ); number of meanings ( Graf & Williams, 1987 ); length in letters (Graf and Williams;  Olofsson & Nyberg, 1992 ); number of letters provided ( Olofsson & Nyberg, 1992 ); redundancy of the provided letters ( Jacobs et al., 2001 ); presence of the first letter ( Olofsson & Nyberg, 1995 ); presence of last letter ( Erickson, Gaffney, & Heath, 1987 ); location of given letters in the word ( Goldblum & Frost, 1988 ); pronounceability of fragment letters ( Goldblum & Frost, 1988 ); presence of low-frequency letters ( Olofsson & Nyberg, 1995 ); presence of letter clusters ( Olofsson & Nyberg, 1995 ); proportion of filled-in letters ( Olofsson & Nyberg, 1995 ); earlier implicit priming ( Graf & Schacter, 1985 ); number of words that match the pattern ( Shaw, 1997 ); and orthographic neighborhood of completions (Present study).

Our theory of word-stem completion attempts to provide an account for all of these factors. For example, familiarity, frequency, number of meanings, and number of completions each have their impact because these provide more opportunities to learn the stem-word mapping. A number of factors that influence the probability of attending to individual letters in a word (serial position, coherence of a cluster, presence of a low-frequency letter), which should lead to an increased probability of forming the memory associations in the first place. Our model suggests that the length of the word has an impact independent of each of these factors, and it instead arises because of the neighborhood density of words, and the strategy of editing previous words to find new words. Implicit and explicit priming effects are a natural consequence of our model, and deliberate or incidental practice with a wordstem-to-word combination should be more likely to form associations, and have a greater impact later, than simply studying the complete word, or alternate word stems that lead to that word.

At present, the model lacks a specific mechanism for explaining how or why difference in the number of given stem letters might impact stem completion ability. We speculate that providing more stem letters will serve as a better retrieval cue, but will also reduce the number of possible completions, leading to opposing effects on completion ability. We have explored these ideas in more detail elsewhere ( Mueller & Thanasuan, 2013 ), where we assumed that words were associated with both individual letters and adjacent letter pairs, using the same retrieval mechanics as in the current model. Like the present model, both rely heavily on memory-based activation to generate candidates, and then a more deliberate process to adapt and filter the candidates to the current constraints.

A number of consistent phenomenon related to word-stem completion can be understood when the orthography and frequency of words in natural language are taken into account. These include the natural language frequency of completed words, the length of completed words, and the orthographic neighborhood density of completed words. In this paper, we proposed a model that incorporates both association-based memory and orthographic manipulation to account for these phenomena. The model assumes that: (1) letter-position effects and frequency effects have their impact during learning; (2) an orthographic manipulation process is used to find words that are unlikely to be found via frequency, which (3) can explain some, but not all, effects of word length on generation probability. Consequently, the model provides a useful starting point for understanding lexical memory processes studied under the guise of implicit memory and verbal fluency, and provides an example of how memory and mental manipulation are used together to solve problems.
