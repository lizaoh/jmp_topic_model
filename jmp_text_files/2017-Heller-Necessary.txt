In  Doignon & Falmagne (1985)  the theory of knowledge structures was developed from a purely behavioristic point of view by considering the responses to items in a domain of interest. Skills representing the underlying cognitive abilities entered the stage only later on. There are several largely independent major contributions to this skill-based extension  (Doignon, 1994; Düntsch & Gediga, 1995; Falmagne, Koppen, Villano, Doignon, & Johanessen, 1990; Gediga & Düntsch, 2002; Korossy, 1997, 1999) . Integrating them into a common framework,  Heller, Ünlü, & Albert (2013)  emphasize that in these approaches skills appear in two roles. First, they characterize the possible subsets of skills an individual can have available, which will be called competence states below. Second, they specify the skills that are sufficient for solving some item. There is no a priori reason to assume that the two collections of subsets of skills should coincide. Neither should the derivation of the competence states be tied to a particular set of items, nor should the characterization of the items be linked to a certain collection of competence states. For example, it is by no means clear that a subset of skills sufficient for solving an item should be a possible competence state. Imagine a hard item that can be solved through a single skill which is acquired only after a number of other skills have been learnt. However, whenever the underlying skills are to be assessed by observing whether certain items are mastered or not, then this can be done in a unique way only if there is an intimate relation between their occurrences in both roles.

Heller, Stefanutti, Anselmi, & Robusto (2015, 2016)  wanted to identify conditions for uniquely assessing the skills underlying the observed solution behavior. The focus was on conjunctive skill functions, which assign to each item a unique subset of skills that is minimally sufficient for solving it. The conditions proposed by the authors, however, turned out to be either sufficient, or necessary. The present paper provides a condition which is both necessary and sufficient. This condition is also an indispensable prerequisite for the identifiability of probabilistic extensions, like the Deterministic Input Noisy AND-gate model (DINA;  Haertel, 1989; Junker & Sijtsma, 2001; Macready & Dayton, 1977 ), and the conjunctive version of the competence-based basic local independence model (CBLIM;  Heller et al., 2015 ). Thus, it will be called the  identifiability condition .

The paper is organized as follows. Section  1  introduces some basic notions, and Section  2  reviews and slightly generalizes the results reported in  Heller et al. (2015, 2016) . Section  3  presents the announced identifiability condition. Reconsidering previously suggested conditions within the developed theoretical framework, Section  4  identifies the reason for their lack of necessity. Section  5  extends the obtained results even beyond conjunctive skill functions. Finally, Section  6  derives practical recommendations for designing a test that allows for unique skill assessment, and discusses the implications of the presented results for checking identifiability of the CBLIM and the DINA model.

As already mentioned in the introductory section, the above results are also relevant to the identification of probabilistic models formulated on top of the structures considered here. The most important probabilistic model is the basic local independence model (BLIM) developed by  Falmagne & Doignon (1988a,b) . Recently,  Heller (2017) ,  Spoto, Stefanutti, & Vidotto (2012, 2013) , and  Stefanutti, Heller, Anselmi, & Robusto (2012)  provided theoretical results concerning the (local) identifiability of this model, and a numerical procedure for testing it with arbitrary knowledge structures. The propositions presented in this article apply to a competence-based extension of the BLIM, the so-called CBLIM. This notion was coined by  Heller et al. (2015) , who also showed that a CBLIM based on a conjunctive skill function is equivalent to a prominent cognitive diagnostic model known as the Deterministic Input Noisy AND-gate model (DINA;  Haertel, 1989; Junker & Sijtsma, 2001; Macready & Dayton, 1977 ).
