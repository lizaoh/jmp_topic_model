INTRODUCTION  It is almost 60 years since L. L. Thurstone announced his "Law of Comparative Judgement" (Thurstone, 1927a, 1927b, 1927c). In  that theory (for it is no empirical law) Thurstone layed out a general scheme for psychophysical discrimination, one that has  remained largely untouched over the intervening years. At the foundation of his theory Thurstone introduced, for each stimulus  x, a corresponding random variable U_,-called a discriminal process-which represents a summary of evidence concerning x. A  pair of stimuli (x, y) are compared with respect to some ordered attribute by invoking the most natural decision rule  imaginable, namely, select as larger that stimulus which possesses the larger discriminal process. In terms of binary choice  probabilities P,r,v representing the observable probability of choosing stimulus x over stimulus y in a forced-choice  paradigm, these basic assumptions translate as  P(U_Y%UV). CONCLUSION  The theory developed in Section 2 enables us to view the most practical Thurstone models, Cases III and V, from a richer  probabilistic viewpoint than accompanies conventional expositions. The ubiquitous normal assumption has been clarified  considerably: it is the notion of stability, not normality as such, that allows the construction of Case III representations.  In large part stability is captured in our theory of comparative judgement via assumptions (A2), (A3) acting in concert. THURSTONIAN PSYCHOPHYSICS: CASE III 245  Another approach to the matter of empirical verification would be to study the representation (3) along the lines of algebraic  measurement theory (Krantz et al., 1971; Roberts, 1979; Falmagne, 1985). Except to venture a guess that such an attack would  focus on the pair of operations, addition and subtraction occurring in (3), we know practically nothing about the form of  "key" testable conditions on the choice probabilities necessary for (3). While we believe that valuable lessons would be  gained for measurement theory as a byproduct of such an approach (because of the linkage between the functions a, a), we are  not presently of the opinion that an axiomatic basis for (3) would be particularly decisive in view of the rather narrow data  base it would support. To put matters simply, typical discrimination data are well fit by Case V (Fechnerian) models; a  fortiori we expect (3) to fit even better.  A more profitable approach to the problem of testing Thurstone models would seem to require freeing oneself from the  restriction to discrimination data. The sine qua non of Thurstonian psychophysics is surely the identification of external  stimuli with internal random variables. This identification provides a general cloth out of which a line of models may be  fashioned, each tailored to the requirements of a particular experimental task. That such a viewpoint can be extremely  productive is evident in the work of Luce, Green, and Wandel on stochastic neural models; for an excellent summary of that  work, see Luce (1977). In such stochastic models Thurstone's discriminal processes are estimators of signal-dependent  parameters, and are usually given explicit form as a sum of independent random variables. This allows the central limit  theorem to apply, at least in the strong signal limit. (Thurstone himself involved a similar, but less detailed justification  of the normality assumption.)  It is noteworthy in this last respect that stable distributions, and only stable distributions, arise as limits of the  distributions of sums of independent, identically distributed random variables. This fact, due to Paul Levy, is perhaps the  most significant feature of the theory of stable distributions; (see, e.g., Lamperti, 1966, p. 79, for an explicit statement  of Levy's theorem). In this context we can view our theory of comparative judgment as dictating the "strong signal" form of  process models which accumulate statistical evidence about a signal parameter as a sum of small independent contributions.  Historical Note  There is, to my knowledge, no evidence that Thurstone realized the intensive research on limit laws undertaken by Levy,  Khintchine, and other probabilists during the late 1920(s) and throughout the 1930(s), a period that strongly overlapped  Thurstone's own most active years as a theoretician. One is tempted to speculate, however fatuously, that had Thurstone  thoroughly acquainted himself with the probability theory of his day, his unease over the arbitrariness of the normality  assumption might have been assuaged. On the other hand, in his very illuminating account of the connection between Luce's  choice axiom and the double exponential distribution, Yellott (1977) has offered a very different prospect:  246 GEOFFREY J. Our theory of comparative judgment allows for both possibilities.
