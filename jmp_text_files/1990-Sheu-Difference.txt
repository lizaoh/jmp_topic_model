This method actually includes a  variety of experimental procedures. Chief among them are the method of triadic combinations as used by Richardson (1938) and  the complete method of triads as used by Torgerson (1958). Usually, these procedures are accompanied by  multidimensional-scaling models. Recently, Ennis, Mullen, and Frijters (in press) have investigated their unidimensional  variants in the spirit of Thurstone's law of comparative judgments (1927). Krantz (1967), in a study of color differences for  monogromatic stimuli, has employed a task akin to the one to be examined in a moment. His observers were asked to judge which  color differences are smaller, that between comparison stimulus a and standards or that between comparison stimulus b and  standards. Luce and Galanter (1963) mentioned this triad design in an  analysis of similarity scaling and suggested that such data be collected. It should also be pointed out that, in the framework  of a forced-choice paradigm, the task has a close relation to the bisection paradigm via a stochastic approximation procedure  (Falmagne, 1985).  In this paper, the context of the theory will be that of psychophysical experimentation. Hence, a, b, c are regarded as  numbers measuring physical stimulus parameters such as intensity. Over repeated presentations of a given triad (a, b, c) it is  clear that subjects' choices will not always be identical from trial to trial, especially when the stiumuli are very similar.  Thus, the basic theoretical notion is a probability P.b, representing the observable relative frequency of selecting a as  closer to b than is c. For any triads (a, b, c), the choice probability can be regarded as an index of the relative sizes of  the subjective similarities (a, b), (b, c). Thus, we have the representation  Pabc=F[g(a, b), h(b, c)] (1)  in which the functions F, g, and h in the right member are unspecified, except for their monotonicity and continuity  properties. The premise is that although judgments of the triadic task require comparing three things, it actually reduces to  comparing two pairs. Representation (1) is quite weak, and in many contexts a stronger representation is more appealing. More  specifically, if the similarity magnitudes can be represented as the subjective differences between the two members of each  pair, then  P,b,.=H[l(a)-l(b), r(b) -r(c)], (2)  in which H, 1, and r are arbitrary functions subject only to monotonicity and continuity conditions.  This paper investigates necessary and sufficient conditions for the two representations. They are, in fact, scale models for  unidiminsional similarity judgments by the method of triads. The result concerning (1) amounts to a representation theorem.  The result concerning (2) is essentially a translation of well-known algebraic conditions into probabilistic ones. The  relation between the two results will be discussed.  PHYCHOPHYSICAL TRIADIC SYSTEM  Generally speaking, if data are to be explained by a model, then some constraints must be satisfied. But exactly which  constraints Representation (1) imposes on the data is the question we shall address shortly. To be more concrete, the problem  we set out to answer is: Under which conditions can we assert the existence of three functions F, g, and h such that (1)  holds? In the measurement literature, this  44 CHING-FAN SHEU  problem is referred to as the representation problem (Luce & Galanter, 1963; Krantz, Luce, Suppes & Tversky, 1971; Roberts,  1979; Falmagne, 1985). In the case of probabilistic measurement models, the answer is a list of axioms on the choice  probabilities placed on appropriate background conditions mimicking an empirical relational structure. Though the usual  emphasis is on finding the list of axioms, the construction of the background conditions is equally important. Logically,  these side conditions are the groundwork of the entire edifice. Accordingly, we will first describe them in this section. We  remark that, except for certain technical conditions, the axioms and background conditions are formulated with the possibility  of their empirical verification in mind. If these conditions are found to hold experimentally, then we have a genuine  psychophysical theory of triadic judgments (here and throughout the paper, the term "triadic judgments" is used as a short  form for similarity judgments by the method of triads).  In anticipation of the difference structures in Representation (2), we extend the formalization of a Discrimination System  that is defined in Falmagne (1985, p. 118; see also Doignon & Falmagne, 1974) to a system of triadic judgments. Here, the  premise is that judgments of the triadic task can be reduced to a comparison of two differences. The fundamental representation for the triadic choice data to be discussed below,  however, does not commit itself to the details of underlying mechanisms. CONCLUDING REMARKS  We have provided a probabilistic theory of similarity judgments by the method of triads. The basic notion is a probability  Pab, of selecting a as more similar to b than is c. Necessary and sufficient conditions for the two representations of triadic  choice probabilities are formulated. The monotonicity condition is fairly weak and seems natural for a measurement model of  triadic judgments. We suspect that, in most cases, this condition will be fulfilled. However, Krantz's (1967) data in a  similar paradigm have indicated that even simple metric assumptions such as the symmetry condition and the triangle inequality  may sometimes fail to hold. Thus, empirical verification of the two conditions presents itself as a problem for future  research. Statistical methods to test these probabilistic axioms can be developed along the principles described in Iverson  and Falmagne (1985). Currently, the implications of certain empirical relations such as Weber's law for the present  representations are being investigated (Sheu, in preparation). This law has the virtue of constraining data in a very direct  way.  ACKNOWLEDGMENTS  I am indebted to Professor J.-C. Falmagne for his supervision of my research related to this paper.
