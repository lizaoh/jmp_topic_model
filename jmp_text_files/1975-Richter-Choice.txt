The special class of polynomial measurement problems are defined, and their solutions are reduced to solving polynomial  inequalities. An algebraic criterion is given for the solvability of arbitrary finite sets of polynomial inequalities,  resolving a conjecture of Tversky.  I. INTRODUCTION  In the study of rational choice, we frequently encounter the following problem. The choice behavior of a decision maker (an  individual, a small group of individuals, or a society of any size) is observed, and it is to be determined whether the choice  behavior could have arisen from some particular "rational" decision-making process. For example, if an economist observes a  consumer's market purchases over a period of time and under various price and income situations, he may wish to know whether  the consumer's purchases could have arisen from the maximization of some numerical ("utility") function subject to the given  budgetary constraints. (cf. Richter, 1971). For another example, if we observe an investor's decisions under uncertainty, we  may wish to know whether these decisions could have arisen from the maximization of the expected value of some numerical  ("utility") function with respect to some probability measure.  Approaching the same problem from another direction, a theorist may find it very convenient to assume that a decision maker  maximizes a utility function or the expected value of a utility function with respect to some probability measure. But then he  will want to know how much this assumption limits the resulting theory, so he will want to postulate axioms governing choice  behavior that are strong enough to justify the desired behavior, but no stronger than necessary.  In this paper we place choice theory in the framework of polynomial measurement theory (Section II), and give (in Section III  C) necessary and sufficient conditions for finite choice behavior to arise from any particular decision-making process in a  very large class. Analogy with Groups and Additive Measurement  We have seen that polynomial measurement problems reduce to solving polynomial inequalities in the reals. If there are only  finitely many inequalities (e.g., arising from only finitely many observations, or revelations), we should expect a simpler  criterion for solvability. Indeed, there exists an algorithm for deciding, in a finite amount of time, the real solvability of  any finite set of polynomials. For with such a finite set, solvability in the reals can be expressed as a (existential)  sentence of the first-order predicate language of algebra (cf. Tarski, 1951, Section 1), and theorems of mathematical logic  guarantee that algorithms exist for determining the truth or falsity of any such sentence. [Tarski (1951, p. 42, theorem 37)  gave such an algorithm, and simplifications have been given by Seidenberg (1964), Kreisel and Krivine (1967), and others. The  existence of such algorithms is also derivable (cf. Lyndon, 1966, p. 37, or Enderton, 1972, pp. 146-7, theorem 26F(b)) from  the fact that the theory of real closed ordered fields (van der Waerden, 1958, p. 225) is countably axiomatizable (Kreisel &  Krivine, 1967, p. 60) and complete (Robinson, 1963, p. 105, Theorem 4.2.28).]  However, the algorithms available at present are exceedingly difficult to apply in even the simplest polynomial measurement  models, and attempts to apply them to specific problems, such as in (4), would involve overwhelmingly complex calculations. Then in Section III C we show how to  modify the criterion of the first step to give the desired extension, and we also show that the second step does indeed go  through.  B. NONSTANDARD MODELS AND ARCHIMEDEAN AXIOMS  Sometimes we are interested in solving an infinite number of polynomial inequalities and equalities involving perhaps an  infinite number of indeterminates (even though each polynomial involves only finitely many). Then Theorem 1 and Corollary 1  are not directly applicable, because Il , 1, , I,, I4 , and X are not all finite. Nevertheless, it may be true that every  finite subset of the relations (inequalities and equalities) may satisfy the criterion of Theorem 1 or Corollary 1 and thus  admit real solutions. In that case, it follows easily from the Compactness Theorem of first-order predicate logic (Lyndon,  1966, p. 56, Corollary; Enderton, 1972, p. 136) that the whole set of relations is satisfiable in a nonstandard model (cf.  Robinson, 1966; Bell & Slomson, 1969) of the reals.  For some purposes, solvability in a nonstandard model of the reals may be adequate. Even when it is not, and satisfiability in  the standard reals must be determined, the conditions given by Theorem 1 and Corollary 1, with respect to each finite subset  of the relations, are still of interest for the following reasons. First, they must necessarily hold for each finite subset of  relations, in order that a standard real solution may exist for the whole set of relations. And further, the only condition  lacking to guarantee solvability in the standard reals is an archimedean axiom, for the nonstandard models of the reals are  precisely the nonarchimedean ones. Thus we can distinguish what we might call the "proper axioms," those determined by the  finite sets of the relations and guaranteeing solvability in a nonstandard model, from those that guarantee the solution  values are in an archimedean model. In many axiomatizations a single axiom may serve both purposes,23 and while that may have  merit from the point of view of intuitive appeal or economy of axioms, it may nonetheless still be of interest to discover  proper axioms.
