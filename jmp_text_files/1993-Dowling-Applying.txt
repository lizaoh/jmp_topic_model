Applying the Basis of a Knowledge Space for Controlling the Questioning of an Expert 

CORNELIA E. DOWLING 

Technische Universitat Braunschweig 

Procedures for constructing a knowledge space by imteractively questioning an expert have been recently developed. Knowledge spaces are structures appropriate for the assessment of student’s knowledge. A knowledge space is defined as a specific collection of subsets of examination questions from a given field. The dasis of a knowledge space is the smallest subcollection of a knowledge space from which the knowledge space can be reconstructed. During the procedures for constructing a knowledge space, the expert is asked to accept or reject certain assertions on the set of examination questions. The number of possible judgments is reduced by omitting those judgments which can be inferred from previous judgments. The results of this paper can be applied to construct the basis of a knowledge space from the judgments of an expert. In this manner, previously suggested procedures for questioning experts can be improved, First, the number of iterations required to decide whether or not a judgment can be inferred from previous judgments is reduced considerably, and second, the new procedure does not require assumptions on the sequence in which the assertions are presented for judgment. Third, the worst case, in which the expert has to make all possible judgments, can be avoided. © 1993 Academic Press, Inc. 

1. INTRODUCTION 

Doignon and Falmagne (1985) have suggested combining systems for computer assisted instruction with a sophisticated procedure for the assessment of knowledge. Such procedures for assessing student’s knowledge are comprised of a large number of possible examination questions from the relevant branch of knowledge, but actually pose only a few pertinent questions to any given student (cf. Degreef, Doignon, Ducamp, & Falmagne, 1986; Falmagne, 1989; Falmagne & Doignon 1988a, b). The information concerning which of the possible examination questions are posed during the assessment process is derived from a collection of knowledge states. A knowledge state is a subset of questions some student is capable of solving. A specific collection of knowledge states discussed below is called Anowledge space by Doignon and Falmagne (1985). 

Such a knowledge space can be derived from the judgments of an expert (cf. Koppen & Doignon, 1990; Miiller, 1989), Recently, procedures for constructing a knowledge space from the judgments of an expert have been developed (cf. Koppen,

In this paper, we suggest a new procedure for consulting an expert on his or her judgments on assertions of the form (1.1). Koppen (1989) has improved the previously suggested algorithms by Koppen and Doignon (1990) and Miiller (1989) for determining those assertions which are inferences from previous judgments. These algorithms assume that inferences from previous judgments are encoded as a subsets-by-items table of the size |¥|-|Q|, where Q corresponds to the set of questions, and ¥ ts a family of subsets of the set Q. The algorithms repeatedly iterate through such a table. Therefore the size of this table is crucial] for the time required to decide whether or not a new assertion to be presented to the expert is already an inference. The algorithm suggested by Koppen (1992) starts with a table of the size {Q|? and terminates with a table of the size |.|-|Q|, where is the knowledge space derived from the expert’s judgments. The results in Sections 3 and 4 of this paper yield an algorithm which starts with a table of the size |Q|* and stops with a table of the size |@|-|Q!, where Z is the basis of the knowledge space derived from the expert’s judgments. The basis of a knowledge space is the smallest subset of a knowledge space from which the knowledge space can be reconstructed (cf. Doignon & Falmagne, 1985) and is introduced in detail in Section 3. 

For many applications, it does not suffice simply to avoid presenting assertions to an expert for judgment which are inferences from previous Judgments. Koppen (1993) has pointed out that it would be desirable to minimize the total number of the expert’s judgments, provided this optimalization problem can be solved in this generality within a realistic period of time. The algorithm by Koppen (1993) is based on the assumption that assertions with large premise sets have to be judged by the expert after the assertions with smaller premise sets. We show that this assumption is responsible for the fact that the algorithm by Koppen cannot avoid the worst case where the subsets-by-items table has the size 2'°!.|Q|, while the expert would have to judge all assertions not corresponding to tautologies. The algorithm suggested in Sections 3 and 4 of this paper does not depend on assumptions on the sequence in which the assertions are presented to the expert. We can therefore recommend a strategy for querying an expert which avoids the worst case mentioned above (see Section 6).

7. DISCUSSION 

A new procedure for questioning an expert on judgments of assertions of the form (1.1) was introduced. The number of possible assertions of that form increases extremely rapidly with the size of the set QO of questions; for example, if |Q| = 50, then approximately 2.8x 10'° standard from assertions are possible. In order to reduce the number of judgments, the new procedure avoids presenting assertions for judgment the acceptance or rejection of which can be inferred from previous judgments. These assertions were called positive and negative inferences, respectively. Recall that the judged assertions form a subset of the set of inferences. 

A condition for terminating the querying is that all the possible assertions of the form above have been obtained as inferences. In practical applications we may be able to terminate the querying after a comparatively small number of judgments. even though the number of positive assertions of the form (1.1) may be extremely large. Koppen (1992) mentions a study in which experts judged standard form assertions on 50 examination questions concerning U.S. high school mathematics. In this study the experts only judged between 1,000 and 2,500 assertions until all 2.8 x 10’° standard form assertions were deduced as inferences. The conclusion that all standard form assertions were deduced as inferences was derived from a knowledge space equivalent to the set of inferred assertions. 

We suggested representing the potentially very large set of positive inferences as the basis of a knowledge space equivalent to the set of these inferences. In order to determine whether or not the acceptance of a candidate assertion can be inferred from previous judgments, we compare the candidate assertions only with each member of the basis instead of iterating through the complete knowledge space or the set of all positive inferences. A formula for updating the basis after a new assertion is accepted was suggested. This formula refers only to the newly accepted assertion and the old basis, and not to the corresponding knowledge space or the set of all positive inferences. The negative inferences are represented and updated in a similarly efficient manner. At the end of the querying we obtain a basis representing all positive and all negative inferences. 

The new querying procedure was applied in two studies which will be described in detail in later articles. The size of the set O of questions was 28 in one study and 48 in the second. The querying program was executed in real time on a Hewlett Packard work station HP9000/318 in the first study and on a 80386 IBM PC in the second. 

The procedure for checking whether or not a candidate assertion is an inference from previous judgments has to be combined with a procedure for generating the candidate assertions. Since judgments on assertions with large premises are time consuming, we can start the querying by presenting only assertions with small premises for judgment. In the study mentioned by Koppen (1993), all but one expert judged only assertions with a premise of size less than six until all possible assertions were inferred as inferences.

Redundant querying does not only cost the expert’s valuable time. It may also cause careless errors and therefore affect the agreement between the judgments of different experts. Developing further method for reducing redundant judgments, as the one suggested by Koppen (1993) is of paramound importance. 

We must expect that different experts may have different opinions and that some of the judgments are erroneous. In a later article, we shall develop and discuss methods for comparing the judgments or knowledge spaces of different experts. Here we mention a simple method for roughly describing the agreement between different experts. 

In a continuing study, four tutors for a CAD system judged assertions of the form (1.1) on 28 skills required to use this system. The number of students each expert had trained varied between 20 and 100 students. We described the agreement between the experts by considering the four collections of assertions judged by the four experts during the questioning. The judgments of expert E,, for 1 <i<4, were compared with the corresponding inferences of each of the other experts, E,, with 1<j<4. For experts E; and £;, we computed the number p,, of assertions which were accepted by expert £; and were positive inferences from the judgments of expert £;. We also computed the number n, of assertions which were rejected by expert £, and negative inferences from the judgments of expert £,, The number p,+n, therefore represents the number of judgments of expert £; that agree with inferences of expert £;. We computed this number as the percentage a, of the number of assertions judged by expert E;. The agreement a, between judgments and inferences varied between 53 and 91%. The average of the percentages a,, was 76% for i#j. The agreement of the judgments of the most experienced tutor with the inferences of the other tutors is 71, 77, and 91%, respectively. 

In a future analysis, it would be interesting to derive a knowledge space from those assertions which are positive inferences for al] but one expert. This common knowledge space could be used to asses the symmetric difference between the knowledge spaces of the individual experts and the common knowledge space. To determine the assertions which are positive inferences for all but one expert, we need a procedure that avoids generating all possible assertions on the set of questions.
