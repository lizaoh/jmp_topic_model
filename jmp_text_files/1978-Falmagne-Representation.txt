INTRODUCTION  In a common paradigm in the study of choice behavior, the subject is requested to select one from an offered set of stimuli.  In many applications, the subject's behavior turns out to be unreliable. That is, if the same set B is offered several times,  the subject may not always pick up the same stimulus. This unreliability is sometimes attributed to the fluctuations over  trials of the effects, on the subject, of the various stimuli to be compared. In the framework of Probability Theory, this  leads naturally to postulating the existence, for each stimulus a e B, of a random variable Ua representing the  momentto-moment variations of the effects of a. The subject then choses a e B if the momentary, or sampled, value of Ua  exceeds that Of Ub for all stimuli b e B - {a}. This notion can be traced back to Fechner2 and Wundt. It has been developed by  Thurstone (1927a, b), and more recently by Block and Marschak (1960), and many others (Torgerson, 1958; Luce, 1959; Morrison,  1963; Burke & Zinnes, 1965; Luce & Suppes, 1965; Thompson & Singh, 1967; Bock & Jones, 1968; Tversky, 1972a, b; Sattath &  Tversky, 1976; Corbin & Marley, 1974; Manski, 1977; Yellott, 1977). In many cases, these developments consist in specializing  the basic idea by making assumptions regarding either the choice processes, or the form of the distribution of the random  variables. Exceptions are Sattath and Tversky (1976), Corbin and Marley (1974) and especially Block and Marschak (1960) where  testable predictions are derived, which do not depend on specific assumptions.  t This work is supported by a National Foundation Grant to New York University. That is, no necessary set of constraints on the choice  probabilities is known, which also ensure the existence of the random variables underlying the subject's manifest choices. The  purpose of this paper is to fill this gap. (We shall assume however that the universe of stimuli is finite.)  One aspect of our work deserves to be stressed at the outset, since it has a potentially important impact on empirical  applications. Our method of proof of the representation theorem is constructive, in the sense that it provides an algorithm to  "compute" the joint distribution of the random variables Ua (obviously, up to some uniqueness properties), from the choice  probabilities. Assuming that some multiple choice data has indeed been generated by a collection of random variables Ua as  outlined above, an application of the algorithm would permit to recover part of the (ordinal) structure of these hypothetical  random variables. Our results on uniqueness indicate that the picture obtained by this analysis will be quite accurate, if the  number of observations is large enough. UNIQUENESS  The Proof of Theorem 4 provides an algorithm permitting, if the Block-Marschak polynomials are nonnegative, the construction  of a random representation of a system of choice probabilities. (The successive steps of the algorithm will be reviewed in the  next section.) Such algorithm has practical uses. Assuming that some data has been generated by a random scale system, an  application of the algorithm might possibly uncover part of the structure of the underlying random variables. The empirical  interest of such analysis is obvious. A number of cautionary remarks are in order however, which will prevent any excess  optimism.  To begin with, there are statistical issues to be dealt with. Even if the data is indeed a sample from a random scale system,  there is no garantee that the sample (relative) frequencies will give nonnegative Block-Marschak polynomials. REMARKS ON APPLICATIONS  The results of this paper have some theoretical interest. For example, Theorem 4 provides a general method for proving that a  set of axioms on a finite system (A, P), imply that (A, P) is a random scale system: using Theorem 4, it suffices indeed to  show that these axioms constrain the Block-Marschak polynomials to be nonnegative. In some instances, this may turn out to be  an easier enterprise than that involved by the usual technique of constructing a probability measure on the set of  permutations on A, relying on Lemma 3 (e.g., Tversky, 1972b; Corbin & Marley, 1974). No examples of such application will be  given here.  The most foreseeable use of our results lies on the empirical side. As pointed out, the algorithm defined in the proof of  Theorem 4 provides the possibility of uncovering part of the ordinal structure of the hypothetical random variables having  generated the observed frequencies, in a multiple choice experiment. Moreover, Theorem 7 and its corollary show that the  marginal distributions of the ranking (of the random representations-in the sense of Definition 4) are essentially unique.  This means that-except for statistical variability-the accuracy of the picture revealed by the analysis will be quite  substantial. The empirical interest of such analysis may be considerable in some cases.
