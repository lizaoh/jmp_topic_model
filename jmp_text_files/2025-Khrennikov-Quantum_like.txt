The quantum information revolution (commonly referred to as the second quantum revolution) has had a profound impact, not only technologically — ushering in advancements in quantum computing, cryptography, and simulation — but also in foundational understanding. One key revelation is that the methodology and formalism of quantum theory can be extended beyond physics to model phenomena in diverse fields, such as cognition, psychology, decision-making, biology (including genetics and epigenetics), evolutionary theory, economics, finance, social and political sciences, and game theory. This interdisciplinary area of research is broadly referred to as quantum-like (QL) modeling (e.g.,  Plotnitsky & Haven, 2023 ).

                       It should be clearly distinguished from the use of genuine quantum physics in cognition, as explored by researchers such as  Hameroff (1994) ,  Penrose (1989) , and  Vitiello (2001) , as well as in biology, particularly in the framework of quantum biophysics ( Arndt, Juffmann, & Vedral, 2009; Igamberdiev, 1993; Sergi, Messina, Vicario, & Martino, 2023 ). QL models, on the other hand, describe the behavior of macroscopic cognitive systems (e.g., human behavior), where the functioning of these systems can be modeled as information processing that adheres to the laws of quantum information theory and probability, deviating from classical laws.

In the past 10–15 years, QL studies have expanded into new domains of science, such as genetics and biological evolution theory ( Asano, Basieva, Khrennikov, Ohya, Tanaka, & Yamato, 2013; Asano, Basieva, et al., 2015; Basieva, Khrennikov, & Ozawa, 2021 ). As a result, the second quantum revolution has been complemented by the QL revolution ( Plotnitsky & Haven, 2023 ). A key challenge for the QL approach lies in the fact that the neurophysiological mechanisms responsible for generating macroscopic QL behavior remain unknown. The experimental statistical data collected in cognitive psychology exhibits phenomena like the order, conjunction and disjunction, response replicability effects, and contextuality. Such data can be successfully described using quantum formalism.

                       However, there is still no proper mathematical model that links the neurophysiological processes in the brain with a QL description. This issue was highlighted in  Busemeyer, Fakhari, and Kvam (2017) . Currently, this is not merely a theoretical concern. QL methodology has recently been applied in the medical diagnosis of neurological diseases ( Shor, Glik, et al., 2021; Shor et al., 2023 ), and the first “QL patent” has been granted ( Shor, Benninger, & Khrennikov, 2025 ). While this method provides highly accurate predictions, the lack of a neurophysiological foundation hinders its widespread use in hospitals, as physicians require a neurophysiological justification for its application.

                         One might refer to quantum biophysics or to genuine quantum physical processing in the brain, such as in the microtubules ( Hameroff, 1994; Penrose, 1989 ). And the QL approach does not contradict the quantum-brain hypothesis, despite the latter’s numerous drawbacks, as pointed out by many critics, including  Tegmark (2000) . From our perspective, the main issue with the quantum-brain theory is that it does not consider neurons as the fundamental units of mental information processing. To identify such units, one would need to explore finer scales of space and time, possibly down to the Planck scale, as in Penrose’s theory of consciousness and quantum gravity. However, we aim to focus on neuronal processing as the core basis for cognition and consciousness.

This is an opportune moment to highlight prior research on the neural network foundations of QL models.  De Barros and Suppes (2009)  (see also  De Barros, 2012 ) demonstrated that neural oscillators can generate interference patterns analogous to those observed in unitary evolution.  Takahashi and Cheon (2012)  proposed a nonlinear neural network model that uses squared amplitudes to compute response probabilities within a quantum cognitive framework.  Busemeyer et al. (2017)  explored how a neural-based system could perform the computations required for applying quantum probability to human behavior. More recently, QL modeling on treelike graphs has gained traction in medical research ( Shor, Glik, et al., 2021; Shor et al., 2023 ), reflecting the hierarchical nature of information processing in the brain. Notably, one of the earliest works in QL modeling of cognition ( Khrennikov, 2000 ) focused on the quantum mechanics of treelike graphs, which are mathematically represented using  
                         p 
                      -adic numbers. This approach was later extended to QL modeling in studies of petroleum reservoirs ( Lozada Aguilar, Khrennikov, Oleschko, & de Jesús Correa, 2017 ), where the treelike structure of capillary networks was analyzed. Additionally, in  Khrennikov, Basieva, Pothos, and Yamato (2018) , mental information was represented QL through the distribution of action potentials in neuron networks. The present work also draws inspiration from discussions with Gregory Scholes on QL states in neural networks represented by specialized classes of graphs ( Amati & Scholes, 2024; Scholes, 2024 ). However, our approach considers arbitrary graphs. Finally, we mention recent publications on neurally inspired QL models of arousal and the Yerkes-Dodson law based on neural activity in the locus coeruleus ( Huang, Cohen, & Busemeyer, 2025; Rosendahl & Cohen, 2022 ).

This paper is intended for those interested in applying quantum methods to cognition, psychology, and decision-making. Researchers in these areas are generally unaware of GPT. Moreover, GPT is divided into several models that may appear to be different, but are mathematically equivalent ( Ozawa, 1980 ) (for reviews, see  Holik et al., 2021; Plavala, 2023 ). In this paper, we employ a specific model based on  ordered linear spaces , or more precisely, base norm spaces. To make the paper accessible to experts in mathematical psychology, we briefly introduce the basics of GPT within the ordered linear space formalism in Section  5.1 .

GPT is “cleansed” of the quantum mysteries and prejudices, offering a more straightforward general mathematical framework that describes probabilities in all kinds of observations — whether physical, cognitive, social, or financial.

Before formally introducing GPT, we begin by intuitively exploring and, we hope, clearly using the concepts of GPT. In Section  4 , we present a model that bridges QL cognition and the brain’s networks of communicating neurons. We return to this model in Section  7  to demonstrate the effects of order, interference (disjunction), and non-repeatability.

This paper is conceptual in nature, aiming to introduce the concept of coupling neuronal activity with the quantum cognition project through GPT. Further papers will delve deeper into the technical details. While this paper focuses on cognition and neuronal networks, the formalism and methodology presented here can be easily applied to a variety of biological and social networks, including QL modeling in genetics, medicine, and social science ( Alodjants, Tsarev, Avdyushina, Khrennikov, & Boukhanovsky, 2024; Khrennikov, Iriyama, Basieva, & Sato, 2024; Shor, Glik, et al., 2021; Shor et al., 2023 ). Moreover, the application of our bridging construction to networks of mechanical or digital oscillators supports the supremacy claims of quantum-inspired computing (QIC) ( Goto et al., 2021; Goto, Tatsumura, & Dixon, 2019; Huynh et al., 2025 ). It can also serve as the foundation for the development of QL-based Artificial Intelligence (QLAI).

We draw attention to the connection with the paper by  Effenberger, Carvalho, Dubinin, and Singer , which reported the enhanced computational power of special oscillatory neuronal networks in the cerebral cortex and hippocampus. The authors of  Effenberger et al.  emphasized the similarity between the functioning of these networks and QIC. In light of our work, this is not merely a similarity — these frameworks can be unified through our bridging construction. The key role here is played by the exploration of the tensor product QL representation for specially connected compound networks (see Sections  8 and 9 ). Additionally, Section  9.1  discusses the impact of electromagnetic fields, including the brain’s basic rhythms, on establishing inter-network correlations, while Section  9.2  explores the relationship between the QL representation of compound neuronal networks and QIC, as well as the enhanced computational power of certain oscillatory networks in the brain.

Our paper is linked to foundations of quantum theory, especially its probabilistic and measurement counterparts, and cognitive and neural sciences. Some of the foundational problems are of great complexity and it would be naive to expect their clarification here. Nevertheless, some preliminary discussion can be useful for understanding of our approach.

By the quantum probability theory (QPT) we understood the conventional quantum theory formulated by  Von Neumann (1955)  (and Lüders), but not non-commutative probability theory based on von Neumann algebras. The classical probability theory was formulated by  Kolmogoroff (1933) .

Probability theory needs two kinds of rules:

In QPT, the state update rule follows the projective measurement postulate (Lüders’ postulate), whereas in CPT, it follows Bayesian updating. These two rules allow us to compute joint probability distributions for arbitrary sequences of observables.

Regarding the question order effect (QOE) and the response replicability effect (RRE), sequential joint probability in CPT exhibits RRE but not QOE. Conversely, in QPT, sequential joint probability exhibits QOE if and only if it does not exhibit RRE ( Khrennikov et al., 2014; Ozawa & Khrennikov, 2023 ). Therefore, to reconcile QOE and RRE, a new theoretical framework beyond both CPT and QPT is required.

We have demonstrated that such a model exists within the framework of GPT or through QPT combined with instrument theory for state updates ( Ozawa & A., 2021; Ozawa & Khrennikov, 2023 ). GPT provides a general structure for observables (encompassing both QPT and CPT) and describes state updates using the concept of instruments, as introduced by  Davies and Lewis (1970) ,  Ozawa (1984) ,  Ozawa (1985a) ,  Ozawa (1985b) ,  Ozawa (1986) ,  Ozawa (1989)  and  Ozawa (1997a) . Thus, GPT is not merely the union of QPT and CPT but extends beyond them.

The instrument theory in GPT (Section  5.4 ) is emerged to fill those gaps in both probability theories QPT and CPT (see article  Fuyama, Khrennikov, & Ozawa, 2025  for detailed discussion). In fact, non-invasive measurements cannot be studied in QPT, and our Model 1 (Section  3.2 ) cannot be described neither in QPT, nor in the standard CPT.

Our position that QL modeling of cognition strongly requires integration with the activity of networks of communicating neurons is not self-evident. One of the reviewers noted:  
                            “The authors raise the key issue of what kind of processing at the neuronal level is consistent with quantum-like cognitive models. In our field, in general, which has been a key challenge, that is, how to reconcile neuronal function with behavior ... most cognitive scientists/ neuroscientists might take the view that the neuronal explanatory level and the behavioral level are so detached that, essentially, they do not constrain each other. Explain why they think something quantum-like at the behavioral level makes it plausible why there should be some quantum-like at the neuronal level; this cannot be taken for granted. Note, a recent discussion of these issues is in  Huang et al. (2023), Huang, Epping, et al. (2025) . It would help a bit to explain what the overall assumption is: are we to assume that behavioral effects (such as whether we respond in a certain way to a question) can be directly mapped to processing of neuronal assemblies? ”

In fact, we don’t claim that “there should be some quantum-like at the neuronal level”. Neuronal networks are purely classical random dynamical systems. QL representation is a metarepresentation of neuronal activity (cf. with a quantum–classical model of brain dynamics  Sergi et al., 2023 ). We emphasize that individual neuronal dynamics is present at the QL-level only indirectly — through the covariance matrix. The latter doesn’t determine random dynamics in a neuronal network.

Our mathematical model supports the “QL-brain hypothesis” ( Khrennikov, 2006 ) that  the brain really can create such  
                         meta-representation  and operate with QL states and observables, and that cognition is its facets.

We start with recollection ( Caire, Reddy, & Varacallo, 2025 ) on the basics of brain’s neuronal signaling:  
                            “The human brain is made up of approximately 86 billion neurons that talk to each other using a combination of electrical and chemical (electrochemical) signals. The places where neurons connect and communicate with each other are called synapses. Each neuron has anywhere between a few to hundreds of thousands of synaptic connections, and these connections can be with itself, neighboring neurons, or neurons in other regions of the brain. A synapse is made up of a presynaptic and postsynaptic terminal. The presynaptic terminal is at the end of an axon and is the place where the electrical signal (the action potential) is converted into a chemical signal (neurotransmitter release). The postsynaptic terminal membrane is less than 50 nanometers away and contains specialized receptors. The neurotransmitter rapidly (in microseconds) diffuses across the synaptic cleft and binds to specific receptors”. Voltage-dependent channels endow neurons with active signaling capabilities. Specifically, they enable neurons to generate nerve impulses, also known as  action potentials.  Despite variations in duration, amplitude, and shape, action potentials are typically treated as identical events. The duration of an action potential (approximately 1 ms) is often ignored, and an action potential sequence, or spike train, is represented as a series of all-or-none point events. It is important to note that each neuron, on average, has about 7,000 synaptic connections with other neurons. Axons vary in length; some are only a millimeter long, while those extending from the brain down the spinal cord can be over a meter in length. An axon has numerous side branches, known as axon collaterals, allowing one neuron to send information to several others. These collaterals, much like the roots of a tree, split into smaller extensions called terminal branches. It is crucial to recognize that the integration of inputs in neurons is an intricate process, influenced by multiple factors at various scales. While the perspective summarized in the book of  Kandel, Koester, Mack, and Siegelbaum (2021)  provides a foundational understanding of synaptic integration, it does not fully capture the vast complexity of neuronal computation. Each neuronal output is not merely the result of synaptic summation at the dendritic level but is shaped by a vast array of biophysical, biochemical, and even systemic influences that interact dynamically. At the most immediate level, neuronal activity is driven by the integration of excitatory and inhibitory synaptic inputs, primarily mediated by a multitude of ion channels/receptors. However, synapses themselves exhibit a high degree of complexity:  Nonlinear synaptic interactions:  Dendrites do not simply sum inputs linearly. Instead, they exhibit nonlinear summation through mechanisms such as local dendritic spikes, NMDA receptor-mediated voltage dependence, and active back-propagation of action potentials ( London & Häusser, 2005; Spruston, 2008; Stuart & Spruston, 2015 ).  Short-term and long-term synaptic plasticity:  Synaptic strength is dynamically modified by mechanisms such as short-term facilitation/depression and long-term potentiation/depression (LTP/LTD), influenced by prior activity patterns ( Abbott & Regehr, 2004 ). Neuromodulation of synapses: Neurotransmitters like dopamine, serotonin, and acetylcholine influence synaptic efficacy and dendritic excitability, regulating attention, learning, and memory ( Nadim & Bucher, 2014 ) . Beyond classical synaptic integration, a variety of local and intracellular factors further shape neural output. we name a few below:  Ion channel diversity:  Neurons express a diverse and changing (with functionality of the neuron based on various genetical modulations) range of voltage-gated and ligand-gated ion channels, including potassium, calcium, and sodium channels, each contributing to intrinsic excitability and synaptic integration ( Hille, 2001 ).  Intrinsic membrane properties:  Neurons have unique electrical properties due to specific combinations of ion channels. These properties influence how inputs are transformed into outputs, leading to differences in firing patterns such as regular spiking, bursting, or adapting responses ( Marder & Goaillard, 2006 ).  Dendritic morphology:  The physical structure of dendrites affects how signals propagate and interact. Thin dendrites can filter and attenuate signals, while dendritic spines can compartmentalize biochemical signals ( Spruston, 2008 ). Moreover, Glial cells, once thought to serve only as support structures, are now recognized as active participants in neural computation:  Astrocytic regulation of synaptic function:  Astrocytes modulate synaptic transmission by releasing gliotransmitters (e.g., glutamate, ATP) that can enhance or suppress neuronal firing ( Araque, Carmignoto, & Haydon, 2014 ).  Oligodendrocyte-mediated myelination:  The myelination of axons by oligodendrocytes affects conduction velocity and synchrony of neural firing across distributed networks ( Fields, 2008 ).  Microglial immune responses:  Microglia dynamically survey the neural environment and modulate synaptic pruning, plasticity, and inflammation, which in turn affect neuronal function ( Tremblay, Lowery, & Majewska, 2010 ) . Given the sheer complexity of neural influences, classical reductionist approaches may be insufficient to fully describe brain function. As the integration of neural inputs is a profoundly complex process (which up until now could not be fully achieved by classical network dynamics formulation) influenced by multiple factors at different scales — from molecular and synaptic mechanisms to network-wide and systemic modulations. Traditional reductionist models struggle to encapsulate this complexity, particularly in the face of infinitesimal influences that accumulate to shape neuronal function. By adopting a quantum-like formalism, we may achieve a more holistic and adaptable framework for understanding neural computation, allowing us to isolate and examine specific influences while preserving their broader interactions. In our model, we will create an abstract representation of interactions based on a synaptic connection between two neurons/system of neurons or even differently localized but connected brain tissues each with all its components, that encapsulates the multitude of minuscule influences shaping neural inputs, ranging from synaptic interactions and intrinsic neuronal properties to glial modulation and broader network dynamics. Rather than explicitly modeling each micro-level interaction from the outset, our approach will employ a simplified yet comprehensive framework that integrates these influences into a unified state. Crucially, unlike traditional models with fixed weights, our framework allows for a dynamic evolution of influence weights over time, analogous to quantum systems where states continuously evolve based on interactions and external conditions. This abstraction serves as a foundational model that can be systematically refined and decomposed into progressively more distinguishable components, enabling an adaptive level of complexity depending on the scale of analysis. By doing so, our framework not only preserves the holistic nature of neural computation but also provides a structured methodology for isolating specific influences while maintaining their interconnected relationships within the system.

In general this is the basic scheme of GPT, states and effects. To proceed further, one should appeal to theory of measurement instruments. We want to do this properly and this needs more formal mathematical considerations that will be presented in the next Section  5 .

GPT was developed in the 1970s as an attempt to understand the foundations of quantum formalism. Within the framework of GPT, the quantum formalism based on complex Hilbert spaces is viewed as a special case of the general operational formalism for all types of measurements. GPT unifies classical and quantum measurement theories and paves the way for a variety of QL formalisms.

It is important to note that the probabilistically non-classical properties of mental phenomena do not necessarily imply that they should be described using quantum formalism (such as the complex Hilbert space), or quantum probability, information, and measurement theory. Some GPT-based models may better align with cognitive information processing and decision-making, as highlighted in  Khrennikov (2024) . 
                         6 
                      
                      
                         6 
                         In 2015, one of the co-authors (AKH) had extensive discussions in Vienna with Anton Zeilinger about possible applications of quantum theory to cognition. These discussions were prompted by Zeilinger’s debates with the Dalai Lama on the relationship between spiritual and quantum mechanical phenomena. Zeilinger strongly advocated for generalized theories that are neither purely classical nor quantum. He also suggested that the ultimate QL theory of cognition might be so mathematically complex and exotic that the complex Hilbert formalism would no longer appear as advanced. However, this is not the case for the model proposed in this article, which is “less quantum” (“more classical”) than traditional quantum mechanics.

In this paper, we employ GPT based on the theory of ordered linear spaces ( Davies, 1976; Davies & Lewis, 1970; Ozawa, 1980, 1984, 1997a, 2004, 2023 ). Unlike the majority of GPT studies, we focus not on the structures of state and effect spaces, but on the calculus of measurement instruments that provide probabilities (via the generalized Born’s rule) and the updates to states generated by the feedback from measurements. This approach, introduced by  Davies and Lewis (1970)  and known as  quantum instruments theory,  has been further developed by  Ozawa (1980, 1984, 1985a, 1985b, 1986, 1997a, 2004, 2023) . A recent article  Khrennikov (2024)  restructured this approach within a contextual framework. The contextual perspective of GPT and quantum instruments theory illuminates their potential applications to cognitive psychology, decision-making, and social science ( Khrennikov, 2024 ).

GPT research, which focuses on generalizing the concepts of state and observable, was initiated in  Mackey (1963)  and significantly developed by  Gudder (1964, 1973) .  Ozawa (1980)  established the equivalence between Gudder’s and Davies–Lewis’ approaches.

Its connection with the disjunction effect in cognitive psychology was established in  Busemeyer et al. (2006) . It is important to note that in classical probability theory, probabilities do not interfere.

The tensor product representation of a compound network system allows for “local manipulations” that can update the global state of the system. In quantum computing, exploring this local–global interface leads to powerful computational schemes. Building on the results from  Effenberger et al. , which suggest an analogy between brain function and QIC, we hypothesize that the brain utilizes the tensor product representation.

At the same time, we recognize that modeling networks with enhanced computational performance requires more complex GPT models than those considered in this paper.

Recently cognitive effects started to be modeled with quantum computational circuits, see  Khrennikov (2023a) , p. 125, on the quantum circuit representation of quantum instrument modeling of the combinations of the question order and response replicability effects, see also article  Widdows, Rani, and Pothos (2023)  offered an explanation of the quantum-like model for Prisoner’s Dilemma using quantum circuits. Such studies also stimulate search for potential biophysical basis of quantum computational schemes and generally QL representation.

As noted, QL modeling is actively developing as a phenomenological approach. However, it strongly requires integration with brain functioning, specifically with the activity of networks of communicating neurons. In this article, we established the basics of such integration within the GPT model, which represents networks using ordered weighted graphs. These graphs are described by weight matrices that quantify the intensities of signaling between neurons. The graph-matrix GPT model (Model 1) has a classical state space — a polygon — and its QL properties are generated by the calculus of measurement instruments.

We presented several QL effects — such as the order, interference (disjunction), and non-repeatability effects — within the graph-matrix GPT model. The discussion of compound systems and correlated neuronal networks was brief, and we plan to explore the QL properties related to the entanglement of neuronal network states in a future publication.

In our studies, GPT emerged as a framework for bridging classical information processing in neural networks with the QL representations used in decision-making and cognitive psychology. Initially, we turned to GPT not to uncover cognitive effects beyond those described by standard QPT (Quantum Probability Theory) in complex Hilbert space, but because certain GPT models align more naturally with the matrix representation of correlations in real-valued signals, particularly in the case of neurons’ firing patterns as random variables. Specifically, these matrices contain elements that are real numbers.

A notable feature of the particular GPT model we examined in detail (Model 1) is that, from a probability theory perspective, it is classical, with a state space that forms a polygon. Thus, while developing the neural-QL bridge, we also demonstrated that all the cognitive effects previously modeled within the QPT framework can, in fact, be reproduced using classical state GPT models. This is an important foundational insight: the distinction between classicality and quantumness is not solely determined by the nature of the state space. Instead, the essence of quantumness lies in measurement theory and the calculus of instruments. Our work shows that by applying the calculus of instruments to a classical polygon-like state space, we can account for key non-classical cognitive effects — effects that were previously modeled in QPT using a quantum state space based on complex Hilbert space. From this perspective, cognition appears to be less quantum than previously assumed in QL modeling — yet it remains non-classical when viewed through the lens of measurement theory.

A reader can be curios about comparing GPT and QPT in terms of novel cognitive effects that GPT can describe but QPT (with complex Hilbert space) cannot is well-founded. However, at this stage, we have not identified such effects. We hope that our work will attract the attention of experts in cognitive psychology, decision-making, and neuroscience, paving the way for future discoveries of novel effects and open problems in this area.

By the quantum probability theory (QPT) we understood the conventional quantum theory formulated by  Von Neumann (1955)  (and  Lüders (1951) ), but not non-commutative probability theory based on von Neumann algebras. The classical probability theory was formulated by  Kolmogoroff (1933) .

Probability theory needs two kinds of rules:

                            rules for state update.

rules for probability distribution of observables,

In QPT, the state update rule follows from the (von Neumann–Lüders) projection postulate, whereas in CPT, it follows from Bayesian updating. These two rules allow us to compute joint probability distributions for arbitrary sequences of observables, as pointed out by  Wigner (1963)  for QPT.

Regarding the question order effect (QOE) and the response replicability effect (RRE), sequential joint probability in CPT exhibits RRE but not QOE. Conversely, in QPT, sequential joint probability exhibits QOE if and only if it does not exhibit RRE ( Khrennikov et al., 2014; Ozawa & Khrennikov, 2023 ). Therefore, to reconcile QOE and RRE, a new theoretical framework beyond both CPT and QPT is required.

We have demonstrated that such a model exists within the framework of GPT or through QPT combined with instrument theory for state updates ( Ozawa & A., 2021; Ozawa & Khrennikov, 2023 ). GPT provides a general structure for observables (encompassing both QPT and CPT) and describes state updates using the concept of instruments, as introduced by  Davies and Lewis (1970)  and developed by  Ozawa (1984, 1985a, 1985b, 1986, 1989, 1989) . Thus, GPT is not merely the union of QPT and CPT but extends beyond them.

The instrument theory in GPT is emerged to fill the above gaps in both probability theories, QPT and CPT. In fact, non-invasive measurements cannot be studied in QPT, and our Model 1 cannot be described neither in QPT, nor in the standard CPT.
