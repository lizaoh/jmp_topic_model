A Practical Procedure to Build a Knowledge Structure 

Eric Cosyn and Nicolas Thie�ry[1] 

University of California, Irvine 

Computerized assessment of knowledge is one of the most promising applications of knowledge space theory. The first requirement of any such assessment is to produce as accurate a picture as possible of the organization of the knowledge. So far, all procedures designed for this purpose rely exclusively on the query of an expert. Several experiments have shown the limitations of that approach in realistic conditions. One source of difficulty is the very high sensitivity of the querying algorithms to an expert's mistakes. Another source of difficulty concerns the validity of the expert: His or her knowledge structure may diverge greatly from the knowledge structure of the actual population. To solve these difficulties, the present paper proposes and simulates a two-step procedure. The first step implements a modification of an existing querying procedure. The modification implements an error-handling mechanism which lowers the incidence of an expert's careless errors. The second step consists in a refinement mechanism which relies on the knowledge assessments of many subjects to refine the very structure used by these assessments. For each step, it is shown that the underlying knowledge structure can be recovered. � 2000 Academic Press

The five knowledge spaces constructed by the query routine were similar in size and in other overall characteristics but differed substantially in details. The discrepancies were sufficiently large to cast doubt that a valid knowledge space could be obtained in this manner. The purpose of this paper is threefold: The first is to scrutinize the reasons for the discrepancies between the spaces in the Kambouri et al. study. This is a follow-up on these authors' own analysis of their results. The second is to design a more elaborate procedure for obtaining a knowledge structure representative of a particular population of subjects. This procedure is based in part on implementing an improvement of query suggested by Kambouri et al. (1994) and in part on a bootstrapping technique due to Villano (1991). Villano's idea consists in using the empirical results of the knowledge assessment itself to refine the very knowledge structure used in that assessment. Roughly speaking, the knowledge states that are revealed to be unlikely by the assessment are removed from the structure. The third purpose of this paper is to evaluate this new procedure by a large scale simulation. It will be shown that, in a realistic situation in which the simulated experts and the simulated students are allowed to be unreliable, the underlying knowledge structure can be recovered nevertheless.

3. PRACTICAL DIFFICULTIES AND HOW TO ADDRESS THEM 

The query procedure has been extensively tested in a study conducted by Kambouri et al. (1994). The experiment related how five experts performed in constructing a knowledge space on a 50-item domain within the high school mathematics curriculum. Although the five knowledge spaces had similar general characteristics, their discrepancy distributions exhibited dissimilarities larger than one would have expected. Few states were common to several knowledge spaces and the discrepancy indices ranged from 2.4 to 5.6, with most of them exceeding 4. In other words, any state in a given knowledge space differed, on average, by at least four items from the most resembling state in any other knowledge space. 

Interpreting the individual differences between experts, Kambouri et al. dissected the notion of expertise in two components. We will express this distinction according to the following terminology. First, an expert has to be closely acquainted with the domain of knowledge and the student population. The adequacy between the 

387

actual states of the student population and the states that the expert would, at least implicitly, estimate possible defines the validity of the expertise. Second, the expert might be more or less consistent in his or her judgments. This defines the consistency of the expert. 

In the discussion of their paper, Kambouri et al. (1994) investigate the possible causes and remedies for the consistency issue. They give some reasons suggesting why experts equally acquainted with a same domain and student population could nonetheless produce different knowledge structures by giving invalid answers to some of the questions asked by query. We will retain that it is an empirical and unavoidable fact that sometimes an expert happens to give a response which is in disagreement with his or her own expertise. Another related difficulty is the tediousness of the querying process. Tapping the human expertise on a 50-item domain requires the expert to answer several thousand questions. We can expect those lengthy sessions to be prone to producing misjudgments. Moreover, the rigidity of the query procedure amplifies the consequences of these errors. Through the inference engine, each expert's answer with all its logical implications contributes to the construction of the final space. This feature accounts for the efficiency of the algorithm by holding down the number of questions submitted for judgment. But a consequence is that any misjudgment will also irreversibly affect the space under construction. An investigation of the above phenomenon is found in Schrepp 6 Held's simulation study (1995). They show that, even in a situation where the error rate is small, the knowledge structure is dramatically altered. 

A solution, suggested by Kambouri et al. (1994), is to make query resistant to the variability of the expert's answers. This can be achieved by delaying the implementation of an answer and its inferences in the entail relation until they are confirmed by a subsequent answer or inference. In other words, we confer them a pending status. Moreover, if further inferences appear to be inconsistent with some pending inferences, then the two incompatible sets of data are discarded. A drawback of the above method is the increase of the number of questions submitted to the expert. Certainly, extending the length of a query session can only worsen the coherence of an expert. Also the issue of the expert's validity is left unresolved. Relying only on a modified query procedure with pending status is thus insufficient to obtain a valid knowledge structure. 

In the present study, we propose to build a knowledge structure by combining an improved version Of query with an assessment-based procedure. This approach is composed of two steps and proceeds along the following lines. First we shall redesign query in order to implement the pending status mechanism. The new procedure, called ps-query, will be restricted to singletons and thus will yield a quasiordinal knowledge structure. This restriction is not intrinsic to ps-query but a deliberate choice of ours. Stopping the querying process early will reduce the number of questions presented to the expert. Moreover, as these questions involve only singletons, they are easier to judge. Because of the pending status mechanism, the resulting quasi-ordinal space will presumably be very similar to the expert's implicit one. However, as seen in (1), this space will be larger than the expert's actual knowledge structure. At this stage��this is our second step��another procedure will be used for trimming the space.

We have defined the overall features of ps-query which make it consistent. We still have to define how it handles the pending status mechanism. Precisely, we have to provide a confirmation mechanism to decide whether a pair is confirmed or not. Suppose, for instance, that the expert accepts pi . The strategy that we adopted is straightforward. ps-query manages two tables where the positive and negative inferences, respectively, are momentarily stored. At step (i), the confirmation mechanism compares the new inferences drawn from pi with the ones in the tables. If the mechanism finds that a new positive inference is already stored in the table of negative inferences, or that a new negative inference is already stored in the table of positive inferences, it removes all the new inferences from the tables and no pair is confirmed. If there is not such a discrepancy, the confirmation mechanism takes as confirmed the new pairs which are already stored in the tables, and the remaining new inferences are added to the tables. However, to limit the redundancy brought about by this procedure, the mechanism always takes the pair pi as a confirmed pair if there is no new inference other than pi itself. Such a pair has less impact, at least on the short term, than a pair for which there are nontrivial inferences. An important goal of the next section is to test this particular trade-off between the number of questions presented for judgment and the sensitivity of ps-query to occasional contradictions. 

5. SIMULATION OF PS-QUERY 

For the simulation, a reference space Kr , was set as the true knowledge structure of some student population. As our goal is to simulate the conditions that one would face in building a large knowledge space, we chose for Kr a knowledge space built by an expert using query. The expert was a high school teacher and the domain of knowledge was made of 50 items covering the arithmetic curriculum from grade 4 to 8. The construction of Kr required the expert to answer 3982 questions. query first builds an order relation on the items and then extends this order relation to a general entail relation. The quasi-ordinal space derived from the order relation had 14,346 states, which is remarkable considering that, when starting the querying process, the knowledge space had potentially 2[50] r10[15] states. Because of its important role in the rest of the paper, we will refer to this quasi-ordinal space as the reference order K1 . More questioning of the expert by query gradually brought down the size of the knowledge space, eventually yielding the final space Kr made up of 3043 states.

He or she may accept a pair when the correct answer was to reject it, or he or she may reject a pair when the correct answer was to accept it. In case of a wrong rejection, query does not add the pair and its inferences to the entail relation. The final knowledge space will then have states which are not in the true space. 6. REFINING A KNOWLEDGE STRUCTURE 

The refinement procedure that we are using is based on a technique first tested by Villano (1991). In his study, five experts answer query on the same 10-item domain. Since the five knowledge spaces constructed differ largely from one another, a combination structure is constructed using a majority rule on the five 

396 

experts' entail relations. The answer patterns from 60,000 students are then used to refine the combination structure. (An answer pattern is represented by a binary vector indicating, for each item of the domain, if the answer of the student is correct or not.) Two refinement procedures are presented. The first procedure is based on a goodness of fit. Its efficacy in constructing the knowledge structure is superseded by the second procedure which uses the assessment procedure to estimate the likelihood of each of the 2[10] possible patterns. The resulting knowledge structure turns out to be both easier to construct and more efficient when used by the assessment procedure to recover a student's knowledge state. 

This technique is not directly applicable to the large domains that we may encounter in real situations for three reasons. First, the power set of such domains is too large to handle. Second, reliable estimates for the likelihood of each of the many possible patterns requires too many students to be practicable. Third, this technique would require each student to answer systematically all of the questions in the domain. 

We propose an alternate procedure where Villano's technique is adapted for larger domains. As the power set of a large domain is not manageable, the refinement procedure is applied to the quasi-ordinal knowledge space generated by ps-query. This knowledge space is both small enough to be manageable and large enough to contain most of the knowledge states that will constitute the final knowledge structure. Incidentally, this refinement method can be applied to other subsets of the power set. Such subsets need only include most of the knowledge states in the population.

8. DISCUSSION 

The approach taken here is a two-stage procedure which combines human expertise and empirical refinement. Figure 8 compares this approach (right diagram) to the regular query approach (left diagram). The shaded areas represent the latent

knowledge structure that has to be uncovered. The expert's latent knowledge structure is represented by the thick-bordered area. Both knowledge structures are within the domain power set, which is represented by a large ellipse. Note that, for a valid expert, the latent knowledge structure that has to be uncovered and the expert's latent knowledge structure are almost equal. 

The shortcomings of query are well documented (Kambouri et al., 1991; Schrepp 6 Held, 1995). Nevertheless, when restricted to the construction of a quasi-ordinal space, an expert-based procedure provides us with an economical way to get a first approximation of the knowledge structure. Indeed as soon as the size of the domain exceeds a few dozen, it becomes practically impossible to construct a structure just on the basis of empirical data. ps-query answers some of the known weaknesses of query. Its error handling mechanism makes it stable against small fluctuations of the expert's opinions. However, it does not address systematic errors induced by the structure of the querying process (see Held et al., 1995). 

The second step of our simulation explores the ways to prune down a knowledge structure by use of an empirical refinement mechanism. In the simulation, a precise refinement was obtained with a sample size from 1000 to 3000 for a 50-item domain. Remarkably, refining with a number of students much less than the number of states in the latent structure is still acceptable. We have seen that, right after a few hundred assessments, the population's latent knowledge structure, 3043 states here, can be almost entirely recovered. Thus, assessing more students essentially removes remaining superfluous states but does not bring dramatic change. 

A qualitative explanation of this phenomenon could be the following. The organization of knowledge in a population may be crystallized around a relatively small number of basic patterns, much smaller than the size of the population. Each of these patterns could be interpreted as a neighborhood of close knowledge states in terms of symmetric distance. As the assessment procedure increases the likelihood of a latent state together with the likelihood of its neighbors, the states selected by the pruning procedure will tend to be organized in such neighborhoods. This mechanism could explain the excellent values of the discrepancy index di(Kr , KM), as was computed during the simulation of the refinement procedure. However, it has to be noted that, in our simulation, the student population was endowed with a flat distribution. Such a regularity is evidently not expected in a real situation. 

When simulating the refinement process, we set ourselves in the ideal situation where the initial structure is a strict superset of the latent structure. We started from the quasi-ordinal space provided by a perfectly valid expert. Several remarks pertain to such a situation. First, the initial structure could be the union of several quasi-ordinal spaces built by as many experts. If so, we would increase the chance of covering the latent structure. Second, if the initial structure misses states from the latent structure, we have all the more reason to believe that the refinement process will behave as well to recover the latent states which are in the initial structure. As for the missing states, the smoothing process should boost the likelihood of the states in the initial structures which are close to them, increasing the probability that such states will be selected by the pruning process. The above claims call for a formal study of the behavior of the assessment algorithm when it attempts to

recover a state not in its underlying structure. It could lead to a refinement procedure able to recover not only the latent states already present in the initial structure, but also the missing ones.
