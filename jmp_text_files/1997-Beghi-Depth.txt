The Depth Effect of an Oscillating Tilted Bar 

L. Beghi, E. Xausa, L. Tomat, and M. Zanforlin 

Universita�di Padova, Padua, Italy 

An oblique line segment moves on the frontal plane. One extremity is fixed while the other oscillates from left to right at uniform speed along a horizontal line. After a few seconds of observation, the segment appears as a bar oscillating in depth and longer than its maximum length when stationary on the frontal plane. From a perspective point of view the moving segment can be a 2D projection of a rigid bar of any length; in order to explain how the bar appears of a well defined length, a new hypothesis is here advanced. The hypothesis is based on a process of minimization of the relative velocity differences between the various points of the segment. The mathematical model here presented allows quantitative predictions of the apparent length of the bar and qualitative predictions of its apparent movement. These predictions that are well supported by the experimental results. ] 1997 Academic Press 

INTRODUCTION 

The oscillating tilted bar is one of the simplest stimuli that give rise to a stereokinetic phenomenon. It is produced by a line segment with one extremity fixed while the other moves on the frontal plane oscillating from left to right and conversely at uniform speed along a horizontal line; i.e. the line alters in length while moving (see Fig. 1a). 

After a few seconds of observation, the line segment appears as tilted in depth, of well defined constant length, and oscillating. Moreover it appears longer than when it is stationary (Zanforlin 6 Tomat, 1995). 

The fact that the bar appears as having a well defined length is particularly interesting because, as is well known, perception of a 3D structure from 2D moving patterns poses the so-called inverse problem of projective geometry, i.e., a problem that has no unique solution (Poggio 6 Koch, 1985). 

To constrain the number of possible solutions Ullman (1979, 1984) proposed a ``rigidity assumption''; i.e., if we can assume that the moving pattern is the 2D projection of a ``rigid object,'' then in many cases a unique 3D solution is possible. 

Various other theories of 3D perception from 2D moving patterns are based on a rigidity assumption (Musatti, 1924,

1955; Longuet�Higgins, 1986; Hildreth et al., 1990; Proffitt et al., 1992; Caudek 6 Proffitt, 1993; general review of Nakayama, 1985; Cutting, 1987)). 

In our case, as can be clearly seen in Fig. 1b, the line segment can be the 2D projection of a ``rigid bar'' of any length slanted in depth. 

However, as the length of the tilted line segment changes during motion, if we apply Ullman's method (1984) of minimizing distance differences between identifiable points of the configuration in subsequent times or frames, a unique solution is obtained: the bar should appear to move in depth with its length constant and equal to its maximum length when stationary on the frontal plane. 

The same results are obtained if Hoffman and Bennett's (1986) restriction of ``fixed axis'' rotation is applied. 

Caudek and Proffitt's (1993) hypothesis that the visual system will attribute a ``default value'' of depth when the real depth cannot be geometrically determined, will also predict that the moving bar should not appear longer than when it is stationary at its maximum extension on the frontal plane. 

But, as reported above, the experimental results show that the tilted bar, when moving, appears as displaced in depth and of well-defined length, which is longer than the maximum length of the bar when stationary and lying on the frontal plane. 

From a mathematical point of view, a bar that moves around a fixed axis normal to the line of sight is a limiting case of little interest. But as a situation that may contribute to our general understanding of how the visual system works, the tilting moving bar is particularly revealing. After all, it can be compared to the not-so-uncommon perceptual situation of a man who is moving along a straigth line fixating an object in the landscape (Hoffman 6 Bennett, 1986). 

To explain the perceptual depth effect of the oscillating tilted bar, an alternative hypothesis is considered here. 

The hypothesis previously advanced by Zanforlin (1988a, 1988b), Zanforlin 6 Vallortigara (1988), Beghi et al. (1991a, 1991b), to explain the depth effect in other stereokinetic phenomena, is based on a process of velocity difference minimization and was derived from the Gestalt theory of visual perception.

The general idea is that if the points of a pattern moving on the frontal plane have different velocities, the differences between the lengths of these velocity vectors can be annulled or minimized by adding to them a velocity component oriented in depth, in such a way that the length of these new velocity vectors is equal for all the points or else their difference is minimal. The velocities to be taken into consideration are the relative velocities with respect to the perceptual centre of image. This hypothesis can be mathematically formulated and the depth effect quantitatively calculated and predicted. 

The predictions of the mathematical model can be compared with the experimental results.

EXPERIMENTAL CONTROL OF THE HYPOTHESIS 

Mathematical processing of the hypothesis reveals that the apparent length of the moving tilted bar depends only on the h�b ratio as reported in Table 1 and that the apparent movement of the bar in depth follows a conical surface as described in Fig. 5b. This last point is particularly interesting. When the extreme P of the moving bar on the frontal plane oscillates from point B$, whose coordinate is, along 

the horizontal axis, xB $=+b, to point C $, whose coordinate is xC $=&b (see Fig. 5a); i.e., the bar moves from the extreme right to the extreme left, it should appear to describe a complete circular cone around the vertical axis z, and the trajectory of the bar should cross the frontal plane when the bar is at its extreme positions. 

But, as the moving bar for certain h�b ratios will appear much longer than when it is stationary at its extreme position on the frontal plane, it should never appear on the

frontal plane when it is moving. Hence, the moving bar should not appear to describe a complete circle on the conical surface, but, rather it should oscillate along a semicircular trajectory in depth. 

When, however, the h�b ratio is high and the apparent length of the moving bar is not much longer than when it is stationary on the frontal plane, then the moving bar should appear to cross the frontal plane and describe a complete cone. 

To test the quantitative and the qualitative predictions of the hypothesis the following experiment was performed. 

MATERIALS AND METHOD 

The configurations consisted of various tilted line segments or bars of which one extremity was kept stationary, while the other moved horizontally at uniform speed alternatively from left to right and conversely. The moving bars were produced on the screen of an IBM-type computer on which the various parameters could be independently modified. 

The variables considered were: 

(a) the h�b ratio, i.e., the ratio of the projections on the z (vertical) to the x (horizontal) axis of the tilted bar when at maximum extension. Three different values were considered 0.3; 0.7; 2.2. 

(b) the amplitude of oscillation along the horizontal axis, when the bar moves from the right to the left:[1] 2[b][;][ b][.] When the bar crosses the vertical axis (Oz axis) as illustrated in Fig. 4a, this oscillation is 2b. The uniform speed of the moving bars was 4 cm�s. Illumination of the experimental room was approximately 10 cd�m[2] so that the configuration produced on the computer screen could be clearly seen at a distance of 2 m. At the same time the subjects could easily locate their position in the room and clearly evaluate their distance from the screen. 

The subjects were seated in front of the screen with their head coaxial with it and at 2 m distance from it. They were asked to look at the configuration with one eye only and: 

(i) to describe the apparent movement of the bar and, for the maximum oscillations, to say whether it described a complete cone or not; 

(ii) to estimate in centimeters the apparent length of the moving bar by comparing it with a series of line of segments of standard length drawn horizontally on a piece of cardboard at the side of the screen. 

The subjects were 10 university students, naive as to the aims of the experiments and with normal or corrected to normal vision.

RESULTS 

The results of estimated apparent length for the different h�b ratios are reported in Fig. 6, where it can be seen that the predicted values of apparent length are quite close to the estimates made by subjects. The statistical test ANOVA has been applied to the estimates of the three different values of h�b and we obtained F (2.18)=18.936 ( p<0.0001). 

As expected, no statistically significant differences were obtained for the three different values of oscillation amplitude: F (2.18)=0.537 ( p=0.594). 

As regards the qualitative impressions reported by the subjects, when the oscillation amplitude of the bar was 2b we had, for h�b=2.2 and a predicted L*�L=1.08 a prevailing impression, for all the subjects, that the bar described a complete circular cone. But for some oscillations, subjects had also the impression that the bar described a semicircle tilted either towards or away from them. The reverse situation was observed when the value of h�b was 0.3 and the predicted L*�L was 1.38. In this situation the prevailing impression was that the bar described a nearly semicircular cone and only three subjects reported that they could also perceive the bar as describing a complete circular cone. 

An intermediate result was obtained with a value of h�b=1. 

DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION 

The experimental evidence here reported has confirmed previous results (Zanforlin 6 Tomat, 1995), i.e., that the moving tilted bar appears longer than when it is stationary on the frontal plane. 

The results clearly support the predictions of the mathematical model based on the hypothesis of the relative velocity differences minimization. That is, that the apparent

length of the moving bar should depend only on the h�b ratio or the angle made with the horizontal plane when the bar is stationary at its maximum extension. A prediction that has been confirmed by both the quantitative and qualitative impressions described by the subjects. 

The clearly different impressions between a bar that rotates around a vertical fixed axis and a bar that oscillates slanted in depth, never reaching the frontal plane, leave no doubt as to the fact that the bar appears longer that when it is stationary on the frontal plane at its maximum extension. This result cannot be predicted by applying the rigidity hypothesis method of minimizing differences in distance (Ullman, 1984), or the fixed axis method of Hoffman and Bennett (1986) or even the ``default value'' of Caudek and Proffitt (1993), as pointed out in the introduction. 

The relative velocity differences minimization model applied here, does not presuppose that the moving stimulus is the 2D projection of a rigid object. And in fact the velocities distribution, that we have obtained, is not exactly that of a rigid object, although it can be very closely approximated to one. The rigid approximation was applied here because the velocity distribution obtained had no unique 3D solution. In previous cases the velocity distribution obtained did have a unique 3D solution, either of a rigid object or of an elastic one. And we are not yet sure whether a different method of minimizing relative velocity differences may yield a unique solution to the case here considered and make approximation to a rigid object unnecessary. 

The phenomenon of the oscillating tilted bar here described is, in fact, not the only one that can be explained by the velocity minimization hypothesis. As pointed out in the introduction, the hypothesis has already been successfully applied to various other phenomena, such as the stereokinetic phenomena in rotatory motion (Beghi et al., 1981a, 1981b) or the ``swinging gate'' a pattern in translatory motion (Beghi et al., 1994; Xausa et al., 1994; Zanforlin et al., 1994). The oscillating tilted bar phenomenon thus provides further support for the general validity of the hypothesis of the relative velocity differences minimization as being the way in which the visual system extracts 3D patterns from 2D moving configurations.
