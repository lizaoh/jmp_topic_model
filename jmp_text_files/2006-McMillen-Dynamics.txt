When one is presented with a choice among several options, there is a trade-off between speed, characterized by the  reaction time  (RT),

                       and the probability of choosing incorrectly: the  error rate  (ER). If more time is allotted to the decision, more information can be gathered, improving the chance of accuracy, but fewer decisions will be made in a fixed duration block of trials. The best trade-off is often one that maximizes the  reward rate  (RR) ( Gold & Shadlen, 2002 ), the number of correct decisions per unit time, although considerations such as energy cost can modify this metric.

In this paper, we consider neurally based noisy accumulator models for choice tasks with  
                         n 
                         ⩾ 
                         2 
                       alternatives, and derive relations among speed, accuracy, signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) and  n . Networks of such accumulators, also called integrators, have been proposed as cognitive mechanisms (see  Ratcliff, Van Zandt, & McKoon, 1999; Usher & McClelland, 2001 ). Each accumulator is assumed to be preferentially sensitive to one of the  n  alternatives and to accrue ‘evidence’ at a rate determined by the strength of its incoming signal and choices are made based on absolute or relative evidence levels. Various response protocols are employed, and here we shall consider two common ones:  free response  and  interrogation  (sometimes called time-controlled), which correspond, respectively, to sequential and fixed sample size tests in hypothesis testing. In both cases, the subject is presented with a stimulus on each trial and instructed to identify it among  n  alternatives. Under the free-response protocol, the decision may be made at any time after stimulus presentation; under interrogation, the decision must be rendered at a fixed time

                       after presentation.

Most theory in statistical hypothesis testing has been concerned with tests to distinguish between two hypotheses. In that case, the Neyman–Pearson lemma ( Neyman & Pearson, 1933 ) provides the best fixed sample size test in that it minimizes ERs, and Wald's  sequential probability ratio test  (SPRT) is optimal for sequentially accumulating data, in the sense that it minimizes the expectation of decision time among all tests for which the probabilities of error do not exceed pre-determined bounds. See  Wald and Wolfowitz (1948)  or  Lehmann (1959)  for a simpler proof.

Several multiple hypothesis tests have been proposed in attempts to generalize the SPRT. Most of these involve combinations of pair-wise SPRTs, and exploit the optimality property of that test.  Sobel and Wald (1949)  designed a test among three hypotheses by combining pair-wise SPRTs, and  Armitage (1950)  extended this to an arbitrary number of hypotheses: see the review by  Eisenberg (1991) . While optimal tests for three or more options have not been found, ERs and RTs can be bounded and tests that are optimal in an asymptotic setting have been designed.  Lorden (1977)  derived multiple hypothesis tests that were nearly optimal and provided a framework for further developments.  Golubev and Khas’minskii (1983)  discussed tests for signals embedded in Gaussian noise, and tests for other particular signal types were considered in  Verdenskaya and Tartakovskii (1991) ,  Veeravalli and Baum (1995) ,  Tartakovsky (1998a, 1998b) .  Baum and Veeravalli (1994)  discussed the relationship between Bayesian optimality and the multihypothesis sequential ratio test (MSPRT) that we will consider in this paper, and they showed why tests that achieve optimality for all ERs are difficult to design, if they exist at all. Recently, general results on tests that minimize the expectation of stopping time in the limit where ERs approach zero were given by  Dragalin, Tartakovsky, and Veeravalli (1999, 2000) ; these will be especially relevant to our work.

We consider both  absolute tests , in which the first accumulator to cross a preset threshold wins, and  relative tests , in which the  difference  between one accumulator and some combination of the others must exceed a threshold. In each case we describe how tests can be implemented by neural integrator networks.  Dragalin et al. (1999)  note that optimal tests for general data may not exist, and that even if they do, they could require several levels of computation. Thus, sub-optimal tests might perform slightly worse but be easier to implement neurally. Since computation carries energy costs, we suggest that decision-making may have evolved in animals to be non-optimal in the sense of RR, but energetically cheaper.

This paper is structured as follows. In  Section 2  we present the leaky accumulator model, of which the race model is a special case, and we review results on two-alternative choices; here and until  Section 6  we restrict our analysis to linearized models. In  Section 3  we place the investigation in a more abstract setting and complete our background review by describing key results for multihypothesis sequential tests from  Dragalin et al. A fourth problem with data reported in the literature is the lack of information on ERs. We have seen that the factors  A  and  B  depend on ER (Fig.  4 ), hence, averaging RTs from trials with differing ERs may skew results. Much of the literature reports MRTs only for error-free performance or ‘low’ ERs; e.g. in  Hick (1952)  and  Teichner and Krebs (1974)  ERs are unspecified but assumed small, and only RTs corresponding to correct answers are given. A proper comparison of experimental data to the leaky accumulator model should compare MRTs at a fixed ER, and also report the MRTs for incorrect responses.

Several of our modeling assumptions deserve further investigation. We assume that parameters such as SNR, leakage and inhibition remain constant while  n  is varied. This would be difficult, if not impossible, to control in practice, since it implies that the alternatives must, in some sense, retain fixed ‘distances’ from each other as their number changes. We have also assumed that each accumulator is preferentially sensitive to a single alternative by the same margin  a , also irrespective of  n . If preferential sensitivity changes with  n , the model would have to be modified to account for this. We are unaware of experimental data relevant to this question.

A final issue is that of nonlinearities that limit dynamic range. We have seen that the simplest SDEs  (101) – (102)  incorporating the essential features of the stochastic and nonlinear properties of the leaky accumulator model diverge from the behavior of the linearized system when the baseline input strength  
                         
                            
                               S 
                            
                            
                               0 
                            
                         
                       is small, due to lower bounds on accumulator states. This suggests a further line of experimental research. As noted in  Section 6 , the ‘linear’ performance may be restored by increasing input levels to all units, but this may be accomplished  internally  and/or  externally . The neural system itself could increase input levels in expectation of an incoming stimulus; this might be seen as a model for ‘concentration’ by a subject who directs attention to the task on hand. Alternatively, an experimenter may be able to externally manipulate the baseline strength of all alternative stimuli without changing the value of the difference between the correct and incorrect signals.

In either case one would expect to encounter limits to baseline input increases or to run into upper limits of neural activities, suggesting that the degree to which the attenuation effect of reflecting boundaries can be assuaged is limited and thus predicting that performance should diverge from Hick's Law for large numbers of alternatives. The studies of  Section 6  predict that this occurs as shown in  Fig. 20  
                      if it is solely due to lower cut-off effects . Other phenomena may appear if saturation at high firing rates also intrudes, and as noted at the end of  Section 6  a full study of the nonlinear network would be required to resolve issues such as the improved discriminability noted by Usher (personal communication). The number of alternatives has generally been limited to 10 or fewer, and we are unaware of experiments that test Hick's Law for larger  n . It would be interesting to see if the scaling breaks down as predicted in  Fig. 20 , or otherwise, as  n  increases.
