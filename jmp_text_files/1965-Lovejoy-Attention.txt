JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY: 2, 342-362 (1965)  An Attention Theory of Discrimination Learning'  ELIJAH LovEJOY  University of Pennsylvania, Philadelphia, Pennsylvania  Consideration of the data on the Overlearning Reversal Effect (ORE) suggests that attention is an important variable in  discrimination learning. A linear model is proposed for such learning in which one of three responses occurs: (a) the subject  attends and makes a correct choice; (b) the subject attends and makes an error; or (c) the subject does not attend (and is  successful with probability 2). A 3-vector of response probabilities is introduced and linear transformations of this vector  are proposed corresponding to the four possible experimental events. The expected mean learning and extinction curves are  found, and the expected number of total errors in learning is computed. For appropriate parameter values, an ORE is predicted  in terms of total errors, and in terms of probability of correct.  The idea of attention is once again becoming respectable in psychology. This is in part due to recent neurophysiological  studies of attention and arousal. And it is perhaps partly due to the conception of the overt "observing response" which has  much of the same explanatory appeal as attention, without the troublesome mentalistic overtones. (See, for example, Atkinson,  1960.) As has been pointed out independently by Sutherland (1959), it appears that the idea of attention can be useful in  explaining the paradoxical "overlearning reversal effect" (ORE)-the finding that overtrained animals can sometimes reverse a  discrimination habit more quickly than nonovertrained controls (Capaldi and Stevenson, 1957; D'Amato and Jagoda, 1961; Komaki,  1961; Mackintosh, 1962, 1963; North and Clayton, 1959; Pubols, 1956; Reid, 1953).  Described below is one of a pair of mathematical models which were motivated by the thought that attention must be an  important variable when the ORE is found. The other model has not been analyzable mathematically, but some Monte-Carlo  explorations have indicated a basic similarity between the two models. This alternative model is discussed at length in a  companion paper (Lovejoy, 1965).  Intuitively it is argued that overtrained animals continue to attend to the relevant stimulus dimension throughout reversal,  while nonovertrained subjects are more likely to stop attending to the relevant dimension for a period during reversal  learning. Thus they have a number of trials when they are learning less about the relevant stimuli and they reverse more  slowly as a consequence. In several overlearning-reversal experiments it has been found that the length of the initial post-reversal string of errors  increases with overtraining (e.g., Reid, 1953; Mackintosh, 1962, 1963). But as Reid observed, overtrained subjects tend to  improve comparatively rapidly once they have had their first success in reversal, so that they reach a reversal criterion  sooner than nonovertrained controls. From these two data, we can infer that the learning curve for the overtrained group  should start with lower probability of correct than that of the control group, but that after a moderate amount of reversal  learning the curves for the two groups should cross. Because the overtrained subjects make more errors early in reversal than  the nonovertrained animals, the total number of errors in reversal may be about the same for the two groups, even though the  overtrained group reaches criterion first (and thus is said to learn faster). With a stiff criterion, the trials-to-criterion  score depends primarily on the relative positions of the learning curves late in reversal, and it is this measure which  generally has been used in experimental work.  Under certain conditions, the present model predicts a crossover of reversal learning curves for overtrained and  nonovertrained subjects. DISCUSSION  The model which has just been presented has successfully predicted the Overlearning Reversal Effect-at least with a high value  for the limit point X11. In this case it predicts an ORE with difficult (not obvious) problems, but none for very obvious  problems. This prediction fits in nicely with the experiments mentioned in the introduction. It also accords well with a  recent experiment by Mackintosh (1965). He found no ORE when training chicks on a brightness problem; but when he investigated  a more difficult horizontal-vertical orientation discrimination, a large ORE was found. Chicks are very visual animals,  compared with rats; so it is not surprising that brightness is an "obvious" cue for chickens. It is startling, although quite  in agreement with the present model, that by making the problem more difficult you can "produce" an ORE. Mackintosh's results  are encouraging.  ATTENTION THEORY OF DISCRIMINATION LEARNING 361  In order for the present model to predict a sizable ORE, aI must be high. Let us consider some of the implications of assuming  this: in the limiting case where X11 = 1, the subjects learn just as much from event AB as from event AW: as long as they  attend, it does not matter which response they make. The changes in the probabilities will be the same. And for both events,  no probability will be transferred to A. This means that once a subject has a very high probability of attending, then he will  always attend from that point on-even if reward is reversed. The only event that reduces the probability of attending is AW:  rewarded nonattention. And once the animal has P(A) = 1, A can never occur again. P(A) = I is an absorbing barrier in the  present model, when aI = 1.  When Ai = 1, Theorem 3 says that the limiting value of P(A) in extinction is unity. This is another strong prediction, and it  seems unpleasantly counterintuitive. For consider a very difficult problem, such that PI(A) = 0.1. The present model says that  by inserting a sufficiently long strong of nonrewarded "extinction" trials before introducing reward, the probability of  attending to the relevant dimension will increase to unity, and the problem will be very "obvious" when reward is finally made  available. This is clearly a testable prediction-and it seems unlikely that it would be fulfilled.  Although this model thus seems to make a somewhat unlikely prediction, the larger class of models of which this one is a  member need not. The limiting value of P(A) in Theorem 3 was large because it was assumed that there was a general sort of  symmetry between attending and nonattending. Clearly this symmetry is less and less justifiable as a dimension becomes more  and more obscure, or improbable. However, it should be possible to extend the present model to a more general case in which an  animal may attend to any one of several independent dimensions. For such a model extinction conditions would distribute the  probability of attending over all the possible dimensions, so that no single one would "dominate" as it does in the  single-dimension case treated above. It seems a fair guess that the analysis of the parent class of models will be  sufficiently complicated that the present one may well find use as an approximation, even if it may not be applicable to very  "unlikely" or obscure dimensions.
