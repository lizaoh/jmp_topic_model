The idea that the adoption of an action by a large group of agents can directly influence the adoption decisions of subsequent agents has a long history in Economics. This phenomena is called a  herd behavior , since individual rationality does not seem to fully explain such behavior. As first mentioned by  Keynes (1936)  in his beauty queen contest example, this notion can empirically explain investment decisions in asset markets for instance (see  Scharfstein and Stein (1990) ).

The main theoretical explanation to this phenomena so far is the idea of  informational cascade , as developed in  Bikhchandani, Hirshleifer, and Welch (1992)  and  Banerjee (1992) , for instance. The basic idea is that massive adoption of a particular action is a signal to subsequent agents about the value of this action. When unsure about the actual value, this large public signal may overwhelm any private individual belief, and future agents adopt the same action on the ground that the public signal is a more reliable information than their. New adoptions reinforce the value of public signal, and every further agent will adopt this same action on the same biased ground.

In this paper, we develop and formalize a new theoretical explanation, which is based on the idea that herd behavior may not have psychological (or belief-based) origins. Our view is that the adoption of a particular action by a large group of agents may generate some form of Externalities, as described next, and mimicking the action of this group (joining the pack) allows to benefit from such Externalities. The key idea emphasized here is that prior large adoptions of the same action generates cross-effects of subsequent agents’ marginal utility when comparing one action to another

New adoptions also reinforce such an externality, leading further agents to choose the same action. We call this phenomena a  pack behavior , since for instance it is observed that species such as wolves form pack to facilitate hunting, for instance, and thus joining a pack increases survival chances.

Concrete examples illustrating this idea, without explicitly introducing our concept of pack behavior, can be found in  Katz and Shapiro (1985) ,  Arthur (1989)  and  Kirman (1993) , for instance. Another concrete example of pack behavior, illustrating the self-reinforcement of such Externalities, is as follows. Consider a firm investing to develop a software, and facing the choice of making the software compatible with one of two available operating systems. The operating systems are incompatible in the sense that a software developed for one cannot be used with the other. When making its investment decision, the firm must consider the relative market shares of those operating systems. Indeed, when development costs are roughly identical, the firm is better off developing the software for the operating system with the higher number of users, since there simply are more potential customers. Thus, any software firm should find it optimal to write software for the dominating operating system. In turn, more available software make the dominating operating system more attractive; its market share thus becomes higher and new software firms have even more incentives to develop products for it.

In this context, it is difficult to see why some users would decide to switch to the dominated system unless a significantly better software is developed for it. However, given the externality generated by the large number of products already written for the dominating system, the development of this innovative product may well require significant investments to reach a reduced market. This investment decision thus has limited potential profits, and it seems more profitable to use large investments to write products for the dominating operating systems. Altogether, firms’ rational decisions reinforce the pack behavior associated with using the dominating operating system. In our example, a lock-in situation where the dominating system eventually dominates the whole market is to be expected, because of the Externalities generated by massive adoption of a particular product.

We next informally describe the model and our results. We consider an infinite population of agents, who have a type drawn by nature from a finite set that determines their preference for a particular action. Agents sequentially play once. We say that an action has a  pack externality  if, and only if, when a level of population is reached and a large fraction of the population has previously chosen this action, then every subsequent agent of any type finds it optimal to choose this action to benefit from the group externality. We consider Nash Equilibrium for this game, and we say that an equilibrium play path has a pack behavior if, after some period, every agent chooses an action that has a pack externality, regardless of her type.

Proposition 3.1  shows that, if there exists an action with pack externality and if the ex-ante probability that an agent chooses this action is greater than the fraction of population needed to trigger the externality, then on almost every equilibrium play path has a pack behavior. The basic insight of this result is that, when the above conditions are met, the fraction of agents choosing this action without externality will become large enough to trigger a pack behavior on almost every path that do not have yet a pack behavior. Critical to this result is that, from a statistical standpoint, the fraction of population capable of triggering a pack behavior is large enough.

When this last requirement is not met,  Proposition 3.2  shows that there are cases when an action with pack externality cannot trigger a pack behavior. In particular, it states that if the overall population needed to trigger a pack behavior is too large, and if the ex-ante probability that an agent chooses this action is less than the fraction of population needed to trigger the externality, then this action will trigger a pack behavior on almost no equilibrium path. This action may be the most efficient outcome that the group can reach, but nevertheless individual decisions will prevent the group to generate this externality. In particular, this result shows that a pack behavior is not Pareto-efficient.

There are other cases of inefficiencies, as described in  Proposition 3.3 . Consider two actions with pack externality starting at the same history, and chosen by different fractions of the population. Consider also that, once a pack behavior is triggered by those actions, the payoff from the first action is greater than the payoff to the second. If the ex-ante probability that an agent chooses the first action is less than the fraction needed to trigger a pack externality, if the ex-ante probability that an agent chooses the second action is greater than the fraction needed, and if the overall population needed is large enough, then the dominating action will trigger a pack behavior on almost no equilibrium path. Here again, individual choices prevent social coordination on the most efficient outcome.

The previous result gives a situation where an action has a pack externality, but can trigger a pack behavior on almost no path for statistical reasons. It states that, if this action requires a large population to generate externality, and if an insufficient fraction of the population (in a statistical sense) is willing to choose this action without pack externality, then a pack behavior triggered by this action will appear on almost no path. The key aspect of the previous result is that the fraction of population that can trigger this pack behavior is not large enough to start it. Thus the group cannot coordinate itself on this action to generate a potentially beneficial externality, because individual choices lead to abandon this opportunity. Clearly, if agents were reordered so that the first to choose would be those choosing this action, then the group as a whole would be better off. Thus, the ordering of the agents is a determinant of Pareto-optimality, as in herd behavior.

The previous result considers the case of two actions having pack externality at the same population, with one dominating the other. If the pack externality occurs at a large enough population, and if there is not enough population (in a statistical sense) willing to choose the dominating action without externality, then no pack behavior triggered by the dominating action will occur. Still, pack behavior will be witnessed by  Proposition 3.1  on almost every path, but they will be triggered by inferior actions. In this case again, failure to coordinate the choices of agents to generate the dominating pack externality leads to socially inferior outcomes.

We have developed and formalized the concept of pack behavior, which is observationally equivalent to an information cascade but has different foundations. The main difference is that, in our approach, agents rationally choose to follow the group behavior to benefit from Externalities generated by this group.

Another significant difference with the notion of informational cascade is that a pack behavior is much slower to start, since we require that a large enough population must have chosen an action without beneficiating from pack externality to generate this phenomenon. Since one can reasonably assume that a large population is less likely to make wrong choices than a smaller one needed to trigger an informational cascade (see  Banerjee (1992) ), our concept is more likely to lead a given group to better outcomes. Nevertheless, our notion is shown to be ex-ante inefficient in some cases.
