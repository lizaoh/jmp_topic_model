As we have already observed, cues are predictive indicators of a respective target variable. In their capacity as predictive indicators, every cue may be treated as a simple method of prediction, namely, the method that predicts in strict accordance with the given cue. This observation yields a novel interpretation of cue-based prediction tasks, namely as a framework for meta-learning and meta-induction. According to this interpretation, cues are regarded as  independent  or  object-level  prediction methods and the cue-based prediction methods are regarded as  dependent  or  meta-level  prediction methods, designed to choose an optimal method, or combination of methods, for given tasks in given environments. The research program of  meta-induction  was developed within philosophy, partly as a means to addressing Hume’s problem of induction ( Henderson, 2018; Schurz, 2008, 2019; Schurz & Thorn, 2016 ). Similarly,  meta-learning  has been coined in computer science as a general label for the problem of learning to choose an optimal method for different tasks and environments ( Lemke, Budka, & Gabrys, 2015 ). In psychological research on cue-based predictions, this problem has been called the problem of strategy selection ( Gigerenzer & Selten, 2001 ). Key results about meta-induction are based on insights on  regret-based  learning that were developed in a branch of computational learning theory known as  online learning under expert advice  (cf.  Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi, 2012; Cesa-Bianchi & Lugosi, 2006 ).

In the present paper, we call a meta-level prediction method “meta-inductive”, if its reliance on a cue increases monotonically with the cue’s observed predictive success (or observed cue-event correlation). Two families of meta-inductive prediction methods are often contrasted in the psychological literature:  one-reason  methods and  weighting  methods ( Şimşek & Buckmann, 2015; Hogarth & Karelaia, 2005; Martignon & Hoffrage, 1999 ). A famous one-reason method, which is perhaps the simplest meta-inductive method, is  Take-the-Best  (TTB). 
                         3 
                       
                      
                         3 
                         Note that with “one-reason method” it is meant that the decision, but not the search procedure, is based on a single cue. 
                       As its name suggests, TTB emulates the prediction delivered by the most successful accessible object-level method (or cue). TTB is prominent in research on cue-based prediction methods because of its  ecological rationality , i.e., its comparatively high predictive success in spite of its cognitive simplicity ( Todd & Gigerenzer, 2012  Sec. 3, 9). Unlike one-reason methods, weighting methods predict a weighted average of the predictions delivered by the accessible object-level methods (or cues). The most straightforward meta-inductive weighting method is  Success-based Weighting  (SW), which has also been called “Franklin’s rule”. SW predicts a linear weighted average of cue predictions, where the observed success rates of cues serve as weights ( Gigerenzer & Todd, 1999  83). More computationally intensive weighting methods include  Multiple Linear Regression  and  Multiple Logistic Regression . These regression-based methods make predictions according to an optimized linear combination of the values of the accessible cues. In addition to SW and regression-based methods, there are Bayesian prediction methods whose predictions are formed by non-linear functions of cue predictions. Bayesian methods make predictions by attempting to judge the conditional probability of various event values given observed cue values.

It is well-known that, in many environments, the predictive success of weighting methods can be significantly greater than TTB ( Bröder, 2012; Gigerenzer & Gaissmaier, 2011; Katsikopoulos, 2011; Katsikopoulos, Durbach, & Stewart, 2018; Martignon & Hoffrage, 2002 ). It is often suggested that the class of environments where weighting methods outperform TTB is the class of (so called) ‘compensatory’ environments.

Despite these results, there is no characterization of the class of environments in which weighting methods are able to outperform TTB (or vice versa) that can be applied without prior knowledge of cue validates or of some other sort information that is often unavailable in the case of ‘real world’ prediction tasks. Matters are further complicated when we consider cases where inductive learning must be performed on data streams that are not generated by independent and identically distributed (IID) random variables, in which case, event frequencies and cue validities may change over time without convergence towards limiting probabilities. For the preceding reasons, it would be highly attractive to employ a meta-level prediction strategy that achieves the “best of both worlds”, performing (almost) as well as TTB in the environments in which TTB is successful, and (almost) as well as SW (and other weighting strategies) in environments in which the latter strategies are successful, so that whenever we do not know which type of environment we are in – a rather realistic case in real life situations – the strategy achieves top performance. In this paper, we investigate a prediction strategy called  Attractivity Weighting  (or Regret Weighting), abbreviated as AW. We think this strategy offers “the best of both worlds” to a high degree. AW is a weighting strategy (of sorts), though in many cases it assigns all weight to a single cue, thereby emulating the behavior of TTB. Rather than using the success rates of object-level methods (or cues) as weights, AW uses so called  attractivities  (or regrets) as weights.

From the viewpoint of a given meta-level method, the attractivity of a cue (at a given time point) is defined as the cue’s success rate  minus  the meta-method’s success rate, if the cue is more successful than the meta-method. If the meta-method’s success rate is greater than the cue’s, then the cue’s attractivity is set to zero, or in the case of exponential weighting methods close to zero. The use of attractivities as weights is the basis of so called regret-based learning ( Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi, 2012; Cesa-Bianchi & Lugosi, 2006 ). The crucial advantage of using attractivities, rather than success rates, as weights, is that any cue whose success rate is worse than the meta-method’s is  ignored . We will see below that it is  this  feature that guarantees that the success rate of AW converges to the success rate of TTB in those environments where TTB performs best.

Despite emulating the performance of TTB in environments where TTB performs best, AW has an advantage over TTB, namely: it is (provably)  universally access-optimal , which means that it is guaranteed to have at least as good long-run success as the best object method (or cue) to which it has access, in any environment whatsoever. Access-optimality (a generalization of Hannan-consistency, cf.  70 Cesa-Bianchi & Lugosi, 2006 ) is a key desideratum of methods of regret-based online prediction under expert advice. In  Schurz (2008, 2019) , it is argued that this feature is the key to providing a non-circular solution to the problem of induction, since the application of an access-optimal method to any finite collection of accessible prediction methods can only improve one’s predictive success in the long run, in comparison to employing any of the other accessible methods. We shall see below that, in distinction to AW, neither TTB nor SW are universally access-optimal, which means that there are environments where their performance is substantially worse than that of the best object-level method or cue. In fact, there are no alternatives to AW, aside from ones that exploit attractivity weighting (and are thus variants of AW), that are known to be universally access-optimal.

The remainder of this paper is structured as follows. In Section  2 , we introduce the concept of a layered prediction game, which generalizes the notion of a prediction game introduced in  Schurz (2008) . We then outline the major mathematical properties of the prediction methods studied in this paper, including TTB, SW, and AW. In Sections  3 and 4 , we present the results of two empirical simulation studies of the predictive performance of AW in the short run, in comparison with the other mentioned methods: Section  3  describes our application of AW retrospectively to the data of the Monash University Footy Tipping Competition; Section  4  describes the application of AW to the twenty data sets used by  Czerlinski et al. (1999)  in evaluating the performance of TTB. Thereby, the following insights and new results are achieved:

– In Section  2 , central mathematical results about the mentioned prediction methods are presented, concerning the long-run access-optimality as well as the failure of access-optimality and worst case bounds for short-run regrets. Mathematical results are titled as “theorems”. For results that have been proved in previous works, we refer to the places where their proof can be found; all results for which a proof is given are new.

– While some of the empirical simulation results presented in Section  3  were previously published in  Schurz and Thorn (2016) , this section focuses on new results concerning the meta-methods introduced in Section  2 , and new results concerning binary-valued predictions.

– The empirical simulation results presented in Section  4  are entirely new. Taken together, the results obtained in Sections  3 and 4  confirm that AW’s short-run performance is (almost) as good as that of TTB in the environments in which TTB is optimal and (almost) as good as SW and other weighting strategies in environments in which the latter strategies are optimal.

In the end, our conclusion will be that the universal access-optimality of AW, combined with its excellent performance in real-world environments, speaks in favor of its adoption as the meta-level method of choice.

Each round of a prediction game begins with the object-level methods delivering their predictions (based, for example, on the frequencies of past events). Next, the meta-level methods compute their predictions (based on the predictions and so far observed success rates of the object-level methods). Finally, the event of the given round is revealed and the performance of the participating prediction methods is evaluated. A prediction game can be considered as an iteration of the procedure of selecting the first n events as a training set, and the next event as a test set. If the event sequence is generated by a series of IID random experiments, then the results are not different from a performance evaluation by the random selection of training and test sets, but (as we mentioned above) our investigation also considers non-random sequences of any sort including induction-hostile sequences whose event frequencies and corresponding predictive success rates do not converge to limiting probabilities but oscillate endlessly. Likewise, we do not require that object-level methods be cues in the ordinary sense. The object-level methods may be computational algorithms, real-life experts, or even genuine clairvoyants. If an object-level method is realized by a human forecaster, then each meta-level method corresponds ipso facto to a  social learning  strategy.

It is important to notice that prediction in the setting of prediction games is ‘online’, where players have the opportunity to update their estimates of the performance of cues (and object methods) in the course of making successive predictions. Online prediction tasks also present players with the problem of using cues whose predictive validities change over time or are even dependent upon the behavior of players, and differs significantly from ‘offline’ prediction tasks (where players do not update their estimates of cue performance). Because the performance of real-world cues does change over time and is, in some cases, dependent on the behavior of human actors, it is plausible to suppose that human cognition is adapted to making predictions in an online manner. Further, because real-world predictions must frequently be made in an online manner, it would be of considerable value to include methods of online prediction in instructions aimed at improving human performance in judgment and decision making.

Note that when comparing the predictive performance of methods, we will always compare success rates rather than validities. We take this course, since a prediction method can aim to maintain a high validity, at the expense of its success rate, by refusing to make a prediction whenever the risk of error is high.

There are two kinds of object-level prediction methods (or cues):  persistent  and  intermittent . Persistent methods deliver a prediction at each time (regarding each object or event), while intermittent methods do not — because, for example, the method is based on a comparison of two cue values that may fail to discriminate. In fact, the crucial distinction is not between persistent and intermittent methods, but between  persistent-based  and  intermittent-based evaluation . We say that a meta-level method employs an intermittent-based evaluation, if it  ignores  non-predictions in forming its predictions. In other words, an intermittent-based method ignores  ersatz  predictions when evaluating the success of object-level methods, and ignores  ersatz  predictions in determining its own predictions of the events. In contrast to intermittent-based methods, we say that a meta-level method employs a persistent-based evaluation, if it treats all object-level methods as persistent methods, by the convention of treating  ersatz  predictions as if they were genuine predictions. This means that a variant of TTB that employs a persistent-based evaluation will predict 0.5 in those cases where the most successful object-level method fails to deliver a (genuine) prediction. In what follows, if MI designates a particular meta-inductive strategy (such as TTB, SW, or AW), then pMI designates its persistent-based variant and iMI its intermittent-based variant. In situations where all of the object-level methods (or cues) are persistent predictors, the two variants coincide, but in situations involving at least some intermittent object-level predictors, the performance of the two variants may differ significantly.

In other words, pTTB is access-optimal in the class of all prediction games, or environments, that contain a unique best object-level method, or cue. The short run performance of pTTB depends on how fast the success frequencies of the object-level methods (or cues) converge against stable probabilities.

The intermittent-based variant of TTB, iTTB, selects as its favorite, at every time n, that cue whose validity is greatest among those cues that delivered a genuine prediction (with ties resolved according to an arbitrary ordering). At time 1, and when no method delivers a genuine prediction, iTTB predicts 0.5. While pTTB can never perform better than the best object-level method, iTTB can. The access-optimality of TTB (both variants) breaks down whenever the success rates of two or more leading cues oscillate endlessly around each other in negative correlation to their position of being TTB’s favorite. In environments of this sort, the cues act as so-called  deceivers , i.e., methods that are highly accurate when they are not imitated by TTB, but highly inaccurate when they are imitated. In such cases, the predictions of TTB will be highly inaccurate, while the cues are highly accurate (assuming a sufficient number of cues), because at each time only one cue is imitated by TTB and predicts inaccurately, while all other cues are not imitated and predict accurately. Detailed simulations of such scenarios are found in  Schurz (2008, p. 288, Fig. 2)  and  Schurz and Thorn (2016, p. 42, Fig. 2) .

A realistic example of a task that is adverse for TTB consists in making judgments about where one should go in attempting to gather a seasonal resource (e.g., fish or berries). Widespread adoption of TTB applied to cues concerning the past productivity of given locations will drive each member of a population of TTBers to attempt to gather resources at the same location, resulting in a poor mean payoff for the TTBers.  Fig. 1  presents the results of a simulation that illustrates this dynamic. Within the simulation, players must predict which of 10 locations will be productive, where a player’s score in a round is equal to the productivity of the location that the player selected. Within the simulation, there are 1000 TTBers, and one additional player, AW, who makes her predictions via attractivity weighting and is described below. In each round, the TTBers select that location that was most productive, on average, in the previous rounds. As can be seen in  Fig. 1 , the average score for the TTBers (green line) is near zero (converging to a value slightly less than 1/1000), while the average score for AW (blue line) converges to a value slightly greater than 9/10, which is explained below. Similar dynamics may be observed in a wide range of tasks, including market entry, career choice, route selection, departure time selection, etc. For empirical studies showing that human performance is excellent in such tasks, see work by  Rapoport, Gisches, Daniel, and Lindsey (2014), Rapoport, Seale, Erev, and Sundali (1998)  and  Rapoport, Stein, Parco, and Seale (2004) .

Is universal access-optimality achievable at all? Yes it is, based on weighting methods that use  attractivities  (or regrets) as weights. Attractivity-based weighting methods, abbreviated AW, are protected against the sub-optimality illustrated by the example in the proof of  Theorem 4 , because they attach a weight of  zero  to all object-level methods whose success rates are smaller than their own. This precaution ensures that, in the above example, the success rate of AW (in all variants of AW) grows, after some convergence time, above that of the suboptimal cue

How successfully AW protects itself against deception is also seen from  Fig. 1  (above). Unlike the TTBers, AW sets her ‘attractivity’ for each location to be the difference in the average score earned at the location in previous rounds minus the average score of AW in previous rounds. AW selects a given location with probability equal to the location’s normalized attractivity (i.e., the location’s attractivity divided by the sum of the attractivities for the ten locations).

                          Similar to pAW, we have defined iAW so that it simulates the predictions of the corresponding version of TTB, in cases where no accessible method has positive attractivity. This measure entails that iAW will behave similarly to iTTB in situations where iTTB is access-optimal.

As with TTB and SW, we expect that iAW will frequently perform better than pAW in prediction games that include highly intermittent cues. In fact, pAW suffers from the same problem as pTTB and pSW in the example that was used to illustrate the advantages of iTTB and iSW over pTTB and pSW. The preceding presents us with a dilemma. The method iAW has a  local  advantage: in environments with highly intermittent object-level cues, its predictive success is significantly greater than that of the best cue and, thus, than that of pAW. However, iAWs local advantage comes at the  cost  of losing universal access-optimality. A similar phenomenon emerges in regard to comparing pAW with other meta-level methods. For example, as mentioned above, SW (in its p or i version) performs better than pTTB in certain environments – mainly those with compensatory cues – and pAW tends to behave as a one-reason method in these environments. Thus SW outperforms pAW in these environments, but again at the cost of not being universally access-optimal.  Schurz and Thorn (2016)  call this phenomenon the “revenge of ecological rationality” at the meta-level.

Should one give up universal access-optimality in order to achieve local advantages in some environments? We think:  definitely not , in particular not in dynamical settings with unpredictable changes of the environment. Here the use of a meta-level method that is guaranteed to perform as well as the best accessible method, independently of the environment one is in, is of inestimable value. Generally speaking universal access-optimality is also a rare feature. We have already seen that highly reputed meta-level methods such as TTB and SW fail to be universally access-optimal. Theorem 7  applies to all methods whose predictions converge to the limiting frequency of 1s in (e), in the case of minimal convergent binary games. This includes a wide range of plausible prediction methods, including linear regression, logistic regression and typical Bayesian methods. Admittedly, the range of the result is limited, since it assumes a linear loss function. Whether logistic regression or some Bayesian method is universally access-optimal, with a restriction to quadratic or other loss functions that correspond to proper scoring rules is an open question. In contrast, pAW is guaranteed to be universally access-optimal, given the modest assumption that the loss function is convex.

Universal access-optimality is an important and rare property that one should notgive up easily. Fortunately there is the possibility of solving the “revenge problem” concerning pAW, while still maintaining the key desideratum of pAW, namely, its universal access-optimality. The solution to this problem is based on the observation that meta-level methods may access, and imitate, other meta-level methods. So if we are worried that we are in an environment in which iAW will outperform pAW, we may address our worry by employing a variant of pAW that treats iAW as an accessible prediction method. Similarly, if we are worried that we are in an environment in which iSW will outperform pAW, we may address our worry by employing a variant of pAW that treats iSW as an accessible prediction method. Conjoining these two recommendations it would be reasonable to adopt a variant of pAW that treats  both  iAW and iSW as accessible prediction methods.

This concludes our discussion of the theoretical properties of TTB, SW, and AW. Our discussion is informative regarding the worst-case short run performance of the various methods, but not regarding their short run performance in real-life environments. The studies presented in the following sections will serve as an empirical evaluation of the short run performance of the intermittent and persistent-based versions of TTB, SW, and AW, along with some variants of pAW, such as pAW({iAW, iSW}) and pAW*({iAW, iSW}).

In this section, we present the results of an empirical simulation-based evaluation of the meta-level prediction methods described in the preceding section. The meta-level methods were applied retrospectively to the results of one of the world’s longest-running prediction competitions, the Monash University Footy Tipping Competition (MUFTC) (with all data gathered at  http://www.csse.monash.edu.au/ footy/ ). The event sequence for our evaluation consisted of the binary-valued results of all 1514 matches of the Australian Football League over 8 seasons from 2005 to 2012. The possible results of each match were: first team won (coded as 1), and first team did not win (coded as 0). The object-level methods (or cues) for our study were the 1071 human predictors who participated in the MUFTC from 2005 to 2012. For each element of the sequence of 1514 games, the human predictors, along with the meta-level methods, had the opportunity to assign a probability to the statement that the first of two competing teams was the winner. Predictions were scored according to the natural loss function (i.e., the linear distance between the actual and predicted values).

The data presented in this section derive from a reimplementation and extension of the simulations presented in  Schurz and Thorn (2016) . The data in  Figs. 4–6  review results from ( Schurz & Thorn, 2016 ), with new data added concerning the performance of pAW*({iAW, iSW}). In the present section, we evaluated the performance of TTB, SW, and AW in the context of sports forecasting, where human predictors were treated as the accessible object-level methods (or cues). As expected, the intermittent-based variants of the three methods outperformed the persistent-based variants. We also considered the performance of two variants of pAW, pAW({iAW, iSW}) and pAW*({iAW, iSW}), that treat both iSW and iAW as accessible prediction methods. Both variants performed well, quickly matching the performance of the best of iSW and iAW, when appropriate.

To begin with, the performance of pAW and pTTB was similar, as was the performance of iAW and iTTB (cf.  Thorn & Schurz, 2016 ). The fact that pAW performed relatively poorly did not come as a surprise. So far, we have not said anything concerning the frugality of the prediction methods we considered, nor anything concerning their computational complexity. In a number of past studies, including the study of  Czerlinski et al. (1999) , the frugality of a method is identified with the number of cues the method must access (on average) in order to make a prediction. Data concerning the number of cues accessed within our study is presented in  Table 4 . For the variants of pAW that access other methods, we counted all the cues accessed by the other methods as accessed by the variant of pAW.

The average number of accessible cues was 7.5, and  Table 4  shows that MLR, pSW, iSW, and Dawes’ normally access all the accessible cues. At the other extreme, pTTB always accesses exactly 1 cue, save in round 1. The number of cues accessed by a method makes sense as a measure of frugality within the sorts of tasks considered in the study of  Czerlinski et al. (1999) . In that study, methods do not update their estimates of cue performance. Rather, methods are either (1) given access to the full data set as a basis for learning the true cue validities and the best fit regression coefficients, or (2) given access to half of the data set as a basis for estimating cue validities and regression coefficients. Within our study, methods estimate cue performance in an online manner, with the possibility that cue performance estimates are updated following each prediction. So, in our study, the number of cues accessed by a method makes less sense as a measure of frugality, since methods will be monitoring all of the predictions of each and every cue.

In making online predictions, all but one of the methods studied here needs to keep up-to-date records of either the success rate or the validity of each of the cues to which they have access. The exception is MLR, whose application depends on recording the cue and criterion values for each observed object, in order to successfully compute an up-to-date regression model. The memory demands for MLR are, thus, significantly greater than those of the other methods. There are also differences in the amount of  computation  required to implement the various meta-methods. Once again, MLR demands the most computation, involving complex matrix operations that could not be realistically implemented by a person in a reasonable amount of time. Setting MLR to the side, it is clear that computing the predictions of a weighting method such as iSW is more demanding than computing the predictions of iTTB. The computational demands of iTTB are also less than those of iAW. However, unlike iSW, iAW assigns no weight to cues whose validity is less than iAW’s, with the consequence that iAW often assigns zero weight to most of the accessible cues. As a result, the difference in the computational demands of iTTB and iAW was small, within our simulations, since iAW frequently based its prediction upon the value of a single cue.

If computational costs are high, one should probably opt for a method such as pAW*({iAW}), or even pAW*({iTTB}), whose performance and computational costs are similar.

A prediction method is access-optimal in a given environment just in case its long-run predictive success rate is at least asgreat as the success rate of the most successful method (or cue) to which it has access. A prediction method is  universally  access-optimal just in case it is access-optimal in  all possible  environments. Universal access-optimality is obviously a very desirable feature. But as we saw in Section  2 , universal access-optimality is rare: There are  no  one-reason prediction methods that are universally access-optimal ( Theorem 3 ), and none of a wide range of well known weighting methods is universally access-optimal, including success weighting ( Theorem 4 ), linear regression, logistic regression, and typical Bayesian methods ( Theorem 7 ). Furthermore, there are many prediction methods (including all one-reason prediction methods) that are subject to ‘catastrophic’ failure in some realistic environments, that is, subject to success rates that converge to zero, in the presence of highly successful accessible cues (as illustrated by the example corresponding to  Fig. 1 ).

As shown in previous work, there is a prediction method known as Attractivity Weighting (AW) that is universally access-optimal ( Cesa-Bianchi & Lugosi, 2006; Schurz, 2008; Schurz & Thorn, 2016 ). The basic variant of AW is the method pAW which only has access to object-level methods or cues and applies persistent success-evaluation. Although pAW is universally access-optimal in regard to object-level methods, there are prediction methods that are capable of outperforming pAW in many environments. In order to address this limitation of pAW, we introduced two refined variants of pAW, which differ from pAW in having access to other meta-level methods, and, in particular, to intermittent-based weighting methods. We showed that these refined variants of pAW are still universally-access optimal in regard to their long-run performance ( Theorems 8 and 9 ); however, the short-run performance of these methods is a theoretically open question. So, in Sections  3 and 4 , we evaluated the variants of pAW (along with a number of other methods) using simulation studies with real world data sets.

In Section  3 , the task was to predict the results of Australian Rules Football matches in a situation where human predictors were treated as accessible cues. We considered the problem of predicting probabilities of the results of matches, as well as the problem of making binary-valued (all or nothing) predictions. The latter format represents a case where success weighting is an excellent prediction method. We also considered prediction games with different subsets of the full set of human predictors as accessible cues. Some of these variations included only highly intermittent players (corresponding to cues with very low discrimination rates). Such variations are expected to be challenging for pAW, but not, we hoped, for the refined variants of pAW that were introduced in Section  2 . In fact, the refined pAW variants performed well in all of the simulations presented in Section  3 , quickly matching the performance of the best performing meta-level competitors, and exceeding the performance of the best performing human predictors (a feat that is impossible for pAW).

In Section  4 , we presented a simulation study using twenty real world data sets that were compiled by  Czerlinski et al. (1999) . In this study, we evaluated a wider range of meta-level methods (including multiple linear regression), and wider range of refined pAW variants. In general, we saw that these variants quickly matched the performance of the best accessible meta-level competitor (usually multiple linear regression).  Table 3 , which presents the regrets for various pAW variants, probably provides the best evidence for the efficiency of AW in matching the performance of the best accessible meta-level competitor. The regret values are very small, and much smaller than the worst case regret values established by  Theorems 8 and 9 .

Both the theoretical results of Section  2 , and the simulation results of Sections  3 and 4 , speak for the virtues of refined pAW variants. Of course, the proposal that one adopt a variant of pAW that accesses the best alternative meta-level methods leaves us with the problem of choosing which variant to apply in a given environment. Although this problem is certainly a non-trivial one, it is not as difficult as it might seem. Among the meta-level methods that are typically regarded as  plausible , there are significant limits in the variety of predictive behavior. While the predictive variety among the set of  all  prediction methods is great, the set of plausible methods can be divided into a relatively small number of categories (e.g., one-reason methods, weighting methods, and Bayesian methods, cf.  Martignon and Hoffrage (2002) ), where the methods within each category are relatively similar in their predictive behavior. Our recommendation, then, is the adoption of a variant of pAW that is given access to a relatively small selection of meta-level methods, where each of the chosen meta-level methods is representative of a category of methods. In the case of the environments considered in the preceding section, excellent performance was achieved by a pAW variant that included iAW, iSW, and MLR as accessible methods. Performance would be improved by a non-negligible degree, if the variant was given access to additional complex meta-level methods, such as logistic regression or Bayesian methods that are sensitive to dependencies in cue reliability.

                         Some follow up simulations to the ones reported here bear out this claim. In the follow up simulations, we tested some further methods on the data sets of Czerlinski, Gigerenzer, and Goldstein, including logistic regression using a very parsimonious form of stochastic gradient descent, and a Bayesian method that  Martignon and Hoffrage (1999)  call “profile memorization”. Both of the two mentioned methods outperformed MLR, on average, with profile memorization achieving a mean success rate of 0.834 at round 10,000 (with logistic regression coming in at 0.787). Surprised by the impressive performance of profile memorization, we re-ran all the methods on the twenty data sets, with event sequences formed by random sequences of object pairs chosen  without replacement , again with 1000 prediction games for each environment. The result was a precipitous decline in the performance of profile memorization, to an average final success rate of 0.650. Logistic regression was the top performer in the ‘without replacement’ simulations, with an average final success rate of 0.775, exceeding MLR, the next best performer, whose average final success rate was 0.756. Averaged over both batches of simulations (with and without replacement), logistic regression achieved an average final success rate of 0.781, outperforming both MLR and profile memorization. We also tested a wide range of pAW variants. The best performer was pAW*({MLR, Profile_Memorization, Logistic_Regression}), which achieved an impressive mean final success rate of 0.803 across the two batches of simulations (with and without replacement). 
                       That said, we are doubtful that the addition of further  plausible  meta-methods would result in further non-negligible improvements in performance.
