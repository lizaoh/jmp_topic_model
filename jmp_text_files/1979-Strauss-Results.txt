JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 20, 35-52 (1979)  Some Results on Random Utility Models DAVID STRAUSS  Department of Statistics, University of California, Riverside, California  This paper extends earlier work on the properties of Thurstone choice models to the  general random utility model (RUM), in which the components of the random utility  vector are not necessarily independent and identically distributed, even apart from  location shifts. The condition for two RUMS to be equivalent is established, and relation-  ships between the choice axiom, decomposition, and other conditions are explored. In  the special case of the Thurstone model, the conditions considered are all equivalent to  the double exponential distribution.  INTRODUCTION  Random utility models have played a prominent role in the theory of choice, perhaps  because in many situations they offer a natural interpretation of choice behavior. Most of  the theory has concerned random variables which are independent and identically  distributed (i.i.d.), apart from location shifts. 2 An exception is the recent paper by  Falmagne (1978), giving the necessary and sufficient conditions for a set of choice proba-  bilities to be consistent with a random utility model.  To motivate both the random utility model and the applicability of the general (non  i.i.d.) case, consider the well-known example of Debreu (1960). In one version of this there  are two similar choice objects, such as bicycles, and a third, dissimilar one, such as a pony.  If subjects were asked to give their utility for these alternatives, perhaps as the price they  would be willing to pay, we would presumably find:  (a) there would be a probability distribution of prices put on each alternative,  (b) subjects would choose the alternative on which they put the highest price,  (c) there would be a strong correlation between the price put on one bicycle and the  price on the other.  The random utility model, in which the variables corresponding to the bicycles are  correlated rather than independent, seems to account for these observations convincingly. This paper is concerned with the relationships between the joint distribution underlying  the RUM and certain theoretical or observable properties. Section 2 introduces the  relevant notation and definitions of the properties. Section 3 begins with a theorem on the  conditions for two random vectors to be equivalent as the basis of a RUM. This generalizes  a theorem of Yellott (1977) on the i.i.d. case. Conditions for the choice axiom to hold are  then given, together with some relationships between the axiom, decomposition, and some  probability distributions. Section 4 specializes to the i.i.d. case; here the properties  considered are all equivalent to the double exponential distribution. Several of the results  of this section are known (including, for example, the equivalence of the double expo-  nential distribution and the choice axiom).
