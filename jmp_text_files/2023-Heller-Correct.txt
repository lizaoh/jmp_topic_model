Knowledge structures provide a highly flexible set-theoretic framework for representing the organization of knowledge elements in a domain. Although knowledge structures were first introduced by  Doignon and Falmagne (1985)  within a purely deterministic approach, this perspective was broadened later on by developing a probabilistic framework ( Falmagne & Doignon, 1988a, 1988b ). The so-called  basic local independence model  (BLIM;  Doignon & Falmagne, 1999 ) forms the standard probabilistic model in knowledge structure theory (KST). The BLIM makes strong assumptions that help limiting the number of parameters and keep it tractable, even in large scale applications ( Falmagne et al., 2006 ). In particular, it assumes that the probability of a correct response to a problem is constant for all individuals that master the problem, and accordingly, for all individuals that do not master it, irrespective of the mastering of other problems. These assumptions have recently been put under scrutiny, and  Doble et al. (2019)  and  Cosyn et al. (2021)  present data that seem to provide evidence against them. Their results are reviewed after introducing the relevant KST notions. The subsequent sections then consider various scenarios of the response-based assessment of the mastering of the problems, and in each case derive the problem correct rate predicted by the BLIM as the data generating model. This analysis reveals that deviations from constancy in the observed direction are to be expected under the BLIM. It shows that at least some of the apparent evidence against the BLIM is due to the inaccuracy inherent in any response-based assessment. Finally, the paper outlines a route to validating the BLIM assumptions within the framework of a generalized local independence model.

In practical applications we may not assume that a person solves a presented problem if and only if the person masters this problem (i.e. it is an element of the person’s knowledge state). There are two kinds of response errors that can occur. In case of a careless error the person actually masters a problem, but does not solve it, whereas solving a problem without actually mastering it is called a lucky guess. Handling these types of errors calls for a probabilistic framework. Within such an approach we can also take into account that knowledge states will not occur with equal probability.

There are several known issues with the BLIM. Over the last decade, a series of publications was devoted to investigate its identifiability (e.g.,  Heller, 2017; Spoto et al., 2012; Stefanutti et al., 2012 ). This work describes structural properties of a knowledge structure that result in non-identifiability of the corresponding BLIM (i.e., different sets of parameters predict the same distribution on the response patterns), and characterizes the kinds of parameter trade-offs that occur. For an overview see  Doignon et al. (2018) . Moreover,  de Chiusole et al. (2013)  suggest a statistical test of the assumption that the careless error and lucky guess probabilities do not depend on the respective knowledge state, and report data that led them to conclude that it is violated. A detailed discussion is deferred to Section  5  to allow for highlighting communalities and differences of this approach and the subsequent developments addressing this assumption.

As already mentioned above, no rationale is provided for the assumption that the correct rate of problem  
                         q

Intuitively, however, a careless error on an in-state problem may be more likely to occur if an individual does not know much beyond what is needed to solve the critical problem, compared to a situation where the knowledge is settled and the individual masters all the problems in a domain. At first sight, for lucky guesses on out-of-state problems this line of argument is not so evident if guessing probability is thought to be tied to the problem format, as with single choice, or multiple choice problems. But even in this case mastering other problems may increase the probability of guessing the correct alternative by being able to exclude some of the distractors. Within an open response format, it is absolutely plausible that guessing a problem is more likely if someone is ready to learn it (i.e., masters all prerequisites of the problem), compared to a naive person.

Doble et al. (2019)  report data that they interpret as providing empirical evidence against the BLIM’s assumptions on the error probabilities. For this they ran an adaptive assessment to uncover the state of an individual, which was called the  assessed state . Starting from an initial likelihood of the knowledge states, after each presentation of a problem the likelihood of the states in line with the response (i.e., those containing the problem if correct, and those not containing it if incorrect) is increased, while the likelihood of the remaining states is decreased. Each assessment consisted of up to 29 of the 314 problems of the considered domain (stopping before reaching this number whenever all the remaining problems have either a very high or a very low probability of being answered correctly) as well as a randomly drawn extra problem. Limiting the presentation to 30 problems should guarantee that enough information is gathered about the underlying knowledge state without overwhelming the student with too many questions. While the response on the extra problem did not affect the assessment, this data point was used to estimate the rate of a correct response. To this end, the data points were aggregated according to the layer of the extra problem relative to the assessed state.  Fig. 2  shows a prototypical schematic illustration summarizing the findings of  Doble et al. (2019) . They concluded that correct rates depend on the layers in a systematic way, which may be described by a kind of sigmoid-shaped curve. The results of a similar analysis in  Cosyn et al. (2021, Figures 3 and 5)  are seen to corroborate this interpretation. However,  Cosyn et al. (2021)  also express the suspicion that some of the evidence against the BLIM may be due to inaccuracies in assessing the state, but they do not elaborate on this conjecture. The goal of the present paper is to cast light on this issue.

The following traces the effects that emerge from estimating the problem correct rate when an individual’s true state is unknown and need to be assessed based on observed responses. It considers two different scenarios. While in the  complete data scenario  inferences are based on responses to all of the problems in the given domain, in the  extra problem scenario  only responses to a proper subset of problems are collected. In both scenarios two cases are distinguished.  Full information assessment  takes into account all possible assessments as captured by the full posterior distribution of the states given the responses. According to this view, an individual is not located in a single state, but is conceived as being in each of the states with a certain probability. This, of course, is a theoretical, kind of quantum-like perspective, which avoids to impose additional constraints by confining consideration to a particular method that assigns a single state. Such a method is  modal state assessment , which assigns the state with maximum posterior probability given the responses, and was employed by  Doble et al. (2019)  and  Cosyn et al. (2021) .

The present study is motivated by recently published data on the extra problem correct rate plotted as a function of the layer of the problem relative to the assessed state ( Cosyn et al., 2021; Doble et al., 2019 ). These data (illustrated schematically in  Fig. 2 ) seem to contradict the constancy of the correct rate for all in-state problems and out-of-state problems, respectively, as assumed in the basic local independence model (BLIM). The previous sections consider various scenarios of the assessment of the knowledge state underlying an observed response, and in each case derive the problem correct rate predicted by the BLIM as the data generating model. The results show that deviations from constancy in the observed direction (illustrated in  Figs. 3 and 5 ) are to be expected, and thus do not per se speak against the BLIM. The derivations reveal that the major cause for the observed data pattern is the inaccuracy inherent in any response-based assessment, and thereby confirms a supposition of  Cosyn et al. (2021) . In principle, the same pattern arises even in the straight case within the extra problem scenario, where there are no response errors at all, and we have a deterministic dependence of the responses on the knowledge states. Substantial response error rates add to this effect since they increase the uncertainty about the assessed state.

At first sight one may think that the “true” problem correct rates can be estimated by a kind of reverse engineering from the empirically observed rates via the above theoretical derivations. But this only allows for recovering the correct rates as implemented in the model on which the assessment is based (i.e., the BLIM estimated from the data), and not the correct rates of the data generating process which in general is unknown.

Notice that the graphical illustrations of the correct rate predicted by the BLIM in  Figs. 3 and 5  were obtained by plugging the true parameters into the respective equations (i.e.,  (11)  and its subsequently derived variants). In applications, like those of  Doble et al. (2019)  and  Cosyn et al. (2021) , with only the underlying learning space given, however, parameters need to be estimated. As it was shown that any BLIM defined on a learning space is not identifiable ( Heller, 2017; Spoto et al., 2013 ), estimation may result in quite different sets of parameter values (e.g., depending on initial values of the EM algorithm in maximum likelihood estimation). This non-identifiability causes serious problems to any response-based assessment. Since state probabilities are not determined uniquely, the assessed state may not be determined uniquely, too (for an example see  Heller, 2017 ). For specific parameter estimates, this may lead to an increased proportion of misclassifications, and thus may even amplify deviations of the empirically determined correct rate from the BLIM assumption.

The results of the present paper opt against a direct modeling of the extra problem correct rate by generalized sigmoid curves ( Lei & Zhang, 2004; Richards, 1959 ) as suggested by  Cosyn et al. (2021) , since the observed sigmoid shape seems to be largely due to artifacts emerging from inaccuracy in the assessment. For an empirical test of the assumptions on careless error and lucky guess probabilities as captured by  (3)  the BLIM is to be generalized.
