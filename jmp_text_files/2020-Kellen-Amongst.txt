Throughout his long career, Bill Batchelder (1940–2018) championed the use of ‘cognitive measurement’ models that bridge the gap between off-the-shelf data-analytic procedures and fine-grained computational accounts. The idea is that such models can be used by both researchers and practitioners to characterize the data in terms of the main cognitive processes at play and to test substantive hypotheses. Many successful applications of this approach to modeling can be found within the class of  Multinomial Processing Tree  (MPT) models, originally proposed by  Riefer and Batchelder (1988)  and further developed in a series of followup papers (e.g.,  Hu & Batchelder, 1994; Purdy & Batchelder, 2009 ). MPT models assume that individuals’ categorical responses are produced by a  mixture of processes  that are assumed to occur in an  all-one-none fashion . These processes are typically organized in a serial fashion that can be conveniently represented in a tree-like structure. Since their introduction over three decades ago, MPT models have been developed across several domains such as memory, perception, reasoning, and decision making (for reviews, see  Batchelder & Riefer, 1999; Erdfelder et al., 2009; Hütter & Klauer, 2016 ).

As an example, let us consider a MPT model developed for the  simplified conjoint recognition paradigm  ( Stahl & Klauer, 2008 ; see also  Brainerd, Reyna, & Mojardin, 1999 ). In this paradigm, participants first study a list of words and subsequently engage in a recognition-memory task comprised of (1) studied targets, (2) semantically related distractors, and (3) unrelated distractors. In each recognition trial, the participant is asked to judge a single test item using one of three response alternatives: “ target ”, “ related distractor ”, and “ unrelated distractor ”. Fig. 1  illustrates the  simplified conjoint-recognition (SCR) model , a simplified instantiation of the  fuzzy-trace theory  proposed by Brainerd, Reyna, and colleagues (for an overview, see  Brainerd & Reyna, 2002 ). The success of measurement modeling and the MPT class in particular is sustained by the regular application of sophisticated methods coming from different statistical frameworks (e.g.,  Chechile, 1998; Hu & Batchelder, 1994; Klauer, 2010; Klauer & Kellen, 2011, 2015; Matzke, Dolan, Batchelder, & Wagenmakers, 2015; Smith & Batchelder, 2010; Vandekerckhove, Matzke, & Wagenmakers, 2015; Wickelmaier & Zeileis, 2018; Wu, Myung, & Batchelder, 2010a, 2010b ). One of the goals of such applications is to address a fundamental question:  which model is the best?  In some cases, the candidate models correspond to completely different theoretical accounts. In others, they state specific hypotheses regarding the processes within a given theory (e.g., which processes systematically differ across experimental conditions). The general problem of model selection is not a trivial one, given that researchers can adopt many different goals and the selection criteria adopted by researchers in their scientific practice reflect these differences (for discussions, see  Kellen, 2019; Navarro, 2019 ). Many different model-selection methods have been developed with the purpose of finding the model that strikes the best balance between these two tensions, goodness of fit and parsimony (for overviews, see the three special issues in this journal;  Mulder & Wagenmakers, 2016; Myung, Forster, & Browne, 2000; Wagenmakers & Waldorp, 2006 ; see also  Shiffrin, Lee, Kim, & Wagenmakers, 2008 ). One of these methods is  Normalized Maximum Likelihood  (NML), which stems from the Minimum Description Length framework (MDL;  Grünwald, 2007; Myung, Navarro, & Pitt, 2006; Rissanen, 1996; Wu et al., 2010a, 2010b ).

The goal of the present manuscript is to advocate for the use of NML in the context of models of multinomial data such as MPTs. Using MPT models for the simplified conjoint-recognition task as examples, we will discuss some of the most popular model-selection methods in the literature and how NML has characteristics that make it an attractive option in researchers’ toolboxes. We will also show how NML penalties can be conveniently approximated for any measurement model of multinomial data using an adapted Gibbs sampler, which we implemented in the statistical programming language  R 
 ( R Core Team, 2019 ).

The preference for parsimonious models leads to model-selection criteria that introduce some form of penalty (implicit or explicit) that attempts to capture the complexity of the model ( Klauer, 2015 ). The sections below discuss some of the approaches often used by researchers to quantify flexibility. Using the SCR model as an example, we will discuss different indices in an ordering that reflects how successive indices address issues identified for the preceding ones.

Under a Bayesian framework, the parameter priors are an integral part of a model as much as the likelihood function and thus, they play an equally important role. This is especially true in the case of Bayes Factors, given that the marginal likelihoods correspond to prior-weighted average likelihoods ( Lee & Vanpaemel, 2018; Liu & Aitkin, 2008 ). The sensitivity of Bayes Factors to the priors is especially relevant in the context of MPT modeling given the way modelers traditionally handle different tree structures. Specifically, we will show that the  customary use  of flat, so-called ‘non-informative’ priors over the [0,1] interval when working with MPT models under a Bayesian framework (e.g.,  Klauer, 2010 ;  Lee & Wagenmakers, 2014 ;  Smith & Batchelder, 2010 ;  Vandekerckhove et al., 2015 ) can be quite problematic.

On one hand, a variety of priors (e.g., Haldane’s prior or Jeffrey’s prior) have been referred to as non-informative (e.g.,  Bernardo, 1979; Jeffreys, 1946 ). For instance, one could establish priors that are uniform across the parameter space, or instead, across the permissible range of predictions (for relevant discussions, see  Heck & Wagenmakers, 2016; Segert & Davis-Stober, 2019 ). On the other hand, one can make the case that ‘non-informative prior’ is a misnomer in the sense all priors contain information and often end up representing beliefs that are not explicitly endorsed by the modeler ( DeGroot, 1982 ).

The seasoned Bayesian will in all likelihood brush off these results by pointing out that they are exactly what one should expect when computing Bayes Factors while using so-called ‘non-informative’ priors and ignoring models’ prior predictive distributions (for a related discussion, see  Lee & Vanpaemel, 2018 ). For instance, one solution when confronted with alternative tree structures yielding the same likelihood function is to use priors that are invariant across them (Jeffrey’s prior) or to translate priors from one tree structure to the other ( Heck & Wagenmakers, 2016; Segert & Davis-Stober, 2019 ).  We completely agree.  To be clear, we are not criticizing Bayes Factors per se but how they can lead to undesirable scenarios when researchers make use of them without taking into account their very nature. When adopting any selection criteria, researchers need to ensure that they are in line with their modeling goals and their systems of practice. Also, one should not confuse scientific goals with statistical tools, nor forget that the former take precedence over the latter. In the case of Bayes Factors, its sensitivity to priors and process ordering in the trees can be at odds with researchers’ understanding of model flexibility through notions of testability and falsification that pretty much focus on the relative size of a model’s prediction space relative to the space of possible outcomes (which data are permitted and which are not; e.g.,  Dunn, 2000; Dunn & Anderson, 2018; Roberts & Pashler, 2000 ) rather than on the relative likelihood that one attaches to each possible outcome.

For instance, accuracy in our example experiment was quite high, which turned out to be in line with the SCR’s predictive distribution. In the previous example, we focused on different parameterizations of a saturated model of recognition memory. In our next application, we will compare three MPT models designed for process-dissociation designs  Buchner, Erdfelder, and Vaterrodt-Plünnecke (1995)  and  Jacoby (1991) .In this particular case, we will focus on a process-dissociation study concerning the role of automatic and controlled processes in the identification of objects as weapons ( Bishara & Payne, 2009 ). Process-dissociation designs are used to disentangle the contribution of (presumably-independent) processes assumed to underlie a individuals’ responses. This is achieved by contrasting  congruent  trials in which the different processes are assumed to support the same response, with  incongruent  trials in which they are expected to support opposite responses (for discussions, see  Buchner & Erdfelder, 1996; Graf & Komatsu, 1994; Klauer, Dittrich, Scholtes, & Voss, 2015 ; see also Footnote 8). Consider the study by  Payne et al. (2002) : At each trial, participants were shown a target item, which could be a tool or a weapon. Prior to the target presentation, participants were primed with a face that was either white or black. Payne et al. also varied the response deadline across trials (200 ms, 450 ms, and 700 ms), which is expected to selectively influence the controlled processes (see  Payne, 2001; Yonelinas & Jacoby, 1994 ).

The data reported by  Payne et al. (2002)  was used by  Bishara and Payne (2009)  to compare three different MPT models that instantiate alternative views about the relationship between automatic and controlled processes.

The goal of the present work is to make a case for the use of NML when selecting among models of multinomial data, which includes the MPT model class originally proposed by  Riefer and Batchelder (1988) . By quantifying model flexibility in terms of the general ability of a model to fit any possible data under a given experimental design, NML overcomes the limitations of parameter-counting approaches such as AIC and BIC, without requiring researchers to make the kind of prior commitments that Bayes Factors require. Moreover, the derivation of NML explicitly emphasizes models’ ability to provide a good compression of the data, irrespective of their truth status. These characteristics make NML a quite attractive option: It relies on a quite intuitive notion of model flexibility, and its clear lack of ontological commitments highlights the fact that none of our models is more than a (hopefully useful) approximation. The examples we covered here demonstrate how NML changes the way that one usually thinks about models and their relation with data. In the case of  Stahl and Klauer ’s ( 2008 ) data, it showed that even the most flexible SCR model which assumes separate parameters in each experimental condition is quite constrained. It also captured the fact that an apparently distinct LT model is in fact nothing more than a reparametrization. Moreover, the reanalysis of  Payne et al. ’s ( 2002 ) data showed how NML can overturn previous model-selection results that hinged on penalties based on parameter counting. NML comparisons in all of these examples were implemented using the  R  package  NMLmulti , which we make available here.

Although NML is a well-established index, it is still the target of many research efforts, with different refinements and extensions being proposed (for a recent survey, see  Grünwald & Roos, 2019 ). Among recent developments, one of the most promising ones concerns the introduction of a  data prior  (e.g.,  Zhang, 2012 ), which among other things can be used to constrain data to a region of permissible values (e.g.,  Dunn & Anderson, 2018 ). For example, one could constrain data to be at or above chance, or to conform to a certain number of axioms (e.g., transitivity;  Cavagnaro & Davis-Stober, 2014 ). One of the advantages of introducing such constraints is that it would enable the application of NML to model comparisons involving unbounded random variables (see  Zhang, 2012 ). It should be noted that the MPT model family has been recently extended in order to accommodate other types of data such as mouse-tracking data and reaction times ( Heck, Erdfelder & Kieslich, 2018; Klauer & Kellen, 2018 ).

Finally, despite the attractive features of NML and the MDL framework in general, it is important to keep in mind that it is only one of many solutions currently available in researchers’ toolboxes. Which model-selection index one should adopt ultimately depends on a set of general evaluation criteria, which can vary according to the researchers’ goals ( Kellen, 2019; Navarro, 2019 ). Also, there is the matter of feasibility: For instance, unlike Bayes Factors, NML cannot be realistically applied in the context of hierarchical models. No matter how sophisticated and/or virtuous any method might be, it will never excuse the researcher of reasoning about their target research problem and the numerous constraints they face. Not surprisingly, we find this closing thought echoing earlier words by Batchelder and colleagues:  
                         model selection lies at the core of the scientific inference process. Accordingly, a theoretically well-justified and widely applicable methodology can help advance science. We believe that MDL represents such a methodology and that it provides versatile yet powerful tools for assessing the validity of MPT models [...] Statistical model-selection techniques alone, however sophisticated, are not a panacea for all inference problems. Other, nonstatistical means of model evaluation, such as plausibility, interpretability, and explanatory adequacy, are equally—if not more— important. Instead of automatic tools implemented in software, statistical model-selection methods can be most useful if they are combined with the judicious use of sound subjective but scientific judgment ( Wu et al., 2010a  p. 284).
