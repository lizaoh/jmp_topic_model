Under normality assumptions, a strict t test of a point hypothesis about the coefficient  a parameter is derived. The test is then converted to yield confidence intervals for a. These techniques remain applicable  even when the initial distributional assumption is considerably relaxed. The methods developed here are complementary to  certain large sample techniques of the same intent. Worked examples are appended by way of illustration.  1. INTRODUCTION  Previous results on sampling properties of reliability coefficients are relatively scarce. Most existing reliability formulas  express originally only relationships between parameters. They become estimation formulas, possibly for a lower bound, by  substitution of sample statistics for parameters. However, the statistical properties of the resulting estimators remain  largely unknown. Formulas by Guttman (1945) and Rulon (1939) and Cronbach's (1951) coefficient a are familiar examples of this  kind.  Reliability formulas derived from a stochastic model were first given by Kristof (1963). It was shown that a, expressed in  terms of second moment sample statistics, is the maximum-likelihood estimator of the reliability parameter when a test has  been divided into essentially parallel parts with multinormal distribution. Two measures may be called "essentially parallel"  when the equality of means requirement is dropped from the usual definition of parallelism. The small sample distribution of a  in terms of an F-distributed quantity was also given under the same assumptions. Some of the results were rederived by Feldt  (1965). Abandoning the assumption of essential parallelism of the parts of a total test but retaining joint normality of the  1 This work was supported in part by Contract N00014-69-C-0017 between the Office of Naval Research and Educational Testing  Service. The validity of these techniques is claimed only for large sample sizes, however.  A sampling experiment approach enabled Payne and Anderson (1968) to estimate significance levels for an observed value of the  familiar Kuder-Richardson function KR20 under variation of the number of the examinees, the number of items in a test and true  score distribution.  A considerable body of research concerning the a parameter has accumulated. Numerous references are contained in Lord and  Novick (1968, p. 90). An important result was obtained by Novick and Lewis (1967). It was shown that the a parameter coincides  with the reliability parameter p of a total test if and only if a is based upon essentially -r-equivalent parts of the total  test.' Otherwise a will be smaller than p.  The view of a taken in this paper is that adopted by Lord and Novick (1968). It is fully compatible with Cronbach's (1951)  general definition of a whole class of a coefficients. Every partition of a given total test into parts defines a particular  a. For instance, on the item level the well-known formula KR20 results. However, Cronbach (p. 321) does not restrict himself  to this level. A Spearman-Brown formula obtains if a total test is split into only two parts with equal variance parameters.  Equation 1 (Sec. 11, below) is another member of the class of a coefficients. Here equality of the variance parameters is not  necessarily assumed. In each instance, however, the reliability parameter of the total test is an upper limit for a. This  limit is attained precisely when the parts are essentially -r-equivalent.  The reliability parameter of a total test is viewed as a constant for a given population of examinees, coefficient a is not.  However, statistical inferences concerning the reliability parameter may be based on statistical inferences concerning the a  parameter when the latter is appropriately chosen by an appropriate split.  The present study is intended as complementary to Kristof's (1969) paper. Under the same assumption of binormal score  distribution on two parts of a test an alternate procedure of testing a point hypothesis concerning the a parameter and a  technique for establishing confidence intervals for a will be derived. The new methods will be strictly valid for any sample  size 1\T > 2. Their validity will be only immaterially affected even when the initial binormality assumption is considerably  relaxed as long as a certain continuity requirement is met and sample size does not become very small. However, general  superiority will not be claimed since the power functions of the tests have not been worked out.  z For the definition of various types of equivalent measurements see Lord and Novick (1968) and Novick and Lewis (1967).
