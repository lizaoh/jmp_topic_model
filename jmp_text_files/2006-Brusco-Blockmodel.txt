A common representation of cluster analysis occurs when  n  objects are measured on  m  items, and the distance between each pair of the  n  objects is computed based on  all  of the  m  item measurements. This process results in an  n × n  symmetric distance matrix that is one-mode because both the rows and the columns correspond to the objects. A cluster analysis of the distance matrix can be performed via a host of different procedures, often producing cluster structures with considerable within-group homogeneity and between-group heterogeneity.

But what about situations where only a subset of items defines structure for each cluster? Traditional one-mode clustering methods are no longer applicable because both objects and items are important for uncovering cluster structure. As observed by  Krolak-Schwerdt (2003) , these  two - mode  clustering problems occur in a variety of psychological domains, such as clinical classification, perception, and social cognition. Not surprisingly, there has been a significant body of research devoted to the problem of analyzing two-mode data ( Arabie & Hubert, 1990 ;  Arabie, Hubert, & Schleutermann, 1990 ;  De Soete, DeSarbo, Furnas, & Carroll, 1984 ;  Friedman & Meulman, 2004 ;  Hartigan, 1972 ;  Krolak-Schwerdt & Orlik, 1998 ;  Mirkin, Arabie, & Hubert, 1995 ;  Rosenberg, Van Mechelen, & De Boeck, 1996 ). Throughout the remainder of this paper, we focus on the special case of binary two-mode data.

There are many applications in the social sciences where data arise in the form of a rectangular binary matrix, where each element of the matrix assumes a value of one (zero) if there is (is not) some type of  bond  between the  object  corresponding to the row and the  item  corresponding to the column. The data in such circumstances are two-mode because the objects corresponding to the rows are different from the items corresponding to the columns. Examples include subjects being asked to pick any subset of a group of items ( Coombs, 1964 ;  Hubert, 1974 ), the attendance or nonattendance of individuals at events ( Davis, Gardner, & Gardner, 1941 ), and affirmative or negative votes cast by a panel of judges on various issues ( Doreian, Batagelj, & Ferligoj, 2004 ).

Two-mode binary proximity data are particularly relevant in social network applications ( Wasserman & Faust, 1994, Chapter 8 ).  Borgatti and Everett (1997)  proposed a network framework for the analysis of two-mode data, which facilitated the measurement of centrality for these data, as well as clustering of the rows and columns. More recently,  Doreian et al. (2004)  extended the blockmodeling paradigm for one-mode data ( Brieger, Boorman, & Arabie, 1975 ) to two-mode data. Doreian et al. devised an algorithm that simultaneously partitions the rows and columns of the binary matrix to create a block structure. The number of clusters for the rows need not be the same as the number of clusters for the columns. An optimization criterion was formulated based on block structure, requiring that the number of clusters for the rows and columns must be prespecified in advance. The algorithm uses a heuristic interchange procedure and is not guaranteed to produce an optimal solution. Doreian et al. recommend running the algorithm multiple times using different starting solutions to mitigate problems of local optimality.

The two-mode partitioning algorithm devised by  Doreian et al. (2004)  has sufficient flexibility to develop blockmodels that are concordant with one or more types of equivalence. The two most important types of equivalence for our current paper are structural equivalence ( Batagelj, Ferligoj, & Doreian, 1992b ;  Brieger et al., 1975 ;  Lorrain & White, 1971 ) and regular equivalence ( Batagelj, Doreian, & Ferligoj, 1992a ;  White & Reitz, 1983 ). Structural equivalence, which is based on the formation of ideal blocks that are either null (all zeros) or complete (all ones), is of particular relevance for our application. A common objective criterion for blockmodeling of two-mode binary matrices based on structural equivalence is to minimize the number of inconsistencies in the ideal blocks (i.e., the number of 1 s in a null block and the number of 0 s in a complete block). Like structural equivalence, blockmodels based on regular equivalence are also characterized by null blocks. However, rather than complete blocks, the important property of non-null (regular) blocks is the presence of at least one entry of 1 in each row and column of the block.  Batagelj et al. (1992a)  and  Batagelj (1997)  discuss optimization criteria for regular equivalence and its variations (e.g., row-regular equivalence, column-regular equivalence).

For any given two-mode binary matrix, the a priori establishment of the number of clusters for objects and items can be a difficult challenge, as can the identification of complete, null, and regular blocks in the data. In this paper, we propose that a good method for inducing a blockmodeling structure of two mode data is to employ seriation procedures rather than clustering methods. There are several motivations for the seriation approach, which can be used as a standalone procedure in its own right, or to assist with the subsequent implementation of a blockmodeling algorithm. First, the establishment of an ordering for the rows and columns of the binary matrix does not require an a priori specification of the number of clusters for rows and columns, but provides a reordering of the data that can facilitate a visual determination of these parameters. Second, there is a well-established theoretical framework for seriation of rectangular matrices based on incidence matrices and interval graphs, which can be exploited for selecting a plausible objective criterion for ordering the rows and columns ( Fulkerson & Gross, 1965 ;  Hubert, 1974 ;  Kendall, 1969 ). Third, recent advancements in branch-and-bound methods can facilitate guaranteed globally optimal solutions for seriation problems of practical size ( Brusco, 2002 ;  Brusco & Stahl, 2005, Chapter 9 ).

The seriation approach proposed herein does not explicitly specify partitions for the rows and columns of the binary matrix. Instead, it produces a reordered binary matrix that strings blocks of 1 s and 0 s together within both the rows and columns. Accordingly, the proposed seriation method is congruent with structural equivalence because the reordered matrix frequently exhibits null and complete blocks. To a lesser extent, the reordered matrix can also help to suggest placement of regular blocks, which are consistent with regular equivalence. Therefore, one potential benefit of our approach is that it provides a visually appealing reordering of the data, which facilitates a choice of the number of clusters for rows and columns of a blockmodel and the types of blocks that might be appropriate. In addition, the reordered binary matrix obtained from the seriation procedure offers a potential starting solution for heuristic procedures, such as the one designed by  Doreian et al. (2004) , which minimize the number of inconsistencies for a blockmodel. Finally, blockmodels based on structural and regular equivalence are invariant to permutations of rows (or columns) within a given block. In other words, the number of inconsistencies does not change when the rows (or columns) within a given block are permuted. The permutation of rows or columns within a given block does matter for the seriation procedure because, by definition, this method produces continuums for the rows and columns.

In the next section of this paper, we present a seriation based approach for facilitating the blockmodeling of two-mode proximity data. This includes a formal justification for the selected objective criterion, and an overview of the optimal solution procedure. We subsequently demonstrate the proposed procedure using three binary matrices from the empirical literature. The paper concludes with a brief summary.

The motivation for our proposed seriation procedure draws heavily from the work of  Hubert (1974) , as well as earlier developments related to analysis of incidence matrices ( Fulkerson & Gross, 1965 ;  Kendall, 1969 ). As observed by Hubert, a perfect scaling of the objects, with items as intervals, can be achieved if there is a reordering of the rows of  A  such that the 1 s in each column are consecutive. This  consecutive  1 
                            s property  can also be extended to finding a permutation of columns that induces consecutive 1 s in the rows as well. The identification of a row permutation,  ψ , and column permutation,  ω , that yields a reordered matrix  Φ = PAQ 
                         T  with consecutive 1 s in both rows and columns is consistent with Coombs’ (1964) framework for scaling both objects and items along the same continuum. The consecutive 1 s property is also a fundamental aspect of  Leenen and Van Mechelen's (2004)  recent generalization of Coombs’ parallelogram model.

For object-by-item matrices in the empirical literature (e.g., those considered in the social network literature), the  consecutive  1 
                            s property  is not achievable in most cases; however, it is often possible to obtain a nearly perfect structure. We advocate that a seriation of the rows and columns of  A  to achieve the consecutive 1 s property to the greatest extent possible should be conducive to achieving a blockmodel type structure in the reordered matrix. By stringing together consecutive 1 s in the rows and columns, we should also produce strings of 0 s as well, thus forming both complete and null blocks in the reordered matrix. An important by-product of this procedure is that we obtain a natural ordering of the rows and columns of the matrix. Whereas existing partitioning methods for blockmodeling assign the objects and items to clusters without concern for similarity within the clusters, our proposed method captures a flow of relationships for objects and items because of the consecutive 1 s property.

Our selected seriation index for producing the consecutive 1 s property is based on the work of  Kendall (1969)  and  Hubert, Arabie, and Meulman (2001, Chapter 4) . Kendall showed that if the permuted binary matrix  Φ  possesses the consecutive 1 s property in all columns (rows), then the permuted matrix  Δ = PRP 
                         T  ( Γ = QCQ 
                         T ) has a perfect Robinson form. A square symmetric matrix with a perfect Robinson form ( Robinson, 1951 ) has nonincreasing elements when moving left to right or bottom to top from any point above the main diagonal. Similarly, when matrix elements are always nondecreasing when moving left to right or bottom to top from any point above the main diagonal, the matrix is said to have perfect anti-Robinson structure.

Hubert et al. (2001)  presented a number of possible indices for inducing an anti-Robinson structure for symmetric dissimilarity matrices, and we have adapted one of these indices for finding permutations ψ and ω that induce a Robinson structure for  R  and  C , respectively. The optimization problems posed by (1) and (2) are designed to maximize an unweighted within row and column gradient index. The order of solution of these two problems is interchangeable. In other words, phase 2 could be implemented prior to phase 1 and the reordered matrix obtained in phase 3 would be the same. The unweighted within row and column gradient index represents a count of the number of consistencies of Robinson structure minus the number of inconsistencies. Optimal solutions for problems of practical size can be obtained using dynamic programming or branch-and-bound methods. A dynamic programming solution strategy is described by  Hubert et al. (2001, Chapter 4) , and can produce optimal solutions for values of  n  (or  m ) up to about 26 on microcomputer platforms with 1 GB of RAM. Although their computational efficiency is more sensitive to the characteristics of the input matrix, branch-and-bound methods can often provide optimal solutions for  n  (or  m ) >30. In this paper, we use an adaptation of a branch-and-bound method originally described by  Brusco (2002) , and later enhanced by  Brusco and Stahl (2005, Chapter 9) .

Although the branch-and-bound method is computationally feasible for many of the data sets analyzed in the blockmodeling literature ( Borgatti & Everett, 1997 ;  Davis et al., 1941 ;  Doreian et al., 2004 ), problem instances where  n  (or  m )>40 will almost certainly require heuristic procedures. For these larger datasets, we recommend the use of iterative quadratic assignment methods ( Hubert & Arabie, 1994 ;  Hubert & Schultz, 1976 ). A particularly effective strategy is to iteratively apply pairwise interchange, object block reversals, and object block relocations until none of these operations can produce further improvement in  f r 
                         ( ψ ) or  f c 
                         ( ω ). The solution resulting from this process is locally optimal with respect to these operations, but is not a guaranteed global optimum. Hubert and Arabie recommended restarting this iterative quadratic assignment heuristic from different initial starting sequences to guard against the realization of a poor local minimum.

Another important issue pertaining to the proposed seriation method is the uniqueness of optimal permutations for the rows and columns of the binary matrix. We have explored the possibility of alternative optima by applying the pairwise interchange heuristic to a large number of randomly generated initial permutations. For the matrices evaluated in this paper, the optimal row and column permutations appear to be unique, except for a few trivial instances where two rows or columns of the matrix were identical to one another. In such cases, the adjacent identical rows (columns) could be interchanged with one another in the permutation without any effect on the seriation index; however, this circumstance has no material effect on the blockmodel interpretation.

After implementation of the branch-and-bound algorithm in phases 1 and 2, a reordering of the two-mode matrix,  Φ = PAQ 
                         T , is the output produced in phase 3. This matrix does not yield a crisp partition of the objects (rows) and items (columns), but instead represents a fuzzier representation of the proximity data. Because  Φ  often contains large blocks of 0 s and 1 s, visual inspection of this matrix can provide a basis for selecting the number of clusters for rows and columns, as well as the types of equivalence that might be appropriate for different blocks. An analyst could obtain a blockmodel from simple visual inspection of  Φ  and possibly use this as a starting solution for a heuristic blockmodeling algorithm. A by-product of the seriation algorithm is that reordering the two-mode binary matrix can often help to identify more interesting information around the boundaries of blocks, which could reveal transitional information from one block to another.

Blockmodeling of two-mode proximity data is emerging as an important area of study, particularly in the social network literature. Our goal in this paper has been to demonstrate that seriation methods for rectangular binary matrices based on Robinson structure can provide a valuable tool for facilitating the development of blockmodeling strategies. The proposed procedure generates an  optimal  ordering of both the row objects and the column items, which provides a simple and often effective tool for uncovering structure. Specifically, the blocks are permuted in such a manner that they are visually recognized immediately. A second advantage is that the number of row and column clusters does not have to be prespecified in advance, avoiding a large amount of wasted computational effort in fitting several different models corresponding to differing values of row and column clusters.

Finally, and perhaps most importantly, the orderings (i.e., final permutations) of the rows and columns are usually substantively relevant, making the final permuted binary matrix richer with information. Under this formulation, interpretation can be given to adjacent blocks (both row and column) because the blocks exist on an underlying continuum that was obtained when the matrix was permuted. Furthermore, the continuum interpretation holds at the individual object and item (i.e., row and column) level, meaning placement in the block is now relevant and interpretable; whereas for traditional approaches the row and column blocks can be arbitrarily permuted while the observations within-blocks can also be arbitrarily permuted (within-blocks). At the very least, the proposed procedure (a) provides a permuted version of the original binary data matrix where the orderings of the row objects and column items typically have substantive consequences, (b) enables a visual determination of the block structure associated with the permuted matrix that facilitates selection of the number of clusters for rows and columns and the appropriate types of ideal blocks, and (c) produces a potential starting solution for a two-model blockmodeling heuristic algorithm, such as the one designed by  Doreian et al. (2004) .
