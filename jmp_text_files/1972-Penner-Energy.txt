JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 9, 286-293 (1972)  Neural or Energy Summation in a Poisson Counting Model  M. J. PENNER  University of California, San Diego  McGill (1967) proposed a neural counting model describing signal detection. In this model, detection was based on the number  of neural pulses generated by a Poisson device which was driven by the stimulus energy in the independent auditory channels.  The number of neural pulses generated by a narrow-band pure-tone stimulus was assumed to be related by a power law to the  stimulus energy (McGill and Goldberg, 1968). Broad band stimulus energy is mathematically constructed from the convolution of  many independent narrow-band channels. Thus the Poisson device might operate on the sum of the energy in all the channels (the  energy summation model), or the energy on each channel might undergo Poisson conversion to neural counts and the resultant  neural counts summed (the neural summation model). To distinguish between neural and energy summation, the predicted slopes of  masking functions, the logarithm of the just detectable increment as a function of the logarithm of the masker, for tone in  noise and noise in noise are calculated. It is found that the slope is correctly predicted if and only if it is neural pulses,  not energy, that are summed.  INTRODUCTION  McGill (1967) proposed an auditory detection theory based on the number of neural impulses reaching a counting center.  Essentially, his model supposes that the stimulus energy in the auditory channels is converted into neural impulses and that  these impulses are collected at a counting center. A comparison of the total number of counts arising from samples of noise  alone and samples of signal plus noise forms the basis for the detection decision. An essential component of the model is the  conversion of the stimulus energy in the auditory channels into neural counts. This conversion is known as transduction. We  completely specify the transducer by relating the energy E at the input to the number of neural counts n at the output. When  the mapping of the input to the output is assumed to be Poisson, we call the process Poisson transduction. Following McGill's  notation, the distribution of n is called the neural counting distribution.  McGill (1967) originally assumed that the mean of the Poisson transducer was directly proportional to the stimulus energy. A general problem in extending McGill's, or any channel model, to a situation with more than one channel carrying energy is  whether the energy in each channel undergoes Poisson transduction and the resulting neural counts ni are summed (the neural  summation model) or whether the energy xi is summed and then undergoes Poisson transduction (the energy summation model). An  equivalent way of phrasing the problem concerns the location of the internal noise. Without the Poisson transducers, McGill's  model is effectively an energy detection device. The introduction of the Poisson transducer adds an additional variance to the  detection situation. Therefore, the Poisson transducer can be thought of as a source of internal variability or, identically,  of internal noise. Internal noise might arise on each auditory channel (the neural summation model) or transmission might be  noiseless until after summation of energy (the energy summation model). Since the slopes of the masking  functions for tone in noise and noise in noise equal one (Hawkins and Stevens, 1949; Miller, 1947), we conclude that neural  impulses are summed.  Energy summation produces a slope of one if and only if p is identically zero. This is absurd because if p is zero, then the  mean of the Poisson transducer is a constant a, and no change in output could ever be detected.  Zwislocki (1969) shows that "the temporal decay of neural activity can make the inherently nonlinear auditory system act as if  it were an acoustic energy integrator." He concludes that apparent energy summation is a result of neural summation. Our  conclusion is analogous: It is neural pulses, rather than energy, that are summed.  In conclusion, we have examined the slopes of masking functions and found that McGill's (1967) model predicts slopes correctly  only if Poisson transduction of energy preceeds the integration of neural counts. Stated succinctly, the correct slopes of  masking functions are obtained if and only if it is neural counts rather than energy which are summed.
