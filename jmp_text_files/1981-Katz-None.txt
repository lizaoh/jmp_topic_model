JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 23, 195-213 (1981)  Difference Measurement Spaces  MICHAEL KATZ  Queen's University, Kingston, Ontario, Canada  An absolute-difference measurement space is a pair (X, e) where the real-valued function e on X' satisfies conditions which  are shown in the paper to be necessary and sufficient for its representability by the absolute distance on the real line. A  positive-difference measurement space is a pair (X, t), where the real-valued function t on XZ satisfies conditions necessary  and sufficient for its representability by positive distances on the real line. The conditions imposed on e and l make these  functions extensive measurements of proximity and dominance, the two basic predicates of social enquiry. Another way of  treating these conditions is to translate them to the formal language of multivalued logic. The translation is easy and the  sentences obtained have plausible intuitive meanings such as reflexivity, symmetry, and transitivity. The two sets of  conditions thus become formal theories of proximity and dominance. Our difference measurement spaces are relational structures  for the multi-valued logic and models of the two formal theories. Thus proximity and dominance are considered dichotomous in  principle and the multiple truth-values represent degrees of error. We suggest adopting multivalued logic as a framework  within which the problem of measurement error can be treated together with the formal axiomatization of social and  phychological theories.  1. MOTIVATIONAL REMARKS  As we stressed in Section 1, behavioral data quite often come in the form of binary functions like e and 1, and not in the  form of binary relations. Matrices of correlations, similarities or preferences are a few of the many examples of data in the  form of numerical binary functions. Thus, one possible motivation for looking at theories for e and 1 is the following.  We suggest that when a matrix of numerical assignments for pairs is given there is no need to rush to ordering the entries and  searching for qualitative conditions. No harm would come from first checking the fairly simple quantitative conditions given  above. If they are satisfied, or reasonably approximated, we may conclude that the cardinal properties of the numbers mean  something in this particular context. More precisely, we may conclude that the entries in the matrix represent an extensive  measurement of dissimilarity (to be compared with, e.g., the ordinal dissimilarities in Tversky and Krantz (1970)) if they  satisfy our conditions for e (Definition 2.1 or Lemma 2.2) and an extensive measurement of preference if they satisfy the  conditions for 1 (Definition 3.1 or Lemma 3.2). If these sets of conditions are not satisfied we can then concentrate on  ordinal properties and qualitative conditions.  206 MICHAEL KATZ  However, our main motive for studying functions such as e and 1 is imbedded in a new approach to (formal) measurement theories  which we want to outline in the rest of this section and in the following one.  We would like to offer a framework for measurement axioms which would enable us to incorporate into the axioms the problem of  measurement errors. In particular we want to face the error inherent in the well known fact that repeated observations yield  conflicting outcomes. This problem, while encountered also, as Giles (1974) noted, in the natural sciences, even when  attention is restricted to "elementary" (i.e., dichotomous) experiments, is perhaps more critical in the social sciences.  Consider, for instance, the notion of error implicit in the works of Luce (1956) and of Scott and Suppes (1958) on the binary  relation P of (definite) preference. It is precisely the problem mentioned above, of the dispersive nature of experimental outcomes, that makes it necessary to  speak about noticeable differences. Since subjects may find x preferred to y in one trial and be indifferent between x and y  (or even prefer y to x) in the next one we are in a situation where we have to say something like "x is definitely preferred  to y iff x is preferred to y in more than half of the cases." Similarly, differential threshold is usually defined to be that  E such that if the gap between two stimuli is less than E then they are indistinguishable in more than half of the cases.  But now note that the preceding discussion implies in fact that there is a continuum of degrees of indifference and  preference. So the crisp "half-cases" cutpoint can be replaced by a varying E, representing a variable degree of the  (measurement) error arising from dispersive experimental outcomes. In some cases,  DIFFERENCE MEASUREMENT SPACES 207  for instance, when we deal with the ability to distinguish between two visual stimuli (e.g., lights) on a screen, this  variability of e could well be along a continuum related directly to some kind of distance between the two stimuli.  If we wish to consider indifference and preference, and other psychological relations, as basically dichotomous, but at the  same time to be able to speak about degrees of error, which are perhaps related to "distances" between stimuli, we are led, in  a very natural way, to the multi-valued logic of error which we are now going to introduce. (This is not meant to imply that  the multi-valued approach is necessarily, or always, preferable to the statistical-probabilistic approach to variable error.)  Within our multi-valued logic the quantitative conditions of Sections 2 and 3 above are "qualitative," and our difference  measurement spaces are relational structures. In particular absolute-difference spaces are multi-valued structures of  indifference (to be compared with Roberts' two-valued indifference graphs) and positive-difference spaces are multi-valued  structures of preference (to be compared with Luce's twovalued semiorders).  5. So what in fact we suggest in this paper is that some kind of multi-valued logic should be adopted as a framework for formal  theories in the social sciences. This will  be similar to the situation arising, for instance, in Krantz and Tversky's (1971) conjoint measurement treatment of Hull's  (1952) theory of learning. If a structure satisfies the mathematical conditions given there then not only do we have the  required type of measurement but we also verified the theory itself in the model. Within the framework we are suggesting here  such situations would find an explicit formulation in the formal-logical level.
