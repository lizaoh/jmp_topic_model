Selective attention is an important part of learning. Many otherwise puzzling phenomena can be explained by supposing that organisms filter out unnecessary cues, paying attention only to those which experience has shown to be useful ( Sutherland & Mackintosh, 1971 ). Attention is thought to increase the amount learned about cues and how much they are used in decision making.

EXIT has been a particularly successful model of selective attention in cue-based associative learning ( Kruschke, 2001b ). It takes the assumptions of additive prediction and error-driven association learning of the influential Rescorla–Wagner model ( Rescorla & Wagner, 1972 ) and adds attention weights, which serve to magnify or diminish the salience of cues. First, the current stimulus (i.e., cue configuration) is compared to previously encountered stimuli (exemplars), and attention gain is allocated to each cue on the basis of past experience. Then the attention gains are normalized in a process representing cue competition to determine attention weights. These attention weights are multiplied with the cues of the stimulus vector, and then combined with association weights to produce a prediction of various outcomes (e.g. the correct response in a classification task). After receiving feedback, the model rapidly shifts attention in a way that reduces error. This new allocation of attention is used to update the model’s prediction and thence to drive learning of cue-outcome associations. Finally, some fraction of the rapid attention shift is retained as permanent learning.

Equipped with this attentional apparatus, EXIT can reproduce many phenomena observed in human learning. Perhaps the simplest of these is the finding that people attend more to cues that are correlated with outcomes than those not so correlated and that this enhances learning toward the former when new cue-outcome associations are introduced ( Le Pelley & McLaren, 2003 ). EXIT can also produce the inverse base rate effect, a phenomenon in which people choose rare instead of common outcomes when given ambiguous cue combinations ( Medin & Edelson, 1988 ), as well as a related effect called highlighting ( Kruschke, 2009 ). Another effect that EXIT can produce is the reduction in attention to a blocked (redundant) cue ( Le Pelley, Beesley, & Griffiths, 2011 ). Finally, EXIT’s exemplar-mediated attention mechanism allows it to attend to a cue in some stimulus configurations but not in others ( Aha & Goldstone, 1992; Uengoer, Lachnit, Lotz, Koenig, & Pearce, 2013 ).

Although EXIT can explain many established phenomena, it can be criticized as highly complex ( Winman, Wennerholm, & Juslin, 2003 ). EXIT’s many parameters make fitting practically difficult, as a high-dimensional parameter space must be explored. More importantly, the large number of parameters may make the model overly flexible. Finally, with so many mechanisms contributing to its behavior it is difficult to understand  how  EXIT explains experimental data. The rapid attention shifts entail that the attention weights that determine behavior on any given trial are not the same as those which determine learning. Moreover, the exemplar-mediated attention system makes it very difficult to speak of attention  to a cue ; that attention depends on where in the stimulus space one is (i.e., on the values of other cues). Kruschke himself sometimes abandons these mechanisms when analyzing EXIT’s performance, instead considering a simplified model ( Kruschke, 2001b, 2006 ).

We suspect that some of EXIT’s attentional mechanisms, while well-motivated from normative considerations, are not needed to explain experimental data. To test this suspicion, we first dissect EXIT into its component mechanisms, building a series of simplified models. We then test these models on important experimental phenomena, specifically the learned predictiveness effect ( Le Pelley & McLaren, 2003 ), the inverse base rate effect ( Kruschke, 1996 ), reduced attention to blocked cues ( Beesley & Le Pelley, 2011 ), differential cue use across stimulus space ( Aha & Goldstone, 1992 ), and the conditional learned predictiveness effect ( Uengoer et al., 2013 ). Our main conclusion is that EXIT requires only attentional competition or rapid attention shifts to produce highlighting effects, while the usefulness of exemplar mediation of attention depends on the stimulus representation.

We shall describe EXIT’s anatomy piece by piece, starting with fundamental mechanisms and then adding more complex ones. At each stage we specify a learning model that incorporates these features, culminating with the full version of EXIT. To start (Model 1), we explain non-attentional mechanisms: prediction, association learning and decision making. We then describe a non-competitive form of attention (Model 2) and how this can be elaborated by adding temporary rapid attention shifts (Model 3). Next we discuss how EXIT represents attentional competition between cues (Model 4) and how EXIT implements rapid attention shifts (Model 5). Finally, we describe how EXIT uses an exemplar similarity mechanism to allocate attention on the basis of current and past cue configurations. The logical relationship among the six models is shown in  Fig. 1 .

EXIT, along with all of the models which we consider here, is based on the delta rule, as used in the Rescorla–Wagner model ( Rescorla & Wagner, 1972 ) and many reinforcement learning algorithms ( Sutton & Barto, 2018 ). Learning is called  error-driven  because it is based on prediction error, the difference between what the learner predicts and what it then actually observes. The Rescorla–Wagner model also assumes that cues combine additively to determine the learner’s prediction. These basic mechanisms are the foundations upon which various forms of selective attention can later be added.

As mentioned above, Model 3 somewhat resembles EXIT’s precursor ADIT ( Kruschke, 1996 ). Both feature rapid attention shifts based on Eq.  (8) , but differ in several important respects (see the comparison of ADIT to Model 2 above). In particular, ADIT lacks permanent attention learning (Eq.  (10) ). ADIT can thus explain effects based on a single set of cues and outcomes (e.g., inverse base rate effect:  Kruschke, 1996; Medin & Edelson, 1988 ) but not those in which attention learning is transferred from one set of outcomes to another (e.g., learned predictiveness:  Le Pelley & McLaren, 2003; Lochmann & Wills, 2003 ). Kruschke later added permanent attention learning to EXIT, ADIT’s successor, along with several additional mechanisms as described below ( Kruschke, 2001b ).

One of Kruschke’s innovations in EXIT was to model attentional capacity limits via normalization of the attention vector (Kruschke, 2001b; see also Nosofsky, 1986). At least the relationship between attention and salience is monotonic, so we can analyze Model 4 in similar terms to Model 2, but with additional considerations of how a cue’s attention depends on other cues. Rapid attention shifts (Models 3 and 5) make analysis much more complicated: prediction and learning are governed by different attention weights, and attention updates can no longer be computed in a single step. Exemplar-mediated attention in EXIT makes it even harder to analyze which cues are attended and which are ignored, because this can vary totally between different stimulus configurations. These empirical findings provide a thorough test for EXIT’s attentional mechanisms. The learned predictiveness effect ( Le Pelley & McLaren, 2003; Lochmann & Wills, 2003 ) is such a fundamental result that any decent model of attention learning should be able to replicate it. It is known that EXIT ( Kruschke, 2001b ) can produce the inverse base rate effect ( Kruschke, 1996; Medin & Edelson, 1988 ) and reduced attention to blocked cues ( Griffiths & Le Pelley, 2009; Kruschke & Blair, 2000 ). Therefore, they form a good test of whether simplifying EXIT reduces the model’s explanatory power. In particular, Kruschke’s explanations of the inverse base rate effect emphasize rapid attention shifts ( Kruschke, 1996, 2001a, 2009 ), so Model 2 and Model 4 (which lack rapid shifts) may have trouble replicating it. Finally, differential cue use ( Aha & Goldstone, 1992 ) and the conditional learned predictiveness effect ( Uengoer et al., 2013 ) both seem to argue for exemplar-mediation of attention, and thus might be a stumbling block for any model other than EXIT itself. We shall see whether any of the simplified models can survive this gauntlet of simulation tests.

People pay more attention to cues that consistently predict reward, or the correct response, than those that do not (e.g.  Le Pelley & McLaren, 2003 ). We call this phenomenon the  learned predictiveness effect . To show a learned predictiveness effect, one needs to distinguish  attention  (which involves stimulus processing) from mere cue-response association. Researchers accomplish this by using a two-stage design, with separate sets of response options in each stage. The experiment of  Le Pelley and McLaren (2003)  is a typical example (see  Table 4 ).

In the first stage, the participant must choose between response options 1 and 2. Some cues (A, B, C and D) are correlated with certain responses, making them predictive (e.g., response 1 is always correct when A is present). These are paired with non-predictive cues (V, W, X and Y). In the second stage, the choice between responses 1 and 2 is replaced with one between responses 3 and 4, and all the cues are equally well correlated with these new responses. This design ensures that any difference in learning during the second stage between the formerly predictive and non-predictive cues must be attributed to stimulus processing (i.e. attention). In the test stage, participants show much stronger associations between the cues that were predictive in the first stage (A, B, C and D) and the stage two responses (3 and 4) than between the non-predictive cues (V, W, X and Y) and those responses. This result suggests that people learn to ignore cues that do not provide useful information and pay attention to those that do, and that this adaptation of attention affects subsequent learning. People also spend more time looking at the predictive than at the non-predictive cues throughout the experiment, which supports this attentional interpretation ( Le Pelley et al., 2011 ).

If the context and base rate cues are the same, this is evidence against Model 3 and  Le Pelley et al. ’s ( 2016 ) explanation of the COFED. However, people might learn outcome base rates in a manner independent of context cues, which would weaken this argument. As it stands, it appears that Model 3 provides an adequate explanation of the COFED, but the topic deserves further investigation.

Simulation 2-d revealed that models with attentional competition (Model 4, Model 5 and EXIT) could not produce an inverse base rate effect without shared cues ( Table 11 ). Surprisingly, Model 3 could produce either a base rate or inverse base rate effect for outcomes with no common cue depending on its parameters. Simulations 2-b and 2-c suggest that Model 3 more accurately represents human behavior on this task when it has a base rate cue, but even then the above result holds true. The fact that Model 3 can behave in a manner opposite to humans while Model 4, Model 5 and EXIT cannot is a mark in the latter’s favor, although we cannot totally dismiss Model 3 on this account.

Blocking is a phenomenon in which an organism learns less about a cue when it is accompanied by another cue that has already been associated with the outcome ( Kamin, 1968 ). In a simple version of the design, cue A is first paired with an unconditioned stimulus (US) such as food or shock. Cue B is then added to cue A and training continues. In Models 4, 5 and EXIT, competitive attention dynamics (Eqs.  (14) and (15) ) cause blocked cues to  lose  attention during stage 2. Kruschke added exemplar-mediated attention to EXIT in part due to a pair of category learning experiments by  Aha and Goldstone (1992)  which show that certain exemplar models require more flexible attention weights. Each stimulus consisted of a square that varied in size and a vertical line that varied in position within the square. Thus—in contrast to all of the experiments we have described so far—the two cues (size and position) varied quasi-continuously rather than being merely present or absent. The relevant dimension for classifying stimuli into categories A and B was size in one part of the stimulus space and position in another part ( Fig. 4 ). Responses to test stimuli indicated that participants learned this category structure; in particular, they generalized to novel stimuli according to different dimensions in different parts of the stimulus space.

This result poses a problem for configural (exemplar) learning models such as the GCM ( Nosofsky, 1986 ) and ALCOVE ( Kruschke, 1992 ). Such models usually represent attention by stretching and shrinking the stimulus space prior to computing stimulus similarity, using the same attention weights for all exemplars. Consider the critical test cues marked as W, X, Y and Z in  Fig. 4 . Each of these is equally close to a category A training exemplar and a category B training exemplar, and yet participants extrapolate the appropriate category boundary for each part of the stimulus space. If responses are governed by similarity to training exemplars, this result implies that attention to position is greater than attention to size for one cluster of stimuli, while the reverse is true for the other cluster.  Aha and Goldstone (1992)  explain this finding by giving each cue configuration its own set of attention weights, calling the resultant exemplar model GCM-ISW. EXIT implements this idea in a less extreme way (there is some sharing of attention across cue configurations) using exemplar-mediated attention. Despite  Aha and Goldstone ’s ( 1992 ) experiments being influential to the development of EXIT ( Kruschke, 2001b ), EXIT has not yet (to our knowledge) been used to simulate them.

The ability of EXIT and the other models to reproduce  Aha & Goldstone ’s ( 1992 ) results depends on the way that one represents stimuli. The most obvious way is to make each stimulus a pair of Cartesian (position, size) coordinates. In that case, we have found that exemplar mediation allows EXIT to learn the category structure by attending to either position or size in different parts of the stimulus space. This is remarkable, and shows that exemplar mediation can transform the stimulus space in a way that allows EXIT to learn certain non-linear category structures. Because it is clear that none of the other models can do this, we have decided not to report simulations using the Cartesian representation.

While EXIT can thus use a complex attentional mechanism to compensate for a simple stimulus representation, it is worth asking whether we should instead choose greater representational complexity as the price for simpler attention. For simplicity, we used a separate learned attention weight or salience value for each unidimensional and each configural unit. This superficially resembles the exemplar-specific attention weights in GCM-ISW (Aha and Goldstone’s model), but differs in several important respects; in particular, in our simulations configural units do not have any way to attend to one dimension more than another (i.e., to reshape their receptive fields). We could have made our attention mechanisms resemble those of GCM-ISW even less by using a single shared attention weight (or salience value) for all of the size receptors, another for all the position receptors and a third for all of the configural units. As we shall see, however, these considerations are not very important: under the representation used here, the task can be solved with no selective attention at all.

The stimulus representation described above renders selective attention of any kind unnecessary for reproducing  Aha and Goldstone ’s ( 1992 ) results. As seen in  Table 14 , Model 1 learns this category structure just as well as humans do, generalizing along different dimensions in different regions of stimulus space. Examining how this simple model learns the task, we find that the association weights of unidimensional cues are on average an order of magnitude larger than those of configural cues at the end of training. We view this as a psychologically compelling explanation: learning defaults to simple representations based on individual cue values, using configural information only when necessary. Simulation results were similar when we removed the configural cues, and also tolerated minor changes to the decay constant ( 
                            k 
                         ) or number of receptors. We must conclude that the results of  Aha and Goldstone (1992)  do not show any need for exemplar mediation of attention in elemental models (whereas they do for purely configural or exemplar models).

This suggests that the sort of purely configural representation examined by Aha and Goldstone may not reflect how humans solve such simple task structures. Instead, configural cues may only be needed when unidimensional ones fail (e.g., exclusive-or designs). This principle of trying unidimensional cues first has long been implemented in rule-based hypothesis testing models ( Nosofsky et al., 1994 ); as pointed out by  Gluck and Bower (1988) , the configural cue approach translates this into an associative framework.

Simulation 4 shows that the results of  Aha and Goldstone (1992)  can be explained without EXIT’s exemplar-mediated attention. However, we should not yet dismiss that mechanism: other studies provide good evidence that feature use differs across stimulus configurations ( George & Kruschke, 2012; Uengoer et al., 2013; Uengoer, Pearce, Lachnit, & Koenig, 2018 ). The basic design of these experiments can be described as a conditional learned predictiveness effect, in which so-called context cues indicate which other cues are relevant.

We shall describe and simulate Experiment 2 from  Uengoer et al. (2013)  because of its simplicity (see  Table 15 ). Each stimulus consists of three features: A or B, X or Y, and a context cue (denoted I or II). Stage 1 features all eight possible stimuli. In stimulus configurations that include I, A and B are correlated with the correct responses, while X and Y are irrelevant. The reverse is true in configurations with context cue II.

                       For stage 2, participants are randomly assigned to two groups. In each case, the stage 2 task uses new response options which are correlated with A and B but not with X or Y. For group 1, the context cue is I on all trials, while for group 2 it is II. Group 1 solves the task more quickly than does group 2. Related experiments ( George & Kruschke, 2012; Uengoer et al., 2013, 2018 ) use new stimulus features in stage 2 that belong to the same classes (i.e., are new values on the same dimensions) as those in stage 1; such dimensional attention is beyond the scope of this paper.

This result can be explained in terms of conditional attention. In stage 1, participants learned to pay attention to A and B when accompanied by context cue I but to attend to X and Y with context cue II. In stage 2, group 1 learned more quickly than group 2 because the context cue in the former’s case indicated that they should pay attention to the relevant features (A and B). EXIT can naturally accommodate such an effect via exemplar-mediated attention. Thus,  Uengoer et al. ’s ( 2013 ) results seem to vindicate this mechanism.

However, we can also explain this result without exemplar mediation of attention, using configural cues. As in Simulation 4, it is natural to include configural cues when applying featural models to a non-linear category structure such as this one (e.g.,  Gluck & Bower, 1988 ). Each configural cue would have its own attention weight that could change as a result of experience just like attention to elemental cues (cf.  George & Pearce, 1999; Griffiths & Le Pelley, 2009 ). At first glance, it may appear that the attention to configural cues arising in this simulation is merely exemplar-specific attention in another form. However, this is not the case: attention to cues is not the same as attention to stimulus dimensions within a (configural or exemplar) cue. The former weights the predictive contribution and learning rates of whole units, whereas the latter reshapes the receptive fields of those units. Selective attention among whole exemplars, via learning that (similarity to) some exemplars are more predictive than others, is a mechanism we have explored elsewhere ( Foster & Jones, 2017 ) and that we believe merits further study.

By dissecting EXIT into simpler models, we have discovered which of its mechanisms actually explain various human learning phenomena. Our results are summarized in  Table 17 . The learned predictiveness effect ( Le Pelley & McLaren, 2003; Lochmann & Wills, 2003 ) and reduced attention to blocked cues ( Beesley & Le Pelley, 2011 ) require only basic attention learning (present in all models except Model 1). The inverse base rate effect ( Kruschke, 1996; Medin & Edelson, 1988 ) requires rapid attention shifts (Models 3, 5 and EXIT) or attentional competition (Models 4, 5 and EXIT), but not both. Differential cue use across the stimulus space ( Aha & Goldstone, 1992 ) can be explained without selective attention (i.e., Model 1 is adequate) when we represent stimuli using receptor units (unidimensional exemplars) instead of Cartesian coordinates. Finally, one can explain the conditional learned predictiveness effect ( Uengoer et al., 2013 ) by applying simple attention mechanisms to configurations of stimulus features, without any need for EXIT’s exemplar mediation, attentional competition or rapid shifts (i.e., Model 2 is adequate). We shall use these results to trim EXIT down into a more compact model.

We can almost get by with Model 2, which has the simplest form of attention learning considered in the paper. Model 2 can produce the both the ordinary and conditional learned predictiveness effects, as well as inattention after blocking. However, it cannot handle the inverse base rate effect. As mentioned above, Model 2’s attention learning rule (Eq.  (7) ) is closely related to the concept of derived attention ( Le Pelley et al., 2016 ): attention to a cue increases with the magnitude of its associations. Therefore it is interesting that the derived attention model can explain the same set of results as Model 2.

We need something more complex than Model 2 to explain the inverse base rate effect. Which one should we prefer? Either rapid attention shifts (Model 3) or attentional competition (Model 4) will do the job: combining them (as in Model 5 and EXIT) appears to be redundant. Model 5 and EXIT do not make any firm prediction about attention to blocked cues (being able to behave in the same manner as humans or the opposite), which gives another reason that we should not combine rapid shifts with competition.

This leaves Model 3 and Model 4. We find attentional competition (Model 4) easier to interpret than rapid attention shifts (Model 3): Both models explain experimental results in terms of attention, and it is difficult to keep track of “attention to a cue” when that quantity rapidly fluctuates. As discussed above, Model 4 appears to provide a somewhat better account of the inverse base rate effect (e.g. with regard to eye-tracking:  Don et al., 2019; Kruschke et al., 2005 ), but Model 3 can still reproduce the main behavioral results. As it stands, both mechanisms are thus valid alternatives.

Whether or not we need exemplar mediation of attention depends on the stimulus representation. If we use traditional Cartesian representations, with just one input unit for each stimulus dimension, then the models analyzed here cannot produce differential cue use ( Aha & Goldstone, 1992 ) or the conditional learned predictiveness effect ( Uengoer et al., 2013 ) without exemplar mediation. However, a more sophisticated stimulus representation (adding receptors or configural cues, respectively) makes exemplar mediation of attention superfluous. We believe that this second option (sophisticated stimulus representation without exemplar mediation of attention) is far easier to interpret, and offers an elegant unification of the major representational schemes traditionally used in learning models (viz., elemental, configural, cue-based, and exemplar-based).

Another reason to reject exemplar mediation of attention is that it often does not have any detectable effect. Many experimental designs produce attention effects that transfer across cue configurations (e.g.,  Beesley & Le Pelley, 2011; Le Pelley & McLaren, 2003 ). Further,  Griffiths and Le Pelley (2009)  found similar levels of attentional transfer to old and new configurations in an inattention after blocking design. (It would be useful to perform similar experiments using a modified learned predictiveness effect design.) In contrast, models using the elaborated representation proposed here will naturally attend to configural cues when these are most predictive (as in stage 1 of a conditional learned predictiveness effect design) but attend to elemental cues when those are predictive (as in the other designs).

Our final conclusion is that the success of EXIT’s attention learning rule can be explained either by rapid attention shifts (Model 3) or attentional competition (Model 4). There is no need to combine these mechanisms, and doing so only leads to excess model flexibility (see Model 5 in Simulations 3 and 5). Effects involving non-linear category structures can be explained by either exemplar mediation or a sophisticated stimulus representation: we prefer the latter.

This leaves us with Models 3 and 4 as viable replacements for EXIT. We feel that these models deserve more descriptive names.  Simplified EXIT  is not appropriate for either model, given that the name  EXIT  refers to exemplar mediation ( Ex emplar-based attention to distinctive  i npu t ;  Kruschke, 2001b ). We can call Model 3  RSWAC  (Rapid Shifts Without Attentional Competition). We propose the name  CompAct  for Model 4, which follows Kruschke’s example in being a pun which refers to the model’s attributes ( Comp etitive  act ivation of attention units, and also “compact”, i.e. simplified). Future experimental work will let us decide between these two models.
