COHEN  Center for Adaptive Systems, Boston University  This paper provides necessary and sufficient conditions for the existence and uniqueness of a weak qualitative probability  representation of an arbitrary qualitative probability space. Should a weak probability representation exist, necessary and  sufficient conditions are obtained for each weak probability representation to also strongly represent the qualitative  probability space. These are equivalent to the Archimedean conditions of P. Suppes and M. Zanotti (1976, Journal of  Philosophical Logic, 5, 431-438). In addition, upper and lower bounds for the probability of a specific set consistent with a  given qualitative probability relation are obtained. These results are obtained by embedding the qualitative probability space  in an ordered vector space and studying the properties of positive linear functionals on this space. It is shown that if a  weak qualitative probability representation of an arbitrary space is unique, then the set of weak probability representations  of finite subalgebras weakly converge to the representation of the entire space. We briefly study uniqueness of finite  qualitative probability spaces. For this case, uniqueness is entirely determined by the equivalences of the system. ;C'1 1991  Academic Press, Inc.  1. INTRODUCTION  Part of the Bayesian development in statistics and decision theory during the past 30 years rests upon the idea of subjective  or personal or comparative probabilities. By this is meant the assignment of a number to each chance event so that these  assignments obey the usual (Kolmogorov) axioms of probability, but which need not in any sense arise from limits of relative  frequencies. Indeed, they may play a role in situations when repetitions of the events are unthinkable, such as the event of a  person being born on a particular day. In another part, attempts have been made to construct them from just qualitative orderings  on pairs of events, i.e., statements of the form: event A is at least as probable as event B, which is symbolized as A>, B and  > is referred to as a qualitative probability relation. By "construct them" one means that it is shown that there exists a  numerical probability measure P over the ensemble of all events such that for all events A and B, if either A > B or B > A is  defined, then  A>BpP(A)>P(B)  which is known as a strong probability representation, or such that  A>B=> P(A) >P(B),  which is known as a weak probability representation.  The hypothesis that A > B or B > A be defined in the definition of a strong probability representation is not the standard one  found throughout the literature (see, for example, Scott, 1964). This is to allow an arbitrary binary relation to have a  strong probability representation. The existence of a strong probability representation for a relation > without this  hypothesis implies that this relationship must be at least a weak order on the set of events. In particular, it must be  connected.  The major questions to be faced by such a theory of subjective probability are two. First, one wants conditions on > that  ensure the existence of such a representation. Ideally, one would like conditions on > that are both necessary and sufficient  for the existence of such a representation; however, certain systems of sufficient conditions are of interest provided they  are in some sense simple. The second problem is to ascertain further conditions which are necessary and sufficient to force  the representation to be unique.' More generally, one would like to characterize the entire set of subjective probabilities  representing a particular ordering >,.  Partial results exist on these problems. The case where the binary relation > is connected and usually is a weak order has  been most heavily studied. In this case for example, the simplest conditions which were easily seen to be necessary and hoped  to be sufficient for the existence of a representation were proposed by de Finetti (1937). That these conditions were not  sufficient was shown by Kraft, Pratt, and Seidenberg (1959), who also provided necessary and sufficient conditions for the  existence of a strong representation where > is connected and the ensemble of all events is finite. These conditions were  later rephrased and generalized to other cases by Scott (1964) and Tversky (1964).
