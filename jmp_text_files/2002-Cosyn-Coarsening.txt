Coarsening a Knowledge Structure 

Eric Cosyn 

ALEKS Corporation, Santa Ana, California 

This paper describes an assessment procedure which does not deal explicitly with the whole knowledge structure, but rather operates on a faithful summary of the structure. The summary is achieved through the notion of coarsening of a structure, where the knowledge states are grouped into equivalence classes. We first define a stochastic assessment procedure running on the coarsening. Then we specify a set of possible properties for the equivalence relation. In particular, we define a notion of convexity for a knowledge structure. The results are justified by a simulation study. © 2002 Elsevier Science (USA) 

1. INTRODUCTION 

Knowledge space theory (Doignon & Falmagne, 1985, or 1999, for a comprehensive exposition) provides a precise framework for building an automated system for the assessment of knowledge. In particular, an efficient computerized assessment procedure trying to delineate a student’s knowledge must implement a mechanism handling the randomness of the student’s behavior. When answering to the queries of the procedure, the student may indeed be prone to careless errors or lucky guesses. Knowledge space theory offers several stochastic assessment procedures designed to deal with such a situation (Falmagne & Doignon, 1988a, 1988b). Among these procedures, the most promising one is based on a discrete parameter continuous Markov process (Falmagne & Doignon, 1988a) which, though effective, involves lengthy computation. This creates a difficulty because the practical application of knowledge space theory may involve potentially very large knowledge structure. 

The solution proposed here is to substitute to the original knowledge structure a coarsening of the structure gathering the knowledge states into clusters. Then, through a process of reduction , we collapse the states inside each cluster. We finally adapt the Markovian assessment procedure to the resulting representation of knowledge. 4. SIMULATIONS 

We ran simulations on two knowledge structures, K 1 and K 2 . Both structures have been developed for implementation into an actual assessment system. Structure K 1 encompasses the arithmetic curriculum with a 95-item domain, from 1 -digit addition to negative exponent. The structure contains 37,370 states. Structure K 2 covers the beginning algebra curriculum with an 84-item domain, from simple linear equation to polynomial division. That structure contains 101,502 states.

We now turn our attention to the assessment time. For the three reductions, the values show the linearity of the time in terms of the number of questions and the size of the coarsening. Compared with the assessment time on the full reference structure, the improvement is still more dramatic than expected. This is due to an alternative representation of the structure in the computer memory. If the reference structure was coded as the reduction of the coarsening for the identity relation, we would observe an assessment time in direct variation with the structure size. 

5. DISCUSSION 

The simulation results of the previous section indicate that our approach is valid. The loss of information due to the reduction of the coarsening does affect the accuracy of the assessment routine. That is, in the ideal condition of a perfectly consistent student, the routine is not able to recover exactly the student’s knowledge state. Nevertheless, a right choice for the equivalence relation keeps the inaccuracy within reasonable limits. More important, when the student is prone to lucky guesses and random errors, the performance of the assessment is only slightly degraded. It is as if the noise in the student’s behavior was somehow covered by the existing noise due to the reduction process. 

We generally observed better results from h1 to h3 . It is not easy to tell if the improvement is due to the property of the equivalence relation or to the mere increase in the size of the resulting coarsening. For example, in K 1/h1 , 71.5% of the equivalence classes, covering 63.8% of the states, are not connected. The imposition of connected classes results in a higher number of classes for K 1/h2 . 

Looking at the features of the coarsenings in Tables 1 and 3, we see two points where we can improve the algorithms building the equivalence relations. The first point is to lower the standard deviation of the class size (Column 3). The second point is to lower the systematic difference between the distance to class and the distance to structure (Columns 4 and 5). 

We can also make use of alternative equivalence relations to build the coarsening in some special cases. In particular, we may take advantage of the algebraic properties of a knowledge structure. A knowledge space is a family of states closed under arbitrary union with a bottom element. So it is a complete lattice under inclusion, with set union as join operation (see, for instance, Corollary 2.17 in Davey & Priestley, 1990). If the knowledge space is also closed under intersection, it is a lattice of sets, with set union and intersection as lattice operations. On the states of such a structure, we can define a congruence relation: It is an equivalence relation such that the quotient map sending an element to its equivalence class is a lattice homomorphism.
