                       In this context where there is a scientific theory, the second type of GOF question concerns the quality of the prediction model. Yet, as we shall see, the existing frequentist methods for statistically assessing the  difference in the fit quality  between two rival scientific functions still depends on the assumption of IID Gaussian error, but it is often difficult to satisfy this requirement. Moreover if this statistical requirement for a valid parametric test is not satisfied, then it is theoretically unappealing to nonlinearly transform the theoretical function simply to enable a statistical test of the difference in the fit quality.

However, there is a new Bayesian method for doing this second type of GOF question that does not depend on any distributional assumptions about the errors. The Bayesian distribution-free method provides a measure of the fit quality of each scientific theory and provides for a straightforward statistical assessment of the differences among the rival scientific theories. The current paper focuses on describing and applying the new Bayesian procedure for an application from mathematical psychology where there are very similar theoretical functions that have been difficult to validly test with existing frequentist methods.

It is notable that the Prelec model outperformed the ratio models despite the fact it has one less parameter for each fit. The Prelec model also has one less parameter than the exponential-odds model, but the exponential-odds model did have a reliably better fit. Consequently it is important to see if the better fit of the exponential-odds model is an artifact of the additional parameter. Complex models with a large number of parameters can in principle have a better fit than more simple models because the extra parameters can account for random error ( Burnham & Anderson, 1998 ). But sometimes complex models are required. For example the  Standard model  from particle physics is arguably the most successful theory in physics; it has enjoyed remarkable success in making accurate predictions. The  Standard model , with 18 free parameters and many elementary particles, is also very complex, but it is not thought to be more complex than what is needed for physicists to explain the known effects in particle physics ( Cottingham & Greenwood, 2007 ). Generally, scientific models are idealized representations of a set of processes or phenomena. In new fields of scientific inquiry, it is typically the case that scientists see things differently, so there are rival hypotheses and models. Debate among scientists is an established method for sharpening and clarifying a new research domain. So, it is a common scientific problem to evaluate competing models. There are many issues to consider in assessing any model, such as the completeness and testability of the model. But an essential feature of a successful model is that it should have a good fit to high quality data. Regardless of the elegance of the model, if there are failures in accounting for reliable measurements, then a different model is required.
