In an attempt to model the accumulation of stress caused by waiting, Osuna (1985) postulated that anxiety stems from a perceived harm or loss to the person's well being that he represented by an increasing function of the total time to service. This paper proposes an extension of this model to situations in which the psychological cost of waiting is not functionally related to time but merely correlated with it. It is shown that the properties of Osuna's model recently reexamined by Suck and Holling (1997) remain valid in this broader framework. � 2001 Academic Press 

1. INTRODUCTION 

Everyone is familiar with the feeling of annoyance, frustration, and impatience that typically grows with time when one needs to wait for service in a bank, at a post office, on the phone, or in front of a computer terminal. This phenomenon, which can be objectivized through a series of physiological measurements such as increased heart and respiratory rates, blood pressure, or perspiration, is generally referred to as anxiety or stress in the psychological literature. According to cognitive stress theories (e.g., Lazarus 6 Folkman, 1984) this process would be rooted in the general sense of waste induced by idleness and by the uncertainty associated with the total waiting time.

6. CONCLUSION 

Since its publication in 1985, the work of Osuna has generated renewed interest in the literature for developing a mathematical model that could explain the process 

129 

STRESS CAUSED BY WAITING 

by which psychological stress builds up when individuals are waiting for service. His model treats stress as the anticipation of a subjective loss and gives conditions under which stress intensity and accumulated stress increase with time when a functional relationship exists between costs and waiting times. The developments described in this paper show quite clearly that these conclusions extend far beyond this special case and remain valid in fairly broad conditions of positive dependence between variable costs and times. The model presented herein also makes it possible to look at comparisons between individual cost functions and waiting time distributions and to further Osuna's work on the identification of strategies for disclosure of time to service that can help reduce total stress. 

More importantly, perhaps, the introduction of variable costs in Osuna's model opens the door to the formal statistical analysis of time and stress data in an experimental situation. Differences in the perceived sense of waste that the subject experiences over subsequent trials may well form the basis for variability in repeated measures of stress commonly observed for a fixed waiting time. Since stress intensity is defined in terms of conditional expected costs, it is not yet clear how to identify an appropriate copula or family of copulas for modeling the dependence between variables C and T, unless of course cost values are elicited directly. While it may well be that different models for cost and time provide an adequate fit to stress data, varying the dependence structure of the model via the choice of copula provides a useful tool for carrying out sensitivity analysis and for checking the robustness of the predictions and conclusions derived from the model. For additional information on ways of fitting copula models and testing their goodness of fit, the reader may refer to Joe (1997, chap. 10) for a parametric approach or to Genest et al. (1995, 1998) for a nonparametric solution.
