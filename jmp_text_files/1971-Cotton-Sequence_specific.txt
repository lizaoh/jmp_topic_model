COTTON  University of California, Santa Barbara, California, 93!06  A concept identification model is presented for the purpose of predicting trial by trial performance as a function of the  specific stimulus sequence. For different randomly selected sequences with equal probability, the model reduces to the Bower  and Trabasso theory (Section I-III).  Experiment 1 tests the model by studying the amount of stimulus change from Trial 2 to Trial 3, Trials 1 and 2 having involved  identical stimuli on both trials for all subjects (Section IV). Experiment 2 tests the model on a 24-trial task in which the  stimulus sequence was the same for all subjects versus one in which the stimuli were randomized (Section V). Partial  conformity to theory was found in each experiment (Section VI).  Because of difficulties observed with the original sequence-specific model, a twotrial memory model was developed for use with  Experiment 2. Improved, though not fully adequate, predictions resulted. Assuming unequal salience of hypotheses was  detrimental to the fits; assuming that some persons are in the conditioned state at the beginning of the experiment had only a  slight advantage (Sections VII-VIII).  1. INTRODUCTION  Several investigators (e.g_ Anderson and Guthrie, 1966; Detambel and Stolurow, 1956; Dodd and Bourne, 1969; Falmagne, 1968,  1970; Haygood and Bourne, 1964; Peterson, 1962, 1968; Restle and Emmerich, 1966; Richter, 1965) have shown the importance of  stimulus sequence or order in the learning of concept identification tasks. The present paper provides two basic variations of  a theory to predict such effects to a first approximation. The first variation has the advantage of collapsing into an  existing one (Bower and Trabasso, 1964) if a different random stimulus order is employed with each subject and if each  possible stimulus is equally probable on each trial. Only two parameters are identifiable and estimated by Greeno and Steiner in Eq. 62; they are c and q' in  our terminology. EXPERIMENT 1: TESTING THE SEQUENCE-SPECIFIC MODEL  This experiment compares performance on a very few trials of a three-dimensional binary concept identification task as a  function of k, the number of dimension values unchanged between successive trials (note that k is not the total number of  dimensions K), and i, the number of dimensions (including the relevant one) congruent with the relevant one in their change or  lack of change from trial to trial. A second variable was whether or not experience with a similar concept identification  problem (the practice task) was given before the basic problem (the learning task). Previous experience was hypothesized to  reduce the parameter f because subjects could be expected to make better use of information from immediately preceding trials  if they had been familiarized with the task.  General Outline of Experimental Design  Practice task. The basic stimuli for this task were eight symbols: E, e, R, r, W , , , 94 and ~-, presented either with all  eight on a single slide or with only one symbol on a slide. Three dimensions were explicitly involved in the problem in the  sense that subjects were told of the existence of all three and informed that one was the basis for solving the problem: E vs  R, upright vs sideways, and upper case vs lower case were the dimensions; the solution was that "1" was to be the response to  all upper case letters and "0" to all lower case letters. Subjects given a practice task received one trial with the eight  stimuli presented all at once, as a means of testing for original hypotheses and nine trials with individual stimuli.  Following each individual stimulus trial, the correct answer was shown. At the end of these trials, subjects were presumed  likely to have learned the solution, but it was told to them nonetheless in order to equalize knowledge among subjects prior  to the next task.  Learning task. The basic stimuli for this task were the eight symbols, A, a, B, b, m, W and a, presented as in the practice  task. Dimensions were the same as in the practice task except that A vs B replaced E vs R. All subjects received five trials.  Trials 0 and 4 were the presentation of all eight symbols as a single stimulus, and subjects were instructed to guess the  correct answer for each symbol. Knowledge of results was not provided after Trials 0 and 4, but was given immediately after  each other trial. The solution was that "1" would be called correct if an upright letter was presented and "0" if a sideways  letter was presented. On Trials 1 and 2, the symbol "a" was presented to every subject in order to check theoretical  predictions for the case i = 3. Table 1 shows the specific stimulus used for each group on Trial 3.  Subjects and assignment to groups. Subjects (students at Indiana University) were run in sets rather than individually. The  basic method was an incomplete blocks design with successive sets of approximately 50 subjects being randomly divided into four  INFRA-STRUCTURE FOR THE BOWER AND TRABASSO THEORY 345  subsets, each to receive a different treatment of Table 1. Use of many such sets ensured that all 12 treatments were given and  that any group's data were based on information from at least three sets. About one fourth of the subjects, approximately balanced across treatments, were  run in each successive replication of the experiment. EXPERIMENT 2: A FURTHER TEST OF THE SEQUENCE-SPECIFIC MODEL  This experiment employs a variable sequence group (called the Variable Group hereafter) and a constant sequence group (called  the Constant Group hereafter) in the same three-dimensional binary concept learning task used in the experiment reported  above. Twenty-four individual stimulus trials were given in order to produce complete learning in most subjects. Since a  different random sequence of stimuli was given for the 24 training trials of each subject in the Variable Group, the Bower and  Trabasso model should apply directly to that group. Since all subjects in the Constant Group received the same sequence for  the 24 training trials, our Eqs. 1, 4, and 5 should apply to the behavior exhibited by that group. In addition, Eqs. 1, 4, and  5 may be applied separately to each subject in the Variable Group and the result averaged over subjects in order to make an  alternate prediction for that group.  Method  Learning task and slides. The same symbols were used as in the learning task of the previous experiment. Additional slides  were constructed as necessary to permit 24 reinforced trials rather than the three of Experiment 1. All eight possible symbols  (fl, a, B, b, <~, ro, =, and n) were available for use, any one of them having an equal likelihood of occurrence on any trial  in a given sequence. One exception should be noted: To prevent unusually high frequencies of any particular symbol, not more  than six occurrences of any symbol (contrasted to the expected number of three) were permitted in any sequence. Fifty-one  sequences were employed, one for each of the members of the Variable Group, and the other for all the members of the Constant  Group. Answer slides identical to those of Experiment 1 were used on all 24 training trials, with "1" the correct answer  following upright symbols and "0" the correct answer following sideways symbols. REFLECTIONS ON EXPERIMENTS I AND 2  These two experiments show that the sequence-specific model has advantages over the Bower-Trabasso model in that it predicts  some of the effects of a specific stimulus  INFRA-STRUCTURE FOR THE BOWER AND TRABASSO THEORY 359  order and yet also has implications for data averaged across stimulus sequences. One question arises concerning the values of  c in the two experiments: the c of 0 (or even the .0189 maximum for the practice groups) in Experiment 1 is below the .167  value which would hold if, after each error, one of the six possible hypotheses were drawn at random with equal probabilities  for each hypothesis and conditioned if correct. It is also smaller than the .191 value obtained for c in the most comparable  group (the Constant Group) of Experiment 2. Chumbley (1967, p. 98) states that the number of concept identification studies in  which the learning parameter is below 1/2K is quite small. However, we are dealing with a slightly different theory in the  present paper; the zero or near-zero c in Experiment 1 probably indicates that performance on a very few trials can be  predicted by reference to retention of information from one trial to the next plus random guessing when information is not  retained, conditioning being an unnecessary assumption to invoke for such a small number of trials. This conclusion is  consistent with a reanalysis of data of Trials 1-3 of Experiment 2, Constant Group, performed exactly like the Experiment 1  analysis except that only one group was involved. With all three parameters free to vary, the maximum likelihood fit was  obtained with c = 0, f = .180, and p = .424. This superiority of fits using c = 0 for Trials 1-3 of each experiment suggests  that c does indeed shift from a small value on early trials to a larger value on later trials, as various investigators have  suggested (see e.g., Trabasso and Bower, 1968, pp. 47-48; Levine, 1967).  An added indication of difficulty in predicting early trial performance with a specific sequence comes from Trial 4 for the  Constant Group of Experiment 2, for which our records show the observed proportion correct to be .24, compared to a  theoretical value of .69. The sequence of stimuli for the first four trials was "a a A B"; therefore, the hypotheses "upright  = 1 and sideways = 0" and "aye = 1 and bee = 0" had both been confirmed up to Trial 4. We know from Table 7 that the latter  hypothesis was originally expressed 8 times as often on Trial 0 as the former. But the latter would lead subjects to respond  incorrectly on Trial 4 if they considered it to be the appropriate hypothesis. Thus the failure of the theory at this point  seems to reflect two factors-a strong preference for one hypothesis and retention of information obtained prior to Trial 3  while responding to Trial 4 stimulation. VIII. CONCLUDING REMARKS  Two theoretical conclusions seem to follow from the experiments and analyses just reported: (a) For a group of subjects each  of whom is given a different randomly constructed stimulus sequence, the Bower-Trabasso model is as successful in prediction  as originally claimed and can even make satisfactory predictions of all three-trial response patterns in a 24-trial  experiment. However, there is evidence based on Table 6 (p < .001 and p < .10) that the presolution probability of a correct  response is not constant when conditioned upon i values. (b) Though some predictions from the BowerTrabasso model are adequate  when applied to groups of subjects having constant stimulus sequences as in Experiment 1 or in Experiment 2, Constant Group,  and  366 COTTON  though the Bower-Trabasso microanalyses of data for Experiment 2, Constant Group, generally did not contradict that theory,  the inference of constant presolution probabilities conditionalized on i and the prediction of trial-by-trial performance of  subjects given a constant stimulus sequence is generally unsatisfactory with all models we have examined. Prediction is  improved, however, as we move away from the BowerTrabasso model to a one-trial memory model and is further improved as we move  on to a two-trial memory model. Overall prediction is not materially improved either by assuming unequal salience of  hypotheses or by assuming that some persons are in the conditioned state at the beginning of the experiment. The former  assumption actually impairs prediction, and the latter improves it only slightly. Furthermore, sequence-specific models  averaged across sequences work as well as or better than the Bower-Trabasso model applied to the same data.
