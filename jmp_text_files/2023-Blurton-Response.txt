John is asked to hurry because  the train will depart soon . Mary is asked to hurry because  the train will depart in 15 min . What will happen? John will grab his suitcase and will run to the platform. Mary will run to the station too, but then will notice that there is some time until the departure, so she might buy a cup of coffee and only then enter the train. In this article, we argue that in experimental psychology, the typical response time experiment resembles Mary’s situation, and we investigate what happens if we use a task that resembles John’s situation.

Response time (RT) plays a central role in psychology, and many theories make explicit predictions on the duration of mental processes (e.g.,  Luce, 1986; Posner, 1978; Sternberg, 1969a ). In RT experiments, the latency between the imperative stimulus and the observable response is the primary dependent variable, and RT differences between experimental conditions or higher-order interactions are used to reveal the architecture of cognition ( Sternberg, 1969b; Townsend & Nozawa, 1995 ). To make sure that participants concentrate on the task, and only the “relevant” mental processes contribute to RT, these experiments are typically conducted under time pressure; that is, participants are asked to respond as fast and as accurately as possible to the imperative stimulus ( Pachella, 1974 ). From the perspective of the researcher, an emphasis of both speed and accuracy makes perfect sense; whereas for the participant, it is easily recognized as a contradiction that entails all kinds of guessing behavior and implicit or strategic trade-offs between speed and accuracy ( Wickelgren, 1977; Yellott, 1971 ). For this reason, guessing and other unwanted forms of behavior are often explicitly discouraged in the instruction—“respond as fast and as accurately as possible without guessing”.

Even if correctly interpreted by a fully compliant participant, the canonical instruction still leaves open which strategy is eventually used, quick or more cautious responding. This dilemma might encourage response styles that maximize reward,

For example, participants engage in action monitoring (see  Botvinick, Braver, Barch, Carter, & Cohen, 2001 ), such as rechecking responses before they are given or scheduling responses at a time before some internal representation of the deadline ( Miletíc & van Maanen, 2019 ). Participants could thereby refrain from very quick responses, and instead gather enough information until they feel sufficiently secure to respond.

Typical RT experiments are rather boring, and many stimulus repetitions are required to obtain reliable statistics from the sample of RTs. The vital importance of motivation has sporadically been investigated, for example, in studies relating task performance to semester time ( Navarick & Bellone, 2010; Nicholls, Loveless, Thomas, Loetscher, & Churches, 2015; Richert & Ward, 1976 ). Moreover, within an experiment, cognitive processes suffer from fatigue ( Mackworth, 1948 ). Therefore, it is obviously not helpful to give participants contradictory instructions such as “respond fast and accurately as possible” and, at the same time, giving them room for strategic responding with fixed time windows for responding or no deadline at all. Of course, there is always a non-negligible proportion of participants who ignore the instructions and adopt a response style they feel comfortable with ( Bogacz et al., 2010 ). This adaptation can either follow from a general lack of motivation (e.g.,  Nicholls et al., 2015 ), or because the double-bind nature of the instruction ( Gibney, 2006 ) does not leave any other choice: it is simply not possible to respond fast and accurately at the same time. The problem exists in virtually all speeded response tasks but is exacerbated in experimental tasks are designed to provoke errors (e.g., flanker tasks, ( Eriksen & Eriksen, 1974 ); stop signal tasks, ( Verbruggen & Logan, 2008 )).

We think it is plausible to assume that in any sufficiently difficult task some kind of action monitoring might be activated, such as rechecking the correctness of a response before it is given. Such processes emerge, for instance, in the form of extremely fast corrective responses after an error ( Laming, 1979 ). Other examples include an internal representation of a deadline to account for situations in which a decision must be made within a certain amount of time ( Frazier & Yu, 2007 ). In experiments with a fixed time window for responding, the participant might set an internal deadline right before the end of the response window. It should be noted, however, that an internal representation of a deadline for responding will necessarily be noisy, thus introducing randomness even when the duration of the deadline is fixed ( Frazier & Yu, 2007 ). Alternatively, it has been suggested that decision making under time pressure may be modeled with a sequential sampling model with narrowing boundaries, or with an urgency signal that signifies the necessity to respond ( Ditterich, 2006; Hawkins, Forstmann, Wagenmakers, Ratcliff, & Brown, 2015; Miletíc & van Maanen, 2019 ). The assumption of an urgency signal that increases with time as well as decision boundaries that become narrower typically lead to the prediction of slow errors, that is, errors that are slower than correct responses ( Ditterich, 2006 ).

When there is no time pressure and processing of the imperative stimulus has finished, we may recognize Mary’s situation from the introductory example: If there is residual capacity for action monitoring, the remaining time might be used for additional checks of the correctness of the intended response ( Roger, Castellar, Pourtois, & Fias, 2014 ). Obviously, such action monitoring selectively distorts the RT distribution for the easier conditions of an experiment—in the more difficult conditions, there would not be enough time and capacity for rechecking.

Consistency in the response strategy is particularly relevant for comparisons across experimental conditions and within multifactorial designs ( Sternberg, 1969b; Townsend & Nozawa, 1995 ), where researchers usually assume some kind of context invariance. In the literature, this assumption appears under different names with slightly different implications (e.g., pure insertion, ( Donders, 1868 ); selective influence, ( Ashby & Townsend, 1980; Dzhafarov, 2003; Sternberg, 1969b )). Loosely stated, the experimental manipulation is assumed to affect only a specific subset of mental processes—ideally, only a single process—while leaving everything else untouched. In RT models, like  Ratcliff ’s ( 1978 ) diffusion decision model, context invariance might correspond to the assumption that the response criteria are set by the participant, but do not differ between experimental conditions, whereas the processes related to the accumulation of evidence vary with the salience of the condition-specific stimuli. Due to the optional and adaptive nature of action monitoring, the bias due to rechecking cannot be expected to cancel out in comparisons between experimental conditions. If the conditions vary in difficulty, context invariance might not hold anymore, and comparisons between conditions would be contaminated by context effects.

In most experiments, there is some time limit for responding (even without such a time limit, excessively slow responses are typically removed before the data is analyzed; e.g.,  van Selst & Joliceur, 1994 . Most often, a fixed deadline is used, but response deadlines with varying lengths have also been employed in the past. In the latter studies, deadlines were systematically manipulated between experimental blocks to induce and investigate speed–accuracy trade-offs (e.g.,  Link & Tindall, 1971; Pachella, 1974; Wickelgren, 1977 ). In those studies that varied the duration of the response window on a trial-by-trial basis ( Diederich, 2008; Diederich & Busemeyer, 2006; Link, 1971 ), a warning sign prior to the stimulus typically announced the length of the deadline. Shorter deadlines create more time pressure, and indeed, responses typically become faster and less accurate compared to longer deadlines. In perceptual decision tasks, the length of the deadline determines response caution, with longer deadlines being associated with more cautious responding, as indicated by elevated response thresholds in sequential-sampling models ( Diederich & Busemeyer, 2006 ). Moreover, manipulations of response deadlines have shown to cause qualitative changes in cognitive processes. For instance, in mental calculation problems,  Campbell and Austin (2002)  observed that the length of the deadline determined whether participants employed procedural strategies or memory retrieval.

Even if variable deadlines are used, the length of the deadline is usually known to the participants (e.g.,  Karşilar, Simen, Papadakis, & Balci, 2014 ). One notable exception is Experiment 1 by  van Zandt, Colonius, and Proctor (2000)  who asked participants to judge the similarity of stimulus pairs. In what they denote as “mixed blocks”, participants had to do the similarity judgement within a random deadline of either 500, 750, or 1000 ms to avoid a “TOO SLOW” feedback screen. A related technique is the response-signal procedure, in which a quick response is requested by a prompt that appears at some time after the imperative stimulus (see  Dambacher & Hübner, 2013 , for a review of the method). This prompt can be presented at fixed ( Schouten & Bekker, 1967 ) or unforeseeable timepoints (e.g.,  Reed, 1976 )—the latter shares similarities with variable deadlines.

In the present study, we wanted to mitigate some of the issues raised above by using a speeded response task with an unpredictable deadline, which we define in this context as a randomly distributed deadline following a non-aging distribution. We therefore adopt the idea of  van Zandt et al. (2000)  and develop it further, including the methodological implications for the analysis of the results. In our experiment, the deadline varied from trial to trial without announcement, so that participants never knew how much time was left for responding, similar to John’s situation in the introductory paragraph. We kept the instruction: Participants were asked to respond as fast as possible without guessing. However, we expected the variable deadline to reduce the room for participant’s subjective interpretation of the researcher’s intentions. The rationale was that due to the unpredictability of the external deadline, action monitoring and strategic delayed responding would be unadvisable, because it would effectively reduce the probability of responding correctly  and  in time.

The deadline was sampled from an exponential distribution, so that the hazard of a time-out was constant, regardless of how much time passed since the presentation of the imperative stimulus ( Nickerson & Burnham, 1969 ). To avoid anticipations and fast guesses, the use of a non-aging distribution has been recommended for foreperiods in RT tasks (( Green, Smith, & von Gierke, 1983; Nickerson & Burnham, 1969 ); see also ( Luce, 1986 )); in this study, we adopted this important design principle to the response phase of the trial. In other words, there was just one way to respond before the deadline and to avoid an omission due to a time-out: to hurry up, as each moment spent for action monitoring or rechecking inevitably increased the risk of a time-out. We also speculated that the constant hazard rate induced a constant time pressure, so that a decision-maker would only sample as much evidence as needed to reach a pre-set internal criterion ( Wald, 1947 ). In the exponential distribution, most events occur early after the imperative stimulus; therefore, we expected a non-negligible number of omissions. To prevent frustration, which might decrease motivation, we informed participants beforehand that a substantial number of time-outs was an integral part of the experimental design.

We tested the effect of a variable deadline on speeded responses in a standard motion discrimination experiment ( Morgan & Ward, 1980 ). The deadline was either fixed or random, in separate experimental blocks. Two different average durations were used for the deadline (900, 1200 ms) to induce different levels of time pressure. To assess possible differential effects of time pressure under varying levels of stimulus confusability (i.e., task difficulty), the level of motion coherence was manipulated as well. We present two analyses that we denote as “experimenter’s approach” and “modeler’s approach” ( Smith & Little, 2018 ). The experimenter’s approach focuses on the calculation of standard RT performance measures, as well as their comparison across experimental conditions. In the modeler’s approach, we fit an RT model for choice responses (Blurton, Kyllingsbæk, Nielsen & Bundesen, 2020) to the data and test if the experimental manipulations selectively affect specific parameters of the model (see methods section for more specific hypotheses).

We tested 12 paid volunteers (age range 20–37 years, all right-handed) in the experiment. All participants had normal or corrected-to-normal visual acuity. All participants gave their written informed consent prior to participation. The study was conducted in accordance with Danish privacy protection laws and approved by the department’s ethics board (IP-IRB/19032019). Data of one participant was excluded because performance did not exceed chance level.

This study is exploratory and is meant to illustrate the implications of the new experimental design for data analysis. Moreover, it is difficult to define a practically relevant effect size for power planning in any response time study. Therefore, the sample size of this study was chosen ad hoc, in the hope that 3,200 replications per participant and 12 participants yield sufficiently precise estimates and good confidence interval coverage. As such, the sample size should be seen as a compromise between the experimenter’s approach that benefits from a large sample and the modeler’s approach that benefits from collecting data from many trials per participant ( Smith & Little, 2018 ).

Participants sat in a dimly lit room in front of a CRT screen with a viewing distance of 60 cm. The temporal resolution of the screen was set to 100 Hz. The stimulation was implemented as a MATLAB script (The MathWorks, Natick, MA) using the Psychophysics Toolbox ( Brainard, 1997; Kleiner, Brainard, & Pelli, 2007 ). Responses were recorded by means of two response buttons at the left and right hand.

The stimuli consisted of 50 white dots moving on a circular black disk presented on an otherwise gray background. The circular background subtended 9 deg visual angle ( Baker & Reddick, 1982 ). Some of the dots were moving randomly with the direction determined by a uniform distribution on the interval [0°, 360°]. A randomly determined subset of dots was moving in a preferred direction, either to the left or to the right, varying between trials. Both coherently and randomly moving dots were removed when they reached the edge of the circular background and replaced by a new dot at the opposite side of the circular background ( Baker & Reddick, 1982 ).

Across the participants, coherence level was between 45% and 60% in the high coherence condition, and between 25% and 40% in the low coherence condition. The individual coherence level was determined based on the performance in the training block. In the training block, all participants started with 40% and 60% coherence in the low and high coherence condition, respectively. In case performance was at ceiling, we reduced the coherence in both conditions by 10% for that participant. In one participant performance was still close to perfect in the high coherence condition, so we reduced both coherence levels by another 5% for this participant. The individual coherence values can be found in the online supplement.

The moving dots had a limited lifetime of 15 frames (i.e., 150 ms, ( Britten, Shadlen, Newsome, & Movshon, 1993 )). Upon reaching the end of their lifetime, their contrast was gradually reduced on the last two frames, creating an impression of fading-out. Conversely, new dots were introduced at a reduced contrast and first displayed at full contrast on their third frame. During the first five frames, all dots were moving randomly. After this phase, some dots began to move in their preferred direction. Response times were calculated with respect to the onset of coherent motion.

Participants were asked to give speeded responses to the random dot motion displays ( Britten et al., 1993 ). After an exponentially distributed foreperiod (mean 3000 ms) the stimulus appeared and the task was to detect the direction of the coherently moving dots moving in a preferred direction and respond with the left response key when this direction was leftwards on the screen, or with the right response key when the dots moved in the opposite direction. Participants were instructed to try to give their response prior to reaching a deadline occurring in every trial. In one condition, the deadline occurred at a fixed time (either 900 or 1200 ms). In the other condition, the deadline was unpredictable since it was drawn from an exponential distribution (discretized to match the screen refreshes, i.e., a geometric distribution) with a mean of either 600 ms or 900 ms to which we added a fixed base time of 300 ms. If a response did not occur before the deadline, participants received the feedback “too slow”. In this case, we increased the inter-trial interval for an additional second. Participants were instructed to avoid this feedback by responding as fast as possible without guessing blindly. The instructions were identical for all experimental conditions. For responses within the first 50 ms period of completely randomly moving dots, the feedback “too fast” was given to discourage anticipatory behavior. When the response was given before the deadline, the participants received feedback on their response time and accuracy.

Each participant was tested in 16 blocks with 200 trial repetitions each, yielding a total of 3,200 trials. The experiment was a 2

Each block took approximately 15 min to complete. At the end of each testing, we had obtained 400 replications in each of the eight factorial conditions. To avoid boredom and fatigue, testing was split into two sessions held on two separate days.

We present two analyses “experimenter’s approach” and the “modeler’s approach”, reflecting two research traditions in Cognitive Psychology and their goals and interests when analyzing RT data ( Smith & Little, 2018 ). We focus on the estimation of RT distribution, but we also present summary statistics such as accuracy and median RT.

Experimenter’s approach. 
                          In this analysis, the focus is on the time to a correct response, and the goal is to test whether responses are significantly faster in one condition compared to another. This was done by performing a standard analysis of variance (ANOVA) on the median RTs (in this experimental setup, mean RT is difficult to estimate due to response omissions by experimental design). We estimated a mixed-effects model with  deadline type  (fixed, random),  deadline duration  (short, long),  coherence level  (low, high) as fixed effects,  session number  (1, 2) as covariate, and  participant  as a random intercept (R package lme4,  Bates, Maechler, Bolker, & Walker, 2015 ). All interactions between the fixed effects were included in the model.

We fitted one overarching model for fixed and variable deadlines and adjusted for the factors coherence level and deadline duration including their interactions. Moreover, a covariate with the trial number was used to test for training effects over time. We account for the repeated measures within individuals using gamma distributed frailties (R package frailtyEM; ( Balan & Putter, 2019 )). In a post-hoc test, we tested the unplanned comparison of inter-subject variance of median RT between variable and fixed deadline with a Pitman-Morgan test ( Mudholkar, Wilding, & Mietlowski, 2003 ). All analyses were performed in R statistical language version 4.0.4 ( R Core Team, 2021 ).

Finally, in a speeded RT task with varying time pressure, it is often instructive to evaluate response accuracy conditional on response speed. A useful way of doing so is to estimate a conditional accuracy function, that is, an estimate of response accuracy across several RT bins. We used five RT bins separated by the 20%:20%:100% quantiles, estimated from the sum of cumulative incidence functions for correct and incorrect responses. Conditional accuracy in these five bins was then calculated as the number of correct responses over the number of all responses observed within the bin.

We used maximum likelihood to estimate the model parameters and assess the goodness-of-fit. The estimation was based on all trials, each trial contributing either the observed response or the length of the deadline in case of time-outs. Fast RTs (RT < 200 ms) were left censored to avoid biases in model parameters in the maximum likelihood approach (84 fast responses in total, or 0.2% of all trials). Trials in which no response was observed before time-out were treated as censored observations. Censored observations were integrated into the goodness-of-fit statistic using the survivor function predicted by the model, that is, the probability of not responding up to that time ( Ulrich & Miller, 1994 ). The models were compared by means of their Bayes Information Criterion (BIC). The BIC of a model is based on its log likelihood and adds a penalty term for each parameter that is added to the model. In the BIC, this penalty is sample size dependent, it grows with the logarithm of the number of observations the likelihood is based on (here:  n  
                         
                            = 
                          3,200 trials per participant). The BIC from the fits to individual data was summed across all participants for the model comparisons and model selection. All fits were performed in MATLAB. To find the maximum likelihood, we used MATLAB’s simplex routine ( fminsearch ). Each fit was repeated several times, with the starting values taken from the previous fit to reduce the risk of the simplex routine get stuck in a local minimum, until the increase of log-likelihood was smaller than .001.

To complement the model selection, we subsequently ran statistical tests on the parameters that were included in the final model. While the model selection approach informs us about which parameters are relevant to model the effect of deadline type, it does not tell us whether there is a significant difference in that parameter, or the parameter varies in an unsystematic way.

Descriptive statistics such as response accuracy and median RT are summarized in  Table 1 . In the fixed deadline condition, participants generally managed to give their response before the deadline ( Table 1 ). These values correspond to earlier findings obtained with fixed deadlines (e.g.,  Dambacher & Hübner, 2013 ). As expected, response frequencies were lower with a random than with a fixed deadline; about 90% of responses were given in time when the deadline was random, and responses were more accurate with high coherence than with low coherence.

Both the type of deadline and difficulty had an effect on those distributions: Participants were faster and more accurate in the high coherence conditions as well as faster, but slightly less accurate in the short deadline and in the unpredictable deadline conditions. The results of competing risk regression analysis are shown in  Table 2 . The type of the deadline, its length and the coherence of the random dot motion have a clear influence on the speed of the correct responses. As expected, hazard ratios were below 1, that is, slower responses were observed for fixed deadlines, long deadlines, and low coherence. In contrast, none of the interaction terms are statistically significant. Limited training effects (approx. 4% speedup per 1,000 trials) were seen with increasing trial numbers.

This pattern of results is consistent with the notion that the overall effect of coherence and deadline length is similar for both fixed and variable deadlines. The combined effect of deadline type and length is close to the 5% significance level, and its hazard ratio below 1 indicates that the participants were more likely to adapt their response speed to the length of the deadline if the latter was fixed (see also  Fig. 3 ). However, the effect is rather weak and not statistically significant. Therefore, despite the considerable differences in the overall speed of responses, the experimental manipulations were similarly effective under both types of deadlines.

We present results of an experiment in which a response deadline was systematically varied in length and type, that is, whether it was fixed or random and therefore unpredictable. The goal was to determine if strategic responding can be suppressed using unpredictable variable deadlines, in the hope that this eliminates or reduces the participants’ ability to perform action monitoring, for example, by scheduling their response right before the expected deadline. The effects of this manipulation were investigated in a perceptual decision task with randomly moving dots at two different coherence levels.

Of particular interest in this context is the comparison between the standard fixed deadline and the new, unpredictable variable deadline. Responses were significantly faster in the variable deadline condition, as indicated by both the ANOVA of median RT and the Fine-Gray analysis (“Experimenter’s approach”). Variable deadlines are thus indeed an effective means to put participants under time pressure, potentially reducing the room for strategic responding. In the Poisson random walk model (“Modeler’s approach”), we found an effect of deadline type on the response criteria ( Table 3 ), these were generally lower for the variable deadline condition compared to the fixed deadline conditions. There were also signs of increased performance with variable deadlines as indicated by processing capacity (

Applications of a Theory of Visual Attention (TVA) in whole and partial report paradigms have demonstrated that arousal increases processing capacity ( Bundesen, Vangkilde, & Habekost, 2015; Sørensen, Vangkilde, & Bundesen, 2015 ). It is tempting to speculate that the random deadline leads to higher levels of arousal; in line with this, our participants reported the random deadline to induce more stress and they felt to be put under more pressure than in the fixed deadline conditions.

The variation of deadline length yielded partially expected, but nonetheless interesting results: Everything else being equal, participants responded slower under longer deadlines than under shorter deadlines, for both variable and fixed deadlines, however, they managed to give their response before the deadline to a greater extent in long as compared to short deadlines. Regarding accuracy, we found a difference between short and long deadlines only with variable deadlines (cf.  Table 1 ), which becomes perhaps clearer, if we look at the responses that are given before the deadline ( Fig. 2 ). The results of the Poisson random walk model suggest that these differences originate rather in the decision-making process than other processes, given the significant differences in response criteria and that none of the non-decision parameters (

The effect of deadline length on performance was such that higher response criteria were set with longer deadlines and lower criteria with shorter deadlines ( Diederich & Busemeyer, 2006; Karşilar et al., 2014 ). Clearly, this leads the model to predict also more accurate responses, a prediction that matched the data only in the variable deadline, but not in fixed deadline condition ( Fig. 3 ). Apart from this, our results in the fixed deadline conditions are basically a replication of earlier results ( Wickelgren, 1977; Yellott, 1971 ), and compared with those in variable deadline conditions, it appears that variable deadlines are as effective, if not more effective, in inducing a speed–accuracy trade-off by different deadline lengths.

The exact reason for the increase in response speed with unpredictable deadlines remains open for discussion. While our goal was to exert time pressure by the introduction of unpredictable random deadlines, the observed results remain somewhat inconclusive regarding their effect: Judging from the observed omission rate, fixed and random deadlines of same (expected) length are difficult to compare, as a random deadline of length  D  does not generally lead to the same time pressure as a fixed deadline of length  D  (and possibly an internal representation of it), confirming results reported in a simulation study ( Frazier & Yu, 2007 ). In the model evaluation, it is apparent that our participants chose lower response criteria under variable deadlines than fixed deadlines, both for shorter and longer durations ( Table 3 ). An interpretation of the effects of different amount of time pressure under the different deadlines in terms of urgency or time-variant response criteria cannot be ruled out but seems implausible, as we found no clear signs of increased slow errors ( Fig. 2 ). This was particularly true for the variable deadline conditions, under which the effect of an urgency signal should be greater than under fixed deadlines due to increased time pressure.

We obtained little evidence for an interaction of deadline length and deadline type (ANOVA and Fine & Gray model). Despite this apparent consistency, it is important to keep in mind, however, that the underlying assumptions of the two analyses differ. The linear model of median RTs investigates additive effects of the factor combinations, so that any deviation from additivity results in an interaction. In contrast, the hazard ratios of the  Fine and Gray (1999)  model reflect multiplicative effects that, due to their semiparametric nature, are invariant across order-preserving transformations. The additive (i.e., ANOVA) model has become very popular in mainstream Psychology. Its usefulness may be questioned, though. Except for  Sternberg ’s ( 1969b ) additive factors method for response time models with selectively influenced processing stages, there is a remarkable lack of theoretical underpinning for additive effects of experimental manipulations on the outcome variable. This should not be taken as an argument in favor of multiplicative effects generally, but rather a caveat that researchers should be aware of in model-agnostic analyses.

One of our motivating reasons for using a variable deadline was to preclude or at least reduce the use of different additional strategies, such as re-checking the validity of the intended response (action monitoring,  Botvinick et al., 2001 ). This is clearly difficult to prove directly, but several indicators in our data could be interpreted in a way that action monitoring was at least reduced under variable deadlines. One aspect of our results supporting this view is the amount of RT variation: If the participants indeed chose strategies based on action monitoring, such a strategy increases between-subject RT variation, because people will likely adapt different strategies in different ways. For example, inter-subject variation in median RT was higher under fixed than under variable long deadlines (cf.  Fig. 2 ,  Table 1 ), even though under variable deadlines, estimation of median RT is subject to more variation due to response omissions. In line with this, the difference between model predictions and data in the highest quantiles, which is frequently found in applications of RT models (e.g.,  Hawkins et al., 2015 ), is more pronounced in the fixed deadline conditions than the variable deadline conditions ( Fig. 3 ). Although speculative at this point, this may be indicative of some slow responses being the result of a process that is not described by the model. This might also reflect yet another of the problems outlined in the introduction. The instruction to “respond as fast and as accurately as possible” is subject to individual interpretations (e.g.,  Pachella, 1974 ,  Fig. 4 ). With such an instruction, the exact meaning of “fast” and “accurate” is left to the participant to figure out. In contrast, the unpredictable deadlines reduced much of that room for interpretation, leaving the participant basically with the choice of weighing the “costs” of a time-out (miss) against a timely, but incorrect response. We did not explicate these costs but emphasized the importance of responding in time (a) in the instruction, (b) with the trial-by-trial feedback, and (c) with a “penalty” of a slightly prolonged inter-trial interval after misses. Leaving the exact costs of making a mistake unspecified reflects common practice in most RT experiments. Depending on the goal of the study, the experimenter might want to provide explicit information about the costs of these outcomes. Making these costs explicit will also help to gain further insights into the effect of unpredictable deadlines in future studies, for example, to investigate optimality in decision making under limited time ( Frazier & Yu, 2007 ).

It therefore seems that variable deadlines might indeed be an effective means to put participants under time pressure in RT experiments and, thereby, to reduce the amount of strategic responding to meet the accuracy and speed requirements—resulting in a less biased and less varying estimate of the underlying processing times. The results from the modeling approach complement the results from the statistical approach. According to those results, the effect of a variable deadline was that it caused the participants to adopt lower response criteria. Contrary to our hypothesis, the observed effect on response criteria suggests that a fixed deadline of length  D  does not have the same effect as a variable deadline of the same expected length.

This may be because participants could only have achieved the observed average omission rate of about 10% by setting an (internal) deadline that is lower than the average deadline length in the random deadline condition. Consequently, comparisons between fixed and variable deadlines include both effects due to the unpredictable nature of the deadline, but also perceived higher time pressure to avoid an omission rate deemed unacceptable for most of our participants. Based on our data, however, we deem it unlikely that a lower  fixed  deadline would produce effects like those observed under random deadlines. From the conditional accuracy functions ( Fig. 2 ) it is evident that participants do not use the increased processing time under long deadlines to improve their accuracy when they know that the deadline occurs at a fixed time. We cannot, of course, exclude the possibility that this pattern, which we observed irrespective of task difficulty, switches to that observed under variable deadlines for fixed deadlines of shorter duration (and vice versa with longer variable deadlines). For this, the time pressure of the unpredictable deadlines would need to be calibrated according to the individual omission rate to match that of fixed deadlines. Certainly, longer expected deadlines can be used to induce more careful responding; the main conclusion from our study is that if they are unpredictable, they will put participants under pressure.

With a variable deadline, more omitted responses are to be expected, so one critical aspect of the entire procedure is the treatment of omitted responses. We have outlined an analysis based on a standard approach in the analysis of survival data that takes trials into account in which no responses were observed. This analysis is based on the Aalen–Johansen estimator ( Aalen & Johansen, 1978 ), which generalizes the empirical cumulative distribution function to apply in scenarios with multiple outcomes and uninformative censoring. On this note, response omissions, even if they occur more frequently with variable deadlines, can be integrated into the analysis more effectively than with fixed deadlines, in which all omissions occur after the observed responses and therefore contribute little information about task performance. Then, the derivation of measures for task performance is simply based on the AJ estimator instead of the usual ECDF. Unlike the ECDF, however, the AJ estimator is unconditional, so that it correctly treats omitted responses as what they are: trials in which the participant did not yet make a response at a given time point. This contrasts with current practice in RT research in which trials with “problematic behavior” (errors, omitted responses, outliers) are excluded from the analysis (e.g., “mean correct RT”). Standard statistical procedures (e.g., ANOVA, or the Fine-Gray model, Fine and Gray, 1999 can be applied to these measures to compare performance across different conditions.

Based on our findings, we can recommend the use of variable deadlines in RT research based on speeded RT. Deadlines have been systematically manipulated in the past ( Diederich, 2008; Link, 1971 ). However, to our knowledge,  unpredictable  deadlines, that is, random deadlines following a non-aging distribution have not been used so far. On the other hand, experiments that use a response signal procedure combine the procedure with unpredictable response signals (e.g.,  Dambacher & Hübner, 2013 ). Both the response signal procedure and varying but fixed deadlines aim at mapping the decision-making process more directly ( Ratcliff, 2006; Wickelgren, 1977 ), instead of an assessment of decision-making based only on the result of the process, the latency of the observable response. In these studies, it has been argued that non-predictability has desired effects ( Ratcliff, 2006 ). The downside of the response signal procedure is that requires sufficient practice to ensure that most responses are given immediately after the response signal. In a sense, the unpredictable deadline used here combines the advantages of the response signal procedure and fixed deadlines, which do not require more than the usual amount of practice in standard speeded RT tasks, though perhaps slightly more motivation.

Our primary aim was to put participants under pressure to preclude the use different sub-optimal response strategies that they otherwise might employ ( Botvinick et al., 2001 ). As outlined in the introductory example, is seems fair to say that common speeded RT experiments resemble Mary’s situation: knowing that there is time left before time-out, people may choose to do other things before responding. By letting our participants in the dark how much time exactly they had left to give a response, they rather found themselves in John’s situation, so that the best strategy they could use was to respond as soon as they had completed stimulus recognition. The procedure can be easily integrated in standard RT experiments with speeded responses but without explicit investigation of a speed–accuracy tradeoff. It does not require a participant to keep payoffs in mind during the experiment and allows for a simple and clear instruction of participants. We have argued and shown that the unpredictability maximizes participants’ performance in terms of processing speed and can lead to less inter-subject variation in RT measurements.
