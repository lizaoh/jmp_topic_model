JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 24, 58-87 (1981)  On the Form and Location of the Psychometric Bisection Function for Time  JOHN GIBBON  New York State Psychiatric Institute and Columbia University  The Psychometric Bisection Function for time relates the discriminability of intermediate duration stimuli to a short and long  training duration. Bisection Functions for animals (R. M. Church & M. Z. Deluty, Journal of Experimental Psychology: Animal  Behavior Processes, 1977, 3, 216-228) confirm Weber's Law and also show indifference between short and long reports at the  geometric mean of the training durations. Two discrimination processes are studied which, in combination with different  constructions of the subjective time scale, result in Bisection Functions which differ in form and location. The two  discrimination processes use a likelihood ratio rule or a similarity rule to compare intermediate durations to the training  durations. These rules in combination with two different constructions of the subjective time scale result in four models  which conform to Weber's Law. For one of the scales subjective time is a power function of real time with the scalar property  on variance (Scalar Timing). For the other, subjective time is a logarithmic function of real time with constant variance (Log  Timing). Both Log and Scalar Timing assume normality on the subjective scale. Only three of these models also entail the  geometric mean at the indifference point. The exception is Scalar Timing with the likelihood ratio discrimination rule. This  model entails indifference at approximately the harmonic mean of the training stimuli. Variants of the remaining three models  differ theoretically but alternatives are difficult to discriminate empirically. A contrast is provided by a Poisson Timing  subjective scale in which variance increases directly with the mean. This scale results in indifference at the geometric mean  for both discrimination rules but violates Weber's Law in both cases.  1. INTRODUCTION  A variety of results in animal timing suggests that the subjective time scale is not linear in real time. For example, Catania  (1970) argued that temporal estimation data imply a subjective time scale which is a power function of real time. The  "undermatching" result in choice procedures (Baum, 1974) may be interpreted in a similar fashion. When the logs of response  ratios are plotted against the logs of reinforcement ratios, a slope less than unity is frequently obtained. This suggests  that if choice is based upon the average time between reinforcements then these average times are subjectively transformed by  a power function with an exponent, p < 1. On the other hand, I argued that the time estimation data are compatible with a  linear representation of time, but that the times being estimated are larger than the programmed minimum time value on  temporal differentiation schedules (Gibbon, 1977). Platt (1979) and Platt, Kuch and Bitgood (1973) show that with appropriate  experimental 58  0022-2496/81/040058-30$02.00/0  Copyright 1981 by Academic Press, Inc. In a more psychophysical context, temporal discrimination data from animal "yes/no" paradigms illustrate a similar contrast  between linear and logarithmic time perception. The contrast arises largely from two findings in time dicrimination work. One  is Weber's Law. In this context Weber's Law requires that the discriminability of two durations remains constant at constant  ratios of these durations, regardless of their absolute values. Weber's Law may be taken to favor a log scale since constant  absolute differences on this scale correspond to constant ratios in real time.  A second and perhaps more compelling observation is that the subjective "middle" between two time values appears to lie at the  geometric mean of these values. Bisection at the geometric mean arises both in operant contexts (Stubbs, 1976) and in animal  psychophysics (e.g., Stubbs, 1968) of which the study by Church and Deluty (1977) provides the most extensive data.  This article studies several models of how the psychophysical data on bisection might arise. Before embarking on the  theoretical analysis, however, the temporal Bisection Function, which is the primary data obtained from the Church and Deluty  procedure, is described. The two major constraints on these data-Weber's Law and the geometric mean-will be seen to place  strong constraints on theory proposed to underlie the function forms.  The substance of the paper is organized in four sections. First, the bisection data and the paradigm which generates them are  described with emphasis on the two major constraints. Then four models to be studied in depth later are first briefly  described. These models result from two different discrimination processes in combination with two different subjective time  scales. The next two sections develop the theory associated with the two discrimination processes and apply it to different  time scales. The results are assessed against the bisection data.  II. EMPIRICAL BISECTION FUNCTION  The temporal Bisection Function is a function relating the discriminability of a sample duration (T) to two training  durations, one which is "short" (S), and another which is "long" (L). Animals are first trained by reinforcing a short report  ("S"), a response on one manipulandum and a long report ("L") on a second manipulandum, after being exposed to a sample  duration of S or L, respectively. If we identify the short duration with "noise," and the long duration with "signal plus  noise," this pretraining procedure will be recognized as the standard Yes/No discrimination paradigm familiar from signal  detection work.  After sufficient training on the extremes, subjects are exposed also to stimuli of intermediate duration, T, and neither  report is reinforced. After training to asymptote on this procedure subjects report "S" and "L" with relatively high accuracy  when the samples are T = S or T = L and show intermediate report probabilities for inter-  mediate stimuli. Thus, the paradigm generates a psychometric function which contains the Yes/No discrimination probabilities  as endpoints. The stimulus duration value (T,12) at which subjects are equally likely to report "S" or "L" is called the  bisection, or indifference point, and is taken to represent the subjective midpoint between S and L.  Weber's Law  A feature of the data from these experiments (as well as many others, cf. Gibbon, 1977) is that they strongly support Weber's  Law for time. Weber's Law for the bisection procedure requires that report probability for either S or L at an arbitrary  intermediate duration value, T, should equal that report probability at the intermediate value, KT, when the short and long  values are KS and KL, K a constant. Equivalently, the Bisection Functions obtained from different absolute S, L pairs in the  same ratio should superimpose when plotted as a function of the ratio of the intermediate values to either end. FOUR MODELS  The analysis which follows develops Bisection Functions corresponding to two discrimination processes under quite general  conditions. The discrimination rules in combination with alternative constructions of the subjective time scale imply  different forms for the Bisection Function. Particular interest centers on those combinations conforming to Weber's Law, the  major constraint in the data. Sufficient conditions for Weber's Law are presented and four combinations arising from the two  discrimination processes in combination with two subjective scales are studied in detail. Before the theoretical development  is undertaken, however, these four cases are briefly described in this section to provide an overview of the extremes of the  models being considered.  Long training with the S and L time durations is assumed to give rise to a noisy representation of these values on a  subjective time scale. One may think of this time scale as "remembered" time, in which variability is introduced either  directly through imprecision in appreciating each single instance of these values, or through the memory process encoding  them. For the two subjective time scales the mean subjective time is either a power function of real time (Scalar Timing) or  linear in the logarithm of real time (Log Timing).  The two discrimination processes studied correspond to two different procedures for classifying a probe stimulus, T, with a  report of "S" or "L." One of these is the familiar likelihood ratio of signal detection theory. For this process, subjects do  the equivalent of examining the odds ratio that the perceived value generated by T was in fact generated by S or L. When the  likelihood ratio exceeds a criterion based on bias and payoffs, the response for the appropriate alternative is made. The  second rule is a "similarity" comparison. On this rule, subjects compare the perceived value of T to internal referents for S  and L. When T is more similar to S than to L, a report for "S" is made. The relationship between the two versions of the time  sense is described first. Here this conception is generalized to allow subjective  time to vary proportionally in a power of real time (p), but with the scalar assumption on the subjective scale. DijTiculties with Likelihood  The likelihood ratio models require that subjects do the equivalent of comparing the odds that the percept of T was generated  by S or L. That is, the probe stimuli, T, must be interpreted subjectively as "possible" sample values from the distributions  associated with S or L. There is reason to doubt that intermediate probe durations were in fact very confusable with S and L.  Even with the rather large coefficient of varation values found in the fitting procedures, samples in the midregion between S  and L should occur very infrequently if the only stimuli represnted were in fact S and L. The likelihood ratio rule, however,  requires subjects to take a ratio of these two frequencies which themselves are experienced only a few times in a hundred over  the course of training at the endpoints.  Another difficulty with the likelihood comparison involves the subjective effect of non-reinforcement. When T is close to S  (L), subjects most frequently report "S" ("L") and are not reinforced. It is not clear what effect this event might have on  the variance of "remembered" S (L). Partial reinforcement of "S" may add variance to XS. Alternatively, non-reinforcement of  "S" might be interpreted by subjects as indicating that this percept was in fact generated by L, thus adding considerable  variance to x,.  This problem is attenuated, though not entirely solved, by relaxing the requirement that intermediate T be strictly confusable  with S and L. Then non-reinforcement may be subjectively attributed to the circumstance that T O S and T * L, rather than to  an incorrect response. Moreover, the S/L = 1/2 data make it clear that subjects can discriminate 4 sec from, say, 8 sec with  much greater precision than they appear to do when the endpoints are 4 sec and 16 sec (cf. SIMILARITY  An alternative discrimination process which captures the thrust of the idea that intermediate values are seen by subjects as  different from either end, is one based on the "similarity" of intermediate durations to training values. Imagine that, after  experiencing a sample duration, T :f- S, L, subjects attempt to discriminate whether T was in fact not generated by S. To do  this, they compare the sample percept of T to some internal referent for S. If the two are sufficiently close, the appropriate  response is "S." However, given the timing precision observed in animal subjects in other contexts, the intermediate stimuli  would be frequently discriminated as different from either endpoint. But a response of "S" or "L" is required anyway. A  discrimination rule which accomodates this requirement is one which compares the similarity of the probe stimulus to both  endpoints simultaneously. Such a rule is constructed by viewing subjects as asking whether the similarity of S to T is greater  than the similarity of T to L. This is effected in different ways for the Scalar and Log Timing systems.  As in the analysis of the likelihood rule, the general cases for changing or unchanging variability may be derived first and  then the application to our Scalar and Log Timing scales made. Since the similarity rules require subjects to access an  internal referent associated with the training durations, two natural extreme cases arise. In one, the referents may be  assumed to be located exactly at the means of the subjective scale values, so that all variability is attributed to variation  in the percept of the current sample duration. At the opposite extreme the internal referents for S and L may be assumed  variable (mnemonic variance) while the percept of the current sample is fixed. These two extremes in combination with  inconstant or constant variance provide four submodels within the similarity development. As before, the inconstant variance  cases are considered first. SUMMARY  The theoretical development for the similarity rule discrimination process has several parallels to that for the likelihood  rule which are summarized in Table 2. The inconstant variance cases require slightly more restrictive conditions for Weber's  Law  than their counterparts for the likelihood analysis. The Weber's Law conditions for the constant variance cases, however, are  the same for both discrimination processes; d must remain invariant under multiplication. The two processes differ in their  implications for the properties of the indifference point. For the likelihood ratio rule, the harmonic mean is implied for the  standard deviation of the indifference point in the inconstant variance case. For the similarity rule, the inconstant variance  cases imply subjective indifference at the geometric mean of the subjective endpoints. Subjective indifference for the  constant variance cases under both discrimination rules occurs at the arithmetic mean on the subjective scale.  The results of the these analyses eliminate all but three classes of the models considered here. Two of them have the Log  Timing subjective scale combined with either discrimination process. The third is the Scalar Timing process with the  similarity rule. Other inconstant variance models such as Poisson Timing are ruled out by Weber's Law. Scalar Timing with the  likelihood ratio rule qualifies under Weber's Law but fails to pass the second test of the data-namely, that the indifference  point lie at the geometric mean of the training stimuli. All of the similarity rule models satisfy the geometric mean  requirement.  Within the Similarity discrimination rule two extreme cases were analyzed. When all variance was assumed to reside in the  percept and none in the memory for the training durations (Referents Known Exactly), both Scalar Timing and Log Timing  appeared to fit the data equally well. When all variance was assumed to lie in the memory for the endpoints and none in the  percept (Sample Known Exactly), Log Timing still fit the data, since the form of the Bisection Function for no bias was  identical to the Referents Knwon Exactly case. The Scalar Timing function form fit the data somewhat less well for the Sample  Known Exactly case.  Unfortunately, perhaps, it is all too easy to specify reasons for a less-than-perfect fit to the data. First, the sensitivity  values identified by the fitting procedures were uniformly higher than those observed under different timing tasks (Gibbon,  1977). One difficulty, of course, is that the functions are averages, and this would tend to inflate apparent variability if  individuals differ much in location on the time scale.  A complementary problem is that the data do not achieve as sharp accuracy at the endpoints as might be expected from other  performances. One possibility is that the obtained bisection functions contain variability induced by inattention to the probe  signal duration. On such trials, subjects may respond randomly, or in favor of their bias, so that the resulting performance  is a mixture of good discrimination when subjects attend and random responding when they do not. This complication was not  elaborated here since both of our major time scales qualified on at least one of the discrimination rules. Inattention would  not affect Weber's Law, and would affect the location of the indifference point only in case of substantial bias. Thus the  major discriminations between alternatives remain robust.  Bias may be expected to vary also from trill to trial and almost surely from subject to subject. Again, this introduces a new  source of inaccuracy in performance which may be hidden in the rather high y values obtained. These as well as other sources  of variance (e.g., p) may contribute to imprecision in the discrimination  performance evident in the data. This possibility makes it difficult to draw a sharp distinction between the Referents Known  Exactly and the Sample Known Exactly cases.  Finally, the power law exponent was not discriminated as different from p = 1.0 by these analyses. For the Log Timing scale, p  is incorporated with the base of the subjective log transform (2a) and sensitivity (37b) and plays no separable role in the  dicrimination. For the Scalar Timing scale, p plays a role similar but not identical to y in the discrimination. However,  allowing p to vary as a free parameter did not improve the fit of theory to data. Thus the question of whether p < 1.0 remains  undecided, though these data do not appear to require curvature in the subjective scale.  REFERENCES  AITCHISON, J., & BROWN, J. A. C.
