We believe that some readers might be misled by subtle differences in definitions of subproportionality as outlined at the beginning and how these definitions relate to the common ratio effect (see  Table A.1  in the  Appendix ). The impression some readers might have got is that subproportionality is always a property of the probability weighting function alone that implies the common ratio effect. Note that this is not correct. When the subproportionality definition uses indifference statements between lotteries (e.g.  Prelec, 1998 ) it implicitly includes the value function. Thus, such definitions of subproportionality should be better understood as a property of the whole preference calculus and not as a property of probability weighting functions alone. We suspect that the indifference assumption is often overlooked by readers, causing the misunderstanding.

The reason why subproportionality of the probability weighting function alone is not sufficient for the prediction of the common ratio effect is easily stated. Subproportional probability weighting functions can be inverse S-shaped  or  convex ( Fehr-Duda & Epper, 2012 ). Convex probability weighting functions, however, do not overweight small probabilities and, therefore, are not able to override the risk attitude predetermined by a S-shaped value function. 
                         3 
                       
                      
                         3 
                         Technical note: If the value function is assumed to be convex over gains (in conflict with the majority of papers in the literature), a convex probability weighting function can produce both risk-averse and risk-seeking behavior. The reason is that the weighting function’s risk aversion can be stronger than the value function’s risk proclivity. We do not consider this case because it has no advantage and it contradicts diminishing marginal utility ( Pratt, 1964 ). 
                       Being able to predict both risk-averse and risk-seeking behavior is, however, imperative when it comes to the prediction of the common ratio effect for choices between equal-mean lotteries (see for examples  Table 1 , Problem 7 and 8 taken from  Kahneman and Tversky (1979) ). Consequently, we advise against the use of convex probability weighting functions to preserve Prospect Theory’s most distinctive implication — the fourfold pattern of risk attitudes.

The accommodation of the common ratio effect is fundamental for descriptive theories of decision making under risk. In CPT, subproportionality as a property of the probability weighting function aims to explain the common ratio effect. In this paper, however, we show that subproportionality of the weighting function alone does not automatically imply the common ratio effect. As a solution, we derive sufficient conditions for the common ratio effect which rely only on the characterization of the probability weighting function and are independent of the specification of the S-shaped value function. By doing this, we propose a new characteristic for probability weighting functions which we call indistinguishability of small probabilities. This property models the behavioral inclination of individuals to neglect the difference of small probabilities and to focus solely on the difference of outcomes in situations of choices among risky prospects. Furthermore, we propose the ratio of decision weights for infinitesimally small probabilities as an informative measure for the curvature of the probability weighting function at zero and, hence, the degree of distortion of small probabilities.

To guide future research, the estimation of the degree of distinguishability of small probabilities among real subjects is important because it has distinct policy implications. For example, willingness to pay for insurance contracts or choices for new medical treatments greatly vary conditional on the degree of distinguishability if tiny chances of an adverse event or side effects double. Therefore, it is imperative to understand how sensitive real decision makers are to changes of tiny probabilities in such situations.
