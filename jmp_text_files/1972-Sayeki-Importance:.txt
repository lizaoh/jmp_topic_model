JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 9, 55-65 (1972)  Allocation of Importance: An Axiom System*  YUTAKA SAYEKI  University of Washington  The proposed axiom system yields utility functions for different orderings of multidimensional objects, functions that, for  each ordering, are expressed as sums of realvalued functions, called constituent functions, weighted by their corresponding  importance measures. Each constituent function is a function specific to each dimension, and importance measures are positive  or negative real numbers. It is shown that if the axioms hold, the constituent functions remain the same for all different  orderings, while only the importance measures dictate the change of ordering. The axioms are stated in terms of a person's  choice behavior under various circumstances.  1. INTRODUCTION  This paper presents a model dealing with the changes in people's choices among multidimensional objects (things, events, and  persons) when their decision goals change from one time to another. A simple example illustrates such changes in choices among  a stable set of objects by a single person: A college senior evaluating job offers might order them one way when geographical  proximity to his fiancee was desirable and another way if the engagement were broken and proximity were undesirable.  In most discussions of utility, a utility function is defined for a particular ordering of objects. Therefore, even a minor  change in choice ordering necessitates reconstruction of the entire utility function. This assumed stability of utility  functions is unreasonable from a psychological viewpoint, and the necessity to reconstruct utility  * This research was supported by the Personnel and Training Research Programs Office of the Office of Naval Research under ONR  Contract No. NOW14-67-A-0103-0011, Contract Authorization No., NR 151-313.  t This work is based upon the author's Ph.D. dissertation at the University of Washington. The present model provides a solution to  these problems by assuming that changes of choice ordering are due solely to changes in the distribution of weights, called  importance measures, on the dimensions that define the objects.  The basic structure of the model is an extension of additive utility theory (e.g., Debreu, 1960; Luce, 1966; Luce and Tukey,  1964); utility functions are expressed as a sum of real-valued functions each weighted by their corresponding importance  measures. These real-valued functions, called constituent functions, are assumed to be specific to each of the dimensions of  the objects. Thus each object retains its particular dimension value from one decision goal to another, but the ordering of  the objects may well change if the importance measures change in response to changes in goals.  The model consists of an axiom system and the theorems derived from the axioms. The former assumes specific relations among  different orderings of objects, while the latter yield utility functions in the form of sums of constituent functions weighted  by importance measures where only the latter will be assumed to change.  Expression of utility functions as weighted sums of constituent functions has been proposed by a number of authors (e.g.,  Churchman, Ackoff, and Arnoff, 1957; Fishburn, 1965). However, they did not deal with different utility functions at different  times for a single person; so it is not justifiable, at least theoretically, to use their constituent functions for a variety  of decision goals and to account for different orderings by the same person.  Miller (1966) proposed that a "worth" index of an object be obtained by a weighted sum of "performance measures." Weights in  Miller's model were provided by individual subjects as their direct estimates for the "relative importance" of each dimension.  Performance measures indicated normalized values of physical performance data (e.g., monetary values, time, and lengths); only  when such physical measures were unavailable, subjects' direct estimates of worth were used. In Miller's model, however,  "worth" indices were not meant to be utilities and no theoretical justifications were made for the underlying assumptions of  Recently, Micko and Fischer (1970) presented a scaling model which is based upon a notion similar to that to be presented.  They assumed that a person has a multidimensional psychological space in which a similarity judgment between a pair of objects  is represented as a distance between the objects. The distance function is assumed to be a weighted sum of the differences  along each dimension defining the objects. Weights are called "importance scores" or "attention scores" and are assumed to be  different under different circumstances and from one person to another. Moreover, Micko and Fischer elaborated on the  assumption that only the differences in importance distributions yield differences in the observed orderings of objects.  However, the basic postulates presented by Micko and Fischer were stated in terms of analytic-geometric structures of  psychological spaces, and their concern was to  ALLOCATION OF IMPORTANCE: AN AXIOM SYSTEM 57  investigate the possibility of scaling repeated response data. By contrast, the axioms of the present model are stated in  terms of the person's choice behavior, and the concern is to prescribe the consistent relations between the allocations of  importance and subsequent choices among objects.
