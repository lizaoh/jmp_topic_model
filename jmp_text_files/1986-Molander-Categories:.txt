Simple, archetypical problems of discriminating between two categories of objects are used to show how multiple  equilibria may arise. INTRODUCTION  The object of the present paper is to discuss how alternative conceptions of the reality experienced by an individual are  possible. A somewhat idealized but nonetheless widespread model of concept formation is that our perceptual framework is  constantly being tested in practice, and that this practice acts as a persistent questioning and correction mechanism. It is  often tacitly assumed that sufficiently intense contact with the environment will produce unique and correct models of reality.  This view may be criticized for at least two reasons. First. there is in general no unique solution to the categorization  problem. The different color systems used in various cultures provide but one example of this non-uniqueness. Second, even  when one solution to the categorization problem can be defended as the "natural" one, the correction mechanism of everyday  practice may fail for several reasons. This has been observed for instance by Bruner, Goodnow, and Austin (1956; quoted in  Fried & Holyoak) and Bourdieu (1977, passim).  Some of these reasons are fairly well understood. One is selective exposure, that is the subject is exposed to a sample of  reality which is biased in some direction. The selection may be intended or unintended. The former case may have been somewhat  overstated traditionally, but is doubtless very common.  A second, no less important reason for persistent errors is related to the perceptual apparatus of human beings. There is, however, a third and deeper reason for failure that is inherent in the very process of conept formation. It is this  effect that is the subject matter of the following discussion. The source of the problem is that concepts are based on  information which is filtered through the same very concepts. In other words, concept formation does not consist in a simple  accumulation of data but is recursive by nature.  The group of models to be analysed below concern learning without feedback. The approach is close in spirit to that of Fried  and Holyoak (1984). There are several reasons for studying learning without feedback. Although, within a given population,  learning with explicit error correction may be very frequent, the acquisition of concepts by the population as a whole is not  guided by explicit error feedback.  There is, secondly, a large number of situations in everyday life where people do not accept tutors. This is largely the case  in political and commercial matters, where information is often distorted by various interests. In such cases, a person  receiving information from the environment must rely on his previously acquired knowledge for an appraisal of the information  Even in areas where concept formation is guided by error correction, as in children's language acquisition and later in the  socialization process, concepts are not fixed forever. Sensitivity to new information does not decline to zero, and there  remains a possibility of later switches [cf. Erikson (1954) for a discussion of this problem in the political arena]. The  analysis below will shed some light on the possible outcomes of such drastic changes.  2. ANALYTIC FORMULATION  The group of models to be analysed below are based on the dimensional approach (Smith & Medin, 1981, Chap. 5). It is assumed  that the phenomena under observation can be categorized using one or several dimensions, and that the categories to be  identified are subsets of the space spanned by these dimensions.  The process of concept formation is highly dependent on the a priori information. In some cases, the appropriate number of  categories and their approximate location in space may be assumed known, whereas in other cases even the number of categories  is unknown. The examples analysed below are intermediates in this respect.  The basic assumption of the dimensional approach is that each category is characterized by some abstract representative or  prototype, which may be modeled for instance as the average over the category in question. The extension of each category may  be controlled by variances, separating lines between the sets, etc. The models to be analysed are recursive, that is old data  are not stored in their original form but condensed into a few variables, which are adjusted to new information.  44 PER MOLANDER  The price paid for this economy of thought is the risk of errors, as will transpire from the analysis to follow. Subject to the assumptions A1 through A8, several results connecting the original algorithm (1) to the system of differential  equations (3) can be proved. Two of these are particularly interesting for the applications. The first, which should be clear  in the light of the preceding discussion, is:  (i) The paths of the process (1) asymptotically approach the trajectories of the differential equation (3) as t tends to  infinity.  A second, and far from trivial, result is:  (ii) Possible convergence points for the algorithm are the stable equilibria of Eq. (3). Convergence should be understood in  the almost sure sense.  In general, the resulting set of ordinary differential equations will not be solvable by hand computation (the examples below  are exceptions). Even so, simulation of the differential equations is far more efficient than simulation of the original  algorithm, for two reasons. One is that the stochastic character is eliminated, which reduces the necessary number of  simulation runs substantially. The second reason is that the time scale is compressed (roughly as the logarithm of the  original one), which makes each simulation run much more efficient.  46 PER MOLANDER  3. EXAMPLES  Two examples will serve to illustrate how alternative categorizations may arise. At the same time, the power of the analytical  tool developed by Ljung will be demonstrated. The computational complexity has been kept at the bare minimum of what is  needed. It should be stressed that the analysis can be carried through in far more complex situations.  EXAMPLE 1. This example has been adapted from Ljung (1977). Consider a family of objects modeled as a one-dimensional  stochastic variable Y with an unknown distribution function. The observer wishes to divide the family into two categories, A  and B, and the problem is to find a delimiter, 1, between the categories. The ideal representatives of each category are  denoted by a and b.  The classification proceeds as follows. Starting from initial estimates ao, bo, and 10, the subject makes samples at time  instants t = 1, 2,..., thus generating a stochastic process Y(t) and adjoint processes a(t), b(t), and 1(t). Each sample is  classified as an A or B object, depending on the present value of 1; if Y(t) < 1(t -1), it is classified as A, otherwise as B.  The ideals a and b are updated depending on the outcome. This may be done in several ways. A natural choice is to take the  average over each category, but other, more (or less) conservative, procedures are possible (Edwards, 1968). The delimiter l  is taken to be the mean of a and b; other choices are possible here also. Reasonable deviations from these choices made will  not alter the basic pattern of behavior.  The equations describing the process are [cf. Eqs. (2), (2')]:  a(t) =a(t-1)+c,(t) [Y(t) -a(t-1)], if Y(t)<1(t-1);  =a(t-1) if Y(t)>1(t-1);  b(t) analogously;  1(t) = (a(t) + b(t))/2. CONCLUSION  The above discussion shows that a fairly general form of classification procedures may suffer from non-uniqueness and  sometimes produce incorrect results even in presence of a continuous flow of unbiased information. The results have been shown  for the dimensional approach, but it seems fairly safe to conjecture that the same mechanism can be reproduced within the  framework of other approaches. The mathematical analysis of the problem is of course more immediate using the dimensional  approach.  As was pointed out in the Introduction, the results have some bearing also on learning with feedback. Learning with error  correction increases the probability of convergence to the correct (or traditional) point. Since any learning period is  finite, there remains the possibility of transition to other equilibria.  The remedy against errors of the type analysed here is extensive data storage. If non-recursive methods are used,  categorization can be performed with any chosen degree of accuracy. The drawback is of course increased memory loading, so a  trade-off has to be met. The hybrid solutions that appear in empirical work (Smith & Medin, 1981, passim) may well be a  reasonable compromise in this respect.  If one accepts probabilistic models of the above type as reasonable models of human category formation, a new source of  potential errors may be added to the  52 PER MOLANDER  previously studied ones. The mechanism as such can be grasped intuitively, but the mathematical analysis gives additional  insight. A non-trivial result is that small variations of environmental parameters may produce qualitative changes.
