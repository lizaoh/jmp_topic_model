On the Combinatorial Structure of the Approval-Voting Polytope 

Jean-Paul Doignon 

Université Libre de Bruxelles 

and 

Michel Regenwetter 

University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign 

Falmagne and Regenwetter (1996) proposed a probabilistic choice model of approval voting called the size-independent model. This model was investigated geometrically by Doignon and Regenwetter (1997), who introduced the approval-voting polytope associated with the choice model. In an effort to get closer to a full linear description of the polytope, we provide all edges, two-dimensional faces, and a large group of combinatorial automorphisms. Despite the new insights gathered from these results, the main challenge of listing all facets remains an open problem. © 2002 Elsevier Science (USA) 

Key Words: probabilistic choice model; approval-voting polytope; facet; combinatorial automorphism; adjacency. 

1. INTRODUCTION 

The challenge of characterizing all subset choice probabilities that are consistent with Falmagne and Regenwetter’s (1996) size-independent model for approval voting was recast by Doignon and Regenwetter (1997) as a question in discrete, convex geometry. Geometrically, the problem is to determine the facet-defining inequalities of the so-called approval-voting polytope. Doignon and Regenwetter (1997) determined a partial list of facet-defining inequalities. We start from a set C of n choice alternatives and write N for the set {1, 2, ..., n} of ranks . A ranking r: N Q C of the alternatives is a bijective mapping that assigns an object r(k) in C to each rank k from N, with r(1)=c when c is the most preferred object in C. When we write out the whole ranking we also use the notation (r(1), r(2), ..., r(n)) . A beginning set of r is a set of the form {r(1), r(2), ..., r(k)} , where 0 [ k [ n . Now we consider the real vector space R[P][(][C][)] , where P ( C ) is the powerset of C. In this space, any point p has one real coordinate pX for each subset X of C; this coordinate will be called the X coordinate . To each ranking r we associate a point vr in R[P][(][C][)] whose X coordinate equals 1 when X is a beginning set of r , and 0 otherwise. 

The approval-voting polytope A n , in short the AV-polytope, is the convex hull of the n! points vr , where r is any ranking of C. We essentially follow the geometric terminology of Grünbaum (1967) and Ziegler (1995). For simplicity, we abbreviate convex polytope into polytope . The main problem is to describe the AV-polytope A n by a minimal system of linear equations and inequalities, which, geometrically speaking, means to find all facets of A n . Similar problems have been encountered in both mathematical psychology and operations research about other polytopes, for instance the binary-choice or linear-ordering polytope (see, e.g., Koppen, 1995; Reinelt, 1985; two recent references are Borndörfer & Weismantel, 2000; Fiorini, 2001). 

Since the coordinates of each vr take only values 0 and 1, the n! points vr are exactly the vertices of A n , and the approval-voting polytope A n is by definition a 0–1 polytope. Doignon and Regenwetter (1997) showed that A n has dimension equal to 2[n] −n−1 and provided some general families of facets of A n . Here we offer auxiliary results about A n that could possibly lead to a better understanding of the family of all facets. For instance, we characterize the adjacency of vertices and the two-dimensional faces in Section 4. Doignon and Regenwetter (1997) used automorphisms to derive new facets from known ones. One of their open challenges was to find the full group of automorphisms of A n and possibly use it to derive additional facets. Besides the group S n of n! many automorphisms induced by arbitrary relabellings of the choice alternatives, that paper also took advantage of one other automorphism induced by reversing all rankings of choice alternatives. We study generalizations of the latter case by looking at all possible ways of reshuffling the positions of the choice alternatives within rankings. We show the surprising result that, for n \ 4 , there are exactly eight such automorphisms (including the reversing automorphism). The group generated by all these automorphisms is the direct product of S n and a group isomorphic to the (dihedral) group of the square. It turns out that the action of this larger group of automorphisms on the previously known facets fails to generate any new facets. Using the porta and Magma software packages (see Christof, 1996; Bosma, Cannon, & Playoust, 1997) for the cases of four and five choice alternatives, we establish that the automorphism group is of order exactly n!×8 in these two cases. 

In sum, the present paper focuses on the combinatorial structure of A n , while Doignon and Regenwetter (1997) mostly investigated facet-defining inequalities and

Any face of a polytope that is also a hypercube is called a hypercubical face . In a hypercube, the smallest face containing two diametrically opposite vertices is the hypercube itself. Hence, Theorem 1 has the following consequences. The highest dimension of a hypercubical face of A n equals N n/2 M. When n is odd, the hypercubical faces of dimension (n−1)/2 do not form a partition.

For small values of n , the graph of A n is easily described. When n=3 , it is complete. When n=4 , any vertex is nonadjacent to exactly one other vertex. The structure of the graph for n=5 , and a fortiori for n > 5 , is still to be determined. When n=5 , any vertex is nonadjacent to exactly nine vertices which form a complete subgraph. 

As for any 0–1 polytope, the two-dimensional faces of the approval-voting polytope A n are either triangles or parallelograms. Furthermore, the two cases occur in A n when n \ 4 . This can be deduced from Theorem 4 and the fact that A n−1 is affinely isomorphic to some face(s) of A n .

For n \ 4 , the reversing automorphism R and the initial switch automorphism S together generate a group D 8 isomorphic to the dihedral group of order 8 (i.e., the group of the square). A remaining open question is to determine whether all the combinatorial automorphisms of A n are affine automorphisms as well. No known automorphism is a counterexample (not even in the case n=3 ). 

In Doignon and Regenwetter (1997), the reversing automorphism was used to generate new facets from known ones. It turns out, however, that the new automorphisms generated by adding the initial switch automorphism are not helpful in this respect. They do, on the other hand, show that apparently unrelated facets, such as RSAN ({a, b}, 2) and RSAN ( C 0 {a, b}, 2) , belong in fact to the same orbit under the action of the affine automorphism group. However, when applied to the facets obtained so far, they do not produce new ones.
