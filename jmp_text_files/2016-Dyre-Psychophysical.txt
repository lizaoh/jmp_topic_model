Global psychophysics has been defined as the scaling of subjective intensity across the full dynamic range of a sensory dimension, where differences among stimuli are well above threshold ( Luce & Krumhansl, 1988 ). In one form or another, this involves determining the correspondence between subjective and physical magnitudes. This is commonly done by having the observer assign numerals to magnitudes (estimation judgments), or adjust magnitudes in correspondence to number words (production judgments). ( Marks & Gescheider, 2002 , provide a review of the various tasks.) For  magnitude estimation ,  Stevens (1957, 1975)  proposed that the psychophysical function relating each magnitude estimate,

It is hard to overstate the influence that Stevens’ work has had–and still has–on psychophysical scaling ( Marks & Gescheider, 2002 ; see also  Kello et al., 2010 ;  Petzschner, Glasauer, & Stephan, 2015 , for recent examples). However, the form of the function and the meaning of its variables and parameters have been challenged in various ways over the more than half a century since the publication of “On the psychophysical law” ( Stevens, 1957 ), the article that summarized the “new psychophysics” which Stevens had pioneered. Here we examine two such challenges: (a) the application of  separable  model forms to the description of magnitude estimation; and (b) what functional forms best describe magnitude estimation.

Addressing the first challenge, the application of separable models, it is necessary to account for the possibility that the numerals that observers assign to subjective magnitudes in estimation tasks may not represent mathematical numbers ( Narens, 1996 ). Stevens’ claim that the numbers observers produce represent subjective magnitudes was in keeping with a behaviorist viewpoint in which the psychologist considers the participant’s behavior only: “[T]he only reasonable hypothesis is that... estimates are valid measures of sensation itself” ( Stevens, 1956 , p. 24). However, it is quite conceivable that numerals assigned as subjective estimates are not mathematically veridical ( Narens, 1996 ). Another reasonable hypothesis might postulate a model in which the choice of a number–a cognitive process, presumably–is separable from the subjective magnitude of a stimulus.

Both two-stage models and separable representations thus incorporate a pair of functions, a psychophysical function representing the perceptual component of the task and a weighting (or inverse weighting) function representing the interpretation (or production) of numerals or ratios. In contrast, Stevens’ law is strictly behavioral, describing the combined effects of the perceptual and numerical transforms as a single power function relating stimulus to response. In this paper, we use the terms  two-stage  model and  separable  representation interchangeably to indicate models incorporating a pair of functions, regardless of functional form. For simplicity, in the context of magnitude estimation we refer to the psychophysical and weighting functions as  input  and  output  functions, respectively.

The second challenge to Stevens’ original formulation concerns his characterization of the psychophysical function as a power function. Following Stevens, Attneave’s two-stage model used power functions to represent each stage, as did the related empirical work of Curtis and colleagues ( Curtis, Attneave, & Harrington, 1968; Curtis & Fox, 1969; Rule & Curtis, 1977; Rule, Curtis, & Markley, 1970 ). Alternatives to power functions have been proposed. For example,  Poulton (1989)  argued that non-linearities in the values of the numeric responses represented a set of biases in the use of numbers. Poulton argued that if units of subjective intensity were well known to the participant (e.g., square inches), then the function will be linear. On the other hand, if there are no familiar units (e.g., brightnesses) then participants will revert to units of discriminability (i.e., just noticeable differences or JNDs). When these subjective units are plotted against stimulus magnitude, the resulting function is logarithmic, in accordance with Fechner’s law ( Fechner, 1860 ).

Some studies (e.g.,  Steingrimsson & Luce, 2005 ;  Steingrimsson, 2009 ) have used joint presentation of two pairs of stimuli, in which the observer adjusts the subjective summation of one pair to match another (a matching task). However, it is also of interest to examine  
                         p 
                      -additivity in the context of a JME task, since it is a special case of joint presentation, in which the observer provides a numeric estimate of the subjective summation of a pair of stimuli.

To summarize, JME provides a method that allows an assessment of the influence of input and output transformations on subjective estimates, but also requires some consideration of the method the observer uses to combine the stimulus pair subjectively. JME allows an examination of sequential transformations. There are different “realizations of joint presentation” ( Luce, 2002 , p. 521). Subjective summation occurs when loudnesses are presented to the two ears, but  Luce (2002)  also noted that it may be realized when loudnesses are presented sequentially or brightnesses patches are spatially separated. Models in which a transformation (e.g., the exponent of a power function) is applied before or after the summation of the stimulus pair can be examined with JME.

Bernasconi et al. (2008)  examined how well different functional model forms fit to ratio estimation judgments of distances and areas. They found strong support for separable representations, but also claimed that multiplicativity held, so that power model forms were found to fit as well (and sometimes better) despite fewer parameters, than a model based on the ( Prelec, 1998 ) function. Notably, however,  Bernasconi et al. (2008)  used generic model forms in a number of cases. Their most successful models were SEP (which was entirely generic in form) and RAT (generic on input side, power on output). This outcome is perhaps not surprising when these models are so flexible. Nonetheless the comparison of model forms by Bernasconi et al., including the recent forms proposed by  Luce (2002) , was an important contribution and helped shape our approach.

With a two-stage model applied to the responses from JME, we can consider a variety of functional forms on both the input and output side. We can consider linear, logarithmic, and power forms for the psychophysical function, and linear, logarithmic, power, and Prelec forms for the weighting function. The factorial combination of the input and output model forms has rarely been considered. Moreover, we can assess whether subjective summation is equivalent to mathematical addition or not. We believe that these properties of our approach are novel.

Along these lines, we sought a test of specific separable model forms, using the JME paradigm. Questions of model parsimony become apparent, with power and Prelec functions having a greater number of parameters than linear forms. We may find that linear forms are sufficient for the input (or output) transformation if there is non-linearity for the complementary transformation. We may also find the use of various functional forms on input and output side varies across observers. It is important to consider the potential for individual differences in the use of the different forms, in the manner of  Bernasconi et al. (2008)

Model selection methods

Historically, it has been somewhat problematic to choose among competing quantitative models that vary in terms of the number of parameters. Models with more parameters tend to be more flexible and can generally fit a given data set better than those with fewer parameters. The improvement in goodness of fit (GOF) has to be considered with respect to the parsimony of the model: that is, GOF and parsimony tend to trade off. Moreover, even when the number of parameters is the same, some functional forms are inherently more flexible (i.e., generalizable) than others. In recent years, there have been important developments in methods for selecting among different quantitative models. In particular, the development of Bayesian information criteria (BIC), Akaike information criteria (AIC), and corrected AIC (AICc) has helped to formalize the trade-off between number of parameters and goodness of fit ( Akaike, 1974; Cavanaugh, 1997; Schwarz, 1978 ). These methods, however, do not capture the generalizability of different model forms. Generalizability is an important consideration in model selection because a more flexible model can fit a particular data set better given that it generally fits more data sets, leading to the risk of overfitting ( Pitt, Myung, & Zhang, 2002 ). The development of the minimum description length (MDL) has fully addressed model complexity (both in terms of number of parameters and model generalizability) and combines it with a GOF measure such that when multiple models are fit against the same data set, the model producing the smallest MDL value “wins” ( Pitt et al., 2002 ). The formal comparison of various quantitative model forms becomes tractable using these methods.

An archival data set:     
                         Ramsay (1979)

We were fortunate to have available to us raw data from the third experiment of  Ramsay (1979) , which were provided to us by the author. The Ramsay experiment presented participants with a pair of polygons on each trial. Participants were asked to assign a number to the subjective sum of the areas. In the conventional approach of the day, Ramsay fit a two-stage power model to the data, and reported the input and output exponents for each participant. However, he reported “a strong interaction” (p. 498) between the two exponents and the coefficient, and further noted that this interaction made it difficult to detect when the output exponent did not equal unity. As a result, the input exponents that Ramsay reported were calculated with the output exponent held to unity. Thus, we do not know what the input exponents would have been if the output exponents had been allowed to vary. Even when the output exponent was said not to vary significantly from unity, the table of results (p. 498) shows that the output exponents varied considerably (between 0.81 and 1.57).

A number of questions naturally arise when considering the results of the ( Ramsay, 1979 ) experiment. Did the two-stage model fit better than the model having a fixed output exponent? If the output exponents varied, it is conceivable that this variation would affect the values of the input exponents. It is hard to interpret the reported exponent values as a result. Could other functional forms have fit better? Certainly we would want to examine the super-additive and Prelec forms. Ramsay’s approach assumed mathematical addition in the model form—is that assumption justified? Could a logarithmic function ( Poulton, 1989 ) have fit the data just as well? Judgments of area have been shown to produce a compressive functional form, consistent with a power function with an exponent less than unity ( Teghtsoonian, 1965 ); the fit of a compressive logarithmic function should also be examined.

Overview

In this article, we re-analyze the JME data from  Ramsay (1979) , Experiment 3. We fit a variety of functional forms for the separable model: in particular, we consider the factorial combination of linear, logarithmic, and power, model forms for the psychophysical function (input side), and linear, logarithmic, power, and Prelec forms for the weighting function (output side). We also examined models for which subjective summation was equivalent to mathematical addition, or for which subjective summation produces super-additivity. We use extant model selection methods to choose among competing computational models (e.g., AIC, BIC, MDL). We treat each participant’s data on an individual basis. We then consider the implications for psychophysical scaling.

xIC selection

The results for the xIC model selection were the same as the MDL results for four participants. We focus here on those that changed. In four of the five cases, a model with a Prelec output function was chosen (over either a power or linear output function with MDL). In the fifth case, a model with a power output function was chosen (over a linear output function). On the input side, a power function was replaced by a super-additive function, and a log function was replaced by a linear function. Thus, the main difference between the models selected by MDL and by xIC was the selection of models with a Prelec output function.

Nonetheless, there was good evidence for a power function under xIC, either on the input (5 of 9) or output side (3 of 9). There was good evidence for a linear stage for 5 of the 9 participants, mostly on the input side. The log model was not chosen under xIC selection, and the subjective summation of the stimulus pair did not appear to differ from mathematical addition, except in one case.

What model selection method should we use ?

The xIC method selected a greater number of models with a Prelec function than did MDL. It is important to recognize that by GOF measures, the Prelec models selected by xIC were superior. The xIC method also takes the number of parameters into account, and still selected models incorporating a Prelec function, despite the greater number of parameters. However, as shown in  Table 4 , models incorporating a Prelec function had greater complexity than corresponding power models. According to MDL, any improved fit has to beat the penalty a model must pay due to its complexity.

If we are not concerned about the risk of overfitting (i.e., that Prelec can fit more data sets) then we should choose xIC. However, if overfitting is a concern, then MDL provides the best model selection solution. A stimulus range greater than that used by  Ramsay (1979)  may help determine if models with Prelec can win with MDL. With the larger range, there is perhaps greater opportunity for the double bends in Prelec to take effect.

Comparing the model selection results to Ramsay’s analysis

The MDL results are probably most comparable to the values obtained by  Ramsay (1979) , since MDL was more likely to select power or linear models. Our input exponent (averaged across participants) was greater than Ramsay’s (0.89 vs. 0.71) but our output exponent was also greater (1.38 vs. 1.12). Since our mean estimates are based only on those participants where models incorporating a power or linear function were selected based on MDL (rather than based on all participants, regardless of whether other models should have been selected), they should be better estimates of the parameters in question. Furthermore, since the input exponents reported by Ramsay were calculated with a fixed output exponent of unity, they do not represent the input exponent values when the output exponent is allowed to vary, as ours do.

In this article, we tested a set of functional forms for separable models. We fit the models to data from a JME task, in which participants estimated the combined area of a pair of polygons ( Ramsay, 1979 ). To determine the functional forms, we needed to specify how Luce’s separable representation ( Luce, 2002, 2004 ) could be applied to the magnitude estimation task, and specifically JME. We used model selection methods to choose the best model for each participant.

The form of the psychophysical and weighting functions varied across participants. Functional forms including a power function for input or output were selected in 8 of 9 cases, regardless of model selection method. Linear functions were also selected often (6 of 9 cases for MDL; 5 of 9 for xIC), on either the input or output side. However, the same functional form on both input and output sides was either rare (PowerPower) or non-existent (LinearLinear). There was virtually no evidence in support of logarithmic functions, despite the claims of  Poulton (1989)  that such functions generally underlie magnitude estimation tasks.

The xIC results suggest a strong need to consider the Prelec function on the output side. Even though the MDL results showed a Prelec function for only one participant, it is important to recognize that MDL rejected these models due to their greater flexibility, not because they did not fit the data. If we are to use MDL to choose among models of different complexity, the challenge for a more complex model like Prelec is to increase the goodness of fit enough to overcome the penalty it pays for being more flexible.

It is also clear that the two parameters in the Prelec function tend to trade off.

Finally, we can ask how robust particular models are to individual differences among observers. The best form varied considerably across participants. This suggests that it is inadvisable to fit a single model across a group of participants.

On axiomatizers and scalers

Within the domain of psychophysical measurement, some have described a division between axiomatizers and scalers (e.g.,  Ellermeier & Faulhammer, 2000 ;  Luce & Krumhansl, 1988 ). Axiomatizers (e.g., Luce, Narens, Steingrimsson) argue that if a body of observations satisfies certain axioms, then a particular numerical structure can be used to summarize them. In contrast, scalers (Gescheider, Marks, Stevens) obtain numerical assignments from observers, and then fit models to data, deriving parameter values. Although the current work is rooted in the scaling tradition, like  Bernasconi et al. (2008) , we see the value and importance of the models proposed by  Luce (2002, 2012) . We view the current analysis as a natural complement to the recent empirical work by  Steingrimsson and Luce (2005, 2006)  and  Steingrimsson (2009, 2011) , who tested the implications of particular axioms for the measurement of loudness and brightness, respectively.  Cox’s (1990)  distinction between substantive and empirical models seems relevant here: we see our work and that of Bernasconi et al. as testing substantive models arising from theory, whereas for Stevens the search for a model was more empirically driven.
