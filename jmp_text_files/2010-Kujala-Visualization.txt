Acquisition of reading skill is based on learning to connect units of written language to spoken language. It is widely agreed that children who have difficulties in learning to read have phonological deficits which hinder the development of the ability to manipulate in mind the phonemic units of language (see  Vellutino, Fletcher, Snowling, and Scanlon (2004)  for a recent review). Atypical processing of speech sounds can be observed in children with a familial risk for dyslexia already at infancy (for a review of evidence, see  Lyytinen, Leppänen, Richardson, and Guttorm (2003) , for genetic background, see, e.g.,  Taipale et al. (2003) ). Thus, early identification of the at-risk children is feasible ( Lyytinen et al., 2006 ) and preventive practices to minimize the effects of such deficits are called for.

Literate ( Lyytinen, Ronimus, Alanko, Poikkeus, & Taanila, 2007 ) (known as “Ekapeli” in Finland) is a computer game designed for prevention of reading disability of Finnish children. Underlying the graphics and other features that makes it a game, the player’s task essentially consists of repeated multiple-choice trials, where letters (or longer graphemic sequences) are given as visual choices and the target to be chosen is indicated by an auditory presentation of the corresponding phoneme (or phonemic sequence). Immediately after each selection, brief feedback is given to facilitate learning. The number and content of multiple-choice options are adapted based on the player’s performance so as to maintain an appropriate level of difficulty. As the Finnish writing system is practically 100% consistent (each letter has its own phoneme and each phoneme its own letter), learning the core knowledge of reading by this method should be possible.

In this article, our aim is to analyze and visualize a child’s performance in the Literate game. In particular, we consider methods for estimating and visualizing a child’s confusion (connecting incorrect graphemes to phonemes) as well as any changes in the confusion behavior. Such analysis can provide insights into a child’s phonological deficits. By further analyzing the confusion data, it can be investigated whether the confusions are due to visual similarities (e.g., R–P), phonetic similarities (e.g., k–g), or for some other reason. Furthermore, changes in the confusion behavior over time indicate different stages in learning.

As the game is adaptive, analysis of the game data should be based on principled statistical methods to avoid inconsistent estimates. E.g., one of the design goals of the game is to maintain around 80% success rate for keeping the child motivated, which means that any performance indicators based on estimates of the success rate will measure the success of the adaptation algorithm more than that of the child.

In a previous work ( Vilenius, Kujala, Richardson, Lyytinen, & Okamoto, 2007 ), Bayesian models of the Literate game task were proposed based on the assumption of a binary confusability matrix of the game items. Due to computational constraints, however, the estimation procedure was limited to game sessions with a small number of different game items.

Our present approach is more general in that we allow any number of game items and do not impose any falsifiable constraints on the pairwise confusion probabilities. Thus, the present work does not propose a new model; its validity depends only on the choice axiom through which the pairwise choice probabilities are generalized to larger choice-sets. In future work, more complex models can be built on top of this framework.

In the following sections, we first present a simple general method for consistent estimation of the confusion probabilities between certain items in an adaptive multiple-choice task satisfying the choice axiom. Then, we apply the method to the game data of Finnish children who are in the early stages of learning to read, and consider visualization of the estimated confusability matrices as well as learning effects. We evaluate the empirical validity of the choice axiom for these data. In an appendix, we compare the simple method to fully Bayesian inference under the same model as well as to the minimum variance unbiased estimators which are optimal in the classical frequentist sense.

In this section, we apply the general estimation formulation of the previous section to the data collected using the Literate game.

The game data consists of sessions of around 100 multiple-choice trials, each trial having 2–9 option letters (or longer graphemic sequences). In the analysis below, we consider each player’s data simply as a long ordered sequence of trials, ignoring the session boundaries, the actual times of the trials, as well as the details of the adaptation algorithm used to choose the contents of the trials. The validity of the choice axiom for these data is investigated in Section  4 .

Remark 2 
                         It should be noted that especially in a fast-paced game, the fundamental assumption that each response to given target and choice-set is identically distributed and independent from the preceding trials is suspect. However, as the adaptation logic never presents the same target twice in a row and randomizes the order of the choices on screen, we expect that any sequential effects will average out without yielding significant biases.

The observed confusions of certain items are often explained by their closeness within the subject’s “perceptual space”. Motivated by such models, we visualize high confusion probability of a certain target to a certain distractor by plotting the distractor close to the target. However, as any transformation of the discrimination probabilities into perceptual distances generally does not correspond to any two-dimensional spatial layout of the items (in particular, the confusion probabilities are generally not symmetric), we plot a separate small graph for each target showing its distance to each of the distractors. The shaded areas are colored according to the estimated discrimination probability. The color interpolates from red to green as the estimated discrimination probability increases from 0% to 100%. The color information is redundant as the plotted distance of the distractor gives the same information, but because color is perceived preattentively, it allows one to quickly locate specific difficulties of a child from a huge array of these plots.

The simple estimation method assumes that the discrimination probabilities do not change over time. This is clearly an inappropriate assumption for a learning game which is designed to specifically induce such changes. If the estimates are computed over a long span of trials, they will only indicate the average performance and lose the temporal details.

To assess learning effects, a long span of trials can be split into several time intervals, each of which is plotted separately.  Fig. 4 
                          shows an example where the first half and second half of the sequence of trials are plotted separately, superimposed on top of each other. The earlier data are plotted with less saturated colors so that the most recent performance will stand out from the plots.

If the presentation frequencies of different targets and distractors vary over time, it may be difficult to split the sequence of trials into intervals so that there would be enough data to estimate all discrimination probabilities in each interval. If that is the case, the splitting can be done separately for the sequence of results for each target–distractor pair. This allows balancing the temporal accuracy with the accuracy of the individual estimates, but decouples the evolutions of the discrimination probabilities of different target–distractor pairs from each other.

Ideally, an explicitly dynamic model would be used for estimation of the learning effects, but such models require more complicated estimation procedures and additional assumptions about the learning dynamics.

The playing times of different children vary greatly; some continue even after reaching near perfect performance, some stop early for unknown reasons. Thus, a child’s average performance over all her playing time has a random component due to the random length of the playing time. Furthermore, the children also learn outside the game, and these data generally come from uncontrolled playing situations with varying times between the sessions. Therefore, we focus only on the beginning portion of each child’s performance where ceiling has not yet been reached and the typical difficulties are visible.

Fig. 5 
                          plots the performance of about 2600 children during their first 15  exposures  to each target–distractor pair (recall that a trial counts as an exposure if it had the specific target and distractor and the answer was one of them). The graphical indication of the accuracy of the estimates is not used here because each estimate is always based on the same number of exposures. Instead, the children’s distribution of confusion counts over the 15 exposures to each target–distractor pair is indicated by a density plot. The plotted distance of each distractor letter corresponds to the mean of this distribution.

The numbers in the figure indicate the number of children each density plot is based on. These numbers are much lower than the total 2600 as most of the children do not have enough trials to estimate the confusabilities of all target–distractor pairs. There are in fact 9168 children who have started playing, but only 2600 of those have had the required 15 exposures to at least one target–distractor pair.

It appears that the confusability distributions are unimodal, i.e. there is no clear-cut division of the children into those who confuse and those who do not confuse a given target–distractor pair. However, the first 15 exposures may easily constitute an hour or more of playing time and therefore each child’s confusion probabilities may have changed during that time, evening out a bimodal distribution. As the 15 trials is close to the minimum that allows any accuracy of estimation of a single child’s confusion probability, the temporal accuracy can only be increased by averaging over several children (and thereby losing the individual differences).

Nothing prevents the use of the proposed algorithm for estimation of average performance from the combined results of a group of subjects, as long as it is kept in mind that such average estimates will be weighted by the proportions in which the trials came from each subject.

Figs. 6 and 7 
                         
                          plot the combined results of the 2600 children, showing how the children’s performance plotted in  Fig. 5  develops within the first 15 exposures to each target–distractor pair. For each target–distractor pair, the data of those children who had at least 15 exposures to that pair are combined within four time intervals (1st, 2nd–3rd, 3rd–7th, and 8th–15th exposures) and the group average performance in each interval is plotted (as a circle). That way, the plotted estimates for a given target–distractor pair are always uniform averages of the same subset of children, yielding a true indication of the average learning effects within that subset.

However, the data for different target–distractor pairs may come from different subsets of children and are therefore strictly speaking not directly comparable to each other. In particular, as most children do not have the 15 exposures to any given target–distractor pair, it is possible that the subset of children used for each pair may emphasize those who had trouble with that pair and therefore required more repetitions of the target than others.

In this section, we consider validity of the choice axiom in the task of the “Literate” game.

Experiments reported by  Clarke (1957)  with monosyllable and digit stimuli provide support for the choice axiom. However, there is also evidence against it as a general rule in the contexts of letter and word identification ( Rouder, 2004 ) and categorization of certain composite stimuli ( Wills, Reimers, Stewart, Suret, and McLaren, 2001 ). The phonemic stimuli used by Clarke are probably the closest match to the task in the “Literate” game as the phonetic part of the game task (as opposed to the visual search task) is probably the most significant source of confusions. Thus, the affirmative results of Clarke are encouraging, but in our application, due to the adaptive game environment and the fact that the players are children (who might even have phonological or other deficits), ecological validity of the results of any well-controlled experiments is highly questionable. Thus, it is best if we can evaluate the validity of the choice axiom using the game data itself.

Unfortunately, there is no straightforward way to test the choice axiom from the game data, because there are too few trials to obtain accurate estimates of a single player’s discrimination probability of a given target–distractor pair separately in different contexts of extra choices—this is why we needed to apply the choice axiom in the first place. However, we can compute averages over several players as in Section  3.4 , but we have to be careful to avoid certain subtle ways the estimates may become inconsistent.

In any case, the systematic violation that we observe is small; it has little significance in our application. As per  Luce (1977) , the conditions under which the choice axiom holds are easily disturbed, but the axiom remains a useful approximation for many tasks, including the present one.

We have presented a simple method for estimating and visualizing the confusion probabilities of target–distractor pairs in a multiple choice recognition task. Applying Luce’s choice axiom, the method is able to remain consistent under arbitrarily varying adaptive content of multiple-choice trials. While the choice axiom often holds only approximately, and may fail for some tasks, it is very simple and satisfies the basic requirement that perfect performance should yield 100% discrimination estimates and random selection 50% estimates regardless of the number and content of the choices.

We have considered how to visualize the confusability estimates computed from children’s performance in the “Literate” game. Several visual techniques are applied to make the plots easily graspable. The plots provide a quick overview of an individual’s difficulties. They have been used in providing feedback on learning for children, parents and teachers as well as for research purposes. These plots clearly indicate the possible bottlenecks and origins (visual or phonetic similarity) for the difficulties in learning certain letter-sound correspondences, and this information has been further utilized in schools for focusing the teaching on disentangling these particular bottlenecks. In the future, the goal is to use the measurement information directly in the adaptation logic of the game. Thus, while maintaining the goal of around 80% success rate, the remaining 20% of challenging trials can be automatically focused on the specific difficulties of each child, as revealed by the real-time measurement. That way, the benefits of individualized support can most realistically be made available to all children that are in need of it.

The present estimation method does not make any prior assumptions about the similarities of the items, and therefore it naturally needs examples of each target–distractor pair to be able to estimate its confusability. For small amounts of items, this is not a problem, but e.g., to estimate the confusability matrix of the 23 Finnish letters, there are 506 independent variables that need to be estimated, and therefore thousands of trials are needed for accurate estimation of the full matrix.

In this article, we have assumed that the data already exist and have not considered how to best choose the contents of the trials. Better estimation efficiency could be obtained by choosing the trials specifically for the purpose of measurement (see  Kujala & Lukka, 2006  for a general formulation). Another area for improvement is modeling of learning effects, which has now been considered only as an afterthought in a static model.

Nonetheless, the clear principle and ease of implementation of the proposed simple method should make it useful.

Surprisingly, the full Bayesian formulation appears to yield systematically slightly downwards biased estimates of high discrimination probabilities. While this bias is reasonably small compared to the variance, it can easily become significant if, e.g., several estimates are averaged, as we did in Section  4 . Thus, it is more straightforward to use the simple method in further statistical analyses than the biased point estimates of the fully Bayesian approach. Although it is usually the case in multiparameter models that minimization of the estimation bias leads into counterproductive increases of variance ( Gelman, Carlin, Stern, & Rubin, 2004 ), in our problem, the simple method achieves its unbiasedness with little adverse effect on the variance. This is the case because the model is simple. If the parameters were assumed to be a priori interdependent in some way, any unbiased estimators might not be able to exploit this prior information, yielding significantly larger estimation errors than the Bayesian formulation.

The fully Bayesian formulation, while in a certain sense ideal, does lose the desirable property of unbiasedness. The question arises whether it is possible to improve the simple estimates without introducing any bias. In this section, we answer this question affirmatively.

In practice, the MVUE does not seem very attractive for three reasons:  
                            
                               1. 
                               The difference between the simple estimates and the MVUEs appears to be fairly small except when there are only few trials. 
                            
                            
                               2. 
                               The number of different possible sequences that need to be averaged over may grow exponentially with the number of trials. Thus, the MVUE will run into computational problems soon without some nontrivial optimizations. 
                            
                            
                               3. 
                               The MVUE yields just a point estimate; there is no obvious principled way of applying the Rao-Blackwellization to the uncertainty distributions of the simple estimates. 
                            
                          The MVUE may be useful if several estimates, each based on a small number of trials, need to be averaged in some further analyses.
