The continuous and stunning development of computers allows researchers to collect data of unprecedented size and complexity. An immediate consequence of this fact is that, in many real-life studies, even if only one variable is observed, the collected information could not be conveniently expressed with a single numerical value or category as in the case of classical data, but would be more appropriately represented by means of multiple values such as lists, intervals, frequency distributions, or histograms, among others (see, for instance,  Billard & Diday, 2003 ).

In particular, interval-valued data are a natural extension of numerical data when the outcomes of the considered experiments might be better described by subsets of the real line instead of single-point values. Although interval-valued data have historically been treated in the context of fuzzy and set-valued models, this type of data might also be the direct outcome of specific experimental setups. Specifically, one often finds real-life experiments involving interval-valued data, i.e, experimental setting for which the outcomes can be measured only with some imprecision which is reflected by an interval of real values (a set of values ranging between a minimum and a maximum) rather by a single-point value (see, for instance,  Blanco-Fernández & Winker, 2016  for a detailed review of different data generation processes resulting in interval-valued data).

In this respect, interval-valued data are an effective tool for capturing richer information when measuring intrinsically imprecise-valued human attributes that cannot be directly assessed with exactness (say, for instance, intelligence, personality, anxiety, opinions, or emotions, among others). Additionally, it is worth mentioning that such data may be also useful, among others, for quantifying fluctuations or ranges along a period of time (e.g., the daily fluctuation of the blood pressure of a patient, the tidal daily fluctuation in a specific area, the monthly salary range of a worker over a year, and so on). Moreover, they can also correspond to aggregate information obtained from a cross-sectional setting (e.g., the Federal Open Market Committee – in short, FOMC – members’ individual forecasts with regard to some variable of interest are aggregated by publishing only a range of their expectations to maintain confidentiality of individual opinions or to deal with the situation of frequent changes in the composition of the cross section, including the one due to no response). Likewise, in many surveys respondents are asked to indicate the interval containing sensitive information as their age, salary, etc. in order to guarantee, for instance, certain level of anonymity and make them feel more comfortable when answering.

In this sense, some empirical studies (see, for instance,  Ellerby & Wagner, 2021; Ellerby et al., 2022 ) have shown that participants of questionnaire-based studies feel more comfortable when they are allowed to express the inherent uncertainty of their responses while they are evaluating such kind of abstract variables by selecting a whole range of values rather than being constrained to choose a single answer among a few pre-specified ‘linguistic values’ as in Likert-type scales ( Likert, 1932 ) or within a bounded interval as in visual analogue ones ( Freyd, 1923; Hayes & Patterson, 1921 ). For this reason, interval-valued scales ( Themistocleous et al., 2019; Wagner et al., 2015 ) are becoming an efficient alternative to conventional single-point rating scales in social and educational sciences and many other disciplines. Furthermore, this way of collecting data ensures that, unlike the traditional Likert-type and visual analogue scales, the usual individual differences and the genuine imprecision involved in human assessment are not overlooked (see,  Lubiano et al., 2016a  in a more general context).

The appearance of new types of real-life data that cannot be strictly classified as quantitative or qualitative ones, such as those mentioned above, forces statistical data analysis methodology to be constantly evolving. Up to now, some classical statistical methods have already been extended, among others, to deal with more general experimental scenarios, in which the outcomes are no longer real numbers, but interval-valued, symbolic or fuzzy data. More concretely, different approaches have been suggested for performing hypothesis testing for these types of data, but no attempt has been made as far as it is known in order complement/enhance the results of such inferential technique with some effect size measures.

The exponential growth of scientific publications across diverse disciplines, coupled with the increasing variety of information sources and access channels, underscores the importance of systematically integrating findings from primary studies. Synthesizing quantitative evidence allows researchers to construct more robust theoretical frameworks, identify overarching trends and potential inspiring insights, and draw well-supported, generalizable conclusions that inform both theory and practice.

The first attempts to develop quantitative methods to integrate research studies based on null hypothesis significance testing (NHST) date back to the 1930s, with  Fisher (1932)  and  Neyman and Pearson (1933)  as pioneers contributing to the behavioral sciences by providing a common language and making decisions based on quantitative measures more rigorous. However, this methodological approach did not arouse the interest of reviewers in social and behavioral disciplines until the 1970s, when  Glass (1976)  coined the term “meta-analysis” to refer to the statistical analysis of all the results obtained in different empirical studies related with the same topic and which were to be analyzed together. The problem with single studies rely on the fact that NHST uses the  
                         p 
                       value to reject or not reject a hypothesis. Therefore, the  
                         p 
                       value only allows to determine whether an experimental effect is non-zero.

One way of overcoming some of the limitations of NHST ( Sterling, 1959 ) consists in reporting certain supplementary information, such as effect size measures.  Glass (1976)  has been primarily responsible of the introduction of effect size measures for the quantitative integration of research results.  Cohen (1977, pp. 9–10)  highlighted the advantages of effect size over statistical significance pointing out that “It is convenient to use the term effect size to indicate the degree of presence of the phenomenon. If the null hypothesis is false, it is false to some specific degree, i.e., the effect size is a specific non-zero value in the population. The larger this value, the greater the degree of manifestation of the phenomenon under study”. Furthermore, the estimation of effect sizes is essential for the interpretation of the results of a study because if the null hypothesis is not rejected, it is often interpreted as an evidence of no effect. However, when a non-significant result is obtained, the result is only inconclusive ( Cohen, 1990 ). For this reason, including effect sizes is also effective when interpreting non-significant results.

Consequently, for many decades now, data analysts have been encouraging ( Cohen, 1965; Hays, 1963 ) to enhance the presentation of research findings in behavioral sciences by including an effect size measure along with a statistical significance test. Besides, the American Psychological Association (APA) Publication Manual stated “It is almost always necessary to include some measure of effect size in the Result section” ( American Psychological Association, 2001, 2010  p. 25) and  Cooper (1984)  highlighted the importance of including effect size measures when conducting information integration research (meta-analyses or systematic reviews), due to the misuse and misinterpretation of NHST.

In particular, when the desired analysis involves the comparison of the population means of two different groups, the standardized mean difference is one of the most common effect size measures used to complement the information provided by the  
                         p 
                       value of the considered two-sample-based hypothesis testing methods.

In the case of interval-valued data, several methodologies have been suggested for comparing the means of different groups from an inferential viewpoint (see, for instance,  Brito & Duarte Silva, 2011; Hesamian, 2015; Roy & Klein, 2020 ). In this sense, although different techniques could be applied to solve such hypothesis testing problem, the one considered in this paper follows a distance-based approach, which substantially simplifies the analysis and interpretation of the obtained results by making use of the ideas of  Fréchet (1948)  for random elements taking values on metric spaces. Nevertheless, no effect size has yet been proposed for this comparison.

Therefore, the main aim of this paper will be to extend the definition of one of the best-known families of standardized mean difference effect size measures, the so-called  
                         d 
                       family, as far as interval-valued data are handled and such distance-based approach is considered. To this purpose, some previous results in connection with hypothesis testing about the equality of the so-called Aumann-type means ( Blanco-Fernández & Liao, 2015; Montenegro et al., 2008; Nakama et al., 2010; Ramos-Guajardo & Blanco-Fernández, 2017; Ramos-Guajardo et al., 2014, 2020 ) and Fréchet-type variances ( Grzegorzewski, 2018; Ramos-Guajardo & González-Rodríguez, 2013 ) of two samples of interval-valued data are to be reviewed.

Besides, the two-sample-based bootstrap algorithms for testing the equality of the Aumann type means and Fréchet-type variances for interval-valued data from two different groups are to be explained in this section. The extension of several indices of Cohen’s

The above considered population and sample Aumann-type mean and Fréchet-type variance preserve all the main properties from the numerical case (see, for instance,  Corral et al., 2011; García-García et al., 2025 ). Therefore, these measures can be considered as suitable estimates of central tendency and dispersion, respectively.

When statistical techniques are to be developed for interval-valued data, it should be taken into account the lack of ‘universally accepted’ total order between intervals, the impossibility of obtaining a well-defined difference operator that preserve all the properties from the real-valued case, and the absence of realistic and wide parametric models for the distribution of IVRSs following the distance-based approach of this paper. For these reasons, asymptotic techniques are to be considered and  
                            
                               
                                  d 
                               
                               
                                  θ 
                               
                            
                          plays a crucial role due to the fact that through this metric the space of nonempty compact real intervals can be isometrically embedded into a convex cone of a Hilbert space of functions endowed with the usual functional arithmetic and a certain metric (see, for instance,  González-Rodríguez et al., 2006  for details in a more general context). However, in general it is not possible to directly apply the so-called functional data analysis because there are no guarantees that the results take values within the aforementioned convex cone and it is necessary to develop  ad hoc  procedures.

It should be emphasized that many statistical studies with IRVSs have been treated as a particular case of those suggested in a more general situation with fuzzy random sets.

Testing methods for Aumann-type means with fuzzy (vector or number-valued) data have been obtained by first developing a functional data analysis approach and later particularizing them also thanks to the (distance-based) bootstrapped Central Limit Theorem for generalized spaces suggested by  Giné and Zinn (1990) . The asymptotic/ bootstrap procedures obtained in this way make use of the same fundamentals as those methods used for real-valued data. Thus, most statistical conclusions are based on the computation of the  
                            p 
                          value, which make full sense in this random fuzzy sets-based methodology. Furthermore, the obtained methods have been proved to be consistent, and they can be suitably applied even with small or moderate sample sizes ( González-Rodríguez et al., 2012 ).

The two-sample-based procedures for testing the equality of two Aumann-type means (from independent and paired data) and Fréchet-type variances have been developed to deal with general fuzzy-valued data (see, for instance,  Colubi, 2009; Lubiano et al., 2016b; Ramos-Guajardo & Lubiano, 2012 ). Therefore, the corresponding bootstrapped algorithms will be particularized to interval-valued data in the following sections.

In the next section, the preceding standardized mean difference effect size measures are to be applied on a dataset obtained from a real-life situation.

The case study addressed in this work was suggested on an educational innovation project (Reference PINN-20-B-011) during the 2020/2021 academic year at the University of Oviedo (Spain) for the planning and improvement of teaching–learning processes of a course of the Bachelor’s Degree in Labor Relations and Human Resources at the University of Oviedo (Spain) for the 2020/2021 academic year.

The COVID-19 pandemic promoted a big change in Higher Education due to the need to face a new scenario where the traditional face-to-face lessons were suddenly replaced by the online teaching modality. Although this accelerated and forced transition took place in an environment of great uncertainty, this experience can be used to improve teaching and learning processes in university studies.

For this reason, the rigorous analysis of students’ reactions to the teaching modality (face-to-face vs. online) and the learning method (expository lectures vs. project-oriented collaborative learning classes) is particularly interesting. For this purpose and in order to adopt the necessary measures for a better development of the aforementioned course, a study with the following goals is presented:

(i) 
                               Compare students’ reactions to online and face-to-face teaching modalities as well as to expository lectures and the project-oriented collaborative learning method. 
                            
                            
                               (ii) 
                               Analyze the influence of respondent’s gender (men vs. women) in students’ reactions for both teaching modalities and both learning methods.

Compare students’ reactions to online and face-to-face teaching modalities as well as to expository lectures and the project-oriented collaborative learning method.

A total of 50 of the 56 students registered for a course of the Bachelor’s Degree in Labor Relations and Human Resources for the 2020/2021 academic year at the University of Oviedo participated in the study. In particular, there were 18 men (36%) and 32 women (64%) and the observed mean age was 23.8 years (SD = 0.8). All participants acknowledged informed consent before completing the questionnaire.

An online teaching and assessment protocol was developed for the course. Specifically, a 2 × 2 design study was followed for the teaching modality (face-to-face vs. online) and the learning methodology (expository lectures vs. project-oriented collaborative learning).

To analyze students’ reactions to online and face-to-face teaching, a computer-based questionnaire called REDIPRE (abbreviation in Spanish of ‘Reacciones a la docencia  
                            y 
                          evaluación a distancia  
                            y 
                          presencial’) was designed and applied. The questionnaire was comprised of biographical information (i.e., age, gender, etc.) as well as 142 items that measured perception of lack of information and isolation, perception of justice, the opportunity to carry out dishonest academic behavior, technical and contextual obstacles in monitoring of online/distance learning classes and satisfaction with the educational innovation project.

In this work, the attention is focused on the items corresponding to the perception of lack of information, isolation and perception of justice. In particular, to measure ‘ Information undersupply ’ and ‘ Isolation ’, 7 items suggested by  Weinert et al. (2015)  and adapted into Spanish have been used, whereas the ‘ Justice ’ scale consist of 22 items selected from the ‘Procedural Justice Scale’ developed by  Bauer et al. (2001)  and translated into Spanish and reduced by  Osca and García Salmones (2004) . This scale includes the dimensions ‘Information about the process’ (2 items), ‘Appropriateness and Consistency’ (6 items), ‘Openness’ (4 items), ‘Treatment received’ (5 items), and ‘Two-way communication’ (5 items).

Given the intrinsic imprecision attached to the constructs and dimensions to be measured, interval-valued rating scales were adopted in this study. As highlighted in the introduction, the main advantage of these psychometric instruments is that they preserve and reflect the uncertainty inherent to human judgments, which would otherwise be lost in conventional single-point formats such as Likert-type or visual analogue scales. This type of response also offers greater flexibility to participants, allowing them to report their position together with the degree of confidence they attach to it.

It should be pointed out that the scales scores have been computed in this case as the average of the involved items instead of their sum since the purpose is comparing all the dimensions but the number of items in each of them is different.

After receiving a set of instructions for completing the REDIPRE questionnaire (see  Fig. 3 ), participants were requested to answer it by means of a custom web tool that implements a dual-range slider (DRS) response format, as shown in  Fig. 4 . More concretely, respondents have been asked to select the interval that best represents their level of agreement to the statements proposed in accordance with an interval-valued scale bounded between 1 and 7, with these endpoints representing ‘Strongly Disagree’ and ‘Strongly Agree’, respectively.

All the computations needed for this study have been carried out in  R  ( R Core Team, 2022 ). In particular, the  IntervalQuestionStat  package ( García-García, 2022 ), which provides some basic functions for doing statistics with interval-valued data, has been used to calculate the extended Cronbach  
                            α 
                          coefficient ( García-García et al., 2025 ), while the functions to test the equality of Aumann-type means and Fréchet-type variances and to compute the extended effect size indices were implemented  ad hoc  for the current work.

Cohen (1988)  established values 0.2, 0.5, and 0.8 as thresholds to represent “small”, “medium”, and “large” effect sizes, respectively, although he was aware that such labels “are relative, not only to each other, but also to the area of behavioral science or even more particularly to the specific content and method of research” ( Cohen, 1988  p. 25). However, Cohen was also aware that for most people it is more comfortable to have a shortcut, i.e. a conventional definition as a point of reference. However, these thresholds were defined under the assumptions of normality and can be interpreted as indicators of the degree of overlap between normal distributions. In the case of interval-valued data, normality cannot be assumed, and thus these thresholds cannot be directly extended nor understood as measures of distributional overlap. Following the guidelines of  Hedges (2008) , adopting these conventional benchmarks in the absence of prior information is reasonable. In the real-data example, if these thresholds were applied, the resulting effect sizes would align with the “small”, “medium”, and “large” labels, taking into account the sample Aumann-type means, Fréchet-type variances, and  
                            p 
                         -values of the hypothesis tests reported in tables and figures. Nevertheless, more detailed studies are needed, as the same numerical effect size may arise from differences in intervals mid-points, differences in interval spreads, or a combination of both, which must be considered when interpreting the results.

Finally, although in this particular real-life example the results may appear similar to those that would be expected with scalar data, this should not be interpreted as evidence that the outcomes of this study would always be identical to those obtained whether the data have been collected using a different rating scale. In fact, previous studies – such as the work by  Gil et al. (2015)  – have shown that statistical conclusions substantially often differ depending on whether the data are collected using crisp, interval, or fuzzy-valued scales. In questionnaires, the key strength of interval-valued responses lies in their ability to preserve and reflect the inherent imprecision attached to human ratings, which is otherwise lost in conventional formats. They allow for the representation of individual differences in the responses that are not captured by single-point responses such as those obtained via Likert-type scales. For instance, two respondents may select the same number, label, or symbol in a Likert-type scale, yet provide interval-valued responses of very different widths, thus expressing different levels of certainty in their judgments. For these reasons, although interval-valued scales and responses may seem to be a little bit more complex, they can capture more information about the respondent’s perception than traditional formats ( Lubiano et al., 2016a , but see,  Kloft et al., 2024 ). However, it should be highlighted that the validity of interval responses requires additional examination in subsequent studies (see, for instance,  Ellerby et al., 2022; Kloft & Heck, 2025; Kloft et al., 2024 ).

In this paper, the extended versions of some standardized mean difference effect size indices of the  
                         d 
                       family have been stated when the random experiment that is to be analyzed involves interval-valued data, filling a much-needed gap in the literature. On the basis of the most popular central tendency and deviation summary parameters, which are the so-called Aumann-type means and Fréchet type variances, respectively, and their sample analogue estimators, the definition of the sample counterparts of the population standardized mean difference index have been extended to the case where interval-valued data is handled by following a distance-based approach. On the one hand, the definition of the extended sample Cohen  
                         d 
                       index has been established for independent data under the assumptions of homogeneity and heterogeneity of variances, and it has also been stated for paired interval-valued data. On the other hand, the extended sample Glass  
                         Δ 
                       index has been suggested for case-control experiments where this type of data is considered.

A real-life study has been conducted to embed some statistical analyses with interval-valued data. Although questionnaires with interval-valued scales are not as immediate to fill as those including Likert-type and visual analogue scales, the study in this paper has served to show that the computation of standardized mean difference effect size indices of the  
                         d 
                       family is feasible also for this type of complex data and does not entail substantial additional complexity. This finding supports the potential incorporation of such statistical analyses into meta-analysis studies with both interval-valued and traditional scales. Nonetheless, it should be emphasized that the validity when interval-valued data in questionnaire-based setting warrants further investigation in future research.

A closely related open problem that should be addressed in the near future is to study the properties of the sample effect size indices introduced in this work, including their unbiasedness, consistency, and both asymptotic and bootstrap distributions. In particular, developing a rigorous distributional theory for these indices would not only complement the point estimates defined in this paper but also allow the construction of inferential procedures, such as confidence intervals for these effect sizes. Such a theoretical framework would undoubtedly enhance the interpretation of the suggested measures and provide a more solid basis for their application in practical settings.

In addition, a comprehensive sensitivity analysis should be carried out in future studies to verify whether the proposed measures are largely unaffected by the choice of  
                         θ 
                      , as the current results suggest.

Besides, since it is also possible to compute these mean difference effect size indices with fuzzy-valued data ( Castaño et al., 2020 ), it is also interesting to compare the performance of this family of measures for different types of data. Furthermore, future research should also be oriented to analyze the relationship of the standardized mean difference effect size indices with other effect size measures.

Another open problem that could be interesting to adress in the near future consists on comparing our methodology with alternative approaches. To the best of authors’ current knowledge, there are not yet realistic and wide parametric models for the distribution of IVRSs following the distance-based methodology of this paper. However, in other studies related to interval responses for psychometric measurement, where location and width of the interval responses are treated separately, parametric models of the full distribution of bivariate interval data, such as those suggested by  Ellerby et al. (2022) ,  Kloft and Heck (2025) , and  Smithson and Broomell (2024) , among others, have been established. When conducting these comparative studies, it should be taken into account that some of these models may yield better results in modeling location than width for some constructs, as highlighted by  Kloft et al. (2024) , whereas our approach avoids these limitations by considering each interval-valued datum as a complete entity.
