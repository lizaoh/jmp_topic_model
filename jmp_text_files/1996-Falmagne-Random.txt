A Random Utility Model for Approval Voting* 

University of California, Irvine 

A random utility model for approval voting is described, based on the notion that each voter has a personal ranking of the alternatives, with the random utilities being defined, in a standard manner, by the probability distribution on the set of all rankings. The set of alternatives chosen by a voter is made of the top alternatives in the voter's personal ranking. The size of the chosen set and the ranking are assumed to be independent. Two independent classes of necessary conditions are derived, which are used to test the model on two known sets of data. ] 1996 Academic Press, Inc. 

``Approval voting'' denotes a system in which the voters are required to select a subset of the alternatives. Any subset is an admissible vote. Thus, if four alternatives are listed on the ballot, there will be 2[4] =16 possible types of votes. In some situations, all types actually appear, e.g., some voters do not approve of any of the alternatives, while others accept every one of them. Two examples of such a case will be analyzed in this paper. This voting system has been advocated by some researchers and is used in various contexts. A standard reference for approval voting is Brams 6 Fishburn (1983). Other books on approval voting are Merrill (1988) and Nurmi (1987). Fairly elaborate theoretical analyses have been given. By and large, these analyses have focused on political aspects of the phenomenon. Typical issues of concern are sincerity, fairness, voting strategies, tallying procedures, and the conditions under which approval voting may result in the election of the Condorcet candidate, all of which have the common goal of studying the relationship between the outcome of the vote and the real (though latent) preferences of the voters.

A random utility model is discussed here, which has been applied to two sets of data. This model is based on the idea that each voter has a personal ranking of the alternatives and votes for the top s alternatives in that ranking, the number s being chosen randomly and independently of the ranking. This model will be shown to fail on the large data set from Brams 6 Nagel (1991). This negative result is of interest because the rejected model is quite general. In particular, no assumptions are made on the distribution of the random utilities. Accordingly, the number of parameters typically (for three or more alternatives) exceeds the number of independent data points. A rejection is nevertheless feasible. However, the features of the data leading to a rejection are by no means obvious, and a sophisticated analysis is required. The second data set comes from a vote of the Institute of Management Science (TIMS), discussed in Fishburn 6 Little (1988). A satisfactory fit of the model will be described. A plot of these distributions may be revealing of critical differences between superficially equivalent alternatives. 2.3. Remarks. (a) At first glance, the independence axiom [I] may appear implausible. However, it is difficult to avoid in the context of a model in which all voters look alike in the sense that each voter's choice is assumed to be guided by a personal ranking (total order) of the alternatives. In a situation in which two voters i and j have different personal rankings ? and ?$, there is no natural or obvious mechanism��except independence��for explaining how voters i and j could choose sets of different sizes. In other words, if independence is rejected, other aspects of the model have to go. An obvious substitute model would come from a minor generalization of the random ranking component of the model: we could assume that each voter has a latent strict partial order (transitive, irreflexive) on the set of alternatives. The approved subset would then be the set of maximal elements in the partial order. (Since the empty relation is vacuously a strict partial order with an empty set of maximal elements, this rule covers the case of the empty approved subset.) We shall return to this idea in our discussion section. 

(b) It should also be realized that the independence condition is not as easy to reject on the basis of the data as it may perhaps seem at the first reading of the axioms. The independence axiom concerns the size of the chosen set and the ranking. The fact that the ranking is not observable means that the empirical import of the axiom is not transparent. In particular, this independence does not imply that the size of the chosen set does not depend on which candidates are chosen. (A counterexample is easy to manufacture.) Theorems 4.2 and 4.3 state two empirical necessary conditions for the model to hold. A close look at these conditions should convince the reader that rejecting the independence Axiom [I] would require more than a cursory examination of the data or an intuitive analysis of the particular voting situation at hand. 

(c) Note that, in the political science literature, the relevance of the vote size for strategic issues has been controversial, as can be seen for instance in Saari 6 Van Newenhizen (1988a, 1988b) and Brams et al. (1988a, 1988b).

7. DISCUSSION 

A probabilistic model for approval voting has been described, based on two axioms paraphrased here informally as follows: 

[V] (Voting Axiom). A voter's chosen set is induced from a personal ranking of the alternatives. If the voter decides to select s alternatives, the first s alternatives in the voter's personal ranking will be chosen. 

[I] (Independence Axiom). The size of the chosen set is independent of the ranking. 

Two independent classes of necessary conditions for this model to hold have been derived��Eqs. (10) and (11)��which have been rejected for the large data set of Brams 6 Nagel (1991) displayed in Table 1, but were verified for the Fishburn 6 Little (1988) data of Table 2. The success obtained in the last case is of interest for two reasons. One is that the approval votes concern three alternatives, a situation in which the two necessary conditions are also sufficient. In other words, the SI model fits the Fishburn 6 Little (1988) data. The second reason is that, despite the paucity of the assumptions of the SI model, when a successful application is achieved, an incisive analysis is

Such an analysis is potentially revealing in that it permits to distinguish between two alternatives having the same average rank. The histogram of Fig. 1 displays the results of these computations for the data of Table 2 and clearly points to the dominance of candidate G. 

In the case of the failure of conditions (10) and (11) for the Brams 6 Nagel (1991) data, the most vulnerable assumption of the SI model may appear to be the Independence Axiom [I]. However, as argued earlier, this independence assumption is almost inescapable in the framework of Axiom [V] according to which all the individual decision mechanisms are formally similar. It is difficult to conceive a mechanism, other than independence, that would have two subjects, one with latent ranking ? and the other with latent ranking ?$, vote for subsets of different sizes. Thus, if Axiom [I] is to be rejected, then Axiom [V] should also be modified so as to break down the homogeneity of the mechanisms guiding the voters' choices. In any event, the random utility model of this paper remains of interest for situations in which the independence axiom may be regarded as reasonable, as seems to be the case for the Fishburn 6 Little (1988) data, or is bypassed altogether, i.e., in an experimental paradigm in which the size of the selected set is imposed to a voter (this assignment varying accross voters). 

Several research problems have been opened by this work. One is mathematical and concerns a representation theorem for the random utility model defined by Axioms [V] and [I] and described by Eq. (6). In the case of three alternatives, we know that the two classes of conditions (10) and (11) are equivalent and sufficient for the existence of the random variables S and (Ui)1�i�n satisfying (6). A proof of this fact can be found in a recent paper by Doignon 6 Regenwetter (in preparation), who have also solved the representation problem for the cases n=4, 5. In the same paper, they establish the necessity of many inequalities for an arbirary number of alternatives. For the general case, the representation problem remains unsolved. Note that a conceptually similar representation theorem has been obtained by Falmagne (1978; see also Barbera� 6 Pattanaik, 1986) for the choices of objects in subsets of a basic set. 

A second problem would address the development of a model based on similar ideas, but without the assumption of independence between the size of the chosen set and the voter's representation of the information about the alternatives. As a natural extension of the model presented here which we mentioned earlier in this paper, we could postulate that each of the voter has a latent partial order (for example) on the alternatives and that the approved alternatives are those contained in the set of maximal elements of that partial order. A parametric version of such a model has been developed by Falmagne (1996) (see also
