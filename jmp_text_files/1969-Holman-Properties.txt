HOLMAN  University of California, Los Angeles, California 90024  In a large class of path-independent models for two-choice learning, it is possible to derive a ratio scale that describes the  relative effectiveness of the learning operators associated with the possible outcomes of a subject's choices. The scale  values of the operators can be used to predict which outcome schedules will cause a subject to fixate on a single response,  and which schedules will cause continued choices of both responses. In a less general class of models, the scale values also  predict the asymptotic proportion of choices of each response when fixation does not occur. These scale values can be  estimated experimentally, without assuming the functional form of the learning operators, using a procedure similar to the  estimation of utility values from preference data.  INTRODUCTION  Three general questions can be asked about the asymptotic behavior of a subject in a two-choice learning experiment. First,  will the subject eventually learn to choose the same response on virtually all trials, or will he continue indefinitely to  choose both responses with appreciable frequency ? Second, if the subject continues to fluctuate between the two responses,  what properties of his response sequence or response probabilities do eventually approach some stable limit ? Third, if the  subject fixates on a single response, how does this final response depend upon his initial response probability? The answers  to these questions should provide information about the theoretical characteristics of the learning process, if necessary and  sufficient theoretical conditions are known for the various possible experimental results. The present paper derives fairly  general theoretical predictions for the first question above and rather less general predictions for the second, but does not  consider the third at all. These results are generalizations of predictions previously derived from more restricted classes of  theoretical models for learning. According to this model, a subject may fixate on a response that  is continuously rewarded, but he will not fixate on a response that is only partially rewarded, unless nonreward is assumed to  have no effect on response probability. Essentially the same prediction has recently been derived by Norman (1968) from a  large class of models with distance-diminishing operators. This prediction also follows directly from most finite-state Markov  models. In addition, quite general theoretical conditions for fixation under continuous reward are presented by Rose (1964).  Not all models behave like the linear model, however; in particular, quite different predictions have been derived by Luce  (1959) and Lamperti and Suppes (1960) from the beta model. According to this model, a subject may or may not fixate on a  partially rewarded response, depending upon the schedule of reward; if fixation does not occur, the asymptotic mean response  probability is also determined by the reward schedule. These findings have been generalized by Luce (1964), Marley (1967), and  Norman (1970) to large classes of models with commutative operators. The present paper extends these results even further-to  models satisfying certain monotonicity conditions. In fact, the conditions for fixation originally derived from the beta model  are found to apply to most path-independent models that have not already been shown to behave like the linear model.  Unfortunately, the asymptotic mean response probability is not generalized so far. MEASUREMENT OF OPERATORS  As in the work of Luce (1964) and Marley (1967), the monotonicity conditions are used mainly to establish a ratio scale over  the learning operators, which will in turn be used to determine asymptotic properties. Intuitively, the scale values attached  to the operators represent the relative effectiveness of the operators in changing choice probabilities. For instance, if one  scale value is twice as large as another, then it would take two applications of the operator with the smaller scale value to  produce about the same change in p as one application of the operator with the larger scale  MONOTONIC LEARNING MODELS 459  value. Positive scale values correspond to operators that increase p, such as reward of response rl or nonreward of r2 , while  negative scale values correspond to operators that decrease p, such as nonreward of rl or reward of r2 . A scale value of 0  does not necessarily mean that the operator leaves p unchanged, but only that the change is negligible compared to the effects  of other operators; similarly, two operators with the same scale value may not have exactly the same effect, but any  differences will be negligible.
