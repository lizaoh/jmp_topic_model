GREITZER  Department of Psychology, University of California, Los Angeles, 90024  Received May 10, 1974, revised  Mathematical models of memory can be evaluated using a continuous paired-associate learning paradigm in which a second  response is requested immediately following an error. This procedure provides discriminability among the models on the basis  of the relationship between the first- and second-response error probabilities. Theoretical predictions are derived for a  variety of models, including finite-state and noncontingent operator models, formulations based on the theory of signal  detectability, the multicomponent memory model of Bower (1967b), and the long- and short-term model of Atkinson and Shiffrin  (1968). None of these is unequivocally acceptable, and many are decisively rejected by the data of a single-trial experiment.  The most satisfactory models are the multicomponent model and the Atkinson and Shiffrin model, both of which assume a greater  degree of internal structure than the rejected models. It appears that internal structure of this sort will be a necessary  part of any satisfactory model of second responses.  The response that a subject makes in a learning experiment may be scored as correct or in error, but this oversimplified  dichotomy does not fully reflect the subject's state of knowledge. A correct response may indicate the answer is known or it  may be a lucky guess, while an error can represent anything from a minor confusion to a total lack of information. Because of  this, a subject who has made an error in a pairedassociate task will often perform much better than chance if given the  opportunity to make a second try. The better performance on the response following the error, or second response, can be  predicted by theories of memory that make sufficiently elaborate assumptions about the memory processes and structure.  Accordingly, these theories can be tested by looking at the second-response performance. This paper develops the predictions  of a number of paired-associate models for second responses and relates these predictions to a set of empirical results.  The paradigm to be considered consists of an ordinary paired-associate task with one addition: if a response is incorrect, the  subject is asked to make a second try. Since this second response immediately follows the initial error, almost no change in  * This research was supported by a grant from the University of California, Los Angeles. Interest in second responses (sometimes, unfortunately, called "second guesses") was initially aroused because of its  potential as a test of all-or-none learning. Usually these experiments have rejected the all-or-none model. Thus, in a  conventional paired-associate situation second responses correct more often than chance have been found with geometric  figure-letter pairs (Binford and Gettys, 1965), with letter-digit pairs (O'Hara and Erickson, 1969) and with digit-letter and  CCC-digit pairs (Brown, 1964). Using a matching procedure in which subjects were asked to match the list of responses to the  list of stimuli, Bregman (1966) observed better than chance second responses with word-word and figure-letter pairs. However,  when the stimuli were formed from three orthogonal seven-valued dimensions, Bregman and Chambers (1966) found chance second  responses within a dimension unless the subject had subcategorized the dimension.  Predictions for second responses from theories other than the all-or-none model are not common, and comparison to data all but  nonexistant. For example, although Bower (1967b) briefly considers the predictions of his multicomponent model and Hintzman  (1968) simulates second and third responses with his discrimination net model, neither author makes any empirical test of this  aspect of their model. A more extensive analysis of second responses is given by Shiffrin (1968, 1970). In the first of these  papers a specific model for second responses is developed from a model of memory search.  Second-response paradigms should be distinguished from two similar, but not equivalent, procedures. The first of these is the  ranking procedure, in which the subject ranks the responses, without feedback between the rankings, thereby giving both a  first-ranked and a second-ranked response. Shiffrin (1968, 1970) has examined this procedure and found that the second-ranked  responses were almost at a chance level, and far worse than performance on a second response after feedback about the first  error was given. This can be interpreted as indicating that following the feedback the subject attempts to get new  information, for example, by an additional search of memory. The second rankings themselves contain little information other  than that already incorporated in the first-ranked response.  Also quite similar to the second-response procedure is the RTT paradigm in which multiple tests of a paired-associate item are  given without intervening study opportunities (Estes, 1960; Estes, Hopkins, and Crothers, 1960). Nevertheless, several  important differences between the procedures exist. First, as with the ranking procedure, no feedback is given to indicate  that the first response was wrong when the second response is made. In this case this has the effect of allowing the same  (incorrect) response to be made on both test trials. Second, the two test trials do not follow each other immediately, but may  be separated by a number of intervening studies and tests  PAIRED-ASSOCIATE LEARNING 227  of other items. Thus, the procedure measures the retention or alteration of the response from the first to the second test,  rather than the residual information that can be recovered after an error.  In the present paper the predictions for second responses of a number of pairedassociate models will be examined and compared.  These predictions are developed in the following section. In the third section an experiment is presented that gives a limited  test of the predictions and in the final section some general comments on the applicability of the models are offered.  SECOND-RESPONSE PREDICTIONS OF PAIRED-ASSOCIATE MODELS  Over the past few years a large number of models appropriate to the pairedassociate paradigm have been proposed. Some of these  have received more support than others, but there remains a considerable collection of models that are of potential value as  approximations of the paired-associate process. In this section a number of these models will be examined with regard to their  predictions about second responses.  An initial consideration in adapting these theories to the second-response paradigm is the effect of the added test itself. In  spite of the differences between the secondresponse and the RTT procedures, some light is thrown on this by several  experiments using separate study and test trials. A series of experiments by Izawa (1971 and papers referenced therein) using  an RTT and other similar paradigms found that the first test had no effect on the second, but that the second test enhanced  the effectiveness of subsequent reinforced (study) trials. These results were interpreted as indicating that the nonreinforced  test trials served to alter stimulus fluctuation in a stimulus sampling theory model (Neimark and Estes, 1967). A related  study (Young, 1971) suggests that the effect of the study trial depends on the placement of the trial between reinforcements  and is maximal for an intermediate placement. In the experiments to be considered here the two test trials immediately follow  each other and immediately precede the next study. Under these circumstances the tests would have a minimal (and constant)  effect on the next study. As has been noted, Shiffrin's (1968, 1970) findings suggest that the second response opportunity may  lead the subject to engage in additional searches of memory. Thus, on the following study opportunity more information may be  available to the subject and this may make the study slightly more effective. In addition, the feedback on the first response  in conjunction with the second-response search could allow the incorrect first response to be eliminated (Millward, 1964),  although this effect is not likely to be large (Bower, 1962). In contrast to these tendencies to increase the learning rate is  the general disruption of the study of other items that may be occasioned by the second response and its possible search.  Which of these factors is the greater is unclear, but it is apparent that the rate parameters estimated for a second response  experiment might be different  228 WICKENS AND GREITZER  from those obtained in a conventional single-response task. This should not effect the overall fit of a model.  The experiment that will be modeled is a simple anticipation paired-associate procedure in which a set of stimuli is paired  randomly with N responses. The stimulus set is assumed to be larger than the response set so that no benefit can be gained by  elimination of responses assigned to other stimuli. Therefore, the probability of an error on a pure guess on the first  response is (N - 1)/N. It will be assumed that the subject never repeats the incorrect response following a first error; thus  the secondresponse guessing probability is (N - 2)1(N - 1). The symbol n will be used to denote the trial number, that is, the  number of times the item has been studied. By this convention any test of an item preceding the first study would be denoted  by n = 0.  For each model the probabilities of two events will be determined. The first of these events, El,, represents an error on  the first response of an item that has been studied n times. Similarly, E2,,, represents an error on the second response for  that item. Occasionally C will be used instead of E to indicate a correct response. For each model a prediction will be made  othe probability of a first error, P(EI,,,). The relationship between  these two probabilities clearly characterizes the second-response predictions, so, where practical, the predictions will be  plotted with P(El) on the abcissa and P(E2 I El) on the ordinate.  In addition to the trial number n, this paper will be concerned with one other variable: the number of intervening items  between the study of an item and its test. This variable, referred to as the lag of the item and denoted by l, will have value  zero when the test follows the study immediately and may take arbitrarily large integer values as study and test become more  and more separated. The motivation for the present study is not the investigation of lag effects, however, so elaborate models  for the effect of lag will not be constructed. Instead, where possible, the weakest models for lag will be considered. For  example, where the model depends on a single parameter, that parameter will be assumed to mediate the effect of lag. If this  parameter is estimated independently for every value of the lag, no explicit model for lag effects is required and the quality  of the fit will depend only on the relationship between P(E,) and P(E2 I El). When more than two parameters are involved some  speculation on the role of the lag variable will be made, but a preference will be shown toward models in which the parameters  of the lag effects are minimally restricted. Multiple-Storage Models  The models that have been considered thus far have been single-process models in the sense that only a single form of mnemonic  representation was postulated. Another important class of models are those in which two or more distinct representations  exist, often in the form of short- and long-term storage of material. When the retrieval properties of the two stores are  different, more elaborate second-response predictions occur. The difference in retrieval properties between the stores is  important if the predictions are to differ from a simple model. For example, although the memory model of Wickelgren (1970)  postulates four different memory stores, their effects combine in a single retrieval process, represented by a single value of  d' (our S,,) for a signal detectability model, and thus give a relationship that is no more complex than the single-process  signal detection model. PAIRED-ASSOCIATE LEARNING 247  AN EXPERIMENT  Most of the results developed in the previous section depend on the number of responses N, usually through the probability of  a guessed error on the first or second response, (N - 1)/N and (N - 2)1(N - 1). If these probabilities are substantially in  error, interpretation of the results would be more difficult. These guessing probabilities may be wrong if the subject is able  to incorporate into the response information that comes from outside the pair itself, that is, from knowledge of the response  set or from other pairs that have already been learned. If these problems are to be minimized, certain constraints must be  placed on the experiment.  The most serious difficulty would occur if the stimuli and responses were paired in a one-to-one manner. In this case each  learned pair would reduce the value of N in the guessing equation by one. A correction for this effect would be possible, but  the situation is almost certainly not so simple. In fact, only those responses that the subject remembers being paired  elsewhere act to reduce the guessed errors, and it is probable that the number of such remembered responses is less than the  number of responses that would be recalled correctly in the presence of their stimuli. Any analysis taking this effect into  account would introduce at least one new unknown parameter, such as the probability that a response could be spontaneously  recalled in the absence of its stimulus.  One solution to this problem is to allow the reduction in the response set to take place explicitly so that a definite  probability model can be constructed. Thus in Bregman's (1966; Bregman and Chambers, 1966; Bregman and Strasberg, 1968) work,  first responses were taken by asking subjects to match the stimulus and response sets, then the second responses were  collected by showing the subject the list of incorrectly matched stimuli and responses and allowing rematching. Predictions  for this paradigm under chance performance can be derived (Cumming, 1968), and allow all-or-none models to be tested. In this  paper a larger collection of models is under investigation, and the postulation of nonchance second-response performance  limits the utility of the matching procedure.  In this experiment a more direct paired-associate paradigm has been used. The problem of response elimination dictated several  characteristics of the experiment. First, the response set was chosen to be small and well known in comparison with the  stimulus set. Second, the pairings were made independently for each stimulus item, so that any learned pairings gave no  information about unlearned pairs. Finally, the material was not organized into lists at all, but was presented as a  continuous stream. The first items in this stream were eliminated from the analysis to avoid any possibility that the set of  responses for the initially presented items could have been memorized.  The desire to obtain the maximum amount of second-response data led to one additional restriction of the experimental task.  Each item was studied only once, that is, results were collected only for n = 1. This also served to simplify consideration  480/12'2-8  248 WICKENS AND GREITZER  of lag effects, for only one lag was pertinent to each item and no averaging was required.  Method  Subjects. The subjects were 12 male and female students at the University of California, Los Angeles. For each session  subjects had the option of receiving one hour of credit for an introductory psychology requirement or of being paid $1.50.  Apparatus and materials. The experiment was run on a computer-controlled apparatus consisting of a storage oscilloscope and a  typewriter keyboard. Only the keyboard digits 0 through 9 were used. The computer, a Hewlett-Packard model 2116-B, was  programmed to select and present for study the stimuli and responses, to select items for test, and to record the subject's  responses. The stimuli were the 1145 CVC trigrams with association values between 25% and 75% according to the Archer (1960)  norms. The stimulus presentation order was randomized for each subject. When an insufficient number of stimuli remained to  complete a full session, the list was repeated in a new random order. All but one subject required no more than one  rerandomization of the list; the remaining subject required an additional rerandomization for the final session. The responses  were randomly chosen single digits from 0 to 9.  Procedure. Subjects were run individually for ten one-hour sessions, no more than one session per day, arranged at the  convenience of the subjects and experimenters. Subjects completed their ten hours of participation in roughly 21 to 3 weeks.  On the first day, subjects were given information about the purpose of the experiment, the type of stimuli and responses being  used, and a general description of the procedure. A continuous paired-associate task was used, similar to that employed by  Freund et al. (1969). At the beginning of each session, a series of s study trials was given (s = 6 or 8), after which the  trials alternated between testing an old item and presenting a new item for study. Study trials lasted 5 sec, during which  time the word "STUDY" was displayed above the new stimulus-response item. The study trial ended as the scope entered its erase  cycle, flooding the screen with light for a 1 sec interval.  On test trials a pair was chosen randomly from among the s - 1 pairs that had not been presented on the immediately preceding  study trial. The stimulus part of this pair was displayed on the scope beneath the word "TEST:". There was no time limit for  the response, but subjects were instructed to respond as soon as possible without sacrificing accuracy. Immediately after the  subject responded, a request for a confidence rating digit between 1 and 5 appeared below the test stimulus.4 Following entry  of the rating, feedback was given: If the response (say 3) was correct, then  4 The confidence rating data will not be considered in this paper. If the response was incorrect the message "3 IS INCORRECT-TRY AGAIN" was  displayed and a second response and confidence rating were obtained. Feedback was given for the second response, but the  correct answer was not provided and no third response was allowed. The trial ended as the scope screen was erased, and after  the 1 sec erase interval, the study trial for a new item began. The item just tested was dropped from the active list and  received neither study nor test again. This sequence of test and study trials continued until the end of the session. They support the visual impression of Fig. 1: no model is even remotely satisfactory. These models were developed in an attempt to  give a more dynamic picture of the structure of memory. They fail here because they remain essentially all-or-none, not  permitting second responses to be correct more often than chance.  Linear model with item selection. These results aid in interpreting the effects of lag in this experiment. The failure of the versions of the model in which  single processes are held constant suggests that interference must affect both linear and all-or-none processes. The failure  of the constant-,q version implies that if conditioning events are to take place in the some-ornone manner of the RTI model,  the probability of these events must be lower at long lags than at short. This can occur if on each intervening trial there is  some probability that the subject may forget in an all-or-none manner the decrement in error probability that occurred at the  last effective trial. Then the probability that an effective and unforgotten conditioning event will have taken place after a  lag of Z trials-the value of 9 at that lag-will decline with l. If the size of the decrement always remains the same, the  constant-0 model results, but this was also unsatisfactory, so both 77 and B must vary with lag. The best fitting model was  the constant-0 version, implying that both an all-or-none loss and a decrease in the size of the probability change must take  place with interference. However, as noted above, this model has the least satisfactory interpretation. The fact that this  version agrees with the data better than the single process version suggests that the RTI formulation may not be the most  economical model. Unfortunately, this experiment provides no independent estimate of the balance between the linear and  all-or-none processes based on the first responses, as would be given by a multiple trial experiment. Such an experiment, by  giving an independent estimate of the constant parameter, would provide a much stronger test.  The problem with the single-process interpretation of lag effects in the RTI model is that the linear and all-or-none models  are so far apart that only very restricted lag effects are possible while holding one process constant. The assumption of the  independence of first and second responses is of prime importance here. In the allor-none model El and E2 are almost  completely dependent, while in the linear model they are independent except for the exclusion of the erroneous first response. To provide better predictions with a more natural  interpretation of lag, models must be considered that contain some, but not complete, dependence. This requires more internal  structure than assumed in the models so far. The remaining two classes of models introduce this structure.  Multicomponent model. To some extent the models discussed here suffer from the same problems as the single-prediction models,  for the predictions are constrained to a single line for any value of m. Because of this the null-state models are most easily  rejected. When m = 1, the model is equivalent to the all-or-none model (or "one-component model") and is unsatisfactory. When  m = 2, the model is a form of two-element model, with predictions very close to the two-element model discussed above, and, as  with that model, the fits are not satisfactory (see Table 1). Models with m > 2 predict even better performance on the second  responses and are even more decisively rejected. Thus, the null-state models fail because they predict too small values of  P(E2, I El), that is, because the defective components are too easily identified following an error. The failure is most acute  when m is large and the number of values is small, in which case it would be easy for the subject to correct the erroneous  assignment of values to the null component(s).  Of the various random replacement models that were considered above, the only plausible one is the model in which a single  component was changed. This, in fact, provides satisfactory predictions for moderate values of m (Table 1). For both values of  s the best fit is provided when m = 3 and the predictions are not statistically rejected by the chi-square. For this value of  m, v = N113 = 2.15 or about 2, so the items are coded in approximately a binary code. Since following an error the defective  component cannot be identified, the subject simply selects a new response similar to the one just attempted, that is, a code  differing from the first by only a single component. This represents the only satisfactory form of the multicomponent model,  but is one that is acceptable for both experimental groups.  Two-stage models. Initially, the duoprocess models can be divided into two classes: those for which the input to LTS takes  place as a single pulse and those for which it depends on residency in STS. In the former case, the relationship between P(E,)  and P(E2 I El) is asymptotically identical to this relationship for the LTS model alone. This fact puts a substantial  constraint on the LTS models that are possible, for in order to fit the data satisfactorily, the LTS model must be able to fit  the long lag relationship without aid from short-term processes. Because of this, models such as the linear model or the  signal detectability model are not satisfactory candidates for the LTS component. Some of the better fitting models, for  example, the RTI model or some of the multicomponent models, would be more satisfactory here. A detailed investigation of  these possibilities has not been made, however. DISCUSSION  As the previous section has shown, the combined first- and second-response data serve to rule out a large number of the  paired-associate models. Indeed, only two models, the RTI model and the random replacement version of the multicomponent model  are not rejected, although the Atkinson and Shiffrin model seems to capture the relationship between the first and second  responses while failing to completely predict lag effects. Many of the rejections are quite definite: the failure of the beta  model, the simple signal detectability model, or the null state version of the multicomponent model is quite unequivocal. The  second-response procedure provides a criterion to evaluate models with sufficient power to make clear distinctions.  258 WICKENS AND GREITZER  One characteristic of the models that has not received much discussion above is their ability, or lack of ability, to make  predictions that range over a large number of (P(El), P(E2 I El)) values. This may be an important factor. Although the  average results are representative of the general character of the individual results, the individual results do not fall  along any single line in the (P(E,), P(E2 I El)) plot. Thus, for example, the random replacement version of the multicomponent  model, although it makes a good showing on the pooled data, makes predictions only along a single line and does not fit all  the individual data closely. This could not be rectified by assuming differences in the overall learning parameters for the  subject, since this serves only to move the predictions along the same line. A simple mix of item difficulties, although it  could strictly solve the problem, is not really satisfactory, since substantially different mixes would be required for each  subject. A more probable explanation is that the true process is a mixture of several different strategies or of memories with  several slightly different characteristics. For example, in the multicomponent model the value of v might not be constant for  every response component and some responses might be represented by fewer components than others. This could conceivably  produce the sort of mixture that is required.  Of the models that were investigated in this paper, only two seem able to handle the range oindividual differences. Both  achieve their versatility by combining two quite different models. One of these, the RTI model, is a fairly arbitrary  combination of the linear and the all-or-none models. In the other, the Atkinson and Shiffrin model, the mixture in question  is created by the division into long- and short-term processes each with different dynamics. This mixture is capable of  placing the lag curve at any height and at most angles in the (P(EI), P(E2 I EI)) plot. The fact that the individual functions  lie at different places suggests that some type of two-process mechanism may be necessary.  The analysis given in this paper makes use of only a limited amount of the secondresponse data. In particular, the actual  responses, exclusive of their correct or error properties, have not been examined. Such an analysis would be interesting,  however. If the responses are encoded by a number of properties, these would be expected to be evident in the second  responses. If one of the encoded features was, say, divisibility by 3, an incorrect response of 6 would be more likely to be  followed by a 3 or a 9 than by a 4 or an 8. This should be reflected in a nonrandomness of the matrix of joint first- and  second-response frequencies. Such effects were observed, as evidenced by significant chi-square tests of quasi-independence  (Smith, 1973) (s = 6: X2(71) = 584.3, p < .001; s = 8: X2(71) = 366.5, p <.001). Because of the large samples involved,  however, these significant effects are not of great magnitude; for example, the relative information transmission from the  first to the second response is only about .08. This apparent low association is in part a result of averaging, for the  information transmission is larger when measured on the individual subjects (mean = .136, range: .098 to .208). The presence  of the dependence suggests that evidence  PAIRED-ASSOCIATE LEARNING 259  about the encoding characteristics could be obtained by looking for appropriate partitions of the joint response matricies.  Such an analysis has been undertaken. There is one important respect in which the experiment discussed here provides a less  stringent test of the models than it might. Because only a single trial was run for every item, parameter estimates based on  the first responses could be obtained only for single-parameter models unless lag effects were also predicted. This means, for  example, that for the RTI model there was no estimate of the ratio of linear to all-or-none properties except that provided by  the second responses. To some extent this disadvantage was overcome by the recording of lags and the requirement that the  model fit the lag relationship in the (P(E,), P(E2 I El)) space. Nevertheless, a more extensive analysis should certainly  include sufficient trials to provide full parameter estimates from the first responses.  In summary, the second-response data in this experiment permit the evaluation of paired-associate models in a number of ways.  First, many models are in sharp disagreement with the data and can easily be rejected. Of those that agree satisfactorily with  the data, some can still be eliminated because there is no good interpretation of their best-fitting versions. The  item-selection version of the linear model and the constant-0 version of the RTI model are examples of models rejected in this  way. Each of the models that remains is characterized by a more elaborate internal structure than the rejected models, a  structure that reflects a combination of different types of storage characteristics. In one of the successful models, the  multicomponent model, this structure was embodied in the relatively complicated representation of the memory trace. If the  model is modified to allow v to differ for different components, or m to vary for different items, or both, this would provide  a combination of properties sufficient to allow a range of predictions that accommodate individual performances. Although the  Atkinson and Shiffrin model was not fully satisfactory, it proved able to predict the essential relationship between P(E,) and  P(E2 I El). It also represents a structured mixture of storage processes, in this case reflected in the differing properties  of STS and LTS. In the end, the most complete model will probably contain processes of both types; with short-term processes  governing recovery at short intervals and the overall strength of the longer-term representation, and with sufficient  structure to the long-term processes to allow a range of predictions at long lags.
