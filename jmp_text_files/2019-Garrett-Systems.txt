Often in life we need to enumerate, or evaluate the number of items in some set to address fundamental questions concerning quantity: how many tomatoes do I have in my shopping bag and would that be enough for tonight’s salad? How many guests have arrived to the party and would I be able to fit them all on my yacht? Some enumeration decisions require us to compare two large quantities to some internal representation or criteria. For example, a soccer referee must ensure that each side has exactly 11 players. Conveniently, the teams are clearly marked by their shirt color — red or blue, but somewhat inconveniently they are physically separated only for a short period till the whistle blows, and then the two teams intermix on the field. The number of items within a single set (say, number of blue team members) may be estimated through the approximate number system (ANS). This system is inherent to many species ( Agrillo, Dadda, Serena, & Bisazza, 2008; Dehaene, 2011; Pepperberg & Gordon, 2005; Woodruff & Premack, 1981 ), and is capable of simultaneously estimating the number of items within a scene ( Dehaene, 2011; Gallistel & Gelman, 1992 ). ANS estimates differ from the exact process of counting and rely on changes in magnitude to detect differences in quantity ( Gallistel & Gelman, 1992 ). This allows the ANS to make rapid representations of quantity, balanced against the inaccuracy of estimation.

The ANS follows a logarithmic scale, obeying the Weber–Fechner law, such that detecting a difference between a small quantity and a large quantity depends on their ratio ( Fechner, 1860 ). For example, detecting a difference between 10 and 20 players is approximately equivalent to detecting a difference between 20 and 40 players. This relationship results in a distinct linear increase in the mean and standard-deviation of response-estimates as a function of their ratio ( i.e.,  coefficient of variance; see  Appendix A  for an example). The detection of differences in magnitude is a key aspect of the ANS, and this has led researchers to an important task design: the comparative numerosity task.

In a comparative numerosity task, participants are briefly presented with two sets of items, and are asked to decide which item-set has a larger quantity ( Leibovich & Henik, 2014; Libertus, Odic, Feigenson, & Halberda, 2016; Pansky & Algom, 2002; Piazza, Izard, Pinel, Le Bihan, & Dehaene, 2004; Price, Palmer, Battista, & Ansari, 2012 ). Typical findings reveal item-sets that are closer in numerosity  e.g.,  20 and 25, and are less accurate and slower to estimate than item-sets which are further apart  e.g.,  20 and 30. This phenomenon is known as the  numerical distance effect . Substantiating the numerical distance effect is not sufficient evidence of ANS engagement, as comparative judgments may also be performed using natural covariates of quantity. For example, it is possible to identify the larger of two item-sets by comparing the total item-set luminosity, brightness or area ( Halberda et al., 2006 ). As such, studies of the ANS often complete multiple experiments to independently control each numerical covariate ( Inglis & Gilmore, 2014 ). While these stringent designs excel at measuring the acuity of comparative judgments, studies utilizing such tasks systematically ignore a much deeper question:

Workload capacity is a measure of information-processing efficiency that assesses how the rate of processing changes in response to an increase in load  i.e.,  an additional source of information ( e.g.,  another item-set to enumerate). In  Halberda et al. ’s study there is an implicit assumption that the addition of further item-sets would not change the rate of estimation, in what is called an

Estimation response-time (RT) has been shown to increase log-linearly with item set-size ( Whalen et al., 1999 ), and to require very few attentional resources ( Burr, Turi, & Anobile, 2010 ). This could suggest that the estimation of a single item-set is an unlimited capacity process, and that multiple item-sets may be estimated in a similar manner. However, more recent work using the diffusion model challenged this assumption.

Park and Starns (2015)  applied the drift diffusion model ( Ratcliff, 1978 ) to decisions in an ANS acuity task. Participants viewed two sets of items and decided which item-set contained more items. By modeling both accuracy and RT, Park and Starns found a speed–accuracy trade off when estimating set-sizes of different ratios. This suggests people may need to manage limited resources in an effort to maintain the speed of processing while using the ANS (with unlimited resources, participants could have responded both quickly and accurately without the need to trade-off between speed and accuracy). Previously, parallel and serial systems have been identified through patterns in their mean response-times. When mean RT remains stable with an increase in workload, a parallel processing architecture has been considered the only viable model. This is because a serial processing architecture would be required to increase processing speed in an unrealistic way to equal such predictions. However, when mean RT increases with workload, as it does with estimation, this relationship between capacity and architecture becomes ambiguous. This is because a parallel model that becomes less efficient with increases in workload, may produce identical patterns of mean RT to a serial model. This is the issue of  model mimicry  ( Townsend, 1971, 1972; Townsend & Ashby, 1983 ).

Systems Factorial Technology  (SFT) is a set of tools developed by James Townsend and his colleagues, to overcome issues of model mimicry by directly measuring system capacity and processing architecture. SFT has been applied to a range of studies involving visual perception, such as binocular dot presentations ( Townsend & Nozawa, 1995 ), facial feature identification ( Wenger & Townsend, 2001, 2006 ), visual search tasks in the presence of distractors ( Fifić, Townsend, & Eidels, 2008 ), and even the number-size congruency effect ( Fitousi & Algom, 2018 ).

The current study applied the methodology and tools of SFT to assess the cognitive properties underpinning the humanestimation-system. We measured the workload capacity ofestimation-systems across individual subjects, and further extended the results of  Halberda et al. (2006)  by exploring the processing architecture of individual subjects across four experiments. In all four experiments participants were presented with colored discs and had to evaluate whether the number of discs was smaller than a criterion value.

Architecture refers to the arrangement of the channels and can be categorized as parallel (simultaneously operating channels) or serial (sequentially operating channels; see  Fig. 1 ). Stopping rule refers to the number of channels that must finish processing in order for a response to be made. Lastly, workload capacity measures how efficiently the channels respond to an increase in workload.

Application of SFT requires a specific empirical framework termed the  Double Factorial Paradigm  (DFP). The DFP can characterize the above system properties within a single experimental design ( Townsend & Ashby, 1983; Townsend & Nozawa, 1995; Townsend & Wenger, 2004 ). To apply the DFP framework to the enumeration of multiple item-sets, we presented participants with displays containing red and blue discs and asked them to judge whether the number of items in each set was greater or smaller than some criterion value. We assume each set is evaluated by a corresponding processing channel. We also assume that manipulating the discriminability of one channel (altering the number of displayed items relative to the criterion), affects the rate of processing only within that channel  e.g. , the blue item-set, and not the rate of processing in the other  e.g. , the red item-set. To assess workload capacity and processing architecture of the estimation system, we adapted a Double Factorial Paradigm to a comparative estimation task. On each trial, participants were presented with a set of red items, a set of blue items, or both sets, and had to determine whether the number of items in either set was smaller than a criterion value (20). For example, a display containing 14 red discs and 14 blues discs warranted an affirmative response, as did [14, 27] and [27, 14] displays. A [27, 27] display, on the other hand, calls for a ‘no’ response as neither set satisfied the criterion. We used an extended DFP (see  Little, Eidels, Fifić, & Wang, 2018 ) design, where on single-target trials a distractor stimulus is used in place of the target-absent condition. 
                            3 
                          
                         
                            3 
                            The term ‘distractor’ is used here to indicate a non-target stimulus (as is the convention in SFT literature). Enumeration of this additional item-set may not be necessary if the subject first enumerates the target quantity. 
                          This design affords MIC and SIC assessment of processing architecture for both double-target and double-distractor trial types. The presence of distractor sets allows assessment of conflict-target processing architecture when a target stimulus and distractor stimulus are presented together. We further augmented the design by also presenting single target and single distractor trial types  i.e.,  target or distractor item-sets presented in isolation.

The first factor manipulation of our DFP was the presence or absence of a target item-set. Target-sets were defined as containing fewer than 20 items. Distractor-sets were defined as containing more than 20 items. The first factor level, the number of targets present, provides the workload manipulation used to determine the capacity of the estimation-system.

At the second factor level, the salience of each item-set was manipulated relative to the central criterion of 20 items. This was achieved through the  numerical distance effect  ( Buckley & Gillman, 1974 ), whereby quantities closer to the criterion were harder to evaluate  i.e.,  of low salience, relative to quantities further away  i.e.,  high salience. Details on how low and high salience levels were selected are provided as part of our extensive pilot studies (see  Appendix A ). The second factorial manipulation allows for assessment of the processing architecture.

We applied the expanded DFP across four experiments to assess the impact of spatial separation and low-level perceptual covariates on the estimation system.  Fig. 4  illustrates the experimental stimuli, and how they differed across the four experiments. Experiment 1 assessed the estimation of two colored item-sets when the colored items were intermixed. Experiment 2 replicated Experiment 1, with spatially separated color-sets ( e.g.,  blue on top, red on bottom). Experiment 3 assessed the system when item-sets were intermixed, but the total area of each colored-set was matched on every trial. Finally, Experiment 4 assessed system estimates when total color-set area was matched and color-sets were spatially separated. Since the experiments only varied by these stimulus manipulations, we present a single experimental procedure for all experiments. Participants were randomly allocated to one of the four experiments in a between-subjects design and were only allowed to participant in a single experiment.

Until fairly recently, inference on the SIC( 
                         t 
                      ) was limited to visual inspection.  Houpt and Townsend (2010)  developed null-hypothesis significance tests (NHST) to more formally diagnose deviations from a zero SIC. However, these tests are limited by the standard pitfalls of NHST. In particular, the ‘null’ signature actually corresponds to the serial OR model, making direct inference about that model difficult under the NHST framework. More recently,  Houpt, Heathcote, and Eidels (2017)  developed a non-parametric Bayesian approach for categorizing SIC( 
                         t 
                      ). The approach is based on a Dirichlet process that samples thousands of SIC functions based on the empirical probabilities of each condition at certain quantiles, and categorizes each sample based on which model shape it best approximates (as illustrated back in  Fig. 2 ). The Bayesian framework has the advantage of quantifying the strength of evidence for each model (allowing comparison of model weights), and also allows direct inference about the serial models. We implemented this Bayesian approach to help categorize our SIC models. We used a uniform prior on the model space such that the prior likelihood of sample corresponding to a given SIC state (neither, one, or both of a positive or negative deviation at some time  t ) was equally likely. This uniform prior has the advantage of giving very weak evidence for all models in the case an SIC is truly uninformative, whereas the approach presented by  Houpt et al.  tended to show strong evidence of serial processing in such cases ( Houpt et al., 2017 ). To be sure, testing confirmed that using the uniform prior distribution did not hurt model recovery when applied to the simulated data from  Houpt et al. ’s ( 2017 ).

In addition to the SIC analyses, we implemented the hierarchical MIC estimation procedure introduced by  Houpt and Fifić (2017) . This analysis involved hierarchically estimating parameters of a gamma distribution for each subject’s conditional response-time distribution, where the rate parameter of each distribution was constrained by a higher-order ‘MIC state’ model. This procedure allows a parametric evaluation of evidence for processing architecture at the MIC level  i.e.,  serial (MIC  
                         = 
                       0), parallel minimum-time (MIC  
                         > 
                       0), parallel maximum-time (MIC  
                         < 
                       0). Using these two Bayesian approaches we were able to obtain converging Bayesian evidence for serial and parallel processing architectures using both non-parametric (Bayesian SIC), and parametric (Bayesian MIC) approaches.

When asked to estimate the quantity of two item-sets and compare them to an internal criterion, participants were able to complete the task quickly and accurately. Most subjects in Experiments 1–4 displayed a significant redundancy cost when estimating two target item-sets. A parallel architecture – as found in the current study – could violate context invariance and yield severely limited capacity through a number of ways. Under one account, processing channels inhibit each other, acting to slow the rate of processing in the alternate channel. In their investigation of workload,  Eidels et al.  ( 2011 , Figure 3) illustrated how a parallel minimum-time system with cross-channel inhibition (both pre-accumulation and post-accumulation) can predict severely limited capacity and maintain an all-positive SIC. We term this the inhibition hypothesis.

An alternative account suggests that the rate of processing (evidence accumulation) in double-target trials is slower than in single-target trials, we term this the rate hypothesis. The rate hypothesis may occur under several conditions: (i) double-target processing-rates may slow with increases to the total stimulus set-size  e.g.,  the sum of red and blue items, (ii) double-target processing-rates may slow with the number of component item sub-sets  e.g.,  red, blue, green, etc. Or (iii) double-target processing-rates may slow due to context effects  e.g.,  crowding effects ( Anobile, Turi, Cicchini, & Burr, 2015 ) or figure-ground segmentation effects ( Kimchi & Peterson, 2008 ).

To test the feasibility of the rate hypothesis, we simulated a parallel model with limited capacity at the level of the accumulator using the linear ballistic accumulator (LBA) model ( Brown & Heathcote, 2008 ).  Fig. 12  illustrates SIC(t) and C(t) predictions when processing-rates are fixed (top panels), compared to when processing-rates are slower for double-target trials only (bottom panels). Simulated results from slowed double-target rates match observations from the current study. As such, both the inhibition hypothesis and the rate hypothesis can produce an all-positive SIC with severely limited workload capacity. To determine which hypothesis is most likely, we now consider the resilience difference function.

The resilience difference function compares the response-times for processing two-target item-sets, to the response-times for processing a target and distractor (conflict-target) item-set. Resilience difference factors out the effect of the distractor channel, providing a direct assessment of processing architecture and workload without altering the number of processing-channels ( i.e.,  item-sets). Our analysis of resilience matched predictions made by an unlimited capacity, parallel processing architecture. This suggests that target processing was similar between conflict-target and double-target conditions, and that processing channels did not inhibit one another.

Context effects, such as the crowding effect ( Anobile et al., 2015 ) and the figure-ground segmentation effect ( Kimchi & Peterson, 2008 ) disrupt single item identification and slow the rate of enumeration. The above discussion proposed that workload capacity limitations in estimation systems could occur prior to the estimation of item sub-sets. The number of item sub-sets, and the potential presence of context effects, both explain this property and align with predictions made by the aforementioned rate hypothesis. Future research should assess the fidelity of this hypothesis, and determine which of these approaches led to severely limited workload capacity. We now turn to our second set of measures, which assess processing architecture.

Only a limited number of subjects met the inclusion criteria for processing architecture, so we discuss results pooled across the four experiments. Of the fifteen subjects whose processing architecture could be conclusively assessed at the level of the SIC, all displayed a parallel processing architecture. These results align with previous findings by  Halberda et al. (2006) , who used estimation acuity to theorize the parallel estimation of up to three item-sets. Results from the current study validate this theory for up to two item-sets by applying the diagnostic response-time measures of SFT. Hierarchical Bayesian MIC analysis, conducted across a wider range of subjects, also provided moderately strong evidence in favor of parallel processing. These findings were observed at both the individual and group posterior level, and across all four experiments.

The current study is the first adaptation of the SFT framework to the study of estimation systems and came with notable limitations. In our assessment of the SIC, three subjects displayed non-determinable double-target processing architectures, and thirty-seven subjects did not meet the necessary requirement of selective influence. This suggests our manipulation of selective influence  i.e.,  the numerical distance effect, was not effective for a majority of subjects.

Through extensive piloting ( Appendix A ), we determined target and distractor salience levels that would ensure a high-degree of accuracy, and a significant response-time effect. As expected, we observed high accuracy in our primary task, however, our manipulation of response-time did not replicate. We suggest this effect is best explained by (i) context effects, and (ii) small trial numbers.

As previously discussed, the presentation of multiple item-sets may have resulted in the slowing of item-set estimation. This effect may have also compromised our manipulation of salience, leading to a lack of selective influence among the majority of tested subjects. Our salience manipulation was particularly ineffective when item-set area was controlled, and when item-sets were separated. Our control of area and item-set separability, may have afforded a secondary perceptual covariate  e.g.,  item-set density, that subjects utilized to identify target item-sets. This method would circumvent item-set estimation and the numerical distance effect. Alternately, our manipulations may have enhanced the interference produced by context effects, resulting in a poor manipulation of salience.

A second limitation to our task design was the use of relatively few double-target trials (compared to typical SFT studies  c.f.,  
                            Eidels, Townsend, Hughes, & Perry, 2015; Townsend & Eidels, 2011 ). Small trial numbers may result in unstable survivor distributions, leading to a poor manipulation of selective influence and unreliable SIC functions. As such, both small trial numbers and the presence of context effects may have contributed to our poor manipulation of salience.

The current study design faced several limitations, however, these limitations did not appear to change the fundamental processing architectures observed between Experiments. Rather, these limitations altered the number of subjects who could be diagnostically classified. As a result, it is impossible to draw strong conclusions regarding the impact of item-set area, or item-set separability, on processing architecture.

Our findings provide conclusive evidence that congruent item-sets  i.e.,  double-target item-sets, may be estimated through a parallel estimation system. These findings were observed by a majority of subjects whose processing architectures could be categorized. Through the novel application of SFT to an estimation task, we provide a nuanced account of the relationship between processing architecture, workload capacity and stopping-rule. Notably, we observe capacity limitations occurring before sub-set estimation, possibly caused by context effects occurring at the presentation of multiple item-sets. These results are important, as they provide a rational for why high cognitive workload may be experienced when estimating multiple item-sets. Understanding this limitation to the human estimation system is particularly important for circumstances requiring item-set estimation under extreme or irreducible levels of workload. For example, emergency responders to a scene identifying how many people require attention, or theatre attendees choosing the fastest of two crowded exits in the presence of a fire alarm. Importantly, our capacity results seem to extend down the number line, to when item-sets are small, such as when subitizing the number of cups and plates at a table; an activity we engage with on a more frequent basis.

People often need to estimate quantities of item-sets, be it the number of people in line to the bank teller, party guests, or players in football teams. Little research has investigated the ability to estimate multiple sets at the same time, and even fewer studies, if any, applied machinery that can overcome parallel–serial mimicry concerns, and allows assessment of the cost to processing multiple item-sets (workload capacity). The current study is the first known attempt to apply SFT to the question of multiple-set estimation. In a series of four experiments, participants were presented with either one or two sets of 10–41 colored discs (red, blue) and had to estimate whether the number of items in each set was smaller or greater than some criterion value. SFT analyses revealed rather severe capacity limitations, meaning there was a toll in processing efficiency when subjects had to estimate the number of items in two sets relative to just one set. These results suggest that while estimation may be colloquially viewed as an effortless and possibly automatic process, the estimation of multiple item-sets is in fact a rather demanding feat. Specifically, estimating two sets was inefficient to the extent it was comparable (in terms of processing capacity) to serial processing. Understanding the fundamental cognitive mechanisms that cause this limitation is of paramount importance if we are to reduce the effective workload experienced by individuals performing multiple item-set estimates. Doing so will afford faster and more accurate estimates under difficult conditions, when we must make quick and informed approximations of quantity. This is a challenge we leave to future research.

The following appendix details the pilot study that was conducted to ensure our primary task engaged the approximate number system (ANS), and that our target and distractor item-sets engaged the numerical distance effect. By using previously validated methods, we show the ANS was engaged when subjects provided self-reported estimates of quantity across a range of set-sizes (10–46). Using a within-subjects design, we relate these findings to a data-set collected using a comparative judgment task where subjects are asked to report whether an item-set contained less-than a central criterion of 20. Results show these tasks engaged similar numerical distance effects and suggest that the ANS was engaged for both the self-report and comparative judgment tasks. Finally, we use findings from the comparative judgment task to select target and distractor quantities for the experiments applying systems factorial technology. We chose two levels of target salience: high salience (10 discs) and low salience (14 discs), and two levels of distractor salience: low-salience (29 discs) and high-salience (41 discs). As you will shortly see, these conditions displayed high-accuracy and significant differences in response-times, supporting a numerical distance effect relative to an imaginary criterion of 20 items.

The approximate number system allows for rapid estimation of item-set numerosity. Engagement of the ANS may be assessed with a self-report task, where subjects provide their estimates of quantity with a number-pad ( Cordes et al., 2001; Halberda et al., 2006; Whalen et al., 1999 ). When the ANS is engaged, the mean and standard-deviation of response-estimates increase in a linear fashion with the number of items to be enumerated. This results in a coefficient of variance ranging from approximately 0.14 to 0.4 (see  Cordes et al., 2001; Halberda et al., 2006; Whalen et al., 1999 ). The self-report method provides a validated measure of ANS engagement, but at a cost to the fidelity of estimation response-time. When a subject self-reports a numerical estimate, it is unclear at what time the cognitive processes behind their decision finished. For example, does a decision finish when a subject finishes typing their estimate? Or does a decision finish when the subject begins typing their estimate? Might a subject change their decision as they are typing, and how does this factor into the response-time? These methodological issues conflate to make self-reported measures of response-time less useful for inferring the time properties of the ANS.

An alternative method for assessing response-times while potentially engaging the ANS is a comparative judgment task ( Leibovich & Henik, 2014; Pansky & Algom, 2002; Piazza et al., 2004 ). Here, subjects are asked whether an item-set is greater-than or less-than a specified criterion, to which a binary ‘yes’ or ‘no’ response may be provided. In this response-format, decision-time and response-time are tied to a single key-press, creating a more reliable measure of decision finishing-time. Furthermore, the comparative judgment task is designed to engage and measure a specific aspect of the number system: the numerical distance effect. Under the numerical distance effect, item-sets that are closer together are harder to estimate than item-sets that are further apart  i.e ., estimating whether 22 items are greater-than 20 items is harder than estimating whether 30 items are greater-than 20 items. As such, closer item-sets incur a reliable penalty to response-times and accuracy. This makes the distance effect a useful manipulation of difficulty or ‘salience’ in a task that requires a stable manipulation of response-time, such as systems factorial technology (SFT).

We conducted two within-subject tasks: a self-report estimation task and a comparative judgment-task, both utilizing the same stimuli with quantities ranging from 10–46. We assessed ANS engagement using the self-report task. We then compared results from the self-report task to the comparative judgment task and inferred that the ANS was engaged in both task-designs. Finally, we use the numeric distance effect established within the comparative judgment task to choose target and distractor quantities for the primary SFT task. These target and distractor levels were selected on the basis of high levels of accuracy, and a significant response-time effect, so as to be successfully applied within the SFT framework.

Stimulus set-sizes were logarithmically spaced to reflect the approximate number system. Item sets contained either 10, 11, 12, 13, 14, 16, 18, 22, 24, 27, 30, 33, 37, 41 or 46 discs. Target item-sets comprised less than 20 items, and non-target item-sets comprised greater than 20 items. This resulted in seven target item-sets, and eight non-target item-sets.

Each participant completed two within-subject response tasks, a self-report task and a criterion judgment task, over one 60 min experimental session. In the self-report task, participants used the number pad to report their estimate for the total number of discs presented on a given trial, before pressing the enter key to end the trial. During the criterion judgment task participants reported the presence of target item-sets, those containing fewer than 20 discs with the ‘z’ key, and the presence of non-target item-sets, those containing more than 20 discs with the ‘/’ key. Task order and response-keys were counter balanced between participants.

During both the self-report task and criterion judgment task, participants completed a practice block and five experimental blocks. Each block contained 60 trials, with the probability of presenting each item-set equated within a block. Accuracy feedback, ‘correct’ or ‘incorrect’, was displayed at the end of each practice trial within the criterion task. The true item-set numerosity was displayed at the end of each practice trial during the self-report task. Feedback was not provided during experimental blocks. Each participant completed 300 self-report experimental trials and 300 criterion judgment experimental trials. On a trial, participants viewed a central fixation cross for 500 ms (0.28 x 0.28 degrees visual angle) followed by a 500 ms blank. Then, participants viewed a stimulus item-set for 1000 ms followed by a backwards mask for 500 ms. Participants had 10 000 ms to respond from the onset of the stimulus. The blank screen inter stimulus interval (ISI) was 500 ms.

Differences between conditions of set-size were assessed at the group level. Participants were excluded if accuracy in the comparative judgment task was less than 90% in either the highest (46) or lowest (10) set-size condition. In the self-report task, trials were excluded from analysis if the time-difference between the first numeric key-press and the enter-key exceeded 2 standard-deviations of the mean. This method removed excessively long responses where subjects may have changed their decision after starting their response. During the criterion-response task, trials that exceeded  
                               ± 
                            2SD of the mean response-time were excluded. Mean RT differences between comparative judgment task item-sets were assessed using paired sample t-tests, calculated in Matlab 2015b.

One finding of interest is the change from under to over estimation between criterion and self-report tasks. The trend of mean accuracy within the criterion task suggests a tendency to under-estimate item-set quantity, while the trend of converted-accuracy in the self-report task suggests a tendency to over-estimate item-set quantity. These differences are interesting, but not unexpected as the comparison between tasks is not entirely fair. The comparative judgment task utilizes a singular point of comparison  i.e.,  20 items, where the self-report task allows free responding and a post-hoc imaginary point of comparison. While it is difficult to attest whether the difference in over and under-estimating is due to a change in the ANS or simply a change in task, we may reliably attest that both tasks engaged the numerical distance effect and when taken together, fit with predictions made by the approximate number system.

In this final section, we will discuss how the findings from this pilot study may inform us when selecting target and distractor values for the primary task using Systems Factorial Technology. The methodology and suite of analysis tools provided by SFT requires a large number of accurate responses and a significant response-time effect. For this, we will consider results from the comparative estimation task and apply the following restrictions. (i) Target and distractor conditions should be accurate when compared to a criterion of 20 at a threshold  
                               ≥ 
                            90%. (ii) There must be a significant response-time effect between high-salience and low-salience target conditions  i.e.,  item-sets  
                               < 
                            20, and a significant response-time effect between high-salience and low-salience distractor conditions  i.e.,  item-sets  
                               > 
                            20. Finally, (iii) both target and distractor item-set conditions must follow the same Weber-fraction. This will ensure the comparison between processing architectures for target and distractor item-sets within the SFT framework is equivalent at the level of the numerical just-noticeable-difference ( Fechner, 1860 ).
