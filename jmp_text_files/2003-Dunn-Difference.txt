Models of the kind given by  Eqs. (1.1)–(1.4)  can be analyzed into two component parts. One part consists of the set of parameters representing the psychological constructs postulated by the model and a set of rules specifying how they combine to affect performance on each task. The other part consists of a specification of how each combination of parameters relates to the actual level of performance on the corresponding task.  Busemeyer and Jones (1983)  have called these parts of a model, the structural model and the measurement model, respectively, while in information integration theory with respect to a single dependent variable, they are called the integration function and response function, respectively ( Anderson, 1981 ). Since in the present context, we are concerned with sets of functions, and in order to avoid terminological ambiguity, the two components are called the  structural mapping  and the  measurement mapping , respectively.

                      In order to derive numerical predictions from models of this form, it is necessary to specify both the structural and measurement mappings. However, because of the different roles played by these two mappings, they are not equally specifiable. In particular, while the structural mapping is often integral to the theoretical claims of the model, the measurement mapping may be inessential, fulfilling an auxiliary role. Therefore, the exact form of this mapping may be of little relevance to the core features of the model. Often, all that is assumed is that each component function,  f 
                      1 ,…, f

                      , is at least monotonic ( Busemeyer, 1980 ; Loftus, 1978;  Loftus & Irwin, 1998 ;  Michell, 1990 ). In this case, it is not possible to derive numerical predictions from the model using standard parameter estimation techniques and reliance must instead be placed on ordinal predictions. Yet, since each component of the structural mapping may be a function of several variables, it is unclear what, if any, ordinal predictions would follow.

The aim of the present paper is to outline a general method, called  signed difference analysis  (SDA), that can be used to derive ordinal predictions from models of the form given by  Eq. (1.5)  under the constraint that the component functions of the measurement mapping are (strictly) monotonic. The predictions do not depend upon estimation of either the model parameters or the forms of the unknown measurement functions. Instead, they are based on properties of the geometric structure of the model that are invariant under all possible monotonic transformations.

The present article is divided into four main sections. In the remainder of the present section we introduce the basic ideas underlying SDA. In  Section 2 , we present the mathematical theory of SDA and outline how this method can be applied to derive predictions from a model. In  Section 3 , we apply SDA to the three problems presented above, derive sets of ordinal predictions and evaluate these predictions in the context of theoretical issues drawn from the relevant literature. In  Section 4 , we compare SDA to related techniques and discuss some of its limits and extensions.

In the remainder of this section, we outline a procedure for determining the set of zones occupied by a linear subspace.  Theorem 3  states that a non-null zone is occupied by a subspace if and only if there does not exist a vector in the orthogonal complement of the subspace that is sign concordant with the zone's associated sign vector. Although, in general, the orthogonal complement contains an infinite number of vectors, it turns out to be unnecessary to check every one of them. If two subspaces occupy the same set of zones, they are said to be  zone equivalent . Let  A  and  B  be two linear subspaces such that  
                               O (A)= O (B) . Then  A  and  B  are  zone equivalent  and we write  A ≈ B . The model defined by  Eq. (1.1)  has been proposed to account for recognition memory judgments in the remember-know (RK) paradigm ( Donaldson, 1996 ;  Hirshman, 1998 ;  Hirshman, Lanning, Master, & Henzler, 2002 ;  Hirshman & Master, 1997 ;  Inoue & Bellezza, 1998 ). In this paradigm, subjects are instructed to respond ‘remember’ if they believe that a test item is old and are able to recollect some details of its occurrence in the study phase. Subjects are instructed to respond ‘know’ if they believe that a test item is old but are unable to recall any information about its prior presentation. Otherwise, subjects respond ‘new’.  Gardiner, Ramponi, and Richardson-Klavehn (1998)  have observed that, across a wide variety of experimental conditions, remember and know response are functionally dissociated and that “all possible relations between these two states of awareness have been observed” (p. 2). They have interpreted this result as supporting the view that the two kinds of response reflect qualitatively different states of recollective experience. This has been called the dual-component model of remember-know judgments. In contrast, other researchers have argued that the observed pattern of results can be interpreted in terms of a two-criteria signal detection model of the same form as  Eq. (1.1)  where category  C 
                         1  corresponds to remember responses, category  C 
                         2  corresponds to know responses, and category  C 
                         3  corresponds to new responses.

Despite a considerable body of published research, neither the dual-component model nor the signal detection model has been clearly falsified ( Hirshman et al., 2002 ). One reason for this is that it is not possible to generate quantitative predictions from the signal detection model since the forms of the underlying distributions of old and new items are unknown and probably unknowable. In this context, SDA can be used to derive testable predictions from the model in the absence of this information.

Although the present example serves primarily to illustrate an application of SDA, we make the following observations. First, the fact that such a large proportion of observed signed difference vectors are permitted by the signal detection model is both surprising and informative. One way of indexing the degree of surprise is to conduct the following test. We randomly generated different arrangements of 25 permitted and 15 forbidden signed difference vectors and counted the number of observed signed differences that fell into the permitted category. In the course of 10 6  random allocations, none were found to contain more than 98.6% of observations in the permitted category. Having said this, it is also true that the occurrence of just one forbidden difference is sufficient to refute the model. Unfortunately, since the data consist only of summary statistics, it is not possible to determine whether any of the observed violations are reliable or could be attributed to measurement error. Nevertheless, the results suggest that very few, and quite possibly none, of the findings in the remember-know literature are demonstrably inconsistent with the two-criteria signal detection model. Since much of the theoretical development in this field has been predicated on the idea that the data are inconsistent with this model, it is instructive to learn that so much of it is not.

Second, although the results of the present analysis indicate that the signal detection model remains a viable alternative to the dual-component model, it should also be noted that SDA constitutes a relatively weak test for two reasons. First, the results of SDA are invariant under  different  monotonic transformations of each dependent variable. It therefore ignores the fact that the model given by  Eq. (1.1)  requires that the same cumulative distribution function be associated with each dependent variable. We return to this issue in Part 4 below. Second, SDA places no restrictions on the form of the cumulative distribution function,  F , and assumes only that it is monotonic. Yet, some distributions may be more psychologically plausible than others.

The question of whether a set of dependent variables are functions of one or more parameters arises if researchers are accustomed to employing several different tasks to measure a single construct of interest. This issue has been examined recently by  Loftus and Irwin (1998)  in relation to three different measures of visible and informational persistence, by  Busey, Tunnicliff, Loftus, and Loftus (2000)  in relation to a measure of accuracy and two measures of confidence in recognition memory, and by  Cheal and Lyon (1994)  in relation to three different measures of spatial attention. Each of these studies employed the following methodology. First, a series of experiments were conducted in order to measure each dependent variable (task outcome) under a common set of conditions. Second, the rank order of these conditions was calculated for each dependent variable and compared across pairs of variables. Essentially, this is a test of Model 1 as well as Models 2a–2c. If Model 1 is correct then all three variables measure the same construct, and the rank order of conditions should be the same for each of the three variables. Alternatively, if one of Models 2a–2c is correct, the rank order of conditions should be the same for two of the variables but different for the third. However, this is as far as this method extends. It cannot be used to test between Models 2d–2f and 3 since each of these predicts a different rank order of conditions for each of the three variables. In addition, this method is ill-suited to an appropriate statistical treatment since the hypothesis under test is that there is a perfect rank order correlation between two sets of scores, rather than the usual hypothesis that the correlation is zero.

In visual search, subjects search for the presence or absence of a pre-specified target in differently sized arrays of distractors. The dependent variable is the slope of the linear function relating response time to the number of elements in the display (for both target present and target absent trials). The greater the slope, the more spatial attention is assumed to be required. In texture segregation, subjects are presented with an array of targets and distractors, notionally divided into four quadrants. One quadrant contains instances of the target stimulus while the remaining three quadrants contain instances of the distractor. The dependent variable is the time taken by subjects to correctly identify the quadrant that contains the target stimuli. The longer the time taken, the more spatial attention is assumed to be required. In location precuing, subjects are presented with an array of four stimuli one of which is designated as the target. On any one trial, the target location is precued by a rectangle that briefly appears over the relevant position a short time before the target array is presented. Subjects are required to identify which of two possible variations of the target is subsequently presented at the cued location. Response accuracy in this task is found to be an increasing function of the interval between cue and target up to about  100 
                            ms

The logic of model testing in SDA raises the issue of comparing models of different complexity.  Myung and Pitt (1997, p. 80)  define complexity as, “the flexibility inherent in a model that enables it to fit diverse patterns of data”. Generally speaking, the fit of a more complex model to a given set of data will be better than that of a less complex, or simpler, model. This may arise because the more complex model has more parameters or a more dense functional form ( Dunn, 2000 ). Because the fit of a more complex model is always better, this cannot be the sole criterion for comparing models. If so, then the most complex model would always be selected and scientific progress would grind to a halt. For this reason, various attempts have been made to measure complexity and to penalize more complex models accordingly (for an overview, see  Myung, Forster, & Browne, 2000 ).

SDA is not immune to the problem of comparing models of differing complexity. While it is beyond the scope of the present article to conduct a detailed analysis of this question, we make the following three observations. First, the framework of SDA provides a natural means of assessing a model's complexity, related to the suggestion by  Popper (1959)  that a model is informative to the extent that it rules out possible outcomes. In the limit, if a model fails to exclude any possibility (i.e., is maximally complex), then it ceases to be a scientific proposition. In SDA, the set of outcomes ruled out by the model corresponds to the set of unoccupied zones. Thus, the larger the occupied set, the more complex the model. More precisely, complexity can be measured in terms of the smallest volume of difference space that wholly contains the set of occupied zones. This, in turn, is given by the proportion of occupied zones of non-zero measure. We can formalize this idea as follows.

Let

As can be calculated from  Table 3 , the  n -number of Model 1 is 1, for Models 2a–2c it is 2, for Models 2d–2f it is 3, and for Model 3 it is 4.

Second, if several models are tested against a data set, it may turn out that a number of them cannot be falsified. This occurred in the previous analysis of the Cheal and Lyon data, where Models 2d, 2e and 3 could not be falsified. In this case, we adopt the convention dictated by Occam's razor, that the least complex of these models is provisionally accepted until further disconfirming evidence is found. Since the  n -numbers of Models 2d and 2e are equal and both less than the  n -number of Model 3, both of these models are provisionally accepted. On this view, Model 3 is only accepted if  both  Models 2d and 2e, as well as all other alternative models of equal complexity, can be falsified. Similarly, if it is possible to find a less complex model that is consistent with the data, then this model would be provisionally accepted instead of Models 2d and 2e.

Third, if a model is incorrect then given enough data of sufficient precision, it will eventually be falsified ( Forster, 2000 ). The crucial question is how to make this decision given imprecise and hence fallible data. In the approach taken above, we have relied on the principles of hypothesis testing to determine when the data are sufficient to reject a less complex model. While it may be possible to develop other approaches, classical hypothesis testing is both standard practice and a good way of trading simplicity against goodness-of-fit ( Forster, 2000 ). Thus, although the logic of model testing in SDA relies on falsification, the situation is no different from model testing elsewhere in science.

Coltheart and colleagues have proposed a model similar to  Eq. (1.4) , called the dual route model of reading, that is used to account for reading in normal and brain damaged individuals (e.g.,  Coltheart, 1985 ). According to this model, reading aloud of single words is accomplished by either of two possible processing routes. In the lexical route, phonology is accessed from stored lexical representations of known words. In the non-lexical route, phonology is assembled through the application of a set of grapheme–phoneme correspondence rules. This model was proposed, in part, to account for patterns of acquired dyslexia that affect the reading of regular words, irregular words, and non-words. The basic finding is that patients suffering from a condition called phonological dyslexia have a selective impairment in reading non-words, while their reading of regular and irregular words is relatively unimpaired and may even reach normal levels ( Coltheart, 1996 ). In contrast, patients suffering from a condition called surface dyslexia have a selective difficulty in reading irregular words, where they tend to make regularization errors, while their reading of regular words and non-words is relatively unimpaired ( Patterson, Coltheart, & Marshall, 1985 ).

The dual-route model proposes that reading regular words, irregular words, and non-words depends in different ways on each of the two processing routes. Successful reading of irregular words depends upon the lexical route, successful reading of non-words depends upon the non-lexical route, while successful reading of regular words may be accomplished by either route. In terms of  Eq. (1.4) , process  A  corresponds to the lexical route and process  B  corresponds to the non-lexical route. Thus,  p 
                         1  corresponds to the probability of reading an irregular word,  p 
                         2  corresponds to the probability of reading a non-word and  p 
                         3  corresponds to the probability of reading a regular word. According to the model, phonological dyslexia is explained by assuming patients have relatively low values of  b , compared to normal controls. Similarly, surface dyslexia is explained by assuming patients have relatively low values of  a , compared to normal controls.

The dual route model appears to be a plausible explanation of the two patterns of acquired dyslexia and the general principles of this account have been used to develop an extensive computational model of normal reading, called the DRC model ( Coltheart, Rastle, Perry, Langdon, & Ziegler, 2001 ). It is beyond the scope of this article to examine the implications of this model, however it is instructive to investigate the perspective offered by signed difference analysis. Two issues merit consideration. The first of these is that SDA reveals that the dual route model is formally inconsistent with the very data it seeks to explain.

Table 5  lists the set of permitted and forbidden signed difference vectors for the dual route model. A pure case of phonological dyslexia is characterized as having a selective impairment in reading non-words. In comparison to normal controls, this corresponds to a signed difference vector of  [0 +1 0]′  (index number 3 in  Table 5 ). But, as shown in the table, this vector is forbidden by the model. Similarly, a pure case of surface dyslexia is characterized as having a selective impairment in reading irregular words. In comparison to normal controls, this corresponds to a signed difference vector of  [+1 0 0]′  (index number 9 in  Table 5 ). Again, this vector is forbidden by the model. If the dual route model is formally inconsistent with both forms of acquired dyslexia, how can it be regarded as accounting for these syndromes? The answer is that the model accounts for the most obvious deficits, those affecting the reading of irregular words and nonwords. In both of these cases, however, the model also predicts a much smaller impairment in reading regular words. Thus, according to the model, phonological dyslexia is characterized by an impairment in the reading of both nonwords and regular words. This corresponds to the signed difference vector  [0 +1 +1]′  (index number 4 in  Table 5 ). Similarly, surface dyslexia is characterized by an impairment in the reading of both irregular words and regular words, corresponding to the signed difference vector  [+1 0 +1]  (index number 10 in  Table 5 ).

The second issue is that SDA identifies a set of differences that are inconsistent with the dual route model. From the foregoing, it is apparent that the prediction and detection of zero differences is problematic. In the first place, the model may be formally inconsistent with a null difference but avoid falsification because of the impossibility of proving the null hypothesis. In the second place, the model may be consistent with a null difference, but avoid falsification because the true difference is too small to detect. For these reasons, we suggest it is more useful to consider forbidden signed difference vectors that contain no zero components, corresponding to  n -zones of non-zero measure. As  Table 5  shows, the vector  [+1 +1 −1]′  is inconsistent with the dual route model. In relation to the single-case methodology which is often employed in studies of acquired dyslexia, it follows that the model predicts that it should be impossible to find two individuals, A and B, such that A is impaired relative to B in reading both irregular words and nonwords, and B is impaired relative to A in reading regular words. If two such cases were to be found, the dual route model would be falsified.

In this section, we compare and contrast SDA to three related approaches, state-trace analysis, conjoint measurement, and double dissociation. These comparisons help to place SDA in the context of related ideas and to show the extent to which these ideas converge upon similar issues and concerns. We conclude the section with a summary of the main features of SDA and some suggestions concerning its possible development and application.

State-trace analysis ( Bamber, 1979 ) comprises a conceptual framework within which models of the relationships between different dependent variables can be represented. It also incorporates a method for identifying and testing these relationships. While this conceptual framework is almost identical to that which underlies SDA, the two approaches differ in the way in which model predictions are derived and tested.

State-trace analysis (STA) is based on the observation that if  n  dependent variables are functions of  m < n  parameters, the set of all possible values of these variables will define an  m -dimensional manifold embedded in the  n -dimensional measure space. In the terminology of STA, the measure space is called a  state space , the manifold is called a  state-trace , and the function parameters are called  latent variables . Thus, if two dependent variables are functions of the same latent variable, the resulting state-trace is a one-dimensional curve in two-dimensional state space. Apart from a difference in terminology, this conceptualization is identical to that which underlies SDA.

STA and SDA differ primarily in terms of how the implications of different models are tested. In STA, the predictions of different relationships between dependent variables and latent variables are tested by a variant of function fitting. Different families of functions are searched in order to find the best fit to an observed (partial) state-trace. For example, if it is proposed that the state-trace is a one-dimensional curve in a two-dimensional state space, then it should be possible to find, among different families of one-parameter functions, one that fits the observed data with sufficient accuracy. Similarly, if it is proposed that the state-trace is a two-dimensional surface in a three-dimensional state space, then it should be possible find a two-parameter function that fits the observed data. Although both of these statements are true, in practice, this approach can be difficult to apply. Furthermore, since the procedure is essentially ad hoc, it does not allow prior identification of a set of outcomes that are inconsistent with all possible functions. This means that if no function is found to fit the data adequately, there is no guarantee that such a function does not exist. Finally, the space of possible functions increases dramatically for higher dimensional state-traces, as in Problem 2. It is unclear how such spaces should be searched and what plausibility constraints should be placed on candidate functions.

In SDA, model predictions are tested qualitatively rather than quantitatively. This means that, for a given model, it is possible to specify, a priori, a set of outcomes that are inconsistent with the model under all possible (monotonic) functions. Consequently, the model can be rejected if a single forbidden signed difference vector is observed since, given such a vector, it is impossible to find any set of monotonic measurement functions that would fit the data. Thus, SDA may be viewed as a test that can be performed prior to STA. If, using SDA, the data are shown not to be inconsistent with a given model, it may then be reasonable to investigate the form of its measurement mapping.

Signed difference analysis is a procedure for deriving testable consequences from models in which the relationship between model-specific constructs and dependent variables is unspecified but is assumed to be at least monotonic. As a result, the predictions derived by SDA are ordinal and consist of a partition of the set of possible signed difference vectors into those that are permitted under the model and those that are forbidden. If the set of forbidden signed difference vector is not empty then the model is, in principle, falsifiable using SDA.

SDA offers a means of testing models under very general conditions. However, it is necessary to note two present restrictions on the method. The first of these concerns non-orthomestic models. Although all of the models discussed in the present paper are orthomestic, this may not be true of many other psychologically plausible models. For example, a model that proposes that a dependent variable is a function of the distance between points in a parameter space (as in multidimensional scaling) is not orthomestic. Although there is no reason SDA cannot be applied to such models, its potential usefulness is currently limited by two factors. First, at present a general procedure for calculating the occupied set of an arbitrary non-orthomestic model does not exist. All that can be given is an upper bound ( Theorem 5 ). Second, the occupied set of a non-orthomestic model will tend to be much larger than that of a comparable orthomestic model. Consequently, it may be difficult or even impossible to reject such models using the logic of SDA.

The second restriction on the method concerns the question of testing predictions against fallible data. Although aspects of this question have been partially addressed, it is clear that further statistical development is required. Such work should address the appropriate form of the null hypothesis, as well as optimizing the power of the resulting test. Concerning the first point, in the approach taken in Problem 2, the null hypothesis was phrased in terms of signed difference vectors. However, it may be more appropriate to construct a null hypothesis in terms of the dependent variables themselves.

Despite the further work that needs to be done, SDA offers a unique means of investigating models that normally would not be thought to have testable consequences. It therefore enables the formulation of models of psychological phenomena solely in terms of an essential structural mapping, without having to specify the form of an inessential measurement mapping. We believe that this may have important implications for models that address human performance on multiple tasks.
