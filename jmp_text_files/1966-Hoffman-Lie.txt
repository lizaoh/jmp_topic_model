HOFFMAN  Oregon State University, Corvallis, Oregon*  The familiar perceptual constancies of image location in the field of view, image orientation, size constancy, shape  constancy, binocular distortion, and motion, have their natural mathematical expression in terms of Lie groups of  transformations over the visual manifold. If Lie's three fundamental theorems are to be satisfied, three additional perceptual  invariances must also be present: time, efferent binocularity, and what apparently constitutes some sort of circulating memory  in space-time. This Lie algebra of visual perception admits ready explanations for the following visual phenomena: the  developmental sequence of infant vision; orthogonal after-images; after-effects of seen movement; the spiral after-effect and  the spiral images sometimes evoked under flicker; reading reversals; and the visual analogue of the Fitzgerald contraction.  The theory also predicts certain new complementary (orthogonal) afterimages, the existence of which have been verified  experimentally.  I. INTRODUCTION  The visual image mapped by the lens of the eye upon the retina is a two-dimensional brightness distribution, that, apart from  its curved shape, is for all the world like the image mapped upon a photographic plate by the lens of the camera. Here,  however, the resemblance ceases. The retinal image is dissected by rods and cones, further by bipolar and ganglion cells, and  thence passes along a tortuous path back to the visual cortex, the visual stimulus being mediated en route and at its eventual  destination by inputs from various proprioceptors. In the visual cortex there occurs certain further processing which enables  us to "see" through reconstruction of the retinal image from the stimulation and interaction of countless tiny cortical  neurons. The topology and functioning of this process has remained an outstanding mystery.  The thing that apparently constitutes the key to this mystery is an observation that is at once both trite and obvious,  namely, that the retinal image is a surface. By the same token the visual manifold-i.e., the representation of the field of  view within the visual cortex of the brain-carries an intrinsic two-dimensional geometry (Davson, 1949; Zeeman, 1962, p. 244).  Of course, proprioceptor inputs-various postural and  * Work done at Boeing Scientific Research Laboratories.  65  5  66 HOFFMAN  optic reflexes, labyrinthine polarization, and the like-as well as visual cues determine the metric tensor of this  two-dimensional manifold and admit depth perception, masking phenomena, etc. However, the fact that no one has X-ray vision,  with the consequence that the visual manifold is two-dimensional (and thus can be pictured as the inside of a surface), is  essential to an understanding of how the visual cortex processes stimuli.  After all the processing and transformations that are imposed upon the visual image as it is transmitted along the visual  pathway, it finally appears in the visual cortex as a replica of the image itself but with something more. Part of this  "something more" involves the various invariances of perception. We recognize an object for what it is no matter where it may  be in the field of view-right or left, up or down. The same applies to its orientation, apparent size, shape projection, and  state of motion (so long as the object is not "moving too fast for the eye to follow"). Even though the image may be distorted  through being viewed binocularly, as has been demonstrated by Luneburg and others (Luneburg, 1947, 1948, 1950; Blank, 1953,  1958a, b, 1961; Gunther, 1961; Kienle, 1963; Shipley, 1957; Zajaczkowska, 1956a, b), it must still be recognizable and be in  accord with our everyday taetile experience. (A Helmholtz horopter is not really a straight line except at one particular  distance, and Blumenfeld alleys are not really "parallel" or "equidistant" with respect to the sagittal plane. The Ames  equivalent rooms, which are indistinguishable when viewed binocularly, cause severe distortions of size perception. The effect  of binocular distortion is also familiar to anyone who has ever wondered why the objects in his photograph of a landscape look  appreciably smaller and more distant than when he actually viewed the scene.) Thus in spite of all the geometric distortion  that can be imposed upon the visual image, it still makes its appearance after the first stage of visual processing in the  cortex as a complete and well-defined entity, "but without definite size or position, much as in ordinary visual mental  imagery" (Pitts and McCulloch, 1947). This surface, floating in the mind's eye, so to speak, that appears after the first  stage of processing in the visual cortex with all the contours and shapes of the visual image emblazoned upon it, is what we  shall call the visual manifold. It is a manifold in the technical, mathematical sense: it can be completely covered with a  countably infinite number of infinitesimally small circles, and each of these can be mapped in continuous fashion into the  plane. Since color and brightness are not included in the present analysis, the image will appear on the visual manifold as  the set of all contours and textures that go to make it up-the so-called "figure-ground" relationship.  The realization that the visual manifold is in essence a surface carrying the "figureground relation" is the key to the  understanding of the visual cortex. The perceptual invariances listed early in the preceding paragraph are capable of precise  mathematical formulation as Lie (pseudo) groups of transformations (Guggenheimer, 1963) over the visual manifold. We now  proceed to a heuristic, intuitive explanation of what this mathematical terminology implies.  THE LIE ALGEBRA OF VISUAL PERCEPTION 67  II. LIE TRANSFORMATION GROUPS  1. RESUME OF THE BASIC THEORY OF LIE TRANSFORMATION GROUPS  We first of all say what the word "group" means when used mathematically, and will then define "Lie transformation group" (or  "pseudogroup") and try to impart something of a basic idea of Lie group theory (and practice).  Think then of the cortical replica of the retinal image-that is, the visual manifoldas being subjected to a rotation, as for  example might happen when one views a scene from a prone position or a banking airplane. The result of such a rotation is  still another visual manifold. A set of transformations of this sort, wherein successive applications of members of the set  yield only members of the set, is said to be closed. This set of transformations has a unit element (namely the rotation  through a zero angle) and an inverse for each element (which amounts simply to rotating the manifold back through whatever  angle it was turned in the first place). Furthermore the transformations are associative: so long as the order of a sequence  of transformations is preserved, it is immaterial how they are grouped together in performing them. A set of transformations  that have these four properties-closure, existence of identity and inverse elements, and associativity-is said to form a  group, in the mathematical sense. Examples in daily life abound: the ornamental art consisting of a repetitive symmetric  pattern often seen on building friezes (the group of translations); crystals of snow, salt, etc. (the crystallographic  groups); and various organic objects, such as flowers, marine protozoans, the bilateral symmetries of the human body, etc.  (Weyl, 1952).  A Lie group of transformations is a group, as just defined, but of a "continuous" or "infinitesimal" kind. LIE TRANSFORMATION GROUPS FOR THE BASIC PERCEPTUAL INVARIANCES  With these preliminaries out of the way we now return to the basic perceptual "constancies" mentioned in the Introduction. The  basic ones (in the mature higher animal) are shape constancy, size constancy, and motion invariance. The recognition  invariances of object location in the field of view, orientation, and binocular vision (as distinguished from Cyclopean visual  perception) correspond respectively to the group of horizontal and vertical translations, the rotation group, and a group that  may be termed pseudo-Euclidean rotations. These are all subgroups of the planar affine group (or the special linear group)  (Kowalewski, 1950, p. 121), which expresses shape constancy. However, their presence is  necessary for a consistent theory, and a closer investigation of the pertinent neurophysiology does reveal their presence. The Two-Dimensional Lorentz Group  Burns (Glees, 1961, p. 403) found that the surface response of an isolated block of cortex to a single stimulus spreads  outward at a rate of 2m/sec. This value is in accord with that for the conduction velocity along fine nerve fibers of type C.  The existence of such a limiting value of propagation velocity for the cortical impulse is also argued by such phenomena as  critical flicker fusion. No matter how propagation of the cortical signal may take place, whether by dendritic fields, the  fine axons and apical dendrites of the plexiform layer, or whatever, all experimental evidence and inferences from  neurophysiology indicate that the afferent volley travels within the cortex at a finite velocity not greater than some upper  bound c. Though the value mentioned above may appear small, the scales of the cortex and the fovea (of the order  78 HOFFMAN  of fractions of millimeters and microns, respectively) indicate its adequacy for visual perception of objects not moving "too  fast for the eye to follow." The limiting value of cortical propagation speed will be called c in analogy to that of light  (which provides a similar upper limit to the speed of signal propagation in free space).  For a moving image on the visual manifold there will be two frames of reference: one fixed at the observer's center of vision  and the other moving with the image. IV. APPLICATIONS TO PERCEPTUAL PSYCHOLOGY  There are a number of perceptual anomalies-after-effects and visual illusions-that are primarily geometric in nature. There is  compelling evidence that such phenomena as MacKay's complementary (or, orthogonal) after-images, the Spiral After-Effect,  reading reversals (Hoffman, 1964), and the after-effect of seen motion are cortical in origin. All of these involve in essence  a reversal of same kind of symmetry, and so would come naturally within the sphere of the group-theoretic approach. The  phenomena just cited, as well as the Hering illusion, certain flicker phenomena, and a sort of visual relativistic length  contraction, are all readily explained in terms of our Lie algebra of visual perception, and we now pass to a consideration of  how this comes about. THE HERING ILLUSION  The term "complementary after-image" has been introduced by D. M. MacKay to describe the shimmering after-effect seen for a  few seconds on a blank background as a scurry of wavy lines-a sort of flow-moving essentially transverse to the original  stimulus pattern. A perhaps better term would be orthogonal after-images to avoid confusion with the complementary  after-effect, but we shall adhere to the original terminology. MacKay (1961) has employed the patterns shown in Fig. 7 to  demonstrate complementary after images. A streaming phenomenon, at low levels of illumination, that drifts at right angles to  the curves in such patterns can also be evoked. The fact that the stimulus pattern and the evoked complementary after-image  are essentially perpendicular to each other is the key feature that distinguishes this phenomenon from the well-known positive  and negative after-images that follow the sudden cessation of bright illumination of an object.  MacKay (1961) has used several techniques to evoke such complementary afterimages: abrupt cutting off of the illumination of  the pattern, stroboscopic illumination of the pattern, and projection of a motion picture film of random dot patterns using  the stimulus pattern as a mask. AFTER-EFFECTS OF SEEN MOVEMENT  With respect to the perception of motion, Wallach (1959) draws a distinction between motion perceived on the basis of an  "angular (or retinal) displacement" of an object relative to the observer and of an "object-relative displacement" of one  object with respect to another. The first may be regarded as objective motion, the second as motion perceived on the basis of  certain visual cues. The former is the kind of motion that is described by the two-dimensional Lorentz group Y Yms , and our  discussion is therefore limited to objective motion.  Certain after-effects of objectively seen movement have already been taken up in connection with the Spiral After-Effect. A  similar after-effect can be generated by an  94 HOFFMAN  even simpler experimental arrangement in which a disk with a single radial line is rotated for about one-half second and then  stopped. The line will then be seen to rotate counter to the original direction of motion at a speed that is essentially  independent of the angular velocity of the original stimulus. The evocation of complementary after-images from a single  contour by means of intermittently presented stimulus patterns has been described by Wilson (1960). The "Wasserfall Effekt",  in which, after long-continued viewing of a moving surface, such as a waterfall, rapidly flowing river, or highway from inside  a moving car, examination of a stationary surface appears to show a movement of this surface in the opposite direction, is  probably the most familiar of these after-effects of seen motion.  The characteristics of this type of after-effect have been well summarized by Wohlgemuth (1911):  "1. The uniform passage of light-stimuli over the retina in any given direction for a certain time produces the after-effect  of an apparent movement in the opposite direction.  "2. This after-effect is more marked if the eyes do not follow the movement, but remain fixed on a stationary point.  "3. The after-effect is limited to the area affected by the objective movement.  "4. The after-effect follows immediately after the stimulating objective movement. "5. The observation of the after-effect  improves with practice.  "6. If corresponding areas of the two retinae are stimulated by movements in opposite directions, these movements do not  neutralize one another but engender `rivalry.'  "7. If corresponding areas of the two retinae are stimulated by movements in different, but not opposite, directions, rivalry  and fusion of these stimulating objective movements occur. One eye alone open has predominantly its own after-effect, but both  eyes open produce complete fusion.  "8. The after-effect is producible by any rate of the stimulating objective movement, from the slowest which can at once be  sensed as movement to the highest short of flicker.  "9. If one eye alone is stimulated by an objective movement, whilst the other remains closed, the after-effect will appear in  the corresponding part of the visual field of the not-stimulated eye, but it will be somewhat feebler.  "10. Pseudo-movements, e.g., stroboscopic movements, produce an after-effect exactly as an actual movement does.  "11. The after-effect may also be noticed in the subjective field of vision, i.e., with closed eyes."  The after-effect of seen movement is known not to be of retinal origin, since, by a particular experimental arrangement, the  phi-phenomenon can be made to pass right through the blind spot where there are no receptors (Wyburn, Pickford, and Hirst,  1964, p. 178).
