A cognitive architecture (CA) is a unified theory of cognition which describes how sub-systems such as perception, motor action, and memory interact and function as a coherent system to produce a wide variety of human behaviors, ranging from simple behaviors to complex, goal-oriented behaviors (e.g.,  Anderson, 2007; Newell, 1990 ). Unlike a theory of a specific sub-system, such as visual perception, the scope of a CA is more comprehensive, striving to integrate findings across many domains and identify invariant properties in the structure and function of cognition.  Newell (1990)  argued that CAs are useful scientific tools for preventing myopia and fragmentation of theory in cognitive science. His call for action served as a catalyst for many years of research guided by CAs.

During the intervening time, a total of 84 CAs have been proposed, including 49 still under active development ( Kotseruba & Tsotsos, 2020 ). CAs are based on a wide variety of assumptions and theoretical motivations, which fall into the following broad categories: symbolic, hybrid symbolic/sub-symbolic, and neurally-based architectures. Symbolic architectures, such as EPIC ( Kieras & Meyer, 1997 ), represent knowledge as abstract symbols on which rules operate to produce higher-order cognition, such as reasoning. Hybrid CAs, such as ACT-R ( Anderson, Bothell, Byrne, Douglass, Lebiere et al., 2004 ), incorporate elements of symbolic architectures and sub-symbolic architectures. Other CAs, such as SPA ( Eliasmith & Anderson, 2003 ), place more emphasis on describing the neural systems underlying cognition and learning, and may vary in biological parity.

Several interesting questions arise in light of the plethora of CAs: why are there so many CAs, and how should they be analyzed and compared? Regarding the first question, the large number of CAs, to some extent, reflects a wide diversity of ideas for developing an integrated theory of cognition, which can also be specified at different levels of analysis. However, we also believe the large number of CAs stems from the lack of common framework for analyzing and comparing CAs, making it difficult to converge on a smaller set of viable candidates. This leads us to the second question: how should CAs be evaluated and compared? Previously, the Newell Test has been proposed as a method for testing CAs  Anderson and Lebiere (2003), Newell (1990) . The Newell Test is a minimal set of empirical phenomena and cognitive abilities a CA must satisfy to be considered viable. Although the Newell Test is useful for ensuring that a CA accounts for a wide range of empirical phenomena, it suffers from three limitations: (1) it cannot distinguish between multiple CAs which satisfy the test, (2) it does not directly test the core theory of a CA, and (3) it lacks a formal method for distinguishing between information processing characteristics, such as serial and parallel processes. In light of these limitations, we believe the Newell Test should be supplemented with other approaches, such as the one we present below.

One salient challenge in testing CAs is disentangling auxiliary assumptions from core architectural assumptions. A core architectural assumption refers to an invariant property of mental representation, cognitive functioning, or interaction between sub-systems that is central to the theory underlying the CA. By contrast, an auxiliary assumption refers non-critical aspect of a CA which can be changed, such as parametric and distributional assumptions. Theoretical progress occurs when empirical tests are designed to test core architectural assumptions, rather than auxiliary assumptions. Without the ability to disentangle auxiliary and core architectural assumptions, experiments provide little basis for distinguishing between CAs. In addition, this situation makes it difficult to understand the performance of CA on the Newell Test (e.g., is performance robust to changes in auxiliary assumptions?). Taken together, this creates a type of “credit assignment” problem where it is often unclear whether a prediction of a CA is due to auxiliary or core architectural assumptions, or a combination thereof (e.g.,  Duhem, 1954; Kellen, Davis-Stober, Dunn, & Kalish, 2021 ).

Distinguishing between auxiliary and core architectural assumptions is challenging for two reasons. First, compared to other modeling approaches, mathematical analysis of CAs is more difficult due to their sheer size and scope. Second, the hierarchical structuring of assumptions in many CAs leads to larger prediction spaces and thus greater flexibility. In isolation, an assumption regarding a sub-system might be considered a core assumption; however, the sub-system itself might be considered an auxiliary assumption at the level of the architecture. For example, numerous variations of ACT-R’s visual sub-system have been proposed (e.g.,  Nyamsuren & Taatgen, 2013; Salvucci, 2001 ), meaning results from one particular variation does not provide a clear test of the architecture. Any visual system can be substituted into the architecture, provided that it does not conflict with any core assumptions.

The fundamental question in which we are interested is the following: what constraints does a CA impose on its prediction space? In other words, when an architecture is stripped of auxiliary assumptions (e.g., distributional form, sub-systems, and other modifiable constructs), what behaviors does it produce — and more importantly — what behaviors does it preclude? If a CA provides no constraints on the prediction space, then it is merely an unfalsifiable, assortment of sub-systems arbitrarily combined together.

To fulfill this need, we implemented a global model analysis (GMA; e.g.,  Pitt, Kim, Navarro, & Myung, 2006 ) approach leveraging systems factorial technology (SFT;  Little, Altieri, Fific, & Yang, 2017; Townsend & Nozawa, 1995 ), resulting in a framework which we call SFT-GMA. SFT is a mathematical framework and experimental methodology for analyzing the information processing characteristics of a system along four dimensions—architecture, stopping rule, stochastic dependence, and workload capacity ( Townsend & Nozawa, 1995 ). GMA analyzes the entire prediction space of a model to identify testable constraints. SFT can be conceptualized as specific instance of GMA, but this relationship is often implicit in descriptions and applications of SFT. Our rationale for making the role of GMA in SFT explicit is to provide unfamiliar researchers with a systematic framework for testing core architectural assumptions using SFT. In the integrated SFT-GMA framework, architecture, stopping rule, stochastic dependence, and workload capacity form the basis for a SFT model space, and GMA is used to identify constraints the CA imposes on the SFT model space. Using SFT-GMA to analyze and test core architectural assumptions of CAs has three advantages: (1) the SFT model space is based on broad classes of non-parametric models which reduces reliance on auxiliary assumptions, (2) as with GMA methods in general, it can inform the design of critical tests to distinguish between CAs empirically (e.g.,  Pitt et al., 2006 ), and (3) SFT provides established and rigorous methods for carrying out empirical tests.

The remainder of the manuscript is organized as follows. We begin by distinguishing between local and global model analysis, and discussing different types of assumptions as they relate to CAs. Next, we introduce SFT to provide the necessary foundation for describing the combined SFT-GMA framework. After describing SFT-GMA, we use it to derive critical tests for ACT-R as a demonstration of the framework’s utility. To preview our main findings, the SFT-GMA analysis shows that despite many degrees of freedom in model specification, (1) ACT-R’s perceptual systems cannot process intra-modal stimuli in parallel, and (2) ACT-R’s perceptual systems can process cross-modal stimuli in parallel only in limited cases. Next, we review several experiments from the literature which contradict both of ACT-R’s predictions (e.g.,  Blaha, Busey, & Townsend, 2009; Eidels, Townsend, Hughes, & Perry, 2015; Howard, Garrett, Little, Townsend, & Eidels, 2021; Townsend & Nozawa, 1995; Wenger & Rhoten, 2020; Yang, Altieri, & Little, 2018 ). Finally, we conclude with a discussion of alternative assumptions, limitations, and future directions.

Local model analysis methods focus on the behavior of a model in a limited scope of its parameter space ( Pitt et al., 2006 ). An example of local model analysis is maximum likelihood parameter estimation which determines a single set of parameters that maximizes the probability of the data under a given model ( Myung, 2003 ). Interpreting a good model fit is challenging because local model analysis methods do not take into account the size of a model’s prediction space. Observing a good fit would be trivial if the model is capable of predicting a large array of patterns, including those not observed in the data ( Roberts & Pashler, 2000 ).

By contrast, global model analysis methods describe the entire space of a model’s predictions, thus allowing a modeler to understand the behavior of a model and contextualize a model fit in terms of flexibility ( Pitt et al., 2006; Roberts & Pashler, 2000 ). An example of global model analysis is parameter space partitioning, which identifies regions of a model’s parameter space corresponding to different qualitative patterns ( Pitt et al., 2006 ). Broadly speaking, models are based on two types of assumptions: auxiliary assumptions and core assumptions. Auxiliary assumptions are not central the underlying theory of a model, but are often required to test a hypothesis derived from the theory. For example, the survivor interaction contrast in SFT (explained below) requires the auxiliary assumption of selective influence ( Houpt, Blaha, McIntire, Havig, & Townsend, 2014; Townsend & Nozawa, 1995 ). Parametric assumptions are another type of auxiliary assumption, which are commonly selected based on criteria such as mathematical tractability and ease of interpretation. In signal detection theory (SDT), a typical parametric assumption is that the strength of a stimulus is represented as a Gaussian distribution along a single continuum ( Green et al., 1966 ). By contrast, a core assumption is central to the underlying theory, and cannot be changed without fundamentally altering the character of the theory. An example of a core assumption of SDT is  random-scale representation , which posits that stimuli are represented as ordered distributions on a latent strength dimension, and choice probabilities can be obtained by comparing these distributions ( Kellen, Winiger, Dunn, & Singmann, 2021 ). Importantly, random-scale representation implies a set of order constraints which do not rely on parametric assumptions regarding the distribution over the latent stimulus strength continuum ( Kellen, Winiger, et al., 2021 ).

Assumptions for some CAs can be organized as a hierarchy consisting of two levels: (1) an overarching architecture level, and (2) a sub-system level. We use the term  core architectural assumption  to distinguish core assumptions at the architectural level from those at a sub-system level. For example, the visual sub-system of ACT-R may consist of auxiliary and core assumptions, but both are auxiliary assumptions at the level of the architecture because the visual sub-system can be replaced or modified (e.g.,  Nyamsuren & Taatgen, 2013 ) without altering the architecture.  Fig. 1  depicts the relationship between auxiliary and core architectural assumptions for two CAs. The prediction space of each CA is partitioned by all possible auxiliary assumptions, which need not be disjoint.

Core architectural assumptions and auxiliary assumptions play different roles in defining the prediction space of a CA. Core architectural assumptions are responsible for constraining the prediction space, as they describe invariant properties in the structure and function of cognition. Adding more core architectural assumptions will likely decrease the prediction space of a CA, but will not expand the prediction space. One implication is that auxiliary assumptions are constrained by the core architectural assumptions. In other words, the auxiliary assumptions can be conceptualized as specific instantiations of a CA allowed by its core architectural assumptions. Therefore, adding more auxiliary assumptions has no bearing on the prediction space for the CA.

SFT is a mathematical framework and experimental methodology for evaluating the information processing characteristics of a system based on reaction time distributions ( Houpt et al., 2014; Townsend & Nozawa, 1995 ). The mathematical framework underlying SFT is based on broad classes of non-parametric models which vary along four dimensions: architecture, stopping rule, stochastic dependence, and workload capacity. The goal of using non-parametric models is to remove auxiliary assumptions regarding processing time distributions, and to derive rigorous tests which distinguish between information processing characteristics, such as serial vs. parallel processing of multiple information sources.

In this section, we describe the four dimensions which form the basis of a model space for SFT-GMA. Each dimension consists of different qualitative types ( Townsend & Nozawa, 1995 ). In total, there are 45 SFT models which can be defined by crossing each dimension factorially (see  Table 1 ). Excluding the coactive architecture, which can be thought of as a special case of a parallel architecture, the dimensions are orthogonal.

In SFT, architecture refers to the temporal ordering of cognitive processes ( Townsend & Nozawa, 1995 ). There are two basic types of architecture: serial and parallel. In a serial architecture, one process can begin only after the preceding process has completed, meaning there is no temporal overlap. By contrast, in a parallel architecture, multiple cognitive processes operate concurrently. In coactive architectures, evidence from multiples sources is processed concurrently, then is pooled across channels to make a decision. By contrast, in a standard parallel architecture each channel is evaluated separately.

Stopping rule refers to the manner in which information sources are combined and transformed into an overt decision ( Townsend & Nozawa, 1995 ). Consider a detection task in which zero, one, or two stimuli are presented simultaneously on each trial. A person using an exhaustive (i.e., logical AND) rule responds  yes  if two stimuli are present and  no  otherwise. Thus, all information sources must be evaluated before making an affirmative response. By contrast, a person using a first-terminating (i.e., logical OR) rule responds  yes  if at least one stimulus is present and  no  otherwise. Thus, an affirmative response is given as soon as the first stimulus is detected.

Stochastic dependence refers to whether or not cognitive processes influence each other ( Townsend & Nozawa, 1995 ). If two cognitive processes do not influence each other, they are said to be independent. However, if they influence each other, they are dependent, which can generally be grouped into two forms: facilitatory and inhibitory. In a facilitatory relationship, one cognitive process increases the speed of another cognitive process, whereas in an inhibitory relationship, one cognitive process decreases the speed of another cognitive process ( Eidels, Houpt, Altieri, Pei, & Townsend, 2011 ). Although in principle, a coactive architecture could also include dependencies among processes, because of the pooling prior to any observable behavior, dependencies are not identifiable. Similarly, a coactive model can be represented as a separate-channel parallel model with maximal facilitation.

Workload capacity measures how the rate of information processing changes as a function of the number of information sources a system must process. A system can be categorized into one of three categories based on workload capacity. Limited workload capacity occurs when the processing rate of at least one cognitive process slows down as more information sources are added. Unlimited capacity occurs when the processing rate of all cognitive processes are invariant to the number information sources. Finally, super-capacity occurs when the processing rate of at least one cognitive process increases as more information sources are added.

Coactive architectures are normally associated with super-capacity because, all else being equal, they are much faster than standard parallel (and serial) models. However, it is feasible that individual sources of information are processed slower in the context of other sources (e.g., the information contributed to the pool is slower than expected when pooled compared to when the information is processed on its own).

SFT is commonly used with an experimental paradigm called the double factorial paradigm (DFP;  Townsend & Nozawa, 1995 ). On each trial of a DFP, subjects are instructed to indicate the presence of stimuli using either an AND rule or an OR rule. The DFP consists of two nested manipulations within the same experimental design—one manipulation targeting workload and another manipulation targeting salience (DFP;  Townsend & Nozawa, 1995 ). Workload is manipulated by varying the number of stimuli presented simultaneously. For example, an experimenter can independently manipulate the presence or absence of two stimuli—one located at the top of the screen and the other at the bottom. The goal is to determine how the rate of information processing varies with the number of stimuli, and, in particular, to assess workload capacity and stochastic dependence of the cognitive system. Salience is manipulated by varying the strength or quality of each stimulus independently, such as brightness of a visual stimulus or loudness of an auditory stimulus. The goal is to make inferences about architecture and stopping rule by changing the speed with which a stimulus is processed. By manipulating both factors and using the measures described below, it is possible to make inferences about architecture, stopping rule, workload capacity ( Townsend & Nozawa, 1995 ), and stochastic dependence ( Eidels et al., 2011 ).

Throughout the paper, we will make use of two DFP experiments based on a visual detection task reported in previous research (e.g.,  Eidels et al., 2015; Townsend & Nozawa, 1995 ). In the intra-modality (IM-DFP) experiment, stimuli from the same modality are presented. For ease of exposition, we assume the stimuli are visual. However, other modalities could be used. A visual stimulus, such as an illuminated dot, can be presented at the bottom or top of the screen, and can be present or absent, resulting in three workload levels: 0 (both absent), 1 (bottom or top present), or 2 (both present). The CC measures workload capacity by comparing the processing throughput of a system for multiple stimuli relative to one. A model called an unlimited-capacity, independent and parallel model (UCIP) is used as a reference point for measuring the CC. According to the UCIP model, stimuli are processed independently in parallel and RT does not change as a function of the number of stimuli to process. The measures of CC introduced below are formulated so that the CC is one (or zero if the difference version is used) when the UCIP assumptions hold. In most applications of the CC, a ratio between the observed and predicted UCIP response times is used, assuming that a ratio is more appropriate for measuring degrees of change in response speed. Some applications focus on the difference instead of the ratio, following the form of the inferential statistics associated with the CC ( Houpt & Townsend, 2012 ). If the CC falls below the baseline, then some combination of the following is true: the architecture is serial, workload capacity is limited, or there is some inhibition among the processes. By contrast, if CC is above zero, then some combination of the following is true: the processes are coactive, the system has super-capacity, or there is some facilitation among the processes. Some of these possibilities can be ruled out by considering the CC in conjunction with the SIC.

In this section, we describe how SFT and GMA are combined into a single framework for deriving and empirically evaluating critical tests based on core architectural assumptions. The primary role of SFT in the combined framework is twofold: (1) to provide a model space of non-parametric model classes against which the predictions of a CA are compared, and (2) to evaluate critical tests with the SIC and CC. As shown in  Table 1 , the model space consists of 45 SFT models based on four dimensions: architecture, stopping rule, stochastic dependence, and workload capacity. The role of GMA in the framework is to define theorems which describe constraints on the SFT model space imposed by core architectural assumptions. Based on these results, critical tests can be developed and subsequently evaluated with the SIC and CC.

As a side note, elements of GMA have been combined with SFT in previous research to test classes of models. However, SFT can be applied to empirical data without the application of GMA, and the use and rationale for GMA is left implicit. Given our interest in systematizing the tests of core architectural assumptions and making the methodology approachable to the CA community, we explicitly describe the role of GMA and SFT within a common framework.

The application of SFT-GMA involves a sequence of four steps: (1) identify auxiliary assumptions and core architectural assumptions, (2) using core architectural assumptions, formulate theorems to define constraints on the SFT model space, and (3) derive critical tests and design an appropriate experiment, and (4) conduct the experiment and carry out the critical tests with established SFT methods (e.g., DFP, SIC, CC). Note that steps 3 and 4 depend on the results from step 2. For example, a critical test cannot be derived if the prediction space of a CA encompasses the entire SFT model space.

Broadly speaking, identifying auxiliary assumptions involves cataloguing components of a CA which can vary, such as parameters, distributions, functions, and other modifiable constructs. By contrast, identifying the core architectural assumptions involves cataloging invariant properties which constrain the behavior of the CA. Defining constraints on the prediction space involves formulating and proving theorems based on core architectural assumptions. This step is important to identify SFT models which could contradict the core architectural assumptions of the CA. Once constraints on the SFT model space are identified, the next step involves developing a critical test which could lead to an outcome the CA is unable to predict. The last step is to conduct the experiment and use the SIC and/or CC to evaluate the data. Below, we will illustrate this process with ACT-R.

In this section, we provide a brief overview of Adaptive Control of Thought-Rational (ACT-R;  Anderson, 2007; Anderson et al., 2004 ). For a more a through description of ACT-R, we refer interested readers to  Anderson et al. (2004) ,  Ritter, Tehranchi, and Oury (2019) , and the official ACT-R documentation ( Bothell, 2020 ). Next, to show how ACT-R relates to SFT, we describe how specific mechanisms in ACT-R can be manipulated to produce various SFT models. Additionally, we provide an intuitive, high-level explanation of a serial AND ACT-R model and a parallel AND ACT-R model of the CM-DFP to make the application of ACT-R to an SFT experiment more concrete. Finally, we introduce formal notation for ACT-R to facilitate the SFT-GMA analysis that follows.

ACT-R is a hybrid symbolic, non-symbolic CA ( Anderson, 2007; Anderson et al., 2004 ). The basic architecture of ACT-R is illustrated in  Fig. 3 , which consists of two fundamental structures: modules and buffers. Modules are specialized information processing units which perform functionally distinct operations. In ACT-R, modules include, but are not limited to, visual and auditory modules for perception, declarative and procedural memory modules, and a motor module for interacting with the external world via keyboard, mouse, or joystick. Buffers serve as a communication interface between the procedural module and the other modules. Buffers perform the following functions: (1) relay processing requests from the procedural module to the receiving module, (2) inform the procedural module of the state of a module (e.g., whether its busy processing a request), and (3) store the result of a request as a chunk of information.

ACT-R consists of two memory modules: declarative and procedural. The declarative memory module is responsible for encoding, storing and retrieving factual information. The basic unit of declarative knowledge is a set of slot-value pairs called a chunk. An example of a chunk with one slot-value pair is country-Canada. The procedural memory module operates as a production system in which procedural knowledge is represented as a set of buffer conditions and a corresponding set of actions called a  production rule  (PR). As an example, consider a PR for an OR response rule stated informally: “If a chunk is in the visual buffer, press the j key to respond affirmatively”. Cognition emerges over a series of production cycles in which a PR is selected and then its actions are executed. Each PR is associated with a utility value reflecting its ability to accomplish a goal. Utility is typically a function of similarity between the conditions in the PR and the conditions of the buffers, and random noise. On a given production cycle, the PR with the highest utility is selected and its actions are executed. The actions of a PR modify the buffer states, thus allowing the selection and execution of a different PR in the next production cycle. ACT-R can be described formally as a semi-Markov process whereby production cycles drive the transition from state to state ( Weaver, 2008 ).

A basic understanding of ACT-R’s perceptual systems is also important for our SFT-GMA analysis below which uses auditory and/or visual stimuli in the IM-DFP and the CM-DFP. ACT-R shares the commonly held assumption that audition and vision are comprised of a  where  system and a  what  system ( Bothell, 2020 ). Visual and auditory perception therefore follow a sequence of two steps in which the stimulus location is first identified and then the features of the stimulus are subsequently encoded. The auditory-location module and visual-location module identify the location of stimuli and correspond to the  where  system (not depicted in  Fig. 3 ). The visual and auditory modules encode the features of the stimuli and correspond to the  what  system. It is important to note that the visual-location and auditory-location modules are unique in two regards: (1) processing occurs instantaneously (i.e., there is no time cost), and (2) it is possible for each module to automatically orient to novel stimuli without a preceding request from a PR via a process called  buffer stuffing . When buffer stuffing occurs, a new stimulus of a given modality us automatically populated in the corresponding buffer.

The behavior of some aspects of ACT-R can be modified via custom functions called  hooks . Typically, hooks require specific inputs and outputs, but often do not impose restrictions on how the inputs are processed. An example of a hook from the declarative memory module is  :partial-matching-hook , which allows the modeler to define a custom dissimilarity function between a retrieval request and a chunk. Other hooks can be found throughout the architecture, including the procedural module, visual module, and auditory module.

To make the connection more concrete, we also conclude with a high-level description of two ACT-R models for the CM-DFP. ACT-R controls architecture through the structure and organization of PRs. One can produce a serial architecture in ACT-R by chaining together multiple PRs, such that the actions of one PR create the conditions for next PR to execute. By contrast, one can produce a parallel architecture in cases where the processing requests for different modules are combined into the action set of a single PR. When this PR is executed, the modules in the action set operate in parallel. In ACT-R, the stopping rule is determined by the conditions of PRs. For an OR rule, one PR issues an affirmative response if one stimulus is present, and another PR issues an affirmative response if the other stimulus is present. By contrast, one can create an AND rule with a PR that requires both stimuli to be present in order to respond affirmatively.

Stochastic dependencies and limited/super-capacity workload can be created within ACT-R only for serial processes. In ACT-R, stochastic dependencies between serial processes could be introduced through the sharing of buffer states across PRs, or through hooks designed to create correlations between the processing times. Similarly, limited capacity or super-capacity workload can be created via state sharing or hooks designed to make processing rate of serial processes depend on the number of preceding processes. Although the use of hooks to produce stochastic dependencies and to introduce limited/super-capacity workload is possible, it would require some psychological justification as their use in these contexts have not been explored.

Our goal in this section is to provide unfamiliar readers with a concrete example illustrating how ACT-R applies to SFT. Here we will focus on a high-level overview of a serial model and parallel model of the CM-DFP (see  Appendix A  for more details). Although we focus on a trial in which both stimuli are present, the logic extends to other cases in a straight forward manner. For both models, auditory and visual processing are independent and unlimited capacity. Additionally, both models implement an AND stopping rule. The key difference between the models is that the architecture is serial for one model but parallel for the other model. As previously noted, architecture is determined by the structure and organization of PRs. In this case, the serial model includes separate PRs to process each stimulus which are selected and executed one at a time, whereas the parallel model uses a single PR to process both stimuli simultaneously.  Fig. 4  uses a Gantt chart to illustrate how time course of cognition differs between the serial and parallel models.

We begin by describing the serial ACT-R model. Upon presenting the stimuli, a chunk representing the locations of the stimuli are populated into their respective location buffers via buffer stuffing. Next, the procedural module randomly selects one of two PRs: (1) a PR which shifts attention to the auditory stimulus or (2) a PR which shifts attention to the visual stimulus. In the case depicted in  Fig. 4 , the model attends to the visual stimulus first. Next, the procedural module selects a PR to find the location of the auditory stimulus. Once located (which has zero time cost), the procedural module selects a PR to attend to the auditory stimulus, causing the aural module to become active. After the attending to the auditory stimulus, the procedural module selects a PR to issue a response. In the present case, a  yes  response is issued because both the auditory and visual buffers contains a chunk. Different PRs handle the other three cases (e.g., one or both buffers do not have a chunk) which map to a  no  response.

The parallel model begins in a similar fashion to the serial model: chunks representing the location of the auditory and visual stimuli are forced into their respective location buffers via buffer stuffing. The primary difference between the parallel and serial models emerges at this point: the procedural module selects a PR which prompts the auditory and visual modules to simultaneously attend to the stimuli. Once both modules have attended to their respective stimulus, the procedural module selects a PR to issue a response. At this point, the parallel and serial models operate in a similar manner. A  yes  response is issued because both the auditory and visual buffers contain a chunk. However, different response PRs would be used to handle the other three cases in which one or more buffers do not contain a chunk.

Assumption 8 
                            Each perceptual module encodes one stimulus per chunk

Each perceptual module encodes one stimulus per chunk

We assume that the perceptual modules — namely, the visual and auditory modules — encode only one stimulus per chunk. For example, the ACT-R documentation states that when multiple sounds match the conditions of a PR, one sound will be selected at random and placed in the aural location buffer ( Bothell, 2020  p. 391). To be clear, this assumption does not imply that a stimulus must be encoded correctly, nor even at all: both errors of omission — failing to encode a property — and errors of commission — encoding the wrong property — may occur during encoding.

It should be noted that some flexibility remains because what constitutes a single stimulus is subject to debate. The segmentation of perceptual input into distinct stimuli is not well-specified in ACT-R. In this paper, we assume that the two separate dots in the IM-DFP are perceived as two distinct stimuli. However, if one argued that the two dots are perceived as a single stimulus, it would imply a specific coactive architecture. In the discussion, we explore the implications of relaxing our assumption.

From the core architectural assumptions above, we derive several constraints on ACT-R’s perceptual processing of multiple stimuli, and relate the constraints to the SFT models. In so doing, we make two assumptions throughout: (1) perceptual processing in ACT-R is sensitive to the salience manipulation in the DFP, and (2) response selection time is  not  sensitive to the salience manipulation. The first assumption is reasonable given that prior research robustly shows that the speed of perceptual processing depends on stimulus salience (e.g.,  Cattell, 1886; Grice, Nullmeyer, & Schnizlein, 1979; Pins & Bonnet, 1996; Ratcliff, 2002 ). By default, the perceptual processing times are not sensitive to stimulus salience in ACT-R. However, the default behavior can be overwritten with a hook so that perceptual processing time depends on stimulus salience. We believe the second assumption is plausible because salience information encoded into a chunk (e.g.,  
                            
                               { 
                               (brightness,high) 
                               } 
                            
                         ) is symbolic and therefore lacks a plausible physical mechanism to influence processing time of downstream processes—namely, conflict resolution time and motor execution time. Relaxing this assumption without imposing further constraints would enable ACT-R to produce any SFT model, making it unfalsifiable within the SFT-GMA framework.

Theorem 1 
                            
                               Perceptual processing of multiple stimuli in the same modality can only occur in serial.

Perceptual processing of multiple stimuli in the same modality can only occur in serial.

Proof

In this section, we leverage previous research to test ACT-R’s prediction of serial visual processing in the IM-DFP. In the seminal paper introducing SFT,  Townsend and Nozawa (1995)  conducted an IM-DFP experiment and found strong evidence for parallel processing contrary to ACT-R’s predictions. Subsequent experiments have replicated this finding, indicating that the evidence for parallel processing of visual stimuli is robust ( Eidels et al., 2015; Howard et al., 2021 ). More recently,  Wenger and Rhoten (2020)  conducted an experiment to examine perceptual learning in an IM-DFP using slightly more complex visual stimuli (see Experiment 2). When comparing pre-training and post-training,  Wenger and Rhoten (2020)  observed a general shift from limited-capacity, serial processing to supercapcity, parallel processing of visual stimuli. The shift to parallel processing is important because it conflicts with ACT-R’s prediction of serial processing of intra-modal stimuli, and indicates that evidence supporting serial processing could change with sufficient training. Collectively, these experiments provide strong and robust evidence against ACT-R’s prediction of serial visual processing in the IM-DFP. Based on this disconfirming evidence, one can conclude that at least one of the following assumptions is not supported:  Assumptions 4 ,  5 ,  6 , or  8 .

In this section, we leverage previous research to evaluate ACT-R’s prediction of serial or UCIP processing of cross-modal stimuli. Although multisensory integration has been of interest dating back many decades (e.g.,  Colonius & Diederich, 2004; Miller, 1982 ), we leverage an audiovisual discrimination experiment by  Yang et al. (2018)  as the most direct test of ACT-R’s predictions for the CM-DFP. In Experiment 1, participants completed an audiovisual discrimination experiment in which a visual target, an auditory target, or visual and auditory targets were presented ( Yang et al., 2018 ). In addition, the experiment included three analogous conditions using visual/auditory distractors instead of targets. As required by SFT, there were low and high salience versions of the visual and auditory stimuli. Contrary to ACT-R’s prediction of serial processing or UCIP, the SIC and CC signatures for all three subjects indicated parallel exhaustive processing with super-capacity/facilitatory dependence. This disconfirming evidence suggests that at least one of the following assumptions is not supported:  Assumptions 3 ,  6 ,  7 , or  8 .

A fundamental challenge in testing and comparing CAs is disentangling core architectural assumptions from auxiliary assumptions. Unless these assumptions are distinguished, it is unclear whether a given experiment tests a core theoretical commitment of a CA, or instead tests a technical detail which is not critical to the underlying theory. We believe that the lack of common framework for comparing CAs in terms of core architectural properties has hindered theoretical progress, leading to a proliferation of CAs without a clear path for ruling out non-viable candidates. What is needed is a framework for deriving testable predictions from core architectural assumptions while minimizing the role of auxiliary assumptions.

As a first step towards addressing this challenge, we developed SFT-GMA which uses an SFT model space as a basis for global model analysis. To demonstrate the utility of SFT-GMA, we applied it to ACT-R, a widely used CA. Although ACT-R provides many degrees of freedom in terms of parameters, hooks, optional modules, and the specification of declarative and procedural knowledge, we were able to identify important architectural constraints on ACT-R’s ability to process stimuli in parallel. In particular, SFT-GMA revealed that (1) ACT-R is unable to produce any parallel models when processing intra-modality stimuli, and (2) ACT-R can only produce serial or UCIP models when processing cross-modality stimuli. Leveraging previously published experiments, our analysis shows that both predictions are at odds with reported empirical findings. Collectively, these results cast doubt on at least one of ACT-R’s core architectural assumptions.

In this section, we consider the consequences of relaxing some core architectural assumptions listed above. This is important for two reasons. First, it allows us to assess the degree to which a core architectural assumption constrains the ACT-R’s prediction space. Second, in the absence of an official list of core architectural assumptions, others could object to the list we compiled. For this reason, readers may be interested in considering the implications of alternative assumptions.

Relaxing  Assumption 3  — which holds that processing units are modular and encapsulated — would greatly increase the flexibility of ACT-R, allowing it to produce all possible patterns in the CM-DFP. Encapsulation precludes the cross-talk between modules required to support parallel processing with stochastic dependencies, coactive channels, or limited/super workload capacity. However, relaxing the assumption of encapsulation would not affect the predictions for the IM-DFP because it pertains to a single module rather than multiple modules. Thus, the critical tests based on the IM-DFP would still be viable.

Relaxing  Assumption 8  — which prevents perceptual modules from encoding multiple stimuli into a single chunk — would have the opposite effect. By allowing perceptual modules to encode more than one stimulus into a chunk, ACT-R would be able to produce all serial and all parallel SFT models in the IM-DFP. However, relaxing  Assumption 8 , leaves the predictions for the CM-DFP unchanged. As a result, the CM-DFP could still be used for critical tests. One implication is that relaxing both  Assumptions 3 and 8  would allow ACT-R to produce all SFT models in both the IM-DFP and CM-DFP, thus rendering ACT-R unfalsifiable within SFT-GMA.

An interesting question related to  Assumption 8  is what constitutes a stimulus? In our reasoning, we made an implicit assumption that the redundant target consisting of two dots is processed as two separate dots. One could potentially object to this assumption and instead argue that the redundant target is processed holistically as a single stimulus. Under this alternative assumption, ACT-R could produce coactive models in the IM-DFP. Nonetheless, the IM-DFP could still be used for critical tests because ACT-R would be unable produce other parallel models. The predictions for the CM-DFP would remain unchanged.

It is important to emphasize that our list of core assumptions is not exhaustive. As a starting point, we focused on assumptions with which many in the ACT-R community would likely agree. Nonetheless, in our initial analysis, we could have missed some core assumptions. What implications might this have for our conclusions? Generally speaking, adding more core assumptions will not increase the flexibility of the architecture, and may, in fact, decrease its flexibility. In the worst case scenario, we may have overestimated the flexibility of ACT-R and missed the opportunity to derive additional critical tests.

Our analysis focused primarily on core assumptions at the level of the architecture, which includes processing restrictions on modules and capacity restrictions on buffers. However, core assumptions could be specified at the level of individual modules. As a reviewer pointed out, the standard functional form of base-level activation is an auxiliary assumption, but one could argue that base-level activation should satisfy two core assumptions: (1) retrieval time of a chunk is a weakly decreasing monotonic function of number of prior uses, and (2) retrieval time is a weakly increasing monotonic function of time since the last use.

                          Although these are reasonable restrictions to impose on a learning mechanism, deriving and conducting a critical test is challenging because the properties of base-level learning may depend on the core assumptions of other activation components, which have not been specified to the best of our knowledge. Consider a situation in which the activation of chunk A increases during a time interval in which it was not used, leading to evidence against the second assumption. One way this situation could occur is if the spreading activation mechanism was modified such that activation transfers between chunks proportionally to their similarity and activation levels (and does not depend on chunks currently in buffers). In this case, the activation of chunk A could increase if the activation of a similar chunk B was increased from usage during this time interval. Although this is not how spreading activation currently works in ACT-R, we are not aware of an architectural restriction that would prohibit a modeler from implementing this modification. We believe these issues should be addressed in future research.

Using SFT for global model analysis has several limitations worth noting. One limitation of SFT is that the focus on process means that it is relatively neutral on the important role of mental representation in cognition beyond the constraints on representation entailed in the assumed selective influence relationships. In SFT, the contribution of mental representation can only be measured insofar as it influences reaction time distributions, thereby leaving some predictions unexplored. Nonetheless, SFT’s theorem-based approach of differentiating the predictions of qualitatively distinct classes of models could in principle be applied to mental representations. Along these lines, recent research has began integrating SFT with general recognition theory (a multivariate generalization of signal detection theory) which is concerned with mental representation ( Ak, Wenger, Townsend, & Newbolds, 2023; Liu, Townsend, & Wenger, 2023 ). Integrative approaches of this nature have the potential to form more powerful critical tests than SFT by itself.

Another limitation is that some SFT models are not identifiable from empirical data. As a result, situations may arise in which two CAs make divergent predictions which cannot be unambiguously distinguished using SFT measures, such as SIC and CC. In such situations, one may consider comparing the CA’s under a wide variety of assumptions. For example,  Eidels et al. (2011)  used simulation methods to explore multiple, large classes of parallel interactive models across a wide range of parameter values. Although their simulation did not exhaust all possible parallel interactive models, they were able to identify the models using a combination of SIC and CC.

SFT-GMA follows closely in the tradition of Popperian falsification whereby the researcher attempts to find evidence that rules out a theory rather than confirms it ( Popper, 1959 ). One benefit of this approach is that allows modelers to obtain strong evidence against a theory. However, two drawbacks are worth noting. First, dividing the prediction space into possible and impossible regions results in an asymmetry in which strong evidence can be obtained against a theory, but no distinctions regarding degree of support can be made for the possible regions. Using a Bayesian approach as an alternative would allow the modeler to assign graded levels of plausibility to regions of the prediction space which can be updated with empirical data (e.g.,  Jaynes, 2003; Vanpaemel, 2010 ). One downside of this approach is that the modeler must commit to auxiliary assumptions regarding the prior distribution and likelihood function, which would defeat our goal of avoiding auxiliary assumptions. A second limitation with SFT-GMA is related to the Duhem–Quiene thesis, which states that it is impossible to falsify a theory because disconfirming evidence could be attributed to either a false core assumption, a false auxiliary assumption, or both ( Duhem, 1954; Quine, 1951 ). Although we can avoid the trappings of auxiliary assumptions in the derivation of the critical tests, they cannot be avoided when evaluating data with statistical tests, which rely on their own auxiliary assumptions. Nonetheless, by removing one source of auxiliary assumptions, we argue that approaches like SFT-GMA provide stronger, albeit imperfect tests.

One desirable feature of SFT-GMA is its generality: it can be applied to any CA for which the mechanisms and processes are understood and predictions can be formulated in terms of reaction time distributions. Given the wide variety of CAs currently under study, an obvious direction for future research is to use SFT-GMA to compare CAs with theoretically distinct foundations. A comparison of CAs using SFT-GMA could potentially reveal important insight regarding basic properties and constraints on human cognition.

Another potential direction for future research is to further develop methods to apply SFT with neural imaging to improve identifiability (e.g.,  Altieri & Wenger, 2013; Wenger, Ingvalson, & Rhoten, 2017 ). One advantage of neuroimaging is that it provides a more direct measure cognitive processing compared to reaction time data, which could resolve model identifiability issues in some cases. Neural-based methods such as the hidden semi-Markov model with multivariate pattern analysis ( Anderson, Zhang, Borst, & Walsh, 2016; Borst & Anderson, 2015 ) and dynamic causal modeling ( Friston, Harrison, & Penny, 2003; Stocco et al., 2021 ) have been important in understanding cognitive processing, but neuroimaging approaches may benefit from the model space and non-parametric approach of SFT to form stronger tests of CAs. Thus, more powerful methods could emerge by adapting SFT to neuroimaging, leading to downstream improvements for SFT-GMA.

An important role of CAs in cognitive science is synthesizing research across many domains of cognition into a coherent unified system, and identifying invariant properties of cognition. Due to their large size and scope, CAs present unique challenges for evaluating core architectural assumptions, leading to a need for a common framework and language. We believe theorem-based approaches, such as SFT-GMA, provide a productive path for theoretical progress by shifting focus to core architectural assumptions and providing tools for developing and executing critical tests.

The CM-DFP is double factorial experiment in which stimuli correspond to different modalities. In this case, one stimulus is auditory whereas the other stimulus is visual. Otherwise, the design is similar to the IM-DFP: the number of visual stimuli are varied from 0 to 2, and the brightness of the visual stimulus (low vs. high) and loudness of the auditory stimulus (low vs. high) serves as a salience manipulation.
