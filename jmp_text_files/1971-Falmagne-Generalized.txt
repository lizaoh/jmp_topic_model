FALMAGNEI  University of Brussels, Brussels, Belgium  Fechner's proposal of measuring sensation using discrimination thresholds has been criticized by a number of authors. It can  be argued, however, that the historical connections between the experimental study of sensory thresholds and the problem of  measuring sensation is fortuitous. In fact, Fechner's scheme of psychophysics can be generalized in a straightforward manner  to arbitrary discrimination indices. This raises immediately some interesting questions such as: What conditions must satisfy  two discrimination indices to yield identical scales? These and related questions are investigated from a theoretical and  experimental standpoint. The paper also contains a discussion of the usual questions of existence, uniqueness, and  construction of solutions to Fechner's Problem.  I. INTRODUCTION  What is meant by the Generalized Fechner Problem can be stated intuitively as follows:  PROBLEM F. Find a psychological scale such that equidiscriminable pairs of stimuli are represented by equal (scale)  differences.  We discuss this problem which is formulated more precisely below (Problem F) from both psychological and mathematical  standpoints. We make no claim that the psychological scale is a measure of "sensation". Problem F is thus much more modest  than what Fechner had in mind. It is, however, a generalization of his scaling ideas in the sense that it is raised for an  abstract discrimination index, which in practice can be different from the choice probabilities that provided the empirical  foundations for classical psychophysics.  The critical problem is to say what we mean by "equidiscriminable pairs of stimuli."  1 Charge de Recherche at the Fonds National pour la Recherche Scientifique, Brussels, Belgium. This choice, however, is by no means  necessary. Other methods could have been used as well. This remark is important in view of a number of criticisms raised by  Fechner's approach of psychophysics. For example, Stevens (1957) has discarded Fechner's scaling methods on the grounds that  they are based on a statistical analysis of the subject's inconsistencies, and thus on data that are irrelevant to the measure  of "sensation." A weaker form of this criticism calls such methods indirect (Woodworth and Schlosberg, 1954). Luce and  Galanter (1963) argued that these methods rely upon some assumption that permits piecing together the scale from highly local  information (as given by the psychometric functions). Clearly, such critiques concern the particular measure of  discriminability used by Fechner, not his scaling ideas. The obvious answer is to apply Fechner's scheme of psychophysics to  some other measure that would bypass those criticisms-a reaction time for instance (examples are discussed in details in  Sections II and V). A more serious objection can, however, be raised against Fechner, that would hold whatever measure of  discriminability is used to derive a scale of sensation. At present, it is premature to associate such a basic psychological  concept as sensation with one particular method. What if different methods yield different scales ? Clearly, if it could be  shown that a number of discrimination measures lead to the same scale, we would be on safer grounds to adapt this scale, as a  measure of sensation. This suggests two questions. Under what conditions will two discrimination measures yield the same scale  ? Are these conditions fulfilled experimentally? Answering these two questions is the primary motivation of this paper.  We formalize Problem F as follows. II.3 Synchronization of the Perception of Two Stimuli  The intensity of a stimulus is known to affect, in some instances, the moment of its perception. This has suggested the  following paradigm, which I will describe briefly. At each trial two stimuli x and y of the same (short) duration are  presented to the subject. His task is to perform, on a succession of trials, an adjustment of the delay (which can be positive  or negative) between the onset of x and the onset of y in such a manner that x and y finally appear to be presented  simultaneously. Let  26 FALMAGNE  D(x, y) be this final value. (In practice, D(x, y) is an average value. As in the method of the limits, the adjustment is  carried out a number of times with various initial values of the delay.)  This experiment has been performed using visual stimuli by Alpern (1954), Arden and Weale (1954), and Roufs (1963). It was  found that D(x, y) varies with (the intensities of) x and y. The following relation was shown to fit the data reasonably well:  D(x, y) = A log y , (4)  where A is a constant (Roufs, 1963). This result suggests that the following definition would make sense:  G(x, r) = y iff D(x, y) = r.  An account and a discussion of Roufs' results is given in Section V.  III. CONCLUDING REMARKS  When one realizes that Fechner's classical problem can in fact be raised for almost any measure of discriminability, some  questions arise immediately concerning the results-or possibly the scales-obtained in the various cases. Two types of  arguments in this paper point to the possibility that a unique scale could explain, in the sense of Problem F, discrimination  data collected by a variety of experimental methods.  On the one hand, the discussion of Section IV suggests that such a possibility is not unlikely on the basis of a simple  mathematical argument. Theorem 9, or more precisely its proof, indicates that if a discrimination index M yields a given  scale, then any discrimination index would yield the same scale if it is strictly monotonic related to (and if its domain is  included in the domain of) M. In other words, if there exists a Fechnerian discrimination index, there certainly exists a  large number of Fechnerian discrimination indices consistent with it. Take, for example, a reaction time situation. Suppose  that the mean reaction time, considered as a discrimination index, turns out to be Fechnerian. It is frequently observed that  when the mean reaction time increases, the variance also increases. The above argument shows in this case that the variance  would also be a Fechnerian discrimination index consistent with the mean. Notice in passing that this remark should influence  our position toward the choice of a discrimination measure. There is a natural feeling of uneasiness created by the  arbitrariness of this choice, as compared to the supposedly fundamental character of the scale. The discussion suggests that  this feeling is not really justified.s  There is, on the other hand, some experimental evidence to support it. Pieron's (1936) and Roufs' (1963) experiments discussed  in Section V should be replicated. As they stand, however, they point out that a basic discrimination scale might very well  exist. A logarithmic function has been obtained as a scale in the four methods that have been discussed (via Weber's Law (18)  in the case of Pieron's experiment).  This, of course, is far from being conclusive evidence that "sensation" is basically a logarithmic function of stimulus  magnitude, but has certainly reduced the impact of some of the criticisms raised against Fechnerian psychophysics. By and  large, the most serious criticisms, such as those of Stevens (1957) and Luce and Galanter (1963) mentioned in Section I, have  been of the methods.
