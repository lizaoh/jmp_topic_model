POLTROCK  Microelectronics and Computer Technology Corporation, Austin, Texas  To choose the greater of two digits, information in memory about the digit values must be compared. Like comparisons of values  on perceptual dimensions, the response time and the probability of an error for digit comparisons increase as the difference  in the digit values decreases. A random walk model of the comparison process explains these distance effects. Six subjects  participated in three experiments to test the model. When model parameters were estimated from comparison data for all digit  pairs, the estimated internal magnitude of the digit one was far from all other digits, implying that comparisons with the  digit one generally require only a single step in the random walk. When comparisons with the digit one were excluded, the  estimated internal magnitudes of the remaining digits were nearly linear. The model accounted for general features of the  relationship between speed, accuracy, and the digit values, as well as the effects of response time deadlines. Modifications  to the model are considered to account for the observed dependence of response biases on stimulus values. 1989 Academic Press,  Inc.  How do people decide which of two digits is greater, which of two named animals is larger, or which of two artists is more  talented? Each decision requires comparison of information that is available in memory but not in the external stimulus. For  example, there is nothing in the word elephant to suggest that it is larger than an armadillo; the decision must be based on  knowledge about the animals' sizes. When such knowledge is readily available, decisions are reached quickly. In half a second  or less, people can decide which of two digits is greater and indicate their choice by pressing a response key. They cannot,  however, report how the decision was reached. They report "knowing" that one digit is greater than another, or that one animal  is larger than another. The comparison process underlying this experience of knowing is inaccessible to introspection.  To investigate the comparison processes underlying these decisions, psychologists have analyzed response times (RTs) and  accuracy. Similarly, Moyer (1973) found that, when choosing  the larger of two named animals, RTs and the frequency of errors decrease as the difference in the sizes of the animals  increases. These results for digit comparisons and animal size comparisons are strikingly similar to results of psychophysical  experiments in which perceptual dimensions of stimuli are compared. When choosing the longer of two lines or the brighter of  two lights, for example, people require more time and make more errors when there is little difference in the dimension of  comparison. Indeed, an equation proposed by Welford (1960) as a general empirical model of RTs in psychophysical experiments  approximates Moyer and Landauer's (1967) RTs for digit comparisons.  Welford's model can be written as  RT = a + b log((minimum/distance) + 1), (1)  where distance is the difference in stimulus values and minimum is the smaller of the two stimulus values. If this model is  applicable to digit comparisons as well as perceptual comparisons, then RTs for digit comparisons should depend on the value  of the smaller digit as well as the distance between digits. Indeed, Parkman (1971) found that RTs for digit comparisons  increase as the smaller digit increases, an observation that led him to propose a counting model of digit comparison. However,  Moyer and Landauer (1973) showed that the Welford model for perceptual comparison fits both their own data (Moyer & Landauer,  1967) and Parkman's data better than models that depend only on distance or only on the minimum.  The similarity of results obtained for comparisons of properties retrieved from memory and comparisons of perceptual  dimensions suggests that similar processes underly all these comparisons. Thus, Moyer and Landauer (1967) proposed that digits  are converted to analog magnitudes or "percept-like entities" (Moyer & Landauer, 1973, p. 168). They suggested that the same  processes responsible for comparison of perceptual dimensions also compare the analog magnitudes corresponding to digits.  Similarly, Moyer (1973) proposed that animal sizes are compared by an internal psychophysical judgment. The animal names are  converted to analog representations that preserve the animal sizes, and these analog representations of size are compared by  the same processes that compare perceptual dimensions.  The attractiveness of this theory is its parsimony. Similar results for comparisons of different kinds of stimuli are assumed  to come from similar mechanisms. The disadvantage of the theory is that it stops short of explaining how values on memorial or  perceptual dimensions are compared. The theory merges two unsolved problems into one unsolved problem. What mechanism is  causing the observed effects of comparative difference and minimum on RT and error rate for both memorial and perceptual  dimensions? The objective of this research is to explore a class of comparison models that could account for both perceptual  and mental comparisons.  RANDOM WALK MODEL OF DIGIT COMPARISON 133  Random walk models, described in detail below, account for many of the behavioral phenomena of perceptual comparisons. This  research evaluates random walk models of mental comparisons, particularly comparisons of digits.  RANDOM WALK MODELS  Qualitative Characteristics  Ultimately, a complete description of a comparison mechanism should include the biological states that represent stimulus  magnitudes in the brain as well as the biological processes that compare these states. For current purposes, it is sufficient  to note that these magnitudes are represented in a noisy environment, the human brain. Consequently, internal analog  representations of stimulus magnitude should be considered random variables with some unknown distributions. Because the  analog representations are random variables, reliable comparisons of two different but similar stimulus magnitudes require  accumulating information about the difference in magnitudes for some time, with reliability increasing as the time increases.  Random walk models describe how a decision is reached using this accumulated information. According to these models, some  quantity is computed that represents the evidence observed during a small temporal interval that favors one alternative, and  this quantity is accumulated. The value of the quantity computed in each interval varies because the internal representations  of the stimuli vary. When comparing magnitudes of two stimuli, the accumulated value will tend to be positive if one stimulus  is greater and negative if the other stimulus is greater, but from one interval to another it follows a random walk. A choice  between stimuli is reached at the end of the first temporal interval that the absolute accumulated value exceeds a boundary or  Random walk models can account qualitatively for the effects on RT of the minimum stimulus and the difference between stimuli.  As the difference between stimuli increases, the mean quantity computed in each temporal interval increases and the  accumulated value reaches a boundary after fewer intervals, reducing RT. A minimum effect occurs if the absolute value of the  computed quantity decreases as the stimulus values increase. A quantity that has this property is the difference between the  logarithms of the stimulus values. Of course, minimum effects are not a necessary part of random walk models, but any adequate  model of digit comparison must be able to account for this behavior.  In addition to the minimum and distance effects, random walk models can account qualitatively for other characteristics of  perceptual and mental comparison performance that often are outside the scope of information processing models. For example,  errors are not considered an aberration, but occur because of variability in the accumulated information; on some trials the  random walk reaches the wrong boundary. POLTROCK  affects both error rate and decision time in the model because the random walk is more likely to reach the correct boundary,  and reach it more quickly, when the mean of the distribution of differences is large. Furthermore, tradeoffs of speed and  accuracy are modeled by changes in the decision boundaries. As the boundaries move away from the starting point of the random  walk, the mean decision time increases and the probability of an error decreases. Response biases introduced by instructions  or response probabilities are modeled by changes in the initial value of the accumulated difference (i.e., the starting point  of the random walk). In fact, receiver operating characteristics obtained by manipulating response bias in detection models  can also be obtained by simple strategies for selecting the random walk starting point (Link & Heath, 1975).  Research by Buckley and Gillman (1974) illustrates how well the decision times of a random walk model correspond qualitatively  to mental comparison RTs. They proposed a random walk model of digit and numerousity comparisons in which the quantity  computed at each step is the difference between the logarithms of the stimulus values perturbed by some random value. This  quantity is repeatedly computed and accumulated in the model until the accumulated value exceeds a boundary. Computation and  accumulation of these differences requires a constant time interval, so the total decision time is proportional to the number  of repetitions required to reach a random walk boundary.  Buckley and Gillman (1974) compared decision times produced by this model with RTs of subjects who decided either which of two  digits was greater or which of two patterns contained more dots. Reasoning that RTs should increase as similarity between the  internal representations of the stimuli increases, they scaled the mean RTs and their model's decision times using nonmetric  multidimensional scaling. In summary, random walk models account qualitatively for many of the phenomena that characterize both perceptual and mental  comparisons, including response biases, speed-accuracy tradeoffs, and effects of stimulus characteristics on both RTs and  errors. Perhaps the mechanism that compares both perceptual and memorial magnitudes is equivalent to a random walk model. This  hypothesis would be strengthened if random walk models successfully described quantitative characteristics of perceptual and  mental comparison behavior.  Quantitative Characteristics  More rigorous tests of random walk models require derivation of their quantitative characteristics. However, additional  assumptions are necessary to obtain any quantitative predictions about relationships among observable variables. The  quantitative consequences of several alternative assumptions about the quantity accumulated in each interval have been  explored. For example, Stone (1960) proposed a random walk model of choice reaction time in which the quantity accumulated is  the logarithm of the likelihood ratio, which has the virtue of being an optimal decision strategy. This has not proven to be a  satisfactory model of human decision making, however, because it requires equal RTs for correct and incorrect responses, a  requirement that is generally inconsistent with experimental results.  More successful models of perceptual comparison have been based on Link's (1975, 1978a, 1978b; Link & Heath, 1975) Relative  Judgment Theory. According to this theory, stimuli are compared by repeatedly subtracting an internal psychophysical referent  from an internal representation of the stimulus event, accumulating this difference until the random walk reaches a boundary.  Within this framework, models may vary in assumptions about the internal representations of the referent, the stimulus event,  and consequently the quantity that is accumulated in each interval. For example, Link (1975) accounted for stimulus bias by  assuming that the referent is determined by stimulus presentation frequencies when subjects judge which of two stimuli is  presented and the stimulus event is the internal representation of the presented stimulus. When two stimuli are presented  136 STEVEN E. POLTROCK  simultaneously, Link (1978b) assumed that the stimulus event is the difference between the stimuli. The internal  representation of one stimulus is subtracted from the internal representation of the other stimulus, then the referent is  subtracted from this difference to account for any stimulus bias.  Link has explored the quantitative implications of two alternative assumptions about the internal representations of the  stimuli. For experiments in which stimuli vary over only a small range of magnitudes, Link (1978b) assumed that the internal  analog magnitude is well approximated by a similarity function of the physical stimulus magnitude. Given this assumption, the  mean step size of the random walk (i.e., the amount added to the accumulator in one interval) when comparing two  simultaneously presented stimuli is proportional to the difference between the magnitudes of the two physical stimuli minus  the magnitude of the internal referent. Link (1978b) derived a functional relationship between mean RTs and accuracy that is  required by this model. Analyzing data from brigthness discrimination and line length discrimination experiments, he found  that mean RTs and accuracy were consistent with the required relationship. Furthermore, he showed that effects of RT deadlines  on the relationship between mean RTs and accuracy could be modeled by changes in the boundaries of the random walk. A  disadvantage of this assumption for present purposes is that the model cannot produce a minimum effect. The internal  representations of the stimuli must be a negatively accelerated function of stimulus values to produce a minimum effect within  Relative Judgment Theory.  Another assumption explored by Link (1975) is symmetry of the random walks for two stimuli. Consider a task in which a subject  must decide which of two stimuli is presented. In each of many short temporal intervals, according to Relative Judgment  Theory, the referent is subtracted from the analog magnitude of the presented stimulus. The result is a random variable d with  some distribution. According to the symmetry assumption, the probability of any difference d when one stimulus is presented is  equal to the probability of -d when the other stimulus is presented. Consequently, the two stimuli cause random walks toward  opposite boundaries but at equal mean rates.  Given this symmetry assumption, the starting point and the boundaries of the random walk can be estimated (Link, 1975).  Furthermore, mean RTs, according to the model, must be linearly related to a function of the response probabilities (shown  below in Eq. (5)). Link (1975) analyzed data from two-alternative choice response experiments in which the stimuli were two  lines of different length, two lines of different orientation, or the digits 1 and 2. He found that experimental manipulations  of stimulus probability and the response-stimulus interval affected the estimates of the model parameters in ways that are  consistent with the model. Manipulating stimulus probability affected the starting value of the accumulator, which corresponds  to response bias, but had little effect on the random walk boundaries. Manipulations of the response-stimulus interval, on the  other hand, affected the boundaries but not the starting value. Furthermore, the mean RTs and response probabilities were  consistent with the required linear function. The model of digit comparison proposed in this article includes this symmetry  assumption.  RANDOM WALK MODEL OF DIGIT COMPARISON 137  A MODEL OF DIGIT COMPARISON  In this section a random walk model of digit comparison is proposed. Subsequent sections describe experiments conducted to  evaluate the model. This model, like any other random walk model, is a system of assumptions that have various justifications.  Some assumptions are central features of random walk models, others are approximations intended to facilitate computation of  functional relationships, and still others are justified by analysis of the task and stimuli. The proposed system of  assumptions was not expected to correspond perfectly with empirical observations. The purpose of the research was to examine  deviations of the observations from the model and to infer how the model should be modified. This approach should lead to a  better understanding of mental comparisons, and eventually lead to a model that approximates human performance.  The model describes how subjects choose which of two digits is greater. In each trial of a digit comparison task, subjects  encode two digits, decide which is greater, and execute the corresponding response. The model describes only how the subject  decides, including how the time required to decide and decision errors depend on properties of the stimuli and instructional  manipulations. The duration of all other processing is assumed to be unaffected by these manipulations and by the response  that is executed. Thus, the response time RTY for a pair of digits i and j can be expressed as  RT;U= DT;; + R, (2)  where DTV is the decision time and R is the duration of all other processing. Furthermore, all errors are attributed to  incorrect decisions, excluding the possibility of response execution errors.  The central feature of a random walk model is that some random variable related to the choice dimension is accumulated until  one of two boundaries is reached. The imputs to the decision process in this model are analog magnitudes of the digits i and  j. An Empirical Evaluation of the Model  A special case of this digit comparison model with the added constraint of no bias (C=0) was examined by Poltrock and Foltz  (1980; Foltz, 1983, Experiment 3). In each trial they presented two unequal digits simultaneously, and their subjects  indicated which digit was larger by pressing one of two response buttons. All 72 ordered pairs of unequal digits, 1 through 9,  were presented repeatedly to eight subjects in two 1-hour sessions. Collapsing across sessions, they obtained approximately 30  responses for each digit pair from each subject. To test the model, they attempted to fit Eq. (5) (with C=0) to the mean RTs  and proportions averaged over sessions and subjects. They fixed g(1) = 1 and g(9) =10, and searched for values of g(i) for  i=2, ..., 8 that would provide the best fit to Eq. (5).  The results conformed reasonably well to Eq. (5), but the estimated values of the mean analog magnitudes were surprising. With  the analog magnitude of the digit one fixed at 1.0, the estimated values of g(i) for all other digits were between 9.4 and 10.  Thus, the difference between the analog magnitude of one and all other digits was much greater than the difference between the  analog magnitudes of any other pair of digits. This counterintuitive result suggests that one or more assumptions of the digit  comparison model were violated, causing a distortion of the analog magnitude estimates.  Examination of the mean RTs provided further evidence that an assumption was violated. The mean RTs were approximately equal  for all digit pairs that included the digit one. According to the model, mean RTs are equal when the same number of random  walk steps are required to reach a boundary. One alternative is that the boundaries are reached in a single step for digit  pairs that included the digit one, violating the assumption that the mean step size is much smaller than the boundary and  invalidating Eqs. (4) and (5). The model parameters proposed by Buckley and Gillman (1974) for digits were consistent with  this conclusion. EXPERIMENT 1  Experiment 1 was similar to the experiments reported by Poltrock and Foltz (1980), but many more observations were obtained  for each subject and each pair of digits.  Method  Subjects  The subjects were six women from the Murray Hill, New Jersey area. Their ages ranged from 35 to 47 years, and all were  right-handed. Subjects were paid $5 per hour plus transportation expenses for their participation. The same six subjects  participated in all three esperiments.  Apparatus  All stimuli were presented on a video monitor using an Apple 11 computer running the Apple Pascal Tester System developed by  Poltrock and Foltz (1982).  142 STEVEN E. POLTROCK  The monitor was at eye level approximately 60 cm from the subject. Display timing was synchronizedd with the video refresh  cycle, permitting RT measurements with 1-ms precision. Responses were made on two keys (4 and 8) of the Apple II keyboard, and  all other keys were masked.  Procedure  Each subject participated in six sessions on consecutive business days. In each session subjects completed 14 blocks of 72  trials. In each block all 72 ordered pairs of unequal digits from 1 to 9 were presented in a random order.  An asterisk at the center of the monitor screen signaled the start of each trial. After 530 ms two digits (0.46 cm high by  0.36 cm wide) were presented one space (0.37 cm) to the left and to the right of the asterisk. Subjects were instructed to  press the response key on the side corresponding to the greater digit. The two digits and the asterisk were displayed until  the subject responded. The screen remained blank for 500 ms between consecutive trials.  At the end of each block of 72 trials the mean RT for that block was presented. Subjects received no other feedback regarding  RTs and no feedback regarding accuracy. Subjects rested between blocks and initiated the subsequent block by pressing a  respsonse key.  Subjects were instructed to respond as quickly as possible. Speed was stressed over accuracy to encourage a speed-accuracy  tradeoff that would yield errors. The instructions stated, "Because you will be responding quickly, you will occasionally  press the wrong key. Don't worry about these errors, just try to be as consistent as possible, responding with the same speed  on every trial." These instructions were intended to discourage trial-by-trial adjustments of response bias and decision  boundaries as a consequence of errors detected by the subjects.  Results and Discussion  Figures 4 and 5 present the mean RT and error rate, respectively, for each session as a function of the absolute difference in  the digit values. Distance effects are obvious in both figures; RT and error rate decreased as the difference in digit values  increased. These distance effects appear remarkably stable. Although mean RT decreased with practice, particularly between the  first and second sessions, there was little change in the distance effect as evidenced by nearly parallel lines in Fig. 4;  practice and distance affect RT additively. The effect of distance on accuracy increased slightly with practice.  Applying the additive-factor method (Sternberg, 1969), additivity of the effects of practice and digit differences suggests  that they affect the durations of different processing stages. If digit differences affect decision times, then practice  apparently affects only the duration of processes that contribute to the intercept in Eq. (5). If practice does not affect  decision times, then tests of the model based on data averaged over Sessions 2 through 6 should not be contaminated by  practice effects. Each digit pair was presented 70 times to each subject during these five sessions. EXPERIMENT 2  According to the digit comparison model, when encouraged to respond more quickly subjects should decrease the absolute value  of the decision boundaries. As Eqs. (4) and (6) show, both RTs and accuracy decrease when the decision boundaries decrease.  The mean analog magnitudes of the digits g(i) are assumed to be stable characteristics of memory that should not be affected  by the speed of responses.  The increase in error frequency that accompanies faster responses allows another test of the digit comparison model. Equations  (7) provide estimates of 9A;J and 6C;; from the response proportions for each stimulus pair. The ratio of these estimates  (C/A) should be independent of the stimulus pair.  A disadvantage of encouraging subjects to respond more quickly is that as the boundaries decrease, the number of steps  required to reach a boundary decreases, increasing the likelihood of violating the assumption that the boundaries are much  greater than the mean drift rate (A > p,~). This assumption is central to the derivations of Eqs. (5)-(7).  Method  The subjects, equipment, and stimuli were the same as in Experiment 1. The procedure was the same except that each subject was  given an RT target, and RT feedback was presented after every trial. Immediately after each response a line with an arrowhead  was presented, pointing to the left from the screen center if the trial RT was less than the RT target, and pointing to the  right from the screen center if the trial RT exceeded the RT target. The line length was one text character for  RANDOM WALK MODEL OF DIGIT COMPARISON 149  every 10-ms deviation between the trial RT and the RT target. This feedback was presented for 200 ms, and followed by the next  trial 500 ms later. In addition, the mean RT was presented after every block of 72 trials, as in Experiment 1.  The purpose of the RT target was to encourage subjects to respond both fast and consistently over all sessions. The RT target  for each subject was based on her performance in Experiment 1. Subjects were instructed to adjust their RTs to make the  feedback line short and extend equally often to the left and right. Their goal was to make the block mean RT match their RT  target. Within the framework of the random walk model, the dependence of the CIA estimates on the stimulus implies either that the  random walk is not symmetric or that the starting point of the random walk C depends on the stimulus, or both. To understand  the consequences of these alternatives, both types of mathematical modifications to the model were explored. Link (1978b)  considered an asymmetric random walk model in which the mean drift rate was the difference in stimulus values minus an  internal referent value. The random walks of this model are symmetric if and only if the internal referent equals zero.  Fitting this model to the results of Experiment 2 yielded an estimate of the internal referent (-2.8) that was unreasonably  far from zero. With the parameters estimated for this model, more incorrect than correct decisions would be made whenever the  left-hand digit exceeded the right-hand digit by one or two.  To retain the assumption of symmetry, some assumption is required about the effect of the stimulus on the random walk starting  point. A post hoc, empirical observation is that CIA decreases linearly as the sum of the two digits increases. Equation (5)  was amended to include this observation and fit to the results for the digits 2 through 9 of Experiment 2. The resulting  estimates of the mean analog values were nearly identical to those presented in Table 7. The estimated boundary was 65.47 and  the estimated random walk starting point was C = 10.65 - 1.82 (i + j). The maximized correlation between RT;~ and z;; was  0.70. Thus, this modification to the model yielded a modest improvement in its fit to the results of Experiment 2, but at the  cost of an assumption with no theoretical justification. The estimated starting points  of the random walk (C1, C2, C3) are generally large negative numbers, reflecting a strong bias for the right-hand response. In  fact, this bias is so strong that the starting point is estimated to be on a boundary in several instances.  The estimates for the other two subjects (J. K. and M. M.) are clearly inconsistent with the model. The estimated boundaries  are absurdly small and many of the estimated analog magnitudes are nearly identical. These computations were performed using the mean proportions averaged  over all subjects and the model parameters obtained from these means. POLTROCK  The model appears reasonably consistent with the RTs in Fig. 8, but there are two obvious inconsistencies. First, the expected  RT is greater than the observed RT whenever the absolute difference between the digits is greater than 6. Second, for the  middle RT target the observed RT distance effect is slightly greater when the digit difference is negative, but the expected  RT distance effect is greater when the digit difference is positive. The first inconsistency between the observed and expected  RTs may be due to a failure of the assumption that the steps are small relative to the boundary. When the absolute difference  between digits is large, the boundary may be reached in few steps, compromising the use of Eq. (5) as an approximation to the  RTs. The second inconsistency may reflect the way that response bias is modeled. The random walk starting point must be  negative to account for the observation that responses are faster and more accurate when the digit difference is negative.  When the starting point is negative, the distance effect is enhanced for positive differences and reduced for negative  differences.  As in Experiment 2, the conjunction of the hypotheses that the random walk starting point is constant and that the random  walks are symmetric can be jointly tested. For each RT target, estimates of 9,~A and 9;JC were calculated from the response  proportions using Eqs. (7). The ratio of these estimates, C/A, should not depend on the digit pair. To the contrary, for each  RT target, C/A decreased as the sum of the two digits increased. The correlations between C/A and the sum of the digits were  -0.93, -0.77, and -0.85 for the longest, middle, and shortest RT targets, respectively. Thus, the response proportions are  inconsistent with the conjunction of these two hypotheses. The next section addresses how these departures from the model can  be reconciled.  GENERAL DISCUSSION  The empirical findings of this research can be briefly summarized. As Moyer and Landauer (1967) found, digit comparison RTs  decreased as the absolute difference between the digits increased (the distance effect), and increased as the smaller digit  increased (the minimum effect). Furthermore, error rates decreased as the absolute difference between digits increased. When  the mean RT was reduced by imposing an RT target, the RT distance effect decreased, the error rate increased, the error rate  distance effect increased, and error rates increased as the smaller digit increased. As the RT target decreased, the error  rate increased more rapidly for digit pairs requiring a left-hand response than for digit pairs requiring a right-hand  response.  The digit comparison model accounts for all but the last empirical finding. According to the model, distance effects occur  because information is accumulated more rapidly when the absolute difference between the analog magnitudes of the digits is  large. Because the random walk tends more rapidly toward the correct boundary for large differences, the decision time is  shorter and errors are less frequent. The  RANDOM WALK MODEL OF DIGIT COMPARISON 159  model attributes the minimum effect to a negatively accelerated transformation from the digits to their analog magnitudes. The  estimated analog magnitudes were slightly negatively accelerated in Experiments 1 and 3 but nearly linear in Experiment 2. In  Experiment 3, for example, the difference between the mean internal magnitudes of two and three was about twice the difference  between the mean internal magnitudes of eight and nine. This transformation, combined with the distance effect, produces  effects of the minimum on both RTs and error rates; the minimum principally affects RTs when the boundaries are large and  principally affects error rates when the boundaries are small. The fact that the estimated analog magnitudes were only  slightly negatively accelerated is consistent with the size of the observed minimum effects. The analog magnitudes were  successfully estimated for only the digits 2 through 9, and for these digits the minimum effects were relatively weak in all  three experiments. The effects of the minimum on the mean RTs in Experiment 1 (shown in Table 1) and on the error rates in  Experiment 2 (shown in Table 6) are more striking when the digit one is included.  Attempts to estimate the analog magnitudes of all nine digits produced results consistent with these minimum effects. In each  experiment the estimated analog magnitude of the digit one was closer to the boundary than to the analog magnitudes of any  other digit. In terms of the model, the boundary is crossed in a sngle step (cf. Foltz, 1983) producing fast and accurate  responses. These values of the model parameters, however, violate the assumption that the mean step size is small relative to  the boundary. Because Eqs. (4)-(6) were derived using this assumption, its violation invalidates the parameter estimates but  does not contradict any other aspects of the model. The equations are intended to describe the duration and probability of  error for difficult comparisons, and apparently the digit one can be compared with other digits easily.  According to the model, subjects comply with instructions to respond more quickly by reducing the decision boundary. When  boundaries decrease, random walks reach the boundaries more quickly, thereby reducing decision times, but are more likely to  reach the wrong boundary, thereby increasing the error rate. Furthermore, as the boundaries decrease, the probability of  reaching the wrong boundary increases more rapidly for random walks with smaller mean drift rates. Consequently, the distance  and minimum effects increase for error rates and decrease for RTs, as observed in Experiment 3.  The asymmetric effect of RT targets on the error rates for left-hand and righthand responses observed in Experiment 3 poses a  problem for the digit comparison model. By adjusting the random walk starting point C, the model can account for response bias  in the RTs and error rates. Indeed, Link (1975) demonstrated that response bias effects induced by manipulating response  probabilities can be explained by adjusting the starting point. However, in the three experiments reported in this article,  stimuli requiring each response were presented equally often. Under these conditions the random walk starting point should be  close to zero, and both error rates and RTs should be unbiased. Instead the estimated starting points for the shortest RT  target in Experiment 3 were actually on a  160  STEVEN E. POLTROCK  boundary for four subjects. Furthermore, the hypotheses that the random walks are symmetric for symmetric stimuli and that the  starting points are independent of the stimulus were jointly tested and rejected. The estimates of C/A were correlated  negatively with the two stimulus digits.  How can the random walk model be modified to acount for these bias effects? Three alternatives are considered. First, the  random walks may be asymmetric for symmetric stimuli. Link accounted for stimulus bias (Link, 1975; Link & Heath, 1975) by  subtracting a referent from the stimulus event, which makes random walks asymmetric for symmetric stimulus events. Stimulus  bias and response bias cannot be distinguished for the stimuli used in these experiments. Consequently, the bias could be  modeled by subtracting a positive referent from d;; in each interval At. Although this modification to the model provides a  mechanism capable of producing the observed bias, it offers no advantage relative to the proposed model. For these stimuli,  the referent and the starting point of the random walk are virtually equivalent in their effects on performance. Furthermore,  this modification does not suggest why the observed biases exist or why they increase as the RT targets decrease.  A second approach is to assume that the starting point of the random walk depends on the stimuli. Perhaps subjects adjust the  starting point based on some characteristics of the digits. In Experiment 2, the model fit was improved somewhat by making the  starting point a linear function of the sum of the stimulus digits. Presumably, subjects do not compute the sum of the digits  in order to set the starting point of the random walk. Perhaps they base the starting point on the value of one of the digits,  for example, the right-hand digit. This hypothesis is not easily tested because the ratios C/A cannot be computed for ordered  digit pairs. Such a modification of the digit comparison model could account for the observed response bias and its dependence  on stimulus magnitude. It is not clear, however, why subjects would make such adjustments to the random walk starting point or  why the bias should increase when the RT target decreases. The model's performance does not improve when the starting point is  adjusted.  A third alternative is to assume that another source of errors contributes to performance when subjects are given an RT  target. In particular, suppose the subject guesses if the random walk has not reached a boundary by some time T, where T is a  random variable related to the RT target. This strategy would help keep the RTs close to the RT target. Furthermore, suppose  the random walk is unbiased, but the guesses are biased. The most frequent guesses and, therefore, the greatest bias would be  observed for the smallest distances between digits. In fact, in every experiment the greatest bias was observed for the  stimuli (8,9) and (9,8), and the digits eight and nine were closest together on the analog magnitude scale. In general,  however, the estimated bias (see Table 8) did not increase as the difference between the digits decreased.  This guessing strategy accounts for the observed changes in bias in Experiment 3. As the RT target decreases, both the random  walk boundary and the deadline T decrease. Suppose, however, that the boundary does not decrease enough to match  RANDOM WALK MODEL OF DIGIT COMPARISON 161  the RT target. Then the frequency of guesses increases, leading to the observed increase in bias.  Guessing could also explain why the intercept R in Eq. (5) decreased as the RT target decreased in Experiment 3. According to  the random walk model, reductions in an RT target affect the decision time, but no changes in the intercept are predicted.  However, if the proportion of guesses increases as the RT target decreases, and the mean guessing response is faster than the  mean decision response, then the intercept must decrease.  This guessing strategy has advantages not offered by the other alternatives considered. First, it explains why subjects might  exhibit a bias for one response. Because all subjects were right-handed, they might favor right-hand responses when guessing.  Second, it accounts for the observed increase in bias when RT targets decrease. Third, it may account for part of the changes  in the estimated nondecision time. There are also disadvantages to combining this guessing strategy with the digit comparison  model. Although it explains why comparisons of the digits 8 and 9 exhibited the greatest bias, it is not consistent with the  observed correlations between bias and the sum of the digits. Furthermore, a guessing strategy is an unattractive modification  theoretically to models based on Link's Relative Judgment Theory. Further analyses are required to assess which of these  alternatives, if any, provides an adequate account of the response biases.  In summary, the digit comparison model accounts qualitatively and quantitatively for nearly all the empirical findings in  these three-digit comparison experiments. In addition, the model provides a psychological scale of the stimuli. The estimated  values on this scale suggest that one assumption of the model, that the step size is small relative to the boundaries, is  violated when the digit one is compared with other digits. This violation invalidates estimates obtained under those  conditions but does not invalidate the remaining assumptions of the model. Response biases favoring the right-hand stimulus  pose a more serious challenge for the model. These biases increased as the stimulus values increased, in contradiction to  important assumptions of the model. Modifications to the model may account for these biases, but further analyses are required  to assess their adequacy.  Link (1975, 1978a, 1978b) has shown that similar models account for effects of stimulus probability, response deadlines, and  distance effects for perceptual comparisons. More recently, Link (1989) has shown that a similar random walk model can account  for comparisons of two-digit numbers. The ability of random walk models to account for both perceptual comparisons and number  comparisons supports Moyer's (Moyer & Landauer, 1967; Moyer, 1973) position that mental comparisons and perceptual comparisons  are accomplished in the same way. Link's Relative Judgment Theory provides the mechanisms that were missing from Moyer's  proposal. Analog representations of the stimuli are obtained either perceptually or from memory, and the difference between  the analog representations is accumulated until it reaches a boundary.  162  STEVEN E.
