Categorization is of overwhelming significance for mankind; it has been instrumental in the shaping of humankind’s institutions. The age-old Indian Caste System is a product of categorization: humans used to be born into a socio-economic status which determined their whole life and their fate. Categorization played a decisive role in religious conflicts. Categorization of people into the two groups, capitalists and workers, is one of Marxism’s core principles. Nazi Germany murdered some six million humans just because they were categorized as Jews. Although most categorization is more harmless nowadays, all political parties, all sports tournaments, and all contests are defined by categorizations. With respect to welfare, regions are categorized into developed or developing areas. In empirical economics and psychology, research methods that involve categorization are applied as important tools.

Psychophysical scaling originated from studies  by   Fechner (1860)  and  Weber (1834) . Their work was based on just noticeable differences and led to logarithmic psychophysical functions.  Merkel (1888/89)  and  Plateau (1872) , followed by  Stevens (1946, 1957) , pioneered the magnitude approach which led to power psychophysical functions.

Categorical scaling is a variation of psychophysical scaling which maps the continuum of stimuli into the continuum of sensations ( Stevens, 1958  p. 178). For categorical scaling the stimuli are subdivided into stimulus intervals, where the subintervals describe intervals of the sensation variables called  categories . This variation of a psychophysical function is called a categorical scaling function. It was developed by  Sanford (1898, pp. 340–341 and 413–414)  and  Thurstone (1929) .

                         6

                         For descriptions of range-frequency theory see  Birnbaum (1974) ,  Parducci (1963, 1965, 1974) ,  Parducci and Perrett (1971) , and  Wedell and Parducci (1988) . 
                       Following Parducci, the range of a stimulus is its excess over the minimum stimulus, calibrated by the stimulus range. The frequency value is the rank of a stimulus, calibrated by the number of stimuli. A stimulus’ range value is proportional to its magnitude, and its frequency value indicates the tendency to use the categories equally often. The range-frequency judgment of a stimulus is the weighted average of its range and frequency values.

Judgments on stimuli depend also on the discriminative power of subjects  Garner (1952) , and  Stevens (1957, p. 155)  or information over different stimulus areas. Suppose subjects are asked to categorize their possible incomes. It seems reasonable to assume that an individual may have a more discerning opinion about income levels near his or her own, rather than those of a billionaire. The better a subject can distinguish the stimuli along a certain interval, the more stimuli he or she will assign to the respective interval, and, if appropriate, spread them across several categories.

Important influence is exerted by contextual effects of the arrangement of stimuli, for instance, the hysteresis effect or the shape of the distribution of the presentation of stimuli. This will be discussed in greater detail in the next section.

Luce and Galanter (1963, pp. 267–268)  reported that “subjects tend to devote more categories to a stimulus interval as the density of stimuli in that interval is increased. It is as though the subjects were trying to spread out stimuli that are in fact close together, or … to name the categories about equally often. This affects the apparent slope of the [categorical judgment] function: when the stimuli are closely packed, the function tends to be appreciably steeper in that region than when they are less dense”.

This implies in particular that the categorical judgment function is concave for a positively skewed distribution of stimuli and convex for a negatively skewed distribution of stimuli. It is S-shaped when the stimuli bunch in the middle part of the distribution and ogival-shaped when they bunch near the lower and upper ends of the stimulus interval. When the stimuli bunch near the lower end and within the upper two-thirds interval of the stimulus space, the categorical judgment function has a concave–convex–concave shape, and when they bunch near the one-third lower interval and near the upper end of the stimulus space, the categorical judgment​ function has a convex–concave–convex shape.

How is this compatible with equally spaced categories? When subjects are benevolent and intend to convey maximum information to the experimenter, they will spread out stimuli and, making use of their discriminatory power, sort stimuli to the appropriate categories. Our note addresses equal spacing of categories in this context. Rather than ordering subjects to use the “categories as if they were equally spaced along the sensation continuum” ( Luce & Galanter, 1963  p. 264); see also ( Stevens & Galanter, 1957  p. 378), this note presents a motivation for subjects to equally space categories as a result of an optimization problem.

Categories represent a special manifestation of sensation variables. A priori there is no reason to assume that, in terms of categories, sensation variables should be equally spaced. Just persuading subjects to feign that categories are equally spaced is no convincing rationale for this assumption. So far, van Praag demonstrated that equally spaced categories result from a model intent on minimizing measurement errors. 
                         8 
                       
                      
                         8 
                         Van Praag called this condition the  equal quantile assumption . He derived that from a model of minimizing measurement errors. See  van Praag (1968, pp. 58–59) ; and  van Praag (1971, pp. 342–344) . 
                       Our underlying motivation is different: rather than aiming to avoid inaccurate data, we consider an optimization model with the goal of providing the experimenter with the most informative data. The basic assumption is the presence of benevolent subjects which is rather common in psychology. 
                         9 
                       
                      
                         9 
                         This is different from economics, where only incentivized data are considered reliable. But the incentives themselves influence subjects to maximize (expected) payoffs rather than reporting the real data, whenever there is a conflict between both data revelations. Moreover,  Camerer and Hogarth (1999)  and  Toepoel (2012)  showed that the difference in data generation is negligible. 
                       Hence, we assume two aims of subjects to serve experimenters best, viz. to provide maximum information or equal information about various categories.

In our paper we assumed certainty on the part of subjects, i.e. they know definitely to which categories the stimuli should be assigned. But subjects may exhibit uncertainties in assigning stimuli to categories and how to set the stimulus interval limits. Also, subjects may not be consistent in their comparative discrimination and identification of the stimuli. To clarify problems of proper discrimination,  Thurstone (1927a, 1927b)  proposed the law of comparative judgment. Considering two stimuli, a subject may gauge whether stimulus  
                         a 
                       is sufficiently different from stimulus  
                         b 
                       to warrant an assignment to different a category or not. 
                         18 
                       
                      
                         18 
                         Thurstone’s law of comparative judgment is more subtle than this explanation. Obviously, he formulated his law with respect to managing greater amounts of subjects’ data. For this reason, he developed a formula to process subjects’ data. 
                       Thurstone’s law of comparative judgment was generalized by  Torgerson (1958, chapter 10)  to the law of categorical judgment which may govern the delineation of the various stimuli and the stimulus limits.

Later on, several models were developed dealing with subjects’ uncertainty. They started with  Medin and Schaffer ’s ( 1978 ) context model, which was generalized by  Nosofsky (1984)  to the general context model. Later,  Maddox and Ashby (1993)  developed a somewhat different approach, the  decision bound model .

Medin and Schaffer (1978)  developed a formula determining the probabilities that a stimulus will be classified as a member of various categories. This established multidimensional scaling theory.  Nosofsky (1984)  enriched their analysis by also taking the similarity of stimuli into account: similar stimuli are likely to be assigned to the same category.

The more comprehensive decision bound theory was developed dealing with situations of trial-by-trial variability of the perceptual information associated with every stimulus. This affects the outcome of categorization judgments.  Maddox and Ashby (1993, p. 51)  defined an optimal classifier as a discriminant function

Whenever subjects obey the optimal classifier, then the decision bounds would equal our stimulus interval limits. But in real situations, subjects may fail because of informational noise, of misconduct of the decision bounds, and other reasons ( Maddox & Ashby, 1993  p. 52). But  Maddox & Ashby  (p. 51) themselves argue that most categorization errors occur because subjects incorrectly learned the rule for assigning stimuli to the relevant categories. In other words, they are not adequately experienced for the categorization task. Moreover,  Maddox & Ashby  admit that subjects’ failures occur rarely in case of few stimuli and categories.

In cases of trial-by-trial variability, the problem of overlapping categories may arise, possibly assigning identical stimuli to different categories. But this is a problem of human shortcomings, not a theoretical problem. When subjects apply  Maddox & Ashby ’s optimal classifiers, this problem does not occur. Moreover, it may occur only for repeated presentation of the same stimuli. Finally, if subjects are benevolent and want to convey maximum information to the experimenter, they should consider the categories as equally spaced, even if they suffer from some faults. Equal spacing of categories has proven itself for a long time.

Finally, we wish to draw a connection between Likert scales and categories as representations of ordinal data. In a seminal paper,  Liddell and Kruschke (2018)  found that, although Likert scales capture ordinal data, virtually all articles analyzed them using metric models. This led to systematic errors. In order to ameliorate these flaws, they suggested using ordered-probit and Bayesian models. This is a major problem of data analysis, but not a theoretical optimization problem, which this note deals with.

For categorical scaling, psychologists presented their subjects with the stimuli, the minimum and maximum stimuli, and the categories. The categories were either rated by adjectives or by numbers. Subjects were told assigning stimuli to the categories as if they were equally spaced along the sensation continuum. No motivation was provided for their equal spacing. This note showed that, when subjects are benevolent and aim at providing maximum or equal information, they would assign the stimuli to categories considered to be equally spaced.

Suppose a subject is asked to categorize annual incomes of a single-person household: $ 8870, $ 15,620, $ 12,000, $ 250,000, $ 30,000. The categories given by the experimenter are insufficient, sufficient, good. Since the subject knows the respective US poverty line to be $ 12,760, (s)he will assign the incomes of $ 8870 and $ 12,000 to the category “insufficient”, and, harking back to his or her own experience, assign the incomes of $ 12000 and $ 30,000 to the category “sufficient”, and assign the income of $ 250,000 to the category “good”. Suppose, the experimenter approaches the subject, telling him or her that (s)he had forgotten to enter also the income of $ 9500 and asks him or her to revise his or her categorization. Then it is to be expected that the subject will also assign this income to the category “insufficient”. This demonstrates that subjects do not equally distribute the stimuli to the categories.

The experimental literature brims over with instances that different distributions of stimuli generate different shapes of judgment functions, respecting at the same time equally-spaced categories.

Since the stimulus interval limits are decisive to determine subjects’ judgment functions, but were not revealed to the experimenter, (s)he had to ascertain them by some computations of by graphical methods. This was precisely the procedure of  Parducci (1965, pp. 409–411) , except having devised an optimization model. Parducci tested 13 different distributions of stimuli and, accordingly, received different patterns of stimulus distributions across the stimulus interval limits. This gave rise to different shapes of the graphs of the judgment functions. It is not intelligible why the interval limits should constitute equal numbers of stimuli just because they were derived from an optimization problem rather than from subjects’ revealed choices.

The idea for this paper was inspired by our experimental work on income categorization. One third of our subjects were presented questionnaires in the conventional experimental design, i.e. income levels as stimuli and asking subjects to categorize them. For the remaining two thirds we put the experimental work upside down, i.e. we asked subjects directly for their interval limits. For the former approach we received on average 60% concave, 20% ogival-shaped, 15% linear, and 5% S-shaped judgment functions. For the latter approach we received on average 50% concave, 20% ogival-shaped, 20% linear, and 10% S-shaped judgment functions. These results may be interpreted as utility functions of income. Inspections of the questionnaires’ responses seldom showed equal numbers of stimuli (first approach) or equal widths of stimulus interval limits (second approach).
