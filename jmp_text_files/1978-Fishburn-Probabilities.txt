On the other  hand, consumer economics has relied primarily on preference relations and choice functions for its theories of individual  choice. Although there are sizable literatures on the connections between choice probabilities and preference relations, and  between preference relations and choice functions, little has been done apart from their common ties to preference  relations-to connect choice probabilities and choice functions. The latter connection is studied in this paper. A family of  choice functions that depends on a threshold parameter is defined from a choice probability function. It is then shown what  must be true of the choice probability function so that the choice functions satisfy three traditional rationality conditions.  Conversely, it is shown what must be true of the choice functions so that the choice probability function satisfies a version  of Luce's axiom for individual choice probabilities.  1. DISCUSSION  This paper has investigated relationships between choice functions C defined on the nonempty subsets sd of a finite set X and  choice probability functions P for which P(x, A) is the likelihood that x e A will be chosen from A when the individual must  choose  218 PETER C. Given the family of choice functions {C,: A e [0, 1]} defined from P by  QA) _ {x e A: P(x, A) , A max P(a, A)} for all A e cl, a6A  we considered the connection between C and P in both directions. It was noted first what must be true of P so that certain  rationality conditions will hold for a particular C or for the family {C: A E [0, 1]}. We then showed what must be true of  this family of choice functions so that P will satisfy the non-zero version of Luce's choice axiom.  Although the paper is limited by its primary focus on (7), this family of choice functions offers an appealing way of  interconnecting such functions with choice probabilities. In particular it allows a simple interconnection between the two  concepts through the threshold parameter .l. This parameter has the potential of differentiating among individuals according  to their perceptions of acceptability for choice in terms of relative likelihoods of choice.  Experiments to estimate choice probabilities and test hypotheses about P are discussed for example by Luce and Suppes (1965)  and Rumelhart and Greeno (1971). If it is considered appropriate to test (7) or some other interconnection empirically, then,  in addition to a replicated design that permits estimates of choice probabilities, subjects could be asked to indicate which  alternatives in various A e a they feel are the most acceptable or that they would seriously consider choosing from A.  Although the latter task may face a number of experimental difficulties, it would seem within the realm of possibility.  On the other hand, some people may feel that the only viable definition of a choice function on the basis of P is Cmin , i.e.  that x ought to be in C(A) if and only if x has some chance of being chosen from A. Such a viewpoint would be reconciled with  the approach of the preceding paragraph if the type of experiment envisioned there supported Cmin more strongly than other  choice functions based on P.
