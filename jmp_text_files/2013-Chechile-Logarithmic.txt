Gambles capture many important attributes of economic decisions. In an uncertain environment, there often are several possible outcomes from a course of action, and these outcomes can have different economic consequences. What is the fair price that should be assigned to the overall worth of a gamble? This question is a classic problem that arose in the 1650s at a time when probability theory was at an early stage of development. Currently there is no clear agreement on the mathematical form for either the risky weighting function or the outcome utility function. In fact,  Stott (2006)  considered more than two hundred possible models for the utility of a gamble. 
                      2 
                   
                   
                      2 
                      The large number of models in the  Stott (2006)  paper resulted from the factorial combinations of proposals for (a) the risky weighting function, (b) the outcome utility function, and (c) the choice rule for deciding between gambles of different utility. Instead, in the current paper, we are focused strictly on the issue of the risky weighting function. 
                    Given the unresolved status of both the value function and risky weighting function, we are faced with the problem of discriminating among a large set of possible models. One approach towards finding the “correct” utility theory is to use statistical tools for model selection. Statistical model selection incorporates built-in complexity penalties to the overall goodness-of-fit of the model ( Burnham & Anderson, 2002; Grünwald, Myung, & Pitt, 2005; Myung, 2000 ). Both the number of free fitting parameters in the model and the mathematical form of the model itself influence model complexity. A model selection statistic is a value for each candidate model that incorporates fit quality along with a complexity penalty. The goal of this approach is to find the most simple model for the existing data.  Stott (2006)  used this approach to assess the numerous proposals for the utility of a gamble.

Although Stott’s analyses are sophisticated, there are a number of reasons why those results are unconvincing. First, there are a number of different statistical measures available, and the model identified as the “best” depends on the statistical metric used.  Stott (2006)  used only the Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) as a model selection measure. But readers of this journal are well aware of many other model selection metrics, and it is possible that different metrics yield different optimal choices ( Myung, 2000 ). Second, the model comparison statistics are single numbers as opposed to an assessment of the model fit over the full range of possible probability values. Perhaps even the “best” model chosen by a model selection statistic might still result in a nonrandom pattern for the misfit residuals — a classic signal that the model is misspecified.

Instead of employing statistical model selection metrics, we examine in this paper a transform of the data that is very sensitive to the predicted curvature of the risky weighting function and does not depend on knowing the value function or the choice rule. The transformation is a logarithmic derivative, and it is related to the hazard function that has been used by a number of mathematical psychologists to discriminate among a set of similar functions (e.g.   Chechile, 2003, 2006; Colonius, 1988; Luce, 1986; Townsend & Ashby, 1978, 1983 ). For example,  Chechile (2006)  showed that the examination of the hazard function resulted in the rejection of nearly a dozen candidate functions for characterizing memory loss as a function of time.

Subjects ( 
                               n 
                               = 
                               10 
                            ) were recruited via online advertisement. The experimental stimuli were created and presented with e-Prime software. Subjects were informed that they would be playing a game and that they would be compensated according to their relative performance in the game: the subject with the highest score would earn $55, the subject with the second highest score would earn $50, and so on down to the subject with the lowest score, who would earn $10.

To ensure that the paradigm was as clear and understandable to subjects as possible, we designed a task that used familiar representations of stochastic events and did not require individuals to make any explicit mathematical calculations. Throughout the experiment, we used circular figures representing wheels of fortune. These wheels were of two colors, and they were shaped like pie charts with each color representing an outcome value. The wheels were animated to spin in a realistic manner, and had pointers that indicated the outcome values on which the wheels stopped. Thus, we presented subjects with visual–not numeric–representations of the probabilities of gamble outcomes and with opportunities to see examples of various gamble outcomes for various relative probabilities. When individuals had to make choices regarding probabilities, they were not asked to declare probability values, but rather to choose wheels of equal psychological worth. In this way, we sought to reduce reliance on the subjects’ facility with numbers.

The experimental paradigm took the form of a game in two phases, to be played against an unseen “audience” (as in a game show), whose choices were simulated from general utility models. The first phase of the game comprised three training trials, meant to ensure that subjects understood how the gambles worked. Subjects were presented with two wheels of fortune. Each wheel was of two colors. For the wheel on the left, the color red indicated the probability of winning 100 points, and the color blue indicated the probability of winning 2 points. For the wheel on the right, the color green indicated the probability of winning either 80 or 120 points, and the color blue indicated the probability of winning 2 points. Subjects were asked which wheel they preferred to spin, and were told that the audience would be assigned the other wheel. On each of these trials, a triangular pointer appeared on the wheels, and the wheels were spun. The player and the audience each received the number of points indicated by the color touched by the pointer when the wheels stopped spinning. This phase of the experiment was designed strictly as a training phase to familiarize the subjects with the wheel and to learn that there were truly stochastic properties with each wheel of fortune.

The second phase of the game was an exercise in gamble-matching. Subjects were shown a wheel on the left side of the screen that we called the “reference gamble”. The wheel for the reference gamble used the color red to indicate the probability of winning 100 points and the color blue to indicate the probability of winning 2 points. On the right side of the screen, subjects saw options for what we called the “comparison gamble”. Five options for the comparison gamble were shown on the right side of the screen. For the comparison gamble, the wheels used the color green to indicate the probability of winning either 80 or 120 points, and the color blue was used to indicate the probability of winning 2 points. Options for the comparison gambles represented probabilities evenly dispersed between the reference probability and either 0 or 1, depending on whether the larger possible outcome of the comparison gamble (the “comparison value”) was larger or smaller than the larger possible outcome of the reference gamble (the “reference value” of 100 points). For example, if the reference probability were 50% and the comparison value were 120 points, the comparison probability choices were all between 0% and 50%, based on the assumption that a rational actor would require a smaller probability given the chance to win more points.

Once a subject selected a comparison gamble, he/she was presented with another five options, representing probabilities evenly distributed between the choice with the next largest probability and the choice with the next smallest probability, with the previous choice at the center of the range. In total, subjects saw three iterations, allowing them to zero in on the precise probability that they judged to match the reference gamble. After the third iteration, they were asked if they were satisfied with the choice. The instructions stressed that the gamble needed to be equal in psychological value, and the simulated audience would select which gamble that they would play (if the gambles were played). Consequently, the best strategy was to make the gambles equal because the audience was likely to select an obviously superior gamble if given the opportunity. Thus, the matching of gambles was conducted in the spirit of a “cake cutting” — the subject cut and the computer audience selected. The simulated audience consisted of 101 simulated individuals who had a distribution for their coefficients for their value function and for their probability function. Hence, no one simulated audience member was equal to any other simulated audience member. The values for the probability weights and value function span the range of values explored in the  Chechile and Butler (2000, 2003)  experiments. The audience’s choice was the gamble that a majority of the audience preferred. The sole purpose of this simulated audience was to induce a sense of realism to the gamble matching. The decision-making tendencies of the audience were not revealed to the subjects. The subjects were told that their reward was a function of the points earned from their gambles over the experiment in comparison to nine other subjects. For all gambles that were played, there were animated wheels of fortune that were randomly spun in a realistic fashion. The performance of the audience did not affect the reward that any subject received.

For two-thirds (76 out of 114) of the trials, after the subjects indicated that they had decided that the two gambles were equivalent, the program moved on to the next set of gambles to be matched. For the remaining third of the trials, following the matching of the gambles, the simulated audience selected a wheel and left the subject with the other wheel. As in the training phase, a pointer appeared at the top of each wheel, and the wheels were spun. The subjects and the audience each won the number of points indicated by the color the pointer touched when the wheels stopped spinning.

Finally, the experimental procedure used in this paper was designed to keep the subjects interested and engaged in the enterprise of matching gambles to be psychologically equivalent. The cake-cutting methodology created a context that created a strong motivation for equating two gambles. Also, the method did not present probability values for gamble outcomes numerically, but rather the gambles were represented in terms of wheels of fortune that were animated to behave realistically. The subjects did not have to produce numbers or assign utility values, and there were economic consequences of the subjects’ judgments. Under these experimental conditions and with the new tool of using the logarithmic derivative function, each subject behaved in a reasonable fashion that was best captured with the exponential class of risky weighting functions, i.e., the Prelec model and Exponential Odds model. Of course, other proposals for a risky weighting function can be created.
