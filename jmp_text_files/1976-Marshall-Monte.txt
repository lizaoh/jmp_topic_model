The results presented here derive from a computer simulation of Cotton's experiment.2 New estimates of the parameters were  made, and with these estimates a Monte Carlo routine generated a sample of Hoyt coefficients. This sample was then used to  study the extent to which the experimental reliability is statistically unlikely given the Bower-Trabasso assumptions.  THE EXPERIMENT  Experimental Data. Cotton's (1971) Experiment 2 was a three-dimension concept identification problem in which subjects were  asked to classify stimuli according to an initially unknown concept. The stimuli were presented singly and in random order to  each individual, with no two subjects having the same order of presentation. After classifying a stimulus, the subject was  told whether he was correct or incorrect. Using this feedback, most subjects learned (at some point) to identify the correct  concept and thus to make the correct classification.  Fifty subjects were given an 8 trial pretest, a 24 trial training session, and an 8 trial posttest. A Hoyt reliability  coefficient of 0.786 was calculated from the 24 learning trials.  The Model. The model simulated was the Bower-Trabasso (1964) concept identification model. This is a three-state Markov chain  which is described by a transition matrix of the form  Trial N   Trial N + 1  C E S  C 1 0 0  E c (1 - p)(1 - c) p(1 - c)  S 0 (1 - p) p  The three states are learned (C), error (E), and correct guess (S). Each subject is assumed to begin the experiment in state S  and to make at least one error before learning. The probability of learning (moving into C) is c, and the probability of  answering correctly given that the individual does not learn is p. DISCUSSION  Reevaluation and further analysis have confirmed the finding of Cotton and Harris: The experimental results deviate  significantly from the expected results of the BowerTrabasso model with the specified parameters. The simulation does not  explain the discrepancy. It may well be that individual differences are a major contributing factor as indicated by Cotton and  Harris. The implications of individual differences for the Bower-Trabasso model are obviously relevant and deserving of  further attention. Replications of the experiment would be useful to determine whether the discrepancy between sample rH and  expected rH is due to chance variation, individual differences as suggested by Cotton and Harris, or some other failing of the  model.
