The multi-utility representation of preorders, from a purely theoretical point of view, is the best kind of representation, since it fully characterizes the preorder using a family of real-valued (isotonic) functions. If this kind of representation exists, the preorder can be viewed as an intersection of representable total preorders, in the spirit of  Dushnik and Miller (1941)  extension theorem.

The classical main contributions in this field, which also concern the case when continuity or at least semi-continuity of the representing functions is considered, were obtained by  Levin (2001)  and then, by  Evren and Ok (2011)  (see also the more recent papers by  Bosi & Herden, 2012, 2016 ).

In the present manuscript, our aim is to investigate the conditions for the existence of an (either semi-continuous or continuous) countable Richter–Peleg ( Peleg, 1970; Richter, 1966 ) multi-utility representation.

First, we provide an overview of the general background of this theory, accompanied by brief examples adapted to decision-making scenarios. Then we motivate our objectives throughout the present manuscript. After that, in the Preliminaries we present the main concepts, as well as key previous results, relative to Richter–Peleg multi-utility representations of (not necessarily total) preorders. The main Section  3  presents new results on countable Richter–Peleg semi- continuous and continuous multi-utility representations. A final discussion closes the paper.

This article contributes to the decision-making literature, in which the agent cannot always compare alternatives, that is, where the preference relation can be incomplete. This reflects real-world scenarios such as committee decisions requiring unanimity or long-term strategy evaluations under incomplete preferences.

In particular, we delve into some of the results obtained by  Alcantud et al. (2016) , proposing a characterization of countable Richter–Peleg multi-utility representations, both when the representation is upper/lower semi-continuous and when it is continuous. This becomes particularly relevant when optimization techniques are required in the aforementioned contexts, as when a Richter–Peleg multi-utility representation exists, then the maximization of any of the functions in the representation leads to a maximal element of the preorder (for instance, this is the case of an upper semi-continuous Richter–Peleg representation on a compact space).

In addition, a topological framework that fulfills the property of strong local non-satiation, introduced by  Evren and Ok (2011) , is proposed. This property ensures that decision-making situations at points of non-satiation (e.g., negotiations that fail to converge to a common agreement) allow room for the continuous improvement of outcomes within the preference set. This feature is particularly relevant when an agent is engaged in processes of incremental adjustments or negotiations, as it guarantees the existence of intermediate points between differing positions, even if a fully satisfactory point for all parties does not exist.

This research confirms the possibility of working with preference representations using a smaller number of functions than in the non-countable case. In this way, it would ensure the possibility of simplifying and reducing the complexity of both computational decision-making models and the number of key factors that agents need to evaluate before making a decision. However, future research should further explore the case of finite multi-utility representations, as they constitute a particular case of countable multi-utility representations.

Among finite multi-utility representations, we could consider the classical utility (see, e.g.  Debreu, 1964 ) or representations with just two utility functions (see  Candeal, 2022 ). Since they are particular cases of countable multi-utility representations, we may still wonder if  Theorem 3.1  can have an analogous result for the finite case.
