The method of paired comparison provides a straightforward approach for assessing whether a judge is internally consistent in evaluating items. For example, when item  j  is preferred to item  k , and item  k  is preferred to item  l , we expect that item  j  is also preferred to item  l . If a judge selects item  l  in the last comparison then this indicates an intransitive cycle in the judgmental process that may be either systematic or reflective of the stochastic nature of the choice process ( Bezembinder, 1981; Tversky, 1969 ). To distinguish between these two explanations, a model-based approach is needed. Starting with  Thurstone's (1927)  influential paper, a number of models have been proposed over the years for the analysis of paired comparison data ( David, 1988 ). These models are based on three critical assumptions. First, multiple paired comparison judgments of each judge are assumed to be independent. Second, the utility of an item remains invariant across different paired comparisons. Third, intransitive choices can be accounted for by pair-specific error components.

This paper demonstrates that these three assumptions are too restrictive for empirical applications. Especially, when the same item is evaluated in different pairs, as for example in the comparisons of the pairs

                         j 
                         ,

                       and  
                         ( 
                         k 
                         ,

                       independence may not hold. We will also show that the invariant-utility assumption is questionable. Previous work in this area, notably by  Mellers and Biagini (1994) , and  González-Vallejo (2002)  considered the second problem but empirical evidence for this problem at the individual level has not been provided so far. Experimental studies on intransitive choice behaviour have demonstrated that intransitivities can be systematic and cannot be accounted for by pair-specific errors in the choice process ( Lindman & Lyons, 1978; Montgomery, 1977; Tversky, 1969 ).

This paper relaxes the three assumptions by presenting a general random-effects formulation that does not require the utilities associated with the same item across trials to be independent or identical. By modelling explicitly dependencies among multiple paired comparison judgments, we obtain a more flexible framework for the analysis of paired comparison data that facilitates modelling both violations of weak stochastic intransitivities ( Fishburn, 1998 ) and incidences of systematic intransitive cycles observed in (possibly repeated) choices by one individual. Though much work has focussed on developing paired comparison models that can overcome violations of strong stochastic intransitivities ( Carroll & DeSoete, 1991 ), our paper is the first attempt to account for systematic intransitive cycles. In a re-analysis of  Tversky's (1969)  paired comparison data, we also present a special case of our approach, an expected utility paired-comparison model with errors ( Hey & Orme, 1994 ; see also  Loomes & Sugden, 1995 ), that is shown to yield a satisfactory fit of this classical data set.

The remainder of this paper is structured as follows. We first present the random-effects framework and compare it to currently available methods for the analysis of paired comparison data. Next, we focus on the interpretation of the model parameters and consider a number of special cases of the proposed approach. We show that the new model is more flexible in accounting for intransitive choice behaviour. An application, based on  Tversky's (1969)  gamble study, illustrates the approach. We conclude the paper with a discussion of the main results and present an outlook for future research.

The independently distributed pair-specific errors of the IR models account for inconsistencies in the pairwise choices by treating them as a chance event. In contrast, under the DR models, inconsistencies in the choice behaviour are explained by a person's variability in assessing the utility of an item when it is paired with other items. Thus, inconsistencies are viewed as a by-product of the evaluative process. These differences between the DR models and the IR models become apparent when comparing the two probabilities  (25)  and  (26)  for intransitive cycles. The DR models can accommodate a wider range in the incidence of intransitive cycles than the IR models. The usefulness and importance of this feature is illustrated in the application section of this paper.

Difficulties in testing for violations of transitivity on the individual level include the typically small number of repeated comparisons by a judge and violations of the assumption of independence among the pairwise comparisons. A small number of comparisons limits the statistical power in testing for differences between the marginal paired-comparison distributions. If the independence assumption does not hold, tests of differences between the marginal paired-comparison distributions may over-report significant results. Both of these difficulties can be addressed by a joint analysis of the pairwise choice frequencies. As it is shown below, a joint analysis can take into account dependencies among the pairwise judgments and provide more powerful tests about the underlying decision strategies.

Our study on dependencies in individual paired comparison judgment follows the experimental paradigm reported by  Tversky (1969)  with the following three deviations. First, we considered four instead of five lotteries because the number of possible response pattern in the former case is much smaller (64 vs. 1024) which simplifies the presentation of the data for possible re-analyses. Second, we multiplied the payoff values by a factor of four to arrive at figures that are roughly comparable to the 1969 study. Third, in the original study, sessions were conducted on a weekly basis. In our experiment, sessions took place every day for a period of five days. Six pairs as well as 10 pairs of irrelevant lotteries were presented in randomized order within each session. A session was repeated four times with small breaks between sessions that involved unrelated tasks. In total, each pair was judged 20 times, yielding a total of 120 paired comparisons. This paper introduced the DR paired comparison model which simultaneously relaxes both the independence and invariance assumptions assumed in paired comparison models previously proposed in the literature. Utilities associated with the same item across trials are allowed to be correlated and not required to be either independent or identical. The underlying mechanism implied under such a formulation may prove to be closer to the actual judgmental process. In particular, as demonstrated by the application, the DR paired comparison model can provide a more systematic account of inconsistent behaviour than has been available so far.

To provide a powerful test of the independence assumption between item pairs, this paper focused on situations of a single judge evaluating multiple times the same item pair. However, our proposed framework is not restricted to this experimental setting. The DR model can be extended to the more common case of several judges evaluating each item pair only once. Such a sampling framework requires a multilevel formulation of the DR paired comparison model that is similar to the one developed by  Tsai and Böckenholt (2002) . This extension is straightforward provided it can be assumed that the dependency relationship among the item pairs is the same for all judges. Preliminary support for this assumption was provided by the lottery application where the same covariance structure could be fit to the data of the five subjects. Allowing for multiple judges with single evaluations of the item pairs facilitates the applicability of the framework to a wider range of experimental settings since the repeated presentation of the same items is possible only for certain types of stimuli.
