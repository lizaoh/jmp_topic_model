Information theoretic measures can be used as alternative non-parametric association measures that shine an additional light on data analysis in psychology. In that sense, it can be used as an alternative to, say, regression analysis ( Garner & McGill, 1956 ). It can be especially enlightening in a multivariate context as information theory introduces useful concepts to understand higher-order relationships among variables ( McGill, 1954; Watanabe, 1960 ). Some efforts in this direction include the use of entropy in the construction of fit indices used in exploratory graph analysis ( Golino et al., 2021 ). By introducing concepts like redundancy and synergy ( Rosas et al., 2016 ), it can be used to give additional structure and understanding to high-dimensional data. Additionally, the information-theoretic framework facilitates the unification of diverse statistical approaches ( Ince et al., 2017 ) on a common scale (e.g. bits) with, for example, mutual information allowing for the comparison between any combination of univariate/multivariate and discrete/continuous variables. This paper aims to reawaken interest in information theoretic measures that we deem relevant for psychology, comparing them with the more standard measures of association and clearing up some confusion concerning their use and applicability. Information theory, founded on the contributions of, among others,  Nyquist (1924)  and  Hartley (1928)  in the 1920s, and later formalized by  Shannon (1948) , has since extended beyond its original domain of communications engineering. It has evolved into a field of its own, intersecting many other disciplines such as physics, computer science, and statistics ( Cover & Thomas, 2006 ). Over the years, information theory has been applied in various scientific fields such as biology ( Adami, 2004; Quastler, 1953 ), economics ( Maasoumi, 1993; Philippatos & Wilson, 1972 ), linguistics ( Harris, 1991 ), neuroscience ( Borst & Theunissen, 1999; Dimitrov et al., 2011; Victor, 2006 ), and of course psychology which was one of the early adopters of information theory ( Attneave, 1959; Berlyne, 1957; McGill, 1954, 1957; Miller, 1956; Miller & Frick, 1949 ). The various immediate applications of information theory outside of communication engineering were met with some criticism by  Shannon (1956)  himself who believed in the usefulness of information-theoretic concepts in other fields but warned against superficial analogies and wholesale translation into other fields without a good understanding of the theory and strong experimental verification. Also from within psychology, there was cautionary criticism towards the use of information theory.  Cronbach (1955)  argued against the indiscriminate use of information measures purely based on the intuitive analogy between information processing in psychology and information processing in engineering. There was, at that time, already an understanding that information theory and information theoretical measures were different, albeit historically linked ( Quastler, 1955 ), and that we can measure variation in term of bits outside the context of information or communication. The confusion surrounding the non-semantic meaning of information in the Shannon sense ( Susswein, 2013 ) and the confounding of the various technical terms with their everyday use ( Ritchie, 1986 ) led to critiques that psychology is not compatible with information theory. After the initial burst of activity in the 50s and 60s, the use of information theory in psychology slowed down considerably. A renewed interest in the use of information theoretical measures has grown, based on a more mature understanding of information theory and its appropriate applications and shortcomings ( Adami, 2004; Dimitrov et al., 2011; Ince et al., 2017; Timme & Lapish, 2018 ). Beyond applications in experimental and cognitive psychology ( Canales-Johnson et al., 2023; Silverstein et al., 2017; Wollstadt et al., 2023 ), and the use of higher-order information in neuroscience ( Gelens et al., 2024; Luppi et al., 2024 ), information theoretical measures have not yet percolated throughout the general field of psychology. The belief that information theory and psychology are a bad fit lingers ( Luce, 2003 ) and is mainly rooted in the aforementioned confusions and attempts to limit the use of information theory to the modelling of cognitive behaviour. Without dismissing this recently maturing application of information theory ( Sayood, 2018 ), we want to stress that the use of information theoretical measures as promoted in this work is not intended as a model of human action-perception coupling or information processing. We argue that benefits of using information theory in psychology can be found without any assumptions towards an apparent similarity in concepts. In this paper, we argue that information theory has plenty to offer psychology and we will identify 3 main benefits: investigating variable dependency beyond linear dependencies, unifying different methods specific to different variable types, and natural extensions towards the investigation of higher-order behaviour.

In this section, we give a brief overview of two central information theoretical measures: entropy and mutual information. These are the building blocks for understanding variable dependence with information theory and can easily be compared with the more common measures of variance and correlation respectively. It remains important to emphasize the syntactic nature of information in the context of information theory: it is concerned with symbols or items and their statistical properties without addressing their meaning. Or in the words of Warren Weaver ( Shannon & Weaver, 1998 ): “To be sure, this word information in communication theory relates not so much to what you do say, as to what you could say”. Furthermore, we will not use information theory in its communication theoretic interpretation. In this way we do not require any of its assumptions such as the presence of a communication structure and the causal relationship between the parts of that structure. We can look at both variance and entropy to learn about the spread of a random variable, variance will tell us something about the number of outcome values far from the mean, while entropy informs us about the number of different outcome values and their relative frequencies. Variance uses the value of the outcomes, while entropy is independent of the specific values. This means that it can also be used with variables that have non-numeric values and it provides a common scale independent of the variable values and range that allows for easier comparison between variables. On the other hand, this disconnect can make it harder to interpret a single entropy value. It is helpful to think of extreme cases when comparing variance and entropy. A sharply peaked and narrow distribution would minimize both variance and entropy while maximizing variance would lead to a different form for the distribution than maximizing entropy would. Two peaks high away from each other would lead to high variance but high entropy would follow from more outcomes of similar frequency, irrespective of their values. This example also shows one should take care with applying variance to scenarios where a mean value is uninformative, such as a bimodal distribution or a heavy-tailed distribution. This scenario might be the simplest one for estimating mutual information, but also the one with the strongest assumptions on the underlying distribution and potentially the least interesting as we will not learn much more from mutual information than what we can already discern from the correlation. A histogram-based estimator can be used as a non-parametric estimator when we do not want to make any assumptions on the underlying distribution and want to be sensitive to all forms of non-linear relations. Its downsides are that the result will be sensitive to the choice of estimator, bias correction method and binning. Alternatively, the Gaussian copula can be used which is computationally efficient, deals well with multidimensional data and the combination of different data types, but is less sensitive to non-linear dependencies.

Since mutual information incorporates both linear and non-linear dependencies between the variables whereas the correlation coefficient only measures linear dependence, it is important to know how well the above estimators do in capturing these relations.  Fig. 6  compares the values of the various estimators for generated data from two random continuous variables with different (non-)linear dependencies. The top row shows data generated from a bivariate Gaussian distribution with different values for the correlation between both variables. The bottom row shows data from two random variables which are related in various non-linear ways, with zero correlation each. The figure helps to illustrate several differences between correlation and mutual information. For linearly related variables, mutual information and correlation seem in agreement with respect to which pair of variables are more dependent on each other or explain more of each other’s variance and uncertainty. The main difference is that mutual information is non-negative and thus does not differentiate between positive or negative linear relations, and that it does not have a fixed scale which makes isolated values more difficult to interpret. Note that the Gaussian copula estimator is zero for perfectly correlated variables, due to it being equal to the entropy of the copula density (Eq.  (B.8)  in  Appendix B ). The bottom row of  Fig. 6  shows that there are many instances of dependencies between variables that can be picked up by mutual information, despite having zero correlation. It also shows that the way mutual information is estimated makes a big difference, as a parametric covariance-based method will result in zero mutual information when the correlation is zero. The Gaussian copula estimated mutual information is also zero in these examples. This is because copula estimation is based on rank correlation and shares its sensitivity, resulting in it only picking up on monotonic (linear or non-linear) relations. Both correlation and mutual information are used to capture variable association but they differ in a profound way. Variable independence implies zero correlation, whereas zero correlation does not necessarily imply variable independence. Using mutual information, this relationship between independence and returning a value of zero goes both ways, since it equals the relative entropy between the joint distribution and the product of the marginals (Eq.  (A.18) ) and is sensitive to non-linear relations. Because mutual information can be calculated for discrete, continuous and mixed data types, we only need to use, understand, and compare a single measure, whereas the same question of variable dependencies using other statistical approaches requires a large amount of different techniques depending on the variable type ( Ince et al., 2017 ).

More and more voices are calling for a complex systems view of psychology and psychopathology ( Borsboom et al., 2022; Fried, 2022; van der Maas, 2024 ), which allows for and focuses on systems that are open, self-organizing, dissipative, uncertain, and adaptive ( Krakauer, 2024 ). The need to include higher-order associations or information between more than 2 variables in models of complex systems has become more and more apparent across various fields ( Battiston et al., 2021 ). Until this point, all measures that were introduced dealt with a single variable, univariate or multivariate, or with the information one (group of) variable(s) provides about another. Several different ways to generalize information theory to higher-order interdependencies have been investigated ( McGill, 1954; Rosas et al., 2019; Watanabe, 1960; Williams & Beer, 2010 ).

Investigating higher-order dependencies, either using PID or IID, gives us additional knowledge about how variables are related to each other and allows us to identify variables which have influence that is not visible by only looking at pairwise statistical dependencies. This higher-order behaviour could be due both to group interactions or to pairwise interactions on the structural level. It is important to stress that we are uncovering higher-order statistical dependencies between variables, which do not necessarily imply underlying higher-order mechanisms ( Rosas et al., 2022 ).

We have reviewed various information theoretical measures and compared them with more classical statistical measures. The case we want to make is not an either-or one but rather one of complementary approaches that can answer similar questions in different ways and help paint complementary pictures of variable behaviour. Having a broad toolbox and being able to make informed decisions as to when information theoretical measures are useful or not, can only enrich data analysis. A first important distinction is one of scale and units. Where (co)variance uses the squared units of the variables, information theoretical measures have a distinct scale usually with bits (or nats) as units. While for single variables the former may be easier to interpret immediately, for multiple variables or in a higher-order scenario the latter might be more useful than a product of different variable units each having their own scale. Comparisons between variables, variable pairs, or between different orders of variable groupings are made easier due to the single unit scale. Because information theoretical measures use only the distribution, they can be used on different data types and can even mix data types, e.g. the mutual information between a nominal and interval type variable. In that case we reduce the amount of different measures we need, again making comparisons easier. A choice to use or complement our analysis with the use of information theoretical measures would also be appropriate when we are expecting non-linear relations between the variables or are interested in the investigation of higher-order behaviour. Of course, the sensitivity to non-linear relations depends on the used estimator, which is a non-trivial choice that has to be made which can have appreciable impacts on the results. This complication is a downside of the added generality of information theoretical measures, there is no free lunch after all, and when the data (generating process) is well understood it could be more efficient to use the classical statistical measures for specific questions.

We have used the empathy data ( Briganti et al., 2018 ) throughout this work to showcase the information theoretical measures. But they can and have been used to analyse different types of data. Mutual information has been used as the effect size of a statistical test for neuroimaging data ( Ince et al., 2017 ), as an item selection method in computerized adaptive testing ( Wang, 2013 ), and to characterize the statistical structure of language ( Pothos & Juola, 2007 ). In the field of network psychometrics, entropy has been used to create a novel fit index for exploratory graph analysis ( Golino et al., 2021 ) and  Marinazzo et al. (2024)  have used O-information to visualize higher-order patterns in psychometric datasets, which get lost in a pairwise network approach. The use of O-information could be useful to shine new light on variable importance or centrality.

In order for methodology to become widely used, it needs to be available in a practical and accessible way. On the other hand, the development of packages also does not occur in a vacuum but is driven by application. Connected to this work is an R script with standalone functions for information theoretical measures that can be used to reproduce everything in this paper. This code can be found on Github and is linked in the Author note. We will reference a few other existing packages here that can be used to get started with information theoretical measures. In R there exist the packages entropy ( Hausser & Strimmer, 2008 ) and infotheo ( Meyer, 2009 ), which have various uni- and multivariate measures together with several options for biascorrection. JIDT ( Lizier, 2014 ) is a package in Java which has an extensive amount of measures en estimators implemented for both discrete and continuous-valued data and can be used in other languages as well. And in Python there are HOI ( Neri et al., 2024 ) and DIT ( G. James et al., 2018 ), which have implemented various measures for IID and PID.

When we use information theory as a statistical tool to measure variable variation and dependencies, instead of as a theory of communication with all the assumptions it includes, there is no reason why its applicability would be field dependent. We have highlighted parallels and complementarities between information theoretical measures such as entropy and mutual information on the one hand, and variance and correlation on the other. These links and even those between the higher-order measure co-information and analysis of variance have been known for a long time ( Garner & McGill, 1956 ), yet they remain underutilized in psychology. While they do not replace variance-based methods, they can offer valuable additional insights. The main properties that set information theoretical measures apart is their generality, their common scale which makes comparisons easier, and their various extension towards investigating higher-order behaviour. Information theory could play a useful role in the growing field of network psychometrics where it could not only complement the pairwise partial correlation networks but also be used to represent higher-order information between variables and inform ideas with respect to variable importance and centrality. When applying information-theoretical measures, it is important to take great care and consideration with regards to the practical implementation. Differences in estimators and binning approaches could result in different outcomes. In order to avoid replication issues, it is therefore crucial that researchers clearly state and justify why they choose a specific method. Whether the trade-off between higher generality and often more difficult practical implementation is worth it depends on the context, but it is worth considering. As complex systems thinking enters psychology, we should use tools that are flexible enough to capture the supposed complexity of the systems that we investigate. We hope that these tools, when used wisely as all quantitative methods should, can contribute to our understanding of psychological data and phenomena, and dispell any leftover beliefs that information theoretical measures are a bad fit for psychology.

When investigating pairwise or higher-order variable relationships, we can make use of surrogate data testing to understand the bias present in the data. This is different from a bias correction method, which results in a corrected value of the measure, in that it highlights the size and direction of the present bias, allowing us to interpret values of e.g. mutual information or O-information with respect to the surrogate data.
