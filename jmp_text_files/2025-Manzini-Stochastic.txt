A stochastic binary choice function is a stochastic choice function defined on a domain of binary menus, i.e. the subsets of a given finite set  
                         X 
                       that contain exactly two alternatives. The stochastic binary choice problem (henceforth just the  binary problem ) asks under what conditions the binary choice probabilities are  induced by rankings , in the sense that there exists a probability distribution on rankings over alternatives such that each alternative is chosen with the probability with which it is top in the ranking. 
                         1 
                      
                      
                         1 
                         We borrow this terminology from  Fishburn (1990) .

This is a classic problem that remains unsolved, although some necessary conditions are known, such as the Triangle Inequality ( Block and Marschak 1960 ) and others formulated by  Cohen and Falmagne (1990) ,  Fishburn (1990) , and  Gilboa (1990) . More recently,  Petri (2023)  has studied the stochastic binary choice problem by imposing the additional condition that rankings in the support of the rationalizing distribution satisfy the single-crossing condition of  Apesteguia et al. (2017) . 
                         2 
                      
                      
                         2 
                         The insufficiency of all mentioned conditions except the Triangle Inequality was proved by  Gilboa (1990) . A non-exhaustive list of other papers discussing variants of, and approaches to, the binary problem is  Marley (1990) ,  Gilboa and Monderer (1992) ,  Martí and Reinelt (2011) ,  Sprumont (2022) ,  Valkanova (2024)  and references therein.

In this paper we consider a variant of this problem, which we call the  stochastic 2-binary choice problem  (henceforth just the  2-binary problem ). It asks whether the choice probabilities can be induced by  at most two  rankings. In other words, the choice probabilities are induced by rankings and the probability distribution on rankings must have a support of cardinality at most two.

The double binariness restriction, on choices and on rankings, is of some practical relevance. In a large class of occasions, choice data in empirical work come in the form of a selection between binary options, for example for mode of transportation (public/private), voting behaviour (vote/abstain), labour force participation, trade union membership (see e.g.  Blundell and Powell (2004)  for a study of binary response models). In experimental psychology and economics, the “Two Alternative Forced Choice” response task is immensely popular (e.g.  Regenwetter et al. (2011) ). 
                         3 
                      
                      
                         3 
                         Among numerous other examples, see  Tversky (1969) ,  Hey and Orme (1994) ,  Halevy et al. (2018) ,  Alós-Ferrer et al. (2021) ,  Apesteguia and Ballester (2021)  and  Apesteguia and Ballester (2023) .

The focus on two orderings is motivated by a literature stemming from psychology, which has developed a class of “dual process” explanations of behaviour (see e.g.  Kahneman (2003) ,  Strack and Deutsch (2015) ). These theories assume that two different mental processes (or “systems”) – typically leading to differing decisions – are activated in different states which the agent may find itself in (e.g. a “hot” and a “cold” state, or a “utilitarian” and a “deontological” state in ethical dilemmas). A second motivation concerns decisions in long-term relationships (such as households), where alternating priority between the preferences of the partners may be a

more desirable decision method than issue-by-issue compromise. The binary restriction on rankings captures in a succinct manner these types of contexts and behavioural hypotheses.

The characterization results of the paper follow as corollaries of our main result, stated and proved in Section  5  This result shows that the last three axioms mentioned above are equivalent to the existence of a partition of the set of alternatives and an order on the classes of the partition that agrees with the binary choice probabilities in the following sense: whenever two alternatives belong to the same cell of the partition, neither is chosen with probability one; and whenever they belong to different cells, the one belonging to the higher cell in the order is chosen with probability one.

We also provide necessary and sufficient conditions for uniqueness, i.e. for choice probabilities to be induced by a unique distribution of at most two rankings. Finally, we show that in the characterizations the Triangle Inequality could be replaced by the standard transitivity property for non-stochastic binary choices.
