Two-mode clustering problems arise when it is necessary to cluster the object sets corresponding to both the rows and columns representing disjoint sets of entities of a proximity matrix. Although two-mode clustering methods have been developed for general types of matrices ( Hansohm, 2001 ;  Hartigan, 1972 ;  Mirkin, Arabie, & Hubert, 1995 ;  Trejos & Castillo, 2000 ;  van Rosmalen, Groenen, Trejos, & Castillo, 2005 ), we restrict our focus to two-mode binary matrices because of the breadth of their application to practical problems. For example, a noteworthy engineering application is the formation of manufacturing cells consisting of families of parts that require processing on a group of machines ( Brusco & Steinley, 2007a ), whereas the seriation of excavation data is a crucial area of study in archeology ( Kendall, 1971 ). In psychology, pioneering work was conducted by  Coombs (1964) , who developed the parallelogram model for two-mode binary matrices obtained from subjects selecting any subset of a group of items. An excellent early review of Coombs’ work and linkage to the graph-theoretic literature was provided by  Hubert (1974) .

In recent years, two-mode binary data has continued to play a formidable role in the social sciences literature ( Brusco & Steinley, 2006 ;  Doreian, Batagelj, & Ferligoj, 2004, 2005, Chapter 8 ;  Krolak-Schwerdt, 2003 ;  Leenen & Van Mechelen, 2004 ;  Rosenberg, Van Mechelen, & De Boeck, 1996 ;  Van Mechelen, Bock, & De Boeck, 2004 ). Especially noteworthy are the two-mode clustering applications pertaining to generalized blockmodeling in the social network and related literature bases ( Borgatti & Everett, 1997 ;  Doreian et al., 2005 ;  Wasserman & Faust, 1994 ). Examples include clustering a group of women and, simultaneously, social events attended ( Davis, Gardner, & Gardner, 1941 ), clustering Supreme Court justices and a collection of judicial cases ( Doreian et al., 2004 ), and clustering government organizations and political projects relevant to the organizations ( Mische & Pattison, 2000 ).

Doreian et al. (2004)  frame the problem of generalized blockmodeling of two-mode binary data within the context of equivalence relationships for one-mode data (see, for example,  Arabie, Boorman, & Levitt, 1978 ;  Batagelj, Ferligoj, & Doreian, 1992 ;  Brieger, Boorman, & Arabie, 1975 ;  Lorrain & White, 1971 ;  White & Reitz, 1983 ). Structural equivalence, which was originally offered by Lorrain and White, is especially relevant throughout the remainder of our paper. In a one-mode context, structural equivalence between two objects occurs when they share the same relationships with  all  of the objects in the network. The structural equivalence principle generalizes nicely to the two-mode context, facilitating the identification of clusters of row objects that have the same relationships to the column objects and vice versa.

As observed by  Doreian et al. (2005, Chapter 8) , one popular approach for two-mode binary network data involves representation of the data from a one-mode perspective. More specifically, the objects defining the rows and columns are combined to produce a single (larger) object set and corresponding one-mode network matrix. Accordingly, the object set of this new matrix can be partitioned into two mutually exclusive subsets (i.e., the original row and column objects), which facilitates treating the data as a  bipartite graph  (see, for example,  Borgatti & Everett, 1997, pp. 247–248 ). The edges (or arcs) of a bipartite graph occur only between objects in different sets, not between objects in the same set. Building on this principle, a number of authors have chosen to produce one-mode matrices for both the row-object and column-object sets, and apply clustering procedures to these two matrices independently ( Borgatti & Everett, 1997 ;  Brieger, 1974 ;  Doreian, 1979 ,  Freeman, 1980 ).

There have also been a variety of research efforts devoted to the development of permutation procedures for inducing a blockmodel structure of network data ( Arabie & Hubert, 1990 ;  Arabie, Hubert, & Schleutermann, 1990 ,  Brusco & Steinley, 2006 ;  McCormick, Schweitzer, & White, 1972 ). When applied to two-mode binary data, these methods permute the row and column object sets to induce large bands of 1s (and 0s) within the rows and columns of the reordered matrix. For example, Brusco and Steinley's method, which draws heavily from the pioneering work of  Kendall (1969)  and  Hubert (1974) , attempts to permute one-mode matrices for row and column objects to an approximate  Robinson (1951)  form. Robinson structure in a symmetric proximity matrix occurs when elements within any row or column are non-increasing when moving away from the main diagonal.

Brusco and Steinley (2006, 2007a)  have found that Robinson-based permutation of the row and column object sets frequently induces a visually appealing reordered binary matrix that exhibits properties consistent with structural equivalence. The reordered matrix is the principal benefit of permutation approaches because the visual representation of the data can facilitate the selection of the appropriate number of clusters for the row and column object sets. The primary limitations of permutation methods are twofold. First, there is a propensity for these methods to attempt to establish a block-diagonal form (i.e., blocks of 1s along the main diagonal and 0s elsewhere), which might not be appropriate for all two-mode blockmodeling scenarios. Second, permutation methods do not explicitly provide clusters of the row and column object sets, so further refinement of the solution is typically necessary. Because of these limitations,  Doreian et al. (2005, Chapter 8)  advocated the use of direct clustering procedures for two-mode blockmodeling of binary data.

Therefore, we refer to  Table 1  as a ‘(2, 2) blockmodel’ consisting of two clusters for the row objects and two clusters for the column objects. Together, the row and column clusters create four blocks. The resulting solution perfectly satisfies the structural equivalence property because three of the blocks contain all 1s, whereas the remaining block contains all 0s.

For empirical data sets, perfect structural equivalence is typically difficult to achieve using a parsimonious number of row and column clusters. Therefore, it is necessary to use procedures that can produce blockmodels that approximate structural equivalence to the greatest extent possible. Assuming that the location (or placement) of complete and null ideal blocks is predetermined, this goal inherently leads to an optimization problem that requires minimizing the number of inconsistencies within ideal blocks. More specifically, the goal is to minimize the number of 1s that occur in null blocks plus the number of 0s that occur in complete blocks. A straightforward generalization of this criterion can be realized through differential weighting of violations in different ideal blocks ( Doreian et al., 2005, Chapter 8 ).

What if the placement of ideal blocks within the blockmodel structure is unknown, or at least not known with certainty? For such instances, we consider a within-cluster sum-of-squares criterion employed by  van Rosmalen et al. (2005)  that is, effectively, a generalization of the well-known  K -means criterion ( Howard, 1966 ;  MacQueen, 1967 ) for one-mode clustering. We compare two procedures for obtaining solutions to this problem: (a) a multistart implementation of the two-mode  K -means heuristic developed by van Rosmalen and his colleagues, and (b) a new multistart implementation of a variable neighborhood search algorithm ( Hansen, Mladenoviĉ, 2003 ;  Mladenoviĉ & Hansen, 1997 ) that we developed explicitly for this problem. We found that, although the performance of both methods was excellent, the proposed variable neighborhood search method provided solutions with slightly greater explained variation for most test problems.

We also offer a contribution that, to our knowledge, is unprecedented for binary two-mode clustering. Specifically, we measure the capability of heuristic algorithms to recover the underlying cluster structure of data sets, using  Hubert & Arabie's (1985)  adjusted Rand index as a measure of agreement for row and column partitions. In a large experimental study comprised of eight factors and 768 unique test problems, we found that both the two-mode  K -means algorithm and the variable neighborhood search heuristic provided exceptional recovery, despite the fact that these procedures assume that the placement of ideal blocks is unknown.

In  Section 2 , we describe the binary two-mode clustering problem that assumes a known ideal block structure and present a transfer-exchange algorithm for its solution. In  Section 3 , we develop the binary two-mode clustering problem where the ideal block structure is unknown and offer two-mode  K -means and variable neighborhood search algorithms as solution procedures. The results of our computational experiments are provided in  Section 4 , and  Section 5  demonstrates the algorithms using two well-known empirical data sets. The paper concludes with a summary and suggestions for future research in  Section 6 .

The TMTEA algorithm requires an initial feasible solution to problem P1 in Step 0. In our research, we construct initial partitions by randomly assigning each of the objects to a row cluster  k  and a column cluster  l . Step 1a of the algorithm evaluates all possible transfers of objects from their current row cluster to one of the other row clusters, whereas Step 1b provides a similar assessment of transfers of column objects. Step 1c ensures repetition of Steps 1a and 1b until no further improvement is possible via transfers. Steps 2a–c are similar to those in Step 1, except that exchanges of cluster membership for two objects are investigated rather than transfer of a single object. The algorithm terminates when there are no transfers or exchanges that will further reduce the number of inconsistencies. The obtained solution is, therefore, locally optimal with respect to all possible singleton transfers and pairwise exchanges. It does not necessarily, however, represent a global minimum for the number of inconsistencies. To guard against a poor local minimum, we restart TMTEA 500 hundred times with each restart using a different random initialization in Step 0.

One possible solution approach for problem P2 is a generalization of the  K -means algorithm ( Howard, 1966 ;  MacQueen, 1967 ;  Späth, 1980 ) for one-mode clustering. In the one-mode context, the  K -means algorithm uses two alternating phases: (a) computation of cluster centroids—the means for each variable in each cluster, and (b) re-assignment of each object to its nearest centroid. The alternating process terminates when no objects change membership during re-assignment. The generalization to the two-mode case requires consideration of both the row objects and column objects in the alternating process. This procedure provided exceptional performance in a recent comparative study conducted by  van Rosmalen et al. (2005) , outperforming implementations of simulated annealing and tabu search for P2. TMKMA requires an initial feasible solution to problem P2 in Step 0. In our research, we construct initial partitions by randomly assigning each of the objects to a row cluster  k  and a column cluster  l . The final partitions produced by TMKMA are sensitive to the initial partitions provided at Step 0 and, therefore, the algorithm in not guaranteed to produce a globally optimal solution for P2. To guard against the potential acceptance of a poor local optimum, we reinitialize the algorithm with 500 random starting solutions. This practice of using a large number of re-initializations is consistent with recent implementations of  K -means clustering in a one-mode context ( Brusco, 2004 ;  Steinley, 2003 ).

In Step 1 of the algorithm, cluster memberships for the columns are fixed. Centroids are computed in Step 1a, and the distance of each object from each row-cluster centroid is computed in Step 1b. In Step 1c, each object is assigned to its nearest row cluster. In Step 2, each object's row-cluster membership is treated as fixed. Centroids are computed in Step 2a, and the distance of each object from each column-cluster centroid is computed in Step 2b. In Step 2c, each object is assigned to its nearest column cluster. If the reassignment processes in Steps 1 and 2 produce an improvement in the within-cluster sum-of-squares loss function  (6) , then the incumbent partition is updated in Step 3, and processing returns to Step 1. If no improvement occurs, then the algorithm terminates.

Variable neighborhood search is a metaheuristic for combinatorial optimization problems that allows for a systematic search of a solution space. This procedure, which was formalized by  Mladenoviĉ and Hansen (1997)  (see also,  Hansen & Mladenoviĉ, 2003 ), has been demonstrated as an effective solution method for one-mode clustering problems in previous experiments ( Brusco & Steinley, 2007b ,  Hansen & Mladenoviĉ, 2001 ;  Pacheco & Valencia, 2003 ). The fundamental aspect of variable neighborhood search is to investigate increasingly larger neighborhoods of the current best solution (incumbent) solution. For a given neighborhood size, a perturbation scheme is used to generate a neighboring solution, which serves a starting point for a heuristic algorithm. If the heuristic finds a solution that is better than the incumbent, then that solution is accepted as the new incumbent; otherwise the neighborhood size is incremented to expand the scope of the search. Relative to TMKMA, the advantage of variable neighborhood search is that it offers a more vigorous exploration of the neighborhood of incumbent solutions.

The engine of the variable neighborhood search process is TMKMA, which is implemented in Step 1. If this algorithm produces a solution better than the current incumbent, then the improved solution becomes the new incumbent in Step 2, and the parameter  nsize  is reset to 1 to enable the full range of neighborhood search sizes to be investigated for the new incumbent. If the TMKMA solution is not better than the incumbent, then control passes to Step 3 where the  nsize  parameter is incremented and the termination criterion ( nsize  >  max _ size ) is checked.

Step 4 modifies the incumbent solution by perturbing the cluster means for randomly selected row/column cluster pairs. Our preliminary tests revealed that the perturbation scheme offered by Eq. After perturbing  nsize  of the cluster-mean pairs of the incumbent solution, control returns to Step 1 where TMKMA is applied using the newly modified centroids. For small values of  nsize , the computational burden is modest because limited reassignment is needed to return the solution to a local optimum. Larger values of  nsize  can require somewhat more effort, but also facilitate the exploration of a larger neighborhood around the incumbent solution.

Like TMKMA, TMVNSA is not guaranteed to obtain a global minimum for problem P2 and is sensitive to the initial partitions. Therefore, we use 30 restarts of TMVNSA with each restart using a different randomly generated permutation. Effectively, our comparison of TMKMA and TMVNSA results in the following question: Is it better to use TMKMA with a large number of restarts (e.g., 500), or is it better to embed TMKMA in TMVNSA and use fewer restarts (e.g.. 30) but allow greater thoroughness in the neighborhood search of local optima? Our choices of 500 and 30 restarts for TMKMA and TMVNSA, respectively, were based primarily on a desire to keep the computation times roughly comparable during the experiment. Our experiments in the next section compare multistart implementations of TMKMA and TMVNSA, which we refer to as multistart-TMKMA and multistart-TMVNSA, respectively.

                         Recovery of true cluster structure is measured using  Hubert and Arabie's (1985)  adjusted Rand index, which is the gold standard for measuring partition agreement in the classification literature ( Brusco, 2004 ;  Brusco & Steinley, 2007b ;  Steinley, 2004, 2006 ;  Steinley & Brusco, 2007a ). The adjusted Rand index evaluates the placement of all  N ( N −1)/2 (or  M ( M −1)/2) object pairs in each of two partitions: (a) the true partition associated with the data generation process, and (b) the partition produced by the clustering algorithm. Concordance between the two partitions occurs when both members of an object pair are placed in the same cluster in partition (a) and in the same cluster in partition (b), or if the members are placed in different clusters in partition (a) and different clusters in partition (b). Discordance occurs when both members of an object pair are placed in the same cluster for one of the partitions, but in different clusters for the other partition. The adjusted Rand index achieves a value of 1 when two partitions are in perfect agreement, and a value of 0 is indicative of agreement that is equal to that expected by chance.

The experimental test conditions consisted of eight factors measured at two levels each. The first factor, the number of rows of the two-mode binary matrix was tested at levels of  N =60 and 120. The number of columns, which represented the second factor, was evaluated at levels of  M =60 and 120, respectively. The true number of clusters for the rows and columns were the third and fourth factors, respectively. The levels for the number of rows (columns) were  K =3 ( L =3) and  K =6 ( L =6). The fifth and sixth factors corresponded to the density of the clusters for the rows and columns, respectively. For both rows and columns, the density levels were: (a) an equal number of objects in each cluster, and (b) 60% of the objects in one cluster and 40% of the objects equally spread among the remaining clusters.

We refer to the seventh factor as “block mix” because it represents the mix of null and complete blocks in the blockmodel structure. The two levels for this factor were: (a) 50% complete blocks and 50% null blocks, and (b) 70% complete blocks and 30% null blocks. For example, if  K =6 and  L =3, then 9 of the 18 blocks would be complete for the 50% block mix setting and 12 or 13 blocks would be complete for the 70% setting. The eighth factor of the design is referred to as “block strength”, which corresponds to the proportion of zeros (ones) in a null (complete) block. At the high strength setting, the probability of generating a value of zero (one) for a null (complete) block is 0.9. This setting will tend to produce a strong structure where null blocks are well differentiated from complete blocks. At the low strength setting, the probability of generating the correct binary element for each block is only 0.75, which will produce a weaker structure.

Our experimental design produces 2 8 =256 cells and we generated three replications per cell, resulting in a total of 768 unique test problems. For each test problem, the true cluster memberships for both the rows and the columns were stored in a vector. The multistart-TMKMA and multistart-TMVNSA algorithms were written in Fortran and implemented on a 2.2 GHz, Pentium IV PC with 1 GB of RAM. Because of its greater efficiency, multistart-TMKMA was permitted 500 random initializations, whereas multistart-TMVNSA was allowed only 30. The VAF results are broken into two categories: (a) the average VAF across all 384 test problems for each factor level setting, and (b) the percentage of best VAF's identified across the 384 problems. Multistart-TMVNSA (0.4343) holds a narrow advantage over multistart-TMKMA (0.4327) with respect to average VAF across all test problems, and is also superior at all factor level settings. Although the average VAF differences are typically small, the results for percentage of best VAF's reveals that multistart-TMVNSA almost always yielded the best VAF, whereas multistart-TMKMA did not. Across all test problems, multistart-TMVNSA obtained the best VAF for 94.14% of the test problems; however, the corresponding figure for multistart-TMKMA was only 53.26%.

Across all test problems, the CPU times for multistart-TMKMA and multistart-TMVNSA were very close, with averages of 3.12 and 3.00 s, respectively. The most important factors affecting CPU time were the number of clusters for the rows and columns. Relative to multistart-TMKMA, multistart-TMVNSA required less CPU time at smaller cluster sizes, but more CPU time for larger cluster sizes.

The results indicate that multistart-TMVNSA often found solutions with better VAF's than multistart-TMKMA, but the actual differences in VAF measurements were very small. Similar results have also been observed in comparisons of highly effective algorithms for other combinatorial optimization problems, such as least-squares unidimensional scaling ( Brusco, 2006 ;  Murillo, Vera, & Heiser, 2005 ). In the two-mode clustering context, the natural question is: Do the small improvements in VAF associated with multistart-TMVNSA translate into any meaningful differences with respect to recovery of cluster structure?

The ARI is also reported for multistart-TMTEA solutions for P1. Because multistart-TMTEA is applied under the assumption that the types of blocks are known, this algorithm should produce better cluster recovery than either multistart-TMKMA or multistart-TMVNSA, which assume the placement of block types is unknown. Effectively, the ARI results for multistart-TMTEA provide a benchmark that can be used to measure how well multistart-TMKMA and multistart-TMVNSA are doing with respect to structure recovery.

The most salient findings gleaned from  Table 3  are as follows: (1) multistart-TMTEA produced excellent recovery of true cluster structure, with average ARI's of .8416 and .8586 for rows and columns, respectively, (2) both multistart-TMKMA and multistart-TMVNSA provide good recovery of row and column cluster structures, with average (across all 768 test problems) ARI's ranging from .7213 to .7562, and (3) there is very little difference in the recovery performances of multistart-TMKMA and multistart-TMVNSA, with each method providing slightly better average ARI results than the other for certain factor levels. The first two of these findings were anticipated. Multistart-TMTEA provides exceptional recovery because of the assumption that ideal block placements are known. The average recovery performances of multistart-TMKMA and multistart-TMVNSA, although definitely inferior to multistart-TMTEA, are impressive given their lack of information on block placements. The third finding suggests that multistart-TMKMA and multistart-TMVNSA yield comparable ARI's, and that the slightly better VAF performance of multistart-TMVNSA does not imply superior recovery.

The multistart-TMKMA and multistart-TMVNSA algorithms have the advantage of not requiring the advance placement of complete and null blocks. However, upon completion of these algorithms, each of the  KL  blocks in the final solution can be judged as complete or null based on the number of 1s and 0s in the block. Any block with more 1s than 0s (more 0s than 1s) can be specified as complete (null). Once this specification is made, the multistart-TMTEA algorithm can be applied to refine the solution. This potential refinement process suggests a two-stage solution strategy. In stage one, multistart-TMKMA (or multistart-TMVNSA) is used to solve problem P2 and obtain the placement of complete and null blocks in the two-mode clustering solution. In stage two, multistart-TMTEA is used to solve problem P1 given the block placements from stage one.

We tested the two-stage solution approach by applying multistart-TMTEA to each of the 768 test problems using the block placements obtained by multistart-TMKMA and multistart-TMVNSA. The ARI results for the two-stage procedure are displayed in  Table 4 
                         . The two-stage process facilitated markedly better recovery than using multistart-TMKMA or multistart-TMVNSA alone. For example, observe from  Table 3  that the average row ARI's for multistart-TMKMA and multistart-TMVNSA were 0.7213 and 0.7346, respectively. However,  Table 4  shows that refinement of these solutions with multistart-TMTEA improved the recovery figures to 0.7846 and 0.7944, respectively. This is indicative of considerable progress toward the benchmark average row ARI of 0.8416 obtained when block placements were assumed known.

We applied both multistart-TMKMA and multistart-TMVNSA to the Supreme Court data under the assumption of a (7,4) blockmodel and obtained the same solution with both methods. We also investigated a variety of cluster sizes in the neighborhood of the (7,4) solution to determine whether changes in the VAF criterion could provide some basis for  blockmodel specification . The TMKMA and TMVNSA algorithms yielded VAF's (number of inconsistencies) of 0.7670 (20), 0.7756 (20), 0.8215 (14), 0.8493 (11), 0.8579 (11), 0.8804 (9), and 0.8914 (9) for (6,3), (7,3), (6,4), (7,4), (8,4), (7,5), and (8,5) blockmodels, respectively. These results indicate that, for this range of blockmodels, increasing the number of clusters for justices leads to greater improvement in both VAF and the number of inconsistencies relative to increasing the number of clusters for cases. Moreover, it is clear that the progression from a (6,3) blockmodel to the (7,4) blockmodel is legitimized by the marked increase in VAF (0.7670–0.8493) and sharp decrease in the number of inconsistencies (20–11).

Using the VAF measure, an analyst could build a case for moving from the (7,4) to the (7,5) blockmodel because of the improvement from 0.8493 to 0.8804. This selection also reduces the number of inconsistencies from 11 to 9. However, it is at this point that we see the formidable challenges that arise regarding blockmodel specification. Although both criteria improve, parsimony is sacrificed. Closer investigation revealed that expanding to 5 clusters for only 9 justices resulted in the splitting of the liberal wing, placing {Breyer, Ginzberg} and {Souter, Stevens} in separate clusters principally because of their voting differences on the {Citizenship, Seat Belts, United Foods} cluster for cases. Accordingly, we concur with  Doreian et al.'s (2005, Chapter 8)  decision to accept the (7,4) blockmodel as an appropriate solution. Regrettably, however, there is no clear information from the VAF statistic that will indicate the best blockmodel specification.

To the best of our knowledge, our study is the first to assess the recovery performance of the multistart-TMTEA algorithm within the context of blockmodeling of binary data. Our conclusion is that the algorithm performs extremely well, but the value of the method is diminished by the assumption that block placements are known. Therefore, we considered formulation P2, which makes no such assumption and can be used with continuous as well as binary data. We compared two procedures for P2: (1) an existing two-mode  K -means algorithm ( van Rosmalen et al., 2005 ), and (2) a new two-mode variable neighborhood search algorithm. We found that multistart-TMVNSA generally provided slightly better VAF's than multistart-TMKMA; however, differences in recovery of true cluster structure were inconsequential. In general, both procedures did an excellent job recovering structure for problem P2 given their lack of information regarding block placement. Additional improvement was realized when applying multistart-TMTEA using the block placements provided by multistart-TMKMA or multistart-TMVNSA.

There are two principal limitations associated with the method and analyses reported in our paper and both of these pertain to blockmodel specification. First, and foremost, is that all of our simulation analyses in this paper were restricted to the case of structural equivalence under the assumption of equal weights for inconsistencies. As described by  Doreian et al. (2004, 2005, Chapter 8) , the adaptation of TMTEA for alternative forms of equivalence is relatively straightforward. Moreover, simulated annealing, genetic algorithm, tabu search, and variable neighborhood search methods can be designed for problem P1, as well as variations that permit other equivalence structures. Problem P2, however, is especially designed for structural equivalence. One possible strategy is to use multistart-TMKMA or multistart-TMVNSA to obtain a solution to problem P2 and inspect the resulting blockmodel. Visual identification of blocks could lead to the establishment of regular, row-regular, or column-regular conditions for one or more blocks, and the resulting blockmodel could be refined using an appropriately modified version of multistart-TMTEA.

The second limitation of our simulation study was the assumption that the number of clusters for both row and column objects was known, which also creates the potential for blockmodel misspecification. Determination of the appropriate number of clusters remains a challenging issue in one-mode cluster analysis, and is even more complex in two-mode clustering. In the one-mode context, ad-hoc scree plots of clustering objective criterion indices can be used to facilitate the selection of the number of clusters for discrete partitioning methods. For mixture-model-based clustering methods, information criteria, such as the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) proposed by  Schwartz (1978) , can be used to facilitate determination of the number of clusters. Unfortunately, recent computational experience in one-mode cluster analysis suggests that current methods for choosing the number of clusters are problematic for both discrete partitioning and mixture model approaches ( Steinley & Brusco, 2007a, b ).

In the absence of any definitive measures for choosing the number of clusters in two-mode clustering, we recommend using permutation methods for obtaining a visual determination of a reasonable range for the appropriate number of row and column clusters. The bond-energy algorithm ( Arabie & Hubert, 1990 ;  Arabie et al., 1990 ;  McCormick et al., 1972 ) or Robinson methods ( Brusco & Steinley, 2006 ;  Hubert, 1974 ) would seem to be plausible candidates for making this decision.
