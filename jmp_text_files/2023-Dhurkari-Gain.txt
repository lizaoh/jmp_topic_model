Understanding an individual’s decision-making process in the selection, consumption, and disposal of products and services is essential for marketers. For more than four decades, understanding the consumer decision-making process has been one of the core areas of research in consumer behavior. During the past several years, marketing and consumer research focused intensively on the multi-attribute models of attitude. These studies were carried out with the proposition that product attributes form the basis of consumer attitudes toward products.

The rational choice theory has contributed considerably to the development of models of consumer decisions. One of the assumptions of rational theories is that the decision-maker has a well-defined preference for alternatives, not depending upon the description of alternatives. Another major assumption is that each alternative in the choice set has an associated independent utility or subjective value that does not depend upon the other alternatives in the choice set. Almost all the models whether it is an adequacy importance model ( Bass & Talarzyk, 1972; Bass & Wilkie, 1973; Bluestein, Capon, Farley, & Howard, 1973; Cohen Joel, Fishbein, & Ahtola, 1972; Krantz David & Tversky, 1971 ) or Fishbein model ( Calder & Lutz, 1972; Fishbein, 1972; Lutz, 1973; Weddle & Bettman, 1973 ) follow a linear compensatory strategy and build upon the linear aggregation of consumer attitude towards attributes of alternative products.

The linear aggregation model follows a compensatory strategy where the advantage offered by an alternative on one attribute can be compensated by the disadvantage offered by the same alternative on another attribute ( Fishburn, 1978 ).  Fishburn (1976)  introduced non-compensatory preference structures and aggregation procedures in which the advantage offered by an alternative on one attribute cannot be compensated by the disadvantage offered by the same alternative on another attribute. Non-compensatory preference indicates the absence of tradeoffs. Since the linear aggregation model was repeatedly found to be inconsistent with the actual choice behavior (e.g.,  Kahneman & Tversky, 1979 ), there were many proposals in this direction (for example, refer  Attardi, Cerreta, Sannicandro, & Torre, 2018; Mazziotta & Pareto, 2016; Munda & Nardo, 2009 ) but still, non-compensatory preference structures in multi-attribute settings deserve more attention and accurate discussion.

For instance, according to  Fishburn (1974, 1975, 1976) , the overall evaluation of an alternative depends upon a handful of attributes in the case of non-compensatory aggregation procedures because of the absence of tradeoffs among various attributes under consideration. Therefore, it is difficult to arrive at a composite score of the alternative using all the attributes and the only aggregation procedure that can provide the rank order of alternatives in the absence of tradeoffs is the lexicographic order. This seems to be very restrictive and closes the discussion on interesting non-compensatory aggregation procedures that can be used to derive a composite score of all the alternatives which later can be used to rank order the alternatives. Recently,  Greco, Ishizaka, Tasiou, and Torrisi (2021)  attempted to construct non-compensatory composite scores using the PROMETHEE (Preference Ranking Organization Method for Enrichment Evaluations) method.

In addition, the context-dependent model ( Tversky & Simonson, 1993 ) has shown that the preference for an alternative also depends upon the choice set. According to the tradeoff contrast hypothesis ( Simonson & Tversky, 1992 ), “ the tendency to prefer an alternative is enhanced or hindered depending on whether the tradeoffs within the set under consideration are favorable or unfavorable to that option” . The preference for alternatives (of any complexity) is often constructed, not merely revealed ( Payne, Bettman, Coupey, & Johnson, 1992; Slovic, Griffin, & Tversky, 1990 ). This means that the decision-maker does not refer to a master list of preferences while defining his/her preference structure. Rather, the decision-maker is often found to use contextual information like the number of alternatives, the number of attributes or criteria, and the similarities among the alternatives over different attributes or criteria while defining his/her preferences.

The linear aggregation model interprets attribute weight as “ per unit contribution of the score of an alternative to that attribute on the overall evaluation of that alternative ”. However, according to  Kauffman ’s ( 1993 ) complexity theory (NK Landscape), the fitness contribution of an attribute on the overall evaluation of an alternative also depends upon that alternative’s performance on other attributes. For example, suppose three candidates are to be evaluated based on their performance in three subjects and suppose all the subjects are equally important. Then, as per the linear aggregation procedure, each unit score of a candidate on any subject will have a 0.33-unit contribution to the overall score of that candidate. However, the complexity theory provides an opportunity to look at the problem holistically. In Kauffman’s NK model, N is the number of attributes and K is the level of interaction between attributes. The landscape is considered to be smooth when there is no interaction (K  
                         = 
                       0) and the fitness contribution of one attribute on the overall evaluation of an alternative is independent of that alternative’s performance on other attributes. However, the landscape is rugged when the fitness contribution of one attribute on the overall evaluation of an alternative depends upon that alternative’s performance on all other N − 1 attributes. In the example mentioned above (N  
                         = 
                       3) it is possible that the per unit contribution of the score of a candidate in one subject on the overall score also depends upon that candidate’s scores in the other two subjects. In other words, the per unit contribution of the score on the first subject of candidate 1 (subject1 25, subject2 60, subject3 60) on that candidate’s overall score is more than the per unit contribution of the score on the first subject of candidate 2 (subject1 25, subject2 90, subject 30) and candidate 3 (subject1 25, subject2 25, subject3 95). If the three subjects were given equal importance or weights, all three candidates will receive the same score (and same rank) because the linear aggregation procedure compensates a unit score on one subject with a unit score on any other subject. However, when the decision-maker is asked to rank order the candidate, it is likely that the decision-maker may prefer candidate 1 over the other two candidates by considering the scatter of information not only within a subject but also across different subjects. In such a situation, K is equal to N − 1.

This paper proposes a new method named as Multi-Attribute Gain Loss (MAGL). MAGL is an attempt to explore nonlinear, non-compensatory cognitive processes in order to map the judgment of the decision-makers to their choices. In the MAGL method, a few of the descriptive theories of decision-making (like prospect theory, complexity theory, norm theory, and context-dependent choice theories) are hybridized with the principles of the Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA) to deal with the complexities of MCD problems. The MAGL method is an attempt to model the decision-making process of an individual under the broader framework of MCDA. In MCDA, the decision-maker provides his/her preferences for each alternative under each of the attributes in addition to the relative importance of attributes. But how these preferences are aggregated, interpreted, and assimilated by the same decision-maker when asked to make a choice on the same set of alternatives and attributes? This vital question is addressed by the proposed MAGL method.

The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section  2  covers the particular segment of MCDA where MAGL fits. Section  2  also covers relevant literature. The proposed method is detailed in Section  3 . To assess the predictive power of the proposed MAGL method, two studies were conducted. The methodological information and the results are provided in Sections  4 and 5  respectively. Implications of MAGL in predicting choices in competitive market conditions are discussed in Section  6 . Section  7  concludes the paper.

There are various methods to elicit and measure consumer attitudes toward products & services and their attributes. These methods are either classified as compositional or decompositional ( Green & Srinivasan, 1978; Pessemier, Burger, Teach, & Tigert, 1971 ). In compositional techniques, the decision-maker defines his/her preference for attribute levels of various alternatives independently within each of the attributes. These independent preferences are then combined and aggregated to estimate the overall attitude towards each of the alternatives, independent of the context (the choice set or the alternatives). On the other hand, the decomposition methods seek the preferences of the decision-maker on the profile of alternatives (context-dependent). These preferences are then used to estimate part-worth values or utilities of various attribute levels. The full profile conjoint analysis is one of the popular techniques which follows the principle of decomposition. Self-explicated methods follow a compositional approach, where the consumer defines his/her preferences separately and independently on each of the attributes, comparatively having a lesser information load for the decision-maker.  Day (1971, 1972)  provides an excellent comparison between compositional and decomposition attitude models.

Further, there are two schools of thought promoting self-explicated methods. Methods like ELECTRE (election et choix traduisant la realite) by  Roy (1968, 1976)  and PROMETHEE ( Brans, Vincke, & Mareschal, 1986 ) are outranking methods and often referred to as representing the European School of thought, whereas the methods like MAVT/MAUT (Multi-attribute Value/Utility Theory) ( Keeney & Raiffa, 1976 ), AHP (Analytic Hierarchy Process) & ANP (Analytic Network Process) ( Saaty, 1977 ), TOPSIS (Technique for Order Preferences by Similarity to Ideal Solutions) ( Hwang & Yoon, 1981 ) and Choquet Integral ( Grabisch & Labreuche, 2010 ) are their American counterparts. The methods originating from European schools often use standard preference structures to fit the preferences of the decision-maker while methods originating from American schools provide an opportunity for the decision-maker to define his/her preference structure using various scales.

In addition, some of these methods support the processing of quantitative information only (like TOPSIS) while some support processing of quantitative as well as qualitative information (like AHP). Some methods support the processing of only crisp and certain information (like AHP) while some support processing of uncertain information (intervals or range or having associated probabilities). The proposed MAGL method is aligned with the American schools of thought and supports the processing of quantitative as well as qualitative information (certain or crisp and without any uncertainty). In this segment of discrete alternative MCDM methods, the alternatives and the attributes are identified a priori. Thereafter, the alternatives are evaluated using the preference structure defined by the decision-maker on various attributes. Thus, the role of the decision-maker is very important which necessitates that the MCDM method used by the decision-maker to solve an MCD problem must abide by the cognitive capabilities and the descriptive realities of the decision-maker. One of the popular descriptive theories of decision-making is the prospect theory.

According to Prospect Theory, individuals are found to assess given alternatives in terms of gains and losses with respect to a fixed reference point. The reference point is defined using the current status quo or aspirations or norms. The gains are associated with positive outcomes while the losses are with negative outcomes. The unfavorable or negative outcomes (losses) always loom larger than the favorable or positive outcomes (gains) of the same magnitude ( Kahneman & Tversky, 1979; Tversky & Kahneman, 1991 ). Initially, the prospect theory was used to explain decisions making under risk and uncertainty, but since the value function does not depend upon associated probability value, prospect theory can very well be used to explain decision-making under a riskless environment ( Abdellaoui, Bleichrodt, & L’Haridon, 2008; Tversky & Kahneman, 1991 ).

For example,  Vommi and Kakollu (2017)  proposed a multi-attribute decision-making method that uses different loss functions (linear, quadratic, or cubic) to measure the loss while selecting an ideal or best alternative. The loss in an attribute is measured from the best value of that attribute and not from the reference point. As there is no preference elicitation from the decision-maker, the work of  Vommi and Kakollu (2017)  can only be used when the attributes are quantitative in nature. Further, the loss value is common for all the alternatives in an attribute. However, according to prospect theory, the value curve for losses generally has a non-linear tendency.  Fan, Zhang, Chen, and Liu (2013)  proposed a prospect theory based MCDM method in which the attribute values and aspiration levels of various alternatives are represented in two different formats viz. positive crisp (absolute) numbers as well as positive intervals. The decision-maker defines the preferences using positive numbers and not using a bipolar scale. The gain matrix and loss matrix are constructed by calculating the gain and the loss for each alternative from the reference point of the decision-maker in an attribute. These gain-loss values inside the matrices are normalized with respect to the ideal best value (or the ideal worst value if the best value is zero). The value function of prospect theory and its standard parameters are used to compute the value for each alternative by using the normalized gains and losses. This aggregated value is then used to rank order the alternatives. The gain and loss associated with various alternatives are analyzed within an attribute and not across attributes. In order to make the prescriptions of the MCDM method comparable with the choices made by the very same decision-maker, it is important to include an analysis of the performance of alternatives not only within an attribute but also across attributes. The choice processes involve the analysis of alternatives in both directions (across attributes and across alternatives). In the TODIM (an acronym in Portuguese for Iterative multi-criteria decision-making) method, the dominance of an alternative over another alternative in an attribute is computed pair-wise and using the value function of prospect theory. The generalized version of TODIM uses the standard parameters as proposed by prospect theory. The overall performance of an alternative is computed by the additive function. The work of  Krohling and de Souza (2012)  proposed Fuzzy TODIM which combines the prospect theory and fuzzy numbers to handle uncertain decision matrices. However, the TODIM method and its extensions ( Gomes & González, 2012; Gomes & Lima, 1992; Gomes & Rangel, 2009 ) have the same limitation of not analyzing the performance of alternatives within an attribute and across attributes, simultaneously.

Some other attempts were also made to extend the prospect theory in a multi-attribute setting ( Bleichrodt, Schmidt, & Zank, 2009; Lahdelma & Salminen, 2009; Salminen & Wallenius, 1993; Zank, 2001 ) but there were fewer attempts to map the judgments of the decision-maker to their choice processes by analyzing the performance of alternatives within an attribute and across attributes. Further, most of the prospect theory-based work uses the standard parametric values of the prospect theory value function, independent of the choice set or the context in place.  Fig. 1  provides the classification of MCDM methods developed using Prospect Theory and where the proposed MAGL method fits.

Further, a fundamental characteristic of all choice-based models is that they view choice as a constructive process where the decision-maker modifies their preferences as per the given choice set ( Bettman, Luce, & Payne, 1998; Payne et al., 1992 ). It is, therefore, necessary to capture the interaction within an attribute and across the attributes and accordingly either adjust the attribute weights or include some bias measurement that can escalate or demise the prominence of attributes at different intensities. By doing this, it is likely that the solution prescribed by the MCDM method will become more realistic. Therefore, if it is at all required to predict the choice behavior of the decision-maker under the frameworks of MCDA, it is necessary to analyze how the decision-maker assesses the two-way interaction (across the attribute and across the alternatives). According to  Luce (1959) , the probability that an alternative is selected from a choice set is a simple ratio of the attractiveness of that alternative to the sum of the attractiveness of all the other alternatives under consideration.  Meyer and Johnson (1995)  describes that the probability that an alternative is selected is not only a function of its attractiveness relative to others but its attribute-wise proximity or similarity with other alternatives. The proposed MAGL method is empowered with a modified preference elicitation process that incorporates the way the preferences needed to be processed and aggregated in order to resemble the actual decision-making process of the decision-maker. This is shown in  Fig. 2  in which the upper part exhibits the actual decisions or rank order defined by the decision-maker without using any method and by analyzing the alternatives and attributes simultaneously (rank order “A”). The lower part represents the computed decisions using the preferences defined by the decision-maker independently on each of the attributes, resulting in the rank order of alternatives (rank order “B”). The MAGL method is an attempt to resemble rank order “A” and rank order “B” by modifying the preferences elicitation procedure and the aggregation procedure indicated in the lower part of  Fig. 2 . The next section covers the details of the proposed MAGL method.

The proposed MAGL method is novel in two ways. First, the preferences are elicited from the decision-maker using a bipolar scale and second, the elicited preferences are processed and aggregated using the context or the choice set. The identification of the attributes and alternatives is in line with the principles of multi-attribute decision-making as described in  Keeney and Raiffa (1976) . For the purpose of preference elicitation from the decision-maker, the attributes and alternatives are structured hierarchically which means that the evaluation of alternatives depends on the evaluation of attributes but not vice-versa. In the proposed MAGL method, preference elicitation is a two-step process that is as follows.

Step 1 — Assign weights to each attribute:  The decision-maker assigns weights to each attribute using a scale of 1 to 100. The sum of the weights of all attributes must be equal to 100. This score is a measure of the relative importance of attributes. Here, the number of preferences defined by the decision-maker is equal to the number of attributes.

Step 2 — Define preferences for all the alternatives, independently in each attribute:  Firstly, the decision-maker has to define his/her reference point separately and independently for each attribute. The reference point defined by the decision-maker could be his/her minimum requirement ( Koop & Johnson, 2012 ) or aspiration level required, or status quo. For instance,  Hardie, Johnson Eric, and Peter (1993)  used recently purchased brands as the reference point while modeling loss aversion and brand choice. The decision-makers were also found to recruit or evoke a set of counterfactual internal referents to define the reference point. Sometimes, the elements of the choice set may also evoke or provide a set of norms for evaluation. As the alternatives are compared with respect to a reference point or status quo, norm theory can provide an explanation in the decision situations where the decision-maker does not possess a status quo or is not able to exactly pinpoint a reference point. In such cases, their knowledge & experience about the category or stimulus helps in defining counterfactual norms or reference points against which the reality can be compared. Norms can also be tokens (episodes of experience) or types (concepts of lower-order categories) ( Kahneman & Miller, 1986 ). For a detailed discussion and explanation of how normality is recruited and construed, refer to  Kahneman and Miller (1986) .

Once the reference point is defined for an attribute, the decision-maker is required to arrange the attribute levels in the order of preferences and with respect to the defined reference point of that attribute. Thereafter, the decision-maker has to define the preference for these attribute levels using the bipolar scale provided in  Fig. 3 . The preference can be defined using any number between −100 to ＋100 with a reference point fixed at 0 (zero). Further, this scale uses preferences in verbal form for a few of the intermediate points on the scale. It is noted that the verbal form of scale is more reliable than the scale that provides labels for the endpoints only ( Krosnick & Berent, 1990 ). The preferences defined by the decision-maker are in the form of gains and losses, representing the extent of satisfaction and dissatisfaction associated with different attribute levels from the reference point of the same attribute. Since the attribute levels are arranged by the decision maker in the order of preference before defining his/her preference values for the same, the problem of intransitivity in preferences is least likely to occur in MAGL. For example, if the decision-maker prefers A over B and B over C, then it is likely that the decision-maker prefers A over C. If the decision maker prefers C over A, it leads to intransitivity. The intransitivity mostly occurs when there are many alternatives and the decision maker is defining preferences pair-wise. In MAGL, the decision maker is asked to define the preferences pair-wise but with respect to a fixed reference point. Further, the decision maker is asked to first arrange the alternatives in a preferences order across the reference point before assigning a preference value to each attribute level. In the AHP method, the problem of intransitivity is mainly because of pairwise comparisons. An individual can easily compare two items, but when several comparisons are to be made, the decision maker may find difficulty in remaining transitive. When the alternatives are evaluated together using an interval scale as it is done in the MAGL method, the chance of any intransitivity is negligible. The preferences elicited from the decision maker in steps 1 and 2 are processed and aggregated in the third step which is as follows.

Step 3 — Processing and Aggregation of preferences:  In this step, the preferences defined by the decision-maker in steps 1 and 2 are analyzed and aggregated to produce an overall score for each alternative. Then, the overall score is used to rank order the alternatives and prescribe them to the very same decision-maker. When the importance score (in step 1) is elicited from the decision-maker for various attributes, the decision-maker does not use the performance of alternatives on these attributes and therefore the importance score defined by the decision-maker is a general indicator of the decision-maker’s importance for these various attributes and often independent of the performance of alternatives present in the context. According to  Goldstein (1990) , the importance score elicited like this are treated as global importance scores which are independent of the context in place and therefore describe a fixed attitude of the decision-maker towards these attributes. The attribute weights are also the scaling factor to relate a unit of measurement on one attribute over the other attribute. The aggregate value of an alternative can be estimated by using a linear weighted model (Eq.  (1) ) referred to hereafter as MAGL-Linear. This aggregate value can be used to rank order the alternatives. The objective of splitting the multi-attribute problems into multiple independent single-attribute problems and then aggregating the solution of these multiple problems to arrive at an overall solution is to reduce the information load on the decision-maker while solving multi-attribute problems. However, the attributes cannot be treated independently and because of this, it is likely that the solution developed using assumptions of independence of attributes may not resemble the actual or real decision of the very same decision maker. Therefore, in order to keep the preference elicitation process simple and at the same time resembling the solution prescribed by the method with the actual decision, it is necessary to adjust the preference structure defined by the decision maker (in steps 1 and 2) in order to reflect the inherent interdependencies and the interactions among the attributes and the alternatives.

In addition, it also includes the superiority (inferiority) of all the alternatives on various attributes. This research takes a clinical approach to study the decision-making process of informed individuals. It is based upon the assumption that the normative MCDA framework is oversimplified and does not necessarily always fit well with the complex, real-world, and informed decision processes of individuals ( Wallenius et al., 2008 ). Since the MAGL method follows a compositional approach and uses normative MCDA frameworks, its effectiveness in modeling the choice behavior of the decision maker is tested against the AHP method using two multi-attribute problems. A hypothetical mobile selection problem was formulated with four attributes and four alternatives. This served as a pilot study ( Fig. 4 ). The results of the pilot study were in favor of the MAGL and therefore another decision problem was systematically formulated with four attributes and twelve laptop alternatives ( Fig. 5 ). A list of 20 important attributes of laptops was circulated among a random sample of 40 post-graduate level students in the form of a questionnaire. The students were asked to rank order the attributes in the order of their importance. Various price range (interval of 5000 Indian Rupees) of the laptop was also provided in the questionnaire and the students were asked to provide their preferred price range for buying a laptop. The rank order was aggregated across all individuals to arrive at the top four important attributes. While the price attribute is one of the important attributes, it was intentionally discarded from the top attributes. This is because the price is a function of other attributes of a laptop which may violate an important assumption that the attributes should be mutually independent of each other ( Keeney & Raiffa, 1976 ). Thereafter, a list of all possible laptop configurations available on  www.flipkart.com  (a popular e-commerce website in India) was prepared in the most preferred price range. The available configurations were further filtered by the top four brands in India as per the latest IDC report (International Data Corporation – a premier global market intelligence firm) on the Indian laptop market. All unique attribute levels in the chosen four attributes were used to design a laptop selection problem using the orthogonal design principle in order to avoid dependency among attributes. This resulted in a laptop selection problem with four attributes and twelve alternatives. The rest of the details are as follows.

The pilot study was personally administered among 51 individuals (37 post graduate level students, 07 professors, and 07 working professionals). The respondents were conveniently sampled within the university where the author was working as a researcher. The respondents were approximately in the age group of 20–60 years. The second study was personally administered among 105 postgraduate-level students of the same university who were enrolled in a course on decision-making. It was ensured that all the participants of both studies are users of mobile & laptop devices. In addition, it was also ensured that the participants were directly involved in the choice of their devices and therefore familiar with important attributes of both devices. All the respondents of the second study were post-graduate level students in the age group of 20–30 years. In this experiment, informed consent was obtained from all participants. No personal information was collected about the respondents including their gender.

In both studies, the response was collected using paper and pen. A questionnaire was prepared and separately circulated in person to all the participants of the pilot study. In the case of the second study, the questionnaire was prepared and administered during a session of the course on decision-making. In the case of the pilot study, the participants were given one-to-one training on how to use AHP and the MAGL method in solving mobile selection decision problem. In the case of the second study (laptop selection), the responses were collected during a session of the course on decision-making and after a brief session on how to use AHP and the MAGL methods for solving a multi-criteria decision problem like the laptop selection problem. In both studies, the questionnaire contained all the necessary steps for preference elicitation of the AHP method and the MAGL method.

These two multi-attribute problems were strategically chosen because most of the respondents from the target sample regularly use a mobile phone and a laptop. In the case of AHP, a total of 30 decision points on 30 pairwise comparisons were collected from each individual for the mobile selection problem. Subsequently, in the same questionnaire, each individual is required to define his/her preferences using the MAGL method. Firstly, each individual is required to define the weightage of each attribute in terms of percentage (0–100) with the condition that the sum of weights of all four attributes should total 100 (step 1 of the MAGL method). Thereafter, each individual is required to define his/her reference point (minimum expectations or status quo) for each attribute and preference judgment for each alternative with respect to the reference point in each attribute, independently and separately (step 2 of the MAGL method). In comparison to the AHP method, the MAGL method needs only 20 decision points (33% lesser in comparison to 30 decisions collected using the AHP method). Thereafter, each individual is required to define the rank order of all the alternatives (mobile phones) using all four attributes simultaneously and without using any MCDM method. In this paper, this rank order is referred to as the direct rank order of alternatives.

The direct rank orders were collected strategically after collecting preferences using the AHP and the MAGL method in order to avoid outcome bias of the decision-makers in defining their preferences while using the AHP and the MAGL methods. In addition, the preference collection exercise using the AHP and the MAGL methods familiarizes the decision makers with the problem in context i.e., the attributes and the alternatives before the decision maker is asked to provide the direct rank order of alternatives without using any method. Therefore, while defining the direct order of alternatives, the decision maker is dealing with a truly informed decision situation where the decision maker is fully informed about the alternatives, attributes, and consequences. If the decision maker is asked to provide a rank order of alternatives without giving adequate time and attention, it is possible that the direct rank order provided by the decision maker may bring some randomness rather than the decision-maker’s true or actual rank order. Further, if the decision-maker is asked to define the preferences using AHP and MAGL methods after he/she defines the direct rank order, the decision-maker might define his/her preferences in order to match the outcome of such methods with their direct rank orders by intentionally increasing or decreasing preferences.

The questionnaire of the second study contained 25 pair-wise comparisons on various attributes and alternatives of the laptop selection problem and used a 1–9 ratio scale of the AHP method. Thereafter, the questionnaire contained the steps of the MAGL method where a respondent is required to define the weights of all four attributes using percentage (0–100) with the condition that the sum of the weight of all four attributes should total 100 (step 1 of MAGL method). To operationalize step 2 of the MAGL method, an individual is required to define a reference point (minimum expected or status quo) for each attribute and arrange the alternatives across the reference point (above and below reference point). After this, an individual is required to define a preference for alternatives in each attribute with respect to the reference point defined by the very same individual in that attribute but using the bipolar scale of −100 to ＋100. While using the MAGL method, a total of 18 decision points (28% less in comparison to 25 decision points of the AHP method) were collected from each individual for the laptop selection problem. Subsequently, each individual is also required to define a direct rank order of the alternative laptop configurations defined on all four attributes simultaneously and without using any MCDM method. The next section discusses the results of the study.

The rank order of alternatives prescribed by the AHP and MAGL methods for a respondent is compared with the direct rank order provided by the same respondent. Five measures were used to assess the performance of both methods. Three measures (Hit1, Hit2, and Hit3) for predictive validity and two (Hit123 and Kendall’s Tau) for convergent validity. Hit1 is the percentage of individuals for whom the topmost rank (Rank 1) alternative prescribed by the method to an individual is the same as the topmost rank (Rank 1) alternative defined directly by the very same individual. Similarly, Hit2 and Hit 3 are for the second (Rank 2) and the third (Rank 3) rank alternatives. Hit123 is the percentage of individuals for whom the top three rank alternatives (Rank 1, Rank 2, and Rank 3) prescribed by the method to an individual are the same as the top three rank alternatives (Rank 1, Rank 2, and Rank 3) defined directly by the very same individual. Kendall’s Tau is the measure of association between the rank orders of all the alternatives defined directly by an individual and the rank order of alternatives prescribed to the same individual by the MCDM method. It is found that the MAGL (both linear as well as non-linear version) method outperformed the AHP method across all the measures of predictive validity and convergent validity. The prescribed rank order of the MAGL method closely resembled the direct rank order in comparison to the AHP method ( Tables 1 and 2 ). It is interesting to note here that the MAGL method outperformed the AHP method even though the number of preferences collected from each individual in the case of the MAGL method were lesser (complexity in the order of n) than the AHP method (complexity in the order of n 2 ).

The success of the MAGL (linear) method is attributed to its novel preference elicitation procedure. When the preferences are collected using a bipolar scale and in the form of gains and losses, it represents the natural judgment process of an individual. Individuals often judge the utility of an alternative on a specific attribute either using the status quo or with respect to his/her minimum expectations. When the preferences in the form of gains and losses are aggregated using the traditional linear model as it is done in the MAGL (linear) method, the prescribed rank order of alternatives resembles more closely with the actual rank order of alternatives in comparison to the prescriptions of the vanilla AHP method ( Tables 1 and 2 ). The traditional linear model adapts a compensatory approach where the negative aspect of an alternative on one attribute can be equally compensated by the positive aspect of the same alternative on any other attribute. The rate of compensation is based on the attribute weights. Therefore, when the preferences in the form of the gains and losses are aggregated using the traditional linear model, the predictive validity and the convergent validity of the MAGL (Linear) method significantly improve in comparison to the AHP method ( Tables 1 and 2 ).

The success of MAGL (Nonlinear) method is firstly attributed to a better preference elicitation procedure using the bipolar scale (as it is done in the MAGL: Linear method) and secondly to its non-compensatory context-dependent aggregation procedure. When the preferences used in the MAGL (Linear) method are aggregated using a non-compensatory and context-dependent strategy as it is done in the MAGL (Nonlinear) method, the predictive validity of the MAGL method improves further. In  Tables 1 and 2 , Hit1, Hit2, Hit3, and Hit123 improves in the case of the MAGL (Nonlinear) method. However, if we consider all the alternatives (four in the case of the mobile selection problem and 12 in the case of the laptop selection problem), the MAGL (Linear) method performs better than the MAGL (Nonlinear) method followed by the AHP method. This is because, in the MAGL (Linear) method, the attribute weight solely determines the rate of compensation between different attributes. But in the case of the MAGL (Nonlinear) method, the spread of the alternatives from each other and from the reference point within an attribute and across the attributes determines the rate of compensation, and the focus shifts to preferred alternatives rather than all the alternatives. MAGL (Linear) gives better prescriptions in case of the rank order of all the alternatives while MAGL (Nonlinear) gives better prescriptions in the case top few alternatives. Therefore, MAGL (Linear) is more suited for predicting the decision-making processes of individuals while MAGL (Nonlinear) suits more for predicting choice processes.

The percentage of individuals preferring any particular alternative (from the list of 12 alternatives) on the basis of the direct ranking, the AHP method, the MAGL (Linear), and the MAGL (Nonlinear) method are provided in the left part of  Fig. 6 . The right part of  Fig. 6  provides the absolute difference in choice share between direct rankings and rankings obtained using three methods. The mean absolute error of MAGL (Nonlinear) in predicting the preferred choice of each individual in the laptop selection problem is found to be minimum in comparison to the other two methods.

The analytical model proposed in the MAGL method is consistent with the findings of academicians ( Anderson Eugene & Sullivan, 1993; Oliva, Oliver, & Bearden, 1995 ) and practitioners ( Coyne, 1989 ) that the overall satisfaction function is not linear or symmetric. The results of the two studies confirm that individuals assess the given alternatives with respect to their reference point. It can also be inferred here that the individual follows non-compensatory strategies to aggregate the performance of alternatives on different attributes. Therefore, the integration of attribute-wise evaluation of alternatives into the overall impression follows the non-compensatory and context-dependent procedure and thus overweighs the negative evaluation (the losses) of alternatives on any attribute. Further, in line with  Drolet, Simonson, and Tversky (2000)  and  Hardie et al. (1993) , MAGL is able to incorporate relative (reference-dependent), absolute (global), and context-dependent elements of consumer choice. The success of the MAGL method is attributed to better preference elicitation and aggregation procedure based upon the principles of prospect theory, norm theory, complexity theory, and context-dependent choice theories. The MAGL method is the first of its kind to collect preferences from the decision-maker using a bipolar scale. The preferences are collected in the form of gains and losses from a fixed reference point in each attribute which is consistent with the proposition of prospect theory that the carriers of value for alternatives are gains and losses with respect to a reference point. According to norm theory, individuals prepare norms for the alternatives either by referring to their memory or using the available alternatives or context in place. Once the preferences are collected using a common scale, the aggregation procedure of the MAGL method analyzes the proximity of each alternative in the multi-attribute space. This is done by constructing superiority and inferiority matrices which compute the distance of alternatives from each other, independently in gain and loss spaces (Eqs.  (3) – (6) ). The MAGL method assumes a rugged landscape (K

                       N − 1) and the unit contribution of an attribute on the overall evaluation of an alternative is assumed to be depending upon the performance of the alternative on other attributes (Eqs.  (7) – (8) ). Further, using the principles of context-dependent choice theories, the attractiveness of an alternative depends upon the attractiveness of all the other alternatives in the multi-attribute space (Eqs.  (9) – (10) ). In the end, the valuation of an alternative is asymmetric above and below the reference point which means that the positive evaluations do not necessarily compensate for negative evaluations at the same rate. Individuals are often found to underweight gains and overweight losses (loss aversion phenomena of prospect theory) and therefore the superiority values and the inferiority values accordingly are used to underweight and overweight the weighted preferences of alternatives on various attributes (Eq.  (11) ). The power of MAGL in predicting consumer choice processes has important practical implications.

For example, when designing new products, marketing managers not only have to consider the features of the product but its likely position in the choice set available in the market. The MAGL and its context-dependent aggregation model can be used by marketing managers to predict the choice behavior of consumers when the choice set is altered by adding or deleting alternatives, thus offering a different combination of the same attribute levels on which the consumers have already defined their preferences. This is further illustrated using the following application.

Suppose the preferences defined by one of the consumers on unique attribute levels on the laptop choice set ( Fig. 5 ) are given in  Fig. 7 . The rank order computed using the MAGL for this particular consumer is given in the last column of  Fig. 8 . This consumer is likely to choose alternative 3 from the given choice set. The brand attribute is the least important for this consumer. Similarly, the MAGL rank order for various consumers can be computed after collecting preferences from them for the same attribute levels. The preferred choice (rank 1) of various consumers can be used to compute the choice share of alternatives of the choice set (as computed in  Fig. 6 ).

A marketing manager can experiment by adding or deleting or modifying alternatives having a different combination of the same attribute levels and their likely impact on the choice share of alternatives. For example, suppose the marketing manager adds one more product (alternative 13) to the choice set which is a combination of existing attribute levels. For the same consumer ( Fig. 7 ), the updated MAGL rank order is computed ( Fig. 9 ). This particular consumer-preferred alternative 3 from the choice set of 12 alternatives but is likely to prefer alternative 13 from the choice set of 13 alternatives. Similarly, the preferences defined by various other consumers can be used to derive a rank order of 13 alternatives independently for each consumer. The preferred choice (rank 1) of all the consumers can be used to predict the choice share of 13 alternatives ( Fig. 10 ). According to the predictions shown in  Fig. 10 , the total choice share of the Dell brand with a portfolio of three products in a market of twelve different products is 39%. With the introduction of one additional Dell product, the total choice share of Dell offering four different products is expected to grow to 56% (up by 30%).

The marketing manager can also experiment by revising the product offering in the market. For example, rather than offering three different Dell products in the market ( Fig. 5 ), the marketing manager can experiment with the product portfolio in the given competitive market condition and can arrive at a decision like offering just two Dell products ( Fig. 11 ). Initially, the combined choice share of three Dell products ( Fig. 6 ) was 39% but after modifying the product portfolio to just two Dell products, the combined choice share of Dell products is likely to increase to 50% (up by 28%).

By collecting true preferences from the consumers on some of the unique attribute levels of various products available in the market, marketing managers can use the MAGL method to arrive at decisions like whether the attractiveness (market share) of a product line can be increased by introducing more product(s) (dominated or non-dominated) having a different combination of same attribute levels. Marketing managers can also simulate the market share of products by adding or deleting various products or modifying the attributes (using only the attribute levels on which the preferences are already collected from the sample consumer population) of existing products in the given context. Such predictions are very useful in predicting consumer choices as well as in the design of product positioning, promotions, communications, and competitive strategies. Prescriptions of MAGL can also be used by managers facing the dilemma that which attribute levels maximize customer satisfaction.

In this paper, a new method named Multi-Attribute Gain Loss (MAGL) is proposed to model and predict the choice behavior of consumers. Since the MAGL method follows a compositional approach and uses normative MCDA frameworks, its effectiveness in modeling the choice behavior of consumers is tested against Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP) using two multi-attribute problems. The prescribed rank order of the MAGL method closely resembled the direct rank order in comparison to the AHP method even though the number of preferences collected from each individual in the case of the MAGL method was lesser (complexity in the order of n) than the AHP method (complexity in the order of n2). The higher predictive power of the MAGL method in comparison to the AHP method strengthens the argument that the alternative valuation function is not linear and not symmetric across the reference point. The results of both studies conclude that the performance of an alternative on different attributes cannot compensate for each other equally, added with asymmetry in value function across reference points (losses loom larger than the gains of equal magnitude). The overall impression of an alternative is a context-dependent, non-linear, non-compensatory, and reference-point-dependent function of the performance of alternatives on different attributes. The intermediate computations of the MAGL method can be used by the marketing/product managers to analyze the superiority (dominance) or inferiority (subservience) of various attribute levels in the selection or rejection of an alternative by the consumers. A decision support system can be designed and implemented for marketing/product managers where market share for a similar consumer population can be simulated by adding or removing products or modifying the attribute levels in the given context or choice set.
