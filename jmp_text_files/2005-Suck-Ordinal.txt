In research to find out about people's opinions, attitudes, and even achievements the use of ranking scales is ubiquitous. For example, in an investigation concerning the acceptance of atomic energy a subject has to answer questions like: ‘The government should support the foundation of additional nuclear reactors’ and is presented with a five point scale ranging from 1 to 5 or from ‘fully disagree’ to ‘fully agree’. Questionnaires or tests of this kind can consist of a few up to several dozens of questions.

The categories can be numbers, or can be verbally described. Their number, 5 in the above example, is usually fixed throughout the questionnaire. We are concerned with the case where they form a linear order.

In the present paper, we will deal with data of this kind and try to devise a scheme analogous to that used for paired comparison data in random utility theory. Such an approach seems worthwhile because there are a number of parallels between both methods of data collection. Moreover, some of the traditional methods of data analysis of paired comparison data have a counterpart for categorical judgements. In particular, Thurstonean scaling has been adapted to categorical judgements (cf.  Torgerson, 1958, pp. 205–246 ). In this connection the categorical judgement method is but an abbreviation of paired comparison methods of data collection. Thus, the idea to exploit modern results from random utility theory, in particular the consideration of the linear ordering polytope seems worth pursuing. The more so as there is another tradition of evaluating questionnaires and data like the one mentioned above for which the theoretical foundation is rather shaky, and an understanding of what is going on is still an outstanding problem. The different modes of analyzing these data are discussed in later sections.

We can construct representations and suggest a new approach to the analysis of correlations which has some features in common with the usual factor analytic evaluation of questionnaire data, but is better justified by the random utility kind of thinking.

Two aims of the evaluation of such data can be distinguished:

Values : The point is to find scale values for the items in  A , or the categories in  C , or the subjects.

Correlations : Find out how the items of  A  are interrelated.

Correspondingly, the established methods of data analysis utilize different techniques:

In the values mode : Mean values over the subjects.

In the correlations mode : The item-by-item correlation matrix is calculated and subjected to a factor analysis.

Combined : The factor analysis yields so-called ‘scales’ or ‘subscales’. The values of these scales for the subjects are interpreted along with factor loadings, etc. Subscales are often interpreted as dimensions of the construct under scrutiny.

Despite the ubiquity of these techniques there is almost no attempt made to justify these procedures from a measurement theoretic point of view.

The modern random utility theory has its origin in the Thurstone scaling procedure. This method is a bit more sophisticated than the evaluation techniques mentioned in the previous section. However, it is restricted to the ‘values’ mode. We summarize it in the version which has become known as categorical judgement.

The literature in the 1950s and 1960s gives numerous approaches to evaluate data with this model. However, its theoretical status is still unresolved. In particular, it seems by no means easy to clarify the role played by the normality condition stipulated for the random variables.

Thus, an attempt was made to get rid of these assumptions. What other conditions are imposed by the existence of random variables describing the data? This question led to the random utility approach.

This approach is the most general as it does not draw on distributional assumptions. In the case of complete choice data  Block and Marschak (1960)  formulated conditions which are imposed by the assumption of the existence of random variables describing the data. In a famous paper,  Falmagne (1978)  proved these conditions to be sufficient. Recently, a different and short proof was published by  Fiorini (2004)  drawing on the methods of polyhedral combinatorics.

For the case of paired comparisons the situation proved to be even more complicated. If one aims at a minimal description the problem is equivalent to finding the set of facets of the linear ordering polytope. It is not likely that an algorithmically tractable complete linear description can be found because that would yield the famous  
                         P 
                         = 
                         NP 
                       of complexity theory.

In this section, we propose an alternative to the widespread analysis of categorical judgement data, namely the calculation of interitem correlations and the subsequent evaluation of the correlation matrix. In most cases product moment correlations are calculated elevating the ordinal scale type of these data to an interval scale. Sometimes it is argued that a careful naming of the categories of  C  justify this assumption. However, the arguments (if any) mustered to substantiate this claim are not satisfactory from a measurement theoretic point of view.

The calculation of ordinal correlations such as Kendalls  
                         τ
