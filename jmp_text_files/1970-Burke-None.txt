BURKE'  California State College at Hayward, Hayward, California 94542  An axiomatization is provided of the density dimension together with a parallel axiomatization of length, which is completely  equivalent to the classical one. The strategy is to weaken the traditional identity and associativity axioms for length,  respectively, to the solvability and interchange axioms of the present paper-these weaker axioms are true for density as well  as length. From an empiricist view, the crucial axioms are intermediacy (the density of a composition is intermediate between  those of the components) for density, and extension (the length of a composition is no smaller than that of either component)  for length. From a rationalist view, explored in the Appendix, the crucial axioms are intermediacy for density and the  Archimedean property for length. From either view, every axiom of either dimension is also true in the other, save for the  crucial ones.  This paper is a further step in one of my continuing research themes: the examination of measurement scales from physics and  chemistry and their implications for psychological measurement. Previous papers have been primarily concerned with statistical  practice (Burke 1953, 1963). This paper considers some logical foundations of measurement.  An axiomatization of density will be produced; this will be accompanied by a parallel axiomatization of length, equivalent to  the classical one (Campbell, 1920). The strategy which accomplishes the parallelism is to weaken, respectively, the customary  identity and associativity axioms for length to the solvability and interchange axioms of the present paper; these axioms are  true for density as well as length. The crucial axiom for density is that the density of a mixture is intermediate between the  densities of the constituents; for length, that the length of a composite stick is no smaller than that of either component.  These are called, respectively, the "intermediacy" and "extension" axioms. As the axioms are given here, every axiom for  density is true for length except the crucial one, and conversely. Thus the axioms of the two dimensions are very similar and  in one-one correspondence.  This parallel axiomatization has considerable philosophic interest. Traditionally, such dimensions as length, weight, and mass  are called "fundamental magnitudes" and given very special status, presumably because of their amenability to axiomatization. Before my systematic development, let me make two points, the first outlining my position on measurement and the second  characterizing the mathematical methods of the paper. (i) Measurement is concerned with dimensions (collections of physical  objects on which there are order relations and, often, binary compositions) and scales (sets of real numbers with appropriate  order relations and binary compositions). My view is that the dimension is primitive; it is determined by laboratory  experiment directly or by laboratory experiment and theoretical structure (Bergmann and Spence, 1944). A scale is a theory for  a dimension. In the theory, order is preserved and the preservation of order is compatible with the binary composition. This  view is empiricist, in contrast to rationalist viewpoints which take the scale as primary. (ii) My mathematical methods are  exceedingly simple. Most proofs are ordered concatenations of equalities and inequalities. The reason for each step is entered  above the equality or inequality sign as a number indexing the statement that provides the justification. Familiar properties  of classical order and equivalence relations (e.g., a < b G a indicates a contradiction) and of the field of real numbers  (e.g., the absence of divisors of zero) will simply be taken for granted. SOME EMPIRICAL CONSIDERATIONS  For length it is well known that one can select an arbitrary stick as a unit and construct, via the axioms, a fixed set of  standard sticks to which all others are referable. The situation for density is more complex, as the following remarks will  show.  Remark 1. Suppose the dimension contains liquids A and B of least and greatest density, respectively. Then a and b are,  respectively, least and greatest elements of the corresponding scale. Every point of the interval [a, b] has a binary  representation in terms of a and b. In classical physics these points would be approximated empirically as mixtures of A, B,  and previous mixtures of A and B in all orders and combinations. As a result, every other liquid C in S would be equivalent to  some binary combination of A and B. It follows that for any choice of a and b the axioms will be satisfied. Hence choice of a  single unit fails to determine the entire system-two values can be chosen arbitrarily.  Remark 2. If S contains an object of zero density, then the choice of a unit will determine the entire system. There is an  object of zero density, namely a vacuum, but unfortunately it does not mix with liquids in accordance with my axioms.  Remark 3. It makes sense to introduce a vacuum into mixing operations with gases, but definition of S as a set of gases  introduces difficulties in the specification of the mixing operation, *. I have found no way of specifying * with gases which  does not introduce volume considerations explicitly-and introduction of volumes makes density again a derived scale, at  variance with the basic intent of my work.  Remark 4. If classical physics had started with the axiomatic formulation of density instead of the physical solution of the  problem, a linear relation would have been found between density, on the one hand, and the mass-volume ratio, on the other.  Such discovery would have been an empirical law rather than a definition of density as a derived variable. Adjustment of the  density scale to make the empirical relationship a line of unit slope through the origin would have been easy and would have  led to the classical scale for density.  474 BURKE  THE THIRD CASE  Axioms 0-5 define a class of measurement systems having closure; an equivalence relation; a classical order relation; a binary  composition; compatibility between equivalence, order, and the composition; and the interesting Axiom 5. I have  provided parallel axioms for the dimension of length which are logically equivalent to the familiar ones but which emphasize  the similarity of length and density. Differences between the two dimensions are minimal since, in my system, every axiom for  either dimension is true in the other with the exception of a single crucial axiom in each case.  For those who work at systematic models for psychological measurement, a study of Axiom 5 of my paper may be fruitful. Some  axiom which gives strong structure over the binary composition is useful. In the traditional "fundamental magnitudes" the  associative axiom is crucial. But associativity is very restrictive and therefore not likely to hold for many psychological  dimensions. The interchange axiom, Axiom 5, while much less restrictive than the associative axiom, does impose a strong  structure on the dimension.  These results should be interesting for psychologists, since many of us are studying physical measurement for its relevance to  our problems and since there is great current activity in axiomatizing scales for psychological measurement (Zinnes, 1969).  Lacking a handy set of axioms for density, few attempts have been made to use the density scale as a model for psychological  measurement. Moreover, a change on the methodological level from considering density as a derived scale to viewing it as an  axiomatic one may be salutary for the philosophy of psychology.  APPENDIX. A NOTE ON THE ARCHIMEDEAN PROPERTY  In my paper I have adopted an empiricist viewpoint and dealt with the dimensions of length and density in the traditional  terms of classical physics. In psychological measurement the modern rationalist (Suppes and Zinnes, 1963) explores  mathematically possible scales and for him the Archimedean property, to be defined later, is crucial in the length scale and  perhaps for other scales as well. As shown below, the Archimedean property is incompatible with my density axioms; also it is  compatible with, but not derivable from, my length axioms. However, each of my three examples of a length scale possesses the  Archimedean property. But I know of no way to put the Archimedean property to laboratory test for the dimension of length.  Hence this is a case in which the known theories carry excess meaning over the empirical facts. Such a situation is neither  reprehensible nor unusual in science. 1 have preferred in the paper proper to stay with those properties of length which are  empirically verifiable, especially since 1 can imagine a physical universe in which length is not Archimedean.
