INTRODUCTION  In the present paper an exponential waiting time model is stated. The geometric distribution of discrete waiting times and its  continuous analogue, the exponential distribution, have been used for the description of a great diversity of  neurophysiological and psychological latency mechanisms. The distribution of interarrival times between successive neural  pulses (interspike intervals) on a single cochlear fiber appears to be approximated very well by a geometric distribution of  multiples of the period of the input signal (except for times below 1 msec; Green & Luce, 1974, p. 376). The interspike  intervals in the retinal ganglion cells upon stimulation with single quanta of light (Barlow & Levick, 1971) and the  interspike intervals in the spontaneous activity of single spinal interneurones are distributed exponentially (McGill, 1963,  p. 318). The interresponse times during early and middle stages of operant conditioning with certain types of reinforcement  schedules sometimes show the exponential distribution (McGill, 1963, p. 317). There even is evidence that the intervals  between occurences of "deviant behavior" of children may be distributed exponentially (Ehlers, Ehlers, & Makus, 1978).  Series latency mechanisms are another traditional field of psychological applications of the exponential distribution. A brief  light flash triggers a chain of nerve impulses of increasing latencies (McGill, 1963, p. 347). The length and temporal  structure of the chain depend on the previous duration of the dark adaptation and the intensity of the stimulation. The chain  may be considered as a sequence of exponential variates whose  0022-2496/79/010018-21$02.00/0 Copyright 1979 by Academic Press, Inc. The visualization of verbal reproduction and association as random memory  search process dates back at least to W. James (1891, p. 654) and geometric and exponential latency models accordingly have  been used in free recall and verbal association and learning experiments (McGill, 1963, p. 344; Restle & Greeno, 1970; Albert,  1973). Suppose, for instance, that several subjects are instructed to name as many cities in the United States as they could  remember (McGill, 1963, p. 344). The latencies tend to increase with serial position within each series of associations and  there exist typical individual differences as regards the speed of association. The extinction chain of the Bousfield &  Sedgwick mechanism for verbal association and (linear) death or birth processes are simple models for such situations.  The exponential distribution either is used as a "functional" or "descriptive" model in psychological applications. It is a  functional model if an explicit statement of the stochastic process is provided which generates the probability function. The  purpose of functional models is the unique identification of the underlying psychological process. In order for a model to be  considered a functional model it is theoretically necessary to supply supportive empirical evidence for each testable  assumption. There are various stochastic properties orandom variables and stochastic mechanisms that generate the  exponential distribution. The psychologically most suggestive of them shall be briefly mentioned. The exponential distribution  is characterized uniquely by the Markov property or "lack of memory" (Feller, 1966, p. 8; Johnson & Kotz, 1, 1970, p. 208).  Given that the latency has not been terminated before s seconds, the probability that the residual latency exceeds t seconds  is the same as that the latency lasts for more than t seconds from the beginning (that is, R(t + s I s) = R(t), where R(t) =  prob (T > t) = 1 -F(t) is the survivor function). The Markov property of the exponential distribution is equivalent to its  maximum entropy property. Given a non-negative continuous variable with finite expectation, the entropy is a maximum if the  distribution is exponential (Rao, 1968, p. 176). Further characterizations and statistical arguments leading to the  exponential distribution are given by Johnson & Kotz (1, 1970, p. 219).  The exponential distribution can be generated by "serial", "parallel" or "stepwise" sampling (or search) mechanisms. First,  consider a sequential sampling process with independent trials and constant probability of success. The random variable  "number of trials to the first success" has a geometric distribution. The exponential distribution is the limit of the  geometric distribution that is attained as dt, the time interval taken by one selection, approaches zero under the restriction  that p/dt approaches A, where A is a constant (p is the probability of a success and A = pf (1 - p) are the odds of a success;  McGill, 1963, p. 316; Feller, 1966, p. 1). Second, consider n independent superposed (parallel) renewal processes of average  intensities Ai = 1/E(Ti). The distribution of the waiting time to the nearest renewal epoch approaches the exponential  distribution with intensity approximately equal to the sum of the average intensities provide that n is large and the  individual Ai are small (Feller, 1966, p. 355). Third, McGill (1963, p. 322) derived the exponential distribution as the  distribution of the total latency of a "serial search process with a random number of unobservable subresponses" where the  number of subresponses is distributed geometrically and the components are independent identically distributed exponential  variates. The multiplicity of latency mechanisms generating the  20 HARTMANN SCHEIBLECHNER  exponential distribution prevents the unique identification of the underlying stochastic mechanisms solely on the base of the  observed distribution of latencies. In McGill's terms (1963, p. 323) there is an "uncertainty principle governing attempts to  work backward from the shape of a distribution of latencies to the latency mechanism".  2. Two parameter models present considerable  difficulties for statistical estimation. Neyman & Scott (1948) showed, that in the case where the number of parameters  increases as the number of observations is increased the maximum likelihood estimator may lack the asymptotic properties  (consistency, efficiency) characteristic for the simple case where the set of parameters is finite. Consider the sequence of  independent random variables T,; i = 1, 2,..., k, v = l, 2,... . The distribution of Tvi depends on the parameters 0and Ei ,  where the value of Ei is the same independent of v, while the value of 0 changes with v. The parameters Ei are finite in  number and are called structural and the 0's, whose number is infinite, incidental or nuisance parameters (since the model is  symmetric in the parameters, the situation can also be reversed). The problem of obtaining consistent estimates for the  structural parameters in the presence of infinitely many incidental parameters was discussed by Neyman & Scott (1948). A  maximum likelihood method based on the conditional distribution given minimal sufficient statistics for the incidental  parameters was suggested by Rasch (1960, 1961) in connection with some exponential family distributions. Instead of maximizing  the likelihood directly, such that the likelihood function has to be maximized with respect to an increasing number of  incidental parameters, the incidental parameters are eliminated by considering the conditional distribution given (minimal)  sufficient statistics for them. The value of the structural parameter that maximizes the conditional distribution is called  the conditional maximum likelihood estimate. Conditioning upon the (minimal) sufficient statistic for the incidental parameter  means to abondon the information about the structural parameter eventually contained in the density of the conditioning  statistic. The conditional maximum likelihood estimate therefore need not in general be fully efficient. It is so only if the  conditioning statistic is ancillary for the structural parameter (that is, if the density of the conditioning statistic is  independent of the structural parameter; Andersen, 1973, p. 95).  Rasch (1960, 1961) developed the conditional maximum likelihood method and the concept of specific objectivity from a  fundamental investigation of the conceptual status and the properties of measurement rather than from the consideration of the  asymptotic properties of estimators. In short, measurement is viewed as a question of comparison and a comparison is said to  be "specifically objective" if it is independent of the particular instrument used. In mental testing theory this means that  comparisons of individuals should not depend on the particular items included in the test for the psychological dimension in  question. More generally, an empirical proposition is specifically objective if its truth value does only depend on the  objects which enter the relation and is invariant with respect to the auxiliary instruments used to establish the relation. A  two parameter statistical model for measuring is defined to be specifically objective if statistical inference procedures  exist such that if we intend to draw statistical inference about only one of the parameters it does not depend on the other  parameter. Tests of fit of the model must be free of all parameters and depend on observable quantities only. If sufficient  statistics exist for both parameters it follows from the sufficiency that the probability distribution of the observations  given the sufficient statistic for one parameter is independent of that  22 HARTMANN SCHEIBLECHNER  parameter and the conditional distribution given sufficient statistics for both parameters is independent of both parameters.  Consequently the conditional distribution given sufficient statistics can be taken as a specifically objective statistical  model. In Rasch's terms a theorem establishing the independence of a statistical model of nuisance parameters is called a  "separability theorem" and the specific objectivity of a two parameter statistical model is proved by deriving separability  theorems for both parameters. Since the expectation of a specifically objective probabilistic comparison (measurement) is  independent of the nuisance parameters it is unaffected by the distribution of the nuisance parameters in a given population  and by the sample of particular values entering the comparison. In particular, comparisons need not to be based on  "representative" or "randomized" samples. This property has been termed the "samplefreeness" (Birnbaum, 1968; Bock, 1972;  Fischer, 1974) of specifically objective statistical models.  4. THE LATENCY MODEL AS A TOOL FOR SPEED TEST CONSTRUCTION  In test theory the paradoxical situation arises that although proper speed tests are quite common and almost all other tests  are time limited and therefore speeded at least for some subjects, there exist virtually no models for speed test construction  (Lord & Novick, 1968; Fischer, 1974). Rasch's (1960) model for oral reading speed is a noteworthy exception with which the  present model bears a major resemblance. In standard text books on test theory speed is primarily treated as a nuisance factor  which invalidates traditional (correlational) methods of analysis. The present model is an attempt at opening and applying  familiar latency considerations to speed test construction and thus to base the measurement on a rational analysis of the  latency mechanism under investigation.  If the model (2) is applied to speed test construction, the economy of the experimental procedure becomes of major concern.  The model requires the collection of the matrix ((tvi)) of observed latencies, i.e. each latency must be measured separately. The most interesting psychological applications of the linear exponential model are related to series latency mechanisms.  Series latency mechanisms are generated from the linear exponential model if one or more basic parameters are made to depend  on the serial position. If the corresponding column in the model matrix A is a linear function of the serial position we have  generalized versions of linear death or birth processes, for example letting ail = k - i + 1 gives the extinction chain of the  Bousfield & Sedgwick mechanism for verbal association where k is the total number of events in the series and i is the serial  position. If the coefficients ail represent the values of an exogeneous variable at or up to the i-th position we have series  latency mechanisms whose rates depend on some external source of stimulation, e.g. the intensity of the stimulus input. The  coefficients ail may also be functions of the preceding latencies. Then we have series mechanisms where the past course of the  process acts directly upon the present state of the mechanism. These models are related to inhomogeneous or semi-Markov chains  and have been called "dynamic" in the context of the logistic model (Kempf, 197419, 1974b).  8. GENERALIZATIONS OF THE DISTRIBUTIONAL ASSUMPTIONS OF THE MODEL  The simplest generalizations of the exponential model result from distributions which by simple transformations of the time  scale reduce to the exponential distribution. A variable T having a Weibull distribution (Johnson & Kotz, 1, 1970, p. 250) can  be transformed into an exponential variable by the power law transformation T* = (T - Tv) %  LATENCY MECHANISMS 33  c > 0. The parameter To may be interpreted as a minimal response time or fixed delay which can not be lowered for theoretical  and/or experimental reasons. The lower limit To may be assumed to be different for individuals or experimental situations. The  exponent c is the shape parameter, where c = 1 gives the exponential distribution and c < 1 and c > l correspond to monotone  decreasing and monotone increasing hazard functions respectively (Cox & Lewis, 1968, p. 139). If c = 2 the hazard increases  linearly and c in the neighborhood of 3,6 gives a distribution which is very similar in shape to the normal distribution. The  parameters To and c can not be estimated directly by the routines for the exponential model but should be known from other  sources. A reasonable estimator of Tis the smallest observed latency or a value slightly smaller than this. Inserting some  trial values and testing for goodness of fit of the exponential model gives an estimate for c and can be used for testing  hypothesis about the hazard function as well as obtaining hints about eventual shortcomings of the exponential model. As  behavioral examples Rasch (undated) obtained a good fit of Weibull distributions for the intervals of staying at and away fron  the nest of male sticklebacks (with c typically greater than 1 for staying away) and for the intervals of looking at and away  from self of students being confronted with a sound film of their behavior in a stress situation (with c typically less than 1  for looking at self).  Extreme value distributions also may be reduced to the exponential model. If T has a type 1 extreme value distribution, then  T* = exp(T) or exp(-T) has a Weibull distribution (Johnson & Kotz, 1, 1970, p. 221, p. 273, p. 282). Only the list length 10 is  considered further here. The names appeared separately in random order for three seconds on a screen and were read aloud by  the subject. Immediately after the last presentation the subject had to perform a numerical computation task for 40 seconds  and hereafter started the free recall. The reproductions were registered by sound-recording and the interresponse times from  the beginning of a correct recall to the next were measured in millimetres on an oscilloscript. The data quite strongly support the adequacy of a two parameter model containing an individual speed and serial position  parameter (the latencies increase with serial position and, for series of given length, the rank order of the individual  latencies is quite stable over serial positions). The point of specific objectivity may be intuitively transparent for the  present data. The subjects who reproduce longer series also need a shorter time in the average in order to reproduce a given  number of list elements, or more generally, the speed parameter of the series is related to their length. But then comparing  the latencies of different serial positions means that systematically changing populations of subjects are involved. The  difference in length of the series suggests the following basic psychological question: do the subjects who reproduce less  have learned and stored less during acquisition or do they have stored the same number of list elements but fail to retrieve  parts of the information during reproduction? If the serial position  effect of the short series corresponds more closely to the last positions of the long series this favours the hypothesis that  the subjects have retained different amounts of information but do not differ with respect to the retrieval mechanism. If the  correspondence is closer to the beginning, individual differences with respect to retrieval will be more likely.
