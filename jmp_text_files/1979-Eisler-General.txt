Testing the GPDE with empirical data poses practical difficulties. The choice of  measures of central tendency as well as of the scatter cannot be dictated solely by theoreti-  cal considerations. Percentile deviations, for instance, are hard to come by, and the mean  is often an unsuitable measure of central tendency when the distribution is mixed, as in  group data. Therefore geometric means or medians were used as scale values for ratio  scales and arithmetic means or medians for interval scales. As a measure of scatter or  uncertainty SDS have been used. However, a good fit is not sufficient proof that an  empirical law has been adequately operationalized. The aim of the present paper is to give  rigorous mathematical derivations of three operationalizations of the GPDE.  We will first present a deterministic derivation of the GPDE. Thereafter we will  elucidate the GPDE by means of two approaches of which one can, but need not, be  regarded from a stochastic point of view (the functional equation approach), and the last is  wholly stochastic (the error calculus approach). The stochastic point of view implies also  taking the distributions of the stochastic variables and not just the scale values into  consideration. The GPDE and the Fechner Problem  The typical procedure used in the Fechner tradition is to construct a subjective scale  from an experiment in which a Weber function is determined in physical units. The  scale construction is based on the qually-Often-Noticed-Differencepostulate.  Although the idea of constructing a subjective scale from Weber functions is ingenious,  it does seem to us that such a scale in itself is of limited psychological interest. It did,  however, give rise to a number of exciting mathematical problems. Itwas not until  Falmagne (1971) pointed to the possibility of extending the concept of discrimination  index to quite different experimental situations that the importance of the Fechner  scale for theories of sensation became convincing. (Cf. also Falmagne, Note 4, 1974.)  The subjective variables dealt with by the GPDE are not constructed but given by the  observer directly-we have various rating tasks in mind. The aim of the GPDE is not to  scrutinize the validity of the scales obtained, still less to elect any one scale as he sensation scale. Rather we hope that the GPDE will reveal something about an observer  rating behavior.  2.5. The Role of the Physical Variable @  As can be seen, in all three approaches the physical variable @ is used merely to identify  the stimuli and thus only the properties of a nominal scale are being utilized. We want to  remind of the two examples mentioned before-beauty of handwriting and goodness  of taste of vegetables-where there is no physical scale of higher level. The mediating  role played by @, both for stating monotonicity between x and y, and for linking the  increments in x and y by an increment in @, is by no means necessary, though it is given  here partly to sketch the development of the GPDE and partly as a heuristic device. An  instance where it does not seem to be meaningful to nserta physical variable is where  the two subjective variables are subjective ratio and similarity of pairs of stimuli (Eisler,  1963a, 1965). An increment d@ is particularly hard to imagine in this case.  3. SUMMARY AND CONCLUSION  In Section 2 we presented three specifications of the GPDE and developed a number of  mathematical methods in order to answer the following questions:  (1) Given two subjective Weber functions, is there a solution to the functional equation  (5) ?  (2a) If so, does this solution agree with the integral of the GPDE as to form of  function ?  (2b) If so, what discrepancy, if any, is there between the parameter values obtained from  the GPDE and from the functional equation ?  (3) What discrepancy, if any, is there between the GPDE (expected values) and the  corresponding theoretical expression, if a function between the stochastic variables Y and  X is assumed ?  Section 3 with Table 2 attempted to answer these questions. On the one hand, it is  seen that no general answer, independent of the particular Weber functions, can be given.  On the other hand, all discrepancies for empirical data are small, below empirical scatter.  The finding that all three specifications approximate each other closely may be regarded  as theoretical support of the connection found empirically between subjective variables  and their Weber functions (GPDE).  By and large, the GPDE is perhaps not so surprising. Possibly it could be deduced from  certain assumptions regarding neural processes, may be in a way comparable to the  general approach of Lute and Green (1972). The functional equation approach (Eq. (5))  seems to express perceptual and, first and foremost, judgmental adequacy inasmuch as it  specifies invariant properties in subjectsresponses across different scaling tasks. This may  also explain why the GPDE should be, at least approximately, true in many circumstances.
