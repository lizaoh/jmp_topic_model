JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 27, 201-215 (1983)  Distinguishing between Stochastic Models of Heterogeneity and Contagion  STANLEY WASSERMAN  Department of Psychology and Division of Statistics, University of Illinois, Champaign, Illinois 61820  Historically, the Poisson process has been the "benchmark" model for many stochastic processes. When event counts from a  particular process fail to be described adequately by a Poisson distribution, a researcher may turn to generalized Poisson  processes to model the empirical data more accurately. Two common generalizations are the (1) heterogeneous Poisson process,  in which the process rate is a Gamma random variable, and (2) contagious Poisson process, in which the process rate depends  linearly on the current state of the process. Paradoxically, both the heterogeneous and contagious processes yield the same  theoretical distribution for the number of events that occur in a single interval of time. Consequently, distinguishing  between these two using event counts can be difficult. This situation is discussed, first reviewing the models and  distributions, then giving strategies for choosing between them using event count data, and demonstrating these techniques on  episodes of hospitalization for schizophrenia.  1. INTRODUCTION  For many years, the Poisson process has been a "benchmark" model for behavioral processes, a standard to which empirical  longitudinal observations are often compared. The lack-of-fit of a Poisson process may allow the researcher to construct a  more realistic model, incorporating assumptions that generalize the simple postulates of the purely random Poisson process.  Two common generalized Poisson processes are (1) the heterogeneous or compound Poisson process, in which the rate of the  process differs across individuals, and (2) the contagious Poisson process, in which the process rate depends on the current  state of the process. These two models are quite different in their underlying structure, the first allowing individuals to  evolve by distinct Poisson processes with rates independent of their current state, and the second specifying that individual  movement is governed by a single set of transition probabilities but that this movement occurs at system dependent rates. Eaton (1973, 1974)  and Eaton and Whitmore (1977), who first looked at this application, are responsible for our interest in the problem of  generalized Poisson processes. Data from the Maryland Psychiatric Case Registry revealed that the number of times that  individuals diagnosed as schizophrenics are hospitalized varies greatly. Two models that could account for this variation are  (1) a heterogeneity model and (2) a positive reinforcement model of contagion. However, distinguishing between these  alternative explanations is not trivial, and attempts to do so have spawned much statistical research, mentioned here in later  paragraphs. This paper is yet another approach to and review of the problem, the first, however, written with psychological  overtones. We also note in passing that application of the methods described here does not support the earlier beliefs that .a  heterogeneous Poisson process best describes the rates for schizophrenic hospitalizations.  This paper studies an interesting paradox that arises when attempting to distinguish between such models of heterogeneity and  contagion. If we model individual heterogeneity by postulating a gamma mixing distribution for the rate of the process or if  we build our contagious process so that the rate of the process is a positive linear function of the current state, then we  find that X(t), the number of events that occur in the time interval (0, t), is a negative binomial random variable under both  models. Thus, with only event count data giving the number of events that occur in a fixed time, we cannot distinguish between  these two quite different sources of nonrandomness.  There is an extensive psychological literature on theory and application of continuous time Markov chains (of which the  Poisson process is a special case). For background, see the elementary discussion of Wickens (1982, Chap. 10). Such models  have been applied to many areas of mathematical psychology, including learning or stimulus response theory. While it is true  these psychological processes were initially modeled in discrete time, one can effectively argue that these processes truly  evolve in continuous time, and that a discrete time index is an unnecessary approximation. Rumelhart (1970) discusses this  concern. The problem addressed here arises when one wants to generalize a basic Poisson process, by either allowing the rate  of the process to be itself a random variable or letting the current probability of the next event depend on the number of  events that have already occurred. Certainly, such situations could easily arise in many psychological processes changing  continuously over time.  We note that there are two broad classes of contagious processes: infection and addiction. The distinction is based on whether  the entire population of individuals or objects can or cannot affect the probability of the occurrence of an event for a  single individual. In an infectious process, the probability of future events depends on the past history of all the  individuals subject to the "infection;" e.g., in an epidemic, the probability of contracting a contagious disease is certainly  a function of how many people in the population currently have the disease. In contrast, for an addictive or  MODELS OF HETEROGENEITY AND CONTAGION 203  "self-infectious" process, the probability of future events depends only on the past history of the single individual being  modelled. An example of an addictive process is our hospitalizations for schizophrenia. Future hospitalization depends only on  the number and length of past schizophrenic episodes for a specific patient and not on the event histories of all  schizophrenics. Davis, Duncan, and Siverson (1978) make this distinction between addictive and infectious processes in a study  of dynamic models for warfare. The models that we discuss in this paper are all addictive stochastic processes, primarily  because of the mathematical advantages of statistically independent individuals.  The heterogeneous Poisson process was introduced by Greenwood and Yule (1920) and the contagious Poisson by Eggenberger and  Polya (1923). Lundberg (1940) and Feller (1943) were first to note the double nature of the negative binomial distribution  arising from these two processes. Arbous and Kerrich (1951), Bates and Neyman (1952), Bates (1955) and Lundberg (1940),  applying these processes to accident statistics, discuss the problem of distinguishing between the models. Coleman (1965)  introduces these models to sociologists and emphasizes the need for methods to distinguish between alternative causes of  nonrandomness. The sociological history of these models include Spilerman (1970, 1971) and Ritterband and Silberstein (1973),  who use the models to study racial disturbances and group disorders. As noted earlier, Eaton (1974) and Taibleson (1974),  study hospitalization of schizophrenics, and Rockhold (1978), episodes of general illness. Taibleson states that statistical  methods cannot "pin down" exactly the cause of the departure of data from the random Poisson process. His argument can, of  course, be made for any statistical procedure used by social scientists to prove causation. We have found that these methods  are useful in practice and do provide a first exploratory step in the discrimination procedure.  In the next section, we present two generalized Poisson processes, a model for heterogeneity and a model for contagious  reinforcement, a linear birth process. We discuss data observational plans, and briefly describe how parameter estimation and  model discrimination strategies depend on the observational plan and the number of time points at which longitudinal  observations are made. We show in Section 3, using results of Cane (1977), that our heterogeneity model is equivalent to a  nonstationary linear birth process, and thus, in Section 4 we apply Keiding's (1974) methods for estimation of parameters in  pure birth processes to the two models. The remainder of the paper describes strategies for distinguishing between the models  primarily using likelihood ratio tests, presents an ideal model incorporating both heterogeneity and reinforcement, which  unfortunately cannot be used for distinguishing because the probability mass function does not exist in a simple form, and  concludes with a brief discussion of modeling episodic data on schizophrenia.  Important statistical antecedents of this research are the papers of Bates and Neyman (1952) and Bates (1955). The bivariate  and marginal distributions of event counts from adjacent time intervals are derived by Bates and Neyman (see also Arbous and  Kerrich, 1951) who choose to use waiting times between events to distinguish between models.
