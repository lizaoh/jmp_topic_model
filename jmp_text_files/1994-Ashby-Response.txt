A Response Time Theory of Separability and Integrality in Speeded Classification 

F. GREGORY ASHBY

A theoretical framework is developed for the speeded classification tests of perceptual separability and integrality, which were popularized by Garner (1974, The pracessing of information and structure, New York: Wiley). The theory generalizes general recognition theory (Ashby & Townsend, 1986, Psychological Review 93, 154-179) by incorporating the assumption that response time decreases with the distance between the perceptual representation of a stimulus and the decision bound. We show that if decisional separability holds and if there are no stimulus context effects, then the filtering and redundancy tasks provide good tests of separability and integrality. However, if the subject responds optimally then redundancy gains are likely, regardless of whether the dimensions are integral or separable. Context effects also may invalidate both tests. Finally, we propose a new test of separability that can be performed on data collected in the filtering task, which is not susceptible to context effects or to changes in the subject’s decision strategy. © 1994 Academic Press, Inc. 

A fundamental problem in perception is to determine how different stimulus components combine and interact in perceptual processing. Although a variety of perceptual interactions have been studied (Ashby & Townsend, 1986; Garner & Morton, 1969}, it is especially popular to characterize perceptual dimensions according to whether they are separable or integral (Attneave, 1950; Garner, 1974; Garner & Felfoldy, 1970; Shepard, 1964). A pair of dimensions are said to be separable if it is easy to attend to one and ignore the other (Shepard, 1964). Consequently, the perception of a value on one dimension is unaffected by variation along the other. Prototypical separable dimensions are hue and shape (Garner, 1974; Handel & Imai, 1972; Imai & Garner, 1965). On the other hand, with integral dimensions, it is extremely difficult, if not impossible, to attend to one dimension and ignore the other (Lockhead, 1966). Thus, the perception of one dimension is affected when the level of the other is varied. Prototypical integral dimensions are brightness and saturation (e.g., Garner & Felfoldy, 1970; Handel & Imai, 1972; Torgerson, 1958).

A variety of methods for testing between separability and integrality have been suggested (see, e.g., Maddox, 1992a), but two of the most popular involve speeded classification tasks in which the subject is told to name the level of one stimulus component. A pair of dimensions are assumed to be separable if response time (RT) is unimproved when the stimulus dimensions are correlated and if no RT interference is found when the dimensions are varied orthogonally (e.g., Garner, 1974). If RT decreases when the dimensions are correlated and an RT interference is found with orthogonal variation, then the dimensions are assumed to be integral. Unfortunately, despite their intuitive appeal, these speeded classification tests are operational and have no rigorous theoretical justification. 

Ashby and Townsend (1986) proposed rigorous theoretical definitions of separability and integrality and they developed several simple methods for testing between the two (see also Ashby, 1988; Kadlec & Townsend, 1992), One limitation of these recent tests, however, is that they are ali stated in terms of response probability and therefore they ignore response time (RT). This article develops an RT theory of separability and integrality. Such a theory is important, first, because it will allow us to examine the validity of the speeded classification tests and, second, because it will unite two large and growing literatures. 

The theory that provided rigorous theoretical definitions of separability, integrality, and other independence related concepts is called general recognition theory (GRT; Ashby & Lee, 1991; Ashby & Maddox, 1993; Ashby & Townsend, 1986). GRT is a generalization of signal detection theory (e.g., Green & Swets, 1967; Peterson, Birdsall, & Fox, 1954; Tanner, 1956) to experimental paradigms in which the stimuli vary on two or more physical dimensions. Like signal detection theory, GRT makes no specific processing time assumptions. Therefore, to create an RT theory of separability we add an assumption called the RT-distance hypothesis. Briefly, it states that on each trial, processing time decreases with the (Euclidean) distance between the perceptual effect and the decision bound. As shown below, the RT-distance hypothesis has widespread empirical validity and is compatible with many currently popular processing theories. 

The development begins, in the second section, with an overview of the speeded classification tests of seprability and integrality. The third section briefly reviews GRT and the definitions of separability and integrality that it suggests. The fourth section introduces the RT-distance hypothesis and considers its empirical validity. In the fifth section the two most popular speeded classification tests of separability are examined critically through analytical investigation and Monte Carlo simulation. Tables 5 and 6 summarize our theoretical findings. Finally, we close with some conclusions and general comments,

Components A and B are said to be separable if the mean RT in filtering condition A is equal to the average of the mean RTs from control conditions A-1 and A-2 and if the mean RT in filtering condition B is equal to the average of the mean RTs from control conditions B-1 and B-2. In this case, we say that an RT invariance has occurred. If the filtering mean RTs are greater than the averages of the control mean RTs, then an interference has occurred and components 4 and 8 are said to be integral. 

The idea is that if the components are separable, the subject should be able to “filter out” the irrelevant component and so categorization time should not depend on whether the level of the irrelevant component varies. If the components are integral, then the subject is unable to attend selectively to one component and so variation in the level of the irrelevant component interferes with categorization. As a test of separability versus integrality, the filtering test makes two assumptions. The first is: 

Assumption 1. A subject is selectively attanding to component A if and only if variation in the level of an irrelevant component has no effect on the perception of A. 

Separability was defined originally as the ability to attend selectively to a stimulus component. Unfortunately, however, there is no accepted definition of selective attention. As a consequence, tests of separability must operationalize the term. As indicated in Assumption 1, the filtering test does this by equating selective attention with the phenomenon in which the perception of one component is unaffected by variation in an irrelevant component. 

If a subject is attending selectively to component A, then it makes sense that variation in the level of component B should not affect the perception of A. However, the reverse implication is not so obviously true. It is not hard to envision circumstances in which a subject is unable to attend selectively to component A but in which variation in the level of B has no effect on the perception of A. This situation causes problems for the logic of the filtering test because by the selective attention definition the components are integral, but by the perceptual invariance definition they are separable. There are two ways to solve this problem. One is to define selective attention rigorously. The disadvantage of this approach is that almost surely it would require new empirical tests of separability. In particular, for reasons just discussed, it might invalidate the filtering test. A second solution is to abandon the selective attention definition of separability and, instead, to define separability as the condition in which the perception of one component is unaffected by variation in the level of the other. This saves the filtering test, since this is the separability definition tested by the filtering conditions. 

Even if we agree on the perceptual invariance definition of separability and we have agreed on an observable measure of perception, Assumption| cannot be tested until we have decided on a method for testing whether the perception of one component changes with variation in the level of another component. In the filtering test, this is done by comparing performance in the filtering condition with performance in the control conditions. Since these conditions use different stimuli, the filtering test is valid only if the following second assumption is also true. 

Assumption 2. The perception of a stimulus does not depend on the number or the identity of the other stimuli in the ensemble (i.¢., there is no effect of context). 

To see that this assumption is critical to the validity of the filtering test, suppose that the time to identify the level of component A in stimulus A,B, is greater in

Context effects are ubiqutious in perception (e.g., Treisman, 1986; Tversky, 1977). One type occurs when the percept depends on the number of stimuli in the ensemble—that is, on the stimulus uncertainty. Uncertainty effects are well documented in the vision literature, where performance often deteriorates as uncertainty increases (¢e.g., Graham, 1989}. They are thought to occur because the subject typically must monitor more neural channels when the size of the stimulus ensemble is increased (Graham, 1989). This theory predicts that if the size of the ensemble were increased in such a way that the subject need not monitor more channels, then no uncertainty effects should occur. Some evidence supports this prediction. For example, detection performance {i.e., accuracy) is reduced by uncertainty about spatial frequency (Davis & Graham, 1981; Davis, Kramer, & Graham, 1983; Graham, Robson, & Nachmias, 1978) but not by uncertainty about contrast (Davis et al., 1983; Thomas, 1983) and there is abundant evidence indicating that separate neural channels exist for different spatial frequencies, but not for different contrasts (e.g, Olzak & Thomas, 1986). Instead, different contrasts of the same spatial frequency pattern are thought to be encoded in the strength of the output of the spatial frequency channel. 

The filtering test is valid only if there are no context effects. For example, if variation in the level of an irrelevant component does not affect the perception of a relevant component, an RT interference might still occur because of context effects. One way to save the filtering test is to argue that in such a case the components should be labelled integral. The problem with this solution is that most other popular tests of separability would conclude that the components are separable. This is because most other tests use data from a single experimental condition, so the context is the same on every trial. For example, in one popular separability test, multidimensional scaling is performed on ratings of judged dissimilarity. If the best fit is provided by the city-block distance metric then the components are assumed to be separable and if the ratings are best described by the Euclidean metric then the components are assumed to be integral (e.g., Attneave, 1950; Maddox, 1992a; Shepard, 1964; Torgerson, 1958). Similarity judgments typically are collected in a single experimental condition and, thus, no assumptions about context are needed with this test. 

Redundancy Test 

In the second separability test, the secondary component provides redundant information on some trials. If RT does not depend on whether the redundant information is present, then the components are said to be separable (Garner, 1974; Garner & Felfoldy, 1970; Lockhead, 1972; Monahan & Lockhead, 1977). More specifically, the test is as follows.

(i) & (ii) Control conditions A-1 and A-2. Same as in the filtering test. 

(iii) Redundancy condition A+. The stimulus ensemble consists of stimuli A,B, and A,B, and the subject’s task is to identify the level of component A. 

(iv) Redundancy condition A—. The stimulus ensemble consists of stimuli A,B, and A,B, and the subject’s task is to identify the level of component A. 

(v) & (vi) Control conditions B-1 and B-2. Same as in the filtering test.

Components A and B are said to be separable if the average of all four control condition mean RTs equals the mean RT in each redundancy condition. If the mean RT from either redundancy condition is less than the average of the four control mean RTs, then a redundancy gain has occurred and components A and B are said to be integral. 

The idea is that, because the subject is told to name the level of component 4, if the components are separable all attention will be focused on A and so redundant information in B will provide the subject with no advantage. If the components are integral, however, some attention is automatically drawn to B and so a redundancy gain should occur. This argument does not seem to require Assumption 1. Instead, it appears to depend on a more straightforward interpretation of selective attention. But if so, then the redundancy and filtering tests seem to be based on different definitions of selective attention. Even if this were true, it would be fruitful to interpret the two tests from within the same theoretical framework. This is one of the goals of the present article. 

Because two stimuli are used in all conditions of the redundancy test, stimulus uncertainty is constant, so uncertainty effects are not a worry. However, the identity of the stimuli used in the various conditions changes, so other kinds of context effects could cause a problem. For example, even if the subject selectively attends to component A, an RT invariance can be expected only if the representation of stimulus A,B, is the same in control condition A-1 (when the other stimulus is A,B,) as in redundancy condition A+ (when the other stimulus is 4,8,). The redundancy test is valid only if there are no such context effects.

This strategy encounters difficulty when the difference between levels 1 and 2 is more salient for one component than for the other (e.g., Garner, 1983). This is because a subject in either redundancy condition A+ or A— could respond correctly by identifying the level of component B, instead of the level of component A. In this case, a redundancy gain would occur even if Assumption | held. Thus, including all four control conditions guards against this possibility. 

Another strategy, even more conservative than using the average of the mean RTs from the four control conditions, is to use the minimum of the two control condition A mean RTs and the two control condition B mean RTs (e.g., Biederman & Checkosky, 1970). This strategy assumes that in the redundancy conditions, the subject will always classify according to the most salient dimension, regardless of the experimenter’s instructions. The mean RTs from the control conditions associated with the most salient dimension should be less than the mean RTs from the control conditions associated with the other dimension and, so, using the minimum of the two control condition A mean RTs and the two control condition B mean RTs should guarantee the appropriate control. 

One might argue that this strategy is too conservative. First, suppose that a discriminability difference exists along the two dimensions. Now if Assumption 1 holds and the subject obeys instructions, then a redundancy loss should occur on the less discriminable dimension and, so, the results are ambiguous. Second, suppose that the differences along each dimension are equally discriminable and that Assumption 1 holds (in this case it does not matter which dimension the subject classifies along). Somewhat surprisingly, the results are again ambiguous. GENERAL RECOGNITION THEORY 

General recognition theory (GRT), upon which Ashby and Townsend (1986) based their definitions of separability and integrality, assumes that the perceptual effect of any single stimulus presentation can be represented as a vector. However, the theory assumes that there is trial-by-trial variability in the perception of every stimulus, so the appropriate perceptual representation of a stimulus is a multivariate probability distribution. For example, consider an experiment with stimuli A,B, A, By, A, B,, and A, B,. Suppose that component A is associated with perceptual dimension x and that component 8 is associated with dimension y.1 GRT assumes that the perceptual effects of each of these stimuli are described by a unique bivariate probability distribution. Call the joint distribution (e., probability density function) of perceptual effects on trials when stimulus A;8; is presented, Fi (% Y). 

Figure 1 illustrates hypothetical contours of equal likelihood for the entire stimulus ensemble. In filtering condition A, for example, GRT assumes that the subject divides the perceptual space into two regions (not necessarily contiguous), as in Fig. 1. Each region is assigned a response. If the densities are continuous, the decision bound is the partition between the two regions. On each trial, the subject determines in which region the perceptual effect is located and then emits the associated response (Ashby & Gott, 1988). 

The dimensions in Fig. 1 are the perceptual analogues of the physical dimensions varied by the experimenter. For example, suppose the experimenter creates the stimulus ensemble by varying two physical parameters. Variation in the first parameter creates physical dimension XY and variation in the second parameter creates physical dimension Y. Suppose that Fig. 1 is the GRT representation of this experiment. Then dimension x in Fig. 1 is the perceived value of physical dimension X and dimension y is the perceived value of physical dimension Y. These GRT dimensions need not be the most psychologically meaningful perceptual dimensions. For example, a strong argument can be made that the most important perceptual dimensions correspond to the eigenvectors of the covariance matrix of the perceptual distribution (Ashby, 1989; Ashby & Lee, 1993). 

In addition to the distribution of joint perceptual effects, the distributions of marginal effects are also of interest. Whereas the joint distribution describes the simultaneous effects of both components, the marginal distributions describe the percepts associated with only one component and ignore the effects of the other. Decisional separability holds if and only if the subject’s decision about the level of one component does not depend on the magnitude of the perceptual effect associated with the other. With two dimensions, a percept on a particular trial can be represented by the point (x, yp). The x value represents the percept of component A and the y value represents the percept of component B. A subject obeying decisional separability will therefore decide that A is at level 2 if x is greater than some criterion value x9, regardless of the value of y. This divides the perceptual plane into two regions, one for which x<xg and one for which x>x,. The decision bound x=x, is the vertical line that separates the two regions. Decisional separability always leads to decision bounds that are parallel to the coordinate axes. An example is shown in Fig. 1. 

Depending on the value of x9, there are many different bounds that satisfy decisional separability. This article focuses on bounds that maximize response accuracy. Therefore, when discussing decisional separability, we refer specifically to the most accurate of all decisionally separable bounds. It is important to realize, however, that in most cases there will exist decision bounds that predict higher response accuracy that any bound that satisfies decisional separability. The most accurate of all decision bounds 1s called the optimal bound. For example, consider control condition A-1. Given a perceptual effect (x, y), a subject responding optimally computes the likelihood ratio L(x, y)=f,(x, y)/fii(x, y) and responds “1” when L(x, y)<1 and “2” when L(x, y)>1 (assuming that each stimulus is presented with equal probability). The set of all (x, y) for which L(x, y)=1 is the optimal decision bound.’ In general, this bound will be nonlinear (Ashby & Gott, 1988).

Ashby (1989) proposed a processing model that clarifies the differences between perceptual separability and perceptual independence. The model, which is illustrated in Fig. 4, contains a processing channel for each stimulus dimension. The output of a channel is a value on a GRT dimension. Perceptual noise is assumed to be generated within each channel. Two kinds of perceptual interactions are illustrated in Fig. 4. The overlapping funnels on the left represent overlapping tuning curves. If channels x and y have overlapping tuning curves, then a stimulus attribute that stimulates channel x will also stimulate channel y, at least if its value is high enough (e.g., its intensity). If this happens then changing the level of stimulus component X will cause a change in the output of channel y, even if stimulus component Y does not change. In such a case perceptual separability is violated. If two channels have nonoverlapping tuning curves then variation in stimulus component X will have no effect on the output of channel y, so perceptual separability is satisfied. In this model, stimulus components X and Y are perceptually separable if and only if they are processed by separate channels with nonoverlapping tuning curves. This is very similar to ideas proposed by Treisman {e.g., Treisman & Gormican, 1988; Treisman & Souther, 1985).

Thus, stimulus components X and Y are perceived independently if and only if they are processed by separate channels with no central lateral interactions.

Perceptual Integrality 

If a perceptual integrality exists, then decisional separability generally will not be optimal, even in the filtering conditions. Even so, as discussed above, it is plausible to expect a naive subject to adopt decisional separability in both the filtering and redundancy tasks, regardless of whether the components are perceptually separable. 

Perceptual integrality is difficult to study because of the many different forms that integrality can take. A particularly simple form was illustrated in Fig. 3. In this case, a perceptual integrality exists because increasing the level of component B tends to increase the perceived value of component A (and vice versa). The integrality is of a simple form because, for example, the 4;B, and A,B, marginal distributions on dimension x have the same variance but different means. Empirically, this seems the most likely form of perceptual integrality, but unfortunately, it is also the most difficult to investigate. Therefore, we will be able to draw strong conclusions only about the simpler mean-shift and varianceshift forms of perceptual integrality. As a consequence, our results about perceptual integrality are incomplete. Nevertheless, they illustrate the type of effects that might be associated with perceptual integrality.

Regardless of whether the subject uses decisional separability or optimal decision bounds, marginal RT invariance is satisfied if the components are perceptually separable and it is violated if they are either mean-shift or variance-shift integral. 

Interpreting results from the filtering and redundancy tests is especially problematic if subjects use optimal decision bounds. For example, this is the only way in which perceptual separability can yield results that are characteristic of perceptual integrality. A number of studies have shown that subjects can learn decisionally separable bounds when the stimuli are constructed from components that are either integral or separable (e.g., Ashby & Gott, 1988; Ashby & Maddox, 1990). However, Ashby and Maddox (1992) showed that subjects also can learn optimal bounds, even when they are quite complex, and regardless of whether the components are integral or separable. 

Several factors, however, might limit the degree to which the Ashby and Maddox (1992) results generalize to the filtering and redundancy tests. First their experiments did not use speeded classification. Subjects were under no time pressure to respond. Second, subjects were highly experienced. Most completed at least 1600 trials in the same categorization task. In the filtering and redundancy tests, decisional separability is optimal only in the control and filtering conditions and only when perceptual separability holds, Of course, a subject might adopt decisional separability, even when it is not optimal, but this possibility probably depends on the subject’s experience and motivation. This hypothesis predicts that experienced and/or motivated subjects should be more likely to show a redundancy gain and RT invariance in the filtering task than naive and/or unmotivated subjects, regardless of the stimulus components. Historically, experience has not been considered a critical issue with regard to the speeded classification tasks and, consequently, there has been no agreement on the proper amount of pre-experimental practice. We suspect that this inconsistency across labs could lead to some seemingly contradictory conclusions about the integrality or separability of a particular pair of stimulus components. 

The results about the filtering and redundancy tests presented in Table5 all assume the absence of context effects. Since there are many different kinds of context effects, it is difficult to draw strong conclusions about how they would affect the Table 5 results. Nevertheless, some inferences are possible. First, there are two general kinds of context effects—those that depend on the changing identity of the stimuli in the ensemble and those that depend only on the changing stimulus uncertainty in the ensemble. The filtering test is susceptible to both kinds of effect since the identity and number of stimuli is different in the filtering and control conditions. In contrast, the redundancy test is susceptible only to context effects that depend on changing stimulus identity since the contro] and redundancy conditions all contain the same number of stimuli. Thus, we might expect context effects to be a greater problem in the filtering test than in the redundancy test.

Second, uncertainty effects, at least, are fairly well understood. As discussed above, they are presumed to occur because the subject must monitor more neural channels when the ensemble size is increased (e.g., Graham, 1989). In GRT, this should cause the perceptual variances to increase with stimulus uncertainty. In other words, if uncertainty effects exist then the perceptual variances should be larger in the filtering condition than in the control conditions. Larger variances have a predictable effect on accuracy, but an unpredictable effect on RT. Specifically, if there is an uncertainty effect, accuracy should be lower in the filtering condition than in the control conditions. However, RT could be either faster or slower. If the variances are increased but the means are unchanged, there will be more percepts near the bound, but also more percepts far from the bound. Thus, there will be more fast RTs and more slow RTs. This implies an increase in the RT variance, but it is impossible to know what will happen to the mean without knowing the exact form of the RT-distance function. 

Third, uncertainty effects are most likely when the stimulus components are perceptually integral and especially when the integrality involves a mean-shift. To see this, compare Figs. 1 and 3. When the components are perceptually separable (as in Fig. 1), the value on perceptual dimension x tends always to be in one of two regions. It is small when the stimulus is A, B, or A, B, and large when the stimulus is A,B, or A,B,. Therefore, in this case, the subject can focus attention on two narrow regions along dimension x. On the other hand, when the components are mean-shift integral (as in Fig. 3), the value on dimension x tends to be in one of four regions. It is smallest when the stimulus is A, B, and largest when the stimulus is A, B,. Thus, in this case the subject must divide attention among four regions on dimension x. 

If uncertainty effects only occur when the stimulus components are perceptually integral, then they will cause a problem for the logic of the filtering test only if they facilitate RT in the filtering condition. This is because the natural RT interference associated with integrality would be cancelled by the RT facilitation due to the context effects. On the other hand, if the uncertainty effects increase RT in the filtering condition, the natural RT interference associated with integrality would be exaggerated. 

Context effects due to changes in the identity of stimuli in the ensemble can be of many different types. One possibility is that changing stimuli causes some new property or feature to emerge or become apparent. For example, consider the stimuli constructed from all possible combinations of a right and left parenthesis [ie., the four stimuli are ((, (), )(, and ))]. In two of these stimuli, there is an emergant property of “parallellness” [i.e., in (( and })] and in one stimulus there is an emergent property of “roundness” [i.e., in ( )]. Thus, in this case performance can be strongly affected by changes in the identity of the stimuli in the ensemble (Pomerantz & Garner, 1973).

SUMMARY AND CONCLUSIONS 

The goal of this article was to develop a theory relating RT to perceptual separability and integrality. In the past, the RT tests of separability have been operational in nature. Earlier tests of these concepts that have had a rigorous theoretical basis examined only response accuracy (Ashby & Townsend, 1986; Ashby, 1988; Kadlec & Townsend, 1992). The critical assumption that allowed us to extend this earlier work into the domain of response time is the RT-distance hypothesis: RT decreases with the distance between the perceptual representation of the stimulus and the decision bound. 

When incorporated into GRT, the RT-distance hypothesis provides theoretical justification for the speeded classification tests of separability versus integrality. This new theory revealed that the validity of these tests depends critically on two assumptions: (1) decisional separability and (2) the absence of context effects. In addition, we hypothesized that the amount of practice a subject ts given should interact strongly with the viability of the decisional separability assumption. Specifically, decisional separability is most likely to occur when subjects are inexperienced. As subjects gain experience in the task, they are likely to switch from decisionally separable bounds to decision bounds that increase accuracy and/or decrease RT. 

Because of this fact, monitoring a subject’s level of experience is vital in the speeded classification tasks. With experienced subjects, redundancy gains cannot be taken as evidence of separability. The filtering test is less susceptible to practice effects, but more susceptible to context effects. Fortunately, marginal RT invariance provides a method of testing for separability that makes no assumptions about context. 

In conclusion, the ideal application of the filtering and redundancy tests has the following four properties. First, the levels of the stimulus components are chosen so that the A, versus A, difference and the B, versus 8, difference are equally discriminable. Second, the experimental conditions include ali four control conditions
