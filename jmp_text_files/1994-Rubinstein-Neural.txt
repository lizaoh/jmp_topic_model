Neural networks related to the process of mental imagery are analysed, It is shown that under appropriate conditions the delta rule algorithm of Widrow and Hoff converges. Special emphasis is given to mental rotations, but the analysis is applicable to a much larger class of transformation groups. It is further shown that associative (Hebbian) networks are capable of projecting transformed patterns into the invariant subspaces of the underlying group. © 1994 Academic Press, Inc. 

1. INTRODUCTION 

The ability of humans to perform mental transformations has been demonstrated in many experiments by Shepard and his colleagues and others (Shepard & Metzler, 1971; Shepard, 1984). A probiem that attracted particular attention is mental rotation. It is possible, however, to perform other imaginary transformations such as scaling, shift, etc. In fact, a thinking process can be interpreted as a particular transformation of a given set of initial concepts and objects. The goal of this paper is to study neural models associated with mental transformations. In particular we use the algebraic structure of the transformation group to analyse the relevant algorithms. 

A computational model for mental transformations has been proposed recently by Goebel (1991). The model is based on the parallel distributed processing (PDP) approach (Rumelhart, Hinton, and McClelland, 1986a, p.61). An initial pattern (object) is represented as an M-dimensional vector ¥. In the next section we give sufficient conditions for the convergence of the DR algorithm for the periodic shift operator, which is mathematically equivalent to a rotation of functions defined over the circle. A general result in the theory of Lie groups says that all one-parameter transformation groups are equivalent to a shift through an appropriate change of coordinates. Thus our result applies to other transformations as well. We conclude Section 2 by discussing the other questions mentioned above. 

In contrast to the idea of mental imagery, Gibson advanced the point of view that only matertal images can participate in a thinking process. His thesis (Gibson, 1974, 1979) is that we extract invariant parameters from real world objects and situations. These invariants characterize the objects even after they (or the observer) have been transformed. Gibson did not specify any particular mechanism by which we can manufacture these invariants. In Section 3 we study associative neural networks related to (1) and its output. It is shown that the equilibrium state of the associative net is determined by the algebraic structure of the group. In particular it is argued that associative networks are capable of decomposing the input into invariant subspaces. Therefore the algebraic approach we adopt here provides a natural tool for computing invariant features. These characteristics are what Shepard calls first-order invariants. Gibson’s high-level invariants might be extracted from them through further processing that we do not consider here.

3. CONNECTION WITH ASSOCIATIVE NETWORKS 

Several psychological theories maintain that organisms are able to extract invariants to characterize relevant objects (Gibson, 1979). Shepard (1984) argued that there should also exist dynamical representations, such as a mechanism to perform mental imagery. We now propose that the two approaches can be related to each other through the formation of asseciative neural networks. In order to obtain invariants, one has to decompose the pattern into invariant subspaces. In the context of transformation groups, the subspaces are related to the group representations. The idea is to feed the output of the network considered in Section 2 into an associative (Hebbian) network. The weights of the new network evolve towards some equilibrium that is shown to correspond to the natural spectral decomposition of the transformation group. To simplify the presentation we consider the particular group SO(2) with sampling at the M=2N-+1 roots of unity. Other compact groups can be analysed similarly.

Our analysis applies to other transformation groups, since the spectrum of the autocorrelation matrix Q is always related to the representation of the group (Rubinstein, 1991). Specifically, every eigenspace of Q is a union of subspaces that are invariant under the operation of the group. The decomposition of an image into subspaces that are invariant under the transformation group is necessary in order to compute invariants. Therefore the model we propose here provides a first step towards the computation of invariant features. 

4. DISCUSSION 

The convergence of a connectionist network that performs mental transformations has been analysed. We have given sufficient conditions for the convergence of the delta rule algorithm suggested by Goebel (1991). In particular, we found that the connection matrix is intimately related to the irreducible representations of the group. The convergence of the algorithm and the algebraic structure of the network is not sensitive to the sampling precedure. 

We have further considered the possibility of using associative PDP algorithms to obtain invariants. We assumed that the network is presented with a sequence of transformations of a given image. The analysis of Section2 indicates that this sequence could be produced by another network. When the learning rate is slow, the associative net converges in general to one of the leading eigenvectors of the autocorrelation matrix of the input. Since each eigenspace of that matrix is a union of subspaces that are invariant under the group operation (Rubinstein, 1991), an associative network provides a decomposition of patterns that is convenient (and in fact necessary) for the extraction of invariant features. This extraction is a higher level process that we are now investigating. 

The invariants mentioned in Section 3 are global invariants. For example, in the case of planar rotation these invariants are provided by the absolute value of the angular Fourier transform of the image (Gourd, Gauthier, and Younes, 1989; Rubinstein et al, 1991). Therefore these features are unchanged regardless of the magnitude of the deformation. Such invariants are considered to be more efficient for the recognition task than the dynamical approach, which requires transforming the image until a satisfactory match is achieved with a given pattern. Note, however, that if the deformations are small, the dynamical algorithm can be quite fast, particularly since it has (cf. {1)) a natural parallel implementation. Therefore mental transformation can be preferred in such environments over global invariants.
