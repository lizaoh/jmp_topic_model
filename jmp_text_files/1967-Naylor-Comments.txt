In a recent article Wickelgren and Becker (1965) report a study evaluating the choice preference of individuals for cues  having di - erent accuracies, where the accuracy of a cue was defined in terms of the proportion of times that cue was  "correct" in predicting between two alternatives. They point out that in situations where only two predicted states are open  to a cue (e g , yes or no), those cues having p values of less than 0.5 can provide just as much information to the decision  maker as can cues with p values greater than 0.5. Thus a cue which is correct on only 20 % of the trials could be just as e  ective an aid to prediction as a cue which had a p of 0 8, even though the two cues would di7er considerably in their accuracy.  However, Wickelgren and Becker also state that "in real life a cue that predicts accurately less than 50 % is generally of  little help in choosing the correct course of action except in those very rare instances when only two courses are open."  (1965, p. 188.) Such a statement causes one intimate with regression analysis to wince just a bit, since in the majority of  those prediction problems for which a least-squares prediction equation has been employed the accuracy of a predictor (using  their definition) is often considerably below 0 50. Indeed, to find a predictor variable which was perfectly accurate half of  the time would probably cause most investigators to become ecstatic with joy.  The difficulty in the Wickelgren and Becker statement would seem to lie in their definition of the term accuracy and in their  corresponding failure to clearly differentiate between the accuracy of a cue and the validity (predictability) of that same  cue. For example, if one allows for the possibility of more than two alternative states of nature, it becomes necessary to  seek some other definition of cue accuracy than simply "percentage correct responses." This is particularly true if one admits  to the likelihood  154  ACCURACY AND VALIDITY OF CUE VARIABLES 155  that there are not just two relevant states of accuracy (accurate vs. inaccurate) but that instead there are varying degrees  of accurateness which depend upon how close the predicted alternative is to the correct alternative on any particular trial. The answer to this question must of necessity depend  upon the purpose or objective of the individual. For example, if the individual must use the cue information as a basis for  making his own predictions or choices, then the validity of a cue would seem most relevant, since he is only concerned with  how well he can predict using the cue information and is not really concerned with the actual cue accuracy.  On the other hand, however, suppose the task was one of selecting a single cue variable which could most closely estimate the  criterion directly without the interposition of an intervening equation. Now cue accuracy would probably be most critical.  Thus as long as the individual reserves the role of prediction making for himself, his best strategy would be to surround  himself with cues of high validity which could then be utilized to make very accurate predictions. If, on the other hand, he  must delegate the role of prediction to the cue, (as is often the case when cues are people) then he should select cues having  maximum accuracy, since the payoff associated with a set of predictions is most apt to be a function or error magnitude.  In summary, accuracy and validity of cues are separate but interrelated concepts, such that selecting the most accurate cue  does not assure that one has the most valid cue, and vice versa. Even in multiple-alternative-choice situations cues with very  low accuracy can be exceedingly useful in a predictive sense.  Finally, numerous questions concerning the ability of individuals to make inferences concerning the validity of a cue (both  positive and negative), the accuracy of a cue, and possible tradeoffs between accuracy and validity need examination  empirically. Although a number of recent studies have dealt with the ability of individuals to assess positive cue-criterion  relationships using a correlational model (e.g., see Hammond and Summers, 1965; Schenck and Naylor, 1965; and Summers, 1962),  none have considered the accuracy of the cue and none have examined negative correlations or the effect of varying cue  variances. Hopefully, data on the inferential process as a function of these variables will soon be forthcoming.  REFERENCES  HAMMOND, R. R. AND SUMMERS, D. A.
