It determines analytically the probability of choosing the first alternative (of a given pair) when  using an additive utility function with uniformly random weights. Two uniform random weight models are examined. In one, the  weights (i.e., scalar coefficients) of the additive utility function are each uniformly distributed on [0, 1 ] prior to being  resealed to sum to one. In the other, the weight vector iv = (d,, , ..., l~n) is presumed to be uniformly distributed on the  simplex defined by Y_ wi = 1 and w 0 for all i. Differences between these two types of uniform random weight models are  discussed. Since the second (or vector) model implies maximum ignorance (in the entropy sense), only its distributional  aspects are analyzed in detail. The middle of the paper explores analytically the probability that a given vector x is  preferred to a given vector y, when using an additive utility model with uniformly random vector weights. Closed form  solutions for this probability are derived up to four dimensions using analytic geometry. Algorithmic solutions are offered  for n > 4, using existing computer codes. The analytic results are demonstrated numerically with four dimensions, using data  from a college admission's experiment. Various applications of this particular measure for probabilistic dominance are  examined. Which of these  approaches is best depends on one's circumstances, training, resources, and the importance of the decision. Also, it depends  on how difficult the choice is, i.e., how close the alternatives are in overall attractiveness. Unfortunately, the latter may  only be fully known after conducting a complete MAU analysis. However, some indication of the extent to which x might be  preferred to y (or vice-versa) can be obtained prior to assessing utilities and weights.  To do so, we propose to measure the probability that a given alternative x is preferred to a given alternative y when using an  additive, linear utility function whose weights are uniformly random. This probability measure constitutes a type of  probabilistic dominance index scaled on [0, 1 ]. The closer it is to .5, the more difficult the choice in the sense of  choosing the truly best alternative. The nearer it is to one or zero, the less carefully utilities and weights need to be  estimated (other things equal).  Of course, the degree of difficulty of a choice will depend not only on the probability of being wrong, but also on the  associated opportunity cost, and possibly other factors such as time pressure, resource availability, etc. Hence, our paper  explores only one aspect of choice difficulty, namely how close the alternatives are in overall attractiveness when assessed  over all positive weight combinations.  One appealing feature of our particular dominance measure is its comparability across pairs of choice alternatives, and its  ease of interpretation in isolation. Moreover, the measure can serve a variety of other purposes as well. For group decisions  it may indicate how likely there will be disagreement, or to what extent consumers might prefer one product over another. As  shown in Section 4, the measure may also be used to develop a lower benchmark to assess whether a constructed MAU function  predicts the person's basic preferences better than chance. Related to this is its use in experimental designs, which is also  addressed in Section 4.  Finally, our index can be used as a sensitivity measure in MAU analysis. Instead of making the weights random and fix the  alternatives, one could test how sensitive the choice is to uncertainty in the attribute levels, for a given set of weights  (see Russo & Dosher, 1983). Because of symmetry, this is the same problem mathematically as that of random weights (assuming  an additive utility function). However, throughout this paper we assume that the alternatives x and y are given, and that the  weights are uniformly random.  The reason for using uniformly randomly weights is that they imply maximum ignorance about the true weight values. The  attribute utility functions are presumed to be known or assumed to be linear. If the latter, they can be scaled without any  assessment, requiring only knowledge about the direction of higher utility. Of course, this simplification would not be  appropriate for non-monotone attributes. Additivity of the multi-attribute utility function is assumed as a first  approximation, and to keep the proposed dominance index relatively simple. Another important application of our dominance index concerns the development of a lower benchmark to assess how well a  particular MAU function captures a decision maker's basic preferences. In our earlier paper (Schoemaker & Waid, 1982) we  examined various methods for constructing MAU functions, such as multiple regression techniques, trade-off analyses as  proposed in Keeney and Raiffa (1976), etc. Specifically, we compared how well linear and non-linear MAU functions constructed  via different methods would predict subjects' binary preferences on a new set of 20 randomly selected choice pairs. For  example, we found that Saaty's (1977) paired comparison method predicted on average 84% of subjects' preferences correctly for  the 20 binary choices in Table 2 (across a sample of 71 subjects). This "hit rate" was as low as 60% for one subject and as  high as 100  for another, with a standard deviation of 10%. Given such statistics, it seems important to assess whether this performance  level is better than chance (for either a single subject or a group).  One way to test this is to calculate the probability that our random weights model would correctly predict at least x out of n  binary choices. For example, how likely is it that say 18 or more of the 20 majority preferences shown in Table 2 would be  predicted by our random weighing model? Using simulation (N= 3000) we found that our random weight model predicted about 16 of the 20 majority preferences correctly  on average, with a standard deviation of 1.6. If the simulation assumes dependence across the choice pairs, the standard  deviation increases to 4.9 (while the mean remains around 16). CONCLUSIONS  This paper examined analytically the probability that a given multi-attribute alternative x would be preferred to a given  multi-attribute alternative y when evaluated with an additive utility function whose weights are uniformly random. Two types  of uniform random weights were discussed, uniform coordinate weights versus uniform vector weights. Using maximum ignorance  and entropy arguments, we favored the random vector method, according to which w is uniformly distributed on the (n -  1)-dimensional simplex defined by Y_%, w; = 1 and w, >, 0 for all i.  First the probability distributions of both w and its random coordinates w; were examined. Next, the probability of preferring  alternative x to y (using random weights) was shown to equal the ratio of the contents of a convex polytope and the above  simplex. Theorems were then developed and proved (see Appendix A) concerning the verices of this polytope, and methods were  described to determine the contents of simplices (see Appendix B). Closed form solutions were derived for n < 4, and implicit  procedural solutions for n > 5. The uniform random weight model was proposed as a sensitivity measure, and illustrated  numerically using actual MAU predictions and preferences.  Another important use of our probabilistic dominance index lies in assessing the performance of an MAU function against a  random benchmark. This seems appealing for at least two reasons. First, it establishes a lower bound for MAU models which,  together with a reasonable upper bound, allows one to position a model more precisely along some scale of performance. Second,  in the absence of upper bounds, it still allows for some evaluation. For example, in the presence of stochastic preference or  complicated decision tasks, optimal behavior may not be easily determined or even defined (e.g., in game theoretic  situations). However, the question whether people or models perform better than random choice is generally answerable and of  interest.  In psychological research on multiple cue judgments, random weight benchmarks are commonly examined. A well-known example is  Dawes and Corrigan's (1974) bootstrapping analysis, showing that equal or random weight linear models typically perform as  well as human decision makers. Their findings emphasized that  188 SCHOEMAKER AND WAID  the success of linear models (especially bootstrapping) reflects both human information processing limitations and  environmental (i.e., task) features. Although lower benchmarks such as random or equal weight models have been used for  prediction tasks, they have not been applied to preference tasks. Our probabilistic dominance index provides one approach to  evaluating preference models against a reasonable lower benchmark.  Given its greater complexity, should our method be preferred to equal weighing? The answer of course entails trade-offs. For  example, EW is insensitive to redistributions of scores across the attributes, when the total remains fixed. Also, EW is  difficult to interpret in isolation. What does it mean to say that the EW index (when scaled from 0 to 1) is 0.55 for a choice  pair? If there are few attributes, this suggests weak dominance; if there are many it suggests strong dominance, since EW  exhibits less variance as n increases. Indeed, even adding or delting nondiscriminating attributes (i.e., those for which d; =  0) will change the EW score. Lastly, EW cannot easily be used to develop statistical benchmarks, for either evaluating results  or designing experiments. However, EW is transitive, a property we deem less critical in a stochastic context.  To us, an important feature of the RW measure is that it is interpretable in isolation and comparable across pairs that may  differ in dimensions, since RW represents a probability measure. Moreover, it satisfies other desirable properties as well. It  is scale invariant in that P[kd - w > 0) = P[d - w > 0] if k is a constant. Also, unlike EW, our measure is sensitive to  redistributions of d; scores within the d vector (keeping Y_ d; constant). For example, consider the standardized alternative  x=(0.6,0.5) which dominates y=(0.5,0.5), since d=(0.1,0). However, if we redistribute the scores of x to x'= (0.7, 0.4), which  does not change the total, this domination vanishes since d'= (0.2, -0.1). And our RW index indeed changes from 1 (strict  dominance) to 3 (probabilistic dominance). Finally, adding or deleting dimensions for which d; = 0 will not change the RW  probability, as proved in Corollary 3 of Appendix B. For example, setting d3 = 0 in Table 1 reduces the threedimensional  formula to that of n = 2. Hence, unlike the normalization approach or equal weighing method, our measure is insensitive to the  inclusion or exclusion of non-discriminating attributes in the choice pair, which seems desirable.  Although our measure satisfies many desirable features, it violates stochastic transitivity and perhaps others. We leave it to  future work to prove that certain features are incompatible with others (e.g., WST and non-linear d; measures), analogous to  impossibility theorems in group choice. As such, the ideal measure may not exist.
