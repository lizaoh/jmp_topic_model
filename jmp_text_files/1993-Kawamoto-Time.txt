Time-Course of Processing in Feed-Forward Connectionist Networks: An Analysis of the Exclusive-Or Problem 

ALAN H, KAWAMOTO 

University of California, Santa Cruz 

Although most’ of the work based on feed-forward connectionist( networks has emphasized accuracy, time-course of processing issues can also be considered by processing the inputs in cascade. In this Monte Carlo simulation study, two functional classes of solutions of the exclusive-or problem that arise in a feed-forward network comprised of two input units, two hidden units, and one ouput unit are described. These two classes of solutions are distinguished by the time-course of activalion of the output unit in response to one particular input: for one class, the activation of the output unit is virtually unchanged over time, but for the other class, the activation initially increases before it subsequently decreases. After examining the basis for this distinction, networks with different architectures are considered to determine the effect of increasing as well as decreasing the degrees of freedom. In the discussion, the implications of a time-course of activation that changes nonmonotonically in feed-forward networks without, as well as with, lateral inhibitory connections for simulating fast errors in chotce-response time experiments (¢.g., noise/compatibility and word naming} are examined, The implications of functionally distinct solutions in simulating individual differences are also considered, — 4) 1993 Academic Press, inc. 

1. INTRODUCTION 

In most of the rescarch using feed-forward connectionist networks, the singic measure Of performance is based on the error, the difference between the desired and actual output. Despite the usefulness of the error measure in simulating a great deal of behavior, this measure cannot adequately address issues related to the timecourse of processing. In fact, when processing in feed-forward networks is carried out as it typically is in a fixed number of steps (the maximal number of connections between an input unit and an output unit), the issue of processing dynamics is moot. 

However, the lack of attention toward time-course issues in simulations using feed-forward networks does not imply an inherent limitation in this class of networks; the time-course of processing in feed-forward networks can be quite naturally simulated as described in the cascade model (McClelland, 1979). Moreover, as McClelland and Rumelhart (1988) have subsequently pointed out, networks in which there is an intermediate layer of units whose outputs are a nonlinear function of their inputs are qualitatively different from the linear networks considered earlier by McClelland (1979). In particular, for linear networks, the activation of an output unit must increase or decrease monotonically over time, whereas for nonlinear networks, an output unit can initially increase and subsequently decrease. (A striking example of this property is the exclusive-or (XOR) problem described by McClelland and Rumelhart (1988, pp. 154-155).) The fact that an output unit can have a non-monotonic time-course of activation has important implications for simulating data from reaction time experiments. That is, for sufficiently low criterion for response, a false alarm would be made despite a correct asymptotic response. In addition to the non-monctonic time-course of activation, the intermediate layer of nonlinear units can also give rise to multiple solutions that are functionally distinguishable. In particular, for some solutions of the XOR problem, the timecourse of activation of the output unit is monotonic for all inputs (Kawamoto & Kitzis, 1991). 

In this simulation study, the XOR problem wiil be considered in detail. After generating different solutions by Monte Carlo simulation, the functional classes of solutions distinguished by their time-course of processing will be described and the basis for the different solutions will be examined. Then, networks with different architectures will be considered to determine the effect of increasing as well as decreasing the degrees of freedom on the number of functional classes of solutions. Finally, implications of a non-monotonic time-course on the latencies and likelihood of errors and correct responses, as well as implications of functionally distinct classes of solutions, will be discussed. 

2. THE XOR PROBLEM 

The XOR function is quite simple; four two-dimensional binary input vectors must be mapped to four one-dimensional binary output vectors such that (0,0) > (0), (0,1) +1), (4,0) (1), and (1, 1) (0). Hereafter, the four inputs, (0, 0), (0, 1), (1,0), and (1,1), will be referred to as inputs “00,” “O1,” “10,” and “11,” respectively, and the two outputs, (0) and (1), will be referred to as outputs “O” and “1,” respectively. The elements in the input and output vectors correspond to the two input units and the one output unit in the connectionist networks shown in Figs. la and 1b. 

For this particular problem, the pair of inputs “00” and “11” that map to output “QO” are not linearly separable from the pair of inputs “01” and “10” that map to output “1.” Thus, an intermediate “hidden” layer of units whose output activation values are a nonlinear function of their inputs is necessary (Minsky & Papert, 1969). 4. MONTE CARLO SIMULATIONS OF THE 2-2-1 NETWORK 

The simulations were carried out using the software provided in McClelland and Rumelhart (1988). Solutions to the XOR problem for 2-2-1 networks were found using 1000 different sets of initial weights. Each initial weight was drawn from a uniform distribution centered around zero. For each different set of initial weights, the identical sequence of input patterns (repeated presentations of the sequence of input patterns “OO,” “O1,” “10,” and “11”) was used in the training, and the connection strengths w, and v, were updated after the presentation of all four inputoutput pairs. Because there were no direct connections from an input unit to an output unit for the 2-2-1 network, the connections strengths u; were simply set to zero and left unchanged. 

The 1000 networks were each trained until a criterion was reached (summed squared error of all four input-output pairs <0.004) or until the training set was repeated 10,000 times. Of the 1000 different networks, the error criterion was met 92.8% of the time. For those networks that reached the criterion, the time-course of activation of the output unit in response to each of the four inputs, “00,” “01,” “10,” and “11,” was determined. Two functional classes of solutions distinguished by their time-course of activation were found. The time-courses for a solution from each of these two classes are shown in Fig. 2. The most striking difference is the time-course of activation of the output unit in response to input “11.” For one class of solutions, the “monotonic” solution, the activation of the output unit is virtually unchanged over time (Fig. 2a), whereas for the other class of solutions, the “non-monotonic” solution, the activation of the output unit initially increases and then subsequently decreases (Fig. 2b). However, the asymptotic activations of the output unit for both classes of solutions are virtually identical.

6.2. Eliminating All Hidden Units—The 3-1 Configural-Cue Network 

Instead of requiring the network to learn higher order features to provide the linear separability between the inputs, the higher order features can be presented as part of the input as in the configurai-cue model of Gluck and Bower (1988). With the higher order feature available in the input, the need for a layer of hidden units is eliminated. 

Because there are only direct connections from the input to the output in the configural-cue network, there is a unique set of weights that minimizes the error. Moreover, the lack of an intermediate layer of inputs that can transform the input non-linearly means that the time-course of activation of the output unit must be monotonic. This is indeed the case; for the 3-1 configural-cue networks, there is only one class of solutions. Furthermore, the activation of the output unit in response to “QQ” and “11” is virtually unchanged over time, whereas the activation of the output unit in response to “O1” and “10” increases monotonically over time. 

6.3. Adding Constraints—The 2-2-2 XOR/AND Network 

Instead of constraining a network by using a network with fewer hidden units and connections, an alternative is to require that another function (eg., OR or AND) be computed in addition to the XOR function. In this case, another output unit as well as connections from each of the hidden units to the new output unit would be needed. With this second output function, the inputs “00” and “11” now have different outputs. Because the activation of the hidden units for the different inputs must reflect this difference in the outputs, it is clear that only the “nonmonotonic” class of solutions (for which the inputs “00” and “11” have opposite hidden unit activations) can satisfy both output constraints. Thus, the time-course of activation of the XOR output unit should be non-monotonic in response to the input “11” for all solutions. Results of Monte Carlo simulations completely support this claim. However, there are still sufficient degrees of freedom to allow rotations and permutations of the weight matrix, but such transformations do not affect the response time predictions of the network. 

7. GENERAL DISCUSSION 

The intermediate layer of non-linear units has two important consequences. First, an output unit can have a non-monotonic time-course of activation. This property has a number of implications for simulating the latency and likelihood of errors and correct responses. Second, topologically identical networks can have functionally distinct solutions. These issues will be considered below.

7.1. Consequences of a Non-monotonic Time-Course of Activation 

When output units have activations that change over time, a natural measure of the response time is the time it takes an output unit or a set of output units to reach some criterion level of activation. If an output unit has a non-monotonic timecourse of activation, an erroneous response could be produced despite the fact that the correct response would be given asymptotically. Consider, for example, the time-course of activation of the output unit in response to each of the four inputs depicted in Fig. 2b. If the criterion varies stochastically from trial to trial according to a normal distribution, an incorrect response to input “11” will arise occasionally. (For the solution depicted in Fig. 6d, the probability of a false alarm to input “11” will always be greater than the probability of a correct response to inputs “01” and “10” because the maximal activation of input “11” is greater than the maximal activation of inputs “OL” or “10.”) Moreover, these erroneous responses will have latencies that are shorter than the latencies of correct responses to inputs “01” and “10” and thus fast responses are more likely to be errors rather than correct responses, whereas slow responses are more likely to be correct responses rather than errors. Thus, the latency of errors will be less than the latency of correct responses, despite the fact that there will be fewer errors than correct responses. Finally, a speed—accuracy trade-off will arise if different mean values of the criteria for response are considered. 

It should be clear that both the cascade mode of processing and the nonlinear nature of the intermediate units are required for this particular pattern of response probability and response latency of errors and correct responses to arise. That is, if a single measure such as the asymptotic error is used to index both response probability (the smaller the error for a possible response, the more probable that response is) and response latency (the smaller the error for a possible response, the faster that response is) in a feed-forward network with hidden units (e.g., Seidenberg & McClelland, 1989), the most likely response must have the shortest latency. Moreover, if the intermediate layer was comprised of linear units as in the cascade model (McClelland, 1979) or if there were no intermediate layer of units as in the configural-cue model (Gluck & Bower, 1988), then the rank-ordered probability of a response would be identical at all latencies because the time-course of activation of the output unit corresponding to different responses do not intersect. 

7.2. Alternative Accounts of Fast Errors 

In the preceding section, it was argued that a non-monotonic time-course of activation could lead to errors and that these errors would have latencies that were less than the latencies of correct responses. Of course, it could be argued that the pattern that arises in the “non-monotonic” solutions of the 2-2-1 XOR network is not directly analogous to most experimental situations because the arguments were made in terms of the responses made to different stimuli—incorrect responses to input “11” and correct responses to inputs “O01” and “10.” However, a situation that is analogous to most experimental situations in which response times of correct and 

570 

ALAN H. KAWAMOTO 

incorrect responses to the same input can be obtained arises in the responses to input “11” in the 2-2-2 XOR/AND network discussed earlier. For this network, the activation of the output unit corresponding to the XOR function increases before subsequently decreasing, as it does in the “non-monotonic” solutions of the 2-2-1 network, whereas the activation of the output unit corresponding to the AND function increases monotonically to an asymptotic value greater than the maximal value of the XOR output unit. Moreover, the activation of the XOR output unit begins to decrease when the activation of the AND output unit is still negligible. (The time-courses of activation of the XOR and AND output units are illustrated in Fig. 1a of Kawamoto and Kitzis (1991).) Thus, the pattern of fast errors discussed in the preceding section does arise for correct and incorrect responses to the same input. 

In the remainder of this section, previous theoretical accounts of fast errors including an example of a mixture model and of an accumulation model will be briefly presented. In addition, alternative connectionist architectures that have recently been used to simulate fast errors from two different domains will be examined in some detail. These alternative architectures differ from the feed-forward networks considered earlier by virtue of the lateral inhibitory connections between units in an intermediate layer. These simulations are important in demonstrating that a single connectionist network can account for results often thought to require two different processing systems. 

One domain in which the response times of both correct and incorrect responses (as well as speed-accuracy trade-offs) have specifically been considered is choice reaction times in which the subject must make one of two responses. As Luce (1986) summarizes, there is evidence that the mean latency of errors can be less than the mean latency of incorrect responses. (Note that fast errors do not always arise; Swensson (1972) argues that for human subjects, errors are faster than correct responses for easy discriminations that have to be made quickly, whereas errors are slower than correct responses for difficult discriminations that have to be made accurately.) The relative latency of errors and correct responses is important because different choice reaction time models make different predictions about this relationship (Luce, 1986; Swensson, 1972). Predictions made by an example from each of two different classes of choice reaction time models, mixture models and accumulation models, will be considered below. 

In “fast-guess” models (an example of mixture models}, the subject is assumed to behave in one of two different modes; the subject either makes a fast simple reaction to the onset of the signal (and therefore performs at chance) or responds more slowly on the basis of information extracted from the signal. It is clear that if errors are made, they would be made quickly when the subject is simply responding to the onset of any signal. As latency increases, the subject is more likely to respond on the basis of the signal itself and thus accuracy increases. In random walk models {an example of accumulation models} there is only a single mode of processing. Upon presentation of a stimulus, the subject begins sampling sequentially to accumulate evidence in favor of one of two stimuli, When one of the stimuli 

TIME-COURSE OF PROCESSING 

$71 

accumulates a certain amount of evidence relative to the other, it is selected as the response. Fast errors can only be accounted for if the distribution of the random walk’s drift rate is not normal (Ratcliff, 1978, 1988). 

However, as pointed out earlier in the discussion, the simulation results not only suggest that errors can have a mean latency that is shorter than the mean latency of correct responses, but that performance can be less than chance rather than merely at chance for fast responses (at least for some stimuli). Such patterns have been found in at least two experimental paradigms, noise/compatibility and word naming experiments. In noise/compatibility experiments, subjects respond to a visual stimulus consisting of a letter flanked by the same letter or different letters (e.g., H flanked by H’s in the compatible condition or by S’s in the incompatible condition). For the incompatible condition, the mean latency of errors is not only shorter than the mean latency of correct responses despite fewer errors than correct responses, but errors are also more prevalent than correct responses at short latencies (Coles, Gratton, Bashore, Eriksen, & Donchin, 1985). In word naming experiments, subjects read aloud words that have either regular or irregular pronunciations (e.g., “PALE” is regular and “PINT” is irregular). For irregular words, the mean latency of incorrect regular pronunciations is shorter than the mean latency of correct irregular pronunciations despite fewer incorrect than correct responses, and there is almost no overlap in the distribution of correct and incorrect responses {Kawamoto & Zemblidge, 1992). 

Both of these results have been modeled using connectionist networks that differ slightly from those considered in this study. In particular, as discussed more fully below, these networks have lateral inhibitory connections between units at an intermediate layer. To simulate the noise/compatibility results, Cohen, Servan-Schreiber, and McClelland (1992) used a network consisting of three layers representing the input, response, and attention. The input layer consisted of six units (one for each letter, H or S, in each of three positions, left, center, and right), the output layer consisted of two units (one for each response, H or S), and the attention layer consisted of three units (one for each position, /eft, center, and right). The attention units formed excitatory connections to input units in the corresponding position, which in turn formed excitatory connections to output units corresponding to the same letter. (There were also connections from input units to attention units in the corresponding position, although it is not clear whether these connections are necessary to account for the results.} In addition, the units within each layer were mutually inhibitory. When a stimulus is presented, units representing the letters in the target position are selectively enhanced by units in the attention module. (This Selective enhancement is critical—a similar approach by Molenaar (1990) was probably unsuccessful because the target letter was not selectively enhanced.) Although the flanking letters are each less active than the central letter, the combined effect of the flanking letters is larger than the effect of the single central letter, at least initially, because the flanking letters both support the same response. Over time, however, the initially most active central letter inhibits the flanking letters through the lateral inhibitory connections and the effect of the flanking 

572 

ALAN H. KAWAMOTO 

letters decreases to the point where their combined effect becomes less than the effect of the single central letter which continues to increase in activation. Thus, performance is worse than chance early in processing but almost perfect late in processing. The naming data can be simulated in a similar way (Kawamoto & Zemblidge, 1992; Kawamoto, in press). For this case, the input layer is comprised of units corresponding to four-letter words and the output is comprised of units representing different phonemes in one of four different positions. Because activation of an input unit is positively correlated with its similarity to the target, the input unit corresponding to the target is the most activated and other units corresponding to words spelled like the target (the target’s “neighbors”) are activated to a lesser degree. When the target is an irregular word, a situation analogous to the incompatible condition arises, That is, the target’s effect is initially less than the combined effect of the target’s neighbors, but as processing proceeds, the mutual inhibition leads to the target becoming increasingly dominant until the effect of the target exceeds the combined effect of the target’s neighbors. The feed-forward networks with an intermediate layer of nonlinear units and those with an intermediate layer of lateral inhibitory units are models comprised of a single system that incorporate multiple constraints acting with different timecourses. Of course, multiple constraints can also be incorporated in models comprised of multiple systems that are quite independent of each other. Such models have been proposed for the noise/compatibility results (Gratton, Coles, Sirevaag, Eriksen, & Donchin, 1988) as well as the word naming results (Coltheart, 1978). 

7.3. Multiple Classes of Solutions 

Although the connection strengths of topologically identical feed-forward networks without intermediate units all converge to the same values for a set of input-output mappings, the connection strengths of topologically identical networks with nonlinear hidden units can converge to different values. The different solutions can be distinguished by the way the hidden units transform the representation of the inputs. In some cases, the hidden unit representations of each input of the different solutions are related to each other simply through rotations and permutations. In other cases, however, the different solutions are quite distinct and have functional differences. As seen in this study, for example, the same input can have different time-courses when processing is carried out in cascade, and an additional input-output mapping can differ in how easily it can be learned. The different solutions would also lead to different patterns of generalization (Pavel, Gluck, & Henkle, 1989; Giles, Miller, Chen, Chen, Sun, & Lee, 1992). 

The existence of multiple solutions can be viewed in two different ways. On one hand, it can be viewed as a manifestation of the “super-powerfulness” of hidden units (Massaro, 1992). On the other hand, it can be viewed as a possible way to account for individual differences. That is, human subjects solving the XOR problem also have a number of different solutions available to them. One solution 

TIME-COURSE OF PROCESSING 

573 

is to compute the XOR logical function on the basis of the OR (or NOR) and AND (or NAND) logical functions. Another solution is to treat each stimulus as completely distinct from the other stimuli and to simply memorize the response to four arbitrary inputs. Alternatively, the subject could treat the problem as a “same-different” problem and determine whether the two units of the input pair are the same or different, or the subject could treat the problem as a parity problem and determine whether the number of activated units is even or odd. For different Strategies, different response time and error patterns would be predicted. Regardless of how one views functionally distinct solutions, the possibility of such solutions for topologically identical, as well as topologically different, networks must be taken into account in simulations of empirical data. Of course, the search for individual differences will require more effort on the part of researchers carrying out experiments, as well as those carrying out simulations. However, such efforts would determine whether certain classes of networks can be excluded and would determine the type of constraints that must be placed on a network to decrease or increase the number of functionally distinguishable solutions. 

7.4. Final Remarks 

Specific connectionist implementations differ along a number of different dimensions including representation (e.g., local vs. distributed representations, higher order features), topology (e.g., the number of hidden units in an intermediate layer, the use of direct connections from input units to output units, lateral inhibition, recurrent connections), activation function (e.g., linear or nonlinear), as well as processing dynamics (e.g., in a fixed number of steps or in cascade). The specific choices can lead to networks with qualitatively different behavior such as an output unit with a time-course of activation that is monotonic or non-monotonic, and the possibility of multiple solutions with time-courses of activation that are functionally distinguishable. In the future, differences in processing dynamics could be used to distinguish between different classes of connectionist models (e.g., Kawamoto, in press), as well as to distinguish between connectionist models and other classes of reaction time models (Anderson, 1991}.
