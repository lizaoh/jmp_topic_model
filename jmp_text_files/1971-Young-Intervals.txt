The sequence of events for an item can be depicted as follows (where I denotes an intervening trial on another item):  RL (iIs) Tl (jls) R2 (101s) T2.  Some items had Tl omitted entirely. The most salient aspects of the results were (a) the inclusion of Tl enhanced performance  on T2, (b) on items with Tl omitted, as the RI -R2 interval increased, the probability of being correct on T2 (P(CI)) first  increased and then declined, and (c) with the RI R2 interval length fixed, as Tl moved from just after Rl to just before R2 ,  P(C2) rose and then fell.  The nonmonotonicity of these results made it impossible to fit these data with any simple variant of stimulus sampling theory  or extant Markov models with long- and short-term memory stores. A new model, with its main innovative feature the postulation  of multiple short-term memory states, each with unique properties, is proposed; its fit to the data was quite satisfactory.  This study was designed mainly to generate evidence concerning the role of the intervals between reinforcements and test  trials in paired-associate learning. Secondly, the study was concerned with the separate roles of the reinforcements and the  test trials themselves. This  procedure affords the subject immediate feedback as to the correctness of his response. The entire list is presented serially  in this fashion, in a different random order each time, until a criterion is reached or a fixed number of cycles through the  list is completed. Often, a cycle of R-alone trials occurs before the anticipation procedure (i.e., the first cycle of TR  trials) is initiated.  An alternative procedure, called by Izawa (1966) the RT procedure, has recently come into use. In it, a complete cycle of R  trials occurs first, then a complete cycle of T trials without any feedback, in a different random order. Complete cycles of  Rs and complete cycles of Ts, the items appearing in random orders, alternate until a criterion is met or a fixed number of  "double cycles" (an R cycle followed by a T cycle) through the list is completed.  In neither procedure is adequate evidence provided about the effects of the intervals between Rs and Ts, because the lengths  of these intervals are not varied systematically and independently. The state of affairs for a typical item in an anticipation  experiment with an M-item list can be depicted diagrammatically as follows:  R (2M - 2 Is) TR (2M - 2 Is) T.  The state of affairs for a typical item in a comparable RT experiment, also with an M-item list, can be depicted as follows:  R (M - 1 Is) T (M - 1 Is) R (M - 1 Is) T.  In the above representations, "Is" refers to intervening events, with each I either an R or a T on another item. The total  number of trials intervening between two successive Rs on the same item averages 2M - 1 in both procedures, and is thus tied  directly to list length. The 2M - 1 trials between successive Rs include exactly one T on the item, though the placement of  that T differs in the two procedures; the constraints on the placement of the T operate so that the ratio of the durations of  of the R-T and the T-R intervals is constant on the average across trials in each method. Thus the R-T and the T-R intervals  for an item do not vary independently in either method. Furthermore, as illustrated above, the T comes sooner after each R, on  the average, in the RT method than in the anticipation method; therefore, responses should be expected to reflect less  short-term retention loss in the RT than in the anticipation procedure. In either of these methods, then, or even in the  best-designed comparison between them, any possible function of the R-T interval in the learning process is not separable from  its function in producing short-term retention loss.  The present experiment was designed to generate evidence about intervals between Rs and Ts (and about Rs and Ts) which is not  available in the anticipation or RT  60 YOUNG  methods or comparisons between them. The main features of the paradigm may be depicted diagrammatically as follows:  R (i Is) T ( j Is) R (101s) T.  The basic feature was that two Rs were given on each of several items, separated in each case by the same fixed number of  trials, i + j + 1, on other items, and Ts on the different items were given at different points in that R-R interval. A final  retention test on any item came a fixed interval of 10 trials after the second R. For a given R-R interval, the T in different  items was placed at virtually all points in the interval, including the end of that interval, so that the item simulated an  anticipation item, and in the middle of that interval, so that the item simulated an RT item. For other items, the T in the  R-R interval was omitted entirely. In what follows, P(CZ) refers to the probability of a correct response on the T following  the second R. Thus the comparison of P(C,,) for different placements of the T (or its absence) in an R-R interval of fixed  length should not be affected by differential retention loss from the two Rs for the different items, as happens with standard  methods; these comparisons should only reflect the differential effectiveness of the T in the learning process as a function  of its placement in the interval. Since different R-R intervals containing no T were used, it was also possible to observe the  effect on P(C2) of varying that interval; this comparison was a replication and extension of the result of Peterson, Wampler,  Kirkpatrick, and Saltzman (1963). All the different conditions were presented to each subject in one experimental session.  METHOD  Subjects  Subjects were 100 volunteer Stanford University students whose native language was English. Each was paid $2.00 for his  participation. One subject had to be replaced because of a complete breakdown of equipment while she was being run.  Stimulus and Response Materials  Stimulus materials consisted of consonant trigrams. In each, the first letter was D, H, L, P, S, or W, the second letter was  C, G, K, N, R, V, or Z, and the third letter was B, F, J, M, Q, T, or X. There are 294 (6 x 7 x 7) such trigrams, and these  formed the stimulus pool. The responses were the ten digits 0-9.  Order of Trials  The bulk of each subject's trials involved 150 items, which were constructed in a similar manner for each subject. Each of  these items received either three trials, two Rs and one T in the sequence RRT, or four trials, two Rs and two Ts in the  sequence RTRT. When applied to R, a subscript indicates which of the two Rs on an item is being referred to, and when applied  to a T, it indicates which of the two Rs on the item the T referred to followed most closely. Thus, in terms of this notation,  the sequence of events on RTRT items was R,-T,-R2-T,, and on RRT items was RI-R2-T2 . Experimental Apparatus  The apparatus used was the verbal associative learning apparatus at the Institute for Mathematical Studies in the Social  Sciences, Stanford University. This apparatus was installed in two rooms, one of which housed the experimenter and the control  equipment and the other of which was for the subject. The capabilities of the apparatus which allowed the experiment to be  executed in the fashion described below, as well as the other features of the apparatus itself, are described elsewhere  (Izawa, 1966; Yellott, 1969).  The subject's room was a sound-deadened, air-conditioned room, kept at 70 for this experiment. It contained only stimulus  display and response apparatus. On a table in front of the subject's chair was a response panel with a column of 10 response  keys numbered 0 to 9 from the bottom in ascending order. These could be illuminated individually. On a second table 5.5 ft  away from the subject were two stimulus display boxes, one of top of the other. The front of each box was a 2 in. x 12 in.  display window in each of which up to eight alphanumeric characters could be displayed.  Procedure  The procedure was identical for all subjects. The subject was ushered into the subject's room and read some instructions.  These gave the subject as much information about the experimental task as it was felt he could use. Following a short practice  task, which was the same for all subjects, further instructions emphasizing that perfection was not attainable on the  experimental task were read. These instructions and attendant procedures are recounted in full detail elsewhere (Young, 1966).  The experimental trials followed according to the order programmed for that subject. On both practice and regular experimental  trials, the same procedure was followed. If a trial was an R, the stimulus trigram and response member appeared simultaneously  on the lower screen, the former in the first three positions and the latter in the last (eighth) position, for one second,  followed by an intertrial interval of approximately three seconds before the onset of the next trial. A T consisted of the  appearance of the trigram alone in the first three positions of the upper screen for one second. However, the subject had as  much time as desired to respond, which he did by pressing the button on the response panel on which the desired response was  written. The button pressed lighted for a second; then there was a three second intertrial interval before the next trial,  during which time the response made and its latency from the onset of the T were recorded automatically by equipment in the  experimenter's room. The experiment continued in this fashion until all the programmed trials had been run, a process which  took approximately 1-1 1/4 hours. Since this device is used considerably in the rest of this paper, and since  it has been neglected in the psychological literature, it is developed in some detail here. One of the best derivations of the  result is in the works of Neyman (1949). Suppose it is desired to fit a mathematical model to a series of empirical  proportions, Pl , P2 ,..., P. There is a vector of the theoretical parameters of the model (01 1 02 ) ..., Bs), where s <_  n, such  that the model predicts each of the proportions as a function of this vector of theoretical parameters. Neyman (1949) uses this  procedure in giving a fundamental derivation of the X2 test.  1 n the present case, the application of this result is direct and can serve as an example. There are 18 independent  proportions, one estimate of P(C2) for each of the 18 values of k. It is possible to fit a constant function, a linear  function, and a quadratic function of k to these points. All three of these functions are of the form P(C2) = A -}- Bk + Ck2  In the quadratic function, A, B, and C are free to vary; in the linear function, A and B are free to vary while C is set at 0;  in the constant function, only A is free to vary, while B and C are both set at 0. The minimum X2 estimation here and in all  other uses in this investigation was accomplished with a computer program which searched the parameter space for the  minimizing values (Chandler, 1965). The fit of a constant yielded X2 = 16.98 on 17 degrees of freedom, which is quite  satisfactory. The straight line fit yielded X2 = 16.93 on 16 degrees of freedom, yielding a X2 for the difference between a  constant and a straight line of 0.05, which on 1 degree of freedom is clearly not significant. The best fitting quadratic function, however, yielded a X2 of 8.65  on 15 degrees of freedom, which is a far better fit. The X2 for the difference between the straight line and quadratic fits of  8.28, on 1 degree of freedom, is significant beyond the 0.01 level. DISCUSSION  The results of this study have relevance to a number of currently active lines of empirical and theoretical inquiry. One of  these is the role of Ts in the paired-associate learning process (Izawa, 1966, 1967a, 1967b). Izawa's studies found that the  inclusion of one or more Ts in an R-R interval increased the probability of subsequent recall. In none of her studies were the  lengths of the R-R intervals precisely equated for items which did or did not have Ts between the Rs; furthermore, her studies  used modified list learning paradigms, with each item undergoing multiple repetitions of a sequence of Rs and Ts. Thus,  interpretation of her results on this point is clouded. The present results unequivocally demonstrate that inclusion of a T in  an R-R interval does enhance the probability of subsequent recall. Moreover, the evidence seems to indicate that the extent of  such enhancement is maximal for placement of the T in the interior of the R-R interval and less for its placement at either  extreme.  A second line of inquiry to which the results of this study are relevant is the comparison between the RT and the anticipation  procedures in paired-associate learning (Battig and Brackett, 1961; Battig, Brown, and Nelson, 1963; Cofer, Diamond, Olsen,  Stein, and Walker, 1967; Lockhead, 1962). A full discussion of this area is found elsewhere (Young, 1966). Briefly, learning  under the RT procedure is typically superior to that under the anticipation procedure. The present results suggest this  superiority may stem, at least in part, from the T in each R-R interval being in the interior of of the interval, an optimal  placement, in the RT method, while the T is at the end of each R-R interval, a less efficacious placement, in the anticipation  method.  A third line of inquiry to which this study is relevant is the issue of spacing of reinforcements (Bjork, 1970). The data from  the RRT items show conclusively what the data of Peterson et al. (1963) only hint at: with a constant (and relatively long)  R-T interval, the probability of correct recall rises to a maximum and then declines as the length of the R-R interval  increases.  A final line of inquiry to which the results of this study have relevance is that of learning models which take into account  theoretically the well-documented phenomenon of short-term retention loss (e.g., Peterson and Peterson, 1959, 1962). These  include models in which a transient short-term memory state, entered with high probability on an R trial if a long-term memory  state is not entered, is postulated as a theoretical entity (Atkinson and Crothers, 1964; Atkinson and Shiffrin, 1965, 1968;  Bernbach, 1965; Bower, 1967; Greeno, 1967) and stimulus fluctuation models (Atkinson and Estes, 1963), in which short-term  retention loss is a consequence of more primitive processes.  It is the task of any theoretical model which would account for the results of the present experiment to postulate what  happens to an association during each of the following kinds of trials: Rs, Ts and Is. Several extant models were extended as  minimally as possible to meet this condition (see Young, 1966, for an account of some of the attempts), and they all failed to  fit the present data, since none of them was able to account for the following two results:  1. P(C2) for RRT item types was a concave function of k.  2. P(C2) for RTRT item types was higher than for the appropriate comparison RRT item types. At the same time, statistical  analysis of the plots of P(C2) as a function of i with i + j constant revealed no significant linear trends, but some evidence  of concavity.  Rumelhart (1967) has used the term General Forgetting Theory (GFT) for the class of finite state Markov learning models in  which there is a forgotten, a short-term memory, and a long-term memory state, with perhaps a transient initial state added, a  class encompassing virtually all such learning models extant. Though it has not yet been proved, it is the author's  conjecture, after a long search for counterexamples, that it is impossible to find a simple variant of either GFT or stimulus  fluctuation theory that can account for both results above. Bjork (1970) has suggested that allowing some retention loss from  the long-term state might allow some variant of GFT to account for Result 1, but such an emendation will not allow GFT to  account for Result 2, the effect of test trials. A different model is in order.  A NEW MODEL AND ITS APPLICATION  Results 1 and 2 cited above, as well as intuitive considerations (cf. Young, 1969), suggest a modification of extant models  involving more than the two states of memory, unitary short- and long-term states, of GFT. Such notions are not new; strength  theories (e.g., Wickelgren and Norman, 1966), for example, essentially postulate a continuum of memory states, one associated  with each possible "strength." In particular, in place of the unitary short-term state of GFT, the model to be proposed here  postulates two short-term states, the "immediate" and the "delayed" short-term states, each with its own properties. The action of an R can be summarized as follows: An item in the unlearned state can remain there; if it does not remain in the  unlearned state, it enters either the longterm or the immediate short-term state. An item in the delayed short-term state can  also enter the long-term state; if it does not enter the long-term state, it enters the immediate short-term state. An item in  the immediate short-term or the long-term state remains where it is.  On a T, the following is the state of affairs: In the unlearned state, the correct response is given with probability g. An  item in the unlearned state remmins there. An item in the delayed short-term state is there because it entered the delayed  shortterm state from the immediate short-term state on the last trial or because it was already in the delayed short-term  state and remained there on the last trial. The a priori probability that the item entered the substate Sc on entering or  remaining in the delayed short-term state is B. Thus, of all the items in the delayed short-term state at any time, the  expected proportion in the substate Sc is B, and expected proportion in the substate S, is I -B. As on an R, an item in the immediate short-term or learned state remains there.  The situation on an I can best be visualized as a series of gates between the immediate and delayed short-term and between the  delayed short-term and the unlearned states. These gates are closed with probabilities H, and H2 respectively. If the item is  not in state L or state U, it slips down until it finds a closed gate and stays in the state just above that gate.  The splitting of the delayed short-term state into Sc and SG deserves comment  REINFORCEMENT-TEST INTERVALS IN PAIRED-ASSOCIATE LEARNING 75  at this time. The reader will note that the lines corresponding to these states in matrix R are identical to one another, as  are the analogous lines in matrix I. Only in matrix T and vector Pdo these substates have different properties. As will become  clear below, states SC and SG embody the two possible outcomes of the process it is hypothesized a subject goes through on a T  if the item is in the delayed short-term state. Sc embodies the termination of the process with a correct response and one set  of transition probabilities, while SG embodies its termination in a guess and a different set of transition probabilities.  Intuitively, the probability of the first outcome is B and that of the second (1 - B); the formal role of B in the matrix is  to assure that the probability of an item's being in Sc on a given trial, given it is in Sc or SG , is B, independent of where  it was on any previous trials.  The motivation for the formal I-D model comes from an extension of Greeno's (1967) rationale for his particular version of GFT  in terms of coding of items. His basic notion is that when a subject gets an R on an item, he arrives at a code for the item,  a memonic device perhaps. Entry into long-term memory is interpreted as coding with an adequate code, i.e., one that can  support retention for the duration of the experiment. Entry into short-term memory is interpreted as coding with an inadequate  code, i.e., one which will not support retention for the duration of the experiment, perhaps because the code is difficult to  remember or because it is bound to lead to stimulus confusion errors in the particular experiment.  In the present model, entry into the immediate short-term state is interpreted as initial coding with an inadequate code. At  this point, the code is supporting recall quite well, just as if it were an adequate one, so that on an R or a T the subject  has no cue that he has an inadequate code. Since the subject has no cue that his code is inadequate, he has no motivation to  try to arrive at a more adequate code, i.e., to learn.  After a few Is intervene, the inadequate code ceases to support recall in so facile a fashion as it could in immediate  short-term memory. On a T, a search process is necessary to retrieve the correct response. The delayed short-term state is  interpreted as the state in which an item resides when its code can no longer support recall in a quick and ready fashion, but  can support recall after a search process. In the delayed short-term state, a search on a T is successful with probability B,  in which case the item is said to have been in state Sc . In that case, the correct response is given, but the subject knows  that he had to search for that response and is thus motivated to recode the item in a more adequate fashion, i.e., to learn.  If the search is unsuccessful, the subject guesses and does not know whether the item is in the delayed short-term state or in  an unlearned state. In this case, the item is said to have been in state SG , and the same process occurs that would have  occurred were the trial an I. In the delayed short-term state, on an R, the subject realizes that he knew the correct response  but would have had to go through a search, which might not have been successful, to find that correct response on a T.  Therefore, he is motivated to try to recode the item in a more adequate fashion, i.e., to learn.  76 YOUNG  From the above discussion, two interpretations emerge of the terms "immediate" and "delayed" as applied to the short-term  states. First of all, when an item enters short-term memory, it is immediately in the immediate short-term state. After a stay  there, and thus a delay after its entry into short-term memory, the item is in the delayed short-term state. Secondly, in the  immediate short-term state, according to the coding interpretation, recall is immediate, while in the delayed short-term  state, recall is delayed while the search process goes on.  The coding interpretation of the inclusion of a nonzero probability of remaining in state U on an R in the model was suggested  by informal postexperimental interviews of the subjects. They usually indicated that there were times when the exposure on an  R was "so brief" that they could not tell what the response term was. These remarks indicated that at some points in the  experiment, for one or another of several possible reasons, the subject did not perceive and code the response term at all  before the exposure time was up (cf. Bjork, 1970). The features of  the I-D model that allow it to account for these aspects of the data can be detailed qualitatively: Since an item never enters  the delayed short-term state directly on an R, but passes into that state from the immediate short-term state on its route  back to the unlearned state, the probability that an item is in the delayed short-term state is at its highest value a  moderate time after an R. The model also predicts a higher P(C,) for RTRT than for the appropriate comparison RRT item types, because some  learning does take place on a T. The model further predicts that the placement of the T at some point intermediate between the  two Rs is optimal in terms of increasing P(C2), for that is the point at which the item has the highest probability of being  in the delayed short-term state, the only state in which a T can increase the probability of subsequent responding.
