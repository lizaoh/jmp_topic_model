INTRODUCTION  Let A be a given set, and suppose that with each pair (x, y) E A X A one may associate an experiment in which the subject is  to judge which of the elements x, y has more of a certain given attribute (e.g., is preferred by him). Situations of this type  are usually analyzed in terms of so-called choice probabilities p(x, y), the probabilities that x will be chosen by the  subject if the (ordered) pair (x, y) is presented to him.  Generally, the choice probabilities are analyzed in the context of a decision model, and it is shown that under a suitable set  of conditions they induce a utility scale (for the relevant literature, see, for example, Luce, 1958, 1959; Luce & Edwards,  1958; Debreu, 1958, 1960; Georgescu-Roegen, 1958; Davidson & Marschak, 1959; Marschak, 1960; Luce & Suppes, 1965; Marley,  1965; Burke & Zinnes, 1965; Tversky & Russo, 1969; Roberts, 1971; Fishburn, 1973; Corbin & Marley, 1974; or Bartoszyfski,  1974; also see Rapoport, 1976).  Under somewhat less stringent conditions one can use choice probabilities for the construction of linear subjective  classification (see Bartoszyfiski, 1971a, b).  In most work concerning choice probabilities, it is assumed that the p(x, y) refer to the choices made by one particular  person. However, in view of the obvious difficulties in estimating choice probabilities for one individual, one occasionally  assumes (explicitly or not) that p(x, y) is the same for all subjects, and then one proceeds with the estimation on the basis  of choices observed in a sample of persons.  249  0022-2496/78/0183-0249$02.00/0 Copyright 1978 by Academic Press, Inc. Section 3 treats certain optimality problems: Out of two sequences of classifying standards, the one with more terms (or  "tighter," in case of infinite sequences) is better, as it leads to a finer classification. The considerations of this paper, however, were originally inspired by a problem with no apparent physical continuum  underlying the choices. The problem (see Bartoszyfiski, 1972) concerned the choice of optimal policy of exchanging by the bank  those banknotes which were "too much used," and replacing them by the new ones. The solution consisted of a stochastic model  of "wearing out" the banknotes in their circulation between the bank and society, enabling one to search for optimal "exchange  threshold." The prerequisite for application of this model (and, in fact, for formulating the optimality  LINEAR CLASSIFICATION 251  criterion), was the construction of a classification scheme (a sequence of "boundary" banknotes, in the sense described  above), which could be used to estimate the distribution of the degree to which the banknotes in circulation are "worn out"  (the conditions by the bank ruled out any "objective" measure of the degree to which a banknote is used, such as the amount of  dirt accumulated, number of wrinkles, etc.; the only admissible procedure consisted on purely subjective pair comparisons). As  it turned out, it was possible to construct a sequence {z;} of five banknotes, thus creating a fairly reliable taxonomy into  six categories (for details of the model see Bartoszynski, 1972).  2. DISCUSSION  The present discussion concerns the problems of plausibility and logical independence of the introduced postulates.  Let us observe first that the set of postulates of this paper is consistent; this may be easily verified by checking that all  postulates hold for the example of function ps(x, y) given in Section 2.  It may be argued that Postulate 1 (symmetry) and Postulate 7 (closure) present no conceptual difficulties. Also, let us for a  moment avoid the difficulties connected with Postulate 3 (variability of p,(x, y)), and assume that there is only one subject  s, or that p,(x, y) - p, ,,(x, y) for all s, s' e S. We may then write p(x, y) instead of ps(x, y) and the question is that of  independence of Postulates 2 and 4, i.e., of (preferably psychologically plausible) situations in which one of these  postulates holds and the other does not.
