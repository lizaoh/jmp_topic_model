Suppes's scientific work spans an exceptionally large array of topics. A short list would necessarily include: foundations of physics (special relativity and quantum mechanics), measurement theory, foundation of probability, decision theory, mathematical learning theory, psycholinguistics, concept formation, education (especially, educational technology). Suppes pursued undergraduate studies in physics and mathematics, majoring in physics, and received a BS from the University of Chicago in 1943. After two years of military duty in the South Pacific, he began his graduate studies in philosophy at Columbia University, obtaining his Ph.D. in 1950. While at Columbia, he was strongly influenced by Ernest Nagel. In 1950, he joined the Department of Philosophy at Stanford, where he remained all his life. Soon after moving to Stanford, he started collaborating with the logicians Alfred Tarski (from Berkeley) and J. C. C. McKinsey (from Stanford), who introduced him to the axiomatic and set theoretical methods that remained his trademark for the rest of his career. Together with Duncan Luce, he can be credited for introducing these methods in theoretical psychology.

Falmagne's scientific interests and works are also quite diverse, and include reaction times, psychophysics, measurement theory, choice theory, knowledge spaces (with applications to educational technology), and media theory. His research is marked by an extensive application of mathematics, and especially axiomatic methods, to the analysis of behavioral phenomena. He received his Ph.D. in 1965 from the University of Brussels. He spent then three years in the US, where he had successive visiting appointments or postdoctoral positions at the Universities of Pennsylvania, Wisconsin, and Michigan. This book may well be regarded as the intellectual biography and legacy of its author as a philosopher of science. Suppes published an early version of it in 1962, named  Set-Theoretical Structures in Sciences , but fragments existed even before, in the form of lecture notes, or as the final chapter of his  Introduction to Logic  ( Suppes, 1957 ), entitled “Set-Theoretical-Foundations of the Axiomatic Method”, not to mention his monograph  Axiomatic Set Theory  ( Suppes, 1960a ), also relevant to his subject. As a matter of fact, a large part of Suppes's extraordinarily diverse scientific works is reflected in the various chapters of the book reviewed here. His general point of view is that both the common sense treatment and the artificial ad hoc languages typically used in scientific discourses are not powerful enough for a proper discussion of the intricacies encountered in the analysis of empirical data, and that set-theoretical “structures provide the right setting for investigating problems of representation and invariance in any systematic part of past or present science.” (p. xiii). As for the emphasis on representation and invariance, it is justified because “Central topics in foundational studies of different sorts through the ages easily fall under this heading. Certainly, it is not the whole of philosophy of science. But it is a major part.” (p. xiii).

What we find in these pages is an elaborate, highly sophisticated discussion of the concept of “scientific theory”, illustrated by a collection of important examples. The scope of the work is extraordinary, and the task of the writer was a daunting one. But Suppes's unique dual background as a philosopher of science with a strong technical bent and a scientist with important contributions to his credit in a wide array of fields, made him an obvious nominee for it.

The concept of a “representation”, however—and especially the “representation theorem” establishing the existence of a representation—is a corner stone of modern mathematics. Its importance derives from the fact that a nontrivial representation of a mathematical structure into (or onto) another offers a different outlook on the structure, possibly capable of providing new intuitions. Moreover, such a representation may also enable a new calculus, making possible computations unavailable in the parent structure.

An important distinction is made between representations obtained by definition and representation resulting from a representation theorem. A representation by definition does not create an essentially new concept. Rather, it constructs a concept by combining existing ones. Such a concept may seem intuitively new but is not formally so. This does not mean that representations by definition are to be automatically regarded as minor achievements. Chapter  5. This long chapter, taking fully one-fourth of the text, could be published by itself as a highly valuable monograph on the foundations of probability. The first section ( “ The Formal Theory ”) recalls the classical notion of “probability space” as axiomatized by  Kolmogorov (1933/1950) . The developments more or less follow the standard line, with one exception, at the end of the section, where we find a short discussion of the “ divergence ” between modal logic and probability. 
                         18 
                      
                      
                         18 
                         Interestingly, no mention of fuzzy logic or fuzzy set theory is to be found in Suppes's book. 
                       Suppes then turns to a historical account of the notion of probability with its evolving definitions and the related foundational concepts. He reviews the contributions of Pascal and Laplace, the latter playing a central role. The staggering difficulties raised by the relative-frequency theory are examined in depth, with the works of Von Mises and especially Church and Kolmogorov. A leading excerpt from  Kolmogorov (1963, p. 369)  is worth quoting. 
                         The frequency concept based on the notion of  limiting frequency  as the number of trials increases to infinity, does not contribute anything to substantiate the applicability of the results of probability to real practical problems where we have always to deal with a finite number of trials. (p. 180). 
                       Kolmogorov's point of view on the “randomness” of finite sequences led him to the idea of measuring the “complexity” of a string, say from the standpoint of its computability by a Turing machine. The idea, which can only be evoked here, is that sequences of sufficient complexity will be judged to be random. Fortunately, for those who would be alarmed by such intricacies, Suppes's opinion on the matter is comforting: “In actual practice, pragmatic rules about the randomness of finite sequences work well when applied by experienced statisticians or scientists.” (p. 183).

The “Logical Theory of Probability” is considered next, beginning with Keynes, who credited Leibnitz for conceiving that probability was a branch of logic, and advocating the view of taking propositions, rather than events, as the basic concept. The contributions of Jeffreys, Carnap and others are then reviewed.

Despite their importance, we only mention those parts of the chapter devoted to “propensity representations of probability” and the “theory of subjective probability”, two topics to which Suppes made important contributions. Included in the latter section is, of course, a discussion of Bayesian statistics, among other topics such as the “theory of beliefs”.

The chapter ends with an “Epilogue: Pragmatism about Probability”, a short section build around the following observation: “Perhaps the two subjects in which we most naturally might expect to find a foundational commitment to some one representation of probability is statistical mechanics and quantum mechanics, but this is far from being the case.” (p. 256). This view is substantiated by long quotations from  Fock (1931/1978)  (the prominent Russian quantum physicist),  Weyl (1928/1931); von Neuman (1932/1955) , and  Mosteller and Wallace (1964/1984) . The spirit of the discussion here and the attitude of working statisticians is well represented by this quotation of the latter two authors: 
                         Even though individual statisticians may claim generally to follow the Bayesian school or the classical school, no one has an adequate rule for deciding what school is being represented at a given moment. When we have thought we were at our most Bayesian, classicists have told us that we were utterly classical; and we have thought ourselves to be giving classical treatment, Bayesians have told us that the ideas were not in the classical lexicon. So we cannot pretend to speak for anyone but ourselves. ( Mosteller & Wallace, 1964/1984 , p. ix, cited on p. 262).

As for Suppes, he “predicts that the physicists’ minimalist program of interpretation will continue to dominate the use of probability concepts in scientific theories, which is why I have included this final section in an already overlong chapter.” (p. 262, footnote 49). By “minimalist” here, he means “having little commitment to a particular representation of probability”.

Chapter  6. The topic is the representation of space and time, with a focus on invariant properties in classical and relativistic physics. We first read a short review of basic geometrical concepts, such as the  n -dimensional affine space and the isomorphism of the  n -dimensional Euclidean and Cartesian spaces, leading to an axiomatic definition of the four-dimensional classical space–time structure with inertial frames and the Lorentz transformations. The relatively modern geometrical language used for the discussion prompts the author to digress into an historical discussion of the evolution of the ideas, centered on a long quotation from the  Amalgamest  from  Ptolemy (1984, written about 150 AD)  expounding the early views on these matters, namely, that the movements of the planets and the stars are circular. These Aristotelian views did not evolve for many centuries, essentially until Benedetti and Galileo, and then only slowly and in complicated ways. Rapid progress was made in the 17th century with Descartes and, obviously, Newton, but the book does not delve in the details of the fast evolution that took place at that time. The next topic is a discussion of the axioms of special relativity, followed by historical remarks about the people and the emergence of the ideas, from the predecessors of relativity—Voigt, FitzGerald, Lorentz and Poincarré “none of whom explicitly formulated a relativistic invariance principle.” (p. 278)—to Einstein via the electrodynamic principles of Maxwell. Later approaches to special relativity concepts are also briefly discussed.

Largely drawing from  Suppes (1977) , two intriguing issues occupy a good part of the second half of the chapter. One is a detailed discussion of the question: “ How to Decide if Visual Space is Euclidean ”. This is a methodological problem and the author considers various experimental methods to answer the question. Because that answer could be negative, he also examines a hierarchy of possible geometries. The second issue concerns: “ The Nature of Visual Space :  Experimental and Philosophical Answers ”, and is devoted to a survey of answers that have been given, going back to Euclid whose  Optics  “ is really a theory of vision, not a treatise on physical optics” (p. 288), and whose answer was positive: the visual space is Euclidean ( Euclid, 1945 ). From Euclid, Suppes moves quickly to Newton's  Opticks , which is mostly concerned with the physical space and thus falls outside the inquiry. It appears that the answer remained positive for many centuries, actually until the publication of Thomas Reid's  Inquiry into the Human Mind  in 1774. As for the moderns (in the 20th century), their views vary widely between the straight “No”, the “Not sure”, and again, the “Yes”, with one “Not but”.

Chapter  7. We turn here to representations in mechanics, beginning with classical particle mechanics. Some basic mathematical notions are first recalled, such as that of vector spaces and related concepts. As is customary with Suppes, the style of the presentation is formal and axiomatic. For example, the first defined concept is that of a “system of (classical) particle mechanics with respect to the classical space–time structure”, a concept specified by four kinematical and five dynamical axioms. These axioms are discussed, and several theorems are derived. While some of the results described in this chapter are classical ones, many others are due to the author, such as an embedding theorem providing the occasion of philosophical comments evoking earlier remarks concerning the finitism in Geometry: “ when we embed a system in a closed system, we are not guaranteeing the actual existence of the additional particles in the larger closed system.” In fact, a large part of the chapter is occupied by two long sections largely derived from Suppes's own work. One section, entitled “ Representation Theorems for Hidden Variables in Quantum Mechanics ” deals with probabilistic representation obtained by Suppes and various collaborators, in particular, M. Zanotti. The second one quotes in details results on reversible processes obtained early on in Suppes's career in collaboration with J. C. C. McKinsey and H. Rubin.

Chapter  8. The psychologist will again find some congenial material in the last chapter, whose title “ Representation of Language ” does not quite describe the breadth of its content. Because this review is already long, I limit my remarks to two of its topics, both of them important and provocative.

The first one, in a section entitled “ Stimulus-response Representation of Finite Automata ”, has its origin in a famous paper published by the author in this journal ( Suppes, 1969 ). In that paper, Suppes proves that an organism functioning according to a particular version of the stimulus–response (S–R) theory of learning can be taught to behave, asymptotically and given an adequate schedule of conditioning, as a finite automaton. 
                         20 
                      
                      
                         20 
                         Needless to say, “stimulus–response theory”, “finite automaton” and “schedule of conditioning” are technical terms requiring precise definitions that cannot be given in the space of this review. We assume that the reader has at least some intuitive acquaintance with them. 
                       Because automata constitute a standard source of representations for language recognition, the enterprise makes sense on general grounds. Yet, this result has generated considerable controversy from the part of psycholinguists and some computer scientists. The most severe criticism, raised by  Arbib (1969) , is that “learning simply could not take place according to stimulus–response conceptions because the number of states required is too large and the amount of time required for conditioning is too restricted.” (p. 390 in Suppes's book). Suppes acknowledges the seriousness of the objection, but contends that its formulation is not precise enough to settle the issue negatively for S–R models, and that, in any event, the proposed alternatives, are too vague. 
                         21 
                      
                      
                         21 
                         One of these alternatives lies in the TOTE hierarchies which Suppes claims to be in fact special cases of the stimulus–response models. 
                       As far as this reviewer can tell, the issue of whether S–R models can offer an adequate representation of complex learning is still wide open at this time. As Suppes points out, negative results of the form “it is not possible for a mechanism of the class  
                         Y 
                       to accomplish a task of the class  
                         Z 
                      ” are delicate to establish and should not be confused with what he calls “ negative dogmas ”. Besides, as any practioner has no doubt experienced, the communication between humans by linguistic means is far from perfect, an observation which has obvious impact on the number of states required. This short outline only evokes one of the key issues covered in this section of Suppes's book, which is highly detailed and obviously essential to the author's creed.

The last topic to be mentioned here is discussed in the “ Language and the Brain ” section of Chapter 8, which covers a recent scientific concern of the author, namely: the extent to which the recording of the brain activity by means such as the classical electroencephalography (EEG), the magnetoencephalography (MEG), the positron emission tomography (PET), and the functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) can be revealing of the way the brain processes language. For Suppes, the choice between these methods is guided by his interest in the processing of individual words or sentences. From that standpoint, PET and fMRI did not a priori qualify because “the processing, although slow by modern computer standards, is much too fast to be able to accomplish anything with the time resolution observation no better than one second. The typical word, for example, whether listened to or read, will be processed in no more than 4 or 5 hundred milliseconds and often faster.” (p. 445). In collaboration with Zhong-Lin Lu, Suppes first attempted to use MEG to recognize the processing of individual words. The results were negative and they were not able to get good recognition result from the MEG recordings, for the seven words used in the experiment. Fortunately, at the Scripps Institute of Research in San Diego, California, where the experiment was conducted, it was standard practice simultaneously to collect the recordings of 20 EEG sensors. They proceeded to analyze these EEG data as well and obtained much better recognition results ( Suppes, Zhong-Lin, & Han, 1997 ). The analysis, the details of which must be omitted here, is based on averaging the data, performing a Fourier transform and filtering. After a while and considerable polishing of the methods, the recognition rates became quite remarkable, reaching 90% in some situations (43 out of 48 sentences of the type “ The capital of Italy is not Paris ”). Among the facts uncovered were the relative invariance of the brain waves across subjects, and also across the mode of presentation: the brain waves were similar for a visual image and its name. These were only the first steps of an engrossing story, part of which is related in Suppes's book, but is still unfolding at this time.

Chapter 8 concludes with a short “ Epilogue :  Representation and Reduction in Science ” which is also that of the entire book and returns to themes already alluded to in this review, such as the role of representation theorems, their scarcity in the development of science, the difficulty of obtaining them in nontrivial cases, and the compromise position that seems practical—cf. the epigraph that I proposed near the beginning of this review.

It is no longer possible to be a universalist. It probably has not been so for more than a couple of hundred years. The absence of universalists around us is not without serious drawbacks. Important facts, methods, or ideas may remain in the province of a scientific subculture for a long time, if not forgotten. At the end of this remarkable book, the reader will have the impression that the author—who calls himself “an unreconstructed pluralist” (p. 52)—is as close to a Renaissance man as is possible to find today in the halls of academia (or anywhere), that is, one uniquely capable of discoursing at a technical level about many areas of science, and most importantly, of bridging critical gaps between disciplines.
