Cognitive modeling of individuals’ data from behavioral experiments is a powerful method of measuring individual differences. Specifically,  fitting  cognitive models to behavioral data results in parameter estimates capturing individual differences in theoretically interpretable parameters ( Dutilh et al., 2019; Farrell & Lewandowsky, 2018; Frischkorn & Schubert, 2018; Frischkorn et al., 2022; Lerche et al., 2020; Schmiedek et al., 2007; Schubert & Frischkorn, 2020 ). These methods rely on the veracity of the cognitive models. To gain trust in the veracity of a model, researchers ensure that cognitive models describe the data well, that models predict data from new experiments, and that the parameters respond to experimental manipulations in the way it is expected given their cognitive interpretation, for example in selective influence studies. In this work we analyze the Diffusion Model of rapid decision-making as a  measurement  model. Our aim is to extend its use for measuring reliable, stable, and theoretically interpretable individual measures, a goal of many researchers (e.g.,  Rey-Mermet et al., 2021; Rouder & Haaf, 2019 ).

Diffusion Decision Models  (DDMs; also known as  Drift-Diffusion Models ) are among the empirically best established models of cognitive processes ( Ratcliff, Smith, et al., 2016 ). DDMs describe simple decision-making via an accumulation of evidence to a threshold ( Ratcliff, 1978; Ratcliff, Smith, et al., 2016; Stone, 1960 ). These models describe data well from experiments with perceptual decision-making tasks (e.g.  van Ravenzwaaij et al., 2012 ), memory tasks (e.g.  Ratcliff, Sederberg, B and Smith, A and Childers, Russ, 2016 ), value-based decisions (e.g.  Krajbich et al., 2010 ), and others.

The advantages of DDMs are that they contain parameters that are interpretable as reflecting certain cognitive mechanisms or processes, which we can compare across participants and patient populations. This is in contrast to computational models that instantiate complex brain mechanistic behavior, that cannot easily be fit to data. This is also in contrast to models in explainable AI that can perform tasks but are very difficult to interpret.

However, drawing inference about a certain set of DDM parameters that we expect to vary over individuals is difficult due to unidentifiablity (i.e., an inverse problem). Specifically, simultaneously estimating drift rate  
                            δ 
                         , the criterion  
                            α 
                         , and the internal noise  
                            ς 
                          is not possible. At least one of these DDM parameters (typically the internal noise  
                            ς 
                         ) is therefore often ignored. In this work we further explore this problem of identifiablity, show how these parameters can be identified, and discuss implications for the identifiability of models in general.

Unidentifiability  is a property of a model such that the parameters cannot be estimated well when fitting a model to observed data. Conceptually this property arises when we can simulate a model from a set of parameters that generates simulated data, but we cannot obtain unique parameter estimates from any data of the same type (true or simulated) when the model is fit to data. As a result, there are multiple different sets of parameter estimates that are probable given the data. This is related, if not identical to, the “inverse problems” in some scientific fields, for example astronomy ( Ellerbroek & Vogel, 2009 ). The term “ unidentifiable ” often describes statistical models, and is therefore the term we will use in this paper, whereas the latter term, “inverse problem”, often describes deterministic models but can describe both types of models in certain scientific fields ( Tarantola, 2006 ).

This identifiability problem means that, in practice, one of the three parameters,  
                            δ 
                         ,  
                            α 
                         , or  
                            ς 
                         , needs to be set to a constant, fixing the measurement scale, so that the other two can be estimated relative to it. This raises the question of what happens if the cognitive process described by the parameter that we choose to fix actually varies between individuals, or between situations. As that variance is expressed in the choice-RT data, inevitably, other model parameters will capture it — but which ones, and how? This is not immediately clear from the likelihood model, because the shape of the likelihood over parameter values (and thus the posterior distribution) in multidimensional space depends on the observed data. In the first part of this paper, we explore this question through simulation.

Most researchers who use DDMs in individual differences research make one of the following assumptions. Assumption (1): The diffusion coefficient is a normalizing constant that is not relevant to understanding individual differences in human cognition. Assumption (2): While not perfect due to the unidentifiability of DDMs, parameter estimates of  
                            α 
                          and  
                            δ 
                          reflect true individual differences in the underlying individuals’ cognition. Assumption (2) is not an unreasonable due to experiments of selective influence ( Dutilh et al., 2019; Voss et al., 2004 ). Assumption (1), however, is not appropriate, as we show below.

In practice, both assumptions result in the diffusion coefficient being used as the scaling constant, usually fixed to  
                            1 
                          or 0.1. This is also built into most packages for fitting DDMs to data (e.g.,  Voss & Voss, 2007; Wabersich & Vandekerckhove, 2014; Wagenmakers et al., 2007 ). In some work the boundary has been fixed in order to estimate individual differences in the diffusion coefficient (e.g.  Nunez et al., 2015, 2017 ). Furthermore, a reparameterization of the DDM has been suggested to fix the boundary relative to the diffusion coefficient ( Boehm et al., 2021 ). Later in this paper, we discuss the circumstances under which all three parameters can be estimated freely, potentially solving the unidentifiability problem if additional data is available.

In summary, our findings demonstrate that reparameterizing standard diffusion model parameters into derived quantities such as the Noise-to-Criterion Ratio (NCR) and Signal-to-Criterion Ratio (SCR) can reveal stronger relationships with individual differences in cognitive abilities. Specifically, while the association between the Criterion-to-Noise Ratio (CNR) and cognitive abilities in the standard parameterization was modest, the relationships involving the NCR and SCR in the alternative parameterization were notably stronger. These results underscore the interpretational flexibility of the diffusion model and highlight the importance of carefully considering parameter tradeoffs (e.g., drift rate, boundary separation, and internal noise) when linking model parameters to external variables. Consequently, when external covariates related to specific model parameters are unavailable, researchers can still gain valuable insights into internal noise processes by leveraging derived quantities like NCR and SCR, which are straightforward to compute from behavioral data using software such as EZ-Diffusion ( van Ravenzwaaij et al., 2017; Wagenmakers et al., 2007 ) or fast-dm ( Voss & Voss, 2007 ).

We also obtained posterior distributions of parameters from various models using the Python package BayesFlow ( Radev et al., 2020, 2023 ). This package allowed us to obtain joint posterior distributions of parameters without having to formulate their explicit likelihood equations. The package BayesFlow uses Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) to learn a mapping function from data to parameter sets, based on data simulated from the model. On that basis, the trained ANNs can infer the posterior distribution of parameter sets from observed data. We used this method for two model classes, those that did not have clear tractable likelihoods, e.g., joint models of choice-RT and external covariates on single-trials following the framework by  Ghaderi-Kangavari et al. (2023) , as well as those models that we expected to be classically unidentifiable to avoid potential convergence problems with MCMC samplers (see following paragraphs). We used the BayesFlow ANN standard of a summary network attached to an invertible network. These ANNs were trained for 500 epochs of experience replay training with 1000 iterations per epoch and batch sizes of 32. Convergence was judged post-hoc by observing the loss value reaching an asymptote well before the end of the 500 epochs. Further details about simulation-based Bayesian inference and the BayesFlow package are found in the papers by  Radev et al. (2020, 2023) .

Where possible we attempted to fit similar, if not identical models, in JAGS and Stan. If these models converged, we report their results, otherwise we rely on model fits from BayesFlow.

Any unidentified model still has a posterior distribution of its parameters. In the extreme case, when the data yield no information about the parameter values, the joint posterior distribution will equal the joint prior distribution (reflecting no gain in information about the parameters from the data). MCMC posterior estimation algorithms can struggle to appropriately identify the posterior distributions of unidentified models because obtaining convergent chains and/or assessing convergence can be difficult (e.g. see  Betancourt, 2017 ). However, when successful, finding posterior distributions of classically unidentifiable models gives us an idea of the true posterior space. For example, we acquired estimates of joint posteriors of classically unidentified DDMs when no parameter was fixed as a scaling constant and only choice-RT data are used to inform parameter estimates. As a result, we determined the extent to which information about drift rate, boundary, and diffusion coefficients can be obtained from only choice-RTs without additional assumptions (see  Fig. 4 ).

One thing that is apparent from  Fig. 2  is that observing information about the scale of evidence (the  
                            y

Nonetheless, there is work that suggests that certain neural measures reflect the evidence scale itself (e.g.  Lui et al., 2021; Nunez et al., 2024; O’Connell et al., 2018; Shadlen & Kiani, 2013 ). Here we present modeling work that shows that the three parameters can indeed be jointly identified if a trial-to-trial external covariate, which is correlated with one of the parameters – namely the boundary  
                            α 
                          – informs parameter estimation. This additional information enables the identification of the other model parameters. Though the methods come with important caveats, discussed below.

According to previous research, there are promising data types as candidates for the measure of the evidence scale in human electrophysiology. Specifically, promising EEG measures include: Event-Related Potential (ERP) measures time-locked to the onset of visual stimuli ( Nunez et al., 2017; O’Connell et al., 2012, 2018 ), ERP measures time-locked to the motor response of participants ( Gluth et al., 2013; Lui et al., 2021 ), EEG power-band measures ( Cavanagh et al., 2011; Frank et al., 2015 ), and ERP measures time-locked to correct and error responses on the preceding trial ( Mattes et al., 2022 ). In addition, the firing rate behavior of neurons in the LIP and superior colliculus in Rhesus macaques suggests that observing evidence-accumulation scales directly is achievable in animal electrophysiological recordings ( Jun et al., 2021; Shadlen & Kiani, 2013 ). fMRI BOLD may also yield indicators of the scale of evidence, as indicated by correlations between certain BOLD activation indices and response boundary ( Forstmann et al., 2008 ). Technically, external covariates for model identification purposes can be any data type, such as physiological or behavioral data, besides choices and response times. However, we know of no other promising data candidate that encodes information about the evidence scale other than neural data.

It is clear from the simulations that while researchers typically fix the diffusion coefficient in evidence accumulation models, the diffusion coefficient is not just a constant for fixing the scale of the other parameters. Rather, it has a substantive interpretation as the degree of noise in information processing, and as such it is of interest to ask how it is affected by experimental manipulations, and how it relates to other variables in individual differences investigations (e.g. see  Garrett et al., 2013; Kaur et al., 2021 ). It is not obvious that one parameter should be preferred to be fixed over the others. For instance, we might prefer a parameterization of the DDM that gives the least correlation in the joint posterior space. The joint posterior distribution from one simulation of Model dcDDM in  Fig. 4  shows that the posterior correlation between the boundary and drift rate is slightly higher than the correlation between the diffusion coefficient and the drift rate. Hence, if the aim is to minimize parameter trade-offs, the boundary might be fixed in models instead of the diffusion coefficient. However, no parameter is clearly preferred to be fixed across all model simulations (see also  Figs. 8 ,  9 , and  10  in the  Appendix ).

In our view, a more important concern for cognitive models of evidence accumulation is that the reality of the diffusion process in brain and cognition may be such that all three parameters—drift rate, boundary, and internal noise—change across individuals and experimental conditions. Changes in one or two of the parameters across participants or conditions can be estimated when assuming another parameter is the same across participants or conditions ( Donkin et al., 2009 ), but assuming a parameter is the same across participants seems unreasonable. We should thus aim to estimate all three parameters.

When applying DDMs to investigate individual differences, we recommend that researchers begin by explicitly considering the assumptions inherent in their chosen parameterization. Because drift rate, boundary, and diffusion coefficient cannot be estimated independently from choice-RT data alone, it is essential to clarify which parameter is fixed and why. We advise comparing multiple parameterizations to see how interpretations of ability- and caution-related differences might shift when the parameterization changes. For instance, if drift rate and boundary are freely estimated and the diffusion coefficient is fixed, then differences in estimated drift rates and boundaries could reflect differences in internal noise. We suggest that researchers regularly compute and report derived ratios, such as the Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR = drift/diffusion) and the Criterion-to-Noise Ratio (CNR = boundary/diffusion), as well as the Signal-to-Criterion Ratio (SCR = drift/boundary) and Noise-to-Criterion Ratio (NCR = diffusion/boundary). Presenting these ratios, rather than focusing only on parameters fixed by convention, allows for a richer interpretation of individual differences: Is a group’s higher estimated drift rate actually signaling lower internal noise rather than genuinely faster evidence accumulation? Is a purported difference in caution best understood as changes in how signal and noise interact, rather than a shift in the decision threshold alone? Such comparisons provide a more nuanced view of how distinct cognitive processes – evidence strength, internal variability, and decision criteria – contribute to performance. Furthermore, whenever possible, researchers should incorporate external covariates that bear on evidence scaling (e.g., neural measures) to help break identifiability constraints and directly link model parameters to measurable individual differences. Such integrative approaches can yield clearer guidelines for interpreting parameter contrasts and deepen our understanding of the cognitive and neurobiological mechanisms underlying performance variability.

We expect our modeling procedures to be informative for individual differences research, but not reveal true computational mechanisms of the brain. One example is that a speed-accuracy tradeoff could be instantiated in the brain as an increase/decrease in both signal and noise, such as described in Section  2.1  when drift rate  
                            δ

                            ς

                            α

Therefore, while the model would be useful to understand individual differences in the parameter triad across individuals for psychometrics, it would not give us information to differentiate the  gain hypothesis  ( Standage, Blohm, & Dorris, 2014 ) from other computational mechanisms of speed-accuracy tradeoff.

We focused on particular methods and models that produced promising results after testing various methods and models. These methods could also be extended to assuming non-linear relationships or embedded computational models of neural data, as long as the entire model parameter inference is restricted by the extra data. However these methods would require extensive testing, similar to the model recovery and misspecification tests we performed. We also expect other methods to be able to shrink the unidentified space, such as clever uses of hierarchical models. However, we were not able to find promising methods in our own work, other than the ones described in this paper.

At the core of the Diffusion Decision Model (DDM) is a triad of parameters that cannot be jointly identified. The pragmatic solution is to choose one of them arbitrarily and fix it so that it serves to scale the other two parameters. We find this practice unsatisfying because all three parameters represent a variable of theoretical interest – the speed of evidence accumulation, the noisiness of the evidence, and the caution of decision making – which could vary between experimental conditions and between individuals. We present alternative ways of interpreting parameter estimates from the DDM in terms of ratios of these true variables. We also identified ways to leverage additional data to estimate all three parameters separately. Future work should further apply these methods to additional real datasets.
