FALMAGNE  University of Brussels, Brussels, Belgium  Choice reaction times are analyzed on the basis of a simple model in which RT's are drawn at random from one of two ideal  distributions. The two distributions correspond'to different states of preparation. Loosely speaking, the subject learns to  expect certain stimuli and not others. Accordingly he draws his response from one or the other of two ideal distributions  depending on whether or not he is prepared for the stimulus that is presented. The probability of this choice is determined by  the sequence of stimuli prior to the response. The proposed model specifies a statistical learning process connecting the  preparatory states to stimuli, and a sampling scheme that generates the RT distributions from the preparatory states. The  model attempts to account for the relation between RT and stimulus probability, as well as for a variety of sequential effects  found in reaction times. An experiment on choice RT is described. The model is applied in detail, parameters are estimated,  and the outcome is subjected to critical scrutiny.  1. Terms such as expectancy,  attention, preparatory set, distraction (a psychological state in an individual under experimentation, prior to the appearance  of a stimulus, and affecting the latency of his response) have appeared frequently in the literature on RT. In some cases,  such a concept is postulated in order to explain awkward facts such as the appearance of uncontrolled variations. For example,  in experiments on simple RT to signals of varying physical character, one of the "betes noires" has always been the wide  dispersion of the readings. It is worth noticing that a similar concept appears in another recent model (Welford, 1960), and is not  incompatible with the models of Stone (1960) and Laming (1962). In fact, a model of RT which does not include an inherent  mechanism for selective preparation, or anticipation of the choice of response, would have difficulty in explaining  experimental results to date. These results, which should be accounted for by any RT model, will now be surveyed briefly (for  a detailed discussion see Falmagne (1961)).  Speed-accuracy conflict. When the RT diminishes, the probability of an incorrect response increases (Hick, 1952).  The number of possible stimuli. The RT increases with an increase in the number of stimuli and responses. If the subject is  asked to give specific responses to n stimuli presented in random sequence, his mean reaction time will increase with n. The  experimental data can be roughly described by functions of the type  RT = a + b log n (Hyman, 1953; Krulee and Sinclair, 1953;  or  Crossman, 1953)  RT = a log (n -f- 1) (Hick, 1952).  Unequal stimulus (or response) frequencies. If the various stimuli are presented with unequal frequencies, a differential  effect appears among the reaction times to the different stimuli. For example, in a situation in which there are only two  possible signals, A and B, each requiring a particular response, if p(A) > p(B), then RT(A) < T(B) (Hyman, 1953).  Sequential effects. The RT to a signal depends on the immediate past of the subject in the experimental situation. Bertelson  (1961) has shown that in a serial binary choice situation, the mean RT's were significantly lower for repeated signals than  for alternated signals. This decrease in RT when the stimulus is identical with the preceding  CHOICE REACTION TIME 79  stimulus, has been termed "the repetition effect". Also Hyman (1953) showed that even with relatively long intertrials  intervals, RT increases with the number of other stimulus events intervening between the stimulus given and the preceding  presentation of the same stimulus. In Hyman's situation, this "recency effect" holds when the total number of stimuli exceeds  two. These results emphasize the importance of the role played by immediately preceding stimulus-response couples in  determining an RT to a stimulus.2  The effects of unequal frequencies, and of sequences imply a memory function. At the nth trial, the subject must have a memory  of the preceding events which influence his RT at this trial. The stored information produces a specific orientation towards  those stimuli (or responses), which are more probable or which were presented at the (n - 1)st or even somewhat earlier trial.  The effect of this trace of the past will be labeled preparation. This preparation will be selective, implying an anticipated  choice of the response.  A. Why a New model?  RT models typically take it for granted that the subject learns from practicing at the experimental task. What this learning  comprises is not made clear. For example, the subjects may have "learned" the probabilities of the various stimuli. This  general view, however, leaves a number of questions unanswered,  How does the subject evaluate the probabilities of the various stimuli? One might think, perhaps, that these probabilities are  estimated from the relative frequencies f /n. In that case, what is the value of n, i.e., what is the memory span of the  subject in a task of this type ? Are the probabilities estimated from samples of 500 preceding signals, or of five ? How is  this information stored ? How is it altered if the different probabilities of presentation are suddenly modified? Is the  immediate past more important than the more distant past ? These questions lead us to observe that the four phenomena that we  have already mentioned (speed-accuracy conflict, the effect of the number of stimuli, the effect of unequal probabilities, and  sequential effects) are not necessarily independent. In particular, the connections between the last three are evident.  When the probability of a stimulus is high, or when the total number of stimuli is small, there are more frequent repetitions,  and one can predict shorter RT's simply from consideration of the sequential effects. This interdependence has an appealing  simplicity. It suggests that a model might be constructed based solely on sequential effects. The system should permit the  continual storage of these effects piece by piece, and should assume a progressive process of forgetting.  2 This list of facts omits the relations involving information statistics. Two Problems Resulting from a Model of Preparation  Two decisions were required preliminary to elaborating a model of RT based on the concept of preparation; the first is  concerned with the selectivity of preparation, the second is its "all-or-nothing" character.  Selectivity of preparation. Let Ki, be the level of preparation of a subject on the nth trial, with respect to signal i, at  the moment of signal presentation. The Ki, determines, with some random variation, the RT on this trial, if signal i is in  fact presented. More precisely, the "random" variation to which we are referring depends neither on preceding recorded events  (series of stimuli and responses) nor on the moments at which they are produced. Another decision concerned the possible values of Ki,n . Should this variable be  assumed continuous or discrete ? If the latter, it might, for example, take only two possible values. In this case,  preparation would be an all-or-nothing process. Either the subject would be prepared for a signal i or he would be unprepared.  I know of no argument, based on experimental results, by which to decide our choice. There are only those subjective  impressions which would suggest a preference for all or nothing, such as: "One cannot think of two things at the same time  ...". In any case, one  can always revert back to a continuous model, if it is suggested by the approximations obtained from an all-or-nothing model.  A model will now be presented, which is intended to cover the following range of experimental situations.  The instructions require the subject to make very rapid responses to successive stimuli. After each response, the following  stimulus is presented. Any one of r different stimuli can be presented, and there are r corresponding responses. The  frequencies of presentation of these r stimuli can be unequal. The subject is urged to avoid errors. In practice, one can  obtain very low error scores, of the order of 2 or 3 %. For simplicity, it will be assumed that the subject makes no errors.  2. THE MODEL  A. Basic Concepts and Definitions  The basic concepts of the model will be presented in this section. This experiment was undertaken with two general objectives in view. One was to determine, in the light of the work of Hick  (1952), Hyman (1953), Crossman (1953), Krulee and Sinclair (1953), Laming (1962), Brebner and Gordon (1964), Stone and  Callaway (1964), and others, how the RT to a stimulus is influenced by its objective probability. A more particular objective  was to explain, using the model presented, the apparent effect of probability on RT, in terms of the sequential eiTects  indicated by the work of Hyman (1953) and Bertelson (1961).  These objectives guided the choice of apparatus, experimental technique, and plan of experimentation which will now be  described.  A. Method  Subjects. Seven subjects, aged 20-30, were employed. They were university research workers or students, and had all undergone  some months before a fairly long psychopharmacological experiment, using a similar experimental setup, with four responses to  four equiprobable stimuli.  Apparatus. The experimental setting was located in an isolated room. The subject was seated in an armchair, facing a grey  vertical panel. The stimulus source was a Nixie type cold cathode lamp fixed on the panel. The subject's hands were placed on  a keyboard with three fingers of each hand (annular, major and index) resting on keys. The digits 1-6 could appear at the  level of the lamp, each presentation being governed by a tape programmer. The control apparatus and the experimenter were in a  second room. A ventilator functioned continuously, the dull noise furnishing the subject with a convenient auditory isolation.  The keys were relay arms. The response pressure required on them were of the order of 90-100 g. These values were independent  of the key considered. After each finger press on a key, there was a lapse of 740-780 msec. before the presentation of the  next stimulus. (A certain variability is inherent in the apparatus. These values, however, were fairly stable from key to key  and also during the course of the experiment.) For each trial, a device recorded the stimulus presented, the response made,  and the latency of the response.  Design. The experiment was divided into two phases: a preliminary experiment (Phase I, using 7 subjects), and the main  experiment (Phase 11 using 6 subjects). Consider, first, Phase II. One could not suppose that the finger used for the response  or the stimulus digit, had no influence on the latency of the response. An attempt was therefore made to control these factors  by employing a 6 x 6 x 6 Latin square design. The variables were subjects, probabilities and finger-digit combinations. The  relation of finger-digit was kept constant throughout the experiment and was the same for all the subjects. It was thus  important to control its influence. The relation was the most compatible (in Fitts' sense) allowed by the experimental setup,  i.e., the left annular corresponded to digit 1, the left major to digit 2, etc. This design assumes the absence of any  interaction between the variables. For the experimental situation envisaged, this was a strong hypothesis. It seemed useful to  test the most important of these interactions during a preliminary experiment (Phase I). In order to test the interaction  "subject" x finger-digit", the third variable was kept constant: all the stimuli were equiprobable.  Procedure. The two phases of the experiment required eight sessions. Sessions 1 and 2 were devoted to Phase I; Sessions 3-8,  which began roughly 15 days after session 2, were for  CHOICE REACTION TIME IOI  Phase II. Each session consisted of two series of 500 stimuli, with a five minutes rest between. The procedures were:  Sessions 1, 2: the six stimuli were equiprobable and randomly presented.  Session 3: 1st series of 500 signals: all the signals were equiprobable; 2nd series of 500 signals: 100 equiprobable signals  were presented, followed without pause by 400 signals of differing probabilities (0.01, 0.03, 0.06, 0.10, 0.24, 0.56). These  probabilities were identical for all subjects. The series of unequiprobable signals were also randomly presented.  Sessions 4-8: Two series of 500 signals, biased according to the probabilities given above, were presented at each session.  Each subject, therefore, gave 8000 responses in the course of the whole experiment. Within each phase, the sessions were  separated by intervals of one or two days. No two sessions took place on the same day. The stimulus sequences were the same  for all subjects and were presented in the same order. The instructions enjoined the subject to respond as quickly as  possible, and emphasized the need to avoid errors. He was also advised to take advantage of the rest intervals in order to  recuperate. Before each series, the subject was informed of his previous results, in terms of his total time, and error score.  About a 3 % error score was obtained for all the subjects. The RT's for incorrect responses were eliminated from the results.  B. Phase I. Preliminary Fxperiment (Sessions 1 and 2)  During this phase, the six stimuli were equiprobable. Discussion and Conclusions  Two questions of different degrees of generality will be dealt with in this discussion.  (1) To what extent has the particular model described above succeeded in the prediction and interpretation of the data from  the experiment on serial choice RT's ? What are the limits of the model and what alterations could be introduced to give  better predictions ?  (2) To what extent does this type of model and the concepts behind it prove useful for understanding the phenomena under study?  The answers to these questions will lead to a discussion of the problems that arise from such an approach and of the  experiments it suggests.  We will discuss separately the results concerning the predictions for the means and those concerning the predictions for the  higher moments of the distributions, since the latter are purely qualitative. OPEN PROBLEMS. SUGGESTED EXPERIMENTS  Constant Number of Stimuli  One of the first questions raised leads to the following suggested experiment. Variable Number of Stimuli  The above hypothesis will probably have to be partially rejected if we vary the number of stimuli, for example with a  probability vector in which all the elements are equal. As Brebner and Gordon write (1964, p. 59): "When ensemble size is  increased, and, as is usually the case, all items are presented with equal frequency, the stimulus situation is changed in at  least two ways. Firstly, more separate categories requiring identification are presented to the subject, and, secondly, the  probability that a particular response will be required is reduced. The slowing observed with larger ensembles in choice  response situation could, then, be due either to reduced signal probability or to an increase on the number of categories to  be indentified, or to an interaction of both effects."  Ignoring the last and more complicated suggestion, the following hypothesis could be made: the parameters of the model are  varying according to some unknown functions, with the difficulty of identification, i.e., with the number of stimuli and  responses.
