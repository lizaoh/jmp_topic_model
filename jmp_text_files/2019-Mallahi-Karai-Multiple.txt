Dynamic models of decision making typically are based on the general assumption that decisions arise as a result of accumulating information over time until a decision criterion (threshold) is met. This idea matches squarely a “biological fact” (natural construct) of evidence accumulation and threshold. These models, often called sequential sampling models and formalized as stochastic processes, have been applied to various psychological research areas and beyond, foremost involving binary choice options, one being correct and the other being incorrect (e.g.,  Diederich & Mallahi-Karai, 2018; Gold & Shadlen, 2007; Laming, 1968; Luce, 1986; Ratcliff & Smith, 2004; Ratcliff, Smith, Brown, & McKoon, 2016; Smith, 2000; Townsend & Ashby, 1983  for overviews). More recently, these models have been extended to preferential choice situations ( Busemeyer & Townsend, 1993 ) with several attributes ( Diederich, 1997 ) and three choice alternatives (e.g.,  Howes, Warren, Farmer, El-Deredy, & Leweis, 2016; Roe, Busemeyer, & Townsend, 2001; Usher & McClelland, 2004; Wollschläger & Diederich, 2012, 2019  for an overview). In this article, the first of two theoretical notes, we focus on the latter application only and present a number of related models for choice with an arbitrary number  
                         
                            d 
                            ≥ 
                            2 
                         
                       of alternatives. They are based on a Wiener process in a  
                         d

In the current, paper we introduce a model, called the cube model, for making a choice between any number of alternatives and derive some of its properties. The second paper is devoted to a model, called disk model, that can be viewed as a modification of the cube model involving three alternatives. Although the disk model has a more limited applicability, it has the advantage that it can be analyzed more easily.

The paper is organized as follows. Section  2  sets the notation and introduces the mathematical objects used in the remaining of the paper. Section  3  gives a detailed description of the cube model and discusses some of its features and variations. In particular, we study the model in the presence of two alternatives, and compare it with standard models. Section  4  shows a modification of the cube model to account for a fall-back option in the choice set. Finally, in Section  5 , we briefly compare this model with other existing models and propose directions for further research. Note however, the comparison is with respect to the  structure  of the models, not about the assumptions how preferences are mapped onto the drift rate.

Models for decision making considered here assume that information is accumulated in discrete or continuous time, i.e., information is sampled sequentially over time. The process has a deterministic as well as a stochastic (noisy) component and is formalized as a stochastic process. To set the stage for the following and fix notation, we briefly recap the basic definitions for stochastic processes in continuous time with two absorbing boundaries. We refer to models based on this process as the  standard binary model .

The accept and reject walls are designed to capture these two sub-processes. In the present paper, we proposed a new model for decision making with multiple alternatives, called cube model. It belongs to the class of sequential sampling models, assuming that the decision making process can be described by using one or several accumulation processes, combined with some noise. A choice is made and a response initiated as soon as the accumulation process exceeds a certain level or decision criterion. The main feature of the cube model is its assumption of accept and reject walls associated with each alternative. The decision maker may choose an alternative only after several episodes of deliberation. For instance, the decision maker may reject all alternatives but one and take this remaining one by default as in Elimination by Aspects ( Tversky, 1972 ). In this case, the choice is made after  
                         
                            d 
                            − 
                            1 
                         
                       episodes, where  
                         d 
                       indicates the number of alternative in the choice set. The other extreme occurs when the decision maker chooses an alternative immediately upon hitting an accept boundary. In this case, the choice is made after only one episode. More typically, the decision maker may reject some of the alternatives before accepting one. We obtained semi-analytic solutions for some special cases of this model. One of the novelties of this model is that the noise components driving the Wiener processes can be correlated, and the correlation is determined by the matrix  
                         Σ 
                      . The dependence structure between the evidence is captured by the drift vector  
                         μ 
                      . In this regard, the model can be viewed as the first model of this kind.

The cube model bears some similarities but also has some important differences with some of the existing models. For example, in both multi-attribute DFT ( Diederich, 1997 ) and the cube model attributes are processed one at a time. Note, however, that MDFT is only for binary choice situations. In multi-alternative DFT ( Roe et al., 2001 ), the LCA model ( Usher & McClelland, 2004 ), and the 2NCT model ( Wollschläger & Diederich, 2012 ) the information about all attributes is combined, which then updates the preference state. The order in which attributes may be considered is irrelevant to these models and therefore, cannot account for any effects arising from presenting attribute information, for instance, sequentially.

In the cube model, it is possible to accumulate evidence in favor (or against) two (or more) alternatives simultaneously. This is in contrast to multi-attribute DFT, in which the accumulation of evidence is relative, that is, any evidence for one alternative is inevitably evidence against the others. Moreover, the cube model in its versions described above is a decision model with  alternative-wise  comparisons, whereas in multi-alternative DFT a decision is based on  attribute-wise  comparisons. In conclusion, studying the cube model with more than two main alternatives is mathematically challenging. For applications, one may go back to simulations (e.g.  Usher & McClelland, 2004 ) or semi-closed form approaches such as Markov chains (e.g.,  Diederich & Busemeyer, 2003 ). A further direction is to find other geometric models that are both rich enough to be able to model all the psychological parameters that play a role in decision making, and are amenable to computational operations. In a subsequent paper ( Diederich & Mallahi-Karai, 2019 ), we will pursue this direction, and also address the question of the equivalence of models.
