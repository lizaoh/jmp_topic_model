JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 10, 204-222 (1973)  An Hypothesis Generation Model for Judgment in Nonmetric Multiple-Cue Probability Learning'  N. JOHN CASTELLAN, JR.' AND STEPHEN E. EDGELL  Indiana University, Bloomington, Indiana 47401  The purpose of this paper is to present a two-phase hypothesis generation model to describe behavior in multiple-cue  probability learning tasks with nonmetric cues. The model assumes that on each trial the subject generates two sets of  hypotheses: (a) a hypothesis concerning which cue dimension (or pattern) will lead to a correct prediction on that trial and  (b) a hypothesis concerning which response will be correct given the cue dimension attended to on that trial.  Five-hundred-twelve subjects were assigned to 20 groups in a binary choice task involving two binary cue dimensions. Each  group observed cues which differed in validity. Analysis of the data indicated that subjects attend to both cue dimensions in  making judgments even when one cue has zero validity. A test of the fit of the observed data to the asymptotic response  proportions predicted by the model indicated a reasonable fit.  Most early treatments of two-choice multiple-cue probability learning did not take into account the dimensional  characteristics of the cues such as shape, size, etc. (e.g., Popper and Atkinson, 1958; Atkinson, 1960; Lee, 1966; Bj6rkman,  1967; Kroll, 1970). In these situations all cues (usually only one binary cue) are considered to be on one dimension only-that  is, only one value of the cue is presented on each trial. A few recent studies have included the dimensionality of the cues as  an important part of the research design (e.g., Castellan, 1973). Other treatments (e.g., Summers, 1962; Hammond and Summers,  1965) consider there to be several cue dimensions; each cue within a dimension can take on several values. It is the purpose  of this paper to outline a model which describes judgment in multiple-cue learning with dimensional cues. An important question is whether cues in this sort of situation are combined in some linear fashion or whether responding is  based upon some sort of patterning of cues by the subject. Much discussion of similar generalization problems indicates that  subjects are configural in some situations but not in other situations. Further, quantitative models of this problem have  tended to become unmanageable when model parameters are introduced to deal with configural or pattern responding (cf.,  Atkinson, 1960; Lee, 1966). One purpose of this research is to determine whether simpler models for performance in  multiple-cue tasks are feasible and whether they are worth exploiting in considerable detail.  Since the outcome of each trial is such that the subject cannot be correct on every trial, it is reasonable to assume that  hypotheses concerning the relevance of the cues are continually revised or reconsidered. The model assumes that the subject  generates hypotheses concerning the relevance of the cue dimensions on each trial and selects one cue dimension as relevant on  a given trial, or possibly considers no cue relevant on that trial. An extension of the model will be described in which the  subject may entertain, in addition to hypotheses about each cue dimension, hypotheses that the cue pattern is relevant on a  given trial. This second model will be called Model II. It is assumed that the subject generates these hypotheses on each  trial and can do so as soon as there has been feedback from the previous trial. If a subject considers more than one dimension  to be relevant on a trial, he `reconsiders' or regenerates hypotheses until only one dimension remains. Once the subject has  hypothesized a cue dimension as relevant on a trial, he observes the cue values on that dimension during the current trial and  generates expectancies for the outcomes which are possible. In summary then, on each trial the subject first selects a cue  dimension (or no dimension) and then, having selected the cue to which attention will be given, generates a response on that  trial as a function of the actual cue value presented. Apparatus  Subjects were run in sets of four. The apparatus for the experiment was a Humphreys-type board with a row of two reinforcing  lights, El and E2 , a row of choice lights, and a row of choice buttons, A1 and A2. The lights and buttons were arranged in  two vertical columns on the board. In the middle of the Humphreys-type board, a single-plane IEE digital readout was mounted  for the purpose of displaying cues. All experimental control and monitoring was done by an IBM 1800 Data Acquisition System. When the validity of the second cue dimension is zero (r2 =  0), then that cue contains no information and should be ignored. Examination of the figures indicates that, in general, that  is the case. As one proceeds to the right in each figure (corresponding to an increase in the validity of the second cue  dimension) increased use of the second cue dimension occurs, as may be seen by noting that the response proportions P[Al C1C2]  and P[A1 I CC,'] begin to diverge as do the response proportions P[AI Cl'C2] and P[Al I C'C2']. There appears to be a  uniformly increasing tendency to use a cue as a function of its validity.  In Figs. 2b and 2c a 'cross-over' effect is noted at the point where rl = r2 . It is also present, but not immediately  obvious, in Fig. 2a. The effect is that when rl == r2 a great deal of ambiguity is generated, in that the elements of the cue  pairs Cl & C2' and Cl' & C2 are each contradictory (in that the components of the pattern each `point' to different  responses). In a real sense, the response strengths of each dimension should `cancel out,' and the subject should respond  randomly (or at least the responses across Subjects should be random). Then, as the validity of the second cue dimension  continues to increase after rl = r2 , there is a sudden reversal in response proportions since the second cue dimension is now  more valid than the first cue dimension. This crossover, however, is only an artifact resulting from the manner in which the  data are graphed in the figures.  The differential use of the second cue dimension results in a paradox when it is less reliable than the first cue dimension.  In the present experiment, the cue-event sequences were generated so that the correlation between cues would be small. (In a  few cases, the correlation was sizeable, and the correlation between cue dimensions becomes larger when the cue validities  were both large since an attempt was made to keep the base rates of each cue value close to one-half. The median correlation  between dimensions was rig = 0.15). Because of the low correlation, a reasonable strategy for subjects to employ in the task  is to pick the better cue dimension and use it exclusively. Such a strategy would maximize the number of correct predictions.  Examination of the figures suggests that there is no tendency for subjects to use such a strategy.  Except when the cue validities were both small, subjects tended to move beyond matching. However, there was no tendency for  subjects to maximize, except for subjects in groups where rl and r2 were equal to 0.8. However, in the latter case, matching  is extremely close to maximizing so that for all practical purposes, matching and maximizing are equivalent. Some groups obviously fit the models better than others.  It appears that the hypothesis generation model, and especially Model II, describes the data from the experiment reported here  rather well. Friedman, Rollins, and Padilla (1968) applied a very similar model to stimulus compounding with similar results.  It should be noted that the predicted response proportions and subsequent fits to data were obtained from a model in which no  parameters were estimated. If, instead of assuming that the subjects' expectancies concerning the appropriateness of a cue  dimension and the appropriateness of a cue value (the z's and x's, respectively) were equal to the conditional probabilities  of event given the cues (or cue patterns), as in the present prediction scheme, the scale values and weights (the s's and w's,  respectively) were estimated from the data, then the Models would fit the data even better. (Further, it is easy to show that  in such a case Model II will always fit the data better than Model 1.) However, at this point further research is needed to  determine the usefulness of Model II under other conditions, for example, in tasks in which there are unusual cue patterns,  and tasks in which there are more than two cue dimensions. While it would appear to be the case that Model II would do much  better than Model I when there are unusual cue patterns, it is possible that the fit of Model II will deteriorate as the  number of dimensions is increased. (However, if there is only one cue dimension, Model I and Model II make identical  predictions.) The Models presented here may also be tested in a variety of ways beyond using a fit of observed response  proportions to predicted response proportions. The hypothesis generation procedure developed in this paper also yields  predictions of response times to various cue patterns. If it is assumed that the number of hypotheses generated per unit of  time is constant, then one can estimate the response times to the various cue patterns in terms of the number of hypotheses  that must be generated in order to generate an appropriate hypothesis which allows a response to be made.
