Although data fits became progressively worse, and parameter estimates became more deviant as the frequency of event  alternations increased, the model does account for several findings not easily encompassed by alternative choice models.  Gambino and Myers (1967) have recently presented a model for binary prediction behavior based on the assumption that runs of  events serve as discriminative stimuli. The model holds that discriminations among these stimuli are imperfect; thus,  reinforcement of an expectancy about one run length may generalize to reinforce expectancies about other run lengths.  Specifically, the model assumes a vector whose typical entry, pn(j), represents the subjective probability on trial n that a  run of length j will continue. Assuming that the subject is aware that the current stimulus is a run of length m, the  probability that he will predict a repetition of the immediately preceding event is pn(m). If the run of length m continues on  trial n, pn(m) is incremented. To a lesser extent, all other p,,(j)s are also incremented, the amount of generalized increment  decreasing with the distance of any given run length j from m, the run length which has just continued. If the run of length m  breaks off, all expectancies are decremented in a manner determined by the postulated generalization gradient. The model can also predict anticipatory and perseverative  errors in partially learnable sequences (Gambino and Myers, 1966; Restle, 1966; Rose and Vitz, 1966). Anticipatory errors are  failures to predict the preceding event when the probability of an event repetition is 1.0, and perseverative errors are  failures to predict the alternative event when the probability of an event switch is 1.0. Thus, in a sequence composed solely  of runs of length two and five, anticipatory errors could occur following runs of length one, three and four, and  perseverative errors could occur following runs of length five. These error measures are of particular theoretical import  because of their sensitivity to sequence structure (Gambino and Myers, 1966) and because previous models which viewed runs as  the critical stimuli were unable to predict the occurrence of such errors (Restle, 1966).  Gambino and Myers (1967) tested the generalization model against data they had obtained earlier (1966) and against Restle's  (1966) data. The model adequately described changes in run curves (response probabilities plotted as a function of run length)  over trials, decreases in anticipatory errors with increases in mean run length, and increases in perseverative errors with  increases in variability of run lengths. Furthermore, the basic learning-rate parameter, 9, exhibited little fluctuation over  experimental conditions, while the generalization parameter, y, varied in a systematic and predictable manner. The one  exception was a deviant estimate of B for an approximation of one sequence of Restle's, which was composed of equal numbers of  runs of length one and four. Gambino and Myers hypothesized that the presence of single alternations in the event sequence  might be responsible for the aberrant parameter estimate. Because the generalization model assumes that subjects encode the  event sequence in terms of run lengths, either a poor data fit or a deviant parameter estimate might be expected if the  subject actually attends to other patterns.  The present experiment had two purposes: first, to explore the role of event alternations in order to better assess one  possible limitation of the model and to provide, hopefully, some basis for revision; secondly, to investigate further the  effects of distribution of run lengths upon binary prediction behavior and determine whether these effects can be explained  within the generalization framework. To these ends, sequences were constructed from runs of lengths four and five, or one and  five, with relative frequencies of the two lengths varying over experimental conditions.  METHOD  Design. Runs were either of lengths 1 and 4 or of 4 and 5, and proportion of long runs, S, was either .2, .5, or .8. Predicted Data  In fitting the data, event sequences were read into a CDC 3600 computer, together with the observed probabilities of  predicting the continuation of runs of length one through five for each of the six trial blocks. Expectancies for each of the  five run lengths were initialized by setting them equal to the observed proportion of subjects predicting that each run length  would continue on the first occasion that it occurred. Trial parameter values were selected according to a computer search  routine developed by Chandler (1965). The expectancy vector was transformed on each trial in accord with the Gambino-Myers  axioms.  Two models were tested. Model I is the model originally proposed by Gambino and Myers (1967). Model II is a variation  developed in order to test whether the fit could be improved by assuming that different degrees of generalization follow from  the continuation and breaking off of runs. Therefore, the parameter y in Model I is replaced by y, and yb in Model II,  parameters for generalization from the continuation and the breaking off of a run, respectively. The reported parameter values  are those which minimized the sum of squared deviations (SS) of observed from predicted probabilities. In computing the SS for  each experimental condition, squared deviations at each point in the run curves were weighted by the relative frequency with  which the corresponding run length occurred, and were averaged over trial blocks and sequences within each condition. In the 4-5 condition, response probabilities do not seem to depend on whether  the preceding run was long or short. CONCLUDING REMARKS  The present investigation lends strong support to the hypotheses that event runs are discriminative cues for prediction and  that a gradient of confusion exists, with maximum confusion at those runs closest in length to the run length sampled on a  given trial. The fits of the Gambino-Myers model provide part of the evidence for these hypotheses, but even more compelling  evidence is provided by several results which are difficult to account for within any other framework. Such results include  the findings that both anticipatory and perseverative errors are more frequent in the 1-5 than in the 4-5 condition, that this  difference is most marked when the proportion of long runs is lowest, and that run statistics vary as a function of the length  of the preceding run in a manner that depends on both the length of the current run and the stage of practice.  The models tested in this paper rest on the assumption that generalization is the basic mechanism underlying the gradient of  confusion; it is assumed that the subject always knows the current run length, but, depending on whether that run continues or  breaks off, a generalized increment or decrement accrues to response probabilities associated with other run lengths. The  results would be equally well accounted for by the assumption that the primary source of confusion is miscounting, that is,  that the subject misperceives the current run length and that only the probability associated with a run of the perceived  length is influenced by the trial outcome. If it is assumed that the subject's count is most likely to be off by a small  amount, miscounting would produce results very similar to those that were attributed to generalization. The data of the  present experiment do not permit us to distinguish between the influences of generalization and miscounting; we are presently  attempting to do this by providing subjects with information as to the current run length on every trial.  These models also rest on the assumption that the subject encodes the event sequence in terms of runs. However, the fact that  data fits become progressively worse, and parameter estimates become more deviant, as the proportion of runs of length one  increases, suggests that subjects also encode event alternation lengths. If this is the case, there are several reasons why  the Gambino-Myers model would encounter difficulty. Consider a sequence in which there are many single alternations, few of  which are longer than length five. If the pattern 12121212 should occur, a subject  454 1MYERS, BUTLER, AND OLSON  operating in accord with the Gambino-Myers model would tend to make a " 1 " response because he has learned that runs of  length one rarely continue. A subject who discriminates alternation lengths would tend to make a "T' response because he has  learned that this pattern has a low probability of continuing.  To the extent that subjects encode alternation lengths, the Gambino-Myers model should have difficulty fitting response  probabilities for runs of length one. In addition, if generalization from single alternations to runs differs from  generalization among runs of various lengths, other points in the run curve may be poorly fit. Finally, encoding event runs  and event alternations would produce a heavier memory load for the subject than the simple generalization model implies. This,  too, might affect goodness-of-fit.  Gambino and Myers have attempted to deal with the alternation problem by proposing two independent expectancy vectors, one for  run lengths and one for alternation lengths. The fits of this model to the 1-5 data were not noticeably better than those of  Model II. This need not mean, however, that the assumption of a set of expectancies for each class of patterns is wrong.  Unfortunately, there are many choice points in extending the theory and the wrong path might have been chosen. For example,  the assumption that no generalization occurs between runs and alternations could be erroneous; it is possible that the  continuation of an alternation influences expectancies about runs, or that the breaking off of a run greater than one is  registered as an alternation. While alternative assumptions could be modeled, the variations appear endless, and thus,  discourage such attempts. It seems much more profitable to put our efforts into the design of experiments that will permit  more direct assessments of the different assumptions that might be entertained.  In summary, the generalization model is a first step toward a better account of the processes involved in binary choice. It  has two advantages over the stimulus sampling models which have dominated the field: reinforcement is defined in terms of a  pattern of events, the run, rather than in terms of the last event, and the concept of response interference, which previous  results clearly demanded, has been introduced. We now require data to provide the basis for tenable assumptions about  processes such as miscounting and encoding, and to enable us to better understand how patterns other than runs are processed,  and how several classes of patterns are simultaneously processed.
