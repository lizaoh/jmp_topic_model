Optimal models of various cognitive systems and associated behaviors have proliferated in recent years. Many, though not all, such models are rooted in the “rational analysis” approach to delineating the relevant environmental information and modeling the most efficient use of this information ( Anderson, 1991 ). Many, perhaps most, such models explicitly employ Bayesian statistical reasoning.

Bayesian models have been developed to account for data in a wide variety of cognitive areas, including, but not limited to, syntax acquisition ( Perfors, Tenenbaum, & Regier, 2011 ), word learning ( Xu & Tenenbaum, 2007 ), perceptual magnet effects ( Feldman, Griffiths, & Morgan, 2009 ), auditory and visual word recognition ( Norris, 2006; Norris & McQueen, 2008 ), audio–visual and visuo-haptic integration ( Burr, Banks, & Morrone, 2009; Ernst & Banks, 2002 ), visual perception ( Maloney & Mamassian, 2009; Weiss, Simoncelli, & Adelson, 2002 ), sensorimotor learning ( Körding & Wolpert, 2004 ), memory ( Xu & Griffiths, 2010 ), decision-making ( Steyvers, Lee, & Wagenmakers, 2009 ), causal inference ( Griffiths & Tenenbaum, 2005 ), inductive reasoning ( Kemp & Tenenbaum, 2009 ), analogical reasoning ( Holyoak, Lee, & Lu, 2010 ), prediction in “everyday cognition” ( Griffiths & Tenenbaum, 2006 ), cognitive development ( Shultz, 2007 ), and the evolution of cognitive systems in general ( Geisler & Diehl, 2003 ).

Naturally, given this wide variety of cognitive domains, the structure of these models varies considerably.  Marcus and Davis (2013)  argue that the presence of Bayes’ rule in a cognitive model does not, by itself, ensure optimality, and there has been some controversy regarding the explanatory power of Bayesian cognitive models in general ( Bowers & Davis, 2012; Jones & Love, 2011; Marcus & Davis, 2013 ), though we note that a thorough understanding of optimal models can provide valuable information regarding the efficiency of information processing, whether or not observed cognitive systems are, in fact, optimal (as in, e.g., ideal observer analysis;  Gold, Tadin, Cook, & Blake, 2008; Pelli, Farell, & Moore, 2003 ).

It has long been known that Bayesian models of classification are optimal (conditional on the assumptions of a given model) in the sense of minimizing classification error ( Fukunaga, 1990; Hastie, Tibshirani, & Friedman, 2009 ). In the present work, we analyze response selection in an optimal general recognition theory (GRT) model in a factorial stimulus identification task (defined below). More specifically, we provide the necessary and sufficient conditions for the Gaussian GRT model with an optimal (Bayesian) response selection rule to mimic decisional separability. These conditions also describe the cases in which GRT models with linear or piecewise linear decision bounds reflect optimal performance; in these models, failure of decisional separability is not uniquely identifiable ( Silbert & Thomas, 2013 ). We also briefly discuss the role of prior probabilities and various payoff schemes in optimal mimicry of decisional separability in GRT.

GRT (also known as multidimensional signal detection theory, or MDSDT) provides a mathematical framework and associated experimental protocol designed to study the relationships between dimensions in cognitive processing (e.g., perception, working memory;  Ashby & Townsend, 1986; DeCarlo, 2003; Hautus, Macmillan, & Rotello, 2008; Kadlec & Hicks, 1998; Kadlec & Townsend, 1992; Silbert, 2012; Thomas, 2001 ). Broadly speaking, it is a two-stage model consisting of noisy perception and deterministic response selection. Noisy perception produces latent perceptual distributions defined over a multidimensional perceptual space, which is, in turn, exhaustively partitioned by decision bounds to form mutually exclusive response regions. If a perceptual effect occurs in a given response region on a particular trial, the associated response is selected. The predicted probability of a particular response to a particular stimulus is calculated as the multiple integral of the corresponding perceptual distribution over the appropriate response region.

Although the model does not require specific  a priori  assumptions about the functional form of the perceptual distributions, they are often modeled as multivariate Gaussian density functions (e.g.   Olzak & Wickens, 1997; Silbert, 2012; Thomas, 2001 ). The specific form of the decision bounds also depends on prior assumptions; decision bounds are often assumed to be linear ( Silbert, Townsend, & Lentz, 2009 ) or piecewise linear ( Mack, Richler, Gauthier, & Palmeri, 2011; Mestry, Menneer, Wenger, & Donnelly, 2012 ).

Although the model extends, in principle, to an arbitrary number of dimensions and a set of perceptual distributions defined by the factorial combination of an arbitrary number of levels on each dimension, we restrict our attention here to the ‘standard’ GRT application, which consists of four perceptual distributions and four response regions corresponding to the factorial combination of two levels on each of two dimensions, an experimental paradigm referred to as a factorial identification task.  Fig. 1 
                       illustrates a 2×2 Gaussian GRT model, with distributions and response regions labeled with the generic ‘low’ (L) and ‘high’ (H) levels on each dimension. These levels could correspond, e.g., to faces with short or long noses and eyes that are set far apart or close together ( Thomas, 2001 ), broadband noises that are short or long and span lower or higher frequency ranges ( Silbert et al., 2009 ), or syllables containing consonants that vary with respect to multiple phonological dimensions ( Silbert, 2012 ). The ellipses are equal likelihood contours of the four perceptual distributions that bound the same volume under the four densities, the plus signs are the distributions’ means, and the horizontal and vertical lines are the decision bounds.

In GRT, interactions between dimensions can occur in three ways. At the ‘micro’ level, perceptual independence holds if and only if the perceptual effect distribution exhibits statistical independence. In  Fig. 1 , perceptual independence holds in the LL and HL distributions, and it fails in the LH and HH distributions. At a more ‘macro’ level, when the marginal distributions of perceptual effects on one dimension are invariant across levels of another dimension, perceptual separability holds on the former dimension with respect to the latter. In  Fig. 1 , the vertical dimension exhibits perceptual separability across levels of the horizontal dimension, while it fails on the horizontal dimension across levels of the vertical dimension (due to the fact that the LH and HH distributions are closer together on the horizontal dimension than are the LL and HL distributions). Finally, if the decision bound on one dimension is parallel to the corresponding coordinate axis, we say that decisional separability holds on that dimension. Decisional separability holds on both dimensions in  Fig. 1 .

Silbert and Thomas (2013)  showed, in part, that failures of decisional separability are not identifiable in 2×2 factorial Gaussian GRT models with linear or piecewise linear decision bounds. Any such model exhibiting failure of decisional separability can be transformed to induce decisional separability while preserving predicted response probabilities.

However, linear and piecewise linear bounds are not the only way to model response selection in GRT. A third response selection rule is based on optimal statistical decision-making. Because perceptual effects are random in GRT, for any given perceptual effect, the response strategy that maximizes accuracy is to pick the stimulus category that has the highest posterior probability of having produced that perceptual effect. With equal prior stimulus probabilities and unbiased payoffs such that no error is more costly than any other and no correct response more beneficial than any other, to respond optimally is to choose the response label assigned to the perceptual distribution whose density is maximal given the perceptual effect (i.e., the particular point in perceptual space;  Ashby, 1992; Ashby & Gott, 1988; Fukunaga, 1990 ). We consider unequal prior probabilities and biased payoffs following our analysis of this baseline condition. Hence, response regions are implicitly defined by discriminant functions delineating the points at which a pair of densities take the same value, or, equivalently, by the relative heights of the perceptual distribution density functions. In this sense, ‘decision bounds’ are artifacts of the decision process and are not explicitly used in selecting responses.

There are two sets of circumstances under which the two-by-two optimal GRT model is, with respect to predicted identification-confusion probabilities, empirically equivalent to a linear-bound GRT model with decisional separability. First, under a highly constrained configuration of perceptual distributions, the discriminant functions delineating the response regions in the two-by-two optimal GRT model perfectly mimic a single response criterion on each dimension (i.e., linear-bound decisional separability); we refer to this as explicit mimicry of decisional separability. Second, the set of models defined by invertible linear transformations of an optimal model that explicitly mimics decisional separability is also empirically indistinguishable from a model exhibiting true decisional separability; we refer to this as implicit mimicry of decisional separability.

In order to prove that the stated configuration mimics decisional separability, we generalize  Thomas’ (1995)  proof of a modification of Theorem 4 from  Ashby and Townsend (1986) . We first show that under a choose-the-maximum-density response rule, a constrained rectangular arrangement of means with a “cloverleaf” pattern of correlations is sufficient to mimic decisional separability, from which the sufficiency of the constrained rectangular zero-correlation configuration follows directly. We then show, via proof by contradiction, that optimal response selection and mimicry of decisional separability imply a constrained rectangular arrangement of means with zero perceptual correlation or the cloverleaf pattern. Finally, we return to the issue of prior stimulus probabilities and payoffs and their relation to optimal mimicry of decisional separability.

It is no coincidence that the discriminant functions illustrated in  Fig. 10  appear very much like the linear decision bounds that produce decisional separability after an appropriate shear transformation described and illustrated by   Silbert and Thomas (2013) . We turn now to a discussion of invertible linear transformations of explicitly mimicking optimal models and the resultant class of optimal models that implicitly mimic decisional separability.

Although the necessary and sufficient conditions for explicit mimicry of decisional separability are highly constrained, the mimicry of linear  failure  of decisional separability illustrated in  Fig. 10  hints at a broader class of models that are empirically equivalent to the explicit mimickers. The model illustrated in  Fig. 10  appears very similar to the models exhibiting linear-bound failure of decisional separability that   Silbert and Thomas (2013)  showed to be empirically equivalent, by virtue of rotation and shear transformations, to models exhibiting decisional separability.

An analogous equivalence holds for optimal response GRT models; an invertible linear transformation applied to an optimal model produces a model that is empirically equivalent. If the model explicitly mimics decisional separability prior to the invertible linear transformation, the transformed model may be said to  implicitly  mimic decisional separability.

Optimal models of cognition have gained in popularity in recent years. Many such models stake claim to optimality by virtue of their use of Bayes’ rule (though see   Marcus & Davis, 2013 ). In the present work, we consider the optimal (Bayesian) response rule in 2×2 Gaussian GRT models, describing the necessary and sufficient conditions for this model to mimic decisional separability.

With an optimal response selection rule, the necessary and sufficient conditions for explicit mimicry of decisional separability are twofold. First, the perceptual distributions must all have zero correlation or they must exhibit a cloverleaf pattern (see  Fig. 2 ). Second, the means of the perceptual distributions must describe a rectangle that, if the cloverleaf pattern holds, has width greater than or equal to the length scaled by the absolute value of the correlation coefficient producing the cloverleaf. These conditions also limn the set of GRT models with linear or piecewise linear decision bounds that exhibit optimality.

In establishing the necessary and sufficient conditions for explicit optimal mimicry of decisional separability, we also showed that decisional separability may be implicitly mimicked by models that are created by the application of an invertible linear transformation to an explicitly mimicking model. The application of such a transformation maps the distributions and the discriminant functions onto a new, empirically equivalent model, despite the fact that decisional separability appears to be violated in the transformed models.

If, in a given experimental setting, there is independent evidence for the use of the optimal response rule, these results indicate what properties the perceptual distributions must have for decisional separability to appear to hold. Optimal response selection can be assumed  a priori   (e.g.   Feldman et al., 2009 ), and one could make a case for optimality based on extensive exposure to a stimulus set and the presence of asymptotic performance. More rigorously, optimality can be tested directly with ideal observer analysis (e.g.   Geisler, 2011; Gold et al., 2008 ). However, using ideal observer analysis to test for optimality in the context of GRT is not straightforward. While the information given to an ideal observer is typically well-defined with respect to physical dimensions, GRT models unobserved perceptual dimensions. Crucially, the mathematical structure of GRT is mute with respect to the mapping between physical dimensions and modeled perceptual dimensions. In some GRT-based studies, ideal observer analysis would likely be fairly straightforward, due to more or less direct relationships between physical and modeled perceptual dimensions ( Silbert et al., 2009; Thomas, 2001 ). In other cases, the modeled perceptual dimensions do not relate directly to well-defined physical dimensions (e.g., phonological dimensions in speech perception;  Silbert, 2012 ), making it difficult to ascertain precisely what information is available to an ideal observer.

In addition, the results described above allow us to infer optimality (or lack thereof) in settings in which decisional separability is assumed  a priori  but in which we are unsure whether or not the optimal response rule is used. If, for example, we have good reason to assume decisional separability, a fitted Gaussian GRT model with perceptual independence or a cloverleaf correlation pattern and appropriately constrained rectangular configuration of perceptual distribution means implies optimal response selection.

Given that independent evidence for optimality may be difficult to come by, given the computational difficulties in fitting optimal multidimensional models, and given that there are compelling mathematical reasons to assume that decisional separability holds in GRT ( Silbert & Thomas, 2013 ), it seems likely that the present work will be most useful in licensing inferences of optimality by virtue of assumed decisional separability and configurations of perceptual distributions consistent with the results described above. It is worth keeping in mind that while there are reported cases of rectangular, perceptually independent configurations (e.g., the best fitting models for most of the participants in Experiment 1 of  Silbert et al., 2009 ), we are unaware of any reported cases of the cloverleaf pattern described above.

Interestingly, the introduction of unequal prior probabilities and/or biased payoffs can shift optimal decision bounds, but it does not change the shape or orientation of the bounds. A model with equal prior probabilities and unbiased payoffs that mimics decisional separability will continue to mimic decisional separability under a loose set of constraints on the ratios of prior probabilities, rewards, and costs.

Future work in the GRT framework would benefit from exploring the possibility of mitigating, or eliminating, problems with model mimicry of the sorts described here (and in   Silbert & Thomas, 2013 ). Experimental conditions that induce (or encourage) optimal response selection or decisional separability make the present results more directly applicable. For example, the so-called concurrent ratings task, in which ratings are given separately for each dimension, has been argued to encourage decisional separability ( Ashby, 1988; Wickens & Olzak, 1992 ). In addition, conditions in which subsets of stimuli are presented (with some additional assumptions about, e.g., perceptual invariance across conditions) may provide rich enough data to constrain models more than the data from a simple 2×2 identification task (e.g.   Kadlec & Hicks, 1998 ). Response times also provide valuable information about cognitive processing and dimensional interactions ( Ashby, 1989, 2000; Thomas, 2001; Townsend, Houpt, & Silbert, 2012 ), which may prove useful in addressing GRT model mimicry of the kind described here and elsewhere ( Silbert & Thomas, 2013; Thomas & Silbert, 2014 ).

We conclude by considering the nature of different conceptions of decisional separability and the role they play in model mimicry and (lack of) identifiability. In the optimal models described above (and in   Ashby, 1992; Ashby & Gott, 1988 ), decisions are made based on the relative values of the densities governing perceptual effects; the ‘decision bounds’ described by the discriminant functions (which are determined by the perceptual distribution parameters) are epiphenomenal and so do not correspond to a substantive psychological construct or process (hence our description of optimal model  mimicry  of decisional separability).

On the other hand, decisions in a GRT model may be determined directly by (functions of) perceptual effects. In such models, the decision bounds are substantive components of the model and, as such, correspond to substantive psychological processes. Of course, a substantive psychological claim is distinct from a mathematical model component. The “independent decisions classifier” described by  Ashby  ( 1992 ; see also  Ashby & Gott, 1988 ) highlights this distinction. In this model, decisions are made separately (and independently) on each of two perceptual dimensions; a single criterion on each dimension determines the response associated with the levels on that dimension. We note in passing that responses determined separately by single criteria on each of multiple dimensions are likely to bear important and interesting relationships to rule-based (as opposed to procedural) decision processes ( Ashby, Alfonso-Reese, Turken, & Waldron, 1998; Thomas, 1998 ).

Ashby (1992)  notes that such independent decisions are equivalent to decisional separability. We believe that there is much value in maintaining a distinction between a claim about psychological processes (e.g., separate, simple decisions on each dimension) and the mathematical implications of such claims (e.g., decisional separability in a GRT model; particular patterns of response times). As has been amply illustrated here (and in   Silbert & Thomas, 2013 ), purely geometric, mathematical definitions of decisional separability are unable, on their own, to fulfill the psychological promise of GRT.

In conjunction with complementary approaches to probing optimality, perception, and response selection, however, the mathematical results of the present work (and that of   Silbert & Thomas, 2013 ) represent a crucial component of GRT and multidimensional cognitive processing. Two other approaches in particular seem particularly well-suited to integration with GRT. Sequential ideal observer analysis (e.g.   Geisler, 1989 ) simultaneously analyzes the information available to perceptual systems and the physiological components of these systems. This approach might provide evidence for optimality, conditional on available information and perceptual system limitations, providing an avenue for direct application of the results described here. Computational and mathematical models based on the physiological components of cognitive systems also provide a way to imbue the mathematical abstractions of GRT (and other models) with psychological substance. For example, incorporation of neurophysiological functions and structures into models of behavior have provided a variety of insights into category learning ( Ashby et al., 1998 ;  Maddox, Molis, & Diehl, 2002 ), in at least one case providing an account of sub-optimality in the identification and categorization of simple visual stimuli ( Ashby, Waldron, William, & Berkman, 2001 ).
