The most famous of these experiments is the preference reversal phenomenon (Lichtenstein and Slovic, 1971; Grether and Plott, 1979}. By continuity, such a lottery may be optimal even when_X is strictly preferred to Y. It may therefore happen that the outcome of the randomization is to play Y, even though YX is preferred to Y.! Machina (1985) shows that even if the preference relation is transitive, it may happen that there are three lotteries X, Y, and Z such that the decision maker will put higher probability on X than on Y when choosing from {X, Y}, and similarly on Y and on Z when choosing from { Y, Z} and {X, Z}, respectively. A natural question is for what preference relations is it possible to find such cycles. This paper answers this question. It turns out that if the preference relation is strictly quasi-concave, then unless it can be represented by a functional which is quadratic in the probabilities, there will always be cycles of such seemingly nontransitive behavior.
