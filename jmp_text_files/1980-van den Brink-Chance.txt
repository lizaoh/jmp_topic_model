VAN HEERDEN  Psychological Laboratory, University of Amsterdam, The Netherlands  In order to investigate misconceptions of chance fluctuations, researchers can use the terminology of the random walk. The  problem at issue can be elucidated by a graph representing, for instance, a series of coin tossings. One must, however, be  careful in using such graphs. It appears that the extent to which people make erroneous predictions also depends on the  initial number of changes of sign of the random walk, presented in the instructions as visualisation of the problem.  1. The mode of the distribution is zero for every number of trials.  People overestimate the number of changes of sign that can actually occur. "Sampling of expert opinion revealed that even  trained statisticians expect much more than 78 changes of sign in 10000 trials, and nobody counted on the possibility of only  8 changes of sign. Feller does not tell us actually how the questioning was. done. It goes without saying, however, that one has to instruct  one's subjects. With less sophisticated subjects the experimenter would probably rely on a figure representing a part of the  graph of a random walk with one or more instances of crossings of the axis. And even sophisticated subjects need to be  provided with a clarifying example.  For the coin flipping example, any graph is equally legitimate as a representation of a random walk, since they are all  equally likely. But when a subject is presented with only one illustration of the random walk he may be misled into  considering the given one as the most representative one. He may not realize that the given graph is only one out of many  possibilities.  The present research was undertaken to answer the question: how far are people misled in their estimation of chance  fluctuations by the representational figure contained in the instructions.  2. EXPERIMENT  Psychology freshmen (N = 244) where invited to estimate the most likely number of changes of sign, or in other words, of  crossings of the graph through the axis. The first five tosses were graphed in a random walk picture to clarify the problem.  The depicted path of the random walk was presented in two variations that differed in their number of crossings of the axis:  variation A HHTHH (no crossings) and variation B HTTHH (two crossings). The above mentioned variations were each subdivided  into two tasks. One with 100 trials and another with 10000 trials. The four different versions of the questionnaire were  randomly assigned to the students.  In the instructions the figure of the random walk was explained as a visualization of the chance fluctuations, given the first  five trials. Subjects had to answer just one question: what is the most likely number of times that the graph will cross the  axis when a new game of 100 trials in the first task and 10000 trials in the second task will be started and completed.  The expectation was that for both the task of 100 trials and that of 10000 trials, the instructions with a graph containing no  crossings of the axis (variation A) would yield a lower estimate than the instruction with a graph containing two crossings of  the axis (variation B).  All subjects cooperated fully. The answers of only two subjects had to be eliminated because of their illogical character.  That brings the number of subjects down to 242. DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSIONS  Independently of the suggested number of trials and the illustration used in the instructions, people tend to overestimate  greatly the number of changes of sign. In variation A the median estimates of the mode were 20 and 275 for 100 and 10000 trials  ESTIMATION OF FLUCTUATION  87  respectively and in the other variation 40 and 3166, as can be seen in Tables 1 and 2. In the first paragraph we have seen  that the mode of the distribution of the number of changes of sign is zero for every number of trials. This shows how far the  subjects are beside the mark.  In variation B with 100 trials the median estimate is 40. With 10000, i.e. 100 X 100 trials, the median estimate is of the  order of 100 X 40 = 4000, namely 3166. One would expect the same in variation A. In variation A, however, with 100 trials the  median estimate is 20 but with 10000 trials is not of the order of 100 X 20 = 2000 but of the order of 10 X 20 = 200, namely  275. This is hard to explain.  The general tendency to overestimate the number of changes of sign is undoubtedly due to a misconception of randomness, as in  the gambler's fallacy. But our results also indicate that the instructions play an important role in overestimation. Responses  are influenced, substantially, by features of the elicitation procedure. This could be described in terms of the well-known  phenomenon of anchoring. In many situations people make estimates by starting from an initial value that is adjusted to yield  the final answer (Tversky and Kahneman, 1974).  The final values of the estimations are determined in part by the representational figure the investigator chose to elucidate  the problem. In that sense an unmodified acceptance of the results can be viewed as the consequence of an artifact, because  the instructions variable was not completely under control.  If one has to select one number of changes of sign to present in an illustration, one could choose the number with maximal  probability (i.e., zero). Although there is no perfect solution to the problem of the contaminating effect of the graph in the  instructions, this seems to be a fair procedure once one is aware of the danger of contamination.  In general, it can be said that researchers must be careful in using diagrams, figures, graphs and drawings in situations  where the presented illustration is one out of many possible ones. So often considered as helpful to enhance understanding of  an abstract problem, they may lead to misconception and bias.  REFERENCES  FELLER, W.
