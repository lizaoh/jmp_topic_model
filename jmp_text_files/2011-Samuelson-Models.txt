In signal detection experiments, human subjects are often asked to respond to an observation with an ordered categorical rating that indicates their confidence in the presence of a signal in that observation. For example, a subject may give a rating

The distribution of these ratings from observations that contain a signal are compared with the distribution of ratings from observations that do not contain a signal to determine a human subject’s ability to perceive stimulus among background noise. An example of such a signal is the presence of disease within an image of a patient. Researchers often use receiver operating characteristic (ROC) analysis to perform that comparison.

Using categorical ratings for signal detection analysis may sometimes be problematic. Categorical ratings may underestimate true ability to differentiate observations. Dorfman and Alf (1969)  did such modeling on the ratings from four observers using two normal distributions. Since then many others have used such modeling with other distributions to estimate diagnostic parameters such as the ROC curve. Some authors have made such models in the presence of covariates ( Pepe, 2003; Tosteson & Begg, 1988 ).

As with other models, two-sample models may have different numbers of parameters. A number of single parameter models can be constructed by modeling the signal present distribution as a location shifted version of the signal absent distribution. These include a two-exponential model, the equal variance two-normal model, and the two-logistic model ( Diamond, 1987 ). A single parameter may be insufficient for modeling the two distributions. When data are plentiful, goodness of fit statistics or plots may indicate that a more flexible model is required.

In an effort to generate more adjustable proper models some authors have presented two-sample models with two free parameters that have monotonic likelihood ratios for ordinal regression of observer rating data ( Dorfman, Berbaum, & Brandser, 2000; Dorfman et al., 1997; Lloyd, 2000; Metz & Pan, 1999 ). Most of these models have very specific forms. In general we would like to be able to construct two-sample models with monotonic likelihood ratios with the flexibility of having many parameters. For sparse data sets some parameters could be fixed to avoid over-fitting and reduce variance. For more plentiful data sets additional adjustable parameters would produce better fits and reduce possible biases.

This paper suggests a method for constructing pairs of distributions that have monotonic likelihood ratios, constructs some example distributions, and uses those distributions in ordinal regression to model data. Like models of previous authors ( Kay & Little, 1987; Lloyd, 2002; Qin & Zhang, 2003 ) this paper focuses on modeling the likelihood ratio. The method first selects a monotonic function to be the log likelihood ratio, and constructs a pair distributions that are proportional to the positive and negative exponents of these functions. In this way two-sample models with monotonic likelihood ratios can easily be constructed with many parameters, if required.

In Sections  2.2 and 2.3  two specific simple parametric functions, a hyperbola and a logistic distribution, are used as monotonic likelihood ratios for pairs of distributions, and some their properties are discussed. In Section  5  models suggested in this paper are fit to the data from the Digital Mammographic Imaging Screening Trial using maximum likelihood ordinal regression. The last section summarizes this work and suggests future work.

Pisano et al. (2005)  published data comparing the performance of mammographers using photographic films (“film”) to that of mammographers using full field digital imaging (“digital”). For each set of patient images the mammographers used a seven level ordinal scale to rate their confidence that they perceived disease within that set of images. The mammographers performed this task using both technologies, film and digital.

Fig. 2 
                          shows a subset of these data. The subset consisted of 15,803 observations of premenopausal or perimenopausal women, 100 of whom had breast cancer, the signal present condition. The other 15,703 observations were considered signal absent. The left graph of the figure shows the true positive and false positive fractions for the seven different rating levels from digital mammography and the right graph shows the same data for screen film mammography. The figure also contains binormal model ROC curves like those of the original paper. Note that the binormal model in the right graph is visibly not convex. The upward hook in the upper right of the graph means that mammographers are reserving their lowest ratings for actually diseased patients, an unrealistic model assumption.

“PROPROC” ( Metz & Pan, 1999 ) was formulated as a model of the distribution of the likelihood ratio itself under an assumption of latent normality. This paper presented a general method for constructing pairs of distributions that have monotonic likelihood ratios, possibly with large numbers of free parameters. Such models may be useful in the ordinal regression of data from signal detection or diagnostic experiments with ordinal confidence ratings, especially when large amounts of data are available. Only models with monotonic likelihood ratios may be realistic for data from such experiments.

The paper presented two specific, simple parametric forms with monotonic likelihood ratios as examples and fit them to a set of published observer data. These new models, like other models, have numerically calculable maximum likelihood estimates, summary statistics, such as AUC, and uncertainties.

While the specific models in this paper have interesting properties that may make them useful for certain data sets, they are given primarily as examples and are not suggested for any specific purpose. Readers are encouraged to construct, demonstrate, and evaluate other specific model forms with monotonic likelihood ratios with properties that are favorable for their particular task or data set.
