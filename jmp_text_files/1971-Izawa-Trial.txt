R and T  trials here are, respectively, defined as presentations of both stimulus and response members of a given verbal pair, and  presentations of the stimulus member alone without any informational feedback from the experimenter. A family of experiments conducted under the Paradigm 1 design supplied a useful tool in uncovering the hidden functions of  unreinforced test (recall) trials by allowing investigations of performances over successive Ts with no intervening  reinforcement (study) trial. The following distinctive characteristics of unreinforced T (test) trials in PAL were consistently replicated through the  present line of research (Izawa, 1966a-1970b):  (a) Over the m successive T trials, 1 < m <_ 19, allowing minute retention losses from Tl to T2 , no changes occur in response  probabilities from the first to the last T in any replication at any stage of acquisition. The constancy phenomena that were  strikingly demonstrated suggest that neither significant learning nor forgetting takes place on unreinforced tests per se.  (b) The effects of unreinforced T trials were separated from those of time taken by the Ts by replacing some of the successive  tests in the RTl --- Tm acquisition sequences. Letting m = 2, comparisons between Conditions RTB and RBT, or between  Conditions RTN and RNT (Izawa, 1966b, 1967a) revealed that T trials are distinctively different from those of mere lapse of  time and that they prevent forgetting which otherwise is occurring (the forgetting-prevention effects).  (c) Although no new conditioning occurs on Ts per se, as indicated in the constancy over successive Ts as well as in  conditioning probability analyses, learning curve analyses unambiguously disclosed processes of supreme importance that occur  on T trials to the overall course of acquisition which potentiate the effectiveness of subsequent reinforcements (the  potentiating effects). Until the optimal point at approximately m = 7 is reached, the more Ts per replication, the better the  learning (Izawa, 1970a).  (d) Conditioning, or new learning, occurs uniquely on R trials, but not on T trials; whereas potentiating effects of the  subsequent reinforcements are generated uniquely on T trials but not on R trials (e.g., Izawa, 1969a).  These new empirical facts brought about by families of experiments generated by Paradigm 1 are extremely difficult to explain  from the extant learning theories.  Nonetheless, when na, the number of Ts per replication, was small (less than 2), the results were fairly well accounted for by  the stimulus fluctuation model (Estes, 1955a,b) adapted to paired-associate learning situations (Izawa, 1966a-1967b). However,  as m increases, the discrepancy between the data and the stimulus fluctuation model becomes increasingly greater. One major  difficulty here is that the two newly specified functions of unreinforced tests in PAL, the forgetting-prevention effects and  the potentiating effects, are mutually contradictory and paradoxical for the theory. The model predicts that either (a) the  more forgetting on Ts, the more effective conditioning on Rs, or (b) the less retention losses on tests, the less effective  learning on reinforcements. Unfortunately however, the fact remains that T trials produce less or no forgetting and yet afford  more acquisition on subsequent R trials.  THE TEST TRIAL POTENTIATING MODEL  With the newly acquired information about unreinforced test trials in pairedassociate learning (PAL) in hand, the task is,  then, to formulate a new model which can encompass both of the empirical functions of T trials: the forgetting-prevention  effects and the potentiating effects. A logical approach to the resolution of difficulties encountered by extant learning  theories is to postulate the unique theoretical functions of T trials in PAL, characteristically different from those of time  or those of acquisition (R) trials, as convincingly established in numerous RT experiments generated by Paradigm 1 and its  variants.  Despite the stimulus fluctuation model's lack of success in accommodating data where the effects of T trials predominate, when  interspersing Ts were entirely eliminated as in Conditions R, RN, RNN, and RNNNN for example, the model was proved to be  valid: Conditions having longer intercycle intervals were, respectively, better than the control, Condition R (each p < .001,  Izawa, 1968a, Expts II and III). Hence, the test trial potentiating model proposed here embraces some of the assumptions of  this older model to account for intercycle processes.  The principal assumptions of the test trial potentiating model are as follows:  (1) The stimulus term of each pair, together with the context in which it occurs, comprises a finite set of stimulus elements,  N*(= N -{- N') in number.  (2) In a given learning situation, the universe for each pair is assumed to consist of two mutually exclusive and exhaustive  states or sets; one being the available set (the active set, A), the other, the unavailable set (the inactive set, A). At any  time, elements in the available state (N in number) can be sampled by the subject, if the item is present; whereas the  elements in the unavailable state (N' in number) cannot be sampled.  (3) Interchanges of elements between the active and inactive states occur  THE TEST TRIAL POTENTIATING MODEL 203  randomly over time during intercycle intervals, for each item. There exists some constant probability of a transition, j from  A to A, and j' from A to A, during any intercycle interval (interpresentation interval), defined as the mean time interval  between one presentation of a given item to the next in an experimental session.  (4) At any time, any given element may be conditioned to the correct response, some specific incorrect response, or neither.  (5) On each R trial, there is some fixed probability, c, that all elements in the sample will be conditioned to the correct  response. c is set equal to 1 in this paper.  (6) On any T trial, no new conditioning occurs.  (7) During every T trial, however, in his efforts to retrieve the correct response, the subject increases the rate of  interchange of elements between the two states so that with probability k' all the elements enter from A to A, and with  probability k the unconditioned elements escape from A to A, while retaining all the conditioned elements in the available set  at the onset of the stimulus presentation on the T. The active retrieval processes increase the number of conditioned elements  in the active state, with highly effective interchanges of elements between the states.  (8) The probability of a correct response on any T is equal to the proportion of the sample that is conditioned to the correct  response.  The predominant characteristics of the test trial potentiating model are summarized in the seventh assumption. In essence, the  model brings the retrieval processes to the foreground. When these active processes of test trials in PAL are postulated, the  seemingly self-contradictory empirical phenomena (unveiled in the present series of RT studies) with respect to other  theoretical positions can be led to a rational resolution. That is, both the potentiating effects and the  forgetting-prevention effects of unreinforced T trials in paired-associate learning can be explained logically within a single  set of theoretical assumptions of the test trial potentiating model.  To account for the phenomena that the more Ts per replication, the better the learning, i.e. the potentiating effect, is  simple and straightforward: Since it is assumed that the interchange of unconditioned elements between active and inactive  states proceeds at a faster rate during test trials than during other intervals, there will be more opportunity for previously  unconditioned elements to enter the active state between Rs, thus resulting in more conditioning, if the interval is filled  with Ts, than if it is not.  Similarly, according to the assumptions made in the model, the interchange during T trials can be described as the same kind  of Markov process as the interchange during interpresentation intervals (time). The T process, like the other (time) process,  goes to an asymptotic constant level, but does so much more rapidly, since the interchange rate on T trials is assumed to be  higher. The constancy in response probabilities over  204 IZAwA  successive T trials, interpreted as the forgetting-prevention effect, is simply the result of an asymptotic constant level of  the T process. Thus, this new model can successfully account for both the potentiating effects and the constant performance  over a block of T trials (the forgetting-prevention effect).  Since the test trial potentiating model can accommodate, at least qualitatively, the major empirical findings through numerous  experiments conducted (e.g., Izawa, 1966a-1970b), a more precise mathematical expression of this new theory will be presented. First, consider the interchange probabilities j and j' of intercycle intervals. Throughout all the cases j is greater than j'  within each experiment. It is also interesting to note that the j-values were greater in the odd-numbered experiments where  harder pairs were learned than their counterparts, even-numbered experiments, in which easier materials were used; whereas the  opposite relationship held for the j'-values. The combinations of the two probabilities were expressed as a. When pairs were  easier, the absolute values of a were greater than when they were harder. These relationships were all expected from the  proposed model, as (a) more elements escape from the available to the unavailable set than in the opposite direction during  intercycle intervals, and (b) the more interchanges of elements during the intercycle intervals, the more conditioning on  subsequent reinforcement trials, resulting in greater conditioning effects with easier materials than with harder ones.  Second, consider unique theoretical effects of T trials that correspond to empirical effects which were specified in the  present series of investigation. Concerning the  THE REST TRIAL POTENTIATING MODEL 223  processes of the subject's subjective efforts to maximize the number of elements that are conditioned in the available set  during the test trials, there are conceptually three possibilities of achieving this objective: (1) To bring as many  conditioned elements as possible from the unavailable set to the available set. This can be translated into quantitative  terms, namely a large P-value; (2) To release as many unconditioned elements as possible from the available set to the  unavailable set, making the k-value large; and (3) The most effective, to have both (1) and (2), namely both large and k.  The first possibility of the subjective maximization of the conditioned elements in the available set at the time of overt  responding seemed to be applicable to Cases 1 and 2 throughout the first four experiments, with some trend of (3) in  Experiment IV, and the second possibility was applicable to the two experiments in Case 3. This state of affairs might be seen  as the g-value in which the combined effects of both k and k' were summarized, and entered in the last column for each  experiment. Throughout all three cases, when items were easier as in Experiments 11, IV, and VI, the /3-values were smaller  than when the items were harder as in Experiments 1, III, and V. Stated differently, the easier the pairs, the smaller g, and  the more effective the subjective maximization. This was also consistent with the expectation from the test trial potentiating  model.
