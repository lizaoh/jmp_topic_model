JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 34, 419-434 (1990)  Revealed Preference and the Axiomatic Foundations of Intransitive Indifference: The Case of Asymmetric Subrelations  TARADAS BANDYOPADHYAY  Graduate School of Management, University of California, Riverside  This paper provides an axiomatic structure for various binary preference relations that are reflexive, connected, and  transitive in their asymmetric subrelations known as quasi-transitive orderings. A choice function generates various  (distinct) binary preference relations, such as the base relation, the revealed preference relation, and the wide revealed  preference relation. For each interpretation of a binary preference relation, we present an axiom that ensures the existence  of a quasi-transitive preference ordering. A description of a choice procedure is considered which one often observes in real  life and it is shown that the path independence of such a procedure is a sufficient condition for a quasi-transitive ordering  of various binary preference relations. (C 1990 Academic Press, Inc.  1. INTRODUCTION  A choice function is a rule which picks some elements from a set of alternatives presented for choice. One can generate a  binary relation of preference from various choice functions, such as the base relation (Arrow, 1959; Herzberger, 1973), the  revealed preference relation (Samuelson, 1938; Arrow 1959), and the wide revealed preference relation (Richter, 1966).1 The  axiomatic foundation of transitive ordering of each of these binary relations is now well established. Consequently, now we  know the kind of consistency one needs in a choice situation to ensure transitivity for all binary preference relations. The  consistency requirement in question is called the Weak Axiom of Revealed Preference; it requires that if an alternative .x is  chosen from a set while y is available, then there is no other set, containing both x and y, from which y will be chosen.' However, with the  exception of the base relation, there is no literature on the axiomatic foundation of binary preference relations that are  reflexive, connected, and transitive in their asymmetric subrelation, which are known as quasi-transitive orderings.' The  violation of transitivity of the symmetric subrelation, i.e., the indifference relation, is the so-called "threshold" effect  (Georgescu-Roegen, 1958). Suppose that an individual is indifferent between any two temperatures that are only one degree  apart, but always has a strong preference between temperatures that are apart by two or more degrees. As  early as 1919, Wiener had initiated work on the theory of choice that would allow intransitive indifference. Observing the  imperfect discriminatory power of human minds and serious empirical shortcomings, Armstrong (1939, 1951) was one of the first  to argue that indifference is not necessarily transitive, although attacks on the transitivity of indifference go back as far  as Poincare in the nineteenth century. Subsequently, Luce (1956), Georgescu-Roegen (1958), Tversky (1969), Fishburn (1970a),  Sen (1971), Jamison and Lau (1973), and Plott (1973), among others, investigated the problems related to the intransitivity of  indifference. In Fishburn (1970b), one can find various arguments against the transitivity of indifference and extensive  references to the literature on this issue. Thus it is of interest to determine the consistency requirement that would  guarantee a quasi-transitive ordering of a binary preference relation which is generated from a choice function. In this  paper, we introduce a revealed preference axiom which is implied by the Weak Axiom of Revealed Preference and show that this  new axiom ensures quasi-transitivity of various revealed preference relations, but not of all binary preference relations.  This result suggests a strengthening of the consistency requirement. In the process, we establish the axiomatic foundations of  various quasi-transitive preference relations. We then characterize these consistency requirements in terms of the regularity  requirement of a descriptive choice procedure that one often observes in real life.  2. The Chernoff condition ensures that alternatives rejected in a smaller set com-  REVEALED PREFERENCE RELATIONS 423  parison must be excluded from a choice set based on a superset comparison.' In other words, if an alternative is chosen from a  larger set then it must also be chosen from a smaller set. The Chernoff condition is referred to as a contraction consistency  condition (Sen, 1977). The weak dominance condition says that an alternative rejected in a smaller set comparison can be  chosen from a larger set only if the winner(s) from a smaller set are also chosen.' The non-dominance condition essentially  requires that if an alternative x is chosen from a smaller set from which another alternative y is also chosen, then in an  expanded set x must also be chosen if y is chosen. A weakening of this condition, called weak non-dominance, says that if x  and y are both best in a subset comparison, then neither of them can be uniquely best in a superset comparison.' According to  the superset condition the set of chosen elements from a larger set should not be a proper subset of the set of chosen  elements from a smaller set.' Sen (1977) has shown that WND is implied by both SS and ND; Bandyopadhyay (1984) established  that CC implies WD. Together WD and ND are called strong dominance (SD), which is the property that if an alternative x is  chosen while y is available in a smaller set comparison, then in a larger set comparison y may be chosen only if x is also  chosen. In other words, the decision cannot be reversed when the set is expanded. Strong dominance is the strongest expansion  consistency condition (Sen, 1977). Note that the strong dominance condition and the Chernoff condition are complements to each  other. Arrow (1959) established that these two conditions together are equivalent to the Weak Axiom of Revealed Preference.  The minimal rationality condition requires that an alternative which is chosen in every subset comparison must also be chosen  from a set which is the union of all these subsets. The choice procedure corresponding to PI has no similarity with the choice procedure corresponding to PI' where the final  choice is made only in two stages instead of JAI stages where JAI is the number of elements in the set A from which a choice  is to be made. However, the requirement of path independence of each of these procedures produces the identical outcome. This  in turn shows the significance of the requirement of path independence of a choice procedure in ensuring the necessary  consistency one needs to have a quasi-transitive ordering of various binary preference relations.  Figure 3 demonstrates the relationship between various path independence conditions and other consistency conditions.  S. CONCLUSIONS  We summarize the major findings of the paper in this section.  First, we introduced the Quasi Revealed Preference Axiom (QRPA), a consistency condition which is weaker than the Weak Axiom  of Revealed Preference. We showed that although QRPA ensures quasi-transitivity of the revealed preference relation A and the  wide revealed preference relation R, we need a stronger condition for quasi-transitivity of the base relation. It was then  shown that every choice function satisfying the Chernoff condition (CC) and the weak  REVEALED PREFERENCE RELATIONS 433  non-dominance condition (WND) is guaranteed to be quasi-transitive in the R, R, and R relations.  Second, we have introduced a path independence condition (Pl) which is weaker than Plott's path independence condition (PI").  We showed that PI ensures quasitransitivity of R, R, and R relations.  Third, it was established that neither PI nor CC and WND are necessary for quasi-transitivity of R, R relations, since CC and  WND together do not imply PI and PI does not imply WND.  Fourth, we showed that if we really insist on the process of choosing in a "divide and conquer" manner as Plott argued when  introducing his path independence condition, i.e., if we require that the first round choices be made only over the smaller  sets, then the Plott type path independence condition (PI') would be equivalent to our path independence condition (PI).  Finally, the significance of introducing the PI condition is that (1) it helps to understand in comparison with Pl' that  completely different choice procedures may ensure identical outcomes once the path independence is required; (2) it helps to  establish the importance of pairwise choice in the context of a sequential choice procedure which ensures transitive  rationalization of a choice function; and (3) it has great computational advantages in examining the extent to which the  consistency requirements are satisfied for a binary relation to be quasi-transitive.
