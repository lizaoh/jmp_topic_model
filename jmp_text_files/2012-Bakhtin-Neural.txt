Throughout the history of experimental psychology, researchers have relied on reaction times (RTs) as a way to gain insight into mental processes. Even as psychology began to emerge as a scientific discipline,  Donders (1868)  argued that differences in RT provided an insight into mental processes. And, the importance of RTs holds firm today. Over the past 140 years, RTs have been used to study topics as diverse as cognitive interference, phobias, memory, visual attention, skill acquisition, attitudes, and self-knowledge.

In the vast majority of these studies, researchers are interested in the average time required to make a response. For example, Donders was concerned with the psychological processes involved in different kinds of choice. He found that, on average, participants responded more quickly when they were required to simply detect the onset of a stimulus (what he called a simple RT task) than when they were required to determine which of two potential stimuli occurred (a choice RT task). Donders reasoned that the difference in RTs to these two tasks provided an index of the additional mental work required by the more complex task. Though Donders’ original ideas have been refined, his basic argument remains a fundamental part of research in experimental psychology.

Although analysis of mean RT has provided a number of critical insights, it has been realized that the complex and dynamic nature of this dependent variable calls for development of a detailed and mathematically solid understanding of RT statistical properties. There is a growing body of literature addressing the distributions of RTs and offering mathematical models for their description, see, e.g.,  Fific, Little, and Nosofsky (2010) ,  Luce (1986)  and  Nosofsky and Palmeri (1997) .

One group that has clearly contributed greatly to the study of RT statistics is that of Ratcliff and his colleagues who have developed elaborate diffusion models to describe the decision-making process, applied those models to a variety of RT tasks and compared them to other potential models ( Ratcliff & Rouder, 1998; Ratcliff & Smith, 2004; Ratcliff, Van Zandt, & McKoon, 1999 ; see also  Fific et al., 2010 ). Critically, these attempts to model RT distributions have been inextricably linked with attempts to understand the psychological and physical operations that produce the responses in the first place. As such, work on diffusion models has helped to clarify hypotheses about factors like response bias and accumulation of information that guide decision-making. In this manner, they provide leverage for understanding phenomena that are central to many areas of psychology.

The basic idea behind diffusion models is evidence accumulation. The random diffusion path reflects the evidence accumulated towards possible decisions, and responses are evoked as certain thresholds of evidence are reached. In particular, the reaction or decision times are interpreted as times of evidence accumulation processes.

Several other relevant models have appeared in the literature motivated by fairness to data, substantiality of the models, their simplicity and computational feasibility. In general, models with fewer parameters are preferable, since they provide more predictive power and interpretability.

The EZ diffusion model introduced in  Wagenmakers, Van Der Maas, and Grasman (2007)  and  Wagenmakers, van der Maas, Dolan, and Grasman (2008)  is a simplified version of Ratcliff’s diffusion model and an attempt to reduce the number of parameters for robustness and computational efficiency.

The Random Ray model is introduced in  Reeves, Santhi, and Decaro (2005)  and is based on the same idea of evidence accumulation, but the diffusion paths are replaced by ballistic steady motion with random parameters. To achieve a fit with the data, one has to introduce 6 parameters into the model. The LATER model for decision times introduced in  Carpenter (1981, 1999)  is also based on random steady motion.

The modeling of  Rouder (2005)  and  Rouder, Tuerlinckx, Speckman, Lu, and Gomez (2008)  is based on Weibull distributions with three parameters. It is stressed by the authors that it should be treated as a descriptive statistical model but not as a substantive one since it does not intend to describe the mechanism of the emergence of RT distributions. In that regard it is similar to the use of ex-Gaussian distribution that fits data reasonably well but lacks a process interpretation in  Ratcliff et al. (1999) .

Our goal is to present two simple substantive models that allow for detailed and rigorous analysis based on modern probability theory and that can be applied to a basic two-choice RT task under conditions of indifference, that is a task in which participants are asked to choose between two stimuli that differ only minimally in their utility, yielding no clear preference for one stimulus over the other. One might imagine a choice between heads or tails in a coin toss, between two equidistant routes to a destination, or two equally attractive stocks. We will refer to this as a situation with no  a priori  bias.

Our main model is based on neural computation, and the other one is a related diffusion model. Both belong to the same universality class and lead to the same shape of the RT distribution. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first attempt to use a neural computation model for analysis of RT.

For the neural computation system we use a simple model called the dynamic Curie–Weiss model, where each neuron in a large neural system can be in one of two possible states +1 or −1 representing the “opinion” of a single neuron, and can randomly switch to the opposite state. The switching rates for individual neurons at any given time depend on the current states of other neurons. The likelihood that a given neuron changes its opinion increases as a function of the number of neurons holding the opposite opinion. This creates a tendency of mutual agreement between the states of different neurons.

If the dynamics are initiated at a completely unbiased neutral state where the number of neurons in state +1 equals the number of neurons in state −1, then microscopic fluctuations (i.e., fluctuations in states of just a few neurons) eventually produce a macroscopic deviation from the neutral state as the system accumulates macroscopic agreement among neurons so that in the end a clearly dominating opinion in the system is worked out by the dynamics. When the fraction of +1’s or −1’s in the system reaches a certain threshold we interpret that as the decision made by the system and the time it took for the system to reach that threshold is interpreted as decision time.

The main advantages of the models introduced in this paper are their simplicity and the availability of mathematically precise and rigorous analysis. This results in extremely specific predictions about the distribution of RTs. The only two parameters we need to fit the data have transparent meaning being just the dilation and translation coefficients of a linear transformation. In other words, the only two parameters involve adjusting the mean and the variance of the distribution. We analyze the data obtained from an experiment with 45 participants and show quantitatively that these two parameters are sufficient and both essential.

In its present form our models have a clear limitation being applicable only to a symmetric two-choice decision task with no  a priori  bias, and they cannot be applied to more complex situations. To treat these situations one needs to adjust the models introducing asymmetry. This can be done in several ways in both a neural agent-based setting and the diffusion setting. The search for an adjustment that is fair to data and does not sacrifice mathematical elegance is beyond the scope of this paper.

Nevertheless, we find it striking that our models lead to reproducible results. For almost all of the participants we tested in the framework of tasks with no  a priori  bias, the shape of the distribution of RT can be accurately approximated by a unique (up to translation and dilation) universal distribution derived from our models. We believe that these models represent an important step in developing a rigorous, testable, and mathematically tractable understanding of RT data.

The behavior of both mathematical models discussed above resembles the process of decision making in an unbiased setting. Suppose the choice has to be made between two alternatives, neither being significantly preferable to the other, and one can spend indefinite time studying them before making the choice. Initially, the decision is completely undetermined and the participant is equally drawn to both alternatives. The resulting small oscillations around the point of indecision can be described as the hesitation phase. Eventually, a small but nonnegligible preference towards one choice develops and from that point on the inclination in that direction becomes only stronger. In fact, the stronger the current inclination is the smaller is the chance to change it. In this conservative phase of bias amplification, the small bias worked out in the hesitation phase eventually leads to the final decision.

A natural question is if the connection of the mathematical model with the complex process of decision making is more than just metaphorical. We designed an experiment to test the model and answer this question.

Forty-five participants were each shown a sequence of 200 pairs of images on the screen of a PC. Each image in the pair contained 9 circles of variable sizes (see  Fig. 3

For each pair, the participant had to guess which image covered a larger area. The participant indicated his/her choice by pressing one of two designated keys. To avoid inertia and memory effects we imposed an intertrial interval of 1 s.

If a participant pressed a key prior to stimulus onset, that trial was discarded, so the total number of recorded trials is often less than 200.

In Section  3 , we showed that our models are consistent with most experimental results. Both models describe a symmetric unbiased situation. This is reflected in the experiment where the participant has no  a priori  knowledge and no bias to guide the response. The pairs of images were generated so that there was no obvious difference in area sizes.

Although our models provide extremely precise predictions about the shape of the decision-making time distribution, they will certainly need some adjustment for non-symmetric situations that typically characterize psychological research. The model symmetry can be broken in many ways and the question of which of them are adequate for various situations requires further investigation. So, the drawback of the described models is the restriction to situations with no bias.

However, the present paper is the first attempt to use a neural computation model for modeling decision times, and it opens the door to studying more sophisticated neural models potentially leading to new insights on patterns arising in decision making. Clearly, models with a more subtle geometry of connections between neurons, more sophisticated modes of interaction between them, accounting for external stimuli, can produce various kinds of complex behavior such as sequential decision making.

Let us summarize other attractive features of our approach. An advantage of our models is that they allow for a rigorous and elegant mathematical analysis that results in a family of exit time distributions that depend only on two parameters. In particular, this analysis justifies the Gaussian nature of the noise that has never been addressed in the literature on diffusion models for reaction times.

Another essential feature of our model is the microscopic nature of the noise. The dynamics near unstable equilibrium transforms microscopic randomness into a macroscopic decision. Interestingly, the dependence of the average exit time on the noise intensity is asymptotically logarithmic, and a drastic decrease of the noise level (i.e. increase of the size of the neural system) results only in a moderate increase of the exit times.

Macroscopically, the models considered in the present paper can be viewed as random motion near an unstable equilibrium. Systems with many unstable equilibria linked to each other by heteroclinic connections have also received attention from researchers, e.g., such dynamical systems were used to model sequential decision making in neuronal systems ( Rabinovich, Huerta, Varona, & Afraimovich, 2008 ). A rigorous mathematical theory of these heteroclinic networks in the presence of small noise has been developed ( Bakhtin, 2010, 2011 ). We view the present paper as a part of this developing interplay between rigorous mathematical dynamics, probability, and life sciences.
