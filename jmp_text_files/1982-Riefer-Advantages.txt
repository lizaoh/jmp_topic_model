JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 26, 97-123 (1982)  The Advantages of Mathematical Modeling over Traditional Methods in the Analysis of Category Clustering  DAVID M. RIEFER  University of California, Irvine  A mathematical model for the analysis of category clustering is developed and tested. The model, which can be applied to  categories of any size, is an extension of a two-item statistical model developed by Batchelder and Riefer (Psychological  Review, 1980, 87, 375-397), and is equivalent to their model when categories consist of two items. The model is based on a  current theory of clustering which postulates that the learning of a list of category items occurs on different hierarchical  levels. Two category list-learning experiments are presented, and the data from these experiments are analyzed using the  general statistical model. The first experiment reveals that the probabilities of storing and retrieving a cluster increase  with category size, while the learning of items as singletons decreases. The effects of withincategory spacing indicate that  the storage of clusters decreases while cluster retrievability increases with an increase in input spacing. In the second  experiment, the storage and retrieval of clusters are shown to be unaffected by whether the presentation of items is uncued or  cued with the name of the category. However, the association of items decreases and the learning of items as singletons  increases with uncued presentation. In the final sections, the general statistical model is compared to other methods for the  measurement of category clustering. The model is shown to be superior to numerical indices of clustering, since these measures  are not based on any theory of clustering, and because unitary measures cannot capture the multiprocess nature of categorized  recall. The model is also argued to have certain advantages over other mathematical models that have been applied to category  clustering, since these models cannot account for situations in which a portion of the items are clustered while others are  learned singularly.  It is an established fact that when categorizable words are presented in a memory task, subjects exhibit a strong tendency to  recall words from the same categories adjacently. This phenomenon, known as category clustering, has been used by many  researchers as one method of studying organizational processes in memory. However, the question of how to empirically measure  the amount of organization, or clustering, in recall data is one which has been widely debated within the literature (see  Murphy, 1979, for a review). This problem is not a simple one, because the recall output of a list of categorizable words is  potentially very rich in information. Not only can one examine the total number of words recalled, but also the average number  of items recalled per category, the number of different categories represented in recall, and whether items were recalled  adjacently versus nonadjacently. A large number of numerical indices have been developed which attempt to measure the degree of clustering based on output  adjacencies (Murphy, 1979, provides a comprehensive review of these clustering indices). The idea behind these indices is to  see if there is enough adjacent recall of items from the same category to reject the hypothesis that the items were output  independently. The measure of clustering becomes, in essence, whatever statistic was used to reject the independence  assumption. However, certain researchers (e.g., Colle, 1972; Rotondo, 1977) have criticized this general approach as a method  for the measurement of clustering. Since clustering indices are not directly based on any theory of how category items are  learned, they can reveal little about the underlying cognitive processes which contribute to clustering.  Instead, these measures begin with a theory of nonclustering (the independence assumption) and then attempt to statistically  reject this theory by looking at empirical trends in the data. With this approach, it is unclear just how much clustering  exists in the data, since these indices only indicate whether or not a significant amount of nonindependence in the data is  observed.  A more direct approach towards the analysis of category clustering, as outlined by Colle (1972), would be to develop a  theoretically motivated measure of clustering. In other words, one could theoretically describe the cognitive processes  responsible for clustering and derive a measure of clustering based on this theory. One approach along these lines might be  the development of a mathematical model which could be applied to the learning of category items. If such a model were to  contain one or more clustering parameters, an estimate of these parameters could be derived from the data which in essence  would constitute measurements of clustering. Recently, in fact, a few attempts have been made to use mathematical modeling  towards a theoretical analysis of category clustering (Batchelder & Riefer, 1980; Ross & Bower, 1981; Rotondo, 1977, 1979).  The purpose of this article is to develop and test a relatively simple mathematical model for the analysis of category  clustering. The following presentation will attempt to demonstrate not only the validity and usefulness of the model as an  analytical tool, but also of its advantages over previously developed methods of cluster measurement. In essence, the model is  an extension of a simple statistical model offered by Batchelder and Riefer (1980). A limitation of that model was that it  could only be applied to the special case when categories consist of two items. The general statistical model presented here  can be applied to categories of any size, and in fact reduces to Batchelder and Riefer's model for categories of size two.  Like the two-item model, the general statistical model is capable of extracting information about the unobservable cognitive  processes which contribute to category clustering.  The organization of the article is as follows: In the next section, a brief review is given of previous mathematical models  for clustering. An understanding of these models will facilitate the eventual description of the current model, as well as  comparisons between the models. In Section 2, the general statistical model is  ANALYSIS OF CATEGORY CLUSTERING 99  described, along with a current theory of category clustering which forms the basis of the model. In Section 3 the validity  and usefulness of the model is tested using data from a series of standard clustering experiments. In Section 4, a comparison  is made between the general statistical model and other methods of cluster measurement. Finally, the conclusion contains a  discussion of the adequacy of the modeling approach for the analysis of category clustering and the proper relationship  between mathematical models and more empirical methods of cluster measurement.  1. THEORETICAL MEASURES OF CLUSTERING  In contrast to the numerous clustering indices which have been developed, there have been relatively few theoretical  approaches to cluster measurement. However, a number of mathematical models for the analysis of clustering have appeared  recently in the literature. Although a number of these models have only been used as a way of quantifying existing theory  (e.g., the models of Greitzer (1976), Rundus (1973), and Batchelder and Riefer's (1980) Markov model), a few models have been  proposed for the specific purpose of analyzing data from clustering experiments. A brief review of three of these models is  given.  Rotondo's Discrete Structural Model  Rotondo (1977, 1979) has developed a theoretical analysis of clustering which specifically examines the co-occurrence of items  within each category. Rotondo's model is based on the theoretical assumption that category items can be grouped together into  "chunks." A chunk represents a higher order memory unit under which subjects have organized a set of memorized items. The  exact structure of the chunks within the list can be specified by different versions of the model. In this way Rotondo's model  can handle the special cases when chunks overlap, i.e., when items belong to more than one chunk.  Rotondo assumes that the recall of an item occurs only if the item is associated to some chunk, and if that chunk is accessed  during recall. Thus, the recall of items is a two-stage process. In the first stage, a specific category is accessed by some  retrieval probability /lfor the nth category. Individual items from that category are then recalled independently, with  probability eifor the i th item in the nth category. Thus, the probability of actually recalling this item is flnern. An  important aspect of this analysis is the attempt by Rotondo to define clusters in terms of the co-occurrence of items, as  opposed to examining adjacencies in the data. Items are assumed to be clustered if their rate of co-occurrence follows a  pattern which cannot be explained if the items were learned independently. By partitioning the recall of items into their  error-success protocols, Rotondo can express these data events in terms of the parameters of the model. Maximum likelihood  estimates can then be derived for# the probability that category n is retrieved, and e; the memory strength for the ith  category item. In one analysis, he compared two versions of the model. In the first  version, the single chunk model, items were assumed to be associated to only one chunk with no items overlapping into other  chunks. In the second version, the independence model, each individual item was assumed to be stored exclusively within its  own chunk, with no more than one item per chunk. Rotondo applied these two models to the recall data from the experiment, and  showed that the single chunk model uniformly fit this data better than the independence model. Rotondo interpreted this to  mean that there existed a significant amount of clustering in the experiment.  Ross and Bower's Trace Models  Another theoretical analysis of category clustering was provided by Ross and Bower (1981); specifically, they considered the  general question of how memory traces for related items are associated in memory. Their approach was to develop three simple  mathematical models, each representing different ways that category items may be organized within memory, and then to evaluate  the models by applying them to the data from an asociative memory task. The experimental procedure was one in which subjects  were presented with groups of words for memorization. Although the words were seemingly pairwise unrelated, all of the words  together formed some kind of schematic unit (e.g., apron, brush, chair, clip: "haircut"). During recall, subjects were cued  with one or more of the items within the group and were asked to recall the remaining items.  The three models compared by Ross and Bower were referred to as the schema model, horizontal model, and fragment model. In the  schema model, words are assumed to be associated, not to one another, but to a grouping node (e.g., "haircut" in the above  example). This assumption is similar to the one made by Rotondo, who assumed that category items are stored independently, but  are associated to a particular category label. In contrast, the horizontal model does not assume the existence of a grouping  node. Instead, individual items within the group are associated to each of the other items with a certain probability. The  fragment model differs from the previous two by assuming that item associations are indirect (as in the schema model), but are  correlated instead of independent. The model, based on a theory by Jones (1976), assumes that the related items which  constitute a particular memory event are organized into one of many possible fragment patterns, each occurring with a certain  probability. Cuing with a category word enables the subject to retrieve all of the items that may reside in the same fragment,  but none of the items in other fragments.  Ross and Bower applied these three models to the data from a series of experiments. In all, the schema model provided a  somewhat better fit of the data than the horizontal model, while the fragment model faired poorly. In one experiment, subjects  were informed about the relations between the items in each group, while control subjects were not. For both the schema and  horizontal models, the learning  ANALYSIS OF CATEGORY CLUSTERING 101  parameters were higher for the informed versus the control group, although these differences failed to reach significance.  Ross and Bower asserted that better control of subjects' memorization strategies in each group should result in more clearcut  differences in the parameter estimates for each model. In turn, this type of further experimentation might yield a better test  of the models.  Batchelder and Riefer's Statistical Model  An additional model for category clustering, and one which forms the basis for the new model presented in the next section,  was provided by Batchelder and Riefer (1980). The general goal of Batchelder and Riefer was to generate a method for examining  the subsurface processes of storage and retrieval in category clustering. They accomplished this by developing a simple  statistical model which can be used to analyze this type of data. By making some elementary assumptions, the model is capable  of dividing recall into separate storage and retrieval parameters for clustering. The statistical model is therefore another  example of a theoretically motivated method of analyzing clustering data. By applying the statistical model to a series of clustering experiments, Batchelder and Riefer demonstrated that the model  could be a potentially useful tool in the analysis of clustering data. However, the model does contain one major limitation.  This is the fact that it can be applied only to categories containing two items. As Batchelder and Riefer point out, this  renders the statistical model inappropriate as a model for clustering in general. One of the problems lies in the fact that  the theory behind the model is not complete enough to enable the model to be generalized in any straightforward manner. This  centers around the question of exactly how clusters are represented within memory for larger category sizes. Although the  statistical model is useful for disentangling the opposing effects of storage and retrieval, it says very little concerning  how clusters are formed and accessed. Basically, the model assumes that when clusters are retrieved, the members of the  category pair are both output adjacently. Thus, the retrieval of clusters is assumed to be all or none, an assumption which  simplifies the formulation and application of the model.  This is in contrast with those theories of clustering (e.g., Rotondo, 1979; Rundus, 1973; Slamecka, 1972) which maintain that  clustered items are stored independently within memory along with information about their categorical relationships. These  theories are able to predict the individual retrieval of items within categories, whereas the statistical model assumes that  the individual recall of items is exclusively a function of nonclustered storage. Although this latter idea may be an adequate  approximation for two-item categories, the all or none assumption is probably too restrictive when three or more items belong  to a category. Thus, if the statistical model is to be extended reasonably to more general clustering phenomena, the all or  none asumption will surely have to be modified. Specifically, the processes of cluster formation and retrieval will have to be  specified within a more detailed theory of how clusters are stored within memory. A GENERAL STATISTICAL MODEL  In this section, a general statistical model is presented which is capable of examining storage and retrieval factors for  categories containing any number of items. The model is developed within the framework of a current theory which specifies how  category items are organized and stored within memory.  Certain researchers (e.g., Mathews & Tulving, 1973; Tulving & Pearlstone, 1966) have theorized that clustering takes place on  two hierarchical levels. Categorically related items are stored within memory on a lower level with no direct associative  connections between them (Slamecka, 1968, 1972). Access to individual items does not occur directly, but instead must be  mediated through a higher order unit, which in essence represents the name of the category. For example, the category items  "tennis, soccer, baseball" might be stored within memory under the higher order labeling "sports." The higher order unit acts  as a control element (Estes, 1972) through which category items may be retrieved, and is thus analogous to Rotondo's "chunk"  and Ross and Bower's "grouping node." During recall, in order to retrieve the category items, subjects must first access the  control element for those items; i.e., they must first remember that "sports" is a relevant category within the list of words.  Item recall thus requires a two-step process in which the higher oder unit is accessed, followed by the retrieval of whatever  individual category items are associated to it.  The general statistical model attempts to capture the cognitive processes mentioned in the above theory as parameters within  the model, in a manner similar to that used in the original two-item statistical model. Specifically, the model assumes that  the recall of category items is a function of the following dichotomous events:  (1) Formation of control elements. For any category of size n within a list, it is assumed that the higher order unit is or is  not stored within memory for that category. Define cto be the probability that the control element is stored. In the present  context, the higher order units are defined only for the relevant experimentergenerated categories within the list. Any  idiosyncractic clustering by subjects is treated by the model as nonclustered learning.  (2) Access of control elements. The higher order unit may or may not be accessed during recall. Define r,, to be the  probability of retrieving a control element.  (3) Association of category items. It might be reasonable to assume that category items are associated independently to the  higher order unit, each with some probability less than or equal to one. However, this allows for the possibility that a  category label might be formed without any individual items associated to it. To eliminate this possibility, the assumption is  made that the formation of the control element takes place when two items are recognized as coming from the same category.  These two category items are then automatically associated to the newly formed category label. A number of different methods are available for estimating the parameters of a model when the number of data events is greater  than the number of unknown parameters. One method which is commonly used is to find those parameter values which minimize the  chi-square difference between the observed data and the predictions of the model. A number of computer subroutines exist which  are capable of searching for the minimum value of such a multivariate function. The minimization program which was used to  evaluate the general statistical model is STEPIT (Chandler, 1965). STEPIT was applied under the constraint that the final  parameter estimates had to be within the [0, 1 ] interval.  One problem which should not be ignored when minimizing a chi-square function is the possibility that some expected cell  frequencies may be very small. Since the squared terms in a chi-square are each divided by the expected frequencies,  artificially high chi-square values and unstable parameter estimates may result if the cell frequencies are too small. This is  a very real problem with the current model, since many of the data events for larger category sizes are particularly rare.  These mainly consist of those events involving the nonadjacent recall of items (e.g., SISISIS or SSISISIS).  One way to ease this problem is to divide the recall for categories into a smaller number of data events, so that the  observations for each combined event are more frequent. One possible method for accomplishing this is to divide recall into  its adjacent and nonadjacent components for each number of items recalled. This simply involves combining for each category  size those events which contain at least one nonadjacently recalled item. Thus, for example, the events SSSIS, SSISIS, and  S/S/S/S would be combined into the single event: 4 recalled, some nonadjacently.  One advantage to this approach is that a single equation can now be used for the predictions of the model for any category  size. In the next section, the performance of the general statistical model will be evaluated using the data from a series of  experiments. It will be of particular interest if the parameter values turn out to change in a reasonable manner as a function  of certain crucial experimental manipulations. Of these, the most important variable is the size of each category. It is  expected that as the number of exemplars in a category increases, the probability of storing a representation of that category  in memory (as represented by the parameter c) should also increase. The effects of within-category spacing will also be of  interest. Batchelder and Riefer (1980) found that, for category pairs, the values of parameter c decreased with lag while the  values of r increased across lag. It will be interesting to see if this finding extends to larger category sizes. In addition,  the variables of repeated trials and type of presentation (cued versus uncued) will be examined in the experiments.  3. APPLICATION OF THE MODEL TO EXPERIMENTAL DATA  In this section, the general statistical model will be tested on the data from two. experiments, in order to see if the model  is capable of providing a reasonable fit to the data, and also to see if the resultant parameter estimates are readily  interpretable. First, however, it will be necessary to describe the experiments. Each of the  108 DAVID M. RIEFER  experiments employs the same basic design and procedure, which will be described first. After this, information specific to  each experiment will be given as the experiments are presented.  Common Method for Each Experiment  Subjects. Subjects in each of the experiments were undergraduate students from the University of California, Irvine, who  earned extra credit in an Introductory Psychology course for participating in the experiment.  Materials. Subjects visually received a list of words for memorization. The words were selected from categories in the Battig  and Montague (1969) norms. Each list contained a primacy and recency buffer region between 7 and 8 items in size, which also  consisted of categorical items. The buffer items were not included in any of the analyses. The categories in the experiments  contain a number of items ranging from one to five items per category. The items were selected randomly from a set of five  category exemplars. The order of presentation for the buffer items, as well as for the items within each category, was  randomized for each of the subjects.  Procedure. Subjects were seated in individual cubicles and told that they would be memorizing a list of words from various  categories. Presentation of the list was computer controlled and items were displayed on either a Tektronix 1040 or Datamedia  1520 terminal. The items appeared one at a time with an exposure rate of five seconds per item. After presentation of the  list, subjects were given a written recall period of a fixed time length ranging from 3 to 4 min to freely recall whatever  words from the list they could remember. For those experiments using repeated trials, this basic procedure was repeated with  the same order of presentation for items until the end of the session.  Experiment 1  Experiment 1 was conducted to study the effects of category size, within-category spacing, and repeated trials on the recall  of category items. Fifty-nine subjects participated in the experiment. Subjects received a list of words which were from  categories of size 2, 3, and 5, with each size represented by five categories. There were also five singletons presented in  the list. Category items were separated by lags of 0, 2, 4, 8, and 12 intervening items. After presentation of the list,  subjects had 4 min to freely recall all the words they could remember. This process was repeated for a total of three study  trials. Discussion of Results  In general, the overall performance of the general statistical model seems quite satisfactory. The model does an adequate job  of fitting the data from the experiments, and the changes in the estimates of the parameters across certain variables are, for  the most part, as expected. Perhaps the most important variable, and certainly the one that separates the general model from  Batchelder and Riefer's two-item model, is the variable of category size. The model demonstrated that the estimates of both  the clustering and retrieval parameters c and r, increased systematically as category size increased. In contrast, the  probability that items were learned singularly decreased with category size, while the probability of associating items to the  category label seemed to be unaffected by the size of the category. Since the original statistical model is restricted to  two-item categories, the effects of category size can only be examined by the general statistical model.  Another result of the general statistical model which could not have been successfully examined with the original two-item  model is the effect of cued versus uncued presentation. The main effects of presentation are on the association and singleton  parameters a and u, and not particularly on the clustering and retrieval parameters c and r. Since parameters a and u are not  estimatable for categories of size two (using the maximum likelihood estimates), any theoretical differences between cued and  uncued presentation would probably not have been discovered using just the original statistical model. COMPARISON OF CLUSTERING MEASURES  The success of the general statistical model in analyzing the data from clustering experiments demonstrates that the model can  be a potentially useful tool in the analysis of category clustering. In this section, the advantages of the model over other  methods of cluster measurement will be discussed.  As a method for the measurement of clustering, the general statistical model has an advantage over clustering indices because  the model attempts to measure clustering from a theoretically motivated background. Clustering indices based solely on  empirical trends in the data, such as those which compute the number of runs or the overall rate of adjacent recall, cannot be  considered to truly measure the psychological process of clustering. In addition, single measures of clustering cannot totally  account for the multiprocess nature of categorical recall. Empirical statistics, including clustering indices, are often the  end result of a number of different cognitive processes. In contrast, estimates of clustering based on a theory of how these  processes operate are capable of separately measuring these processes and providing a clearer picture of category clustering.  Other theoretically motivated analyses of clustering have been proposed in addition to the general statistical model. The most  notable of these are Rotondo's discrete structural model for category clustering, and Ross and Bower's schema model for item  cuing. The basic theory behind these models is essentially the same as that for the general statistical model. In each of the  models, items are assumed to be organized, not to one another, but to a higher order memory unit. This unit is refered to as a  chunk in Rotondo's model, and as a grouping node in Ross and Bower's model. An additional assumption is that, during recall,  the higher order unit must first be retrieved before any of the individual items associated to it can be recalled. Ross and  Bower examined the special case in which category items are cued for recall with other items from the same category. Rotondo's  model, like the general statistical model, can be applied to situations in which the recall of items is either free or cued  with the name of the category. Because they both handle the same type of experimental tasks, the general statistical model  will be compared primarily to Rotondo's single chunk model in the discussion to follow. Although the single chunk  118 DAVID M. RIEFER  model is only one version of the more general discrete structural model, it seems to be the most appropriate version for  analyzing the recall of items in individual, nonoverlapping categories.  The major difference between the new model presented here and Rotondo's model is that the general statistical model attempts  to study clustering by examining the adjacent versus nonadjacent recall of category items, while Rotondo's model does not.  Rotondo instead attempted to define clustering only in terms of the cooccurrence of related items. Rotondo assumed that any  nonrandom co-occurrence of items is the end result of two separate processes. First, during study, subjects attempt to  individually associate category items to a chunk. Second, during recall, subjects try to retrieve the chunks in order to  recall the items associated to them. These are theoretical assumptions which Rotondo's model shares with the general  statistical model.  However, unlike the statistical model, Rotondo's single chunk model incorporates no procedure for the storage of items in the  event that items are not clustered. Rotondo assumes in his model that items are either associated to a chunk or not learned at  all. As a consequence, the model does not allow for the possibility that items can remain unclustered, but at the same time be  learned as singletons. In addition, the single chunk model assumes that the chunk is always formed during study, and thus does  not consider what might occur when the chunk is not formed. Although other versions of Rotondo's more general model can be  generated to allow for items to be recalled as singletons, the model requires that singletons be identified beforehand by a  specific hypothesis. In other words, in order to examine nonclustered recall, it is necessary to specify each item as a  singleton or not in advance of the application of the model to the data.  In contrast, the general statistical model does include the process of recalling nonclustered items, and routinely allows each  item to have some probability of being recalled as a singleton. The model accomplishes this by examining the nonadjacent  recall of items. The assumption is that a decrease in clustering and a subsequent increase in the learning of items as  singletons will empirically result in more nonadjacent recall of category items. This is an important process for any general  model of category clustering, since many independent variables can be assumed to directly influence the amount of clustering  For Rotondo's model, any changes in the overall level of clustering in an experiment would have to be reflected in one or both  of the two clustering parameters of the model. Theoretically, a variable which inhibits clustering would have to do so by  reducing either the parameter #, the "chunk strength," or the parameters e;, the "membership strengths" of items to the  chunks. However, a major assumption behind Rotondo's model is that the successful recall of an item can only be mediated by  these two processes; i.e., by associating the item to its chunk and accessing that chunk during recall. If either of these  processes occurs with less frequency, then the probability of recalling an item, according to the model, will also be less.  As a general rule, it is reasonable to expect that any variable which causes a reduction in clustering will also likely result  in a reduction in the overall recall.  ANALYSIS OF CATEGORY CLUSTERING 119  However, there are many possible cases in which the level of clustering might decrease without a corresponding decrease in the  recall of items. Specifically, if a decrease in the amount of clustering is accompanied by an increase in the probability that  items are stored independently, then the individual item recall may remain unchanged or even increase, depending on the rate  with which unclustered items are stored and recalled. A very striking example of this was found in Experiment 2. In that  experiment, the uncued presentation of items actually resulted in a higher probability of recall P(c), as well as P(cat), the  probability of recalling any item from a category. As another example, researchers who have studied the effects of lag on  clustering (e.g., Batchelder & Riefer, 1980; Borges & Mandler, 1972; Elmes & Wright, 1970) have found that the probability of  recall is often just as high or higher for large lags as for small lags.  Thus, it is not uncommon to find situations in which clustering decreases without a corresponding drop in the rate of recall.  The question can then be raised concerning how Rotondo's single chunk model would perform when applied to data of this type.  The most direct approach towards answering this question would be to consider what happens to the co-occurrence of items as  clustering becomes less frequent. Empirically, a decrease in clustering would result in items being learned more independently  in a some or none (as opposed to an all or none) pattern. The effect this would have on Rotondo's model would be to lower the  values of the association parameters e;. However, if empirical recall remains constant, the only way that the model could  account for this result would be with a corresponding increase in parameter#. Thus, situations might arise in which variables  that theoretically should cause less clustering actually result in higher chunk strengths as measured by Rotondo's model.  As a demonstration of this point, Rotondo's single chunk model was applied to the data in Experiment 2. As indicated by the  general statistical model, items in that experiment were not associated to their category label with as high a probability  when presentation was uncued, but did exhibit a higher probability of being learned as singletons. If Rotondo's model performs  as expected, the inhibition of clustering caused by uncued presentation should actually result in higher values of the  clustering parameter#. Since items in Experiment 2 came from categories of size three, there are 23 or eight error-success  events which can be used to estimate the parameters of the model. The model was fit to this data by finding those parameter  values which minimized the chi-square difference between the predictions of the model and the observable data. Although this  was not the estimation method employed by Rotondo, this procedure was used to help equate the performance of Rotondo's model  to the general statistical model. Any useful mathematical method for the measurement of clustering would hopefully take these two possibilities into  consideration, and therefore contain a clustering parameter which is sensitive to the overall level of clustering in the  experiment. By examining only the co-occurrence in the recall -of items, Rotondo's model cannot measure the amount of  clustering in an experiment with relation to items that may be stored independently. The general statistical model, by also  considering the structure of adjacent versus nonadjacent recall, can measure both the level of clustering in an experiment as  well as the probability that items are recalled as singletons. Because of this, the statistical model has the potential to be  a more useful analytical tool in the study of category clustering.  S. CONCLUSION  The purpose of this article has been to apply the methods of mathematical modeling to create a theoretically motivated  analysis of category clustering. This was accomplished through the development of a general statistical model which can be  used to analyze the cognitive processes involved in the learning of semantically related items. The model is an extension of  Batchelder and Riefer's two-item statistical model for clustering, and has the property of reducing to that model for  categories of size two. The general statistical model also has the desirable property that the number of the parameters does  not increase as category size increases. This is true despite the fact that the number of observable data events increases  with larger category sizes. The axioms of the model are based on current theories about how category items are stored within  memory. This includes the concept of a control element or higher order unit for access to items in a category, and the  independent storage of items within their control element. Finally, the model has been shown to be advantageous over empirical  measures of clustering, as well as having certain conceptual advantages over other mathematical models which have been  proposed for the analysis of clustering.  There are some limitations of the general statistical model that are worth mentioning. First, the model cannot fully account  for the effects of input order on the recall of category items. The data events which are used to estimate the parameters must  first be combined across the possible output orders before the model can be applied. This is a disadvantage because clear  order effects have been observed in previous research (Gorfein, Arbak, Phillips & Squillace, 1976; Greitzer, 1976; Underwood &  Freund, 1968). This is an area where Rotondo's model has a conceptual advantage over the current model, since Rotondo's model  is capable of estimating separate membership strengths for individual category items. Rotondo's analysis is therefore useful  for examining item or subject differences in the data.  122  DAVID M. RIEFER  Another advantage of Rotondo's model is that it can be used to examine situations where items belong to more than one  category, or are hierarchically related. In contrast, the general statistical model assumes that clusters do not overlap and  is thus incapable of analyzing data of this type.  Although the general statistical model is capable of estimating singleton retrieval, this comes at the expense of making  output assumptions that may not be totally accurate in all situations. For example, the model assumes that if an item is  recalled nonadjacently by a subject, then it must be functioning as a singleton for that subject. Yet it is possible that a  given category could be searched several times during the course of recall in order to retrieve items that were not accessed  during the initial search. This could lead to the retrieval of nonadjacently recalled category items at the end of recall. The  model also assumes that at least two category members must be recalled adjacently if the cluster to which they belong has been  accessed. This is an assumption that enables the general statistical model to reduce to the two-item statistical model for  categories of size two. However, the model thus precludes the possibility of a single item being retrieved from a cluster.  Another limitation of the model is the fact that the theory behind the model fails to capture the dynamic processing of  category items. In other words, the model describes no state-to-state transition processes by which items are stored,  consolidated, retrieved, or forgotten. As such, the model is incapable of describing changes in recall over lags or trials  with a single unified set of parameters, such as could be accomplished with a more general Markov model. Instead, the model  must compute separate parameter estimates for each lag and trial.  Despite these limitations, the general statistical model can still be of considerable value as an analytical tool. As  demonstrated in this article, the model provides a way of measuring nonsurface factors in clustering tasks such as the storage  and retrieval of the category unit, the association of items, and the independent learning of nonclustered items. Thus the  model can be used as a supplement to empirical analysis of experimental data as a means of gathering information concerning  the cognitive processes involved in clustering. The general statistical model could be used, for example, to investigate the  effects of aging, drugs, and even more task-oriented variables on these subsurface processes. Some experimental variables  which could be examined to see their effect on the parameters include free versus cued recall and the level of association of  category members. In addition, further studies are being conducted by this author in an attempt to use the general statistical  model to investigate certain research areas involving category clustering, including Slamecka's (1968, 1972) independent  storage hypothesis and the within-category order effect (Greitzer, 1976; Underwood & Freund, 1968).
