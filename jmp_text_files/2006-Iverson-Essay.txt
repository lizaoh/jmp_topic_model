Following the 2004 annual meetings of the Society for Mathematical Psychology at the University of Michigan, Ann Arbor, a banquet was held to honor Jean-Claude Falmagne on the occasion of his 70th birthday. On that happy occasion, Jean-Claude read an essay he had written on the current and possible future state of Mathematical Psychology as he saw things ( Falmagne, 2005 ). Subsequently he asked me to write a short essay on a topic that I thought might provide some direction for future research in mathematical psychology. My thoughts on the matter led to this essay on order restricted inference.

On the one hand, I had long believed that order restricted inference provides an important statistical methodology, with clear and uncontroversial applications to the analysis and interpretation of social science data. On the other hand, this tool was not being put to use by social scientists. In this Journal, for instance, I found exactly one paper in the last ten years that used the methods of order restricted inference ( Logvinenko, Tyurin, & Sawey, 2002 ). Such a lack of interest did not seem to me to accord with widespread applications elsewhere. A Google web search using the phrase “order restricted inference” gave the output: Results  1–10  of about  1,960,000 ; the phrase “analysis of variance” gave the output: Results  1–10  of about  14,400,000 ; and for comparison the phrase “Luce's choice axiom” produced the output: Results  1–10  of about  346 .

I do not mean to over-emphasize the advantages of order restricted inference over ANOVA; these methodologies are complementary, not antagonistic. Whereas ANOVA is about  equations  (that define linear subpaces of a parameter space), the theory of order restricted inference is about testing linear  inequalities  (that define convex polyhedra). One difference between the two methodologies involves the interpretation of  interactions ; often, in order-restricted models, interactions do not arise separately from main effects—they are part and parcel of the same constraints, and this fact facilitates the interpretation and reporting of data.

Relations of “more difficult”, “louder”, “brighter”, “slower” etc. arise frequently in experimental psychology, and it is just such order relations that allows one to define the corresponding notions of equality “just as difficult as”, “as loud as”, “as bright as” and “as slow as”. When experiments are reported, it is a commonplace that results are phrased in terms that reflect order relations:  when the lights are dimmed, reading scores go down ; and not merely  when the lights are dimmed, reading scores are affected. In (frequentist) statistical terms, non-directional tests incur a loss of power relative to their directional cousins and the analysis of experimental data in the behavioral sciences can ill afford to lose power needlessly.

The data tabulated (see  Table 1 
                   ) are from an observational study of social compliance that involved 70 student volunteer “experimenters”, 50 females and 20 males. Each experimenter worked alone; all experimenters performed the same experimental manipulation however, and carried out the same number of trials, namely 30.

Each experimenter visited LAX on a weekend and located a bank of public telephones in one of the many terminals. On each trial a quarter was placed, in an obvious position, on the sill of an unoccupied telephone. The experimenter then withdrew and waited until a member of the public used the telephone. Invariably the quarter was discovered, and was either pocketed or used to make a call. When the caller exited the telephone booth, the experimenter immediately walked up to the caller and made the following request to have the quarter returned:  
                      Excuse me, Sir/Ma’am, I believe I left a quarter by the ‘phone. Did you happen to find it?

Callers who returned the quarter were said to comply; compliance was recorded.

An explicit experimental manipulation (T or T*) accompanied every trial. A trial was labeled T (Touch) if during the request, the experimenter briefly touched the caller on the forearm in a socially appropriate manner. On the other half of the trials (T* trials), the experimenter did not touch callers. (A random sequence comprising equal numbers of T and T* labels was set up for each experimenter prior to data collection, so that experimenters did not choose whom they might like to touch.). In addition to recording compliance (Yes or No) on each trial, experimenters also recorded a caller's sex. On every trial experimenters were instructed to maintain a fixed distance between themselves and callers.

Based on prior relevant literature (see e.g.,  Hornik, 1987 ;  Kleinke, 1977 ;  Willis, 1980 ), and my own past experience replicating the experiment, the following simple predictions emerge: for both male and female experimenters, the rate of compliance on different-sex trials is expected to exceed the rate on same-sex trials; T trials are expected to elicit a higher compliance rate than T* trials; and (for not very interesting reasons) female experimenters are predicted to elicit uniformly higher compliance rates than male experimenters. Bayesian methods for testing order-restricted models in the behavioral sciences are under active development by Herbert Hoijtink and his group at Utrecht in the Netherlands, and by Jay Myung, George Karabatsos and the author. Bayesian methods promise to provide a more flexible theoretical framework for order-restricted estimation and inference than is provided by the frequentist theory briefly sketched above.

Algebraic measurement theory  ( Krantz, Luce, Suppes, & Tversky, 1971 ; see also  Falmagne, 1985 ) provides a slew of inequality constraints that arise as key necessary conditions for the existence of a numerical representation of some qualitative relational structure: transitivity, double cancellation, and the quadruple condition, are just three of a long list of such constraints. Order restricted inference is, arguably, uniquely suited to the task of testing these inequality constraints. A general discussion of the parametric structure of probabilistic measurement models is given in  Iverson and Falmagne (1985) ; in that work a conventional likelihood ratio test of weak stochastic transitivity is developed and applied to the famous ‘intransitive’ preference data of  Tversky (1969) ; a re-analysis of Tversky's data from a Bayesian viewpoint is given in  Iverson, Myung, and Karabatsos (2006) .  Myung, Karabatsos, and Iverson (2005)  discuss how to test decision making axioms in a Bayesian framework.

Signal detection theory  provides a number of order-restricted problems. For example, one might want to estimate a yes–no ROC assuming only that it is a continuous, increasing, concave function (i.e., that the ROC is  proper ). This is a problem of order-restricted  estimation . In a similar vein, how does one test whether or not two empirically estimated ROCs are one and the same? Do two empirically determined points in hit/false-alarm space fall on a common (non-parametric) ROC or do they not?

Order-restricted inference is useful in two ways. First, it provides the machinery to confirm or reject theories that predict specific inequalities among observables. Second, experimental outcomes are often interpreted most naturally not in terms of equations (contrasts) but as order restrictions (one-sided contrasts), and exploiting this fact simplifies the communication of experiment results.

Postscript:  There has recently been an open email exchange between members of the Society for mathematical Psychology reacting to a piece by Jim Townsend, Rich Golden and Tom Wallsten written for  Psychological Science Agenda  ( Townsend, Golden & Wallsten, 2005 ) on the worrisome trend away from rigorous quantitative training in the behavioral sciences. There is much in that exchange echoes Falmagne's essay mentioned above ( Falmagne, 2005 ). It seems to be generally agreed that fewer and fewer students are finding themselves attracted to quantitative areas of psychology, all the more so for the more mathematically demanding disciplines. Susan Chipman points out in an open email that math SAT scores are probably  not  on the decline, with e.g., median scores for Barnard students constant at about 620 since 1994. Now a score of 620 does not indicate a student who is likely to be attracted to a quantitative area of science, including experimental psychology. In my experience (as graduate director of Cognitive Sciences at UC Irvine) the average foreign applicant typically sports a quantitative GRE score of 720 or higher. On paper at least, many domestically trained students are just not competitive with foreign-trained students, and the small but once consistent stream of domestic talent crossing over to the behavioral sciences from mathematics, physics and computer science is rapidly drying up.

To expand on an idea of Susan Chipman, we could put our heads together and come up with a series of MASS (mathematics and social sciences) publications aimed at attracting good undergraduate students to the field. Such an enterprise, however, seems to me to be beyond the rather limited resources of the Society of Mathematical Psychology, and would require the additional support and cooperation of the American Mathematical Association, the Psychometric Society, the APS and probably the APA. Who would be willing to coordinate such an effort?

At all levels, our system of education is geared towards maximizing graduation rates. Unfortunately this emphasis encourages teaching “by the numbers” with the result that concrete problem solving and rote learning are emphasized at the expense of a deeper understanding. In my experience things that are not well understood are quickly forgotten. In my own department all of our incoming graduate students are strongly advised to take a year of calculus before entering the program. Rarely do students appreciate what the fundamental theorem of the calculus is about, they do not recall the mean value theorem, and they have little residual knowledge of the elementary functions that drive much scientific discourse (the powers, the exponential, the logarithm and the trigonometric functions). They do remember once “knowing about” about such things, but really they never did “know” them, despite gaining As and Bs on local tests. Many of our incoming graduate students come in with “computer skills”. Some can design a web page, and many have acquired some experience with SPSS or similar “user friendly” statistics packages. But it is rare indeed to find a domestic student who has even rudimentary programming experience.
