INTRODUCTION  The use of various performance criteria is commonplace in experimental studies of learning and memory. Grant (1946, 1947) analyzed the "run" criterion (correct response in each of a specified number of successive trials) and computed the probability of reaching such a criterion by chance. His tables then permitted the determination of the confidence level with which the null hypothesis (e.g., of no learning) could be discarded.  More recently Bogartz (1965) treated the subject of such criteria in a much more general framework. Two general classes of criteria were analyzed mathematically and discussed from the standpoint of applicability. The first class, not to be considered further here, included criteria based on analysis of blocked trials (at least r correct responses in each of Z' successive blocks of s trials). The second class of criteria contained those based on a running analysis over unblocked trials (at least r correct responses in s consecutive trials); Grant's criterion is a special case (r = s), and our attention will be directed to another special case, r = s - 1.  A subject performing by chance could attain even the most demanding criterion if he were allowed an unlimited number of trials; hence the object of the study of these criteria is a determination of the maximum number of trials for which the probability of chance achievement of criterion is less than some predetermined value, called the "criterion risk" by Bogartz. Tested against exact values for small s, the approximation appeared reasonably good and (for r < s) conservative in the sense that the number of trials permitted was too small. The uncertainty with the approximation, however, raises the dual problems of possibly falsely rejecting the null hypothesis if the limits are not conservative and possible unnecessary experiments if indeed they are conservative.  Since many of the limits in question represent criteria important in practice, we report a significant extension of the exact entries in the tables. Our technique differs significantly from that of Bogartz in a way that permits exact calculations for criteria of considerably longer generalized runs (up to s = 20 in some cases). The computations presented here are concerned with the most important case for which exact limits are lacking-the case r = s - 1, in which the subject is allowed at most one error in an otherwise unbroken chain of correct responses. We shall see that for all specific cases considered, Bogartz's approximation is in fact conservative by about 5 to 7% over a rather wide range of operationally significant experiment duration. SUMMARY  We have reexamined a special type of "run" criterion discussed earlier by Bogartz (1965)-the criterion of committing no more than one error in s consecutive trials. We have determined the maximum number of trials by which the criterion must be reached if the null hypothesis is to be discarded, at three levels of significance (..10, .05, and .01).
