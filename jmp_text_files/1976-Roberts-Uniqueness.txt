FRANKE  Department of Mathematics, Seton Hall University, South Orange, New Jersey 07079  This paper describes problems in the theory of uniqueness which occur when there are two different measurement homomorphisms f  and g with g not attainable from f by a transformation on the range off. In this case, the theories of scale type and of  meaningfulness of statements using scales are in trouble. It is shown that unless extreme care is taken in the definitions, it  is possible for two measurement homomorphisms of the same fixed relational system to have different scale type and it is  possible for seemingly meaningless statements to be judged meaningful.  In the approach to measurement taken by Scott and Suppes (1958), Suppes and Zinnes (1963), Pfanzagl (1968), Krantz, Luce,  Suppes & Tversky (1971), and others, there is a strong emphasis placed on the uniqueness of scales of measurement. One of the  reasons the theory of uniqueness is important is that it allows one to identify certain useful classes of scales. Stevens  (1946, 1951) was the first to make this identification. The theory of uniqueness also can be used to determine what statements  involving scales are meaningful. We shall argue that there are some potential problems with the theory of uniqueness as  spelled out in the measurement literature, unless the definitions are made extremely precise. These problems usually do not  come up, but when they do they can lead to difficulties in both the theory of scale type and the theory of meaningfulness.  These problems are avoided if one limits attention to what we shall call regular scales or if one deals only with irreducible  relational systems.  We shall adopt most of the measurement terminology of Scott and Suppes (1958) and Suppes and Zinnes (1963), in particular  their definition of homomorphism from one relational system to another. We have seen that under the first definition of admissible transformation, the theory of scale type is in some difficulty, at  least for irregular scales. One way around these difficulties is to modify the definition of scale type. Suppes and Zinnes  (1963, pp. 11, 12) say that (9C, 93, f ) is an ordinal scale if for every g in hom(9C, 93), there is a monotone increasing  transformation 0 such that g = 0 -f. (It should be added that conversely, for every monotone increasing 0, 0 o f is in hom(9C,  93).) This definition implies that f is regular. Thus, ordinal scales are not defined, according to the Suppes-Zinnes  definition, unless they are regular. This way out of the difficulty with scale type, it seems to us, hides the difficulty,  whereas it should be made explicit. We prefer the original definition of scale type. In any case, the way to salvage the  theory of scale type seems to be to speak only of regular scales.  The third definition of admissible transformation also can lead to confusion with the theory of scale type. An ordinal scale  would be defined as one whose admissible transformations were defined by composing f with a monotone increasing function 0 on  f (A). Even without the Suppes-Zinnes modification in this definition of scale type, it would follow that every ordinal scale  is regular. For if f is irregular, then there are admissible transformations other than those attainable by composition with  functions 0 on f (A), and so f could not be an ordinal scale (or for that matter a ratio scale, interval scale, etc.). Thus,  the Krantz et al. definition of admissible transformation again hides the fact that the theory of scale type applies only to  regular scales. It should be remarked that if a scale is regular, then the first and third definitions of admissible  transformation coincide: Every admissible transformation in the third sense can be looked at as a composition. It will now be clear that the first definition of admissible transformation also leads to problems in the theory of  meaningfulness. Suppes and Zinnes (1963, p. 66) define meaningfulness as follows. "A numerical statement is meaningful if and  only if its truth (or falsity) is constant under admissible scale transformations of any of its numerical assignments." This  definition runs into trouble under the first definition of admissible transformation, if there are irregular scales involved.  Returning to our semiorder example, let us consider the statement that f (0) = f (0.1). Under the first definition of  admissible transformation, this statement is meaningful according to the Suppes-Zinnes definition, for any admissible  transformation 0: f (A) --- >.Re has the  UNIQUENESS IN MEASUREMENT 217  property that (0 - f )(0) = (0 o f )(0.1). However, the statement is not meaningful under the third definition of admissible  transformation, for its truth value does depend on the choice of scale. It seems that the third definition is probably the  appropriate one as far as meaningfulness is concerned, and it allows us to continue using the Suppes-Zinnes definition of  meaningfulness. A similar problem arises whenever irregular scales are involved. Indeed, if the Suppes-Zinnes definition of  meaningfulness is adopted, then whenever there is an irregular scale (2C, Q3, f ), there is a statement which is meaningless  if Definition 3 of admissible transformation is used but meaningful if Definition 1 is used. For by a previous observation, if  f is irregular, there is a scale g and there are a, b in A such that f (a) = f (b) and g(a) =A g(b). Then the statement f (a)  = f (b) is meaningless under Definition 3 and meaningful under Definition 1.  In conclusion, we believe that it is best to adopt the Krantz et al. definition (Definition 3) of admissible transformation as  an all-inclusive definition, for it at least avoids the problems with meaningfulness. However, it should be made clear that if  f is irregular or the representation % --> !8 is irregular, the usual theory of scale type does not apply, or it applies only  if the equivalence relation E has been cancelled out. Moreover, it should be made clear that if f or the representation 2C -)-  0 is regular, then one may think of admissible transformations in terms of composition, and then the usual theory of scale  type applies.  In any case, the difficulties with the theory of uniqueness should be made explicit and care should be taken to define  admissible transformation, scale type, etc., as precisely as possible.
