When making inter-temporal choices between options, humans and animals discount the value of future rewards relative to more immediate rewards ( Du et al., 2002 ;  Killeen, 2009 ;  Mazur, 1984 ;  Myerson & Green, 1995 ;  Odum et al., 2020 ). That is, the present subjective value of a reward obtained at a later time decreases as a function of time to reward. The exact form of this decrease – the discounting function – remains debated, however ( Gilroy et al., 2017 ;  Gilroy & Hantula, 2018 ;  Mitchell et al., 2015 ;  Smith et al., 2023 ;  Vincent & Stewart, 2020 ). Several much-investigated functional forms exist ( Table 1 
                      ). We discuss their rationale and present an interpretation that encompasses them all.

Exponential discounting (1st function of  Table 1 ) is often considered natural because it is rational in many ways. It means that the recipient of the potential reward (the decision-maker henceforth) is discounting its value similarly over the entire waiting period. The rate of decrease in relative value with time is constant in time. This is rational in the sense that it is parsimonious to not change the rate without a reason. Exponential discounting is rational also from the economic perspective ( Killeen, 2009 ,  2015 ), or from perspective of exponential growth trajectories in nature. Due to the natural role of the exponential function in discounting delayed rewards, for example,  Houston and Houston (2019)  ensured their general measure of delay discounting respects it. Exponential discounting can also be expressed as a moderated vector autoregressive moving average model to find a process model for waiting ( Vanhasbroeck et al., 2024 ).

Quite often, various rewards from good life decisions do compound to some extent. A difficulty from the viewpoint of the decision-maker is that, outside restricted banking settings, the compound interest rate is difficult or impossible to estimate accurately. Indeed, the subjective value of the delayed reward often decreases [relative to immediate alternatives] according to a function other than the exponential delay discounting ( Mazur, 1984 ;  Myerson & Green, 1995 ;  Takahashi et al., 2008 ). Rather than tackling the decision-maker’s difficulty of knowing  λ , people have approached these other discounting functions from different viewpoints. In this paper, we instead present a comprehensive theoretical framework based on compounded interest and uncertainty about the compound interest rate. Furthermore, we develop the mathematics required by this theory as well as in an alternative theory based on the hazard of losing an anticipated reward.

This article is structured in the following way. We first review some of the formal approaches to delay discounting. Second, we review  Sozou’s (1998)  general account that explains several discounting models using the mathematics of uncertain reward-loss hazard. Third, we then argue that the point of view of uncertainty in cumulative returns also maps to  Sozou’s (1998)  mathematical formalism and is general and feasible and perhaps preferable to a hazards-based theory. Fourth, we extend the mathematical formalism, while simultaneously interpreting the novel results [from the viewpoint of uncertainty in returns, not in hazard]. Our contribution is both in re-interpreting the meaning of previous mathematical models and in the new results derived from them [which apply to both the interpretations, ours and Sozou’s], as well as in unifying the literature more broadly, much of which was not available before 1998. From here onwards, we use the general time variable  t  to index delay relative to an immediate reward at  t  = 0 (see  Table 1  for notation). We do not discuss probability discounting in the interest of space and because it may represent a different phenomenon ( Green et al., 2014 ;  Johnson et al., 2020 ).

Mazur first noted that the hyperbolic discounting function fits better to empirically observed choices between delayed rewards than the exponential discounting function ( Mazur, 1984 ). This function is 1/(1 +  kt ), where  k  is a free parameter representing the strength of discounting. Researchers often point out that when  t  = 1/ k  the value of the reward is halved, but this is just descriptive; it provides no rationale for decision-making. The following rationale has been presented, however ( Myerson & Green, 1995 ;  Rachlin et al., 1986 ). Assume that value is proportional to rate of reward and subjects respond as if they had repeated  sequential  choice opportunities with time-interval  m  between the receipt of the reward and the next choice opportunity. Then the rate of reward from the choice option delivering immediate reward  V 0 
                          is  V 0

                         /( m  +  t ). The relative value of the delayed reward is ( V 0

                         / m ) = 1/(1 +  t/m ), implying that the  k  of the hyperbolic discounting function stands for the inverse of inter-choice time,  k  = 1/ m .

The above explanation based on sequential choice has some merit. For example, post-reward delays in sequential tasks are strongly correlated with estimated  m  values in monkeys conducting sequential inter-temporal choices ( Blanchard et al., 2013 ). Yet, the sequential-choice-based explanation seems too simple an explanation for the observed generality of hyperbolic-like discounting [it risks being yet another over-general, monolithic, theory (cf. A. I.  Houston & Rosenström, 2023 )]. Assuming  k  was simply inverse inter-choice time cannot readily explain why amount offered in purely hypothetical questions affects  k  heavily ( Green et al., 2013 ;  Thaler, 1981 ). It is also difficult to explain why should roughly simultaneous hypothetical offers be treated as if they were sequential. The theory we put forth here is more aligned with such findings in humans and also provides possible reasons for the state- and trait-like qualities in human discounting ( Anokhin et al., 2015 ;  Odum et al., 2020 ), thus complementing the rate-maximization view of discounting.

The theoretical explanation for  k  in terms of inverse inter-choice time is also at odds with its frequent interpretation as a “behavioral economic index of impulsive preferences” ( Amlung et al., 2019 ). Low valuation of distant rewards as seen in steep discounting [high  k , low  m ] has been considered a sign of low self-control and psychopathology and has been found in different patient populations ( Amlung et al., 2019 ;  Turner et al., 2019 ). Reconciliation of these interpretations implies that impulsive preference amounts to, or is correlated with, subjective expectation of low inter-choice times. A belief that choices are sequential and that reward rates are maximized by systematically making many choices may not be what one thinks of when considering psychological impulsivity, however. Psychological impulsivity is often conceptualized as a tendency to act on a whim, without forethought or consideration of consequences. Maximization of long-term gain by persisting in behaviour requiring multiple small steps seems to require forethought. Having highlighted some problems in interpreting parameters commonly used in discounting functions, we next review a previous non-sequential model and then outline our preferred interpretation that, to our knowledge, is a novel one.

While taking into account the risk of losing a promised future reward is rational, this does not yet resonate well with many experimental settings (see, e.g.,  Thaler, 1981 ) or with the idea that steep discounting involves impulsive preferences. An expectation of a high hazard could fit with psychopathology in the sense of a pessimistic outlook towards ultimately getting a promised but delayed reward. Still, this is an exercise in mental gymnastics because the amounts offered in experiments are often hypothetical (i.e., not delivered anyway), and experimentalists may also strive to eliminate fear about the loss of a promised reward (e.g.,  Thaler, 1981 ). Furthermore, it makes little sense to generally expect a nearly ten-fold hazard for losing a hypothetical 100$ compared to a hypothetical 10$, as suggested by empirically observed amount-dependence in  k  ( Green et al., 2013 ). Rather, a small debt should be easier to forget than a slightly larger one. Further still, for example,  Peters and Büchel (2010)  found lesser discounting when delays were “tagged” with a personally relevant future event, but this manipulation was not associated with the subjects’ confidence in receiving the reward. Instead, the data of  Benoit et al. (2011)  “indicate that participants were especially likely to delay the receipt of a reward after having imagined its consumption with greatest emotional intensity”. Other authors have described further examples that challenge the generality of  Sozou’s (1998)  theory ( Dasgupta & Maskin, 2005 ).

We propose that humans, and perhaps animals, do not conceive  λ  as a hazard rate but rather as a compound interest rates of sorts. Their intuition on compounding shows in idioms like “success begets success” and “money comes to money”, and perhaps stems in part from personal observations that productive life plans are easier to make when one is well-resourced and not struggling to survive beyond the present-day hurdles. Although it is easy to dismiss the phenomenon of compounded interest as something unique to financial contexts, it is probably ubiquitous in the human condition. Researchers just use different terminologies for it. For example, a review by  DiPrete and Eirich (2006)  recognized how the concept of cumulative [compounded] advantage appears frequently in the social scientific literature, including analyses of themes like social mobility, poverty, race, crime, education, and human development. They listed the following terminologies related to compounding of interests: first-mover advantage, path-dependent increasing returns, and Gibrat’s law of proportional effect in the organizations, markets, and networks literature; multiplier effects in sociobiology literature; and reputational effects, positional goods, halo effects, the scarring effects of unemployment, and cumulative discrimination in sociology, social psychology, and economics.

Field experiments suggest that different kinds of success (money, quality ratings, awards, and endorsements) all produce significant improvements in subsequent rates of success of arbitrarily selected initial recipients ( van de Rijt et al., 2014 ). Evidence suggests “incremental differentiation” in team learning, wherein “teams that can consistently learn and adapt to changing conditions will continue to  compound  their advantages over other teams, […] which emphasizes the importance of speed of learning on performance” [italics added] ( Bradley & Aguinis, 2023 ). Besides its intrinsic reward, each outcome in a discounting experiment provides information about the setting, including monetary outcomes. Hence, it is difficult to experimentally isolate monetary outcomes from other outcomes, but perhaps it is also sometimes unnecessary. Researchers on the evolution of behaviour have argued that attention should be given to simple “rules of thumb” that perform well on average instead of narrow specializations ( McNamara & Houston, 2009 ). Based on this recommendation and the above-referenced literatures, it makes sense to considered compounded interests as something that humans may have evolved to take in account, either by the evolution of appropriate rules or by learning.

Tagging a monetary reward to a personally important “investment” ( Benoit et al., 2011 ;  Peters & Büchel, 2010 ) may simply prime a greater expected [subjective] compound interest rate, rather than greater fear of losing the promised reward – or lesser subjective compound interest rate if one expects to consume without investing. We next explore the discounting literature using this assumption and  Sozou’s (1998)  mathematical formalism of discounting as a Laplace transform. Whereas  Sozou’s (1998)  decision-makers place uncertainty on a hazard rate for losing a promised reward, those of  Dasgupta and Maskin (2005))  place uncertainty on early realization of a reward promised later, and those of  Story et al. (2024)  place uncertainty on reward amount, our decision-makers instead place uncertainty on a compound interest rate, which we show [by mathematical analysis] to lead to a simple, yet general, theory.

We now answer the question of why the hyperbolic function is a natural first approximation to rational discounting and then discuss the multi-parameter discounting functions of  Table 1 . As mentioned in the Introduction, exponential discounting is rational under the [sensible] assumption of compound interest – if one knows the compound interest rate  λ . We also noted that  λ  is typically not known to any high degree of accuracy. If  λ  is uncertain, a rational solution for arriving at relevant point estimates is to average over the uncertainty in the relative benefit from the choices. This leads to the same equation as  Eq. (1) , but now  λ  is merely the compound interest rate rather than a hazard rate. If further characterizations of the uncertainty of  λ  are not available, it makes sense to ask what  f  implies the maximal uncertainty about the magnitude of  λ . A uniform distribution is out of the question, because it makes no sense to assume that both small and arbitrarily high [infinite] compound interest are equally likely. If one is willing to expect some value  k  = E[ λ ] on the interval [0, ∞), then the most evenly distributed [maximum-entropy] distribution is the exponential distribution, that is,  Eq. (2) . The exponential distribution thus makes the least possible assumptions while admitting that the exact compound interest rate is an unknown quantity (e.g.,  Cover & Thomas, 2006 ). Thus, we notice, for the first time it seems, that the frequently observed hyperbolic discounting arises as a maximum-entropy solution. This both explains its ubiquity as the first approximation as well as the need to tune that minimum assumption in many contexts.

Expected exponential discounting (EED;  Eq. (1) ) is thus rational on average and can lead to hyperbolic discounting because  Eq. (2)  solves  Eq. (1)  with minimal assumptions. This is a transparent interpretation of the hyperbolic function compared to, for example,  Rachlin’s (2006)  claim that the hyperbolic form (dividing with 1 +  kt ) is “basic to the transformation of a magnitude (such as odds against) to a fraction (such as probability)”. Rachlin’s claim appears wrong because any cumulative probability distribution supported at [0, ∞) suffices to transform a magnitude to a fraction and infinitely many such distributions exist that lack division by 1 +  kt  specifically. A chi-squared distribution with two or more degrees of freedom would be one familiar example. Unlike explanations based on inter-choice interval, EED makes sense outside sequential foraging contexts [we note that roughly hyperbolic discounting has been observed outside such contexts (e.g.,  Green et al., 2013 ;  Kirby & Maraković, 1995 ;  Ostaszewski et al., 1998 )].

Discounting behaviour has often been considered irrational (e.g.,  Smith et al., 2023 , p. 384) because, e.g., hyperbolic discounting implies a change of preferences of the following kind. For example, a person with discount parameter  k  = 1 prefers 1 dollar after one day [value 1/(1 +  k 1)=½] to 2 dollars after four days [2/(1 +  k 4)=2/5]. Add three more days of waiting to both the choice options and the person suddenly prefers 2 dollars over 1 [i.e., 2/(1 + 4 + 3)=¼>1/5 = 1/(1 + 1 + 3)]. Marginalizing (summing or integrating) over the uncertainty leads to hyperbolic-like discounting. So, in EED, preference switching is rational, not irrational.

The real strength of the EED approach stems from its ability to reproduce, explain, and unify most the discounting functions in the literature, and even to derive new ones. For example, consider  Sozou’s (1998)  above-discussed derivation of discounting with an exponentiated denominator from the Gamma-distributed hazard. In the context of EED, where uncertainty of compound interest rate instead of hazard is in question, a high-skewed ( s  < 1) Gamma-distributed  λ  amounts to a subjective expectation of an environment where little compounding for rewards is the rule, with rare but significant exceptions. An economic analogy could be that most enterprises are small while “rare unicorns” hog profits; an evolutionary analogy could be that most prey go from hand to mouth but capturing a woolly mammoth allows the tribe to multiply and capture more woolly mammoths. A key point here is that observable or theoretically justifiable factors may alter the form of the discounting function from the minimum-information (maximum-entropy) assumption, hypergeometric discounting, to something else. Thus, mixed findings in conceptual replications of empirical discounting functions might look like noise or errors to a researcher operating without EED. Viewed from the EED framework, they may become meaningful and understandable. Importantly, EED can also be used to find new discounting functions. For example,  Sozou (1998)  apparently gave the discounting function arising from uniformly distributed uncertainty simply because that seemed a mathematically obvious thing to look at (at least, he did not give a substantial justification). Much earlier,  Baum and Rachlin (1969) , and later  Rodriguez and Logue (1988) , had used a matching-law-based discounting function 1/ kt , where  k  was a scaling constant added by  Rodriguez and Logue (1988) . The model was seen as attractive but difficult, because “a truly immediate reinforcer will always be chosen over a slightly delayed one, regardless of their respective amounts” ( Rodriguez & Logue, 1988 , p. 106). Like many others, they continued with how Mazur had removed the difficulty by adding 1 to the denominator, leading to hypergeometric discounting. However, uniformly distributed uncertainty in EED (and Sozou’s theory) also appears to remove the difficulty, although the associated discounting function has not been similarly studied in empirical research. Because such approximately-matching discounting historically predates hypergeometric discounting, we give it in the second row of  Table 2 : notice how the discounting function (1 – exp(– kt ))/( kt ) approaches 1/ kt  as  t  grows large, whereas the numerator 1 – exp(– kt ) ≈ 0 for small  t , cancelling the explosion of (1 – exp(– kt ))/( kt ) as  t  → 0, i.e., cancelling “the difficulty”. Hence, if underlying compound interest rate might as well be any value from a closed interval, then according to EED, one might as well (approximately) match the reward value to its waiting time to arrive at the subjective value of a choice option. For illustrative purposes, we have here merely re-discovered a very old discounting function using EED. That is, we agree with the assessment of  Frederik et al. We have presented a novel interpretation of delay discounting – a theoretical mechanism by which decision-makers discount the current value of reward if it is obtained at future time rather than immediately. The theory describes rational behaviour within the limits of imperfect knowledge. It proposes that decision-makers rationally account for the natural phenomenon of compounded interests (use exponential discounting) but need to take an average or expected value over some uncertainty distribution for the compound interest rate. Hence, the name Expected Exponential Discounting (EED) theory of inter-temporal choice. We showed how EED theory provides a mechanism that unifies multiple empirically discovered quantitative discounting functions and fits to key qualitative findings. Below, we strive to point to some empirical evidence, opportunities for research, neural correlates and clinical implications, as well as suggesting future extensions of the theory.

Some relatively direct empirical evidence exists for EED theory. Inflation is related to compounding in the sense that under inflation interests  must  be compounded, lest their worth reduces. Indeed, decision-makers discount currencies under higher inflation rates more steeply than those with lower inflation rates, despite the same current values ( Ostaszewski et al., 1998 ). Furthermore, whereas people usually discount food more steeply than money, they have been found to discount an unstable currency (baht) more steeply than preservable food (rice) during a period of economic instability (M.  T. Takahashi et al., 2008 ). This case is straightforward: getting an amount of bahts now, buying rice, and selling after a month will lead to a gain in baht roughly equal to inflation. The compound interest rate in the currency of baht was high and that was obvious to all, so steep discounting ensues. In most cases, however, researchers may lack insight into what currency a decision-maker [perhaps unconsciously] wants to compound, meaning that it is difficult to directly probe the process of mental compounding.

While there appears to be both state- and trait-like qualities in discounting behaviours ( Odum et al., 2020 ), this too is broadly in line with EED theory. From the theory of evolution by natural selection, one predicts human and animal behaviour to depend on both their current state and their individual qualities (A. I.  Houston & McNamara, 1999 ;  Wolf & McNamara, 2012 ). Obviously, learning may influence behaviour. Hence, expectations about prevailing compound interest rates could, in principle, depend on past experiences, current states and individual body composition. EED allows fluctuating beliefs too. For example,  Sozou (1998)  demonstrates how  Eq. (1)  is mathematically equivalent to Bayesian updating of one’s belief about  λ  [although his interpretation of  Eq. (1)  differs from ours]. These arguments give considerable flexibility to EED, which requires care in empirical testing. We leave the considerable task of a comprehensive empirical review for future work.

Empirical researchers have focused on functions that best characterize discounting behaviour ( Table 1 ). They may benefit from EED. First, and as discussed in  Section 3.2 , otherwise seemingly mixed empirical results about discounting functions may become meaningful and understandable when viewed from the EED framework. This is because EED predicts how test subjects’ uncertainty about the compound interest rate affects their discounting behaviour, and because relationships may exist between the uncertainty and the subjects’ context or evolutionary history. For example, many human and animal behaviours may have evolved to align with the statistical properties of the organisms’ natural habitats ( Fawcett et al., 2014 ; A. I.  Houston, 2009 ;  McNamara, 1996 ). Even the receptive fields of the visual cortex cells appear to reflect natural image statistics ( Hyvärinen et al., 2009 ;  Olshausen & Field, 1996 ). Using EED theory, data from typical species-relevant compound interest rates in natural environments could be used to support the scientific status of a fitted discounting function, in addition to experimental discounting paradigms. Alternatively, a discounting function could be used to make inferences about uncertainty in reward compounding. This expansion of theory-relevant data space would happen through recognising EED-implied latent connections between uncertainty distributions and discounting functions (cf.  Table 2 ), which provide novel empirical reference points in addition to the old one, the discounting behaviour. For a known discounting function, a connection to time-series models may also be possible ( Vanhasbroeck et al., 2024 ), from which one could investigate optimal sampling-rate designs ( Adolf et al., 2021 ).

Second, although it may be difficult to distinguish the two-parameter discounting functions of  Table 1  using the standard discounting paradigms ( Rachlin, 2006 ), methods do exist for estimation of even very heavy-tailed compound interest rate distributions, such as Mittag-Leffler distributions (e.g.,  Cahoy, 2013 ). Therefore, where it holds, EED might improve the distinguishability between existing discounting functions provided that the researcher is able to make inferences on uncertainty in compounding. Humans and animals could form their belief distribution about the compound interest rate in many ways, some of which may involve higher cognitive functions. However, there is also a possible tangible neural account. Firing of the midbrain dopaminergic neurons closely matches the temporal prediction error signal of the temporal-difference reinforcement learning model ( Schultz et al., 1997 ;  Sutton & Barto, 2018 ). The model contains a discount factor that leads to exponential discounting of rewards. When multiple neural circuits instantiate temporal-difference reinforcement models with distinct discount factors and the circuit outputs are averaged, this leads to a distributed representation for the amount of discounting that can take, e.g., a hyperbolic shape ( Kurth-Nelson & Redish, 2009 ). The  f ( λ ) in the EED theory can represent the distribution of the distinct neural discount factors. Linking neuron-level reinforcement learning with majority votes of a population of neurons appears possible ( Urbanczik & Senn, 2009 ). Hence, EED could be further investigated from the computational neuroscience point of view.

Sometimes a “magnitude effect” is observed in which the discount rate decreases with the amount offered ( Baker et al., 2003 ;  Cruz Rambaud et al., 2018 ;  Mellis et al., 2017 ;  Thaler, 1981 ). This means that the discounting function is not separable into [a product of] time and amount. It is perhaps natural to suggest that discount rates could depend on the amount offered. Suppose a person habitually spends five dollars on candies if received as a single bill but places it into a bank account if received as a part of a 1000 dollars payment. The candies go sour fast and therefore suffer from a high inflation but the money in the account follows the domestic inflation rate minus interests of the savings account (until drawn). A future EED-based approach could allow the uncertainty distribution of the compound interest rate to change as a function of such expectations. While this sort of flexibility in the EED theory may appear a shortcoming when testing the theory, a flexible theory may be necessary in the face of discounting anomalies ( Cruz Rambaud et al., 2023 ). Flexibility is also consistent with the ideas that human and animal behaviour depends on internal and external world states (A. I.  Houston & McNamara, 1999 ).

Under the EED, steep discounting corresponds to an expectation of a large mean compound interest rate for one’s rewards, which is not the same as impulsive choice. On theoretical grounds, EED thus challenges the widely-held view of delay discounting as a measure of impulsivity or low self-control ( Amlung et al., 2019 ;  Anokhin et al., 2015 ;  Smith et al., 2023 ;  Turner et al., 2019 ). In empirical studies of sequential choices by animals, this view is challenged by data that suggests the animals are maximizing rates subject to cognitive constraints ( Blanchard et al., 2013 ), but these criticisms “do not apply to data from standard human intertemporal choice tasks, because they do not use postreward delays” (p. 15,495) and/or limit the next choice opportunity via current one. However, humans are generally more confident in their decisions when they reflect a correct or normative answer and  Bulley et al. (2022)  found that their test subjects’ confidence was not associated with choosing larger rewards later and supposedly delaying gratification. Instead, they concluded that their findings “challenge self-control views and instead cast intertemporal choice as a form of value-based decision-making about future possibilities” ( Bulley et al., 2022 , abstract), just as EED does on more theoretical grounds.

If intertemporal choice is not a clear signal of self-control, how then do we interpret the numerous clinical findings? First, the association between clinical status and discounting appears transdiagnostic rather than limited to disorders specifically characterized by low self-control ( Amlung et al., 2019 ), as is case for many individual-differences variables ( Caspi et al., 2023 ;  Caspi & Moffitt, 2018 ;  Rosenström et al., 2019 ). Furthermore, the association with clinical conditions is not particularly strong, with many studies not finding it ( Amlung et al., 2019 ). Interestingly,  Kochanowska et al. (2023)  investigated 116 participants for whom they did not find a significant association between depression or anxiety symptoms and discounting rate, but found associations between their within-individual variations in discounting and those in affect and arousal throughout daily life. Within individuals, pleasant affect and low arousal were associated with high discounting ( Kochanowska et al., 2023 ). This gives rise to a concern that clinical patients who participate in discounting experiments after their worst episodic phase has already passed may feel relatively calm compared to it, in which case a within-individual phenomenon could be mistaken as a between-individual difference [control participants presumably are not in a directed emotional rollercoaster]. More context-sensitive studies may be needed to properly understand intertemporal choices in clinical data. The within-individual findings of  Kochanowska et al. (2023)  are easier to rationalize from the point of view of EED. In a pleasant and calm state, people feel  optimistic  and, hence, they generally expect more lucrative investments compared to when feeling less optimistic [negative affect as well as threatening conditions can reduce unrealistic optimism ( Garrett et al., 2018 ;  Karnick et al., 2024 )]. That is, people may expect higher compound interest rates under recently improved moods and, therefore, discount steeply all investment delays. Yet, we do not rule out the possibility that self-control sometimes plays a role in discounting but rather question the generality of that explanation – nor do we question the wisdom in “examining the  reasons  why people should discount the future” ( Story et al., 2016 ).

For transparency, we repeat the limitations of EED here in one place. First, EED can accommodate many discounting functions and this generality of EED is both a limitation and a strength. The generality makes the theory hard to falsify (though not impossible; cf.  Theorem 1 ) but is also central to unifying multiple other functional forms or theories. Second, while general for functional forms, EED does not apply to all contexts where decision-makers discount future rewards. It is not a model for sequential choices where next choice opportunity depends on previous choice and its associated delay to reward. However, future work exploring whether EED has a role in sequential contexts could be of interest due to their theoretical importance ( Blanchard et al., 2013 ; A. I.  Houston & Rosenström, 2023 ). Third, EED is a new theory and thereby has not yet been a target of systematic empirical testing.

Researchers of intertemporal choice have started to question the field’s accepted wisdoms on empirical grounds, but little integrative theory has so far emerged. EED provides such a theory. It is a minimal theory in the sense of assuming only that rationality (exponential discounting) and imperfect information (unknown exact compound interest rates) are combined by averaging over the uncertainty in the prevailing compound interest rate. EED is also highly flexible, being able to cover multiple contesting descriptive theories. Yet, it is falsifiable in principle. As such, it may be a good baseline candidate to consider before proposing more complex constructs, such as the impulsivity model of steep discounting. It can also be used for predicting context-specificity in discounting behaviour.
