Knowledge space theory is an alternative to the theory of psychological tests. In it the collection of test items is regarded as a set  
                         Q

                         Q

                         Q

In the probabilistic versions of knowledge space theory probabilities are assigned to the states of the structure and handled as parameters. Moreover, one has to take into account that testees may guess the correct answer to an item they cannot solve (lucky guesses) or may carelessly give a wrong answer to an item they can solve correctly (careless errors). Altogether, one has to deal with

Papers keep appearing dealing with the problem of parameter estimation for probabilistic knowledge spaces. Essentially, some kind of initial estimation of the parameters is gradually refined as the testee works on the test items. The results he or she obtains are utilized to update the estimation.  Falmagne and Doignon (1988)  describe stochastic procedures which update a probability distribution over the knowledge states a person might be assigned to, see also  Falmagne and Doignon (2011, Chapters 11–13) . In the more recent developments such as  Stefanutti, Heller, Anselmi, and Robusto (2012)  the focus is to clarify identifiability of the models. Apart from the issue to prove identifiability efficient estimation algorithms are a big issue because the computational load of parameter estimation is enormous due to the large number of unknown parameters.

This problem becomes even more severe when one tries to utilize knowledge space theory as a background for digital educational games (see Section   2  for a short introduction). We want to assess a participants knowledge state. Preferably we would assign him or her to that state which he or she is most likely to belong to. Initially, we have in most cases no information about this state. Thus we take an initial probability distribution over the states. In the course of the game we want to improve this distribution utilizing the observed behavior of the participant. This is the idea of updating. The number of possible states is huge, the number of parameters likewise. This updating has to be done in real time because the selection of the next problem to be presented to the subject depends on the already available results. Thus, the probability that he or she is in a given knowledge state has to be estimated and continually corrected without interrupting the flow of the game (cf. Section   4 ).

In this paper we present a procedure based on updating an initial distribution on the states by partitioning the set of possible states of an underlying competence structure. The idea of partitioning rests on the observation that in a given situation not all of the millions of states are equally relevant for the assessment because the skills needed for most of the states may not be involved to solve the problem at hand. Thus, we can formulate an equivalence relation on the set of states. The probability distribution is calculated on the equivalence classes which is much easier to handle with respect to the time needed for updating. In  Augustin et al. (2013)  updating is done with the probability that a person can solve an item. This probability is transferred to the structure with the help of the surmise relation. This procedure proved too time consuming in the case of digital educational games. For this reason a new development was called for.

Finally, we emphasize that the present paper focuses on the theoretical foundation of the postulated assessment procedure, including the formal definitions and the mathematical proofs. The main ideas of this technique as well as applications are already published in  Augustin, Hockemeyer, Kickmeier-Rust, and Albert (2011)  to assess the skill of a person in an educational game. In that paper no proofs are given; they are presented here. The  Augustin et al. (2011)  paper is a less technical introduction, however it includes several instructive examples from the ELEKTRA ( http://kti.tugraz.at/css/projects/elektra/ ) and 80Days ( www.eightydays.eu ) projects.

Digital educational games (DEGs) are one of the hottest topics in current educational research and development ( Kickmeier-Rust et al., 2007; Prensky, 2001; Van Eck, 2006 ). The basic idea of digital educational games is to utilize the rich possibilities of state-of-the-art computer games for realizing entirely new and compelling learning experiences. The core strength of DEGs is that they serve the educational needs of the “digital natives”, who grew up with computer games, action movies, and the Internet ( Prensky, 2001 ). Note that according to authors like  Prensky (2001)  this setting has emphasized certain cognitive aspects and de-emphasized others. Thus, the demands on education have changed.

Despite the significant interest in DEGs, they cannot compete with non-educational, commercial games in terms of gaming experience. Furthermore, they cannot compete with educational technology in terms of educational quality: Although there are simple games for the very young children (e.g., teaching basic arithmetic), educational games for older children and adolescents are scarce. The reasons are manifold: First and foremost, the costs for developing high quality games are tremendous ( Van Eck, 2006 ). Secondly, an appropriate balance between learning-aspects and gaming-aspects is difficult to achieve. Finally, there are problems in aligning educational games with different national curricula.

It is important to note that one of the main advantages of digital educational games, namely their immersive motivational potential, is closely related to one of their main disadvantages, that is, their lack of adaptation to the learner in a psycho-pedagogical sense. However, an appropriate adaptation to preferences and interests, motivational and emotional states, the observed learning progress, and above all, to the learner’s abilities is of vital importance for being educationally effective and for retaining the user’s motivation to play and to learn. For obvious reasons, such an adaptation is not trivial: It requires a subtle interaction between the learner’s behavior within the game and the challenges throughout the game. Unfortunately, such an interaction is very fragile and more complex than personalization in conventional educational settings, mainly due to the coexistence of learning and gaming aspects.

Basically, research on adaptive tutoring has focused on adaptive presentation and adaptive navigation support ( De Bra, 2008 ). Most often, an adaptation to the user is achieved by asking for preferences, querying the learner, or providing typical test items for assessing the user’s knowledge and learning progress. However, this procedure is not feasible in digital educational games, since typical methods of knowledge assessment would seriously destroy immersion and flow and, consequently, also the gaming and learning process.

Since knowledge assessment within digital educational games is strongly restricted by the gameplay, the narrative, and the flow of the game, the present paper introduces a method to assess knowledge and learning progress in a  non-invasive  way. The basic target is to avoid any queries or interruptions, but to monitor the learner’s behavior in a gaming situation. Subsequently, the learner’s actions are interpreted in terms of his or her knowledge, and the next gaming situation is tailored to the (predicted) knowledge state of the user.

The concepts introduced in  Augustin et al. (2011)  and expanded in the present paper arose from applications in the ELEKTRA project on game-based learning ( http://kti.tugraz.at/css/projects/elektra/ ;  Kickmeier-Rust, Marte, Linek, Lalonde, & Albert, 2008 ). In this project, the probability updates were computed according to the classical updating rule by  Falmagne and Doignon (1988) , which can be formalized according to Eq.  (1) . The experiences with the ELEKTRA project led to the development of the “restricted updating rule”, which is defined on a restricted set of elementary competencies, and therefore, is less CPU-intensive than the classical updating rule (cf. Eq.  (5) ). The “restricted updating rule” was tested in the 80Days project ( http://www.eightydays.eu/ ) on game-based learning in geography and the MedCAP project ( http://kti.tugraz.at/css/projects/medcap/ ;  Albert, Hockemeyer, Kulcsar, & Shorten, 2007 ) on competence assessment in a medical domain.
