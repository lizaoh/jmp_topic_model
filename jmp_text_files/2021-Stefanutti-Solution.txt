Knowledge space theory (KST) was born in the eighties as a mathematical theory for the nonnumerical assessment of knowledge. Its natural area of application is education, as testified by the many theoretical and empirical contributions that can be found in the literature (see, e.g.,  Falmagne et al., 2013 ).

Stefanutti (2019)  recently provided an extension of KST, named procedural KST (PKST), for the assessment of human problem solving and planning. The approach provides a deterministic model for partially ordering individuals on the basis of their performances in problem-solving tasks. Such a deterministic model accounts for both the accuracy of the responses and, especially, the sequence of actions (the “observable solution process”) made by the problem solver.

In its current form, PKST is a deterministic theory. As such, its assumptions and models cannot be tested empirically through, for instance, standard goodness of fit statistics. In the present article the gap is filled, and a probabilistic framework is developed for the theory, where the problem solution process is modeled as a stochastic process. The proposed model satisfies the Markov property in the sense that, in a sequence of actions aimed at reaching some fixed goal, the future actions are conditionally independent of the past ones, given the present configuration of the environment (the “current problem state”), the goal, and the state of knowledge of the problem solver. Stochastic processes of this type are named “Markov solution processes”.

The modeling of problem solution processes is not new, neither inside nor outside KST. Applications of KST to digital learning games is an example. The approach followed by Augustin and collaborators ( Augustin et al., 2010, 2013, 2015 ) is closely related to the one proposed here. It links the notion of a problem space ( Newell et al., 1972 ) to that of a competence structure (see, e.g.,  Heller, Augustin et al., 2013 ) with the aim of assessing, in a noninvasive way, the skills of a user while she interacts with a digital learning game.

The present work has the broader scope of providing a stochastic model of the behavior of a human being while she is attempting to solve a problem or to reach a goal. The model can be empirically estimated and tested and can also serve as the basis for an adaptive procedure for assessing procedural knowledge.

Outside KST there is the important area of Markov decision processes (MDPs  Howard, 1960; Puterman, 2014 ), a mathematical framework for modeling decision making in environments with uncertain outcomes. The aim of MDPs is not much to model human behavior. Rather, they are applied in the area of machine learning – particularly, reinforcement learning – for solving optimization problems. In this context, learning automata ( Narendra & Thathachar, 1974 ) have close connections with the approach followed here.

The article is organized as follows. Section  2  provides the necessary backgrounds. The main contribution of the paper starts in Section  3 , where a special type of problem space, named the “goal space”, is introduced. Section  4  describes a Markov solution process model (MSPM) that is adequate for the empirical validation of the deterministic PKST. Section  5  presents simulation studies aimed at assessing the parameter recovery capabilities of the MSPM’s estimation procedures when a real problem space, and the corresponding knowledge space are used. The Tower of London (ToL;  Shallice, 1982 ) test has been chosen as the problem-solving task for testing the model. Sections  6 and 7  describe two empirical applications of the MSPM to a data set obtained by administering a computerized version of the ToL test. Section  8  concludes the argumentation with some final remarks.

The notions of “skill map”, “competence state” and “competence space” are borrowed from the so-called competence based extension of KST (CbKST, Ref...), where skills are latent constructs by definition. In procedural KST, the skills are replaced by (or eventually regarded as) sequences of operations. The operations can be either observable (like, e.g., in a motor task) or latent (like, e.g., cognitive operations). The set  
                            Ω

In this section, a simulation study is presented that was aimed at testing the parameter recovery of the Markov solution process model (MSPM) presented in Section  4 . To this aim, the framework of the Tower of London (ToL) test has been chosen as an example of the application of the approach.

The ToL is a well-known test for the assessment of planning ability in both clinical and non-clinical populations ( Berg & Byrd, 2002 ). It was developed by  Shallice (1982)  to assess deficits of planning in patients with lesions of the frontal lobe. A single problem state of the ToL consists of a horizontal wood bar on which three equally spaced pegs are mounted. The pegs have different heights. Three balls of different colors can be moved, one by one, from one peg to another. Starting from an initial configuration, called  initial state , the subject must achieve a given configuration, called  goal state . Thus, the subject must plan and execute a sequence of moves until a given goal state is achieved. The task is correctly performed if the goal state is obtained with a minimum number of moves. The parameter recovery of a probabilistic model can be analyzed correctly only if all of its parameters are identifiable. An informal way for testing the identifiability of the parameters of the MSPM was performed in the following way: (i) The model was estimated 50 times on the same sample (one of the simulated samples was used) using different starting points of the parameter space, and (ii) The standard deviation of the estimates that yield the largest and identical likelihoods was checked. Having available two different probabilistic models (i.e., the MSPM and the BLIM) for the empirical validation of a knowledge space, a comparison between the two would be interesting. However, a comparison by means of the standard model selection indexes (e.g., AIC) between the MSPM and the BLIM is not possible. This is because the BLIM is applied to dichotomous (correct/wrong) responses, whereas the MSPM is applied to the sequence of moves that leads to a given response. Nonetheless, a comparison between the MSPM and BLIM was performed on the parameter estimates.

The data were collected by using a computerized version of the ToL which was developed in MATLAB by the authors. Before starting the test, participants filled a brief questionnaire in order to check: (a) their previous experience with ToL test or with the Tower of Hanoi (ToH); and (b) any visual deficits, such as color blindness. Only an affirmative answer to this last question determined the exclusion from the sample.

The 31 ToL problems described in Section  5.1  were administered to each participant in a randomized order. After a detail description of the ToL task, participants were given the following instructions: (a) solving the problems with a minimum number of moves; (b) planning in advance; (c) being as fast as possible. Moreover, it was recalled that some problems could be solved in more than one way.

For every problem, the computerized ToL recorded each move until the participant made an error or correctly solved the problem. Irrespectively of a correct or a wrong answer to the problem, the participant was given a feedback. The feedback was: “Unfortunately, the last move you made was incorrect” in case of a wrong answer, or “Great! You got a solution in  
                            m 
                          moves, the minimum number of moves for this problem” in case of a correct answer.

No time restrictions were imposed for the solution of the problems. Each participant performed an initial practice trial, consisting of four problems whose goal state was different from the one used for the actual test. Two of these trial problems could be solved in two moves and the other two in three moves.

The number of participants was 156. Two participants were affected by color blindness, therefore their data were not considered in the analysis. Thus, the final sample size was 154. Seven participants had previous experience with ToL or ToH and the 76% of the sample were female.

The results of this study show that both the MSPM and the BLIM can be applied for validating knowledge spaces derived from a goal space. In terms of fit, the models provide consistent results. However, they account for different types of information in the data. In particular, the MSPM uses the information from both the accuracy of the responses and the observable solution process, whereas the BLIM only uses the accuracy. This may be the main reason for the differences between the estimated parameters of the two models.

This assumption could be too restrictive for modeling the solution process in this portion of the ToL problem space. However, this analysis is beyond the aims of this study.

In the literature, it is well established that different characteristics of a ToL problem other than the number of moves affect its difficulty. Some of these characteristics are: (i) the number of alternative solution paths of the problem; (ii) the start (or the goal) spacial arrangement of the balls on the pegs (named the “start hierarchy” and the “goal hierarchy”); (iii) the number of edges departing from a problem state (named “number of move choices”) (e.g,  Berg et al., 2010; Kaller et al., 2011, 2004; McKinlay et al., 2008; Newman & Pittman, 2007 ).

An extensive experiment in which it is possible to manipulate these characteristics one at the time, fixing the others could be very hard, for different reasons. Probably, one of the most important reasons is that the number of problems that would have to be administered to each participant is huge, leading to noisy data (e.g., due to learning and/or fatigue effects).

Stefanutti (2019)  recently developed a procedure for deriving a learning space (i.e., well-graded knowledge spaces) from a problem space. This approach makes available a deterministic model of how subjects can be partially ordered, in a given population, on the basis of their performances to a problem solving task. This deterministic model is built not only on the accuracy of the responses to the problems but, mostly, on the sequence of moves that a solver made for solving a problem.

Nevertheless, the problem space itself does not provide any explicit information about the eventual “correctness” of the actions undertaken by an individual, while she is attempting to solve a specific problem.

It consists in a latent class Markov model, called MSPM (i.e., Markov solution process model), in which the knowledge states are the latent classes and two different sets of transition probabilities are assumed. One of them is conditional to the belonging of a problem to the knowledge state (i.e., the problem solver is able to plan at least one of the problem’s solution paths) and the other one is conditional to the non belonging of a problem to the knowledge state (i.e., the problem solver is not able to plan any of the problem’s solution paths). An expectation–maximization algorithm for obtaining maximum likelihood estimates of these two sets of transition probabilities and of the knowledge state probabilities have been developed.

The Tower of London (ToL) test has been chosen as the problem-solving task on which to test the model. In particular, by using two different goal subspaces selected from the original problem space of the ToL, the model was tested in both a simulation study and an empirical application. The goodness of the parameter recovery of the model has been tested in the simulations and the capability of the model of predicting real data has been tested in the empirical application. In both cases, the model performed adequately. Moreover, a comparison between the estimates obtained by the BLIM with those obtained by the MSPM has revealed that despite the BLIM is able of reproducing the item error probabilities quite well, the knowledge states probabilities are estimated with less accuracy. This result is coherent with the fact that the BLIM uses less information in the data than the MSPM.

At least two issues should be explored in future works. First, the development of an adaptive procedure based on the MSPM for administering the problems is desirable. Such an adaptive procedure could update the likelihood function of the model by taking into account the whole solution path made by a problem solver instead of using only the correct or wrong answers to the problems (like in the case of the traditional adaptive assessments). The result will be a more efficient assessment since more information is exploited for performing it. At the same time, having available an adapting procedure for administering the problems, tests with a greater number of problems can be built, without the loss of reliability of the data due to fatigue effect.

The other issue that should deserve attention regards the application of the approach proposed here to other problem-solving tasks that are used for the assessment of cognitive skills. Some examples are the Mental rotation test, the Tower of Hanoi, and, in general, all the logical–mathematical tests where each problem must be solved through the application of a sequence of rules or operations.

Another field in which the approach could be applied is that of the educational games. Educational games can be used at almost all school levels for facilitating and maximizing learning. Typically, they are structured in a way in which students might solve a sequence of rule-based problems of increasing difficulty.  Albert et al. (2007)  and  Kickmeier-Rust and Albert (2010)  explored this possibility in previous works, by using the concept of “microadaptivity”. It is an approach that enables an educational game to intelligently monitor and interpret the learner’s behavior in the game’s virtual world in a non-invasive manner. On this basis, microadaptivity enables interventions, support, guidance or feedback in a meaningful, personalized way that is embedded in the game’s flow. Such type of architecture could be based on a problem space, in which the sequences of rule-based problems are well organized and linked to one other.

Thus, it seems that PKST provides the theoretical framework for several types of applications in the area of both cognitive psychology and education.
