Insight into one’s actions and abilities is often termed metacognition. Metacognition is a recurrent concept in many psychological fields of study ( Metcalfe & Shimamura, 1996 ), and metacognitive disability is a characteristic of clinical diagnoses such as schizophrenia, Alzheimer’s and certain injuries of the brain ( David, Bedford, Wiffen, & Gilleen, 2012 ). Metacognition is further linked to the consciousness literature as successful task performance without insight is often taken as an indication of unconscious processing ( Sandberg, Timmermans, Overgaard, & Cleeremans, 2010 ).

In the cognitive sciences, such processes and disorders are often investigated through cognitive and behavioural experiments, usually involving a complex setup with repeated measurements on several test subjects over various stimulus intensities and possibly other changes of experimental settings such as blocked trial runs. As we will explain further below, data from these experiments has traditionally been modelled based on so-called Signal Detection Theory (SDT) which has also adapted to metacognition through the introduction of sensitivity measures inspired by SDT. A shortcoming of these methods is that they are not sufficiently rich to accommodate more complex designs and for this reason they are often applied in a two stage strategy. First, one would calculate measures of interest using SDT and then afterwards apply a statistical analysis method such as regression on the obtained measures. This leads to an inefficient analysis and bias can occur especially when there is insufficient data to admit the calculations in the first step for all participants. In the present paper, we extend the statistical methods for metacognitive experiments by defining a model that incorporates the two steps allowing for flexible regression modelling of metacognitive sensitivity. We additionally derive some theoretical insights from this framework.

The basic structure of most cognitive experiments can be summarised as follows: In a single trial replication, a participant must first complete a task (e.g. identify which of two images was presented) and thereafter report a quantification of her/his confidence in, awareness of, or feeling of control over the performed task. The first assignment is usually dubbed the type 1 task and the reflection over the type 1 task is called the type 2 task. Various quantifications of the type 2 task have been proposed, such as verbal and numeric rating scales of confidence ( Dienes, 2007; Dienes, Altmann, Kwan, & Goode, 1995 ), awareness ( Ramsøy & Overgaard, 2004; Sandberg et al., 2010 ), or as a probability assessment ( Dienes, 2007; Fleming & Daw, 2017 ), as well as more indirect methods, for example allowing the participant to wager money on the correctness of the type 1 assertion ( Kiani & Shadlen, 2009; Persaud, McLeod, & Cowey, 2007 ).

A common assumption when modelling the responses of a cognitive experiment is that the participant’s decision is based on thresholds in an underlying evidence distribution so that the response changes when the signal crosses a threshold, a model referred to in psychology as Signal Detection Theory (SDT) ( Green & Swets, 1966 ). While the signal detection model remains ubiquitous in modelling cognitive experiments, the question of how to quantify metacognitive capabilities has been revisited and revised multiple times, see  Fleming and Lau (2014)  for a review. Additionally, later years have seen various expansions and alternatives to the SDT, some examples of which can be found in  Maniscalco and Lau (2016) .

An inadequacy shared by SDT and most similar models is that they are not sufficiently elaborate to directly include the complex designs of most cognitive experiments, particularly it is not clear how to account for multiple stimulus intensities or repeated measurements, and it is not obvious how to include experimental factors and participant characteristics as covariates to adjust comparisons or perform predictions. These shortfalls are often addressed by applying the SDT multiple times under various dichotomisations and stratifications with substantial loss of efficiency and potential introduction of bias as a consequence. Moreover, failure to account for sources of variation aside from that of the neurological processes can lead to biased estimates and erroneous inference.  Morey, Pratte, and Rouder (2008) , for example, showed how three classic results in recognition memory can be produced as artefacts of ignoring extra-variational factors such as task difficulty. A similar idea for type 1 SDT also underlies ( Wright, Horry, & Skagerberg, 2009 ) where the authors consider a simpler experiment with only a type 1 task and propose mixed model techniques to enable statistical analysis.

An important influence for SDT in metacognition is the paper by  Galvin, Podd, Drga, and Whitmore (2003) . An obvious alternative to the approach of  Galvin et al.  and an extension of SDT would be instead to consider a two-dimensional evidence distribution for type 1 and 2 responses. In consciousness and behavioural research, one such model proposed for memory research is the Stochastic Detection and Retrieval model of  Jang, Wallsten, and Huber (2012) . A bivariate model for source monitoring is found in  DeCarlo (2003)  while another, Bayesian, approach is explored by  Fleming and Daw (2017) . The introduction of additional processes to govern different response types can be motivated by noting that in metacognition, classic SDT may be said to constitute a “null” model, i.e. a situation in which there is no dissociation between the type 1 and type 2 processes. Thus, the standard SDT can be used to investigate metacognition only in an indirect manner, where one fits the standard SDT and then interprets significant deviations from the model as evidence of second order processing.

In our general model formulation of the meta-SDT in  (9)  we focused on location effects, i.e. effects that shift the mean of the latent variables. We could also have relaxed the variance structure on the latent variables and allowed this to depend on covariates leading to scale effects. A common scale effect in signal detection theory is found in the Unequal Variance SDT model, which in statistics is known as a heteroscedastic probit model (e.g.  DeCarlo, 2010 ), where the variance of the latent variable is allowed to depend on the presence or absence of a signal.

Note that it is not straightforward in which way to include scale effects in the meta-SDT model. A choice must be made not only as to the covariates entering with a scale effect, but also as to how the variance will vary between type 1, noise- and signal-specific responses. Care must be taken to ensure that the resulting model has identifiable parameters.

Additionally, it would be desirable to include random effects, as we have already explored in the simulations. However, estimation procedures for random effects are not implemented in the  metaSDTreg  package at the time of writing, as the likelihood function in this case contains an intractable integral and approximative procedures such as Gaussian quadrature or Laplace approximation are needed ( Demidenko, 2013  Sections 7.1–7.2).

Another aspect that might be worth investigating is the applicability of the closed form estimates in  (23) and (24) . Specifically, even though the error between the cumulative distribution functions of the Gaussian and logistic is small ( Demidenko, 2013 ), it would be interesting to investigate the error on the meta-

Apart from the theoretical value of such an investigation, precise, closed-form estimates may prove a robust manner of obtaining starting values for more advanced procedures.

An aspect that may need to be investigated in future developments is that of serial correlation, although this is generally challenging in non-linear models (e.g.  Breinegaard, Rabe-Hesketh, & Skrondal, 2015 ).

In our approach, we assume that the replications from a single participant may be assumed to be independent given the covariates (in particular the participant’s threshold and sensitivity). However, in many cognitive experiments it is easy to imagine that observations taken close together in time could be more correlated than observations far apart, in which case we say that the correlation is serial. Indeed, one might imagine negative serial correlation to occur if a participant were adverse to give a response having already given the same response multiple times in a row. Conversely in a visual study, the presence of a structure in an image might leave a “residue” that would prompt the participant to claim the structure as present in the following trial, thus leading to positive serial correlation.

The main difference between ( Fleming, 2017 ) and the approach in the present paper is that  Fleming (2017)  starts from the multinomial type 2 likelihood as found in  Maniscalco and Lau (2014) . Additionally, we are not aware that the approach offers regression capabilities. This is in contrast to the meta-SDT model which is a model for all the responses arising from the cognitive experiment, formulated through latent variables which is well-suited for expansions to include covariate information. Another difference is the manner in which inter-participant variation is modelled, where our approach directly parametrises this variation as effects whereas the Bayesian approach in  Fleming (2017)  uses hyperpriors to incorporate the extra variability.

There is no reason that the model we proposed in  (8) – (9)  could not be implemented in a Bayesian manner for example in  R  through the  brms -package. This would entail some considerations, which may be said to apply to Bayesian data modelling in general: namely, the choice of prior distributions and methods for assessing convergence of the Markov chain. Moreover, the importance of these considerations becomes increasingly critical in non-linear models as these often demand more informative priors to converge (as also discussed in  Bürkner, 2017 ). Thus, one would require prior knowledge of some or even all the parameters in the regression model, which may be difficult to obtain as the interpretation of each parameter would change with the inclusion of other covariates.

As a tentative way of comparing the methods, we applied the out-of-the-box response-specific, group level analysis of  Fleming (2017)  to some simulations from Simulation study 1 above. The details and results are presented in  Appendix E . These results, showing better performances of the MLE procedure over HMeta-d, should naturally be interpreted very cautiously as they represent simulations under a very specific scenario. A more exhaustive investigation of the performance of the various methods will have to be investigated in a large-scale simulation study, which is beyond the scope of this article.

We have presented a statistical model for metacognitive sensitivity, the meta-SDT, and have shown how it is well suited for expansion. Specifically, we can derive regression methods for metacognitive experiments. We have also shown that the standard signal detection theory model is equivalent to the proportional odds model, which is a widely used regression technique available in standard statistical software. We have made some theoretical points concerning the meta-SDT model and compared it to the partial proportional odds. Also, we supplied formulas for closed-form approximate estimates of meta-sensitivity and derived an optimal weight to combine response-specific measures. Finally, we described the basics of the implementation of the meta-SDT model in the  R -package  metaSDTreg , which is available on CRAN.

The meta-SDT model constitutes a theoretical contribution to signal detection theory and the theory on meta-
