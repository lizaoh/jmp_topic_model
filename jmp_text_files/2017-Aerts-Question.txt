To explain why the mathematical formalism of quantum theory is so adequate in the modeling of the human cognitive processes, as evidenced by the emerging field of ‘quantum cognition’ ( Aerts, Broekaert, Gabora, & Sozzo, 2013a; Aerts, Gabora, & Sozzo, 2013b; Blutner & beim Graben, 2016; Busemeyer & Bruza, 2012; Khrennikov, 2010; Kitto, 2008; Pothos & Busemeyer, 2013; Wang, Busemeyer, Atmanspacher, & Pothos, 2013 ), several reasons can be given. An important one is that quantum mechanics was created to model and explain physical phenomena exhibiting a high degree of contextuality, and the latter is also the leitmotif in human cognition and decision processes.

For instance, quantum superposition states can describe physical properties only existing in potential terms, that is, available to be actualized during certain measurements in a genuinely unpredictable way. Similarly, conceptual situations that lend themselves to judgments and decisions are also generally describable in terms of indeterministic processes of actualization of potential properties, when subjects are invited to produce an answer in situations of ambiguity, uncertainty, confusion, etc.

So, the quantum formalism can be very relevant in the description of the human cognitive processes, and it was amazing to observe, over the last years, how many aspects that were used to explain the strange behavior of the microscopic physical entities have proven to be also pertinent in the description of the human conceptual entities, like superposition, interference, entanglement, emergence, and even quantum fields effects. The amazement was not only in the observation that the human conceptual entities and the microscopic physical entities were sharing a common “conceptual nature”,

                       but that the very specific Hilbertian structure of quantum mechanics appeared to be sufficient to model a wide spectrum of experimental situations.

In other words, it was not at all expected in the beginning of the investigations in quantum cognition, that the Born rule of assignment of the probabilities would prove to be so effective in the modeling of many quantum effects identified in the cognitive domain ( Aerts & Sozzo, 2012a,b ). To put it another way: Why the Born rule and not other rules, associated with different and/or more general quantum-like structures? This fundamental question was the object of a recent examination by us: starting from a beyond-quantum modeling of psychological measurements, which we have called the  general tension-reduction model  (GTR-model, in brief), we were able to show that the Born rule is characterizable in terms of uniform fluctuations in the experimental context, and that these uniform fluctuations naturally emerge when a  universal average  over all possible forms of non-uniform fluctuations is considered ( Aerts & Sassoli de Bianchi, 2015a,b ).

Let us explain a little more specifically what we exactly mean, as this will be important to properly contextualize the results presented in this article, which can be considered to be the logical continuation of the analysis in  Aerts and Sassoli de Bianchi (2015a,b) . Consider an opinion poll conducted on a large number of respondents. Each of them will bring into it the uniqueness of one’s mind, which means that participants in the poll will have different  ways of choosing  an answer among those available to be selected. This also means that a cognitive experiment, involving a number of different subjects, is generally to be considered not as a single measurement, but as a collection of different measurements, one for each participant. Each of these different individual measurements, however, is in principle associated with different outcome probabilities, so that the overall probabilities deduced from the individual results produced by the participants should in fact be considered averages over individual probabilities.

What we are here affirming is that in a typical cognitive experiment, like an opinion poll, we are in the presence of a sort of abstract “collective mind”, producing a collection of different quantum-like measurements

                       that remain undistinguished, being simply averaged out in the final statistics. This means that we are in a situation of lack of knowledge about the different individual ‘ways of choosing’ employed by the respondents, characterized by different ways of assigning probabilities to the available outcomes. Now, considering the general success of the Born rule in the modeling of data gathered from experiments that are statistical mixtures of different measurements, one may expect to be able to understand the quantum mechanical Born rule as a rule describing the probabilistics of outcomes that are mixtures of a sufficiently vast ensemble of different types of measurements.

This is precisely the result that we have recently obtained ( Aerts & Sassoli de Bianchi, 2015a,b ): starting from the general framework provided by the GTR-model, we were able to define, in a mathematically precise and cognitively transparent way, a notion of  universal measurement , describing the most general possible condition of lack of knowledge in a measurement, expressed as an average over all imaginable types of individual measurements. We have then proved that such huge average exactly yields the Born rule of quantum mechanics, if the structure of the state space is Hilbertian, providing in this way a convincing explanation of why the quantum statistics performs so well, in so many experimental ambits.

However, if on one hand the possibility of describing the Born rule as a universal measurement can explain its “unreasonable” success in modeling so many experiments in cognitive science, this also points to the possibility of observing violations of it, in specific experimental situations. Indeed, there are no  a priori  reasons to believe that in all experiments a universal average, or a good approximation of it, would be generated by the participants’ different ‘ways of choosing’ an outcome. This could be the case because the statistical sample would be too small, or because measurements would be combined together in a way that would prevent the average to be effective in producing the uniform fluctuations that characterize the Born rule.

It is important to observe that possible violations of the Born rule cannot be observed in single measurement situations, as is clear that the Hilbert model, with its scalar product, when equipped with the Born rule becomes a ‘universal probabilistic machine’, capable of representing all possible probabilities appearing in nature, in a given (single) measurement context ( Aerts & Sassoli de Bianchi, 2015a; Aerts & Sozzo, 2012a ). But when sequential measurements are considered, the Hilbertian model is not any more a universal model, and violations can in principle be observed ( Aerts & Sassoli de Bianchi, 2015a,b ). So, it is in the ambit of experiments constructed as multiple processes, where aspects of non-commutativity will be at stake, that one has to look to find quantum-like structures that are possibly non-Hilbertian, i.e., non-Bornian.

Typical experiments of this kind are those exhibiting  question order effects , commonly observed in social and behavioral research; see for instance  Schuman and Presser (1981) ,  Sudman and Bradburn (1974)  and  Tourangeau, Rips, and Rasinski (2000) . Tentative quantum accounts of these and other effects were given by many authors in recent years ( Aerts et al., 2013a,b; Atmanspacher & Roemer, 2012; Boyer-Kassem, Duchêne, & Guerci, 2016; Busemeyer, Pothos, Franco, & Trueblood, 2011; Conte et al., 2009; Khrennikov, Basieva, Dzhafarov, & Busemeyer, 2014; Trueblood & Busemeyer, 2011; Wang & Busemeyer, 2013; Wang, Sollowaya, Shiffrinb, & Busemeyer, 2014 ) and it is generally considered that quantum mechanics can provide an efficacious and consistent modeling of them. This was recently emphasized by  Wang and Busemeyer (2013) , showing that different data sets, like those reported by  Moore (2002) , (almost) obey a parameter-free constraint, called the  QQ-equality , which is able to test the pure quantum nature of the experimental probabilities.

This conclusion, however, was recently called into question by  Boyer-Kassem et al. (2016) , showing that the QQ-equality is actually insufficient to test the validity of the Hilbertian model, as other ‘quantum equalities’ can be shown not to be satisfied by the same data, thus demonstrating a violation of the Hilbertian structure. The results obtained by  Boyer-Kassem et al. (2016)  are restricted to two-outcome non-degenerate measurements, i.e., to binary (yes–no) questions whose answers are represented by one-dimensional subspaces. This means that their conclusion do not necessary apply to Hilbertian models using higher-dimensional subspaces.

However, considering that the introduction of additional dimensions should in principle be justified, and not introduced  ad hoc  to better fit the data, and that, more importantly, the QQ-equality should be  exactly obeyed , and not just approximately obeyed, the following question arises naturally: How can we properly account for the question order effects, considering that, strictly speaking, the Hilbertian model is unable to do so? Because one thing is to show that the Born rule is violated, and another thing is to provide a  non ad hoc  model able not only to  exactly  fit the experimental data, but also, possibly, to shed some light into the nature of the cognitive processes that have generated the data.

This is precisely what we will do in the present article, by means of the GTR-model. More precisely, in Section   2 , we show how two sequential 2-outcome measurements can be generally modeled by using the hidden-measurements representation that is built in the GTR-model. In Section   3 , we add more structure to the model by introducing three simplifying hypotheses, which allows us, in Section   4 , to present an explicit solution. This solution depends on a certain number of parameters (only one of which is free), and standard quantum mechanics is recovered in the limit where some of them tend to specific values.

In Section   5 , we then provide an exact modeling of some typical data from the Gallup survey experiments reported in a seminal article on question order effects by  Moore (2002) , and more particularly the ‘Clinton/Gore’ and ‘Rose/Jackson’ data, showing that they are irreducibly non-Hilbertian. In Section   6 , we continue by investigating the content of Wang & Busemeyer’s QQ-equality, showing how and why it can also be obeyed by non-Hilbertian models. We also use our explicit solution to derive additional parameter-free ‘quantum equalities’ (only valid for two-outcome, non-degenerate measurements) and show that they are strongly violated by the Clinton/Gore experimental data.

In Section   7 , we address the issue of  response replicability , which remains an unsolved problem in quantum cognition. Indeed, the quantum formalism does not allow the joint description of question order effects and response replicability ( Khrennikov & Basieva, 2015; Khrennikov et al., 2014 ). This is so because the quantum model, contrary to the GTR-model, does not allow for the description of different typologies of measurements, associated with a same set of outcomes. On the other hand, by allowing the measurements to “evolve”, taking into account the obtained outcomes, the GTR-model can not only describe response replicability, compatibly with question order effects, but also more complex situations of ‘partial replicability’, in accordance with our intuitive idea of what it means to have an opinion, or to form one, and to possibly change it under the right circumstances.

In Section   8 , we then provide what we think is the appropriate interpretation of the different mathematical objects of the GTR-model, with the states not describing beliefs, but objective elements of a conceptual reality, independent from the minds that can interact with it. In Section   9 , we consider what are the effects of the averaging over the different respondents, for what concerns the effective probability model describing the overall statistics of outcomes. In particular, we show that even when the respondents choose their outcomes not only according to a symmetrical probability distribution, but also in the same way in the two sequential measurements, as soon as their ‘ways of choosing’ are averaged out in the final statistics, the symmetry is broken, in the sense that their collective ‘way of choosing’ becomes non-symmetric and sequentially non-uniform. Finally, in Section   10 , we recap the obtained results and offer some final thoughts regarding the possibility of modeling question order effects by means of the Born rule, in higher dimensional Hilbert spaces.

In the previous sections we have provided an exact modeling of some paradigmatic data exhibiting question order effects, using a mathematical formalism that is more general than that of standard quantum mechanics. If on one hand it was to be expected that the data could not be exactly modeled by the Born rule, on the other hand it may come as a surprise that the structure of the Clinton/Gore probabilities are not only irreducibly non-Hilbertian, but also not at all close to a Hilbertian model (assuming here that such model should be in terms of two-outcome non-degenerate measurements). Indeed, the Clinton/Gore data are structurally similar to the Rose/Jackson data, despite of the fact that they are generally considered to be very different, considering that there is a test, expressed by the so-called  QQ-equality , which is almost passed by the Clinton/Gore data, but not by the Rose/Jackson data ( Wang & Busemeyer, 2013; Wang et al., 2014 ).

The explanation that was given for this difference is that the validity of the QQ-equality in psychological order effect measurements would depend on the critical assumption that, quoting from  Wang and Busemeyer (2013) : “the only factor that changes the context or the state for answering a question is answering its preceding question”. In the Rose/Jackson data this assumption is believed to be violated, as during the survey the subjects were provided with some background information. For instance, before the Pete Rose question, the following information was typically given: “As you may know, former Major League player Pete Rose is ineligible for baseball’s Hall of Fame due to charges that he had gambled on baseball games”. Information was also provided before addressing the Joe Jackson question, typically: “As you may know, Shoeless Joe Jackson is ineligible for baseball’s Hall of Fame due to charges that he took money from gamblers in exchange for fixing the 1919 World Series”.

This means that, quoting again from  Wang and Busemeyer (2013) : “the initial belief state when Rose/Jackson was asked first was affected by the information provided about the particular player. Furthermore, the context for the second question was changed not only by answering the first question but also sequentially by the additional background information on the player in the second question. […] From the perspective of the QQ model, actually the two orders in the data set were Rose background-Rose question-Jackson background-Jackson question versus Jackson background-Jackson question-Rose background-Rose question”.

According to  Wang and Busemeyer (2013) , this would be sufficient to explain why the QQ-equality is almost obeyed by the Clinton/Gore data, but manifestly disobeyed by the Rose/Jackson data. This is certainly a possible explanation, which however would require additional experiments to be tested. For instance, what happens if one repeats the Rose/Jackson measurement but this time providing all the background information up front, before the two questions? Also, what happens if one performs the Clinton/Gore measurement this time providing some background information, as a premise before each of the two question, as it was done in the Rose/Jackson situation? Would this sensibly affect the result of the QQ-equality test?

A possible objection regarding the above explanation is that it is always possible to incorporate the background information into the questions themselves, for instance defining (in the Rose/Jackson measurement) the following modified interrogative contexts: “As you may know, former Major League player Pete Rose is ineligible for baseball’s Hall of Fame due to charges that he had gambled on baseball games; do you think Rose should or should not be eligible for admission to the Hall of Fame?” and: “As you may know, Shoeless Joe Jackson is ineligible for baseball’s Hall of Fame due to charges that he took money from gamblers in exchange for fixing the 1919 World Series; do you think Jackson should or should not be eligible for admission to the Hall of Fame?”

In this way, we have two rephrased questions, with no background information provided anymore (as it became part of the question), and one would expect the QQ-equality to also be approximately obeyed in this case. Of course, one may argue that it is not so important if the background information can be made part of the question, as what truly matters is to know if the respondents will process it in this way, i.e., as a question, or as information, although it is also true that no pure questions exist, in the sense that a question also always provides a context, and therefore some kind of information, which can appear in more or less explicit terms in the way it is formulated.

So, there is undoubtedly something that needs to be clarified, not only as regards the true content of the QQ-equality (what kind of symmetry does it test?), but also the way to interpret the quantum formalism and its GTR-model generalization, when used to model cognitive situations. Let us have a closer look to the QQ-equality, derived by  Wang and Busemeyer (2013)  as part of their QQ-model, where the subjects’ beliefs are represented by state vectors in a Hilbert space and the interrogative contexts by generally non commuting orthogonal projection operators of arbitrary dimension.

A similar conclusion was recently reached by  Boyer-Kassem et al. (2016) , who by reasoning directly in terms of conditional probabilities also pointed out the insufficiency of the QQ-equality in characterizing a (non-degenerate) quantum probability model. Indeed, as we have also seen in the previous sections, in the quantum formalism transition probabilities from one state to another state are expressions of conditional statements, and because of the conjugate symmetry of the inner product the probability of a transition will not depend on its order (a property requiring the elastics to be globally uniform). This introduces a symmetry also in the conditional probabilities, that  Boyer-Kassem et al. (2016)  organize in a set of equations that they call the Grand Reciprocity (GR) equations, which need also to be satisfied by all two-dimensional quantum models, as the three Eqs.  (32)–(34)  (and the QQ-equation) also need to be, but are not satisfied by most of the existing data sets. This does not mean that the symmetry expressed by the QQ-equality, considered here as a beyond-quantum symmetry, would not have some interest in characterizing those data sets for which the ‘relative indeterminism’ and ‘relative asymmetry’ contributions, while non-zero, are nevertheless able to mutually almost compensate, a mechanism the origin of which, we believe, remains to be clarified.

We therefore see that a pure quantum model may not be the right one to account for the typical probabilistic data exhibiting question order effects. One may rightly object that the equalities  (32)–(34) , as well as those derived by  Boyer-Kassem et al. (2016) , are only valid if the two-outcome measurements are non-degenerate. However, as we remarked already, the validity of the QQ-equality is dimension independent and is not exactly obeyed by the existing data. This means that even by increasing the number of dimensions, because of its structural shortcomings a pure quantum modeling will never be able to faithfully conform to the QQ-equality.

Consider the example of classical probability theory, obeying the Kolmogorovian axioms. We clearly expect the Kolmogorovian model to be able to fit all possible data describing “static” propositional systems, where the lack of knowledge is about actual elements of reality. This is the case because the Kolmogorovian axioms describe in a suitable way the Boolean logic inherent in classical situations. This Boolean logic, however, proved to be insufficient when used to describe the behavior of the entities of the micro-world, as contrary to classical observations, quantum measurements are dynamical processes producing an unpredictable change of the state of the observed entity.

The axioms of standard quantum mechanics can certainly be considered to be a first attempt towards a general modeling of non-Boolean “dynamic logic” situations ( Baltag & Smets, 2011 ), where the lack of knowledge is not just about actual elements of reality, but also, and especially, about processes of actualization of potential properties. Now, the Kolmogorovian model can certainly be considered to be a universal one for the description of situations governed by “static information”, but it would be wrong to believe that the Hilbertian model would also be a universal framework for the description of situations governed by “dynamic information”, i.e., situations where an entity is characterized not only in terms of its actual behavior, but more generally in terms of its potential behavior under all possible experimental contexts.

In physics, the question of knowing whether the standard formulation of quantum mechanics is sufficiently general to describe all possible experimental situations is still an open one, but in quantum cognition we already know that this is not the case, as the data already in our possession do not exactly obey the quantum constraints, like the QQ-equality. This means that we need to consider a more general–beyond-quantum–probabilistic model, able to play the same universal role that the Kolmogorovian model plays in the description of ‘actuality’, in the description of the dynamic passage from ‘potentiality to actuality’.

We think that the GTR-model, which we have shown to be able to  exactly  model, and therefore conveniently describe, the full structure of the experimental data, is a good candidate for a more universal model. Thanks to it, order effects in human psychological measurements can be understood as being the combination of two very distinct aspects: the non-compatibility of the interrogative contexts, which can be easily modeled in quantum mechanics by using non-commuting projection operators (or more generally non-commuting observables), and the different ways outcomes can be selected, in the different interrogative contexts, which can be easily described in the GTR-model by considering distinct probability distributions for each measurement, something that is impossible to do in the Hilbertian model, as the Born rule is precisely characterized by the use of the same uniform probability distribution for all measurements.

The advantage and relevancy of using the GTR-model is that it also allows handling another situation that is problematic for the standard quantum formalism:  response replicability . This is the topic we are going to address in the next section.

Quoting from  Khrennikov et al. (2014) : “[…] quantum theory (QT) encounters difficulties in accounting for some very basic empirical properties. In opinion polling […], there is a class of questions such that a repeated question is answered in the same way as the first time it was asked. This agrees with the Lüders projection postulate […]. In many situations, we also expect that for a certain class of questions the response to two replications of a given question remains the same even if we insert another question in between and have it answered. This property can only be handled by QT if the conventional observables representing different questions all pairwise commute, i.e., can be assigned the same set of eigenvectors. This, in turn, leads to a strong prediction: the joint probability of two responses to two successive questions does not depend on their order. This prediction is known to be violated for some pairs of questions. The explanation of the ‘question order effect’ is in fact one of the most successful applications of QT in psychology ( Wang & Busemeyer, 2013 ), but it requires non-commuting observables, and these, as we have seen, cannot account for the repeated answers to repeated questions”.

In the previous sections we have resized this last statement about the success of standard quantum theory in accounting question order effects, as we have seen that the probability models these effects are able to generate are generally incompatible with the specific structure of the Born rule and can only be represented in more general quantum-like frameworks, like the one provided by the GTR-model. As we are now going to explain, different from quantum theory, the GTR-model is also able to jointly handle, in a consistent way, question order effects  and  response replicability. The reason why this is possible is that in the GTR-model not only contexts can change states, through the mechanism of the breaking and collapsing of the elastic bands, but also states can change contexts, by allowing the probability distributions describing the way the elastics break to also depend on the outcomes obtained in the previous measurements, exactly in the same way we humans, once we have formed an opinion, we do not need to form it again, by definition of what an opinion (and consequently an opinion poll) is.

So, we start from the observation that it is natural to conjecture that there is a nonempty class of measurements such that, when they are performed in successive trials, if one of the measurements in the sequence is equal to a previous measurement, the same outcome that was previously obtained will be obtained again, but with certainty, i.e., with probability equal to 1. In the previous sections, we have shown that question order effects and response replicability can both be compatibly described in the GTR-model. This is because, different from the standard quantum formalism, the GTR-model contains an explicit representation of the measurements, by means of a mechanism of actualization of potential measurement-interactions. This mechanism opens to the possibility of associating different processes of actualization of potential outcomes to a same set of outcomes, as described by the different possible  
                         ρ 
                      -probability distributions associated with an elastic band of a given orientation in the Bloch sphere, whilst in the quantum formalism only the uniform probability distribution is admitted.

In other words, the GTR-model, thanks to its greater structural richness, allows for a distinction between ‘a question and its possible answers’, and ‘the way a respondent selects one of these answers’. Different respondents are in principle associated with different set of probabilities for the outcomes, characterizing their ‘personal way of choosing an answer’, whereas in the quantum formalism the different subjects all necessarily choose in the same way, as if they were identical “Bornian clones”. This means that when we use the quantum formalism, order effects can only be described by means of the relative orientations of the different states in the Bloch sphere. This, however, constitutes a severe shortcoming in the description of psychological measurements (and in fact, possibly also in the description of physical measurements, but this is another story), as the Hilbertian geometry is too specific to account for the experimentally observed probabilities.

As we have explained in  Aerts and Sassoli de Bianchi (2015a,b) , the breaking of the elastics (and more generally the disintegration of the hyper-membranes, when more general

The creation of this mental state of equilibrium is described by the abstract point particle plunging into the sphere and reaching its position on the elastic, giving rise to two “tension lines” going from its position to the two end points, representative of the two competing outcomes. At some moment, this mental equilibrium will be disturbed, in a non-predictable way, and the disturbance will cause an irreversible process during which, almost instantaneously, the particle will be drawn to one of the two vertices of the elastic (representing the answers). This ‘weighted symmetry breaking’ process is described in the model by the random manifestation of a breaking point, which by causing the collapse of the elastic band breaks the tensional equilibrium that had previously been built, hence the name “tension-reduction” given to the model (suggested to us by Jerome Busemeyer).

If the elastic band is representative of an aspect of the mind of the subject, here generally understood as a memory structure sensitive to meaning, how should we interpret the abstract point particle that interacts with it? Having explained how the mathematical formalism of the GTR-model can be interpreted, we must take a final step in our analysis and consider the important issue of the average over the different respondents. In a typical psychological measurement, data are collected from a certain number

As we mentioned in the Introduction, in a recent work ( Aerts & Sassoli de Bianchi, 2015a,b ) we have shown that if a sufficiently large number of subjects is considered, exploring a wide spectrum of different ‘ways of choosing an answer’, then the average over all their answers will be well approximated by a pure quantum measurement.

Our result, however, only holds for a single measurement context, and not necessarily for a sequential measurement context. In fact, considering the analysis of the previous sections, it is even to be expected that the equivalence between a universal average and a pure quantum measurement will generally not hold in a sequential situation, as suggested by the fact that the Clinton/Gore and Rose/Jackson paradigmatic data are very far from being well approximated by the Born rule (and the projection postulate).

Let us summarize our findings. We have considered some paradigmatic data exhibiting question order effects and have shown that they are genuinely non-Hilbertian, i.e., that they require a more general framework than that of quantum mechanics. This is particularly evident when the modeling is carried out by means of 2-outcome non-degenerate measurements, as the Hilbertian equalities  (32)–(34)  are strongly violated by the experimental data (as evidenced also by the non-quantum values  (20)–(23)  of the parameters). This remains also true for degenerate measurements, considering that the QQ-equality is not exactly satisfied, but just approximately satisfied by the data.

A more general–beyond-quantum–framework is provided by the GTR-model, where different from quantum mechanics various typologies of measurements, associated with a same set of outcome-states, can be described. In order to explicitly solve the GTR-model, we have considered three simplifying hypotheses: ‘weak compatibility’, ‘local uniformity’, and ‘sensitivity to pre-measurement state’. The solution obeying these three hypotheses remains sufficiently general to allow for an exact fitting of the experimental data. When this is done, one finds that the probability distributions describing the two sequential measurements not only need to be different from one another, but also asymmetric. This, however, could very well be a byproduct of the averaging over the different respondents. Indeed, even if we assume that each respondent selects in a non-contextual way the outcomes (using the same probability distribution in the two sequential measurements), without favoring one of the outcomes (using a parity invariant probability distribution), to the extent that different respondents are characterized by different probability distributions, contextuality and asymmetry will necessarily emerge at the level of the more abstract “collective mind”.

We have also discussed replicability of the outcomes, showing that it can be naturally accounted in the GTR-formalism by allowing the measurements’ probability distributions to change at the individual level (and consequently also at the collective one), when measurements are repeated, and that this process of change of the probability distributions, which adds to the process of change of the states, remains compatible with the question order effects.

We have further proposed the adoption of a non-subjectivist interpretation of the GTR-model formalism, and a fortiori of the quantum formalism, where states do not describe “states of mind”, or “states of beliefs”, but the objective contextual condition of the conceptual entity that is subjected to the measurement, whose reality is independent from the respondents’ belief systems. A mind, instead, would be that entity interacting with the different conceptual entities that are part of the human culture, through of a mechanism of actualization of potential measurement-interactions, as described in the formalism by the (breakable) elastic bands, or by higher-dimensional hyper-membranes, in more general measurement situations ( Aerts & Sassoli de Bianchi, 2014, 2015a,b, 2016 ).

Being the explicit description of measurements absent in the standard quantum formalism, this is the main reason why subjectivist interpretations exist, not only in quantum cognition, but also in physics (so-called QBism is a typical example  Fuchs, Mermin, & Schack, 2014 ). The GTR-model ( Aerts & Sassoli de Bianchi, 2015a,b ), and more specifically its Hilbertian extended Bloch representation ( Aerts & Sassoli de Bianchi, 2014, 2016 ), provide a completed version of quantum mechanics, in which the explicit fluctuations originating in the interaction between the measured entity and the measuring system are explicitly represented, thus explaining the possible nature of quantum indeterminism (so providing a compelling solution to the measurement problem). This allows understanding the collapse of the state vector as an objective physical process, and consequently interpreting the state vector as a description of the reality of the physical entity under consideration, and not merely of the experimenter’s beliefs about it.

Similarly, if the probabilities emerging from psychological measurements are the result of objective fluctuations in the interaction between the human mind and a ‘meaning entity’ prepared in a given context, as described in our beyond-quantum GTR-model, then a state describes the real contextual condition of the conceptual entity, and not the beliefs of a human mind about it. Beliefs are stored in the memory structure of the judging human entity, and when they play a role they do so by altering the dynamics of the elastic structures describing the way answers are selected. But there are additional reasons for adopting an operational-realistic interpretation of the mathematical formalism in quantum cognition ( Aerts, Sassoli de Bianchi, & Sozzo, 2016 ). Different from physics, where the hidden-measurements remain a hypothesis awaiting experimental confirmation, in psychological measurements they are in part manifested by the very existence of the different participants in a survey, and their different ways of responding. Last but not least, another important reason for adopting the proposed interpretational framework is that it has demonstrated its relevance in the modeling of concept combinations, in a comprehensive theory of conceptual representation ( Aerts & Gabora, 2005a,b; Aerts et al., 2016; Gabora & Aerts, 2002 ).

Let us now to offer some concluding remarks. Firstly, we emphasize that although the GTR-model expresses in a very intuitive way what we humans feel when confronted with decisions (a gradual building up of a tension and its subsequent sudden release), this does not mean that it would be meant to describe specific brain processes. Various time scales can be identified in the brain and a number of parallels may certainly come to mind. However, the GTR-model was primarily conceived for the description of general measurement situations, and this regardless of the context in which they would occur (physics or psychological laboratories). Considering Marr’s three levels of analysis ( Marr, 2010 ), the GTR-model is first of all to be interpreted as a ‘computational model’, aimed at the understanding of the basic principles and representations that guide human decision. In other words, the GTR-model is more a model of the mind’s processes than a model of the brain’s processes, which of course need not to be the same. To offer a simple analogy, the modeling of how the human language is spoken is very different from the modeling of the activity of Broca’s area, although it is also clear that there will be numerous correlates.

Another important aspect to emphasize is that the GTR-model is not an ‘ad hoc mathematical model’ constructed with the mere purpose of finding a way to perfectly fit the data. Instead, it is a ‘fundamental model’ for a general situation corresponding to the measurement on an entity in a given state, with the presence of fluctuations on the interaction taking place between the measurement (apparatus) and the measured entity ( Aerts & Sassoli de Bianchi, 2015b ). This is the reason why it can also be used to model the situation of a measurement on a quantum entity in physics.

To test for its falsifiability, we will have to wait until many more different types of experiments have been performed, and the corresponding data collected, on a same state of preparation of the entity, then see whether they can all be jointly taken into account by the GTR-model. Considering its capacity of dealing with different types of experiments, as regards physical systems, and our understanding of the reasons for which the model carries such potential, we believe this will be the case also in psychology. However, this cannot be proven for the time being, since the bulk of these other experiments have not been performed yet. Of course, as soon as more experimental data will become available, we will not fail to check this out, and if things work out as expected, all parameters of the model will be required to fit them, and in this case, we could say that the model has successfully passed the falsifiability test.

To further clarify this issue, let us emphasize the importance of also distinguishing ‘phenomenological models’ from ‘explanatory models’. The former only organize mathematically the different experimental results, whereas the latter try to understand them at a more fundamental level, possibly deriving them from first principles. In the construction of a ‘theoretical (first principle) model’, the main concern is that it must be derived according to the logic of the adopted ‘theory of human cognition’, which in our case is an operational-realistic one ( Aerts et al., 2016 ). Also, the model must be able to deal, without abandoning such logic, all possible experiments and data describing the interaction of human minds with conceptual entities. These requirements are very stringent. Indeed, if we take seriously the violation of the QQ-equality by question-order effect data, we must conclude that pure Hilbert models already fail to satisfy them, and in that sense would have already been falsified. Not because they would be wrong, but because their domain of validity would be too limited.

So, if we should certainly be suspicious of phenomenological models with many free parameters that are just added in the aftermath, to fit the data more closely, we should also evaluate in a different way those fundamental models/theories that not only have a clear mathematical and logical structure, but also a description of the procedures that are to be used to apply them to the different experimental situations. Furthermore, a ‘first principle model’ like the GTR-model, to really become predictive as regards the outcomes of future experiments, and be thoroughly tested (thus also possibly falsified) will need, as we said already, a vast amount of data from many different types of experiments, whereas we are still witnessing a scarcity of the data today available in quantum cognition.

However, quoting from ( Aerts, Broekaert, Gabora, & Sozzo, 2016 ): “The success of quantum cognition is due to it ‘being able to convincingly model data that theoretically can be proven to be impossible to model with any model that relies on classical fuzzy set theory and/or classical Kolmogorovian probability theory’. Hence, a different criterion than predictive power is provisionally used to identify the success of quantum cognition”. In the same way, we can add that the interest of the GTR-model, as opposed to pure Hilbertian models, is due to it ‘being able to convincingly model data that theoretically can be proven to be impossible to model with any model that relies on pure Hilbertian probability models.

It is by taking into account the above considerations that it is possible to appreciate in particular the solution offered by the GTR-model regarding response replicability. One may object that this solution does not really follow from the model, but is somehow implemented by hand. This is true in a sense, but the important point here is not how the probabilities distributions are specifically chosen, to describe response replicability, but the fact that the GTR-model allows one to make these choices in the first place, not restricting the probability distributions to be all identical to the uniform one. Again, we do not have yet enough data to obtain a finer grained description of how humans update their ‘ways of choosing’ an outcome, taking into account their previous decisions, but what matters here is that a pure Hilbertian model will never be able to consistently deal with the very possibility of such an update, for reasons of structural shortcomings.

We have seen that this cannot be done by just considering higher-dimensional projection operators for the outcomes, because of the violation of the QQ-equality.
