Linear Measurement Models��Axiomatizations and Axiomatizability 

Uta Wille 

Jelmoli AG 

This paper examines, in the scope of representational measurement theory, different axiomatizations and axiomatizability of linear and bilinear representations of ordinal data contexts in real vector spaces. The representation theorems proved in this paper are modifications and generalizations of Scott's characterization of finite linear measurement models. The advantage of these representation theorems is that they use only finitely many axioms, the number of which depends on the size of the given ordinal data context. Concerning the axiomatizability, it is proved by model-theoretic methods that finite linear measurement models cannot be axiomatized by a finite set of first order axioms. � 2000 Academic Press 

1. ADDITIVE MEASUREMENT AND SCOTT'S THEOREM 

The analysis of additive representations of ordinal data is an important issue in representational measurement theory (cf. Krantz, Luce, Suppes, 6 Tversky, 1971). In this paper we examine different axiomatizations and the axiomatizability of linear and bilinear representations of finite ordinal contexts in real vector spaces. Ordinal contexts are the formal models of ordinal data to be represented numerically and they describe the interface between reality and mathematics as required in representational measurement theory. 

The investigations of this paper are restricted to additive measurement structures which do not require existential axioms. Many representation theorems in measurement theory are based on solvability assumptions but this raises diverse theoretical and practical problems. Solvability assumptions and richness conditions usually are not satisfied by given data. Furthermore, they are typically not necessary for the existence of a numerical representation which means that they may have contributory empirical content (cf. Adams, 1992). Therefore, we follow in this paper the approach of Scott who formulated the first characterization of finite linear measurement models by necessary and sufficient conditions based on the theory of linear inequalities (cf. Scott, 1964).

In the following Scott's theorem is analyzed which is fundamental for the considerations of this paper. The analysis of Scott's theorem results in the formulation of a bound for a number of conditions of Scott's theorem to be checked against in order to verify the representability of given data sets. 

Section 2 is concerned with linear representations of finite ordinal contexts where the basic notions are introduced at the beginning of the section. Using Scott's characterization of finite linear measurement models by an infinite class of necessary and sufficient conditions, two representation theorems for finite ordinal contexts are derived. These representation theorems allow us to formulate a finite set of necessary and sufficient conditions for the existence of a linear representation into the reals (i.e., into a real vector space), where the number of conditions depends on the size of the underlying ordinal context. This has the consequence that the derived axiomatization is more practical than the original one and also provides tools to test the required axioms by linking it to integer programming. 

In the third section, it is proved that finite linear measurement models cannot be axiomatized by a finite set of first-order axioms. This means that we cannot expect to find more than a finite set of necessary and sufficient conditions whose size depends on the size of the underlying structure. The theorem on non-finite-axiomatizability of finite linear measurement models is proved by means of an interesting infinite set of critical configurations and 4o�s's theorem of model theory. 

Finally, Section 4 is concerned with `simultaneous linear' representations in real vector spaces, i.e., representations of more than n+1 ordinal attributes in n-dimensional real vector spaces. In this section Scott's theorem is generalized which enables the formulation of simultaneous linear representation theorems analog to the linear representation theorems stated in the second section. 

For a less formal treatment and discussion of real linear representations of ordinal contexts by means of an example from music psychology, we refer to U. Wille (1996a).

2. LINEAR REPRESENTATIONS OF FINITE ORDINAL CONTEXTS 

Ordinal contexts formalize ordinal data tables that report, for considered objects, the values with respect to given ordinal attributes. Because object�attribute�value tables occur most frequently in data analysis, we assume that the examined data are given in the form of such a table and choose ordinal contexts as the empirical formal models which are supposed to be represented. The definition of ordinal contexts is adopted from formal concept analysis which is an approach to data analysis based on order and lattice theory (see Strahringer and R. Wille, 1992; Ganter and R. Wille, 1999). 

Definition 1. A quadruple K :=(G, M, (Wm , �m)m # M, I) is called a (complete) ordinal context if G and M are sets, (Wm , �m)m # M is a family of (partially) ordered sets, and I is a ternary relation with I��m # M G_[m]_Wm such that, for each g # G and m # M, there is exactly one w # Wm with (g, m, w) # I. The elements of G, M, and Wm (m # M) are called objects, attributes, and attribute values, respectively. If the ordered sets (Wm , �m) are linearly ordered for all m # M, then K is called linearly ordinal or briefly l-ordinal. Furthermore, an attribute of an ordinal context is called injective if all its values are distinct and an ordinal context with injective attributes has only injective attributes. 

Since an attribute m of an ordinal context K can also be understood as a map from G into Wm , one often writes m( g)=w instead of (g, m, w) # I, and we read ``the object g has the value w for the attribute m.'' Furthermore, finite ordinal contexts can briefly be described by (G, M, (Wi, �i)i # [0, ..., n], I), where the attributes are denoted by M=[m0 , m1 , ..., mn], the sets of attribute values by Wi :=[mi (g)| g # G], and the order on Wi induced by �mi by the symbol �i (i=0, 1, ..., n). 

Note that an ordinal context can also be described by an ordinal structure which is a set S together with a family (pj )j # J of quasi-orders on S (quasi-orders are reflexive transitive binary relations). If K :=(G, M, (Wm , �m)m # M , I ) is an ordinal context, then the corresponding ordinal structure is defined by S(K) :=(G, (pm)m # M) where the quasi-orders pm are determined by gpm h : � m(g)�m m(h). Furthermore, every ordinal structure can be derived in this way (up to isomorphism) from a suitable ordinal context (cf. Strahringer 6 Wille, 1992). This links ordinal contexts as empirical models with empirical relational structures basic for representational measurement theory. Ordinal contexts are chosen as the empirical models to be represented because data tables are most common and, for representations studied in this paper, it is more convenient to work with ordinal contexts.

3. CRITICAL CONFIGURATIONS AND FINITE AXIOMATIZABILITY 

In the previous section, finite sets of necessary and sufficient conditions for the linear representability of finite ordinal contexts are introduced. Thereby, the number of conditions depends on the size of the underlying ordinal context. The aim of this section is to show that the number of necessary and sufficient conditions for the linear representability in real vector spaces has always to depend on the size of the considered ordinal context. More precisely, it is proved that the class of finite ordinal contexts which are linearly representable in a real vector space cannot be axiomatized by a finite set of first-order axioms. The assertion will be shown by means of an infinite sequence of critical configurations and 4os�'s theorem of model theory. In our case critical configurations are minimal not linearly representable finite ordinal contexts, i.e., critical configurations are not linearly representable in real vector spaces but all their proper subcontexts are linearly representable. Therefore, we are first concerned with critical configurations for the linear representability of finite ordinal contexts before we turn to the proof of the main theorem of this section. 

This approach is different from Lindstrom's proof of the nonfinite axiomatizability of additive measurement models which makes use of Fraisse��Ehrenfeucht sequences (cf. Luce et al., 1990, Theorem 18, p. 246). It additionally implies that the assertion is still true for finite ordinal contexts with injective attributes and the class of (not necessaryly finite) ordinal contexts which possess a linear representation into an ultrapower of the reals (cf. Narens, 1985, Chapter 5). 

The ordinal dependency conditions (A0), (A1), ..., (An) were introduced in the previous section as the most fundamental conditions for the linear representability of ordinal contexts. Now we obtain the smallest critical configurations from them. An ordinal context having only two objects forms a critical configuration if one of the ordinal dependency conditions (A0), (A1), ..., (An) is not satisfied. Therefore, at least the axioms (A0), (A1), ..., (An) have to be satisfied if we construct larger critical configurations.
