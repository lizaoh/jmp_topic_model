Researchers routinely report results and in particular, descriptive statistics. Yet, there are few guidelines regarding how many digits should be reported. Some researchers have written their results with five or six decimals (as for example,  Bhargava et al., 1985 ) while others reduced the number of decimals reported to one, two or three. Nevertheless, the exact number of decimals is often unmotivated or a matter of personal taste. Worst, results are occasionally reported with a number of decimals varying from one section to another. This begs the question: how many decimals should be used to report descriptive statistics? What about effect size statistics such as Cohen’s  d  or inferential statistics such as  t  values? What principles should be used to determine this?

Selecting and measuring a subject is always accompanied with both sampling error and measurement error. In the natural sciences, measurement error is an old concern, its impact on results being routinely quantified using  propagation of error  techniques ( Joint Committee for Guides in Metrology, 2008; Ku, 1966; Taylor & Kuyatt, 1994 ). In those fields, sampling error is less of a concern because the subjects under study often behave the same and are interchangeable. In the social and psychological sciences, by contrast, sampling error (error of estimation caused by sampling variability) is the most important source of error. Changing one participant for another one can change the observed scores considerably, to the point that there may be no obvious resemblance between two given subjects. This profound distinction between scientific fields led to two completely distinct ways to assess empirical findings.

Social sciences focused mostly on sampling error through inference procedures (either frequentist or Bayesian). These procedures handle sampling error in various designs (between-subject and within-subject) accommodating various sampling mechanisms (random sampling or cluster sampling;  Cousineau & Laurencelle, 2015; Woltman, Feldstain, MacKay, & Rocchi, 2012 ) in which manifest or latent variables are examined ( Weston & Gore, 2006 ). They do so by estimating standard errors.

In what follows, I review existing proposals from the APA and others. Given the limitations of these proposals, I then propose formal expressions to determine the number of decimals for some descriptive statistics commonly used in the social and psychological sciences. Next, I go through examples showing the impact of rounding on two samples. The approach advocated herein is distribution free; in the fifth section, we examine one parametric approach and compare its outputs. Finally, before concluding, we examine the impact of sample size on rounding and statistical power.

All along, keep in mind the goal of rounding statistics. In the present, statistics are rounded based on the precision of the measurement instrument to communicate the results extracted from the observed data with only the digits that we are confident in. Other goals are possible. For example, one goal could be to describe quantitative methods and have the readers check their ability to reproduce the exact same result. In that case, it would be advisable to report all the digits afforded by the computational precision, not with respect to the measurement precision. Most computer and programming languages, starting with FORTRAN, performcomputations using 64 bits floating numbers, allowing for roughly 16 significant digits (defined as the IEEE-754 standard). Some environments offer other formats or let you decide of the precision (e. g.,  Mathematica ).

A diametrically opposed goal could be to sketch an overview of a situation taking the population point of view, in which case results could be rounded to fewer digits. For example, the average monthly income for a sample could be 3456.7890 $. This very precise number takes advantage of the  computational  precision afforded by the computer it was computed on. However, if the individual incomes are measured to the nearest dollars, the cents are all uncertain and should be left blank, thus reporting 3456 $. This result is based on the  measurement  precision. Finally, a general indication would be to report a monthly income of 3500 $ as long as it is not contradicted by statistical inference with respect to the putative population. This last result is based on the  population characteristics  precision.

This text is devoted to rounding based on measurement precision which could be the default stance in a scientific paper discussing empirical results.

The APA manual proposes a one-size-fits-all recommendation: report all statistics to two decimal places ( VandenBos, 2010 ). This recommendation, however, is quite problematic, particularly when units of measurement are changed ( Cole, 2015 ). Average body weight for the sample illustrated in  Table 1  could be reported in kilograms with two decimals as 64.33 kg or in grams with two decimals as 64,333.33 grams. Clearly, the second statistic is too precise to be realistic.

Altman and Bland (1996)  make three recommendations, all three based on the raw data precision. For means, they suggest to use no more than 1 extra decimal place over the number of decimals found in the raw data. For measures of uncertainty such as standard deviation, they recommend using two extra decimal places over the raw data’s number of decimals because “greater precision may be warranted for these quantities as they are often used in further calculations” (p. 572). Finally, for test statistics such as  t  or  F  statistics, use no more than two decimal places. This last recommendation is made irrespective of the data’s precision because these statistics are standardized statistics. Following that logic, the same recommendation would apply to standardized effect sizes such as Cohen’s  d .

Altman and Bland ’s ( 1996 ) approach is heuristic. It is based on the precision of the measurements, what we call hereafter an  intrinsic measure of precision . However, it does not depend on the sample size; yet, a larger sample should presumably convey more precise descriptive statistics. We examine this question hereafter.

An alternative approach found in  Cole (2015)  is to match the precision of a statistic with the standard error of that statistic. For example, consider again the average weight of the 9 participants from  Table 1 , 64.3333 kg with a standard error of 4.4033 kg. Because the standard error of the mean is about 4.4 kg, the mean should – according to this view – be precise to about 4 kg (i.e., requiring no digit after the decimal point). Consequently, the mean should be reported as 64 kg. This approach is called hereafter an  extrinsic measure of precision . In this approach, it is not the precision of the individual measurements that sets the statistic’s precision, but its standard error whose purpose is to estimate a plausible range of values for the statistic if a new (external) sample of the same size was obtained from the same population.

The extrinsic measure of precision requires that expressions for the standard error of descriptive statistics be known. Most of these standard error formulas can be found in the literature ( Hays, 1973; Kendall & Stuart, 1979; Winer, Brown, & Michels, 1991 ; also see  Harding, Tremblay, & Cousineau, 2014, 2015 , for a review, and  Goulet-Pelletier & Cousineau, 2018  for the Cohen’s  d  and the related  t  statistic). I derived in the supplements (Appendix A) the standard errors for a few descriptive statistics for which I could not find their formulas in the literature.

The extrinsic measure of precisions has limitations. First, it is independent from the instruments’ quality. Whether the weights are measured using an atomic scale – having microgram precision – or measured using a kitchen scale – with barely one tenth of a kilogram precision – has no impact, both results will be rounded the same. Second, the most critical factor in this approach is sample size. Hence, within this approach, the mean of a large number of poorly measured data will be considered more precise than the mean of a small number of very accurately measured data. Finally, because this approach builds on standard errors, it is redundant with null hypothesis statistical testing.

The following requires the notion of  significant digits . In the world of mathematics, numbers are composed of digits, each one having a definite value. In the empirical world, measurement instruments are needed to access these digits. However, measurement precision is not infinite and consequently, digits below the measurement precision are unreadable. We can – in an analogy with microscopes – speak of the instrument’s  resolution . The digits that are in the instrument’s range of readings are clear digits – called the  significant digits  – the remaining ones are called the  non-significant digits .

A more careful course of action is to avoid writing the uncertain decimals and round the result to show only the digits we are confident in. Where the significant digits end depends on the measurement precision. With regard to descriptive statistics, it also depends on the operations we do on the raw data to obtain these statistics. For instance, to get the mean, we perform a summation. In what way does it affect the significant digits of the results? To get the standard deviation, we raise numbers to the square. How does the number of significant digits react to this operation?

In the empirical sciences, there is no doubt that instruments do not have perfect resolution; the non-significant digits are not zeros, contrary to what is seen in  Table 1 , central column. Two scenarios are possible regarding measurement error. To distinguish the two scenarios, it is useful to classify sources of error. One class of error in a dataset is  random error . It encompasses all possible sources of imprecision that are non-systematic. One example of random error is  sampling error : selecting a subject over another creates variability in the data which impacts precision of descriptive statistics. Standard errors were conceived precisely to handle this type of error. Another source of random error is  non-systematic measurement error  caused by the measurement instrument. In the long run, non-systematic measurement error tends to cancel out.

A second class of error is  systematic error . Such error is commonly called a bias. It is an error which is present identical on all readings. However, its magnitude and direction (negative or positive) is not known. Systematic errors can be ingrained in the experimental design if, for example, there is a recruitment bias. It can also be caused by measurement instruments. For example, computers working with  interrupts  to receive information from peripherals have a systematic error in response times caused by the interrupt protocol.  Systematic measurement error  is an error whose magnitude is constant, except that its magnitude and direction are not known (otherwise, it would be trivial to subtract it from the observations). Commonly, the only information we have is that its magnitude is no larger than a certain limit  
                         Δ 
                      . Contrary to non-systematic error, this form of error does not tend to cancel out across observations.

Determining the significant digits has received a lot of attention in other disciplines such as chemistry and physics. The most advanced approach is based on  propagation of error  techniques (see  Ku, 1966  for a review; also see the  Joint Committee for Guides in Metrology, 2008 ,  Taylor & Kuyatt, 1994; Tremblay & Chassé, 1970 ). However, it has received very little attention in the social and psychological sciences. To my knowledge, no introductory textbook to quantitative methods in the social and psychological sciences mention this issue.

Herein, I applied the propagation of error techniques to a list of frequently used descriptive statistics in the social and psychological sciences. They are, for a single variable designs: the mean, the variance, the standard deviation, the standard error of the mean, the confidence interval of the mean, the one-sample Cohen’s  d  and the one-sample  t -test; for two variable designs: the mean difference between two groups, the Cohen’s  d  and the two-sample  t -test. Finally, for multiple variable designs: the pooled standard deviation and the one-way  F  ratio.

The demonstrations proceed by assuming that the instrument measures a continuous quantity and returns its measurement with some imprecision (owing to the instrument’s resolution). The derivations do not assume any population distribution and are valid as long as the measurement imprecision is small compared to the magnitude of the measurements. I also provide convenient simplifications based on additional assumptions, such as the normality assumption or the homogeneity of variances assumption.

I first derived expressions in the presence of systematic measurement error. On average, systematic measurement error does not cancel out. Thus, the derivations are based on the absolute magnitude of the measurement error. I next derived expressions assuming non-systematic measurement error. In that case, measurement error tends to cancel out on average. These derivations are consequently based on the mean squared error of estimation.

Although the mean is probably the most-used descriptive statistics, other statistics play important roles in research such as standard deviations, standard errors and confidence intervals ( Cumming, 2014; Harding et al., 2014 , among others). To my knowledge, no expression has been formulated to determine the number of significant digits for these descriptive statistics. Similarly, there exists no guideline regarding the number of significant digits for effect sizes such as Cohen’s  d  ( Cohen, 1969; Goulet-Pelletier & Cousineau, 2018; Rosnow & Rosenthal, 2003 ). Finally, the  t  and the  F  statistics can be seen as either a test statistics or a descriptive statistics of effect size ( Hurlbert, Levine, & Utts, 2019 ). Such deterioration implies that systematic measurement error could become a serious issue in the future. Samples tend to be larger with national and international surveys, with big data initiatives, and web-based data. To illustrate the trend,  Belmont and Marolla  examined in 1973 the IQ of 386,336 young men. Twenty years later,  Wolfe (1998)  in a seminal article “What can 1 million trials tell us about visual search?” ran analyses encompassing a million data. Another 20 years later, Mitroff and his colleagues wrote “What can 1 billion trials tell us about visual search?” ( Mitroff et al., 2014 ) in which they obtained results from over 11 million distinct persons. They finish their report saying “Today, we can collect over a million trials  per day  through [a specific mobile application]” (p. 5, our emphasis). This begs the question: What is the measurement precision of our instruments and – more importantly – does it have a systematic component?

The intrinsic view formally derived here adopts an internally-set threshold to rounding based on the measurement precision. It therefore describes the sample. In particular, the intrinsic view can determine which effects are observable and which are not. The extrinsic view uses a rounding threshold externally set by the population’s characteristics. It uses standard error and statistical power to determine which effects can be detected from one sample to another. It is therefore suitable to describe the population. This view was expanded in  Lakens (2017)  and  Simonsohn (2015) . Both views therefore accomplish different goals.

The intrinsic approach, based on the instrument’s resolution, is like a microscope view of the results whereas Simonsohn’s approach, based on the population characteristics, is a telescope view of the results. Both approaches are simultaneously true and therefore, both should be checked in order to assess the external validity (population-wise) and internal validity (measure-wise) of a result.

The present results apply to continuous variables only. With respect to questionnaires and ordinal measurement, what constitute a measurement error is undefined and so additional work in that direction is needed. Also, in the present formulas, the measurement error is assumed identical for all readings. However, it is possible that some measures are done with different apparatus having different precisions. How precision of a descriptive statistic can be expressed in such a situation remains to be demonstrated. This is likely to occur for meta-analyses.

Additional work is also required for bivariate statistics such as the covariance, the correlation, the paired-sample  t  test, etc. Likewise, I am currently undertaking additional work to determine the precision of the  p  value.

Herein, I examined two distinct scenarios. The systematic measurement error can be seen as the worst-case scenario because precision is rarely improved by larger samples, and can even be lowered in the case of test statistics (the  t  and the  F ). Consequently, very large samples could be problematic for the study of very small effects ( Lin, Lucas, & Shmueli, 2013 ).

The fact that there are many views on rounding statistics puts an additional burden on the authors of scientific papers. Indeed, it would be necessary that the researchers specify explicitly how numbers are rounded up in a report. The authors could adhere to the APA convention or Bland and Altman proposal, which are heuristics. They could also integrate one of the views formalized herein, the extrinsic view, based on population characteristics, or the intrinsic views (non-systematic or systematic), based on instrument characteristics. When the intrinsic view is adopted, it is also necessary to provide the instrument resolution (noted

If an intrinsic view is adopted, a convenient place to do so would be in the Methodology: when the apparatus is described, its measurement precision should also be provided explicitly. In an additional sentence, the rounding scenario used can be provided. Note that even if a heuristic approach is adopted (e.g., the APA method), it sounds advisable that the measurement precision be given to the readers.

Rounding results is a routine act done dozens of time per article. However, we may be overestimating the precision of the reported results. The APA recommendation to report two decimals implicitly assumes that measurement precision is high. The problem is that we do not know the precision of many of the instruments we use daily.
