We make perceptual comparisons between stimuli that vary in magnitude automatically and without effort. For example, it is easy to detect which of two lights is brighter, or which of two circles is larger. But how is this accomplished? More specifically, how do our perceptual and cognitive systems represent comparisons based on subjective magnitude? Research in cognitive neuroscience has made substantial progress in understanding how spatial, temporal, and numerical information is processed, including the key role of the inferior parietal cortex  (Dehaene, Piazza, Pinel, & Cohen, 2003; Kadosh, Lammertyn, & Izard, 2008)  and the possibility that these dimensions may be subsumed by a generalized magnitude system  (Walsh, 2003) . There is also strong evidence that humans and nonhumans share an ‘approximate number system’ in which numerosity is represented at the neural level in terms of a logarithmic scale  (Dehaene, 2003; Feigenson, Dehaene, & Spelke, 2004; Nieder & Miller, 2003) . Despite this progress, how representations of magnitude are compared remains uncertain.

Psychophysical research has addressed this question in terms of whether observers make comparisons based on ratios or differences of stimulus magnitudes.  Torgerson (1961)  proposed that observers perceive a single quantitative relation between stimuli, but whether that relation is a ratio or a difference cannot be resolved empirically. This proposal – known as  Torgerson’s conjecture  – has been the focus of considerable research, but with inconclusive results. Studies have found that observers can produce numerical estimates of “ratios” and “differences”, depending on instructions, for stimuli of various dimensions including heaviness  (Birnbaum & Veit, 1974; Mellers, Davis, & Birnbaum, 1984; Rule, Curtis, & Mullin, 1981) ; loudness  (Birnbaum & Elmasian, 1977; Schneider, Parker, Farrell, & Kanow, 1976) , pitch  (Elmasian & Birnbaum, 1984) , grayness  (Veit, 1978) , darkness  (Birnbaum, 1982; Mellers & Birnbaum, 1982) , remembered distance between pairs of US cities  (Birnbaum, Anderson, & Hynan, 1989) , line length  (Masin, 2013; Parker, Schneider, & Kanow, 1975)  and perceived sweetness  (De Graaf & Frijters, 1988) . Whereas the majority of studies have concluded that ratios and differences are based on a single comparison operation, most often a difference  (Birnbaum, 1978)  and thus supporting Torgerson’s conjecture, some have found evidence of two operations  (Rule et al., 1981) .  Birnbaum (1978)  proposed a subtractive theory for difference and ratio judgments which assumed that observers could only use a subtraction operation for psychophysical continua that were interval scaled (such as brightness, loudness and heaviness) but that when there was a zero point, such as distances between cities  (Birnbaum et al., 1989) , observers were able to perform both difference and ratio judgments.

Theoretical work by  Narens (1996, 1997)  and  Luce (2002, 2004)  has put the ‘direct scaling’ method used in many studies on a firmer mathematical basis, and led to empirical tests of formal properties assumed by their psychophysical theory (monotonicity, commutativity, multiplicativity;  Augustin & Maier, 2008; Ellermeier & Faulhammer, 2000; Steingrimsson & Luce, 2005a, b, 2007; Zimmer, 2005 ). Although some of these results are consistent with Torgerson’s conjecture  (Zimmer, 2005) ,  Luce (2012)  recently argued that the strong evidence for a nonlinear weighting function in their theory  (Steingrimsson & Luce, 2005a, b, 2007)  suggests that the conjecture is probably false. Overall, the picture that emerges from 50 years of research on Torgerson’s conjecture and psychophysical scaling is complex. Although the majority of studies support the conclusion that difference comparisons are primary, consistent with Birnbaum’s subtractive theory (see also  Masin, 2014 ), performance appears to depend on task variables (e.g., instructions, distribution of stimuli) and stimulus modalities in ways that are poorly understood.

All prior studies on Torgerson’s conjecture have used procedures in which participants were given specific instructions to estimate differences or ratios numerically, or to produce stimuli with specified numerical relationships to other stimuli. If we adopt the popular distinction between explicit and implicit cognition  (Evans, 2008) , these studies have relied on explicit cognition, and moreover have used tasks that are ‘cognitively penetrable’  (Pylyshyn, 1999, 1980, 2003) , because a participant with knowledge of basic mathematics who can estimate magnitudes unbiasedly will be able to produce either differences or ratios according to the instructions. This observation is consistent with  Birnbaum et al.’s (1989)  result that participants could use both difference and ratio operations when judging distances between pairs of cities. However, because our perceptual systems seem to have evolved prior to the invention of mathematics 5000 years ago  (Neugebauer, 1969) , it is important to test Torgerson’s conjecture under conditions in which observers do not use their mathematical knowledge. Although this extends Torgerson’s conjecture beyond the scope of previous research, it is arguably more fundamental to ask whether the perceptual system uses one or two operations to compare stimuli when explicit cognitive processes are not involved. Specifically, if observers can be taught to estimate ratios and differences non-symbolically, without the use of numbers or explicit instruction, do they use a single operation, either a difference or ratio, or can they use both operations? An extension of Torgerson’s conjecture would predict that observers use one operation.

Thus our goal was to develop a non-verbal, behavioral task for ratio and difference comparisons that did not require the use of numbers or explicit cognition. The design was modeledon  Rule et al. (1981) . In their study, observers judged the ratios or differences of the perceived heaviness of pairs of weights. Eight weights were used, giving a total of 28 pairs, and were spaced according to a cube-root transformation to enhance the ordinal discrepancies between ratios and differences. Rule et al. found that rank orders of difference judgments increased with overall heaviness whereas rank orders of ratio judgments decreased, suggesting that observers could judge both differences and ratios depending on the instructions, contrary to Torgerson’s conjecture. In addition, scale values estimated from a non-metric scaling procedure separately from ratio and difference judgments did not agree, suggesting that responses were not based on a common perceptual scale.

We report two sets of experiments in which observers learned to discriminate and produce ratios and differences by feedback over a series of trials. The task was expected to engage the implicit learning system  (Ashby & Alfonso-Reese, 1998; Ashby & Maddox, 2005; Kahneman, 2011; Reber, 1989)  and avoid the problem of cognitive penetrability. On each trial, observers saw a pair of stimuli on a computer screen that varied in brightness, number of dots, or circle area, and clicked on a response bar. Feedback was provided based on a linear mapping between the response bar location and the ratio or difference of the stimulus pair. We used multiple regression, rank-order and non-metric scaling analyses to compare the control by ratios and differences over responding. Applying Torgerson’s conjecture to our task, observers should perform better when the response mapping is consistent with the comparison operation used by the visual system (either ratio or difference), and analyses should show exclusive control by that operation. However, if observers perform equally well with both mappings, and analyses show control by the untrained relation (i.e., differences when ratios are trained, or ratios when differences are trained), that would suggest that Torgerson’s conjecture is false and that the perceptual system represents both relations.

In Experiments 1a–1c the stimuli were circles that varied in brightness (1a), numbers of dots in two circular areas (1b), or circles that varied in area (1c). There were eight stimuli in each experiment, spaced according to a square-root transformation to enhance ordinal discrepancies between differences and ratios of stimulus pairs, similar to  Rule et al. (1981) . On each trial a pair of stimuli was presented, and observers made a mouse click along a 16.5-cm bar.

As in Experiment 1a, there were four blocks of 84 trials in each session, and feedback was based on either the ratio or difference of the numbers of dots.

Procedure.  There were two conditions in which the feedback provided was based on the ratios or differences of the stimulus pairs. In Experiment 1a, observers completed two sessions in each condition (ratio or difference feedback), which were run on the same day (morning and afternoon), whereas in Experiments 1b and 1c they completed one session per condition. The conditions were completed on different days, and their order was counterbalanced.

Observers were tested individually, and were seated at a desk with the computer in a quiet room. They were given the following instructions (for Experiment 1a): “In this experiment you’ll see a series of pairs of gray circles on the screen. For each pair, you need to compare the brightness of the circles by clicking on the horizontal bar. The purpose is to learn how to compare the brightnesses as accurately as you can. You’ll receive feedback for each correct response, but if your response is incorrect, you’ll have a chance to repeat with the same pair of circles. You should respond at whatever pace feels comfortable and natural for you, and will have several chances to take a break during the session”. Instructions for Experiments 1b and 1c were similar but referred to the number of dots and the sizes of the circles, respectively.

Each session consisted of four blocks of 84 trials, separated by brief rest periods. Each of the 28 stimulus pairs was presented in random order on three trials during each block. Using the mouse, participants clicked along the response bar. There was no time limit to respond. After a 100 ms delay, feedback consisting of a green oval (10 mm diameter), centered on the correct value and extending 7% of the response bar in either direction, was presented. Responses that were within the green oval produced audible feedback and were counted as ‘correct’. After 500 ms, the stimuli and response bar were removed, and the next trial began after a 2-s interval. Responses that were outside 7% of the designated value were followed by a correction trial in which the same stimulus pair was presented. Correct responses on correction trials were followed by audible feedback, and only one correction trial was presented. The purpose of the correction trials was to reinforce learning of the task; their data was omitted from all analyses.

After the first condition had been completed, participants were told that how correct responses were determined would change in the next session. However, they were not told the nature of the change and instructed to learn to respond accurately based on the feedback that was presented.

The primary goal of the present study was to determine if responding was consistent with control by a single comparison operation, or whether observers used two operations. To address this question we conducted several analyses. First, we ran hierarchical multiple regressions in which the trained values were entered at the first step, and the trained values from the other condition were entered at the second step. Ratio values were used to predict responding in the ratio condition, and then the difference values were entered as a predictor at the second step (and vice versa). In this way, we were able to test whether responding was singly or jointly controlled by ratios and differences in each condition.

Results are shown in  Table 1 . For 8 of 12 conditions in Experiment 1a (4 ratio, 4 difference), 7 of 12 in Experiment 1b (4 ratio, 3 difference), and 8 of 12 in Experiment 1c (5 ratio, 3 difference), the relation that was not trained contributed significant additional variance. Thus, responding in the majority of cases (23/36; 64%) was determined jointly by ratios and differences. Notably, for 4 cases in Experiment 1a the untrained relation was a significant predictor in the observers’ first condition (Jh and C, ratio; Z and E, difference), 1 case in Experiment 1b (Z, ratio) and 5 cases in Experiment 1c (Jh, M, S, ratio; Z, Cy, difference). This is important because if the evidence for joint control had only been obtained in the second condition, it might be attributed to a carryover effect of prior training. Thus, these observers demonstrated spontaneous control by both ratios and differences even though they had only been trained with one relation.

Previous research has established ( Birnbaum, 1980 ;  Rule et al., 1981 ) that if observers are responding based on two operations (i.e., ratio operation in ratio condition, difference operation in difference condition), then values derived from non-metric scaling of ratio estimates will be linearly related to the logarithm of stimulus values, whereas corresponding values derived from difference estimates will be a nonlinear, positively-accelerated function. To demonstrate these predictions, the training values from the ratio and difference conditions were analyzed by the PROXSCAL procedure in SPSS (ver. 24), assuming one dimension and ratio model with Torgerson initial configuration. Results of Experiments 1a–c confirm that observers can learn to produce both ratios and differences without explicit instruction for pairs of visual stimuli that varied in brightness, number of dots, or area. Three methods of analysis – multiple regression, rank-order pairwise comparisons, and non-metric scaling – converged to identify joint control over responding by ratios and differences of stimulus values. There was considerable heterogeneity across observers and conditions; in  Table 1 , responding was jointly controlled by ratios and differences (defined as statistically significant regression coefficients) in 19 of 36 cases (53%), whereas there was sole control by ratios in 7 cases (19%), and sole control by differences in 10 cases (28%). Notably, considering only the first condition completed by each observer (and ignoring later ones by those that participated in multiple experiments), in 6 of 11 cases (55%; Observers Jh, Z, M and C in Experiment 1a; and S and Cy in Experiment 1c) there was significant control by the untrained relation. Thus, even when observers were trained to respond based on stimulus differences, ratios explained unique variance in their responding, and vice versa. These results suggest that both ratios and differences are computed by the perceptual system, and can singly or jointly control responding in our non-symbolic procedure. The implication is that Torgerson’s conjecture, as applied to the present task, is false.

However, it is important to establish that observers’ responding was based on learning ratio and difference relations, rather than just specific instances of stimulus–response pairs (i.e., exemplar learning). Thus in Experiments 2a and 2b we replicated the conditions in Experiments 1a and 1c but included transfer tests to determine if responding was controlled by relational learning. We also used a between-groups design so that participants were trained on only one relation, and a relatively larger sample size to ensure generality.

In Experiments 2a and 2b, observers responded in a task similar to Experiments 1a and 1c, in which stimulus pairs varying in brightness (Experiment 2a) or circle area (Experiment 2b) were presented on each trial, and feedback was presented based on the location of responses along a horizontal bar mapped to the difference or ratio of the stimulus values. The same procedure and stimuli were used, except that four stimulus pairs were withheld from the first two blocks of training but presented in the last two blocks of training during transfer trials. On transfer trials, no feedback was presented and the next trial was scheduled regardless of the observers’ response (i.e., there were no correction trials for the transfer pairs). Observers were randomly assigned to either the ratio or difference group, and completed one session in which feedback was based on the ratio or difference of the training values. The key questions were whether observers’ responded differentially to the transfer trials depending on whether ratio or difference values had been trained, and whether results from Experiments 1a and 1c would be replicated with a larger number of observers.

Overall, the results of Experiments 2a and 2b replicate and extend those of Experiments 1a and 1c with a larger sample size and between-groups design. Torgerson (1961)  proposed that observers used a single relation when comparing stimuli, either a ratio or difference, but which relation could not be determined empirically. Torgerson’s conjecture was originally posed in the context of psychophysical research using procedures, such as magnitude estimation, which required explicit cognition and use of numbers. Although these studies have helped us understand how observers estimate ratios and differences numerically for many stimulus modalities  (Birnbaum, 1982) , it is arguably more fundamental to apply Torgerson’s conjecture when numbers and explicit cognitive processes are not involved: Does the perceptual system use a single operation in an implicit, nonsymbolic comparison task, either ratios or differences, or does it use both operations?

To address this question we developed a task for estimating ratios and differences that did not require observers to use numbers or receive explicit instruction. In two sets of experiments, observers saw pairs of stimuli that varied in brightness, number of dots, or circle area, and made an analogue response by clicking on a bar below the stimuli. Feedback was provided based on the ratio or difference of the stimulus pair, linearly mapped to the response bar. Observers learned to respond with good accuracy. Averaged across experiments, correlations of responses with training values were very high in both ratio and difference conditions,  r  s  
                         = 
                       .94 and .95, respectively. Thus observers learned to produce responses that were highly correlated with both relations by feedback and without explicit instruction.

The primary question was whether responding depended on one or two operations. Multiple regression analyses showed that in approximately half of individual cases, there was significant control by both ratios and differences, regardless of which was trained, suggesting use of two operations. These results were corroborated by rank-order and non-metric scaling analyses. There was also considerable heterogeneity across observers, with some showing exclusive control by the trained relation, joint control by both relations, and sometimes even exclusive control by the untrained relation (i.e., differences in the ratio condition, or vice versa). Monte Carlo analyses confirmed that the multiple regressions were able to recover ratio and difference coefficients from simulated data, and modeling the data with psychophysical power functions confirmed the evidence for two operations in the multiple regressions.

These results suggest that the perceptual system computes both ratios and differences when comparing stimuli, and therefore that Torgerson’s conjecture is false. The control by untrained relations is the strongest evidence against the conjecture in the present data. If observers had merely been able to learn to respond accurately in both ratio and difference conditions, it might be argued that there was only one perceptual relation, for example ratios, and that performance in the difference condition was based on observers learning to associate ‘difference’ responses with perceived ratio values. Conversely, if the sole perceptual relation was differences, then performance in the ratio condition could be based on similar associative learning. Control by the untrained relation shows that the perceptual system computes both ratios and differences, contrary to Torgerson’s conjecture.

Our experimental task is similar in some respects to function learning, which has studied how participants learn the relationship between two continuous variables  (Carroll, 1963; DeLosh, Busemeyer, & McDaniel, 1997; Kalish, 2013; Koh & Meyer, 1991; Lucas, Griffiths, Williams, & Kalish, 2015) . For example,  Koh and Meyer (1991)  trained participants in a perceptual-motor task in which they learned to associate keypress durations with the line length. Eight stimulus pairs were used for training, and four for testing, and in different conditions participants learned a linear, logarithmic, or power function relationship between stimulus and response. Koh and Meyer found that the power function relationships were easiest to learn. Models for function learning have generally adopted one of two approaches  (Lucas et al., 2015) : rule-based, in which participants learn a specific functional form (e.g., power function) between stimulus and response  (Koh & Meyer, 1991) , and similarity-based in which participants learn several stimulus–response pairs as exemplars via associative learning and generalize to new pairs on the basis of similarity  (DeLosh et al., 1997) .

Our results contrast with recent studies by  Masin (2013, 2014) , who has reported that observers are incapable of judging ratios. For example, in  Masin’s (2013)  Experiment 2, observers judged the ratios of brightnesses of disks displayed on a computer monitor. A functional measurement methodology  (Anderson, 1974)  was used in which standard and comparison stimuli were varied in a factorial design. Masin showed that ratio comparisons would predict a diverging, fan-shaped set of straight lines when plotted against different standard stimuli. He found that the average ratio estimates varied linearly with the luminance ratio, and there was no evidence of an interaction, showing that slopes were similar for each standard luminance. He concluded that observers evaluated the brightness difference rather than ratio, even though they were explicitly asked to estimate ratios. Similar results were obtained in studies which stimuli varied in perceived heaviness  (Masin, 2014) , although large individual differences were reported (see  Masin, 2014 ,  Figs. 4 and 5 ). However, Masin’s studies are based on factorial designs in which a failure to reject the null hypothesis (that is, obtain a significant interaction) is taken as support of difference comparisons. Arguably, designs in which ratio and difference comparisons make distinct  a priori  predictions that can be tested separately (i.e., do not rely on a failure to reject the null), such as the present study, can provide a more sensitive test.

If correct, our conclusion that Torgerson’s conjecture is false and the perceptual system computes two operations rather than one has potentially broad implications. From an evolutionary perspective, perceptual systems may have developed the ability to compute differences and ratios to support locomotion in time and space. For example, an organism that had the functional ability to estimate its velocity (i.e., distance/time) would have considerable advantages in foraging  (Gallistel, 1990) . Note that estimation of velocity as an organism travels from A to B requires both differences (i.e., distance traveled from A to B; time from A to B) and ratios (distance/time).

More fundamentally, an ability to compute two operations suggests that the perceptual system could represent elements of an algebraic field. In abstract algebra, a field consists of two operations over a set (e.g., addition and multiplication), their inverses (subtraction and division) and identity elements, and axioms of commutativity, associativity, and distributivity  (Burton, 1988) . Fields generalize the intuitions underlying arithmetic over the real numbers. A field representation could support behavior that appears to be computationally complex, or require mathematics to describe. For example, there is much evidence that spatial navigation in nonhumans (including insects) is accomplished by path integration, which calculates an update of current location based on vector addition (i.e., distance traveled since last turn and angle with respect to the sun or other landmark;  Etienne & Jeffery, 2004; Muller & Wehner, 1988, 2010; Srinivasan, 2015 ). A foraging honeybee that visits several flower patches is able to return to the hive by the most direct route, as if it computed the vector algebra—although how its (relatively) tiny brain does this remains unknown  (Webb & Wystrach, 2016) . The field axioms are necessary for computations of path integration to be consistent—that is, for different routes to the same location to give equal estimates of the current location.

From this perspective, our experimental task could be described as a nonsymbolic ‘artificial algebra’, because observers are taught arithmetic relations between stimulus magnitudes without the use of numbers or explicit instruction. Similar to how artificial grammars are used in psycholinguistics to explore how the rules underlying language are acquired implicitly  (Dienes, Broadbent, & Berry, 1991; Pothos, 2007; Reber, 1989) , our task could be adapted to study the algebraic structure of perceptual representations – for example whether the field properties are fully satisfied – and eventually provide insight into how computationally-complex behavior such as spatial navigation is accomplished.

In summary, the present study tested predictions of Torgerson’s conjecture applied to an implicit, nonsymbolic task. Observers learned to produce analogue estimates of both differences and ratios of stimuli that varied in brightness, numbers of dots, and area. Results showed that even though only one relation was trained, responding for a majority of observers was significantly controlled by both differences and ratios. The implication is that Torgerson’s conjecture as applied to this task is false, and the perceptual system computes two operations corresponding to differences and ratios when comparing stimuli.
