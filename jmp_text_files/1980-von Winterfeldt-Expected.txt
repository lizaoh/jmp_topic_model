The purpose of the experiment was  to test several independence assumptions that distinguish between models for risky multiattribute preferences. In particular,  the additivity and the expected utility part of the additive expected utility model were tested symmetrically. The degree and  form of model violations were established, and the effects of instructions and of response modes were examined. All  independence assumptions were violated by a bias to prefer a gamble or a commodity bundle that was previously matched against  a standard. Systematic and strong violations that superseded this bias questioned the validity of the additive expected  utility model. In violation of the additivity part of that model subjects consistently preferred the gamble with more balanced  outcomes when comparing gambles with identical marginal probability distributions. This trend, called multiattribute risk  aversion, was independent of subjects' single attribute risk attitude. Instructions and response modes had no noticeable  impacts on these response patterns.  This study examined the structure of human preferences in risky multiattribute decision situations. In such situations  outcomes of decisions are uncertain and have several value relevant aspects or attributes. Examples are choosing a job with  uncertain promotion and salary aspects, or selecting among gambles for commodity bundles. The best known model for preferences  among risky multiattribute alternatives is the expected utility model with an additive utility function (see Fishburn, 1965;  Keeney and Raiffa, 1976). A number of arguments and some experimental evidence suggests, however, that subjects violate this  model systematically.  One such violations occurs, because people are seldom indifferent between gambles with identical marginal (single attribute),  but different joint probability distributions. Consider, for example, the following two gambles which offer identical chances  of either winning a TV or a stereo:  Gamble 1: If HEADS comes up, you win a TV and a stereo. MODELS AND INDEPENDENCE ASSUMPTIONS  For simplicity, consider a set of gambles with m mutually exclusive and exhaustive events E; (j = 1, 2,..., m) and outcomes  which vary on n attributes Ai (i = 1, 2,..., n). A particular gamble x produces a multiattribute outcome xi if event E;  occurs; similarly, x defines a single attribute gamble xi in attribute Ai . xi; is the ith single attribute outcome of x if  event j occures. Thus x can be thought of an an ensemble of m multiattribute outcomes (conditional on events) or as an  ensemble of n single attribute gambles (conditional on attributes).  Multiattribute models will be distinguished on the basis of two independence assumptions called event independence and  attribute independence. Event independence is the usual sure thing axiom (Savage, 1954) applied to multiattribute outcomes x;  . Tversky (1967) analyzed subjects' preferences for gambles that had two outcomes, cigarettes and candy. Subjects expressed  their preferences by stating selling prices for several combinations of cigarettes and candy, as well as for gambles for  either (but not both) outcomes. A riskless additive model as in (6) described subjects' preferences for combinations of  cigarettes and candy very well. Further results indicated that riskless value functions and risky utility functions deviated  consistently (i.e., u; ~/-- v;) if one assumed that probabilities summed to one. If, on the other hand, one assumed that  riskless value functions and risky utility functions were identical (i.e., u; = v;), probabilities no longer summed to one.  Tversky interpreted these findings as a failure of the expected utility model (2).  v. Winterfeldt (1971) directly analyzed the marginality assumption, and a form of joint independence. When subjects were asked  to state their preferences among apartments that varied only on two attributes (distance from campus and transportation  facilities), their responses conformed to the riskless additive value model (6). But when asked to state their preferences  among gambles for such apartments, subjects exhibited a clear trend for multiattribute risk aversion in violation of model (5).  In a third experiment Fischer (1976) analyzed subjects' preferences for jobs that varied on three attributes (salary, city,  type of work). Subjects evaluated both riskless combinations of the three attributes and gambles for different jobs. Additive  statistical models provided excellent approximations to both the risky and the riskless responses, supporting both models (5)  and (6). However, a detailed analysis of ordinal response patterns showed that half of the subjects showed significant  departures from additivity. Violations of the additive expected utility model (5) were more substantial than those of the  riskless additive model (6).  Delbeke and Fauville (1974) conducted an experiment with the explicit purpose of testing the marginality assumption. Subjects  had to state their preferences among gambles for various two attribute stimuli ranging from vacation trips, that varied on the  attributes "location" and "mode of transportation", to combinations of job types and salaries. Although the small number of  subjects and stimuli do not allow a conclusive interpretation of their results, Delbeke and Fauville identified some  violations of the marginality assumption even if riskless preferences were additive.  In summary, all four experiments show that the riskless additive model (6) can predict subjects' riskless preferences quite  well. In these studies the additive expected utility model was violated either through multiattribute risk aversion (v.  Winterfeldt, Delbeke and Fauville), through deviations from additive response patterns (Fischer), or through systematic  differences between risky and riskless single attribute utility functions (TN,ersky). Still, the question remains, whether  these violations were due to violations of the expected utility part of model (6) as Tversky hypothesized or to a failure of  the additivity part, as the remaining experiments indicate. Instructions and response modes. Subjects were presented with the 36 test items twice. In the first run they did not receive  any special instructions on how to compare the test gambles. In the second run six subjects were cued to compare the gambles  in a test item by first comparing single attribute gambles individually; six subjects had to compare the commodity bundles  first separately; and six subjects did not receive any special instructions. Three response modes were used: simple preference  or indifference judgment, rating of the strength of preference on a scale from 0 (indifference) to 100 (very strong  preference), and cash equivalent assessments for each of the two gambles in a test item. In the cash equivalent assessment  mode, subjects were asked to consider each gamble separately and assess an amount of money such that they would just be  indifferent between playing the gamble or receiving the sure amount of money as a gift. To reinforce subjects to state their  true indifference points, Marschak's bidding procedure (see Becker, DeGroot,  MULTIATTRIBUTE PREFERENCES 73  and Marschak, 1964) was applied and it was explained that some of the gambles would be played after the experiment according  to that procedure. Subjects were told that in these gambles they could win up to ten pounds of ground beef, 16 gallons of  gasoline, or $20 if they stated their true preferences. Deviations from true preferences and bad luck could, however, result  in smaller payoffs. Subjects were told their payoff in these gambles would be in the form of non-refundable gift certificates.  Procedure. Subjects were run individually in two sessions. In an initial one hour training session several example problems  had to be solved. Strategies in arriving at preferences and indifferences as well as cash equivalents had to be explained (and  were subsequently probed by the experimenter), and some preliminary assessments were done. In addition, subjects had to assess  cash equivalents for all possible combinations of gasoline and beef twice, answer some simple indifference questions about  gambles for money, and assess the four matches twice. After training the four matches were interactively reassessed with the  help of the experimenter. The first session was concluded with the presentation of the 36 test items. Responses to test items  lasted about 1-11 hours. The second session followed within three days after the first. Subjects were presented with the same  36 test items. DISCUSSION  The main purpose of this experiment was to test independence assumptions for additivity and expected utility in risky  multiattribute preferences. Specifically, the assumption was tested that the additive expected utility model (5) is violated  by multiattribute risk aversion.  A first look at the general pattern of responses in the independence tests revealed a strong bias to prefer the gamble with a  matched value. This bias was independent of context, only to some extent dependent of the type of match, and it held for all  but two subjects. This bias violates all models (1-7) for risky multiattribute preferences since the matches did not hold up  in different contexts as would be predicted by attribute or event independence assumptions. Since the bias showed up even when  the match was presented in the zero context, it is more fundamental than a violation of specific independence assumptions.  MULTIATTRIBUTE PREFERENCES 79  There are two possible interpretations of the bias effect: first, that subjects mismatched by always stating a higher matched  value than their true indifference point; second, that subjects matched correctly but viewed the judgments about test items in  a fundamentally different way than the matching judgment.  Why would subjects have mismatched? One possible explanation is the anchoring and adjustment bias that Tversky and Kahneman  (1975) hypothesized for judgments under uncertainty. This bias can explain some mismatching. For example, in the match (16 0)  - (x x) risk averse subjects may have anchored on the expected value x = 8 as a starting point, realized a preference (for the  certainty equivalent) and adjusted down to accommodate their risk aversion. In fact, this process was described by several  subjects as their way of arriving at the indifference point. If the downward adjustment was done insufficiently, a preference  for the certainty equivalent side would show up later.  Similarly, the anchoring and insufficient adjustment hypothesis can explain the bias for the bundle matches. Here, subjects  may have anchored on a value in the gasoline dimension which equated the cash values of both commodity bundles. Realizing a  preference for the side with more gasoline (the matched side), they would then adjust the gasoline match downward. If done  insufficiently, subjects would later show a preference for the matched side. This hypothesis assumes that subjects would  always adjust the gasoline match downward from the cash equivalent value. Indeed, most subjects indicated that given the same  market value, they would prefer gasoline over beef, thus leading to a downward adjustment of the gasoline match.  A few responses can, however, not be explained by anchoring and insufficient adjustment. Risk prone subjects would presumably  start with the expected value x = 8 in the gamble match (16 0) ti (x x), and increase their match to accommodate their risk  proneness. If done insufficiently they would later prefer the left gamble over the certainty equivalent side. However, even  the seven risk prone subjects showed the bias for the certainty equivalent side.  When confronted with their bias after the experiment, several subjects indicated that they would resolve the inconsistency by  changing their match. But some subjects had not even recognized that the test item with the zero context was identical to the  previous match. They argued that they used a different strategy when matching than when arriving at a preference in the test  items. In their matching responses they compared composites (e.g., expected values with a certainty equivalent), but they  compared the test item gambles element by element. They were puzzled by the bias and they were not quickly ready to resolve it  either way. If forced, however, these subjects tended to be more suspicious otheir test item responses than of their  matches. Thus, although a majority of the subjects may have mismatched-possibly due to anchoring and insufficient  adjustment-some subjects apparently used quite different response strategies in simple matching responses vs. complex  comparisons.  Naturally, the bias overshadowed some experimental effects. The overall violations of attribute and event independence were  not strong enough to conclude whether the bias, or violations of additivity or of expected utility were its cause. However,  the patterns of violations for attribute and event independence show that attribute independence was systematically violated  by multiattribute risk aversion, which overrode the bias, while  HO DETLOF VON WINTERFELDT  event independence violations were largely due to the preference bias. Thus, the additivity part of model (6) was violated  substantively on top of the bias, while the expected utility part was violated through the preference bias only.  Multiattribute risk aversion showed very clearly for all except two subjects. The explanation which subjects gave for their  multiattribute risk aversion is persuasive-they preferred the gamble in which they could win something in each event over the  gamble in which they could win a lot in one event and nothing in the other. Even when the identical marginal distributions  were pointed out to them, subjects did not change their preference. The utility independence version of attribute  independence, on the other hand, could not be shown to be violated beyond the preference bias.  Delbeke and Fauville (1974) hypothesized that violations of the marginality assumption are a result of riskless interactions  between attributes such as complementary or nonadditive substitution. This experiment, in contrast, suggests that  multiattribute risk aversion can occur in the presence of riskless additivity. There was no reason to assume that gasoline and  beef did not substitute additively. In fact, most subjects indicated that they would trade gasoline and ground beef according  to their respective cash equivalents. Such a trade-off can be expressed in a riskless additive model (6). Yet consistent  violations of the marginality assumption occurred.  Multiattribute risk aversion can also be interpreted as a form of single attribute risk aversion after commodity bundles are  collapsed to a single attribute, e.g., a cash equivalent. A subject's single attribute risk attitude should then be identical  to his or her multiattribute risk attitude. However, the experimental data do not support this hypothesis. Even subjects who  were single attribute risk neutral or even risk prone in gas, beef, or cash gambles were found to be multiattribute risk  averse. These results suggest that multiattribute risk aversion is independent of riskless additivity and single attribute  risk aversion.  How would one interpret Tversky's results in the light of multiattribute risk aversion? Tversky found differing riskless and  risky utility functions (i.e., those derived from cash equivalent assessment of commodity bundles and those derived from  judgments about gambles), if he assumed the standard SEU model with probabilities summing to one. On the other hand, if he  assumed identical riskless and risky utility functions, he found that probabilities did no longer sum to one. Tversky  interpreted these results as a failure of SEU.  Multiattribute risk aversion offers another hypothesis: Tversky's results may not be due to a failure of the SEU model, but  rather due to multiattribute risk aversion and thus a failure of the additivity part of the additive expected utility model  (5). Tversky found that his subjects were additive in the absence of risk, supporting model (6). His data do not, however,  bear on additivity in the presence of risk. Assuming SEU, riskless additivity (as in 6), and multiattribute risk aversion  imply that riskless and risky utility functions must be different. For example, Keeney and Raiffa (1976) show that riskless  and risky utility functions must be related by an exponential transformation if both the multiplicative expected utility model  (4) and the riskless additive model (6) hold. Thus, multiattribute risk aversion can explain differences between risky and  riskless utility functions without giving up the SEU assumption. (3) Attribute independence, on the other hand, was systematically violated through multiattribute risk aversion.
