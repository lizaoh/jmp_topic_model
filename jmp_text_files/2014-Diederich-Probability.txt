The book presents a brand new approach to modeling cognition and decision based on principles abstracted from quantum theory. The theory does not rely on the quantum brain hypothesis, but instead it uses the mathematical principles from quantum theory to address behavioral findings from empirical research on human cognition and decision making research. Readers seeking to learn new ideas will find them here, whether they agree with them or not. The preface to this book ends with the claim “People either love or hate these ideas, but no one remains unaffected.” This seems to be a fair assessment. The ideas are quite revolutionary, and any revolution has supporters and reactionists. The book proposes and describes the use of a new probability theory for cognitive and decision sciences. More accurately, the book is about the application of an existing theory developed by quantum physicists, and what is new is the use of this type of mathematics outside of quantum physics. The authors make it perfectly clear that this book is not about quantum brains or consciousness, and instead it is concerned with the use of some of the basic mathematical principles to account for human behavior. The basic principles include the use of vector spaces (technically, Hilbert spaces), projectors, unitary operators, and the Schrödinger equation. There is no Planck constant of course, and instead this parameter from physics is be replaced by some new parameter that describes the dynamics of thinking and decision making. The reader does not need any background in quantum physics, and the book provides all the basic ideas that are needed. The book may be of interest not only to mathematical psychologists, but also to physicists interested in exploring applications of quantum theory to entirely new fields.

The book is approximately 400 pages and it is comprised of 12 chapters plus an extensive appendix (definitions, derivations, computer programs). The first half of the book makes use of what might call the structure of quantum theory, and the latter half engages the dynamic aspects of quantum theory. The first chapter  Why use quantum theory for cognition and decision? Some compelling reasons  is designed to motivate the reader to read the rest of the book. It presents some psychological reasons for adopting a quantum approach, followed by several quick empirical example applications from psychology, and it ends with a historical review of quantum physics and the pioneers (physicists working on the foundations of physics) who initiated the idea of applying the theory to fields outside of physics. The second chapter What is quantum theory? An elementary introduction provides the basic ideas concerning the structural part of quantum theory used in this book. It presents a detailed and elementary introduction to using quantum theory using a simple example from preferential choice, then it provides a comprehensive review of linear and matrix algebra (the essential mathematics needed for this theory), and it concludes with a rigorous presentation of the quantum axioms (as used in this book). Chapter three  What can quantum theory predict? Predicting question order effects on attitudes  is based on  Wang and Busemeyer (2013)  and it gives the first detailed example application. It presents a quantum model for question order effects found with attitudinal types of questions, then it derives a strong and parameter free test of the quantum model, and finally it reports some empirical results of tests based on data from large national surveys. The fourth chapter  How to apply quantum theory? Accounting for human probability judgment errors  is based on  Busemeyer, Pothos, Franco, and Trueblood (2011)  and it applies the theory to some puzzling findings concerning probability judgment errors such as the conjunction and disjunction fallacies and order effects on inference. The fifth chapter  Quantum-inspired models of concept combinations , building on work by  Aerts and Gabora (2005) , goes into another new direction by applying the idea of quantum entanglement to findings concerning conceptual combinations. It presents the use of Bell type inequalities to empirically test whether data from a conceptual combination study can be decomposed into products of components probabilities conditioned on hidden or latent variables. The sixth chapter  An application of quantum theory to conjoint memory recognition  develops an application of the model to account for conjoint memory recognition data (episodic overdistribution effect) and it presents a quantitative comparison of the quantum model with a Markov model previously developed to account for these results. The seventh chapter  Quantum-like models of human semantic space , building on previous work by  Bruza, Kitto, Nelson, and McEvoy (2009) , develops applications of the concept of quantum entanglement for understanding some important findings from the associative memory recall literature. This chapter also tries to make connections with previous vector and matrix based memory models. The eighth chapter  What about quantum dynamics? More advanced principles , building on work by  Atmanspacher, Filk, and Romer (2004)  and  Busemeyer, Wang, and Townsend (2006) , introduces the reader to quantum dynamics and compares quantum probabilistic dynamic systems with Markov dynamic systems. It begins with a simple application to bi-stable perception, and it ends with a more complex theoretical comparison of quantum versus Markov random walk models. The ninth chapter  What is the quantum advantage? Applications to decision making  applies the quantum dynamic model to several example problems in decision making including Allais and Ellsberg paradoxes, the prisoner’s dilemma game, dynamic consistency with two-stage decision task, and categorization–decision tasks. This chapter includes a quantitative comparison of the quantum model with a stochastic version of prospect theory with a large data set on two stage decision tasks. Chapter 10  How to models human information processing using quantum information theory  reviews some basic concepts of quantum information processing, which were originally developed for quantum computers, but could be used to model human information processing. This includes a review of the use of tensor products of quantum qubits and the transformation of these states by control-U gates. Chapter 11  Can quantum systems learn? Quantum updating  explores some new ideas about learning using quantum theory. It presents a quantum Bayesian type of updating rule for learning the state from observations, a gradient descent type of learning rule to learn the transition amplitudes of a unitary transformation that maps states to states, and a quantum reinforcement learning algorithm for learning the probability of taking actions based on rewards. The final chapter  What are the future prospects for quantum cognition and decision?  provides a summary overview and outlines some future directions of research. It also covers some important questions that remain to be answered such as (a) the neural substrate for quantum computations in the brain (which could be classical and not necessarily quantum), and the relation of the ideas presented in the book to the problem of consciousness. The final chapter ends with a dialog between the authors and previous reviewers. A comprehensive appendix is also included that contains some technical material, some proofs, some additional quantum models, and computer code.

The book provides an interesting new account of some very puzzling findings. For example, using a one shot prisoner dilemma game,  Shafir and Tversky (1992)  found that if an opponent was known to defect, then players almost always defected; if an opponent was known to cooperate, then players again almost always defected, but if the opponent’s action was unknown, then players switched and decided to frequently cooperate. This finding violates the law of total probability which in this case would state that the probability of defection in the unknown case should be some weighted average of the probabilities for the two known cases. The quantum model provides a compelling account for this and many other examples of violations of total probability that appear in the psychological literature. This book covers applications that range across topics including attitude questions, probabilistic inferences, memory recognition, associative recall, risky decision making, prisoner dilemma games, information processing and learning. The applications include empirical applications that provide parameter free qualitative tests of the theory as well as quantitative comparison with competing models based on fitting the same number of model parameters. These empirical tests are generally (but not always) supporting the new quantum model. The authors do not claim from these results that the quantum model is better than existing models, for that will require a lot more empirical research, but they do argue that these results provide sufficient support to consider quantum models as serious competitors to more traditional models.

The mathematical concepts used in the book are not too difficult, but not too easy either. On the one hand, the authors stick primarily to finite vector spaces to avoid the complexity of infinite spaces, and so the math can be limited to linear and matrix algebra and first order differential equations. On the other hand, the mathematics of quantum theory is sometimes a bit difficult to understand because heavy use is made of changes in the basis used to represent the vector space, which may be unfamiliar to many mathematical psychologists. Of course complex numbers are also in the theory but this is not really difficult and they mainly come into play when forming the unitary matrix that transforms one basis to another. The book attempts to be very tutorial and this may be good for readers who are not so familiar with these branches of mathematics, but the more advanced reader might prefer shorter expositions that get more directly to the point without so many details. Although it is difficult to check for all errors with such a long book, there seem to be only a few and they are easy to spot and correct because they arise from small typing errors. One final comment concerns the name of this book. Why use the name “quantum” if one is not doing real physics? As the book makes clear, the authors only use the mathematical principles of quantum theory divorced from actual physical principles (such as, e.g., using Planck’s constant). Perhaps a better name would be something Hilbert space probability theory, or maybe less intimidating, projective probability theory. On the one hand, if the theory was re-named projective probability theory, then the new name would help clearly divorce the ideas from quantum physics as they desire. On the other hand, if the book was re-named projective probability theory, someone could claim that the authors were trying to mislead readers that they invented a new theory when in fact it only a re-application of basic quantum principles. Well, at this point, it is too late to change anyway.

And despite the name it made it even into a German popular science magazine (P.M. Magazin,  www.pm-magazin.de , with the article’s title “We think like quanta”) and enjoyed good company with Daniel Kahneman (quantum logical thinking, fast and slow thinking, Nobel laureate for economics); Theodor Hänsch (Nobel laureate for physics); or Roger Penrose (mathematician), just to name a few.

As can be seen from the last discussion, it is difficult not to be affected, one way or the other, by the revolutionary new ideas presented in this book. It challenges many of our traditional ways of thinking about modeling in cognitive and decision sciences. It is very worthwhile reading from which most will learn a lot of new ideas.
