Perceptual decision making is the process by which agents use sensory information to make inferences about the world and select discrete actions. Sometimes these actions result in rewards that are subjectively experienced and evaluated by the agent. The agent’s assumed goal is to act optimally so as to maximize some measure of the reward it accumulates during the course of its behaviour. Perceptual decision making is difficult in practice because real perceptual systems, inferences, action selections and reward outcomes are all subject to uncertainty. Understanding how decision-making agents in the real world operate effectively in the face of this uncertainty is important, since doing so will help to explain animal perceptual decision-making behaviour and could also inform the design of autonomous decision-making systems. A key approach that has been used to explain biological agents’ behaviour in this context is through the use of statistical decision theory ( McNamara & Houston, 1980 ).

The leading decision-making models that have a statistical basis in psychology and neuroscience are signal detection theory (SDT) and the sequential probability ratio test (SPRT) ( Bogacz et al., 2006; Dayan & Daw, 2008; Gold & Shadlen, 2007 ). Although these methods are routinely employed to explain behavioural data ( Laming, 1968; Ratcliff & McKoon, 2008; Stone, 1960 ), the mathematical relationship between these two theories has some subtleties that are key to understanding their implications. Moreover, while it is common knowledge that they are equivalent to other mechanistic theories of decision making, principally the drift diffusion model (DDM), there are also mathematical subtleties in this relation that depend on the probability distributions assumed to underlie the sampled evidence ( Bitzer et al., 2014; Bogacz et al., 2006; Jones & Love, 2011 ). Understanding the implications of the formalism is important because, despite recent single-neuron studies advancing our understanding of perceptual decision making at the neural level, a complete account of how decision making is implemented biologically remains unfinished ( Churchland et al., 2008; Hanks & Summerfield, 2017 ) and will depend on the mathematics of the theory.

In this tutorial review, we explore these issues by examining the relationship between SDT and SPRT from a mathematical viewpoint that bears in mind how biology might implement these methods. Because of the additional focus on biological implementation, we start by framing the background to the perceptual decision-making problem using Marr’s three levels of analysis ( Marr, 1982 ). A fundamental goal of decision-making research is to explain behaviour; that is, to answer the question of why humans (or animals) act the way they do. Marrian approaches to this question (including the related tradition of rational analysis) offer “teleological explanations” for behaviour by proposing that biological decision-makers are adapted so the function of, or  reason  for, their behaviour is to mirror optimal solutions to problems posed by their environment ( Anderson, 1991; Griffiths, Chater et al., 2012 ). The aim is to define specific hypotheses for the decision-making process and to investigate the consequences of those assumptions for the algorithmic and implementation solutions to the environmental problem. Empirically testable predictions naturally follow, which can be used to test the validity of these assumptions for biological decision makers. Some of these tests, however, have in fact shown that humans often appear to behave suboptimally; the most famous examples appearing in economic decision making ( Kahneman & Tversky, 2000 ). In another example from a perceptual decision-making study, the majority of participants were found to perform suboptimally on a binary-choice task, although the authors speculated that this could be explained by individuals setting their own optimality criteria rather than maximizing reward-rate as assumed ( Bogacz et al., 2010 ). As a consequence of such findings, some authors have questioned the persistence of optimality assumptions when modelling behaviour ( Bowers & Davis, 2012; Jones & Love, 2011; Marcus & Davis, 2013 ) (but see also ( Griffiths, Chater et al., 2012 ) for a response to these criticisms). The aim of this article is not to suggest that biological decision-makers act optimally under all conditions, but rather to review the statistical basis for a normative model for binary-choice decisions (the DDM) and to discuss plausible biophysical implementations for diffusion decision models.

Here, we consider the computational problem that a decision-making agent is solving to be binary-choice perceptual decision making. Whilst this is a simplification of the general perceptual decision-making task, binary-choice decisions could be considered to be the core task that underlies multiple-choice decisions ( Eisenberg, 1991 ). Many perceptual decisions are most naturally formulated in terms of ‘yes–no’ binary-choice decisions ( Dayan & Daw, 2008; Green & Swets, 1966 ), and decisions with greater than two choices can be tackled by a system of combined ‘yes–no’ sub-decisions ( Armitage, 1950; Lorden, 1976; Sobel & Wald, 1949 ). With this in mind, we proceed by concentrating first on binary-choice perceptual decision making, although other work has tackled the question of how agents handle more complex multiple-choice problems ( Krajbich & Rangel, 2011; Roe et al., 2001; Tajima et al., 2019 ).

The assumed goal of an agent in the binary-choice task is to act optimally on information, gathered by their perceptual systems, by selecting the best action from a set of two possibilities. We consider two sampling paradigms: a fixed sample-size (interrogative paradigm) and a freely-chosen sample size (free-response paradigm). Empirical studies have shown that in the free-response paradigm, experimental parameters such as time pressure, reward contingencies and sensory evidence quality (for example, percentage of coherently moving dots in the random dot kinematogram experiment) determine the response time (RT) ( Ratcliff et al., 2016 ). If that time comes at a cost, then the agent must balance that cost of deliberation against the cost of making an error – a problem known as the speed–accuracy trade-off ( Bogacz et al., 2010 ).

The optimality criterion we focus on in this paper is the minimization of the expected loss (as a function of accuracy and decision time) associated with a single decision. If losses are simply regarded as negative rewards, then minimizing the expected loss is equivalent to maximizing expected reward. The specific criterion we use is Wald’s linear combination of accuracy and decision time ( Wald & Wolfowitz, 1948 ). An often used alternative is maximization of the long-term reward-rate ( Balci et al., 2011; Simen et al., 2009 ), although some authors have concluded that the reward rate optimization hypothesis for animals lacks empirical support ( Rahnev & Denison, 2018; Ratcliff et al., 2016 ). In fact, the notion that we can meaningfully define optimality in any given context, beyond assumptions that we must make about biological decision-makers, is disputed; it may be that we cannot access the precise computational problem being solved by an animal, even if that animal is indeed behaving optimally ( Rahnev & Denison, 2018 ).

Algorithmic solutions to the problem of maximizing reward over a single binary-choice perceptual decision-making trial include SDT and its dynamical relation, the SPRT. In both algorithms, agents infer the world state to use as a basis for a decision by subjecting a sample of observations to a hypothesis test, where the hypothesis is usually parametric and relates to a generative probabilistic model for the sample. An example of a binary decision task is a signal detection task in which the agent has two hypotheses about the world: the null hypothesis, which says there is no signal present, and the alternative hypothesis, which says a signal is present. The agent may choose the correct hypothesis or make an incorrect choice of the alternative hypothesis when the null hypothesis is true (an error of type I, also known as a false positive), or make an incorrect choice of the null hypothesis when the alternative hypothesis is true (an error of type II, also known as a false positive). In the psychological literature, binary-choice decision making is often modelled using the drift–diffusion model (DDM) ( Balci et al., 2011; Bogacz et al., 2006; Ratcliff, 1978; Simen et al., 2009 ). Although previous work has highlighted the parallels between DDM models and the SPRT, it is possible to view the DDM model itself as eschewing the statistical inference framework that underpins the SPRT and hence to view the decision process as a mechanistic, accumulation-to-threshold process. In the DDM, the weight of evidence in support of one hypothesis over the other follows the trajectory of a particle diffusing with a drift velocity (the magnitude of which depends on the discriminatory quality of the sensory input) to an absorbing boundary ( Gold & Shadlen, 2007; Ratcliff, 1978 ). DDM models successfully explain observed error rates and RT distributions from a great many experimental studies of binary-choice decision tasks over a range of domains (see  Ratcliff & McKoon, 2008; Ratcliff et al., 2016  for comprehensive reviews). They have also been used to explain subject responses in categorization and memory retrieval tasks ( Ratcliff, 1978; Voss et al., 2013 ). It is well recognized that the DDM can be viewed as a continuous-time, special case of the SPRT. By directly showing the equivalence between the SPRT and a discretized version of the DDM, the map between both models’ parameters has been derived ( Bitzer et al., 2014 ).

In both SPRT and DDM, optimal behaviour depends crucially on using the optimal threshold values for the evidence. The optimal thresholds in the DDM have been shown to satisfy a transcendental equation that is therefore solved numerically ( Bogacz et al., 2006 ). However, the derivation of the equivalent transcendental equation for optimal thresholds in the SPRT relies on Wald’s approximations for the thresholds, which can break down when there are higher rates of type I and type II errors ( Edwards, 1965 ). Equal costs for both type I and type II errors are often assumed: an assumption that is not always ecologically valid. Consider for example the drastically different costs associated with an animal making a false positive or a false negative when looking out for predators. In this example, the costs of the type I and II errors (becoming startled for no reason versus possible death) are highly asymmetric. One proposal for how animals could deal with this problem is that thalamus and cortex combine to implement interacting SDT (fast-but-inaccurate) and SPRT (slow-but-accurate) models for predator avoidance ( Trimmer et al., 2008 ). Asymmetric error costs have also been investigated in the context of reward-rate maximization where direct simulation revealed an empirical relationship between the error costs and evidence start-point in the DDM ( Bogacz et al., 2006 ). Recent work that integrates reinforcement learning with diffusion decision-models has shown that adaptation of DDM parameters during learning can account for improvements in task performance over time even if the resulting behaviour is suboptimal ( Fontanesi et al., 2019; Miletić et al., 2020; Pedersen et al., 2017 ).

In their original forms, the SPRT and the DDM incorrectly predict RT distributions under certain conditions and so have been modified over the years to fit the empirical RT data. For instance, experimental studies have shown that when accuracy is stressed in task instructions, error responses are typically slower than correct responses, and when speed is stressed in instructions, error responses are typically faster than correct responses ( Luce, 1986; Swensson, 1972 ). Standard diffusion decision models cannot explain these results because if thresholds are assumed to be symmetrical and the drift rate is held constant across trials, then the DDM predicts equal mean RTs for both error and correct responses — an outcome which is almost never observed empirically ( Ratcliff et al., 2016 ). However, disparities between correct and error RTs can be recovered in diffusion models by allowing inter-trial variability in both the signal strength, which accounts for slow errors, and the initial evidence, which accounts for fast errors ( Laming, 1968; Ratcliff, 1978, 1981; Ratcliff et al., 1999 ). Some authors have commented that allowing this degree of flexibility in DDM models means they can explain any pattern of data, thereby rendering them unfalsifiable ( Jones & Dzhafarov, 2014 ), but these arguments apply only to a more general class of models that allow arbitrary parameter distributions across trials ( Heathcote et al., 2014 ). In reality, inter-trail variability in the DDM is strictly constrained to specific distributions that have been applied consistently in modelling studies ( Smith et al., 2014 ).

A recent development in perceptual decision-making research is the recognition that in the repeated trial version of the computational task, the optimal decision threshold that maximizes the reward rate is non-linear and time-varying rather than constant. In this case, dynamic programming techniques are used to derive the optimal decision boundaries, and the precise nature of the boundary time-dependency is related to experimental factors such as inter-trial delay times or time penalties for errors ( Drugowitsch & Pouget, 2012; Malhotra et al., 2018; Tajima et al., 2019 ). It has been pointed out, however, that optimality in these cases depends on a linear costing of time; in the more ecologically valid case where rewards are time discounted, DDM-like models with collapsing boundaries are not optimal ( Marshall, 2019; Steverson et al., 2019 ). For clarity in introducing the key concepts for perceptual decision making, this paper focuses only on the computational problem of maximizing reward on a single trial, for which it is known that the optimal boundary is constant in time.

Analysis of the physical implementation of the SDT and SPRT algorithms can be broken down so that we consider two interacting systems: the sensory evidence accumulation system, and the action-selection system. A canonical neural theory of sensory evidence accumulation says that evidence is represented in the firing rates of a neuron–antineuron pair that responds selectively to the two possible states in a binary-choice task ( Gold & Shadlen, 2001, 2002 ), specifically in the difference of the neuron–antineuron pair’s instantaneous firing rates, as observed in electrophysiological recordings of single neurons in both primates and rodents during binary-choice perceptual decision-making experiments ( Hanks & Summerfield, 2017 ). For the implementation of action selection, evidence for the precise mechanism underlying its neural implementation remains obscure — although there is a consensus that the basal ganglia plays a role. Indeed there have been several proposals for how a neural circuit might implement such a scheme ( Bogacz & Gurney, 2007; Kropotov & Etlinger, 1999; Lepora & Gurney, 2012; Redgrave et al., 1999 ). Also currently unclear, however, is how the brain would represent and learn the mapping from the sensory evidence to the optimal action, and how actions could remain optimal under changed environmental context ( Lepora & Gurney, 2012; Simen et al., 2006 ).

To obtain a measure of optimality for decision making, we have to understand the agent’s goal or objective. For instance, one objective might be to simply minimize errors by choosing the maximum  a posteriori  probability hypothesis, whereas another objective might be to choose the hypothesis that minimizes the expected decision cost. In this paper, we assume that the optimality criterion is minimization of the expected decision cost per single trial.

Agents can change the relative occurrence of type I and II errors in their decisions by shifting their threshold values. By understanding the costs of type I and II errors, agents can determine the optimal threshold, which is defined as the threshold that minimizes the expected overall cost of the decision.

We used two approaches to produce the necessary datasets for the GP models: the first was to use analytic approximations that relate the thresholds to the expected error rates and expected number of time steps (see  Appendix D.1.1  for expressions); the second was to use a large number of simulated SPRT trials over a range of threshold values (see  Appendix D.1.2  for simulation details).

Comparing the two plots in  Fig. 6  derived from analytic and simulated datasets, there are discrepancies between the ROC curves arrived at using the two different methods. This is because the approximate relationship between the thresholds and the error rates, based on Wald’s inequalities (Eq.  (D.1) ), break down in some regions of ROC space due to the discrete nature of the SPRT observations. Because the evidence arrives in discrete chunks, the evidence is unlikely to be equal to the threshold value when the decision is made; most often, the evidence overshoots the threshold. The smaller the threshold value, the more likely it is that the evidence overshoots the decision threshold by a larger margin, and the larger this overshoot, the less accurate Wald’s approximation relationships become. Smaller thresholds mean faster decisions but less accuracy and so when there are high error rates, Wald’s approximate relationships break down.

However,  Wald (1947)  made clear that his threshold approximations did not represent the relationships between the thresholds and error rates  per se  but that setting the thresholds to the approximate values in Eq.  (D.2)  would not, in practice, harm the efficiency or accuracy of the SPRT, provided the sampling cost is not very high. Given our introductory comments in this paper, let us look again at perceptual decision making using Marr’s three levels of analysis. The computational task is defined as a signal discrimination task between two equal-width Gaussian distributions. The optimal algorithmic solutions to this task are  
                            n 
                         SDT and SPRT, which have mechanistic implementations that could plausibly be carried out by biological neural networks. The question of how the brain represents and deals with uncertainty has lead to two competing proposals in the field: a population probability coding (PPC) framework and a sampling coding framework ( Berkes et al., 2011; Drugowitsch & Pouget, 2012; Lochmann & Deneve, 2011; Ma et al., 2006 ). The results reviewed in this paper complement the view that optimal Bayesian inference does not necessarily require the brain to make calculations using likelihood functions, but instead to sample from the underlying probability distributions ( Griffiths, Vul et al., 2012; Haefner et al., 2016; Sanborn & Chater, 2016 ).

For instance, in the equal-width Gaussian signal-discrimination task, the Gaussian generative model is approximated by sampling from the underlying distribution. Then the difficulties of combining multiple observations in a Bayesian framework in the brain are avoided due to the additive properties of Gaussian-distributed sensory evidence. This is consistent with a long-standing proposal for models of binary-choice perceptual decision making in which sensory evidence is represented by the instantaneous firing rates of a neuron–antineuron pair that respond selectively to the two states under consideration ( Gold & Shadlen, 2002 ). The weight of evidence is calculated by taking the difference in the response of the two neurons, and simply accumulates over time with some scaling. The scaling factor is dependent on the underlying response-distribution parameters and could be acquired and incorporated into the SPRT decision thresholds.

In animals, perceptual decisions manifest as actions. The ideas reviewed in this paper take the view that decision-making systems (action-selection systems) weight the available evidence according to context and initiate actions. One limitation of this perspective is that decisions and actions follow serially: action performance does not influence decisions. This serial model is successful in explaining experimental data from constrained experimental tasks; however, there are a family of models for more ecologically-valid tasks where actions dynamically influence the decision process in a closed loop — this is known as the embodied choice framework ( Lepora & Pezzulo, 2015 ). That said, in both serial and closed-loop decision models, actions result in feedback from the environment, which allows trial-and-error learning of the optimal thresholds.

One biological implementation of such a scheme could involve encoding the thresholds as weights that act as a gain term on the collected evidence. It has previously been proposed that priors could be encoded in network weights ( Ganguli & Simoncelli, 2010; Wei & Stocker, 2012 ). We suggest that these same weights could also be used to encode error and sampling costs. That said, one disadvantage would then be that synaptic weights may not be able to reflect the dynamic changes in relevant contextual information, since it is reasonable to assume that context changes would take place over a shorter characteristic time-scale than that of synaptic plasticity. A work-around for this problem might be for contextual information to be integrated into decision making via neuromodulation of the sensory evidence gain-term ( Cohen & Newsome, 2008; Haefner et al., 2016 ). A reasonable candidate for this gain modulation is dopamine, which is known to encode motivational states and reduce the threshold on action initiation ( Collins & Frank, 2014; Salamone & Correa, 2012 ).
