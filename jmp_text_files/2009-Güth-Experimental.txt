Optimal saving plans were first analyzed by abstracting from the uncertainty of one’s future. Models of life cycle ( Ando & Modigliani, 1963 ) and permanent income ( Friedman, 1957 ) provide a description of optimal behavior when income is deterministic. Due to decreasing marginal utility, allocation of resources to consumption in each period follows a homogenous pattern. Later on, life cycle-permanent income models were extended to account for uncertainty in income ( Hall, 1978 ). When income is stochastic and agents fully and perfectly exploit all available information, consumption follows a random walk with trend. In recent years, the literature on intertemporal allocation of wealth has been further developed in the direction of dynamic optimization (see, e.g.,  Adang & Melenberg, 1995; Pemberton, 1993, 1997 ).

Such optimization exercises, though in principle very appealing, rarely provide practical advice. The decisive reason is that agents do not have well-behaved intertemporal preferences ( Frederick, Loewenstein, & O’Donoghue, 2002 ), nor given probabilities for the possible developments of their life experiences. Moreover, individuals usually do not engage in the often very complex task of intertemporal optimization. Up to now the empirical research has mainly tested qualitative and quantitative aspects of optimal saving.

Unlike the previous studies, we deal with intertemporal decision making in a stochastic environment by drawing on the satisficing approach ( Simon, 1955 ). The basic idea of Simon’s theory of bounded rationality is that people form aspiration levels on goal variables (aspiration formation) and search for alternatives that guarantee these aspirations (satisficing). Depending on how successful this search turns out, an agent decides whether to continue her search or to adapt her aspirations.

Usually, satisficing relies on forming different aspiration levels for different basic goals. When, for instance, searching for an apartment, the decision maker may specify an aspiration for the rent she is willing to pay, an aspiration for the number of rooms she wants, and so on. In saving decisions, the decision maker typically concentrates on one main goal: guaranteeing herself sufficient monetary means for consumption over the years she may experience. We assume that the decision maker applies her aspirations to different scenarios of possible life experiences capturing the uncertainty of her future. More specifically, we suppose that the decision maker forms scenario-specific aspiration levels specifying the financial means she wants to have available for consumption purposes in each scenario, and then plans her consumption stream considering her possible life-time resources and how long she might live. If the selected consumption plan allows the decision maker to meet her aspiration levels in each possible scenario, then we say that the decision maker’s aspiration profile is  consistent  and that her consumption plan is  satisficing . Thus, our specification of satisficing accounts for the uncertainty of one’s own future, but it does not require probabilistic reasoning.

The main purpose of this paper is to test experimentally (i) whether agents comply with our notion of satisficing requiring them to form consistent aspiration profiles, and (ii) how satisficing choices relate to optimal consumption choices as predicted by the classical expected-value approach based on risk neutrality. Although the theoretical literature on satisficing is by now voluminous, only recently have experimental economists started investigating satisficing behavior in the laboratory. Following the tradition of revealed preference analysis, some of these experiments try to detect aspiration levels and their adjustment by statistically analyzing search data (see, e.g.,  Zwick, Rapoport, Lo, & Muthukrishnan, 2003 ). Another branch of experimental work renders the satisficing approach applicable by directly eliciting aspiration levels (see  Güth, 2007 , and the references therein).

Here, we pursue this latter path. We consider an intertemporal allocation problem with uncertainty in both length of life and future income. More specifically, the length of life can be either two or three periods, and the second-period income can be either low or high. Hence, each participant faces four different scenarios. We allow for “reincarnation” in the sense that each participant experiences several “lives” or rounds. To address the two research questions (i) and (ii) mentioned above, in every round we ask participants to form four scenario-specific aspiration levels before choosing a consumption vector.

In our experimental setting, any participant wishing her aspiration profile to be consistent needs to solve a system of four non-linear inequalities. Yet most of the participants may be either unable or unwilling to perform such cumbersome computations, even if they desire that their aspirations are always met. Hence, in order to investigate whether non-satisficing decision makers actually aim at a satisficing consumption plan, we introduce a software-aided satisficing routine. In every round, after aspiration levels have been formed and consumption choices have been made, a so-called decision aid (a) checks whether the aspiration profile of each participant is consistent and (b) informs each participant about the consistency of her aspirations. Participants are given the possibility of revising their aspiration levels and/or consumption choices up to five times in each round. After each revision they receive feedback informing them whether their consumption plan is satisficing or not. This design feature is used to explore if the knowledge of a non-satisficing behavior leads to revisions, and whenever revisions occur, what is most likely to be revised (aspiration levels or consumption choices).

Our only experimental treatment variable concerns how aspiration levels are elicited: either as “cheap-talk” data or as incentivized decisions. In the latter case, for the randomly selected scenario, a participant earns her aspiration when this does not exceed her actual monetary payoff; otherwise, she earns nothing. Our method of incentivizing aspirations should induce agents to set aspirations equal to the intertemporal payoffs implied by the chosen consumption plan, and therefore to comply with satisficing. Moreover, our payment rule matches the interpretation of aspirations in the satisficing approach: one is satisfied if aspirations are met (the realized payoff is greater than the aspired one), while one is unsatisfied if aspirations are not met (the realized payoff is smaller than the aspired one). An important result of our treatment with incentivized aspirations is that participants learn to form consistent aspiration profiles, but not optimal ones, i.e., even after becoming more experienced, participants remain only boundedly rational.

To investigate whether decision makers abide by our notion of satisficing, entailing compliance with inequalities  (2)–(5) , in every round we asked participants to specify the payoff that they aimed to achieve in each scenario. Aspiration levels had to be supplied before choosing a consumption vector. Some participants may be either unable or reluctant to examine the above four non-linear inequalities even if they wish to make consumption choices that guarantee their aspirations. To explore if “non-satisficers” actually yearn for satisficing decisions, we provided each participant with feedback about whether or not her consumption vector was satisficing. More specifically, after all participants had formed their aspirations and made their consumption choices, a computer-based decision aid (a) checked whether the aspirations profile of each participant was consistent, and (b) informed each participant about the consistency of her aspirations. In the case that a participant’s consumption plan was non-satisficing, she was informed of which of her aspirations were not met. The possibility to revise was independent of the information feedback on consistency. Revisions could pertain to consumption choices and/or aspirations levels. To reduce the likelihood of noise in the decisions to revise, a maximum of five revisions per round was warranted.

For each of the two treatments, we ran two sessions with 32 participants each, yielding 64 independent observations per treatment. All sessions were run computerized, via z-Tree ( Fischbacher, 2007 ), at the laboratory of the Max Planck Institute in Jena. Participants (all being students from various fields at the University of Jena) were recruited using the ORSEE software ( Greiner, 2004 ). Saving for an uncertain future is probably one of the most difficult economic decision problems. In this paper, we captured the crucial aspects of this problem by allowing for a stochastic future income and an indeterminate length of life. Besides deriving the benchmark case of risk neutral intertemporal optimization, we developed a satisficing approach that postulates the formation of a consistent aspiration profile, i.e., of scenario-specific aspirations not exceeding the payoffs implied by the chosen consumption vector.

To check experimentally whether people satisfice and how satisficing choices relate to optimality, in every round, before choosing their consumption vector, participants had to specify the payoffs they aimed to achieve in each of the possible scenarios. A software-aided satisficing routine then informed participants whether their consumption vector was satisficing or not (i.e., whether it guaranteed all their aspirations), and gave them the possibility of revising any aspect of their decision in case of non-satisficing. To investigate whether incentivizing aspirations affects behavior, we have considered two treatments that differed only in whether participants had monetary incentives for meaningful reporting aspirations or not.

Our results reveal that, whatever their incentives, subjects save, on average, too much initially and too little afterwards as compared to risk neutral optimality. Moreover, aspirations do not fully exploit payoff potential: under both treatments, elicited aspirations are significantly smaller than allowed by the theoretical benchmark solution for the long life scenarios, and the reverse is true in case of short lives. However, aspirations are closer to optimal values when there exist monetary incentives for compliance with satisficing. An analysis of the time subjects spend forming aspiration profiles reveals that subjects think more thoroughly about the problem if aspirations are incentivized than if they are just cheap-talk.

The data demonstrate that, in the last experimental round, nearly 100% of the participants in the treatment with incentivized aspirations implement a satisficing consumption plan. The respective percentage in the treatment with cheap-talk aspirations is less than 60%. The increase in consistent aspiration profiles over rounds illustrates that satisficing (as the core concept of bounded rationality theory) is not always readily applied, but may have to be “learnt”.

In terms then of general messages, we can confirm that optimal intertemporal decision behavior is difficult to derive. “Learning” to guarantee oneself an aspired consumption plan seems to be easier and more likely when aspirations are incentivized.
