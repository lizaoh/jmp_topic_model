Using information revealed through individuals’ choices when making social evaluations, rather than information reported by them, has well-known advantages. Most notably, revealed preference information is much less costly to acquire and much less sensitive to manipulations. Thus, most of the social choice literature considers only revealed preference information. Unfortunately, this information is often insufficient for constructing a social rule that is both decisive and normatively appealing (i.e., satisfies basic desirable axioms).

In contrast, approval voting ( Brams & Fishburn, 1978 ) asks voters to report which alternatives they approve and selects the alternatives that have the greatest support. Approval voting has an appealing axiomatization ( Brams & Fishburn, 2007 ). Moreover,  Barokas (2022)  shows that approval information can also be revealed from certain choices—namely, those made in situations where it is possible to reject all options. For example, approval voting deems two alternatives indifferent when all members of society approve them, even when it is possible that all members strictly prefer one alternative to the other. Similar observations prompted  Brams and Sanver (2009)  to consider both pieces of information for the purpose of social choice. In this setting, they offer two nonaxiomatic voting rules:  preference approval  and  fallback.  While the preference-approval rule prioritizes approval information over preference information and coincides with approval voting whenever the latter is decisive, the fallback rule combines approval information with information on preferences over approved options and completely ignores the ranking of unapproved options. Adopting  Brams and Sanver ’s ( 2009 ) setting,  Barokas and Sprumont (2022)  axiomatically offer the  broken Borda rule , which, similar to the preference-approval voting rule, prioritizes approval information over preferences; additionally,  Barokas (2022)  axiomatically derives a social rule that prioritizes the preferences of unsatisfied agents.

                       The present note takes a different approach, allowing for both preference and approval information, we adopt the position that, when making social choices, revealed preference should remain the prior source of information. Indeed, whenever a  Condorcet winner  (i.e., an option that beats under simple majority all other options) exists, our rule selects it as the unique social choice. Thus, in line with  May ’s ( 1952 ) famous results, when there are only two options (and an odd number of agents), revealed preference information alone determines the social outcome.

Second, we impose a consistency axiom, which relates social choices with and without approval information and requires that whenever the social choices at two preference profiles coincide in the absence of approval information, they should also coincide when coupled with the same approval information. Together with suitable versions of the familiar neutrality and positive responsiveness axioms, we derive the  majority-approval  rule, which coincides with the simple majority rule when the latter is decisive (i.e., contains no top cycles) and otherwise coincides with approval voting ( Brams & Fishburn, 1978 ) defined on the top cycle set (Theorem 2). We compare our rule to other social choice rules that prioritize preference information over approval information, and thus demonstrate its appealing properties. Additionally, we axiomatically derive a version of our theorem that is consistent with the Pareto criterion.

Theorem 1  implies that unlike PT, the other rationality conditions are consistent with the CP. Thus, together with  Proposition 1 ,  Theorem 1  provides a strong justification for the top cycle rule as the “most rational” rule that is consistent with the CP. We note that this motivation is missing from the other axiomatizations of the top cycle rule (e.g.,  Ehlers & Sprumont, 2008; Houy, 2011 ).

However, the top cycle rule produces many ties (or multiple choices); this, as  Dasgupta and Maskin (2008, p. 958)  explained, is unsatisfactory in many social choice contexts. 
                         8 
                      
                      
                         8 
                         In line with this observation,  Dasgupta and Maskin (2008)  offered a  decisiveness  axiom, which is violated by the top cycle rule. 
                       Until recently, these would be taken as a demonstration that using only information that can be revealed from choice behavior and maintaining all rationality requirements other than PT results in an indecisive social choice rule. However,  Barokas (2022)  recently showed that approval information can also be revealed from choice behavior via non-forced choice situations (i.e., situations in which rejecting the entire opportunity set is possible), provided that they satisfy certain regularities. 
                         9 
                      
                      
                         9 
                         Specifically, to reveal both preferences and approval information, choices must satisfy both the standard rationality axiom (generally known as WARP), and an additional axiom (WARD), which requires consistency with respect to the rejection of all alternatives.  Barokas (2022)  provides experimental evidence showing that WARD is satisfied at least as often as WARP. 
                       Thus,  Theorem 1  strongly justifies the use of approval information as a refinement of the top cycle choice rule.
