Response time is perhaps the most important measure used to investigate hypotheses about mental processes in experimental psychology, going back to the pioneering work of Franciscus Donders in the nineteenth century (for reviews, see  Jensen, 2006 ;  Townsend & Ashby, 1983 ;  Luce, 1986 ;  Van Zandt, 2002 ). From a data-analytic perspective, this predominance has raised important challenges: For instance, response time distributions are positively skewed, with longer tails on the right sight of the probability density function than on the left side. Moreover, reaction-time means and variances are often found to be linearly related  (Wagenmakers & Brown, 2007) . These two features alone are enough to see that response times do not mesh well with the general class of statistical linear models traditionally used to analyze data. As a response to this challenge, several approaches have been developed, which can be roughly divided into two research strands: One has focused on fitting response-time data to a suitable parametric distribution (e.g., the ex-Gaussian distribution;  Matzke & Wagenmakers, 2009 ) in order to provide economical summaries of the data in terms of a few parameters (e.g.,  Schmiedek, Oberauer, Ol, Sü, & Wittmann, 2007 ; for an overview, see  Balota & Yap, 2011 ). The second research strand, which is quite active to this date, instead focuses on the development of mathematical models as psychological accounts for the data in terms of specific mental processes that unfold across time (e.g.,  Brown & Heathcote, 2008 ;  Ratcliff & Rouder, 1998 ;  Townsend & Nozawa, 1995 ; for reviews, see  Townsend & Ashby, 1983 ;  Luce, 1986 ;  Schweickert, Fisher, & Sung, 2012 ;  Van Zandt, 2002 ).

Beyond response times, another widely applicable tool for the study of mental processes is given by the class of  multinomial-processing tree  models (MPT models;  Riefer & Batchelder, 1988 ). MPT models characterize categorical (frequency) data in a given paradigm by postulating a finite set of latent states. For each item type, the observed responses are the outcome of a mixture of the different latent states and associated state-to-response mappings. The probability of each state being reached is generated from a  processing tree , the edges of which represent the outcomes of different processes. MPT models are usually tailored to a particular experimental paradigm, with trees specifying the most important processes believed to be involved in the generation of responses. The family of process-dissociation models  (Jacoby, 1991)  used in many lines of psychological research  (Klauer, Dittrich, Scholtes, & Voss, 2015)  is one prominent member of the MPT model class among many others.

Based on participants’ recognition judgments, we can estimate the 2HT model’s parameters and test some of its properties (e.g.,  Dube & Rotello, 2012 ;  Kellen & Klauer, 2014, 2015 ;  Kellen, Singmann, Vogt, & Klauer, 2015 ;  Province & Rouder, 2012 ).

Although not as ubiquitous as response-time analyses, MPT models such as the 2HT model have been found useful in an enormous range of psychological inquiries (for reviews, see  Batchelder & Riefer, 1999 ;  Erdfelder et al., 2009 ;  Hütter & Klauer, 2016 ). In addition to the ongoing stream of proposals for new substantive models (e.g.,  Gawronski, Conway, Armstrong, Friesdorf, & Hütter, 2016 ;  Meissner & Rothermund, 2013 ), the current fruitfulness of this model class is attested by its growing methodological toolbox: From hierarchical and mixed-model extensions  (Klauer, 2010; Matzke, Dolan, Batchelder, & Wagenmakers, 2015) , to sophisticated model-selection indices  (Klauer & Kellen, 2015; Wu, Myung, & Batchelder, 2010) , and inequality-constraint applications  (Klauer, Singmann, & Kellen, 2015) .

But as useful as this model class may be, there are limits to what one can achieve on the basis of response frequencies alone. For instance, the characterization of the observed responses as a function of a mixture of latent states is ultimately silent about the duration of each of the processes that govern the access to these states, as well as about their exact order. Let us go back to the 2HT example: The tree structure of the model illustrated in  Fig. 1  suggests that the guessing responses occur after a failed attempt to recognize the test item. We can refer to this as the “ detect-guess ” variant of the 2HT. Alternatively, we can conceive 2HT models in which guessing occurs  prior  to any attempt to recognize the test item. For example, according to a “ default-interventionist ” variant of the 2HT shown in  Fig. 2 , the participant first guesses whether the test item is old or new.

                   Only after this process is completed does the participant engage in a memory-retrieval process that – if successful – takes precedence over the previously established guess. Although the original detect-guess and the default-interventionist variants of the 2HT are very distinct in terms of mental-processing assumptions, they are formally equivalent in the sense that they yield the exact same range of predictions with the same parameters and parameter values.

In order to test alternative tree structures and estimate the duration of the different processes they postulate, we need to extend the MPT model so that it can provide a joint characterization of response frequencies and their respective times. We present a framework within which different distributional assumptions can be explored and fully elaborate one model in this framework. The method can be applied to data from any identified MPT model if response times have been recorded along with the responses. It even extends the class of models that can be investigated because models that are not mathematically identified based on only the response frequencies can become so by the inclusion of the response times as illustrated below.

In line with modern developments, the new method is hierarchical, which is advantageous in two different ways: First, it enables models to accommodate the substantial heterogeneity that usually stems from individual differences. These differences can be expressed in many ways such as accuracy, response bias, response speed, and speed-accuracy trade-offs, among others. Second, each participant’s parameter estimates are thereby informed by the other participants’ estimates, which is particularly valuable when the data per individual are sparse, and group-level estimates generalize across them  (Katahira, 2016; Klauer, 2010) . In terms of substantive psychological applications, the proposed method can provide us with deeper insights into the architecture of processes in the modeled tasks, enabling the testing of theoretical predictions regarding the temporal characteristics of the involved processes, and the development of more diagnostic measurement models. In light of these contributions, the proposed method can be cast as a principled alternative to the currently dominant paradigm of simple diffusion-model accounts.

Relatively direct precursors of the present approach are mixture models postulating probabilistic mixtures of response-time distributions (e.g.,  Falmagne, 1965 ;  Ollman, 1966 ;  Yantis, Meyer, & Smith, 1991 ). Mixture models are naturally related to MPT models if each processing-tree path terminating in an observable response category is considered as giving rise to a distinct path-specific response-time distribution. Another important precursor is the study of complex cognitive architectures that followed the seminal work of  Sternberg (1969) . For instance,  Schweickert (1978)  showed how the selective influencing of processes could be used to gain insights into their arrangement (e.g., are two processes serial or concurrent?) along an acyclical network (see also  Dzhafarov and Schweickert, 1995 ;  Goldstein & Fisher, 1991 ;  Townsend & Nozawa, 1995 ; for a review, see  Schweickert et al., 2012 ).

Hu (2001)  applied some of these earlier ideas in the context of the MPT model class, noting that any tree structure belonging to this class is a special type of acyclical network. Specifically, Hu investigated under what conditions it is possible to decompose mean response latency for each response category into mixtures of different path-specific means, which are further decomposed into additive components for each edge along the path, each such component describing the mean completion time of the process (and process outcome) associated with it. The inclusion of response times under this framework enabled Hu to compare two variants of the famous high-threshold source-memory model  (Batchelder & Riefer, 1990)  that postulate distinct relationships between the retrieval of item and source memory. Among the limitations of Hu’s approach is the fact that it only operates at the level of mean response times, ignoring higher-order moments of the response-time distributions. In addition, the method does not develop tools for statistical inference. Finally, it assumes that process-completion times and the probability of a process succeeding are independent, a highly implausible assumption.

An approach based on mixture models was recently developed by  Heck and Erdfelder (2016) . It is based on setting up a number of response-latency bins: For example, when using two bins the responses might be split into fast and slow responses based on the overall median. A new branching is then added at the end of each path of the MPT model, distributing the responses generated along that path into the different bins. The present approach pursues an alternative but complementary route by imposing specific parametric assumptions on the processes along with a serial interpretation of the processing paths as describing a succession or at least a cascade of processing steps  (McClelland, 1979) . In this latter respect, the present approach builds on the one by  Hu (2001)  and follows the intuition of many modelers in formulating MPT models. The downside of imposing parametric assumptions and a serial or cascade interpretation of processing paths is that the resulting method cannot be expected to be useful in situations where there are substantive deviations from such assumptions. More generally speaking, parametric response-time models such as the diffusion model  (Ratcliff & Rouder, 1998)  are most interesting where they provide a good description of observed data. Such an outcome implies that the model and its ensemble of assumptions provide one viable theoretical account of the data. A failure to describe given data is less interesting, inasmuch as it is often difficult to diagnose whether the failure goes back to a violation of central structural assumptions (e.g., the idea of a diffusion process or a particular MPT model architecture) or to a violation of ancillary parametric assumptions (e.g., the assumption of normally distributed residual encoding and response-execution times

As will be shown below, the approach proposed here provides complete descriptions of the observed joint distribution of responses and response times, including accounts of the differences between individuals, and the correlations between the parameters governing responses and response times related to these individual differences. The methods presented here can be applied to any identifiable MPT model as well as to many MPT models that are not identified based on only the response frequencies without imposing further restrictions.

In its simplest form, the present method builds on binary multinomial processing trees (only two branches go out from each node) in which each processing path represents a succession of processing stages for which process-completion times add up  (Sternberg & Backus, 2015) . An additional additive component summarizes encoding and response-execution times. The process-completion times of different processes are assumed to be independently distributed (i.e., conditional independence is assumed), and process-completion time distributions leading to the same outcome of the same process are assumed to be identical wherever that process occurs in the processing tree.

The resulting models provide process-oriented accounts of the joint distribution of response categories and response times.

MPT models usually consist of several subtrees. For example, in the most simple 2HT model, there are two subtrees, one for trials involving studied items, and one for trials involving new items. Each tree has internal nodes, referred to simply as nodes in the following, and leaves. The leaves correspond to observable response categories such as “old” or “new”. Categories for different subtrees are, however, considered different categories. For example, the category “old” is also indexed by the subtree from which it stems and thus, to be precise, there are four response categories in the most simple 2HT model. The categories are mapped on actual responses such as left or right keypresses so that responses are a function of categories. For example, the two “old” response categories may be mapped on the left key, and the two “new” response categories on the right key. We consider only binary MPT models in which each node has two children. But note that non-binary MPT models can be transformed into binary MPT models (  Hu & Batchelder, 1994 ; see  Appendix  for more details).

How the observed reaction times are carved up into process-completion times and response-execution components depends in part on the distributional assumptions. In this respect, RT-MPTs are no different from other process-oriented models of reaction times such as the diffusion model  (Heathcote, Wagenmakers, & Brown, 2014; Jones & Dzhafarov, 2014) . The issue is somewhat mitigated by the fact that the same process-completion components and response-execution components appear in different combinations in different processing paths of the model, imposing structural constraints on these components and associated parameters. Nevertheless, due to such issues, estimation of the model should be accompanied by assessments of model fit as well as by selective-influence studies. In selective-influence studies manipulations are implemented that are believed to affect only one process on theoretical grounds. The model is thereby validated not only in terms of fit, but also in terms of whether or not it maps the effects of the manipulation on only this process  (Heathcote, Brown, & Wagnemakers, 2015; Klauer, Stahl, & Voss, 2012) . Success in these validation steps will increase one’s confidence in the assumption that the parameter estimates are not unduly biased by inappropriate distributional assumptions. We illustrate both validation steps in the application below.

The superior fit of the DI variant of the 2HT model only serves as an example of the feasibility of the RT-MPT approach, not as a direct comparison against the diffusion model. One confounding factor is the difference in the number of parameters between the two models. The DI model requires more parameters per person than the diffusion model: the former needs to capture the outcomes and completion times of three separate two-outcomes processes (2 detection processes, one guessing process), whereas the latter model needs to characterize the outcomes and completion times of only one such process (the diffusion process). For a basic recognition-memory paradigm, the 2HT models used here require, per process, three parameters (one parameter governing accuracy, two parameters governing completion times), and three parameters to model response-execution times (two for the means of old and new responses and one for the variance), for a total of twelve parameters per person. The diffusion model uses only seven parameters in this situation (although the model could be extended to include additional components such as  drift criterion ; see  Starns, Ratcliff, & White, 2012 ). A formal comparison of the fits of the 2HT model and the diffusion model should therefore take differences in flexibility into account via an adequate quantification of model parsimony (e.g.,  Vandekerckhove, Matzke, & Wagenmakers, 2015 ).

The modeling tools currently available in researchers’ toolboxes constitute a major source of constraint for the type of experimental paradigms that are ultimately adopted. In the case of response-time modeling, the need for a large number of observations has been relaxed due to recent advances in hierarchical Bayesian methods, making them available to a large number of applications (e.g.,  Rouder, Province, Morey, Gomez, & Heathcote, 2015 ;  Vandekerckhove, Tuerlinckx, & Lee, 2011 ). However, most response-time models like the prominent diffusion model  (Ratcliff & Rouder, 1998)  are restricted to two-choice paradigms (one exception being the family of linear-ballistic accumulator models;  Brown & Heathcote, 2008 ), which limits their overall usefulness. No such limitations exist in the case of MPT models.

The present work aims to enrich the current toolbox by proposing a method for combining two long-standing modeling traditions that are typically seen as somewhat disjoint — process-oriented response-time modeling and multinomial-processing-tree modeling. As traditionally assumed in MPT models, the probability of observing a certain response corresponds to a mixture of different processing paths. We propose that the latencies associated with a given response can be captured by ascribing a completion-time distribution to each process outcome included in the different paths that lead to that response, in addition to encoding and response-execution times. Because our proposed method can be applied to any existing member of the MPT model class, it imposes no a priori constraints on the type of MPT paradigm that one can consider, as long as response times can be reliably recorded. In fact, the inclusion of response time might even lead to less constrained model accounts, as parameters that were not identified can become so.

As an application example, we extended two variants of the well-known 2HT model and tested their ability to capture recognition-memory data across a range of experimental manipulations. Interestingly, we found that a “default-interventionist” variant of the 2HT, in which the guessing process precedes the attempts to retrieve the item from memory, provided the best account of the datasets considered here. This tree structure deviates from the way the 2HT is typically conceptualized, namely in terms of the “detect-guess” variant. However, it should be emphasized that the two variants are indistinguishable on the basis of the typical categorical data collected and analyzed in recognition-memory experiments. The conceptualization of the model in terms of the “detect-guess” variant has therefore been a convention based on tacit and previously untestable assumptions about processing order. Future work is required to determine whether the processing-tree structure found to be most adequate here depends on the characteristics of the experimental design, a likely possibility in any complex faculty such as human memory (e.g.,  Humphreys, Bain, & Pike, 1989 ;  Meyer & Kieras, 1997 ). One indication in this direction may be the finding that the traditional “detect-guess” variant outperformed the “default-interventionist” variant in one of the analyzed datasets in which instructions emphasized either speed or accuracy. In any case, the present results demonstrate that by incorporating response times, one can overcome a long-standing inability to distinguish between different processing tree structures (e.g.,  Kellen & Singmann, 2017 ;  Kellen, Singmann, & Klauer, 2014 ).

The applications also illustrate two additional advantages of incorporating response times in the present framework: First, models that are not identifiable on the basis of only categorical responses will typically be identified when response times are included via assumptions about process-completion distributions and distributions of encoding and response-execution components. For example, in the above applications, models with different detection parameters

Second, the present method provides a principled alternative to the currently dominant diffusion-model analyses (e.g.,  Matzke & Wagenmakers, 2009 ;  Voss, Voss, & Lerche, 2015 ), at least in the many cases in which theory-driven and validated MPT models have been formulated for the accuracy data in the experimental paradigm under scrutiny. In such cases, RT-MPT models establish a principled competitor for process-oriented accounting of the data. RT-MPT models build on the MPT models that were successful in describing the accuracy data, now addressing the same range of data at the same level of detail as the diffusion-model analyses of them as illustrated here for recognition-memory paradigms.

Note, however, that RT-MPT models and diffusion models differ in the breadth and depth of the process accounts they provide. RT-MPT models postulate that several processes are at work and aim at quantifying the different processes’ relative contributions to observed responses and response times, conditional on the constellation of the processes’ interactions as coded in the tree structure. This is especially helpful in modeling paradigms in which multiple processes are likely involved. These processes are, however, not modeled in any more detail beyond what they contribute to the frequencies of responses and the observed response times. In contrast, diffusion models postulate one (diffusion) process, which they model in more depth. For example, the threshold parameter in diffusion models allows one to model speed-accuracy trade-offs in a parsimonious and compact way, and the starting-point parameter captures negative correlations between response frequency and response time in a natural fashion. Options 2 and 3 discussed in the introduction for alternative distributional assumptions may enable one to graft some of this elegance onto response-time extensions of MPT models.

The proposed approach is also likely to be valuable in areas in which the incorporation of response times into existing MPT models represents a major development. One such area of research is  implicit social cognition  – which includes prominent paradigms such as the Implicit Association Test or the Weapon Identification Task – where several MPT models capturing a diverse set of automatic and controlled processes have been proposed (e.g.,  Bishara & Payne, 2009 ;  Meissner & Rothermund, 2013 ; for reviews, see  Hütter & Klauer, 2016 ;  Sherman, Klauer, & Allen, 2010 ). Despite their merits, these models currently ignore response times, a key aspect of participants’ judgments (e.g., decide as quickly as possible whether the object being held by a Black or White person is a gun) and a key aspect in traditional analyses of such data. Incorporating response times into the model analyses would allow the MPT literature on social-cognition paradigms to speak more directly to the vast social-cognition literature relying on response times as the major dependent variable. One important question that response times can help answering concerns the relative temporal sequencing of controlled detection and inhibition processes and their dominance over automatic processes. Another contribution is that the present method, by disentangling process-completion times, allows one to characterize the speed of the different modeled processes and to test theoretical predictions about their different speeds. For example, one defining characteristic of automatic processes such as the activation of response proposals based on stereotypic associations in the Implicit Association Task or the Weapon Identification Task is that they are believed to complete faster than the controlled detection and inhibition processes that are also assumed to operate in these tasks. Efforts to incorporate response times into some of these models from social cognition are ongoing.

Finally, since its inception, the MPT model class has been framed as a way to obtain theoretically motivated and empirically validated decompositions of the major processes at work, which can be useful when characterizing individuals from different populations  (Riefer & Batchelder, 1988) . When used as a  measurement tool , a MPT model can provide important insights, such as the attribution of a given cognitive deficit to differences in a specific parameter (e.g.,  Riefer, Knapp, Batchelder, Bamber, & Manifold, 2002 ). By providing simultaneous descriptions of accuracy and response-time data, the extended RT-MPT models obviously make use of more information than traditional MPT models thereby likely enhancing the usefulness of such models as measurement tools. For example, the precision of the estimation of the traditional MPT parameters governing the categorical data can be expected to increase as a side effect of including the response-time data. As another simple example, the new RT-MPT method can accommodate differences between persons in the relative emphasis an individual puts on accuracy at the expense of speed and vice versa — individual differences to which traditional MPT models, relying on only the accuracy data, are vulnerable. For instance, it would now be possible to diagnose cognitive deficits that are revealed primarily in processing speed rather than processing accuracy and to pinpoint the processes most affected, addressing important issues in, for example, the study of cognitive aging (e.g.,  Kliegl, Mayr, & Krampe, 1994 ).

To conclude, consider just one more area where measurement modeling using the RT-MPT method seems especially promising — the characterization of  workload capacity  conceptualized in terms of processing speed as a function of the number of concurrent stimuli  (Miller, 1982) . The assessment of individual workload capacity can often be challenging due to the need for a considerable number of trials per person, as well as the possibility of contamination by guessing-based responses (for an overview, see  Gondan & Minakata, 2016 ). Alternatively, one could follow up on  Ollman’s (1966)  earlier work and build an RT-MPT that estimates the latencies of the stimulus-dependent and stimulus-independent (i.e., guessing) processes. Such an approach has the advantage of capturing information in the response-time distributions in a small number of parameters, with workload capacity being assessed by comparing process-latency parameters across conditions (see  Eidels, Donkin, Brown, & Heathcote, 2010 ). Moreover, the RT-MPT method is hierarchical, which improves parameter estimation by allowing individual estimates to inform each other, an advantage of critical importance when data are sparse  (Katahira, 2016; Klauer, 2010) .

Reaping these benefits rests on these models fitting the to-be-analyzed data well and on successfully passing a validation program based on selective-influence studies  (Heathcote et al., 2015; Klauer et al., 2012)  as tentatively illustrated here for the response-time extension of the 2HT model. Selective-influence studies implement experimental manipulations believed to affect only one process in the model. The question then is whether each such manipulation will be reflected primarily in the parameters for the targeted process while leaving parameters pertaining to non-manipulated processes unaffected. As already mentioned, one limitation of the present development in this context is that like diffusion models, RT-MPTs rely on a set of specific auxiliary assumptions about the distributions of process-completion times and encoding and response-execution times. It is likely that cases exist in which these specific auxiliary assumptions do not even approximately describe the data-generating process, leading to misfit and failure of demonstrating selective influence, even though the core structural and psychological assumptions of the underlying MPT model as such may still be viable. We hope to be able to relax this limitation to some extent through future work in which we aim to develop the model for the alternative sets of distributional assumptions outlined above (see Section  2.1 ).
