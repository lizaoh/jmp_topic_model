This note generalizes and extends his findings by showing that for any  number of alternatives n, there exists a pair of Thurstone models with probability distributions of different types that are  equivalent for complete experiments with n alternatives, but which are not equivalent for complete experiments with n -}- 1  alternatives.  When testing choice theories experimentally it is reassuring to know that there is some experiment which will, at least  theoretically, distinguish one model from another. In the case of Thurstone models (i.e., Thurstone, Case V models with some  distribution F substituted for the normal) this is the case only if one assumes that the corresponding characteristic  functions are nonvanishing. This completes the proof. Several things should be noted about the result. (1) If a pair of nonequivalent Thurstone models is  indistinguishable with n alternatives, then it is indistinguishable with less than n alternatives. (2) For each n, there is a  distinct pair of nonequivalent Thurstone models that is indistinguishable with at most n-alternatives (the pair defined in the  proof above). (3) As a consequence of (1) and (2), for each n there is an infinite number of pairs of nonequivalent Thurstone  models that are indistinguishable with n alternatives, namely, those that are indistinguishable for at most m alternatives for  some m > n.  70 ROCKWELL AND YELLOTT  APPENDIX  Yellott (1977) proved that if two Thurstone models TF and T, are equivalent for complete experiments with n objects (n >, 3)  then the characteristic functions f and g of F and G satisfy Eq.
