Premature Sampling in Random Walks 

Jeffrey N. Rouder 

University of California, Irvine 

Premature sampling is a plausible modification of the random walk that is regarded as sufficient in allowing the sequential probability ratio test (SPRT) model of choice response time to account for the frequent observation that mean error response latency is smaller than that for correct responses. This paper establishes a mathematical framework for random walks with premature sampling. Four important points are made. First, Wald's Identity and the small steps assumption fail to make the problem tractable in its fullest generality. Second, Laming's (1968) derivation of the important result that premature sampling leads to smaller mean latencies for error responses than correct responses in the SPRT model may be flawed by a tenuous approximation. Third, expressions for response probabilities and mean latencies are derived for the general model on the assumption that premature sampling is not sufficient, by itself, to trigger a response, although it does influence the process. Fourth, the SPRT model with premature sampling does not necessarily imply that when conditioned on a response, mean response time for error responses is smaller than that for correct responses��and counterexamples to this claim are provided. ] 1996 Academic Press 

PREMATURE SAMPLING IN RANDOM WALKS 

The sequential probability ratio test, or SPRT, may be one of the most studied models for choice response time (CRT). This random walk model is the optimal solution for any decision maker in the sense that no other information accumulation strategy is faster to reach a decision at fixed error rates (Wald, 1947). Laming (1968) showed SPRT implied that when conditioned on a boundary, the mean error and correct response times are equal. In almost all experiments reported in the literature, these response times are not equal. There are various summaries of the relation between error and correct response times (Link 6 Tindall, 1971; Luce, 1986; Swensson, 1972; Vickers, 1980; Wilding, 1971), and it is generally agreed that when time pressure is placed on the subject, mean error response times are quicker than mean correct response times. 

Laming (1968) acknowledged that SPRT fails to describe the data fully, but suggested premature sampling as a modification of the model that may. He reasoned that subjects could not perfectly time the stimulus presentation and

therefore would be biased to start the random walk process early. The SPRT with premature sampling is a random walk, except the drift during the interval before the stimulus presentation is set to zero. Laming claimed that with premature sampling, mean error response latency will be quicker than that for correct responses. The premature sampling notion received considerable support from the empirical results of Green, Smith, and Von Gierke (1983), who found nearly equal mean response times for error and correct responses when they employed procedural measures designed to suppress premature sampling. 

Motivated by the same empirical results in which mean error response latencies are quicker than mean correct response latencies, Fitts (1966) modified the SPRT random walk by randomly varying the decision boundaries from trial to trial (but not within a trial). According to Fitts, the modification is feasible as subjects adjust boundaries with feedback. The claim that boundary variation produces smaller mean error latencies when conditionedon a boundary is stated (p. 850), but not proved. 

The goal of this paper is to explore the mathematical basis for premature sampling with Wald's Identity. First, it is shown that very little can be done with premature sampling using Wald's Identity in the general case. To proceed, I assume that although premature sampling affects the process, the magnitude of these effects is sufficiently small so as never to trigger a response prior to the signal presentation. The mean response latencies and probabilities of terminating at a particular boundary are then derived. An analysis of Laming's (1968) derivation for the SPRT random walk, which does not invoke the above assumption, shows that it is based on a tenuous approximation. It is possible that Laming's premature sampling conclusion, that mean error response latencies are quicker than that of correct responses when conditioned on a boundary, may be an artifact of this approximation. Finally, the derived latency expressions are applied to the SPRT walk to evaluate Laming's claim.

EXPRESSIONS FOR RESPONSE PROBABILITIES AND LATENCIES 

As discussed above, the approach in this paper is to assume that the sum of samples from the premature distribution never crosses either boundary, and the following equations are based on this assumption. This assumption was at least implicitly employed by Heath (1981) in his derivation of a nonparametric test for a normally distributed, prematurely sampled random walk model and, as mentioned, is implicit in Luce's presentation. 

The ``no anticipation'' assumption is much less a limitation than it may appear if the model is applied only to

premature sampling. Anticipations are a rather rare event in choice response time experiments (anticipations are more common in simple response time experiments). However, the framework cannot be generalized to other nonstationary paradigms such as stimulus masking or stimulus decay in perceptual memory (e.g., Heath's tandem random walk, Heath, 1981). In the current framework, the subject must draw a fixed number of samples from the first distribution. However, if the first distribution is signal related, then it is feasible that the running sum of these samples is sufficient to trigger a response��leading to a random number of steps. Consider the case where the samples from the first distribution provide more stimulus information than from the second, such as in stimulus masking. Then, under conditions where the subject responds correctly often, one expects the responses to occur while the subject is sampling from the first distribution. 

An alternative approach to assuming that anticipations do not occur is to assume that the random walk is well approximated by a diffusion process. Ratcliff (1980, 1988) presents integral equations that can be numerically solved for nonstationary diffusion processes with changing drift rates (although constant diffusion coefficients). The solutions to these diffusion equations are valid even when there are anticipations. As the goals of this paper are to evaluate the relationship between the latencies of error and correct responses, and the diffusion equation analysis does not yield closed-form results, it will not be pursued here.

EXTENSION TO THE CASE OF ANTICIPATIONS 

Although the expressions for latency and response probabilities cannot be extended to the case where anticipatory responses occur, the comparison of error and correct response latencies can. It is shown in the Appendix that when conditioned on a boundary, if mean error response latencies are quicker than mean correct response latencies for a random walk with no anticipations, the same is true for a random walk with anticipations. 

CONCLUSION 

The premature sampling framework derived yields close form expressions for response latencies and probabilities. However, it is less fruitful than hoped. The major drawback is that statements about the relationship of error and correct response latencies in the SPRT walk can be made only with distributional assumptions about the premature steps. Applying the framework to normally distributed premature steps yields symmetry of the signal means as a sufficient condition for quicker mean error responses. Without symmetry, it is possible to derive cases where correct responses are indeed quicker than error responses when conditioned on a boundary. In these cases where correct responses are quicker than error responses on one boundary, the reverse will hold on the opposite boundary. 

The premature sampling framework with no anticipations is formally equivalent to varying the boundaries between trials while preserving the sum A+B. Therefore, the premature sampling framework is only a special case of Fitts' notion of varying boundaries, and clearly his claim is not true in this special case. This refutation, however, cannot be generalized to the case where the boundaries vary independently and do not necessarily preserve the sum A+B. 

Rouder (1996) designed a test for premature sampling based on the framework presented here. He differentiated Eqs. (6a) and (8) with respect to n0 and showed that as premature sampling increases, both unconditional response latency and accuracy decrease. He then used a random foreperiod choice response time design with only slight variability in the foreperiods (less than 500 ms). Rouder reasoned that if subjects did engage in premature sampling, there would be more premature samples accumulated in the later foreperiods than the earlier ones. There was an interesting interaction in his experiments. Responses became substantially quicker and less accurate with foreperiod for

weak (but discriminable) stimuli. However, there was little change in latency and accuracy with moderately intense stimuli. Rouder used these results among others to advance a model in which premature sampling occurs only when the stimuli are weak such that the observer cannot always reliably detect stimulus onset.
