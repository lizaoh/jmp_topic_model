JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 21, 1-29 (1980)  The Secretary Problem as a Model of Choice'  RUTH M. CORBIN2  Carleton University, Ottawa, Ontario, Canada K15 5B6 and Bell Canada  The finite "secretary problem" in dynamic programming generates a set of mathematically optimal criteria for search strategies  in sequential decisions. One of the limitations on its applicability has been its failure to allow for the uncertainty of  whether or not a choice alternative which has been inspected sometime previous to the present, will be "available." In this  paper, extensions of the model are presented which incorporate not only a parameter for the uncertain availability of previous  alternatives, but for the uncertain availability of an alternative currently being observed. The paper emphasizes socalled  heuristic strategies which may be used to approximate optimal solutions, and which entail very reasonable demands on the  processing capacity of the decision-maker. Laboratory realizations and potential applications are discussed.  In many decisions in everyday life, such as buying a car, hiring an employee, or finding an apartment, the alternatives are  inspected sequentially. After each new inspection, the decision-maker may either choose one of the available alternatives, or  may exercise an option to search further before choosing. In exercising this option, he must somehow weigh the time and costs  of further search against its potential advantages.  The modeling of such decisions may be treated by the so-called secretary problem in dynamic programming. While "the secretary  problem" is not the problem's original title, this reference to its anecdotal motivation appears to be its most widely  accepted identification. The first published version known to the author appeared under the name "Googol" in Martin Gardner's  Mathematical Games section of Scientific American (Gardner, 1960a) with a solution outlined by Moser and Pounder (Gardner,  1960b) in a subsequent issue. It has also been identified as the dowry problem, the beauty contest problem (Gilbert and  Mosteller, 1966), the parking spot problem (Chow, Moriguti, Robbins and Samuels, 1971) and even, according to Gianini (pers.  comm.), the streetwalker problem. From its simple sources has grown an impressive body of literature.  The basic form of the secretary problem may be stated as follows: a person wishes to hire a secretary from N possible  applicants, and he hopes to choose the best. He is assumed to have no information about the distribution of "utilities" which  govern the applicants. He interviews secretaries one at a time and in random order, and at each stage he must decide to choose  or reject the current applicant. Once rejected, an applicant will not return. Tables for the optimal t are presented, by Gilbert and Mosteller (1966) for all values of N from 1 to 50, plus selected values  up to 1000. DeGroot (1970) shows that the strategy is obtainable as a particular case of more general results on "optimal  stopping decisions." A more general framework still will be presented below.  There are also variations of the basic model, with established theoretical solutions, which enhance the type of decision  situations to which it might be applied. For example, the inspection of further alternatives in a real-life decision often  entails costs of some kind. The effect of costs on the optimal strategy was investigated by the author (Corbin, 1974) and it  was shown that only the value of t might change with the introduction of costs, but not the general form of the strategy.  Another interesting and realistic variation was considered by Gianini (1974). Arguing that the individual does not always know  in advance how many potential alternatives there might be, she allowed N to be a random variable. Finally, Chow et al. (1964)  generalized the hypothesized goals of the individual: instead of assuming that the individual's intentions were directed only  to obtaining the overall best, they considered the case where alternatives other than the best might offer some modified  satisfaction. That is, they derived the solution for the problem where the utility of an alternative varied inversely with its  rank. Their results were extended by Mucci (1973).  A rather limiting characteristic of all these results is that they have involved decision problems in which only the last  alternative seen is available for choice. Alternatives previously viewed cannot be re-called. This condition, in the anecdote  motivating the secretary problem, is reflected by the assumption that if a secretary is not hired on the spot, he will find a  job elsewhere. In many applications of decision theory, such an assumption would be too strict. In problem-solving tasks, for  example, and individual may continue to generate possible solutions without fear that any of them will lose their availability  (unless he has a poor memory!). In general, the availability of previous alternatives may be viewed as being governed by some  probabilistic mechanism. This paper presents a framework for results on the secretary problem which allows for a parameter of  availability. Although the results are intended to be as general as possible, special cases of intuitive interest are also  presented.  Two of the special cases concern availability functions studied by Yang (1974) and by Smith and Deely (1975). The approach  here is more general than in the previous studies, in that it does not restrict itself to the case where the chooser wishes to  obtain only the best alternative in the population. Secondly, we do not assume that the currently-viewed alternative is always  available for choice. In the secretary problem anecdote, for example, it may be that the applicant currently being interviewed  will decide, after hearing more about the working conditions, that he does not want the job after all.  One of the limiting features of the approach here is that, once the decision-maker attempts to retrieve one or more of the  objects he has seen, he will not undergo further search. For the study of single decisions, this assumption is not too  serious, since the  SECRETARY PROBLEM  process by which the chooser decides on an alternative is independent of the future event of whether he actually obtains it.  However, for a more global theory of behaviour, and certainly from the mathematician's point of view, it would be desirable  not to make this assumption. Yang (1974) presents some specific results for occasions when it need not hold. An obvious topic  for further investigation is whether one can obtain a simultaneous incorporation of Yang's extensions and the ones presented  in this paper.  One last feature of the developments below is noted. For psychological applications, it is of interest to know what kind of  language one needs to express optimal strategies, and whether they can be stated in a way compatible with the processing  capacity of real-life problem-solvers. Throughout the developments, we attempt to identify so-called heuristics for solving at  least part of the decision problem. Thus, whether or not the decision-maker can arrive at a strategy which is optimal for the  whole problem, he may find simple rules for approximating the optimal solution. The section following the mathematical  developments describes an experimental realization of the model, and an application to a longstanding problem in decision  research. We now turn our attention to obtaining precise results regarding when the decision to "stop" as opposed to "continue" is  optimal. It is useful to introduce the following verbal distinction, between an optimal decision at a point in the game  denoted [k, w], and an optimal strategy for the game as a whole:  An optimal decision at [k, w] will refer to a decision between stopping and continuing, given that the player is at stage k  and that the current maximum is w stages previous to the current stage. The decision is made according to whether V*[k, w] =  VS[k, w] or V*[k, w] = Vo[k, w].  An optimal strategy for the game is a statement of what the player would do in every possible circumstance in order to  maximize his expected gain. That is, an optimal strategy is the collection of optimal decisions for every possible pair [k, w]  where w > k. With explicit solutions to the induction equations, we can obtain the optimal strategy, although that strategy  will be stated in quite a tedious form.  Our purpose in these developments is to discover whether there is a simpler way of stating the optimal strategy given certain  restrictions on utility and availability. In order to tacke this problem, it will be helpful to consider it in three stages,  according to the following questions:  (i) Is there a simple way of stating the conditions for the optimal decision at [k, w] ?  (ii) Is there a way of grouping the statements of the optimal decisions at [k, w] for sets of values of k or of w, in order to  list the optimal decisions for all [k, w] in a compact form ?  (iii) Can we summarize our knowledge of the optimal decisions at [k, w] for all k and w into a relatively simple verbal  statement which describes the optimal strategy for the game ?  Our main interest is clearly in the last of these three questions, but the first two are posed for the following reason. An  answer to (iii) may not be known, or may only be  480/21/1-2  14 RUTH M. CORBIN  SUMMARY, EXTENSIONS AND APPLICATIONS  This paper has presented a generalized structure for the finite secretary problem in dynamic programming, which specifies  optimal criteria for search strategies in sequential decisions.  It was shown that one could introduce a probabilistic mechanism governing the "availability" of objects previously seen, with  elapsed time determining the probability of retrieval. Absolute number of objects inspected was used as a rather crude  approximation of elapsed time. Future work on the model might concentrate on a more elegant availability function, relying  directly on elapsed time, and including a distribution over time for the inspection of objects.  The paper also presented special cases for which optimal rules reduced to simple and intuitive verbalizations. For this  reason, the optimal model invites consideration as a model of psychological processes.  Another attractive feature of the optimal strategies is their apparent robustness. An illustration can be given with respect  to the basic model, in which only the maximal alternative carries utility, and only the current alternative is available for  choice. When N - 10, and no recall is permitted, the optimal strategy is to pass over the first four observations and select  the next current maximum to come along. The probability of winning is about .399. However, if one changes the main parameter,  e.g. passes the first five observations and selects the next current maximum to come along, observable behaviour would be  compatible with the best strategy 80 % of the time, and the probability of winning would only drop to .373. So even the simple  rule for optimality may be further abused with little damage to one's chances of success. Results I omit here show that  certain linear and "probability matching" rules are also reasonably effective.  Such results enhance the attractiveness of the formal models, especially in light of recent outlooks on decision-making  (Hogarth, 1975; Slovic, 1972). Current theory urges us to admit that "economic man" is a myth, that humans have but limited  processing abilities, and that decision-makers proceed by homespun rules and manageable heuristics to solve their very complex  tasks. And despite the unwieldy complexity of his environment, man is often able to make very good decisions. We might better  understand this paradox if it could be shown that relatively somple heuristics are compatible with, or at least approximate,  mathematically optimal criteria. As illustrated above, the secretary problem model lends itself very well in this regard.  The model also lends itself easily as a paradigm for experimental research. Laboratory realization of the "basic model"  entails the following task. The subject is confronted with a series of N numerical stimuli. He inspects them one at a time, in  random order, and stops when he thinks he has come to the highest number of all N. If he is correct, he receives a reward;  otherwise, he receives nothing. The optimal strategy for the task when the subject knows nothing about the distribution which  governs the numbers was outlined in Special Case A. At least two experimental considerations of the model have led to its  rejection. Yet it appears to the author that the experiments were designed in ways which did not meet the model's assumptions.  They were not fair tests. In one experimental report, Brickman (1972) opens with a discussion of the model, but discards it  for its  SECRETARY PROBLEM 25  inadequacy. His experiment involved presenting subjects with sequences which were almost perfectly monotonic (either  increasing of decreasing). That is, the value of the alternatives was either a positive or a negative function of their  sequential position. The subjects inspected the alternatives sequentially and received, in points, the numeric value of the  alternative at which they stopped. Now the model requires that the (perceived) order of alternatives be completely random. It  is hard to imagine that subjects would have failed to perceive the non-random monotonicity of the sequences. Yet Brickman  alludes to the deficiency of the model. He makes the curious comment that, in environments which are systematically improving  or deteriorating, "the optimal strategy... would yield highly non-optimal results" (1972, p. 53). It seems more accurate to  say that the model was inappropriate for the task.  In an experiment by Kahan, Rapoport and Jones (1967), designed with the very purpose of testing the model, subjects were  instructed to find the largest of a set of 200 randomly-ordered numbers observed sequentially. Only the current observation  was available for choice. In one condition, the numbers were sampled from a distribution with a positive skew, in a second  condition from a distribution with a negative skew, and in a third condition from a uniform distribution. Now the optimal  model is based on the assumption that subjects have no information about the underlying distribution. Its failure to prove  adequate in this study was not surprising, since after sampling a few dozen cards, the subjects could hardly be labelled  ignorant with respect to the underlying distribution.  An experiment reported by Corbin, Olson and Albondanza (1975) disminished the learning effects that may have confounded the  data of Kahan et al. (1967), and examined the model in more detail. Each of 16 subjects underwent 120 experimental trials.  With sequences having a maximum of five observations, and with different distributions generating the sequences for each  trial, subjects had limited opportunity to identify any of the underlying distributions. The results indicated variable  adherence to the optimal strategy, ranging from 42% to 82% with a median of 60% over the 16 subjects.  This variation was inspected more closely by the authors, and they showed that subjects' stopping behaviour could be  attributed to other rules which sometimes coincided with the optimal rule. For example, whereas the optimal strategy always  prescribed stopping on a candidate which appears as the third of five observations, subjects were more likely to stop on a  third-position-candidate if the first two observations were relatively small in value. That is, they were more likely to stop  after the series 9, 47, 537,... than after the series 501, 516, 537,... . They were less likely to stop if the first three  observations increased monotonically (21, 35, 47,...) than if the first three observations entailed a decrease followed by an  increase (35, 21, 47,...)  These and other results suggested that the irrelevant information in the stimuli (the numerical values) was being manipulated  by subjects in order to lend structure to a seemingly complex task.  In a follow-up experiment by the author (Corbin, 1976), irrelevant information was eliminated. At each stage of inspection,  subjects learned only whether the current alternative was a current maximum or not. No numerical value was given. Optimal  behaviour  26 RUTH M. CORBIN  was substantially improved, with a median of 80%, and subjects' verbal reports were quite consistent with the form of the best  strategy.  By now of course, the experimental task has become so restrictive and uncluttered, as to have little correspondence to the  real world. But what the last experiment did show is the empirical soundness of the logic inherent in the model. With such  confirmatory evidence as a basis, future experiments could add on real-life complexities one by one, gaining insights from the  patterns of deviations which such complexities induce. In the context of this approach, the mathematical model is regarded  principally as a formal structure for research, enabling the pursuit of new ideas in a systematic and efficient manner. With  the availability extensions introduced in the current paper, the variety of paradigms for laboratory study is enhanced.  On the topic of potential applications of the model, a particularly interesting one involves the so-called "no-choice option"  discussed in a paper by Corbin and Marley (1974). In typical choice experiments, the subject is presented with a well-defined  set of alternatives. But in real life, the choice set is not always so clear. The individual himself must often generate the  set and evaluate the alternative thereafter. Moreover, he often has the prerogative not to choose but to seek our further  alternatives. It is this prerogative which we may call a "no-choice option" to indicate that a decision-maker need not select  from every given set. In the mathematical notation used earlier, the context of choice at stage k was written as Y,. _ 'Y,, Ya  ,..., YJ. If the chooser reaches stage k, then Yl , Y2 ,..., Yk represent contexts in which he declined to choose.  Other potential applications reside in the areas of economic choice theory (Simon, 1959), problem-solving (Maier, 1960), and  behavioural decision-making (Lee, 1971), where the desirability of including a mechanism for the inspection of alternatives  has already been recognized. Research in memory might draw benefit from the availability extensions, by exploiting the Smith  and Deely (1975) analogy discussed earlier. Availability extensions may also find use in applied areas of consumer choice such  as job search and realestate purchase where the possibility of losing a reasonably good prospect is often an important  determinant of behaviour.  When the generation of alternatives is considered as an explicit segment of the choice process, the typical distinction  between "static" and "dynamic" decisions (Rapoport and Wallsten, 1974) becomes hazy indeed. Bridging the artificial gap in  theory, which has been fostered by this distinction, is another potential contribution of the model.  APPENDIX: PROOF of THEOREM 4  Proof.
