This paper deals with the measurement problem of deriving an overall evaluation of stimuli by n-component bisection. Reflexivity and a generalised form of bisymmetry for two variables will be shown to characterise n-component bisection in terms of quasilinear means, unique up to linear transformations of their generating functions. � 2000 Academic Press 

1. INTRODUCTION 

One of the first measurement procedures applied in psychophysics and psychology is the method of bisection, and the simplest experimental situation may be described as follows. The subject is presented with a pair of stimuli and is asked to indicate a stimulus which is halfway between the two stimuli presented, i.e., a stimulus which lies ``at the same distance'' from each of the presented stimuli. The stimuli might be saturation of colors, pitch, or subjective loudness, among others. In the case of experiments concerning bisections, it is convenient to specify assumptions in which the primitive notation is the midpoint. The key assumption of characterising the midpoint is the so-called bisymmetry property of the mean. Bisymmetry is motivated by the various concepts of mean and asserts an internal consistency of the operation of taking midpoints by bisection. The bisymmetry property for two-variable functions and some generalisations of bisymmetry have been studied extensively by functional equationists, such as Acze�l (1946, 1948, 1966, 1969, 1987) and Acze�l and Dhombres (1989), among others. Reflexivity is an assumptions for an ``average-type'' bisection rule requiring that equal values on both components are mapped on the same value by the bisection function B. Monotonicity of B means that when one endpoint is changed into some specific direction, the ``midpoint'' changes in the same direction. The assumption of bisymmetry as defined by (3) can be seen as a certain consistency requirement concerning the taking of midpoints for two pairs of points, and the sense of this assumption may be illustrated geometrically by the well-known example shown in Fig. 1. 

As stated before, bisymmetry is an assumption that is related to but weaker than those of commutativity and associativity. Its importance is shown in Theorem 1, where adding the reflexivity, monotonicity, and continuity assumptions to the requirement of bisymmetry, a ``functional form'' of the possible bisymmetric operations is determined.

2. THE n-VARIABLE CASE 

In this section, we obtain and solve a system of functional equations that is similar to the one in Theorem 1 and is based on a generalised form of n-variable bisection and the corresponding bisymmetry equation (3). Our specific goal is to introduce and investigate that generalised form of bisymmetry for n variables.

Acze�l (1946, 1948) discussed the symmetric case of (7), but our proof for the general case is completely different. In economics, for linking microeconomic and macroeconomic levels, some related types of functions, called functional equations of aggregation (see, e.g., Pokroff, 1978) have been used extensively (Green, 1964; Gorman, 1968; among others), without reference to bisymmetry. In that different context, results comparable to our main theorem (Theorem 2) were proved, but different conditions of solvability or surjectivity (see, e.g., Pokroff, 1978, p. 126, conditions 6.2.3 and 6.2.4, and pp. 130 and 134) were assumed, which excluded even the simple arithmetic mean from the possible solutions for B. Also, this is the case with the additional surjectivity conditions for solving the ``ultimate generalisation'' of (7) given by Acze�l and Maksa (1996, 1997) and Acze�l (1997). 

We present a hypothetical example for a psychophysical measurement procedure using the bisymmetry equation (7) and n-variable bisection. 

The subject is given, for simplicity, a sequence of three tones of different intensity (but of the same frequency) a, b, and c, and is asked to adjust a fourth tone, denoted B(a, b, c), to the point where the subject deems it to be in the middle of the other three tones in terms of intensity. The bisymmetry assumption then requires that the bisections of the respective bisection points coincide as is the case, for instance, for properties of means of n-tuples of numbers. 

This can be tested with any nine (in general n[2] ) distinct tones x11 , x12 , x13 , x21 , x22 , x23 , x31 , x32 , x33 . The tones can be written, according to Fig. 2, in matrix form (the entries denote the intensities of the input tones).

The obtained ``middle'' loudness should coincide with that obtained by first bisecting the columns (x11, x21, x31), (x12 , x22, x32), and (x13 , x23, x33) and then bisecting the resulting bisection points, that is, the column margins. 

The subject is asked to find the ``middle'' of the three tones and is not supposed to ``a priori'' determine a subjective ``weighted average.'' Nevertheless, in our main theorem (Theorem 2) it is shown that, with the additional assumption of reflexivity, strict monotonicity, and continuity, the resulting bisection intensity is the quasilinear mean of the input intensities, that is, a generalisation of the ``weighted average.'' Hence the input tones are weighted somehow, and the weights may reflect, e.g., the order of the inputs or the memory of the subject. For instance, if too many inputs are presented, it is likely that the last ones will be weighted with higher values. 

Note that the findings for m-variable bisection are not valid automatically for the case of n-variable bisection (n is different from m), although as will be shown later, they are structurally identical (that is, B is a quasilinear mean of the input tones). For example, increasing the number of input tones may change the actual form of B, e.g., by ``simplifying'' the functional form of B from an exponential mean to the arithmetic mean.
