JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 15,209-212 (1977)  The Scott-Suppes Theorem on Semiorders  ISSIE RA13INOVITCH*  Department of Statistics, University of Waterloo, Waterloo, Ontario  Luce introduced semiorders as a natural generalization of weak orders. The basic representation theorem for semiorders was  established in 1958 by Scott and Suppes. In this note this theorem is given a simple contructive proof.  Luce (1956) introduced semiorders as a natural generalization of linear and weak orders and as a model for preference with  intransitive indifference. Since then, the concept has proved useful in a variety of contexts (see Dean & Keller, 1968;  Jamison & Lau, 1973; Roberts, 1971; Scott, 1969; Tversky, 1969).  Scott and Suppes (1958) showed that "P is a semiorder" answers the question: Let X be a finite set and P a binary relation on  X. Under what conditions on P will there exist a real function f on X such that xPy if and only if f (x) > f (y) -f- 1 for all  x, y E X ? In this note we give a simple, constructive proof of the Scott-Suppes Representation Theorem using some facts  discovered while studying the dimension of semiorders (see Rabinovitch (to appear), where it is shown that every semiorder may  be expressed as the intersection of at most three linear orders). In the interest of keeping this paper very short, these  facts will be stated without proof. The proofs are simple in any case. For other proofs of the Scott-Suppes Theorem, see Scott  (1964) and Suppes & Zinnes (1963).  A partially ordered set or poset is an ordered pair (X, P) where P is an irreflexive and transitive binary relation on X. P is  called a linear order if either xPy or yPx for all x and y in X, and a weak order if xPy implies xPz or zPy for any z in X.  Clearly, every linear order is a weak order but not conversely. P is a semiorder if  (1) xPy and sPt imply xPt or sPy, and,  (2) xPy and yPz imply wPz or xPw for all w E X.  Every weak order is a semiorder, but not conversely.  H*(x) = { y c X I xPy} and H*(x) = {y c X I yPx} are called the lower and upper holdings of x, respectively. The elevation of  x, e(x), is the length of the longest chain that x dominates and the depth of x, d(x), is the length of the longest chain  dominating x. The height of P, h(P), is one less than the length of the longest chain in P.
