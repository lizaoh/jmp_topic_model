A stochastic model for synchronization with a metronome is analyzed which generalizes Wing and Kristofferson’s (1973a, 1973b) two-level model for tapping. The model includes a motor component, a timekeeper component, and a linear phase error correction mechanism. The model’s predictions of the asymptotic dependence structure of the synchronization errors and interresponse intervals are derived. Problems of identifiability and estimation of parameters are discussed. © 2001 Elsevier Science (USA) 

1. INTRODUCTION 

Simple finger-tapping tasks, dating back at least to Stevens (1886), have become a popular tool among psychologists and neuroscientists interested in motor control and timing. Two types of tasks can be distinguished. In the continuation paradigm, subjects are asked to tap freely at a preset rate, whereas in the synchronization paradigm, the task is to tap in synchrony with an isochronous stimulus sequence generated by a metronome. Until recently, most researchers were interested either in continuation or in synchronization, an attitude that is reflected in the theoretical tools developed for the analysis of tapping data. 

Wing and Kristofferson (1973a, 1973b) have analyzed continuation data in terms of a two-level model that distinguishes between the timing of intervals and the motor execution of the taps. Both components are subject to random fluctuations.

These authors have shown that the temporal variability contributed by the two levels can be isolated, and the successful breakdown of total variability into these two sources has contributed enormously to the model’s appeal. The Wing– Kristofferson model has been extended to more complex tasks like two-hand performance, rhythmic patterns controlled by hierarchical timekeepers, polyrhythms, etc. (Jagacinsky, 1988; Krampe, Kliegl, Meyr, Engbert, & Vorberg, 2000; Pressing, 1998; Vorberg, Marshborn, Klapp, Jones, & Hambuch, 1978, 1984). For a survey of the theoretical and experimental results, we refer the reader to Vorberg and Wing (1994, 1996); a recent survey of studies with neurological patients can be found in Hazeltine, Helmuth, and Ivry (1997). 

In contrast, synchronization data have been described in terms of error correction models by Hary and Moore (1987a, 1987b) and Mates (1994a, 1994b); see also Voillaume (1971). These authors have assumed that subjects make use of the asynchronies between responses and metronome clicks to adjust both phase and period of the underlying timekeeper. Error correction is essential for successful synchronization, because without correction, the subject’s responses will become unsynchronized due to random jitter in the timekeeper. 

From a theoretical point of view, a unified model for both synchronization and continuation is desirable. However, whereas research on continuation performance has shown that the distinction between timekeeper and motor delay levels is important, it has tended to neglect error correction. On the other hand, research on synchronization has focused on error correction mechanisms, neglecting the distinction between the two levels of timing. For example, the synchronization models of Voillaume (1971) and Hary and Moore (1987a, 1987b) do not explicitly include the motor execution of responses. One of the first synchronization models that combines first-order error correction by phase adjustment with the two-level notion of timekeeping and motor execution has been put forward by Vorberg and Wing (1994, 1996). Extensions of this model to include second-order error correction and to rhythmic metronome sequences have been developed by Pressing and JolleyRogers (1997) and Pressing (1998). The model has been shown to provide an excellent fit of the time series collected in various synchronization tasks (Pressing, 1998) and to predict the effects of experimental perturbations during synchronization (Repp, 2000; Semjen, Vorberg, & Schulze 1998). Recently, we have shown that the linear phase correction model can also face the challenge of accounting for both continuation and synchronization performance (Semjen, Schulze, & Vorberg, 2000). In fitting the model to these tasks it turned out, however, that the problems of estimating and interpreting the parameters of this simple model are much harder than anticipated. The particular techniques developed by Pressing (1998) for estimating the error correction parameters seem to avoid some of these problems, but cannot be extended to the variance parameters which are the main concern of most researchers interested in timing. 

The purpose of the present paper is therefore (i) to give a thorough analysis of the original two-level synchronization model as well as of some extensions, (ii) to discuss problems arising in parameter identification and estimation, and (iii) to present Monte Carlo studies which illustrate these problems and suggest possible

solutions. It should be noted that, while the focus is on the predictions of the model at steady state, the proofs contain the relevant information for model predictions of synchronization performance in the transient state, e.g., in perturbation paradigms (Repp, 2000, 2001). 

We analyze the model in a framework more general than usual: synchronization with a metronome that is stable but subject to random perturbations, a task studied by several researchers before (e.g., Hary & Moore, 1985; Michon, 1967; Schulze, 1992; Vos & Helsper, 1992). Random jitter in the metronome is of theoretical interest because it forces the subject to correct synchronization errors which will grow without bound otherwise (Hary & Moore, 1987b). It will be shown that adjusting phase locally is sufficient for maintaining synchrony in this situation, in contrast to situations where the metronome period undergoes systematic variations, such as drifts, and abrupt or periodic changes (e.g., Thaut, Miller, & Schauer, 1998) which require adjusting the period of the internal timing mechanism as well. Our model does not apply to such complex tasks for which more ambitious models (Hary & Moore, 1987a, 1987b; Mates, 1994a, 1994b) are intended. Without wishing to comment on the empirical adequacy of these models, we see advantages in confining attention to synchronization with a stable metronome. One is that our model allows for a full analysis of its stochastic behavior and derivation of predictions in closed form and thus more stringent tests. Another advantage is that the model includes the Wing–Kristofferson model as a limiting case, making it possible to study continuation and synchronization performance within one framework. 

Terminological note. We use the following notation. For arbitrary sequences of random variables {Xn} and {Yn} , n=0, 1, 2, ..., we describe dependence in terms of the covariances between pairs of variables separated by lag j , e.g., cov (Xn , Xn+j) or cov (Yn , Xn+j). Note that var (Xn)= cov (Xn , Xn) by definition. Using covariances rather than correlations simplifies the derivations considerably; converting covariances into correlations is straightforward. Our derivations make much use of the distributivity of the covariance; by distributivity, covariances between linear combinations of random variables can be expanded: 

cov (aX+bY, cV+dW) 

=ac cov (X, V)+ad cov (X, W)+bc cov (Y, V)+bd cov (Y, W). 

If the means, variances, and covariances do not depend on n , or tend to limiting values as n tends to infinity, the corresponding process is called stationary, or asymptotically stationary, respectively. In this case, the dependence structure is summarized by the auto-covariance (acvf) and cross-covariance functions (ccvf) . We denote them by c(j), using the names of the random variables as subscripts, e.g., cX(j)= cov (Xn , Xn+j), or cXY(j)= cov (Xn , Yn+j). Notice that acvfs are symmetric but ccvfs are not, i.e., cX(j)=cX(−j), but cXY(j) ] cXY(−j) . For convenience, acvfs will be stated for j \ 0 only. For illustration, we sometimes also use the auto-correlation function ( acf ) defined as r(k) = c(k)/c(0).

2. THE TWO-LEVEL MODEL 

In this section we present the main assumptions of and results for the Wing–Kristofferson model for self-paced tapping. In the following section we extend the notion of two-level timing to synchronization with a metronome. 

2.1. The Wing–Kristofferson Model for Continuation

2.2. The Linear Phase Correction Model for Synchronization 

Consider tapping to a metronome that produces clicks at a constant rate of 1/y . Note the different effects of timekeeper and motor variability on synchronization performance: Unless corrected, a single imperfect timekeeper interval misplaces all successive taps relative to the metronome, whereas an imprecisely executed response affects the current tap only and need not be corrected if the timekeeper is perfect. Whether and how much to adjust obviously depends on what caused the synchronization error in the first place, an observation that will be taken up again in Section 4.1.

7. PARAMETER ESTIMATION 

Calculating quantitative predictions from the model for a given set of parameters is straightforward, but the inverse problem, that is, inferring the model parameters from sample data, is more complex. We discuss them in turn with respect to the asynchrony acvf .

7.2. Parameter Interdependence 

In practice, identifiability is not enough because it does not safeguard against numerical problems which may arise when different parameter vectors lead to mathematically distinct yet numerically close predictions. Let h[−] =(h[−] 1[,][ h][−] 2[, ...)][ be] the parameters of the true model. How much will the predictions change if we replace h[−] by some other h =(h1 , h2 , ...) ? Due to limited numerical resolution, it will in general not be possible to distinguish between models if their predictions differ by less than some small value of the goodness-of-fit measure. For given numerical precision we define the e -region by the set of all vectors h which do not differ by more than e from the model with h[−] . Obviously, all models in the e -region will fit empirical data about equally well as the model h[−] . Following Reich (1981) and Li, Lewandosky, and DeBrunner (1996), parameters are called interdependent if changes in a parameter hi may be compensated for by changes in another parameter hj without appreciable loss of fit. Parameter interdepence expresses itself in the shape of the e -region in the subspace (hi , hj). 

We propose estimation of the parameters of the synchronization model by minimizing the discrepancy between the empirical and theoretical asynchrony acvf , defined as their least-square difference at lags 0 through 3 (see the next section). Let d( h ) measure how much the acvf predicted by a model with parameters h =(a, b, m, t) differs from the acvf of the ‘‘true’’ model with h[−] =(a[−] , b[−] , m[−] , t[−] ) . The e -region is given by the set of parameters h such that d( h ) [ e . We check for parameter interdependence by inspecting the parameter space around the true h[−] .

7.4. Sampling Variability 

Sampling variability problems arise in stochastic models because of the inherent noise in the data. Basically, they relate to the questions of how to evaluate the fit of a model to empirical data and whether we can recover the parameter values from the data if we know the true model, apart from identifiability and parameter interdependence problems. 

Because of sampling variability, the predictions that follow from a model need not hold in finite samples. As an example, consider the Wing–Kristofferson continuation model. The predicted correlation between adjacent IRIs is bounded by −[1] 2 and 0 (see (1)), which is true iff the acvf obeys − cI(0) < 2cI(1) < 0. How likely is it that the sample acvf in a sample satisfies these bounds if the model is true? The same problem concerns the variance parameters estimated from sample data. Because variances are nonnegative, negative estimates of variance parameters are troublesome and seem to imply that the model is wrong. In fact, empirical data often show violations of the lag-1 auto-correlation bounds and hence deficient estimates of the variance parameters (for a recent summary and discussion of such findings, see Collins, Jahanshahi, & Barnes, 1998). The problem is that we do not know the risk of falsely rejecting the correct model from negative variance estimates, i.e., the probability that defective variance estimates arise by chance.

The lesson to be learned from these demonstrations is that determining the values of the underlying parameters is a serious problem in applications of the synchronization model, particularly so for the second-order model. Because these problems are basically due to parameter interdependence, a possible solution is to impose constraints on the parameter space, either by determining some parameter from independent data, or by keeping parameters invariant across experimental conditions. Elsewhere (Semjen, Schulze, & Vorberg, 2000), we have used this approach in fitting the synchronization model to the data of an experiment which contrasted the synchronization and continuation paradigm. The phase correction model was found to provide an excellent account of tapping performance across a range of tempi in continuation and synchronization tasks. 

9. CONCLUSIONS 

We have analyzed in detail the extension of the Wing–Kristofferson two-level continuation model to synchronization. Emphasis was on how linear phase-error correction in synchronization performance is reflected in the asynchrony statistics. The theoretical analysis has implications for inferences about the correction parameters on the one hand and the variability contributed by the internal timekeeper and by the motor system on the other hand. 

Identifiability conditions of the parameters are specified. An extension of the model includes perceptual delays of the metronome and the registration of the response. However, identifiability is reduced in the extended model, except when the variance of the perceptual delays is known from other sources. This result suggests that perceptual variability should be assessed across different experimental paradigms. 

In addition to questions of identifiability, parameter interdependence needs to be considered when evaluating the model from the plausibility of the obtained parameter estimates. Parameter interdependence problems were investigated by Monte Carlo simulations and by studying the mapping of the parameters to the theoretical asynchrony autocovariance function. Depending upon the parameters of the true model, there may be wide ranges of parameter combinations that fit a given set of data about equally well. One implication for applying the model to data is to estimate parameters jointly from several experimental conditions, imposing reasonable invariance constraints across conditions.
