Visually perceiving a 3D scene and the 3D shapes of objects within the scene is a difficult problem but we all perceive them veridically (or almost veridically) in our everyday life. Some have assumed that such veridical 3D perception requires some kind of extra-retinal information, usually memories of past experiences of moving ourselves, or our arms, or eyes around in our 3D world. Any effect of such memories has been questioned, however, by psychophysical studies on the effect of memory on 3D visual perception ( Firestone & Scholl, 2016 ). Studies by Pizlo and his colleagues have shown that the veridical perception of the 3D shapes of familiar objects can be explained better by  a priori  constraints than by the memorized shapes of the objects ( Pizlo, 2008; Pizlo, Li, Sawada, & Steinman, 2014; Pizlo, Sawada, Li, Kropatsch, & Steinman, 2010 ). This group has developed and tested computational models that emulate veridical human 3D shape and scene perception rather well by using only a few  a priori  constraints (priors), namely, 3D symmetry, the planarity of contours, minimum surface area, and maximum compactness. The present study addresses another classical visual problem in which oculomotor information has been assumed to be essential for the perception of 3D depth. Here, “binocular disparity”, which is an important input for eliciting and controlling slow vergence eye movements and disjunctive saccades, could provide information needed for the veridical perception of depth (see  Collewijn, Erkelens, & Steinman, 1995; Erkelens, Van der Steen, Steinman and Collewijn, 1989; Erkelens, Steinman and Collewijn, 1989  for studies of vergence eye movements). It has been widely assumed that our visual system uses binocular disparity to perceive depth. The present study will show that the perception of depth can be recovered entirely on the basis of geometrical optics. Our visual system does not need to make use of any oculomotor information. Note that my research problem, when viewed within the rubric called “Inverse Problem Theory”, is a “Direct” problem because its solution does not require the use of any a priori constraints (aka priors, see  Pizlo et al., 2014, 2010; Sawada, Li, & Pizlo, 2015 ). This Direct problem will be solved first by making a computational model that recovers depth without being given any oculomotor information or any  a priori  constraints. Having a computational model that can solve the Direct Problem of perceiving depth by using only geometrical optics prepares the way for finding out whether human beings can do this, too. Now, consider what we know about how the geometry involved in binocular disparity can be used to recover depth.

Human eyes are separated about 6.5 cm which means that the retinal images of 3D scenes will be slightly different from one another. This difference between a stereo-pair of retinal images is called “binocular disparity”. Binocular disparity is one of several depth cues that the human visual system uses to perceive depth within 3D scenes. Depth perception, based on binocular disparity, has been studied for centuries. It is one of the best studied topics in visual science (see  Howard & Rogers, 2012  for a review).

Binocular disparity is often decomposed into its horizontal and vertical components ( Read, Phillipson, & Glennerster, 2009 ). Horizontal disparity plays the major role in the perception of depth when it is based on binocular disparity. It has been assumed that the visual system needs oculomotor information about the relative orientation between the two eyes to recover depth from horizontal disparity ( Erkelens & van Ee, 1998; Mayhew & Longuet-Higgins, 1982; Peek, Mayhew, & Frisby, 1984 ). This kind of oculomotor information can be estimated from the efference copy of the oculomotor signal ( Matin, Matin, & Pearce, 1969; Skavenski, 1971; Skavenski, Haddad, & Steinman, 1972; Sommer & Wurtz, 2002 ). But note that the visual system cannot detect displacements of visual stimuli that are based on the efference signal ( Mack, 1970; Matin, 1974; Wallach & Lewis, 1965 ). Any role of the efference signal for spatial perception in our everyday life is unclear (see  Bridgeman, 2007, 2010  for a discussion).

Another source of oculomotor information is the 2D distribution of vertical disparity ( Howard & Rogers, 2012 ). It has also been shown that depth perception based on horizontal disparity is affected by the vertical disparity distribution. This is often referred to as an “induced” effect. This induced effect is often explained by saying that the visual system estimates the relative eye orientations from the vertical disparity distribution. It has been suggested that the size of the horizontal eye movement can be estimated from the size component of the vertical disparity distribution (e.g.  Gillam & Lawergren, 1983; Vienne, Plantier, Neveu, & Priot, 2016 ). Vertical vergence and cyclo-torsional eye movements can also be estimated from the translational and shear components of the vertical disparity distribution (e.g.  Howard & Kaneko, 1994 ). It is widely believed that the visual system makes use of this estimated oculomotor information to recover the depth from the horizontal disparity.

                         1 
                         The estimated oculomotor information is not necessarily represented as orientations of the eyes. For example, horizontal version can be characterized by a scaling factor of the size component of the vertical disparity distribution ( Backs & Banks, 1999; Backus, Banks, van Ee, & Crowell, 1999; Rogers & Bradshaw, 1993 ). 
                       Psychophysical results also suggest that the human visual system relies on the distribution of vertical disparity, rather than on the oculomotor efference signal, whenever the information in the disparity distribution is sufficiently reliable ( Backus et al., 1999; Bradshaw, Glennerster, & Rogers, 1996; Mitsudo, 2007; Mitsudo, Kaneko, & Nishida, 2009 ). The visual system’s speed, however, for processing the vertical disparity distribution is rather low ( Ames, 1946; Caziot, Backus, & Lin, 2017; Fukuda, Kaneko, & Matsumiya, 2006; Ogle, 1938 ). These authors showed that the visual system needs around 500 msec for processing a change of the vertical disparity distribution. Note, however, that the human beings’ intersaccadic intervals during maintained fixation are often shorter than 500 msec e.g. ( Cunitz & Steinman, 1969; Larsen & Bek, 2017; Steinman & Collewijn, 1980; Steinman, Cunitz, Timberlake, & Herman, 1967 ). This means that the visual system must be able to use a very efficient mechanism for processing binocular disparity if the oculomotor information is going to be estimated at every fixation. This mechanism must work fast whenever saccadic eye movements occur frequently. Now that we have considered the role of vertical disparity in the perception of depth, we will consider depth perception based on horizontal disparity.

The visual system can process horizontal disparity for each point, or for each pair of points, while the visual system processes the distribution of vertical disparity. The visual system encodes horizontal disparity as  absolute  disparity first, and then converts it to  relative  disparity ( Chopin, Levi, Knill, & Bavelier, 2016; Neri, Bridge, & Heeger, 2004; Norcia, Gerhard, & Meredith, 2017 ). This absolute disparity is the difference between the eccentricity angles of a point between a stereo-pair of retinal images. This relative disparity is the difference between the visual angles of two points between the retinal images ( Erkelens & Collewijn, 1985a, 1985b ). 
                         2 
                      
                      
                         2 
                         Relative disparity can also be computed as the difference between the absolute disparities of two points ( Chopin et al., 2016; Schor, 2000; Westheimer & McKee, 1979 ). Note that this method of computing relative disparity assumes that two lines-of-sight from a stereo-pair of foveae intersect with one another at a point within a 3D scene. But note that this assumption may be violated under a natural viewing conditions.  Malinov, Epelboim, Herst, and Steinman (2000)  showed that the two lines may be skewed with respect to one another. 
                       It has been shown that the perception of depth based on horizontal disparity primarily depends on the relative disparity ( Cottereau, McKee, & Norcia, 2012; Erkelens & Collewijn, 1985b; Regan, Erkelens, & Collewijn, 1986; Westheimer, 1979 ). Note that the relative disparity, as well as the visual angle, is invariant against any eye movement (see  Zhang, 2019  for a further discussion about the invariant). Potentially, the invariance of the visual angle could allow the visual system to recover depth from binocular disparity in the presence of eye movements, but note, also, that all prior modeling of depth perception from binocular disparity has assumed that oculomotor information was either given or recovered first.

Models of depth perception that are based on binocular disparity can be categorized into two types on the basis of their mathematical formulations. The first type, either implicitly or explicitly, recovers oculomotor information from binocular disparity itself  before  recovering depth (e.g.  Longuet-Higgins, 1982; Mayhew & Longuet-Higgins, 1982; Peek et al., 1984 ). 
                         3 
                      
                      
                         3 
                         There are many computer vision algorithms that can recover a 3D scene from 2D camera images of the scene that were taken from different positions within the scene. Most of them can be categorized as the first type of model of binocular disparity because they recover the relative orientation between the cameras before they fully recover the 3D scene (see  Hartley & Zisserman, 2004  for details). They proceed by first deriving a fundamental matrix between the images. The fundamental matrix represents epipoles that are at intersections of the image planes with an epipolar axis, a line connecting the optical centers of the cameras in the scene. This property determines the relative orientation between the cameras except for one parameter, namely, the relative orientation around the line connecting the epipoles and the optical centers. Consider the projection of a point in the scene to the two images. The projection of the point in each image can be connected with its epipole by using an epipolar line. The epipolar lines of the point on the two images are coplanar in the scene. This property can determine the relative orientation of the cameras around the line connecting the epipoles. Note that the direction of the point from each camera relative to the epipolar axis can be also determined by computing the angle between the epipolar axis and a line connecting the optical center of the camera with the projection of the point in the image. This is a part of the 3D information contained in the scene. These algorithms recover the rest of the 3D information in the scene after the fundamental matrix is derived. 
                       The second type of model implicitly assumes that the necessary oculomotor information is available. Many existing models of depth perception, based on binocular disparity, use images on a computer screen representing the left and right eyes as input to the models, rather than the retinal images in the eyes. Note that the retinal image is a two-dimensional projection of the image on a screen. This means that the retinal and the computer screen images can be transformed into one another, but doing this requires knowing both the positions and the orientations of the eyes relative to the screen. Some models of the first type simplify the recovery of the oculomotor information by assuming that vertical vergence and cyclo-torsional eye movements are zero (e.g.  Duke & Howard, 2005; Peek et al., 1984; Zhang, 2019 ). The second type also includes a model that represents 2D visual information by using a head-centric coordinate system ( Erkelens & van Ee, 1998; Koenderink & van Doorn, 1976; Zhang, Cantor, & Schor, 2010 ). The retinal image represented in a retino-centric coordinate system can be transformed into the head-centric representation, but, once again, this transformation requires knowing the positions and orientations of the eyes relative to the head.

Our computational model recovers depth from a stereo-pair of retinal images while oculomotor information is neither given nor recovered, not even implicitly.

                         4 
                         Consider a machine learning neural network algorithm that is trained to recover depth in a 3D scene from a stereo pair of retinal images but this algorithm does not explicitly recover any oculomotor information. This algorithm may or may not recover the oculomotor information implicitly in its recovery process, and that the oculomotor information may be represented in hidden layers of the neural network. Examining this kind of implicit information in a neural network is extremely difficult. Note that this study clearly shows that it is possible to recover depth from stereo retinal images without any oculomotor information being given or being recovered. 
                       This model is based entirely on the geometrical optics. It does not use any  a priori  constraints. The depth recovered is represented in a head-centered coordinate system, except for a rotation around the interocular axis between the two eyes. Both the process for recovering depth and the representation of the recovered depth does not vary with eye movements. The model can process stereo retinal images across fixations. It does not need to deal with each fixation. Specifically, depth can be recovered by the model even if the recovery process takes longer than each fixation is maintained. The model proposed shows that, at least from a computational perspective, it is possible for the human visual system to recover depth from stereo retinal images across fixations. Having such a model allows us to discuss psychophysical and neurophysiological phenomena without assuming that the human visual system needs oculomotor information to recover depth in a 3D scene from stereo retinal images.

This study used a “pinhole” camera with a perfectly spherical retina as the model for our human eye. This simplified eye has the center of its optics and its center of rotation at the center of a spherical eyeball. Note that when this simplified eye rotates around its optical center, the position of the eye’s optical center does not change. Lines of projection from any pair of points in a 3D scene will intersect with one another at the optical center of this simplified eye. The visual angle between these lines is the same as the distance between the projections of these points on the spherical retina of this simplified eye, and the visual angle does not change when the eye rotates. The model recovers the depth of a 3D scene from a stereo-pair of its retinal images that are represented as two sets of visual angles. The correspondence between projections of points between the stereo-pair of retinal images is taken as given in this study. This allows our model to recover depth without using any information about the orientations of the eyes. Note that it is mathematically impossible for the model to recover any oculomotor information because the representation of the stereo retinal images is invariant against any eye movement. The model recovers the 3D depth of a scene by using an optimization method. The 2D space in this optimization is characterized by the shape of a triangle formed by the arbitrary selection of any 3 points within the scene. The cost function for this optimization problem is formulated on the basis of 2 or more than 2 additional points in the scene. This triangle is discussed in next section.

Having these separate processes for recovering a 3D scene allowed us to develop a model that used a readily available algorithm that was developed to solve the P3P problem. Unfortunately, this algorithm, which is based on the P3P problem, does not resemble any known mechanism in our visual system.

The model developed in this study can recover depth in a 3D scene from a stereo-pair of retinal images without making use of the relative orientation of the eyes. The model can process stereo retinal images across fixations. The model represents the retinal image of the scene as a set of visual angles between pairs of points within the scene. The model uses only these visual angles as its input. This means that eye movements play no role in the recovery of depth and that the oculomotor information is not even implicitly recovered by the model. This is possible because depth is recovered within a head-centered coordinate system. 
                         9 
                      
                      
                         9 
                         The interocular axis connecting the two eyes in the recovered scene corresponds to an epipolar axis connecting two cameras for a fundamental matrix (see Footnote 3). The directions of points in the scene from each eye relative to the interocular axis can be determined from the recovered scene. This information is contained in the scene, but the fundamental matrix cannot be recovered from the scene. The stereo retinal images are represented as two sets of visual angles in the model, and this representation is invariant against any eye movement. Note that no information about the visual axes of the eyes is used. The visual axes correspond to the principal axes of the cameras in the fundamental matrix. The fundamental matrix cannot be recovered without the principal axes. 
                       The model showed that from a computational perspective, oculomotor information is not needed to recover depth in a 3D scene unless the scene is degenerate. Having such a model allows us to discuss psychophysical and neurophysiological phenomena and their mechanisms without assuming that the human visual system needs oculomotor information to recover depth in a 3D scene from stereo retinal images ( Bogen, 2017; Brewer & Lambert, 2000; Runeson, 1977 ).

This model shows that, at least from a computational perspective, the human visual system should be able to perceive depth by using only a stereo-pair of retinal images without recovering or being given any oculomotor information. It also shows that the perception of depth need not change when the eyes move. This can be described as a constancy of depth perception with different fixation points. Note that these properties of this visual system are consistent with our everyday life experience. Our perception of depth is reliable and it stays that way when we move our eyes ( Logvinenko, Epelboim, & Steinman, 2001; Logvinenko & Steinman, 2002 ). These properties also allow the visual system to process stereo retinal images across eye movements: this could improve the precision of the perception of depth ( Enright, 1991; Wright, 1951 ). The visual system can recover the depth even if the recovery process takes longer time than the duration of a single maintained fixation.

The proposed model does not conflict with models that assume that oculomotor information, such as the oculomotor efference signal, is used to recover depth from a stereo pair of retinal images. These two types of models can co-exist with one another. Note that it has been shown that the visual system makes use of the efference signal to recover depth when the visual stimuli are degenerate ( Backus et al., 1999; Bradshaw et al., 1996; Minkov & Sawada, 2021 ). Ideally, both types of models will be integrated into a single model.

The human visual system is more restricted than our model by built-in physiological and neurophysiological mechanisms in the system, such as the relative orientation between the eyes, the resolution of the retinal image, and the range of stereo fusion. Note also that a projection of a 3D scene moves in the retinal image whenever the eye moves, and that the eye movement should be also restricted in order to recover the depth across the eye movement. When this is done, the projection only moves within a small part of the retina where the resolution is high enough for the depth to be recovered. The model can be simplified by applying these restrictions to the model, or by integrating the model with existing models of stereo depth perception that make use of these restrictions.

The restriction of the eye movement will also simplify the problem of establishing the correspondence between the stereo-pair of retinal images. Establishing correspondence is a particularly difficult problem when the relative orientation between the eyes puts no restriction on their orientation. In Computer Vision, this problem has been studied as “wide-baseline stereo matching” (e.g.  Ferrari, Tuytelaars, & Van Gool, 2003; Leroy, Franco, & Boyer, 2017 ). 
                         10 
                      
                      
                         10 
                         Note that the proposed model does not have to re-establish correspondence after each fixation once the correspondence has been established because the recovered depth is represented within a head-centered coordinate system that does not change when the eyes rotate. This is advantageous in the proposed model. 
                       Instead of trying to solve this difficult problem, the proposed model assumes that the correspondence is given. 
                         11 
                      
                      
                         11 
                         The model also assumes that the correspondence is established between feature “points” in the stereo-pair of retinal images. Note that many neurons in the early visual cortex have receptive fields that can be approximated by 2D Gabor patterns with different frequencies and orientations (see  Sawada & Petrov, 2017  for a review). These neurons are sensitive to edges, contours, and gratings rather than to the points. Some psychophysical phenomena can be explained by the receptive field patterns of these neurons (e.g.  Farrel, 1998; Matthews, Meng, & Qian, 2003 ; see also  Anderson, 1999; Farrel, 1999; Mitsudo, Sakai, & Kaneko, 2013 ). 
                       Namely, the correspondence problem is independent from the recovery and is solved before the recovery in the proposed model for its simplicity (see  Zhaoping, 2002  for a computational model that processes the correspondence and recovery together). The correspondence problem can actually be relatively easy for the human visual system to solve because the range of the relative orientations between the eyes is limited. Note that cyclo-torsional and vertical vergence eye movements are small, but not zero (see  Howard, 2012 ). Also note that this limitation of eye movements restricts the relative position between pairs of corresponding points in stereo retinal images ( Schreiber, Crawford, Fetter, & Tweed, 2001 ). Furthermore, the limitation of the range of eye movements can also simplify the depth recovery process (see above). The correspondence problem, and the recovery of depth with limitations of the range of eye movement will be addressed in my future studies.

This model is based entirely on the geometrical optics of a schematic eye. It is not related to any known mechanisms in our visual system. This fact encourages us to revise this algorithm to make its recovery process plausible with respect to current psychophysical and neurophysiological evidence. Once this has been done, we will have a realistic, as well as an effective, model of human stereo-depth perception.
