IOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY: 1, 351-374 (1964)  Sequential Decision-Making in a Computer-Controlled Task  AMNON RAPOPORT  University of North Carolina, Chapel Hill, North Carolina  Twenty-four undergraduate college students participated individually as subjects in a computer-controlled task, intended as an  investigation of decision-making under uncertainty in a nonstationary environment. Each subject was required on every trial to  predict the value of an unknown P, 0 G P c 1.00, and then to estimate its value after having received partial information  about it from a computer. Each P value, for 120 trials, was generated by one of three different beta distributions. A model  derived from Bayesian decision theory is presented, tested, and partially confirmed. A learning model was constructed and  tried in a computer simulation of the subjects. Implications concerning information seeking and use of computers in  experiments are discussed briefly.  Bayesian decision theory postulates that some mathematical probability distribution, the "prior distribution," represents  appropriately the background information, knowledge, or opinion available to the decision-maker at the outset of an  experiment. This background information can be combined with the information obtained in an experiment, by use of the Bayes  theorem, to determine the "posterior distribution" which appropriately represents the information finally available (Birnbaum,  1962).  Normative models, derived from Bayesian decision theory, have been constructed recently for human decision-making tasks in  different contexts. Bayesian decision theory has been applied to the study of signal detection (Swets, Tanner, and Birdsall,  1961), to the area of probability learning (Toda, 1958), to the area of concept formation (Watanabe, 1960), to the study of  psychophysics (Shuford and Hall, 1959; Wiesen and Shuford, 1962; Wiesen, 1962), and to the area of military information  processing (Edwards, 1963; Edwards and Robinson, 1961). It is the purpose of this study to extend the application of the  Bayesian approach by presenting and testing a model for the prediction and estimation of unknown proportions. A second aim is  to assess the effect of changes in the environment upon the subject's sequential decisions, when asked to guess the values of  the proportions.  351  352 RAPOPORT  MODEL h  Suppose we present to a subject a series of stimuli, each of which is a square 3 x 3 matrix composed of randomly placed  elements of two types, 0 and 1. If we permit the possible values of P to be .00, .01, .02, --- .99, 1.00, then both the courses of action and the relevant  states of nature are these 101 possible values. Without loss of generality the subjective probabilities of the different  states of nature may be approximated by a continuous beta distribution g(P)* defined over the interval [0, 1]. The beta  distribution g(P)*, which is assumed to correspond to the subjective probability distribution, is completely specified by its  two parameters, a* and #*. Using an all-or-none payoff function, we assume only that the utility of being correct and gaining  money is greater than the utility of being wrong and gaining no money.  The justification for the assumption that the prior probability distribution is a beta distribution is based upon the fact  that several important desiderata are fulfilled by the beta distribution in our case (see Raiffa and Schlaifer, 1961, Chap. 3,  for a discussion of prior distributions). First, the beta distribution is analytically tractable in the following respects:  the expectations, given an all-or-none payoff function, are expressed in a very convenient form; the prior distribution is a  beta distribution, as is the posterior distribution (especially convenient when dealing with sequential decision tasks.) It is  preferred that the prior and the posterior probability distributions belong to the same family; otherwise we could not assume  that the subjective probabilities of the different states of nature, measured at any point in the experiment, could be  approximated by a beta distribution. Raiffa and Schlaifer show that whenever (a) any outcome of the experiment can be  described by a sufficient statistic of fixed dimensionality and (b) the likelihood of every outcome is given by a reasonably  simple formula, it is possible to obtain a tractable family of "conjugate" prior distributions by interchanging the roles of  parameters and variables in the expression for the sample likelihood. The subject's estimates and predictions provide two independent measures from which the shape of his subjective probability  distribution might be indirectly inferred. Such an inference might be supplemented by asking the subject to state verbally his  subjective probability distribution, thus obtaining a more direct measure. One way of obtaining more direct information from  the subject is by using a suggestion given by Ramsey (1931), and elaborated by Savage (1954). The subject might be faced with  a set of hypothetical bets. By changing one bet and keeping the other constant, a set of quantitative measures of the  subjective probability distribution might be obtained. The direct measures will serve as another test of the Model l. More  interesting information might be obtained by comparing the inferred and the direct measures to see if they can be justifiably  assumed to characterize the same subjective probability distribution. RESULTS  Three separate measures were obtained for each subject, his predictions, his estimates, and his three verbal responses to each  bet. Both the estimates and the verbal responses can be used independently to infer a g(P) * for each block of trials.  Two major questions might be asked concerning the analysis of each subject's responses. Are the three measures obtained from  each subject derived from the same g(P) * ? Do the parameters of each of the g(P) *'s, inferred separately from the  predictions (for which only the mode of g(P)* can be inferred), the estimates, and from the verbal responses, approximate the  parameters of the actual stimulus distributions? In terms of Model I the second question actually asks if the subject learns  each of the three stimulus distributions, and if the predictions provided by Model I are confirmed by his responses.  The two questions are not necessarily related to each other. The three measures of central tendency of the g(P)* (i.e., the  mean, mode, and median) might be highly correlated over blocks of trials, but not amenable to an adequate description by Model  I. Much has been written lately about  the accuracy and speed of computers, and about their use for simulation purposes. Undoubtedly, the inference of the parameters  for each subject, the construction of Model II, and the simulation of estimates and predictions of individual subjects would  prove to be a very time-consuming task, if a computer were not available. In addition to the use of computers in analyzing  data we would like to emphasize the value of the computer as a technique for experimentation. In the present study, the  computer succeeded in doing well something that many experimenters do not do very well: taking and giving orders. This was  done quickly, accurately, and reliably. Furthermore, it was the author's impression that the use of a computer for.  instructing the subject for evaluating his responses, and for providing him with immediate feedback, changed something very  basic in the experimental situation. It seemed that subjects were ready to trust and to obey the computer much more than they  would believe a human experimenter. The subjects did not think the computer would make mistakes or cheat them, as they often  think the experimenter does in psychological experiments. This impression was conveyed from the subjects' attitude and  comments while being instructed prior to the experiment, from spontaneous remarks throughout the experiment, and particularly  from an informal talk with each subject after the experiment was completed. As the extent to which this phenomenon might  influence the behavior of decision makers is not known, further investigation is needed, particularly within contexts where  subject-experimenter interrelationships are extensive. For our purposes, by using the computer and thus eliminating the  experimenter from the experimental situation, a further step was achieved toward a better control of extraneous variables. We  would not actually be surprised if it could be shown that in part the consistent results obtained in the present experiment  were due to the use of a computer-controlled task.  Bayesian decision theory predicts that given enough information, subjects would learn the shape of g(P), i.e., that the  subjects' predictions will approach the mode of the stimulus distribution, and that the variance among the subjects will  decrease as more information is obtained. The two predictions were confirmed. The differences between the mean of the  subject's averaged predictions and the theoretical model predicted for each block were insignificant except for the first  block of the first and  370 RAPOPORT  second distribution. The decrease in the variance among the subjects' averaged predictions as a function of getting more  information about a particular distribution was significant for two of the three stimulus distributions. Even the variance of  the variances of the predictions decreased significantly from the first to last block of each stimulus distribution.  The difference between the mean of the subjects' predictions and the theoretical mode for the first block of each distribution  would be expected to be significant, if we made the following two assumptions. First, assume that subjects started the  experiment with a prior distribution that differed from the stimulus distribution. Second, assume that it took the subjects  several trials until they noticed the nonstationarity of the environment and detected the change between the stimulus  distributions. Note, however, that the second shift between the stimulus distributions was noticed very quickly and as a  result, the difference between the mean of the averaged predictions and the theoretical mode for block X was insignificant.  Inspection of Table 2 shows similar results for the estimates and for the verbal responses. The subjects increased their  sensitivity to the nonstationarity of the evironment, and this increase was reflected in their various responses. There are  several factors which could account for the fast shift from the second to the third stimulus distribution. Probably the most  important factor was that the subjects had already learned that the evironment was nonstationary, and they became more  sensitive to this characteristic of the environment. A second possible factor was the small variance of the third stimulus  distribution, and the small overlap between the second and the third stimulus distributions as shown in Fig. 1.  The verbal responses obtained after each block provided another measure for estimating g(P) *. Various bets were used in order  to obtain directly three numerical values of g(P)*. As subjects were not actually paid for equalizing the hypothetical bets,  and as several of them had difficulties in understanding the instructions, problems were anticipated in the interpretation of  these results. However, the location of the median of g(P)* was made quite easily, and followed closely the actual medians of  the proportions of each block. The correspondence between the medians of the first and third quartiles (Q, , Qs) and the  actual quartiles of each block was not so close. Figure 8 shows that the subjects as a group tended consistently to  overestimate the interquartile range. Such an overestimation reflects a g(P) * which is flatter than the actual g(P), i.e.,  which has smaller parameters.  The same tendency to overestimate the variance of g(P) was found with the variances of the subjects, which were inferred from  their estimates. The results are in agreement with the results of Shuford and Wiesen (1959), who found that the inferred  parameters tended to be too small, even after the assumed performance criterion was changed from a squared error criterion to  an all-or-none criterion. Subjects undoubtedly tend to overestimate the variance of the stimulus distribution, though there is  evidence that this tendency decreases slowly as more information about the task is obtained. If we  DECISION-MAKING 371  try to interpret these results in terms of information usage, a subjective probability distribution which is less peaked than  the stimulus distribution indicates that a subject has in fact considerably underestimated the amount of information contained  in the data. If the same subject is faced with a task wherein he has to make a decision, and may, if he wishes, purchase  information which will reduce his uncertainty about the decision, he will tend to purchase too much information. In this  respect, the results showing a tendency to underestimate the parameters of the stimulus distribution are in agreement with  experiments considering information-seeking behavior (Edwards, 1962; Tversky, 1963), which generally show that subjects buy  too much information.  The combined estimates for each block provide a third measure for inferring the parameters of the subjective probability  distributions. Inspection of the individual inferred means and variances indicates that most subjects were learning the three  different stimulus distributions. The individual differences, however, were large and cannot be ignored, a fact that calls for  a model of learning the stimulus distribution which takes into account these individual differences.  Of special interest is the fact that the subjects' estimates tend to track the block-toblock fluctuations in the parameters of  the stimulus distributions. The same tendency is revealed in the subjects' predictions. We would not like to elaborate on this  point, both because the individual differences were large, and because the number of blocks per stimulus distribution was  small. However, the results suggest that the subjects "carry" with them a moderately small memory, and retain only the recent  information. Such behavior might be considered optimal for two reasons. The first reason is concerned with factors such as  limited memory, cost of thinking; and calculations which exceed the subject's capacity. The second reason is that if the  subject is not told explicitly that the environment is stationary and if he realizes that the environment does change, he  should consider only the more recent information in order to adapt his behavior to the changing situations.  These block-to-block fluctuations in the subjects' responses are not accounted for by Model I. Model I is incomplete at this  stage in that it only predicts the asymptotic predictions, estimates, and verbal responses of all subjects. Model I predicts  the responses made when the stimulus distribution has been learned, but it has nothing to say about the learning process which  leads to this asymptotic state. Model II was constructed with the intention of accounting for the trial-to-trial changes in  the prediction and in the estimates of individual subjects. It should be emphasized that the purpose underlying the  construction of Model II was not so much to fit the subjects' data as to get information about the importance of the prior  distribution with which the subjects started the experiment, and about the amount of information used by the subjects in  making their decisions.  Within the framework of Model II, the prior distribution with which a subject starts the experiment is based upon a certain  number, R, of hypothetical random observations obtained from a uniform distribution. The parameter R determines the  372 RAPOPORT  weight given to the past experience and is an individual difference parameter. It is, of course, not necessary that R be based  on a uniform distribution, and actually Neimark and Shuford (1959) offer evidence that the prior distribution is a broad,  unimodal distribution. But until more evidence is obtained, we tend to agree with Shuford (1962), and with Edwards, Lindman,  and Savage (1963), that a uniform prior distribution is the most reasonable assumption to make. The other parameter, K, is  also an individual difference parameter, which determines the weight given to the successive most recent observations. The  individual differences with respect to K proved to be large, particularly for the first stimulus distribution. The individual  differences with respect to R were smaller. Actually it was surprising to find that the value which best fitted the average  predictions and estimates was R = 2. This finding supports the common Bayesian notion that a prior distribution is not  important in situations involving successive decisions. The value of K which best fitted the average predictions and estimates  of the subjects was K = 7. It is of course, realized that K probably will not remain fixed for different individuals and for  different "environments." The value of K is expected to be a function of the variance of the stimulus distribution as well as  of the nonstationarity of the environment, and the rate of shifts between the stimulus distributions. Within Model II, the  value of K was held fixed for the different stimulus distributions and this probably caused the large discrepancies between  the values predicted by the model and the actual predictions and estimates for the second and third stimulus distributions.  An additional comment is warranted concerning the construction of alternative models, not necessarily Bayesian, for predicting  the subject's performance in the above sequential task. The block-to-block tracking, exhibited in the study, and the learning  of the nonstationarity of the environment, shown in the quick detection of the shift between the second and the third  distribution, suggests that such models will need some memory parameters. Probably a short-term memory parameter and a  long-term memory parameter will be required. In addition, interviews taken throughout the experiment or a "thinking-aloud"  technique, which has been used successfully in computer simulation models, might reveal valuable information about subjective  strategies used in performing the task. A simulation model like the one used by Feldman (1961), might be a promising  candidate. A more parsimonious model might replace the assumed g(P)* by some confidence limits upon the modal P based on the  information given in the x most recent trials, and use these limits to derive predictions of the subject's responses. Both  models are currently under investigation.  To summarize, it appears that the Bayesian model does moderately well as a descriptive model in predicting the asymptotic  estimates, predictions, and verbal responses of most subjects. Moreover, the accuracy of the model seems to increase as the  amount of information available to the decision makers increases. These results are in agreement with results of earlier  studies (Shuford and Wiesen, 1959; Shuford, 1960; Wiesen, 1962). The confirmation given to the Bayesian model is particularly  promising,  DECISION MAKING 373  because the model predicted three different responses, which were experimentally independent. The data of each subject  consisted of estimates, predictions, and verbal responses, which corresponded to different parameters of his subjective  probability distribution. More important, the estimates, predictions, and verbal responses were obtained at different times  within the experimental session, and they were based on different amounts of information. Qualitatively, the responses  differed from each other, in that the verbal responses served as a direct measure, whereas the estimates and predictions  served as indirect measures of the opinions of the subject. Considering these differences, the correspondence among the three  different measures, w, v, and Qz , averaged over subjects, is good. Consistency within a subject, which is a necessary though  not a sufficient condition for the application of Bayesian model, was found for most of the subjects. Correspondence between  each measure and the corresponding prediction provided by the Bayesian model is also good, though far from perfect.  ACKNOWLEDGMENTS  The author is indebted to Dr. Lyle V.
