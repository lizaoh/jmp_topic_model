GREEN  Laboratory of Psychophysics, Harvard University, Cambridge, Massachusetts 02138  We state a condition on the moment-generating functions of the random walk model derived by Link and Heath (1975), which is  necessary and sufficient to yield the sequential probability ratio test of Wald (1947) and the reaction-time model proposed by  Stone (1960). A restricted form of this condition can also be obtained by imposing the types of symmetries on the  moment-generating functions considered by Link and Heath (1975) and Thomas (1975). The various conditions are contrasted and  their implications for empirical investigation are discussed.  Wald (1947) showed how the "sequential probability ratio test" (SPRT) could be used as a statistically optimal decision  procedure in sequential analysis. The statistic accumulated over observations is the log likelihood ratio between the two  hypotheses. Stone (1960) applied that model to choice reaction times, characterizing the stimulus effect as an accumulation of  log likelihood ratios. He pointed out that this SPRT model implies that the distribution of times to reach either terminal  decision is the same whether or not the decision is correct or erroneous.  Recently, Link and Heath (1975) used moment-generating functions (m.g.f.s) to derive a very general random walk model for  choice reaction times. The model assumes only that the sensory-decision variable drifts in the correct direction for that  hypothesis. This represents a considerable generalization of Stone's (1960) formulation, and yet clearly contains it as a  special case. A major purpose of this paper is to point out the precise relationships between these two formulations.  Link and Heath (1975) considered several special cases of their general model by imposing certain symmetry conditions on the  m.g.f.s of the sensory-decision variable. Thomas (1975) proved that the combination of these two types of symmetries in the m.g.f.s implies the use of an  SPRT and hence identical distributions of terminal decision times for correct responses and errors.  The result that an SPRT can be obtained when two kinds of m.g.f. symmetry are imposed seems to suggest that the SPRT model is  identical to this very restricted case of Link and Heath's (1975) general random walk model. Such an impression is incorrect.  We show that the SPRT is equivalent to the case of m.g.f.s that differ only by a translation constant. Thus the SPRT can be  obtained from Link and Heath's theory without assuming any type of symmetry. However, when the two types of symmetry are both  imposed, the m.g.f.s can differ only by a translation. In this way, Link and Heath's general model can be constrained to yield  a restricted (symmetric) version of the SPRT.  After developing the necessary notation for the general random walk model in the next section, we discuss the SPRT model and  its relation to the special cases of the random walk model defined by m.g.f. symmetries. The intersection of any two of these  three cases leads to the very restrictive case of the SPRT model discussed by Link and Heath (1975) and Thomas (1975). A final  section considers empirical implications of the various special-case assumptions. Estimates  obtained from (7a, b) can be substituted directly into equations for the four mean response times, since the boundary  parameters are always scaled by k. These extremely convenient relationships can only be achieved by the Case 1 or the Case 2  constraints on the m.g.f.s. Other cases are not likely to be empirically tractable; they will necessarily require much more  complicated procedures for estimating parameters.  288 SWENSSON AND GREEN  Although Case l and Case 2 place equally strong constraints on the random walk model's parameters, the Case 1 SPRT model's  predictions are more vulnerable. Since the SPRT model predicts that the mean time to make a given decision will be the same  whether the response is correct or an error, it is easily tested (and generally rejected) within any set of data. Depending  upon the nature of the stimuli and the task constraints (see Audley, 1973; Swensson, 1972) one can anticipate whether error  responses will be either faster or slower than correct responses; only in special situations are they equal (Green and Luce,  1973).  Testing the assumptions of the Case 2 model is more difficult. First, the speed-accuracy trade (mean decision time versus  error rate) must be the same given So and S1 . Such a determination requires a comparison of differences over several  experimental conditions. Second, there is an explicit relation between the mean difference in times to reach correct and  incorrect decisions in favor of either hypothesis and the decision accuracy. For a decision in favor of So this difference  must be proportional to In[(1 - P)/a]; for a decision in favor of S1 it must show the same proportionality with P and a  interchanged (Link and Heath, 1975, p. 89). Again, this requires comparison over several experimental conditions.  Only a few experiments have measured such empirical relations. Link (1975) presented data demonstrating the predicted increase  in the difference between the mean correct response and error times; however, responses were pooled across stimuli and there  was no test of the equivalence of this relation for So and S1 decisions. Other experiments have not obtained the invariances  predicted by the Case 2 model. Using difficult visual form discriminations and a wide range of payoff conditions, Swensson  (1972) found that the difference in mean times between correct and incorrect decisions changed in sign as a function of  accuracy. In a visual intensity-discrimination task, Luce and Wandell (1975) reported differences in mean times between error  and correct responses as a function of accuracy. Unlike the prediction of a Case 2 model, both subjects show quite different  slopes for each of the two terminal decisions. On the basis of available evidence, it does not appear that either the SPRT  model or the Case 2 model has a wide range of applicability.  Further work is needed, either to develop procedures for estimating the parameters of the general model, or to understand the  stimulus situations in which the special cases  apply.
