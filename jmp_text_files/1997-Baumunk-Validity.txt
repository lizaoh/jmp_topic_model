Validity of Spaces for Assessing Knowledge about Fractions 

Katja Baumunk* and Cornelia E. Dowling[-] 

Technische Universita�t Braunschweig, Braunschweig, Germany 

Using a computer-aided procedure, six experts were queried on prerequisite relationships between 77 learning objectives from the field of fractions. From the judgements of each expert, a knowledge space representing these prerequisite relationships was derived. The experts' knowledge spaces were integrated subsequently into knowledge spaces representing only those prerequisite relationships on which all, or a majority, of the experts agreed. For validating subspaces of these knowledge spaces, test data from 138 pupils were collected. The results from querying the experts and from the validation study were used to discuss the applicability of these knowledge spaces for the assessment of pupils' knowledge. ] 1997 Academic Press 

1. INTRODUCTION 

Fractions are an area of school mathematics which causes particular problems to pupils and teachers in Lower Saxonia, and elsewhere. There is one organizational reason for these difficulties. In Lower Saxonia, fractions are taught to pupils in the 6th grade. In the 7th grade, however, most pupils are transferred to another school, dependent on their abilities. Since the performance of different schools varies a great deal, the pupils' knowledge about fractions at the beginning of 7th grade is, as a rule, extremely heterogeneous. There are also reasons inherent in the field of fractions itself which have been summarized by Padberg (1978): 

v In everyday life, pupils get in touch with fractions less frequently than with natural numbers. 

v Fractions are written in a far more complicated way than natural numbers. 

v A new system of concepts has to be learned. 

v A large number of rules exists for calculating with fractions. 

In an optimal case, the knowledge about fractions of each individual pupil is assessed at the beginning of the 7th grade, and appropriate training is provided immediately if necessary. Such individualized assessment and training is, however, seldom realized in practice. Pupils in the 8th or 9th grade still find fractions difficult.

The theory of knowledge spaces by Doignon and Falmagne (1985) can be applied to developing computeraided procedures which assess knowledge in specified domains individually (Falmagne and Doignon, 1988a, b). In this paper, we apply knowledge space theory to the field of fractions. Our goal is to develop a computerized procedure for assessing knowledge about fractions adaptively. Such a procedure requires a collection of items from the knowledge domain considered. As items we use 77 learning objectives from the field of fractions (Baumunk, 1995; Bruchmann, 1993). For any and�or-graph, it holds that the set of all knowledge states derived from this graph has the structure of a knowledge space (Doignon and Falmagne, 1985). Such a knowledge space has the property of being closed under union. Therefore the prerequisite relationships cannot only be represented by a knowledge space, but also by an equivalent structure, which is called the basis of the knowledge space. It consists of all those elements of the knowledge space (i.e., of all those knowledge states) which can not be obtained as a union of other, different members of this space. For example, the basis of the knowledge space derived from the and�or-graph in Fig. 1 consists of the knowledge states [d ], [e], [ f ], [b, f ], [c, f ], [a, c, e, f ], [a, d, e, f ]. 

For querying experts on prerequisite relationships between the items, we use an algorithm by Dowling (1993) and a computer program introduced in Dowling and Kaluscha (1995). This program presents to an expert standard form assertions of the following form: ``Imagine a pupil who does not master the items p1 , ..., pk . Is it then (practically) certain that this pupil does not master item q either?'' The items p1 , ..., pk constitute the premise, and item q constitutes the consequence of the standard form assertion. Both types of items are a subset or an element of the item set, respectively. The expert accepts or rejects such assertions. The querying program has the property that it does not present redundant standard form assertions to the expert, that is standard form assertions which follow logically from previously accepted or rejected standard form assertions are not presented for judgement. 

In Section 3, we describe the querying of experts on prerequisite relationships between the 77 learning objectives from the field of fractions, and we measure the association between the experts' judgements. If the experts' judgements differ, then we have to determine the prerequisite relationships on which all experts, or a majority of the experts, agree. These prerequisite relationships can also be represented by 

knowledge spaces. The theoretical relationships between these spaces is explained in Section 2. The results of Section 3 will also show how efficient the resulting knowledge spaces are for the assessment of knowledge. Sections 3 and 4 can also be read without the theoretical Section 2. Section 2 helps to understand which of the empirical results are already predetermined by the theory, and which are not. In Section 4, prerequisite relationships between 10 of the 77 learning objectives are validated using the data of 138 pupils. These results indicate how suitable the knowledge spaces are for the assessment. The tradeoff between efficiency and validity is discussed in Section 5.

3. QUERYING EXPERTS 

In this section we describe the querying of experts on prerequisite relationships using a computerized procedure. Our knowledge domain is the field of fractions from which we selected 77 learning objectives. 

3.1. Method 

The standard form assertions presented to the experts can be selected according to different querying strategies. In this paper, we use the strategy that first presents the premises of 

size one for a given consequence (Dowling and Kaluscha, 1995), and stops after the expert has judged all of these standard form assertions with a premise of size one. As a result, we obtain, for each expert, the basis of a knowledge space representing the prerequisite relationships derived from the expert's judgements. For each pair of experts, the association between their prerequisite relationships is measured, and correlations which will be explained below are computed. From the experts' bases we compute new bases of knowledge spaces representing those prerequisite relationships on which all experts, or a majority of the experts, agree. In that manner, we exclude standard form assertions which have been accepted by only one expert, or by a minority of experts. 

3.2. Subjects 

Six experts were queried on prerequisite relationships between the 77 items of the field of fractions. Three experts (experts A, B, and C) are mathematics teachers. The years of their experience in teaching mathematics is shown in Table I. Two experts (experts D and E) are computer scientists with a strong background in mathematics and no teaching experience. The sixth expert is the first author and the experimenter (expert F). 

3.3. Procedure 

The experts were queried in their homes, using their own 386 and 486 PCs, and without a fixed schedule. The quering was terminated after all standard form assertions, with a premise of size one, were judged. This is because the experts did not have more time available.

4. VALIDATING THE EXPERTS' KNOWLEDGE SPACES 

In this section, parts of the experts' knowledge spaces and of the integrated knowledge spaces are validated with pupils' data. 

4.1. Method 

Knowledge spaces and the prerequisite relationships which they represent are validated by comparing pupils' data with the states in the knowledge space. Each pupil's data consist of a pattern of learning objectives which are mastered or not mastered by the pupil. To test the mastery of a learning objective, at least one suitable problem has to be presented to the pupil and his or her answer has to be collected. For comparing a pupil's pattern S of learning objectives with the knowledge space K, we compute the mean and the standard deviation of the minimal distances D(S, K) and the mean similarity coefficient A(S, K) mentioned in Section 2. The latter is an indicator for the agreement between the pupils' data and the knowledge space, and hence for the validity of the knowledge space. The similarity coefficient A(S, K) varies between 0 and 1 where the value 1 indicates maximum agreement. 

4.2. Subjects 

Subjects were 138 pupils, 99 pupils from 6th grade, 22 from 7th grade, and seventeen from 8th grade. The age of the pupils varied from 11 to 15 years; 61 pupils were male and 77 were female. 

4.3. Material 

From the 77 learning objectives, 10 learning objectives were selected, and 10 problems were constructed to test these learning objectives. Three of these 10 items were those learning objectives which we assumed to be the most difficult ones and which had been learned by all groups of pupils tested. To the three items, some of their likely prerequisites were added. We assumed that pupils in this age group could solve these problems in about 20 minutes, and that they would not be able to concentrate any longer. Our distance measure and the similarity coefficient yielded first validity data, but have some serious disadvantages. For a given knowledge space, the mean distances are only a lower bound for the expected number of items which might have been assessed wrongly. Moreover, the students' lucky guesses and careless errors are not taken into consideration. In addition, the distance indicates to what degree a knowledge state can be found which is similar to a given answer pattern. It does, however, not consider those knowledge states which are elements of the knowledge structure but which will never be observed empirically. Note that, for our distance measure, the extreme case of the power set of Q is always the most valid knowledge space. Further research on validity measures is required which takes into account these disadvantages, and the size of the knowledge space. 

5. GENERAL DISCUSSION 

From the theory of knowledge spaces it follows that there is a tradeoff between the validity of the integrated knowledge spaces and their efficiency for the assessment. The smallest possible knowledge space K=[<, Q] is always the most efficient one. Using this structure, the assessment algorithm could decide with only one question to the student whether or not he�she is in the state < or in the state Q. The largest possible knowledge space 2[Q] is always the most valid one, but the assessment procedure would have to test the mastery of every single item, and is, therefore, the most inefficient structure for the assessment of knowledge. 

The experts' data show that the knowledge spaces of the three mathematics teachers and the majority space representing the standard form assertions on which three out of five experts agree are efficient for the assessment of knowledge. Pupils' data indicate, however, that subspaces of these spaces are the least valid ones. These spaces are, therefore, not recommended for the assessment. Subspaces of the knowledge spaces representing only the prerequisite relationships on which all experts agree are the most valid ones. The sizes of these spaces indicate that these spaces are too inefficient and, therefore, not applicable for the assessment. The knowledge spaces representing standard form assertions on which a majority of all but one expert agree are expected to be less inefficient, and the validity of their subspaces is still acceptable. These knowledge spaces can be used for refinement with pupils' data (Cosyn and Thie�ry, 1995). 

Comparing the validity of the experts' knowledge spaces, we see that the mathematics teachers yield more efficient 

but less valid knowledge spaces than the experts without teaching experience. Our interpretation of this result is that the teachers falsely assumed correspondences between the pupils' knowledge and the curriculum. For future research on school mathematics within the knowledge space framework, we recommend considering the judgements of both mathematics teachers and trained mathematicians.
