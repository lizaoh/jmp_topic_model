In this article, I describe the properties and an application of an idealized, short-range, neural integration process: the Poisson shot noise process. This process provides a tractable mathematical model of the fluctuations in the postsynaptic potential induced by a sequence of action potentials impinging on a neuron. The shot noise process is spatially localized in its effects and operates plausibly on a time scale of a few tens of milliseconds. It therefore provides a model of the information integration processes found at the cellular level. I also describe the relationship between this process and neural integration processes occurring on a longer time scale: from several hundred milliseconds to a second or more. This is the time scale of interest to psychologists studying sequential-sampling models of decision-making. Integration on such a time scale is too long to be carried out at the cellular level and may be carried out by neural reverberation circuits implemented by recurrent connections within populations of neurons, as proposed by Wang and colleagues ( Lee & Wang, 2009; Lo & Wang, 2006; Wang, 2001, 2002 ). Such circuits can preserve activity for the time needed to make a decision about the identity of a stimulus. By studying the relationship between short-range and long-range neural integration processes we obtain a novel view of the statistical properties of the processes involved in making simple, speeded decisions.

My investigation of the Poisson shot noise process was stimulated by two recent developments in decision-making research. The first was a series of recent neuobiological studies of decision-making in awake, behaving monkeys. A good introduction to the research in this area is provided by Glimcher et al. ’s edited collection,  Neuroeconomics  ( Glimcher, Camerer, Fehr, & Poldrack, 2009 ). Many of these studies have found evidence for a view of decision-making that is strikingly consistent with the one developed in the choice reaction time (RT) literature in psychology during the past 40 years (see  Smith & Ratcliff, 2004 , for a review). In these studies, recordings were made from single units in structures in the oculomotor control system, such as frontal eye fields, lateral interparietal area and superior colliculus, while monkeys performed a saccade-to-target decision task ( Basso & Wurtz, 1998; Roitman & Shadlen, 2002; Schall, 2002 ). The single-unit firing data from these studies, like human choice RT data, appear to be well described by a model in which decisions are made by accumulating noisy information to a threshold or response criterion ( Gold & Shadlen, 2001; Hanes & Schall, 1996; Mazurek, Roitman, Ditterich, & Shadlen, 2003; Roitman & Shadlen, 2002; Shadlen & Newsome, 2001 ). These studies encourage us to seek an explicit theoretical link between the statistics of behavioral decision-making and the statistics of neural representations.

The second development was a study by Ratcliff and me ( Ratcliff & Smith, 2004 ) on sequential-sampling models of two-choice decision-making. In that study we compared different model architectures and different processes for accumulating information. In particular, we compared model architectures in which evidence for competing responses is accumulated as a single, signed total and those in which evidence for competing responses is accumulated in parallel, as separate totals. The striking and unexpected finding from our study was that model architecture was much less important in determining model performance than was the kind of stochastic process used to model evidence accumulation. Models assuming that evidence is accumulated by a diffusion process provided a better account of the experimental data than did models assuming that evidence is accumulated in other ways. These included Poisson counting processes ( Smith & Van Zandt, 2000; Townsend & Ashby, 1983 ) and truncated-Gaussian sums — the latter as assumed in the accumulator model of Vickers ( Smith & Vickers, 1988; Vickers, 1970, 1979 ). Critically, models assuming diffusive accumulation provided a good account of the shapes of RT distributions for correct responses and errors across a range of experimental conditions. This was so for models assuming a single diffusion process ( Ratcliff, 1978 ), or a pair of independent, racing diffusion processes ( Smith, 2000 ), or a pair of racing diffusion processes with competitive interactions between them ( Usher & McClelland, 2001 ).

A further, more explicit, link between diffusive evidence accumulation and the neural substrate came from two recent studies by Ratcliff, Segraves and colleagues ( Ratcliff, Cherian, & Segraves, 2003; Ratcliff, Hasegawa, Hasegawa, Smith, & Segraves, 2007 ). These studies used diffusion models to link behavioral and neural data from a saccade-to-target decision task. They showed that the parameters of a diffusion model fitted to the monkeys’ choice probabilities and RT distributions accurately predicted the time course of lateralized activity in build-up neurons in the superior colliculus prior to the motor response.  Ratcliff et al. (2003)  found an indirect relationship between neural firing rates and the time course of information accumulation predicted by  Ratcliff ’s ( 1978 ) diffusion model.  Ratcliff et al. (2007)  subsequently found a direct relationship, using the parallel diffusion model studied by  Ratcliff and Smith (2004) .  Ratcliff et al. (2007)  termed this model the  dual diffusion model  to distinguish it from  Usher and McClelland ’s ( 2001 ) leaky, competing accumulator model, in which the accumulating evidence totals inhibit each other.

These various studies beg the question: why diffusion? The picture emerging from these studies is that diffusion processes provide a natural model of the process of evidence accumulation in decision tasks, at both the behavioral and neural levels. Diffusion processes are Gaussian processes, so a loose argument based on the central-limit theorem can obviously be invoked. According to this argument, evidence accumulation in decision-making is performed by a population of independent neural units. The Gaussian nature of the process is a reflection of the pooling of activity across the members of this population. Apart from the questionable assumption of independence, this argument is unsatisfyingly vague. My aim in this article is to try to make it more precise, by attempting to link the local, short-range statistics of neural representations to the macro, long time scale and spatially aggregated, statistics of information accumulation functions.

The main elements of the argument are as follows. The difference between a pair of exponential shot noise processes converges weakly to an Ornstein–Uhlenbeck (OU) velocity process. The statistics (i.e., the mean and variance) of this process are assumed to describe the instantaneous evidence for the identity of the stimulus. Decisions are made, as in other sequential-sampling models of decision-making, by accumulating, or integrating, successive samples of the evidence state over time until a criterion quantity of evidence needed for a response is obtained. Under this interpretation, the accumulating evidence state is described by an integrated OU, or OU displacement, process. The long time scale statistics of the integrated OU process closely approximate those of a Wiener diffusion process. In particular, the asymptotic mean and variance of the integrated OU process increase linearly with time, like those of the Wiener process. As a result, the first-passage-time distributions of the two processes approximate each other closely. The first-passage-time distributions give the predicted RT distributions for the process.

This account provides a novel answer to the question of why RT distributions are well described by a Wiener diffusion process. The answer I suggest is that the underlying information accumulation functions are actually integrated OU processes (or a close approximation thereto), which are obtained by integrating local representations at the neural level over time. These processes are indistinguishable, at the level of resolution found in behavioral data, from the Wiener diffusion process. The following sections make these ideas precise.

After deriving these results and presenting them at the 2004 Society for Mathematical Psychology Annual Meeting ( Smith, 2004 , July), I learned that a key part of the argument, namely, the approximation of an excitatory–inhibitory shot noise pair by an OU velocity process, is well known in the theoretical neurobiology literature, where it is it referred to as the Stein model.  Stein (1965)  originally proposed a shot-noise-type representation for the flux of the neuronal membrane potential as a way to introduce time-dependent decay into a model of an integrate-and-fire neuron. Subsequent authors developed the link with the OU process progressively over the next two decades. This culminated with proofs of weak convergence by  Kallianpur (1983) ,  Kallianpur and Wolpert (1984)  and  Lansky (1984) . A good introduction to this literature is  Tuckwell (1988, ch. 9)  and a more recent treatment is  Gerstner and Kistler (2002) . Because these results appear not to be well known by psychologists or by single-cell recording physiologists with an interest in decision-making, and because I use them in a novel way, I present them here in a self-contained, tutorial form.

The Poisson shot noise process plays an important role in at least one existing psychophysical model, the absolute identification model of  Marley and Cook (1984, 1986) . In their model, an array of shot noise processes is used to model the memory representations of the set of stimulus alternatives in the task. The mean and variance of the shot noise array provide the theoretical basis for predictions of the so-called “bow effect”, that is, the reduction in absolute identification accuracy in the middle of the stimulus range. The Marley–Cook shot noise representation is used as the initial stage in the recent SAMBA model of  Brown, Marley, Donkin, and Heathcote (2008) , which provides a detailed account of RT and accuracy phenomena in absolute identification.

The theory of evidence accumulation I describe in this article should be contrasted to the one proposed in the wave theory of  Link (1992) . Although there are some obvious similarities between the two approaches, there are also fundamental differences. Wave theory seeks to connect Poisson coding of sensory events to a random walk decision model (i.e., the discrete-time counterpart of the diffusion model). In wave theory, the increment to a random walk at any time step is given by the difference between a pair of Poisson random variables. These differences are accumulated over time by a random walk. This process, like other random walk models, is an independent-increments process, in which the increments to the walk at successive steps are uncorrelated with each other. In contrast, shot noise has a nontrivial autocorrelation structure, which arises from the extended temporal persistence of the effects of Poisson-distributed events. Much of the theoretical interest in the shot noise process as a model for decision-making lies in its autocorrelation structure.

The shot noise model assumes that two kinds of processes are involved in the construction of neural representations: the sequence of action potentials emitted by a neuron and the postsynaptic potentials generated by the other neurons that synapse upon it. The action potentials are discrete events, whereas the postsynaptic potentials vary continuously. Also, unlike action potentials, which are all-or-none events, postsynaptic potentials sum spatially and temporally. The frequency with which a cell emits action potentials reflects the aggregated effects of all the excitatory and inhibitory postsynaptic potentials impinging on it at any time. Because postsynaptic potentials decay with time and distance, potentials generated by synapses remote from the cell body have a smaller probability of triggering an action potential than do those generated by synapses located near the cell body ( Kandel, Schwartz, & Jessell, 1991 ). The effects of temporal decay and the effects of randomly varying distances between synapses and the cell body both figure explicitly in the models described here.

I assume that neural representations are simultaneously local and distributed. Representations have this dual aspect because of the dense synaptic interconnectivity in neural pathways. As a result of the synaptic ramification among neurons, stimulus information that is represented in a distributed way, by the firing rates in an array of cells, is also reflected locally, in the flux of postsynaptic potentials in the cell body. Here I try to give an account of the statistical properties of the information carried in such postsynaptic potentials, albeit in a highly idealized way. I ascribe a central role to the postsynaptic potential because the summation of postsynaptic potentials is the most fundamental mechanism for information integration in the nervous system. That is, it is the primary way in which spatially distributed information, coded as a pattern of activity across a neural array, can also be represented locally, as a single, time-varying quantity.

                       depicts the main properties of the class of shot noise models. Evidence for competing decision alternatives is coded by the frequencies of neural firing in opponent channels. Opponent-channel coding is a fundamental principle of neural organization in sensory systems ( Kandel et al., 1991 ). In the visual system, for example, contrast increments and decrements are coded as deviations from the prevailing state of adaptation by ON and OFF channels ( Fiorentini, Baumgartner, Magnussen, Schiller, & Thomas, 1990 ). Indeed, single-cell recordings from eye-movement decision tasks suggests that opponency in some form or other may persist right up to the point of movement planning in the superior colliculus ( Basso & Wurtz, 1998; Ratcliff et al., 2003 ). I therefore assume opponent-channel coding as a basic design principle. In the models I describe here, a channel consists of an array of morphologically and functionally similar neurons that redundantly code a particular stimulus attribute. I discuss how information in opponent channels is combined to make a decision subsequently.

The aggregated effect of the postsynaptic potentials on the body of a neuron within a channel are represented as a Poisson shot noise process ( Daley & Vere-Jones, 2003; Papoulis, 1991; Ross, 1983 ). The shot noise process provides a mathematically tractable way to model the cumulative effect of a large number of randomly occurring “shocks” or perturbations on the state of some physical system. Here, the shocks represent individual postsynaptic potentials impinging on the body of a neuron. Originally, however, the shot noise process was introduced to model electron absorption in vacuum tubes ( Campbell, 1909 ) and it has since been used in a variety of other applications, such as the modeling of seismic activity ( Vere-Jones & Davies, 1966 ).

Mathematically, shot noise consists of three components: a random process that describes when the shocks occur; a probability distribution that describes their amplitudes; and a deterministic function of time that describes the time course of their effects on the system. The two kinds of model architecture just described — random walk or diffusion models and accumulator or counter models — have dominated the choice RT literature since they were first contrasted by  Audley and Pike (1965) . The historical emphasis on these models and the properties that might allow them to be distinguished is unsurprising, as they seem to represent two distinctly different ways in which the brain might use the available stimulus information to make decisions. Random walks assume that information for competing responses is accrued as a single, signed total, whereas accumulators assume that it is accrued as a pair of unsigned totals. However, as noted previously, a recent evaluation of sequential sampling models by  Ratcliff and Smith (2004)  yielded the surprising finding that model architecture was less important in predicting empirical data than were the statistical properties of the processes used model information accumulation.

I therefore take as a basic principle of theory construction the fact that evidence accumulation in behavioral data is well described by a diffusion process or an approximation to a diffusion process. One of my goals is to show how diffusive information at the behavioral level emerges as a natural consequence of the shot noise representation at the neural level, irrespective of the model architecture. I do this in two steps. First, I show that the difference between a pair of high-intensity shot noise processes closely approximates an OU diffusion process. I then make use of the fact that the statistics of an integrated OU process approximate those of a Wiener diffusion process. The steps involved in this approximation are summarized in  Fig. 3 
                      . My conclusion is that empirical data that are well described by a Wiener diffusion may in fact be generated by an integrated OU process. To support this conclusion, I present some predicted RT distributions for Wiener and integrated OU processes that suggest they are unlikely to be distinguishable in behavioral data. Before characterizing these processes in detail, I provide an initial broad-brushstroke outline of the main steps in the argument to give the reader an indication of the terrain ahead.

The results of this section show that, apart from initial transient effects, the mean and variance of the Wiener diffusion process and the integrated OU process behave similarly. It is thus plausible that behavioral data generated by an integrated OU process will be well described by a Wiener diffusion model. Later I give examples of this.

In the preceding section, I showed that the mean and covariance of an exponential shot noise pair were the same as those of an OU velocity process under an appropriate mapping of the parameters. I also showed that the statistics of an integrated OU process grow asymptotically in the same way as do the corresponding statistics of the Wiener diffusion process. Together, these two facts provide a way to begin to link the three levels of analysis of neural systems described by  Smith and Ratcliff (2004) . The first is an account of firing rates within individual cells and how those rates depend on external stimuli. The second is an account of how firing rates in individual cells are combined across cells in a population to provide evidence for a particular response. The third is an account of how information in populations coding competing responses is combined across populations to make a decision. I assume that firing rates in individual cells are modeled as Poisson processes, whose intensities depend on the stimulus. The pooling of stimulus information across cells within a population is represented by shot noise processes and the integration of stimulus information to make a decision–either in a random walk or an accumulator model architecture–is represented by integrated shot noise pairs. This identification provides a link between a point process representation at the level of a sequence of action potentials and diffusive information accumulation at the behavioral level.

In this section, we study the relationship of the exponential shot noise process to the OU (velocity) process in more detail. The reader who is uninterested in technicalities may skip to the following section.

These expressions have the same form as the corresponding expressions for an OU velocity process for both homogeneous and inhomogeneous processes. We now wish to study the limiting behavior of the shot noise process as the Poisson rates become large and the amplitudes of the shocks become small. Convergence of the sequence of moment generating functions (or equivalently, of the sequence of characteristic functions) to that of a limiting process suffices to prescribe the finite dimensions of the limit process (the so-called Cramér–Wold device;  Billingsley, 1986  p. 397,  Karatzas & Shreve, 1991  p. 61). However, it does not suffice to fix the distribution, or law, of the process as a whole. Some statistics of continuous processes, such as their minima and maxima, are not determined by the values of the process on any finite set ( Breiman, 1968 ). To prove convergence of the distribution of the process as a whole to that of a given limiting process we need a stronger form of convergence, somewhat misleadingly known as  weak convergence  ( Billingsley, 1999 ;  Karatzas & Shreve, 1991 ; Ch. 2.4;  Revus & Yor, 1990 ; Ch. 13;  Rogers & Williams, 1994 , Ch. 2.6).

In general, weak convergence in distribution is implied by convergence in finite-dimensional distributions in the presence of an additional property, known as the  tightness  of the sequence of probability measures. This property is equivalent to the requirement that the sequence of probability measures is  weakly relatively compact  (Prohorov’s theorem  Karatzas & Shreve, 1991 , p. 62). As the previous results suffice to show convergence of finite-dimensional distributions, in order to show weak convergence it is necessary to show that the sequence probability measures is also tight. These results extend those of Eq.  (37)  from finite-dimensional distributions to the distribution of the process as a whole. They show that the distributions of a suitably normalized sequence of exponential shot noise processes converge in distribution to a continuous process, the OU velocity process. This result establishes the first part of the connection we are seeking to establish between Poisson shot noise and the OU process, on the one hand, and between the integrated OU process and the Wiener process on the other. We investigate the relationship between the integrated OU process and the Wiener process in the following section.

As alternative models for the physics of diffusion, the Wiener process and the integrated OU process both predict that, at large  
                         t 
                      , the mean and variance of the distribution of sample paths grow in proportion to  
                         t 
                      . However, the fine structure of the sample paths of two processes differ in important ways. The integrated OU process, as the integral of a process that is almost surely continuous, possesses (almost surely) a continuous derivative, whereas the Wiener process does not. In addition, the integrated OU process, unlike the Wiener process, is not a Markov process ( Rogers & Williams, 1994 , p. 54).

The models of decision-making considered here assume that decisions are made by accumulating noisy stimulus information to a response criterion, as in classical sequential sampling models. As in those models, the predicted distributions of RT are given by the first-passage-time distributions of the process through an absorbing barrier that represents the response criterion. It is therefore of interest to know how the integrated OU model behaves in the presence of absorbing barriers and the degree to which its first-passage-time predictions are similar to those of the Wiener process.

Unfortunately, the integrated OU process is far less tractable analytically than the Wiener process and very few closed-form expressions for functionals of its sample paths are known ( Rogers & Williams, 1994 , p. 54). However, one useful result was obtained by  Hesse (1991) , who derived an analytic approximation to the first-passage-time density for an integrated OU process through a single absorbing barrier. Although this is a simpler problem than the one we have been considering here, it is nevertheless interesting, because it provides a way to study the properties of the integrated OU process when a barrier is present. Moreover, the single-barrier solution is of some theoretical interest in its own right, because single-barrier models have been used successfully as models of simple RT ( Emerson, 1970; Smith, 1995 ) and because two-choice models can be formulated as a race between a pair of single-barrier processes ( Smith, 2000 ). The  dual diffusion  model, studied by  Ratcliff and Smith (2004)  and successfully applied by  Ratcliff et al. (2007)  to modeling the neural firing rates in superior colliculus in a saccade-to-target decision task, is formulated as a race between a pair of single-barrier processes. As well as providing a more direct theoretical relationship between accumulating evidence and neural firing rates, the dual diffusion model arguably has greater neural realism than its two barrier counterpart because it models decision-making as a race between a pair of positive evidence totals.

Such a process arises as the limit of an excitatory–inhibitory exponential shot noise pair, as we showed previously. In the following section, I discuss how long-range neural integration can be realized by simple, finite-memory neural processes.

The models described here assume that decisions are made by accumulating noisy stimulus information to a response criterion. These models are like classical sequential sampling models, except that the evidence for competing responses is represented by pairs of exponential shot noise processes. When the Poisson intensities are high, the process approximates an OU velocity process, and its integral, which represents the accumulated evidence, approximates a time-shifted Wiener process. The high Poisson intensities required in this approximation are assumed to arise from the synaptic ramification in the underlying neural population. Even when the rate of occurrence of action potentials in individual axons is comparatively low, a large number of postsynaptic potentials will be generated in the body of each neuron in the population during any interval, because of the number of other neurons that synapse upon it. The resulting models will behave very similarly to sequential sampling models in which information accumulation is represented by a diffusion process ( Ratcliff & Smith, 2004 ).

As noted earlier, however,  Wang (2001, 2002)  pointed out a difficulty in identifying the psychological process of evidence accumulation with neural integration, namely, the difference in the time scales involved. Possible candidate mechanisms for neural integrators include fast AMPA receptors and slow NMDA receptors, which have time constants of 2–3 ms and 50–100 ms, respectively. This contrasts with sequential sampling models, which assume that evidence is accumulated over the period of a second or more needed to make a decision. Wang argued that some other mechanism is needed to bridge these different time scales and suggested recurrent activity in cortical reverberation circuits as a possible candidate.

There is a simple and natural correspondence between recurrent processing architectures and diffusion processes. Providing the feedback connections ramify widely within the neural population, and if the distribution of feedback times is itself random (as a consequence of variable pathway length), then the distribution of spikes within a reentrant loop may approximate a Poisson process much more closely than  Fig. 7 (b) would suggest.

It is beyond the scope of this article to investigate the properties of recurrent populations of spiking neurons in detail. There are many properties of real neurons that are not captured in the simple leaky, integrate-and-fire model, such as refractoriness, which are likely to be important determinants of network dynamics. Nevertheless,  Wang ’s ( 2001; 2002 ) simulations have shown that at least some of the properties of diffusion process models are also exhibited by networks of this kind. This appears to imply that the main features of diffusive information accumulation are robust to perturbations in the fine structure of the underlying process. In light of the limits to the neural realism of the autoregressive model, the main conclusion of this article is best expressed in conditional form: If the long-range neural integration process approximates algebraic integration, then the evidence accumulation function will approximate an integrated OU process. The extent to which this is so is an open question, and one that merits further, detailed investigation.

The advantage of thinking of evidence accumulation as an integrated OU process rather than as a Wiener process is primarily theoretical, rather than computational or data-analytic. As a psychological model of evidence accumulation, the Wiener diffusion model suffers from the same difficulties that beset it as a model of physical diffusion, namely, that it is an infinite velocity process. This means that there is a small, but finite, probability that some proportion of sample paths will reach an absorbing boundary located at any fixed distance from the starting point in an arbitrarily short time. This property is clearly evident in plots of the predicted first-passage-time distributions for the Wiener process, which put finite probability mass in every small interval  
                         
                            [ 
                            0 
                            , 
                            ϵ 
                            ] 
                         
                      . This implies that there is a small proportion of trials in which sufficient evidence for a decision can be accumulated in an arbitrarily short time.

In contrast, the integrated OU process does not suffer from this difficulty. As a finite velocity process, it requires a finite, minimum time to accumulate a fixed amount of evidence. Psychologically, this is a desirable property for a model of evidence accumulation to have. The main difference between the first-passage-time distributions predicted by the Wiener process and by the integrated OU process is in their relative locations on the time axis. In comparison to the Wiener process, the distributions for the integrated OU process appear shifted to the right. In the simulations reported here, integrated OU process distributions with zero time shifts were fitted by Wiener process distributions with positive time shifts of varying size.

The simulations show that the most substantive implication of whether the process is a Wiener process or an integrated OU process is in the interpretation of the diffusion model parameter  
                         
                            
                               T 
                            
                            
                               
                                  er 
                               
                            
                         
                      . In  Ratcliff ’s ( 1978 ) model,  
                         
                            
                               T 
                            
                            
                               
                                  er 
                               
                            
                         
                       is the total time required for all nondecisional processes (encoding and responding). In the integrated OU model, some part of this time is decision time. Estimates of  
                         
                            
                               T

                       obtained from fitting the diffusion model to data are often fairly long (e.g., 300–400 ms,  Ratcliff & Smith, 2004 ), so it is not implausible that some part of this time is actually an irreducible minimum component of decision time. The other estimated Wiener model parameters are simple linear functions of the OU process parameters. Consequently, under the integrated OU interpretation, the Wiener diffusion model would remain an appropriate data analytic tool for drawing inferences about underlying psychological processes.
