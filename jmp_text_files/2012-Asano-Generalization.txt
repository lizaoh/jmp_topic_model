Recently  quantum-like cognitive models  ( Accardi, Khrennikov, & Ohya, 2009; Asano, Khrennikov, & Ohya, 2010; Busemeyer, Pothos, Franco, & Trueblood, 2011; Busemeyer & Trueblood, 2009; Busemeyer, Wang, & Lambert-Mogiliansky, 2009; Busemeyer, Wang, & Townsend, 2006; Cheon & Takahashi, 2006, 2010; Danilov & Lambert-Mogiliansky, 2008, 2010; Franco, 2009; Khrennikov, 2006; Khrennikov & Haven, 2009; Lambert-Mogiliansky, Zamir, & Zwirn, 2009; Pothos & Busemeyer, 2009 ) have attracted a new wave of interest as a consequence of developed research in cognitive psychology and economics. This research is related to the problem of  “irrational decision making”  in games of the Prisoner’s Dilemma type. It was shown that in cognitive psychology some experimental studies produce statistical data ( Conte, Khrennikov, Todarello, Federici, & Zbilut, 2009; Conte et al., 2007; Croson, 1999; Shafir & Tversky, 1992; Tversky & Shafir, 1992 ) which cannot be described by classical probability theory. In particular, the process of decision making generating these data cannot be reduced to the classical Bayesian updating. Therefore a number of quantum-like models of decision making was proposed, see above references.

In this paper we develop a general quantum-like model of decision making. Here  updating of probability  is based on linear algebra, the von Neumann–Lüders projection postulate, Born’s rule, and the quantum representation of the state space of a composite system by the tensor product. This quantum-like model generalizes the classical Bayesian inference in a natural way; see Section  3 . In our approach the latter appears as a special case corresponding to the absence of relative phases in the mental state. By taking into account a possibility of the existence of correlations that are encoded in relative phases we developed a more general scheme of decision making.

In Section  5 , we discuss natural situations inducing deviations from the classical Bayesian scheme in the process of decision making by (macroscopic) cognitive systems. We consider the following situation: a decision-making entity called Alice updates her inference by using certain information, but she does not perfectly believe in its accuracy. The uncertainty, which Alice holds for reliability of the information, is represented in a quantum-like superposition. This mental uncertainty can be either objective (Section  5.1 ) or subjective (Section  5.2 ). In the first case Alice knows that a device delivering information for the updating may deliver it with some probability that information is wrong. Alice does not believe completely in obtained information. In the second case (subjective mental uncertainty) Alice simply has subjective doubts with respect to a mental state used for updating. (We remark that our model is not about updating on the basis of classical probabilistic mixtures: an event preceding updating has occurred with probability

In Section  6 , we discuss the problem of  base rate fallacy . We focus on the base rate fallacy seen in  taxi problem  ( Kahneman & Tversky, 1972 ). It is known that in this problem, people frequently get a false solution with neglection of the base rate; see, e.g.,  Bar-Hillel (1980) ,  Dawes, Mirels, Gold, and Schiavo (1979) ,  Kahneman and Tversky (1973)  and  Pollard and Evans (1983) . However, some researchers have proposed that the base-rate effect has, in fact, its root in the inverse fallacy ( Hamm, 1993; Koehler, 1996; Wolfe, 1995 ). Our model will strongly support the latter proposition, see Section  7  for detailed analysis of the inter-relation of our quantum-like model with the traditional studies presented in the psychological literature. The viewpoint of  Villejoubert and Mandel (2002)  is especially close to our approach. These authors coupled the problem of the inverse fallacy and, hence, the base rate fallacy, to violation of the basic law of classical (Kolmogorovian) probability theory: additivity of probability. In our formalism, “irrational” (non-Bayesian) inference is represented by a quantum-like  bias  operation acting on the mental state. This bias operation mathematically explains the inference by  heuristics  that is beyond the classical Bayesian scheme. This quantum-like representation of irrationality will encourage the prospective research on applications of quantum mechanics to the non-Bayesian inference ( Busemeyer et al., 2011; Busemeyer & Trueblood, 2009 ).

Usage of  subadditive probability  is the key in matching of the quantum-like model with traditional psychological models. We note that violation of additivity of probability is equivalent to violation of the formula of total probability. The latter is the cornerstone of quantum probabilistic approach to the problem of “irrational decision making” ( Busemeyer et al., 2011; Busemeyer & Trueblood, 2009; Busemeyer et al., 2009, 2006; Khrennikov, 1999, 2004, 2006, 2009a,b, 2010; Khrennikov & Haven, 2009 ), and, in particular, to the problem of the inverse fallacy. We remark that, although the authors of this paper stress the role of  violation of the formula of total probability  as an important factor of nonclassicality of statistical data, some top physicists, e.g.,  Feynman and Hibbs (1965)  stressed the role of violation of additivity of probability. Feynman analyzed one of the basic experiments demonstrating the wave–particle duality (the principle of complementarity), the two slit experiment for  interference of quantum particles . Our quantum-like approach can be considered as a special mathematical model describing usage of non-additive probability in psychology. This model is based on complex probability amplitudes, mental state vectors belonging to complex Hilbert spaces. By starting with such amplitudes one will automatically violate the law of additivity of probability and the formula of total probability. In such a situation the classical probabilistic axiomatics ( Kolmogoroff, 1933 ) cannot be applied.

The recent works on applications of the mathematical formalism to psychology and cognitive science generated a debate on the place of quantum-like models in these areas of research. We recall that in physics the quantum formalism can be considered as  an operational formalism  providing a formal description of the correspondence between initial conditions of an experiment (a preparation procedure) and the results of measurements; see  De Muynck (2002) . We remark that the quantum physical community is characterized by diversity of interpretations of the quantum mathematical formalism; see  Adenier, Fuchs, and Khrennikov (2007) . The operational interpretation is just one of possible interpretations. This is the original interpretation of N. Bohr. He motivated the use of the operational approach to quantum physics by impossibility to provide a detailed intrinsic description of physical processes in the microsystems. These systems are extremely sensitive and any intervention (e.g., in the form of observation) would change the state of a microsystem. Therefore the only possibility is to proceed in the operational framework. The analogy between microsystems and cognitive systems is evident. Cognitive systems are also extremely sensitive. We cannot obtain data which is so to say intrinsic (in quantum philosophy the notion “ontic” is in usage), independent of the experimental context. Therefore it is also natural to proceed in the operational framework. And the quantum formalism is one of the most developed operational formalisms. (We hope that this argument would provide a justification of the quantum-like modeling in psychology and cognitive science.) The application of the quantum physical operational approach to mental phenomena has to be done with some reservations. One of the main differences is that the brain is able to perform  self-measurements . In physics an observer is always  an external observer , but the brain (which is a part of a biological organism) is a kind of internal observer. However, it is able to perform  (self)-inspection . This makes mental operational approach more complicated, but at the same time more interesting comparing with physics.

As was mentioned, only a part of physical community agrees that it is  in principle impossible  to find intrinsic reasons of quantum probabilistic behavior (these are adherents of Bohr, Heisenberg, Pauli, Landau, Fock, etc.). Others (adherents of Einstein, De Broglie, Schrödinger, Lande, Lamb, Bohm, …) are sure that, in principle, one can find intrinsic (i.e., observer independent) reasons of the very special quantum behavior. However, (they think) at the moment measurement devices are so coarse that it is impossible to approach the internal level of behavior of quantum systems. Einstein was sure that sooner or later we shall create such advanced measurement devices, but Bohr was sure that it will never happen.

Recently Khrennikov has presented “pragmatic viewpoint” on the quantum operational approach ( Khrennikov, 2010 ). This is not important who was right, Bohr or Einstein? May be there exist intrinsic variables (“hidden variables” by the quantum terminology). However, since it is so complicated to approach them, it is more useful to proceed in the operational approach. It is natural to use the pragmatic operational approach in psychology and cognitive science. Here may be possible to find intrinsic reasons for some special psychological behavior. However, they can be so complicated that it is more useful to simply ignore them.

In particular, in this paper we use the operational representation of bias (inducing irrationality of decision making); in general, bias is represented by a unitary operator  
                         V 
                      . As we can see from Section  6 , this operator can be reconstructed from the experimental statistical data.

We remark that the main reason of the use of the quantum operational formalism is that it  describes context dependent probabilities . Hence, the quantum probabilistic model is just a special contextual probabilistic model. A general theory of contextual probability was presented in  Khrennikov (2009a) . In principle, it also can be applied to psychology. It was found that statistical data collected in psychological experiments need not be described by the conventional quantum model ( Khrennikov, 2009b, 2010 ). Other contextual (quantum-like) probabilistic models have to be applied. In some sense cognitive systems are more complicated from the informational viewpoint than quantum physical systems (which in turn are more complicated than classical physical systems). Cognitive systems can exhibit interference patterns similar to those produced by e.g. photons or electrons ( Acacio de Barros & Suppes, 2009; Busemeyer et al., 2011; Busemeyer & Trueblood, 2009; Busemeyer et al., 2009, 2006; Khrennikov, 2004, 2006, 2009a; Khrennikov & Haven, 2009; Lambert-Mogiliansky et al., 2009 ). However, they can exhibit even stronger interference effects than quantum systems, so called hyperbolic interference ( Khrennikov, 2009b, 2010 ).

The operational viewpoint to quantum theory matches well with the  subjective interpretation of quantum probabilities , so called  Fuchsian interpretation of quantum mechanics ; see  Fuchs (2002) . The subjective interpretation of (quantum) probability is very natural in problems of decision making. May be psychology and cognitive science are the right areas for the use of the  Fuchsian interpretation .

The conventional decision making theory is based on the notion of rationality which is closely related to the  Savage sure thing principle  (SSP) ( Savage, 1954 ), one of the basic principles of modern economics, having a large impact to cognitive psychology and cognitive science; see  Pothos and Busemeyer (2009)  for popular presentations for biologists and psychologists and ( Hofstader, 1983, 1985 ) for detailed analysis.

This principle matches well with our intuition. This is a general principle of  consistency of our decisions . If, e.g., Noboru plans to buy some shares under the condition that an event  
                         A 
                       happens and at the same time he plans to buy these shares even under the condition that A does not happen, then it seems natural for him to buy them even if there is no information whether  
                         A 
                       happened or not. This principle of consistency of decision making is related to the very foundation of classical probability theory,  the formula of total probability ; see  Busemeyer et al. (2009) ,  Busemeyer et al. (2006)  and  Khrennikov (2004, 2006, 2010)  for details. The formula of total probability is the basis of the Bayesian approach to decision making. Therefore violation of SPP (which is equivalent to violation of the formula of total probability) implies impossibility to use the Bayesian scheme of decision making.

We can point to a recent detailed analysis of SSP-rationality ( Gilboa, Postlewaite, & Schmeidler, 2009 ). The authors of this paper rightly couple SSP-rationality with the Bayesian approach to decision making. They criticized the Bayesian scheme of decision making pointing to numerous experiments in cognitive science and psychology demonstrating inapplicability of this scheme. However, the conclusion of  Gilboa et al. (2009) –it is impossible to create a general formal mathematical model of decision making–is not acceptable to us. We generalize the conventional Bayesian scheme of decision making by operating with a priori probabilistic amplitudes, instead of Bayesian a priori probabilities. Thus, opposite to ( Gilboa et al., 2009 ), we claim that, in spite of violations of SSP, the notion of rationality can be formalized.

In this paper, in the same way as in  Busemeyer and Trueblood (2009) ,  Busemeyer et al. (2011, 2009, 2006) ,  Khrennikov (1999, 2004, 2006, 2009a,b, 2010)  and  Khrennikov and Haven (2009) , we coupled irrationality in decision making with violation of the law of total probability. This is very natural from the viewpoint of generalization of the Bayesian approach to decision making, the latter is fundamentally based on the law of total probability. However, as was noted in Introduction, in the literature on foundations of quantum mechanics experts typically emphasized violation of additivity of probability ( Feynman & Hibbs, 1965 ). At the same time the idea that irrational behavior can be mathematically described by models with subadditive probability is very popular in “conventional psychological research”. It is surprising that parallel usage of subadditive probability in quantum mechanics and psychology has not been stressed before. We noted this parallelism and proposed to apply the mathematical formalism of quantum mechanics to describe psychological models with subadditive probability. In this framework the following questions naturally arise:

Can any psychological model based on subadditive probability be embedded into the mathematical formalism of quantum mechanics?

If the deviation from the additivity law is relatively small, then data can be represented (as in the conventional quantum mechanics) by complex probability amplitudes. If this deviation is relative large, then it is impossible to proceed with complex amplitudes; statistical data is represented by hyperbolic amplitudes ( Khrennikov, 2009a ). We remark that data from cognitive psychology can violate the additivity law very strongly, i.e., conventional quantum mechanics over the complex numbers is too restrictive to describe all subadditive models created in mathematical psychology ( Khrennikov, 2009b ). However, by considering generalized hyper-complex amplitudes we can describe all psychological models with dichotomous observables. (We note that this case of dichotomous observables–in the form of questions with answers “yes” or “no”–is very important for cognitive and, in particular, psychological applications.) Already in the case of triple-valued observables the situation is more complicated. Here one can find statistical data violating the law of total probability (and its additivity) which cannot be represented neither by complex nor hyperbolic probability amplitudes ( Nyman & Basieva, 2011 ). Hence, in general the approach based on subadditive generalizations of probability is more general than the quantum approach based on representation of mental states by vectors in complex (or hyperbolic) Hilbert spaces and observables by Hermitian operators. We presented a quantum-like model of decision making generalizing classical Bayesian updating. This model is based on the representation of mental states in a complex Hilbert space and quantum information rules for the mental state processing: linear algebra, the projection postulate, Born’s rule, and the quantum representation of the state space of a composite system by the tensor product. We illustrated this model by examples in which the use of the quantum-like scheme is natural and cannot be reduced to the classical Bayesian scheme. As seen in the experiment by Shafir and Tversky, there is an experiment such that the value of posterior probability from its statistical data is not “rational”.
