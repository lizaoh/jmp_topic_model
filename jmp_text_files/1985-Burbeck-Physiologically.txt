INTRODUCTION  On the basis of simple reaction time to the offset of a weak tone in noise, Burbeck and Luce (1982) argued that detection  resulted from the independent and parallel action of two types of detectors. The one, called a level detector estimates the  intensity of a signal. The other, called a change detector, is highly responsive to changes in the signal level, but is  unresponsive to a constant intensity signal. These findings are consistent with other evidence for parallel change and level  detection in the auditory system (reviewed below). The purpose of this paper is to attempt to account for such simple reaction  time data with a model change and level detector.  Model detectors have been proposed by various investigators. Level detector models, i.e., models which depend upon some sort  of estimate of the intensity of the stimulus, are most common: McGill (1967) examined counting models; Luce and Green (1972)  investigated timing models; Duifhuis (1973), Robinson (1974), Viemeister (1977), and Zwislocki (1969) proposed leaky  integrator models. Random walk models (Emerson, 1970; Link & Heath, 1975; McGill, 1963; Stone, 1960; Wald, 1947) represent a  different class of model that is related to, but different from a level detector (Burbeck & Luce, 1982). This paper reports on the investigation of an offset detector, i.e., a change detector that responds to decrements in the  intensity of an auditory stimulus. This detector is explicitly modeled after a post inhibitory rebound (PIR) neuron. PIR units  spontaneously produce output spikes soon after a decrease in the rate of arrival of inhibitory input spikes. Such neurons are  plausible candidates for the "off' units found at many levels of the auditory pathway from the cochlear nucleus to the cortex.  Level detector models are usually simple because the auditory stimuli is initially encoded according to level (intensity) at  the cochlea. Change detection is inherently more complex because it involves more than one level estimate. Complexity is  further compounded because, as Burbeck and Luce (1982) argue, both a change and a level detector are required to account for  simple reaction time data. Because complex models usually contain many assumptions and free parameters, knowledge of the  physiology of single units is used here to guide the construction of the model and to place useful restrictions on many of its  otherwise free parameters. Physiological considerations also lead to an experiment that further restricts the freedom of many  of the parameters.  Before describing the model and reporting the experiment, let us review the psychophysical data that bear on change and level  detectors, and outline the physiological findings that are taken into account in the construction of the model.  EVIDENCE FOR LEVEL AND CHANGE DETECTORS  Physiological Background  The extensive work by Kiang and his coworkers (e.g., 1965, 1967, 1969) and by Rose, Brugge, Anderson, and Hind (1967, 1971)  has provided detailed descriptions of the response of single auditory nerve fibers to tone stimuli. For constant intensity  signals the activity of single fibers can be modeled to a first order approximation as a renewal process (Siebert, 1968, Luce  & Green, 1974) in which the rate parameter (i.e., the reciprocal of the expected IAT) depends upon signal intensity: stronger  signals give rise to larger rates. Intensity is directly encoded by the firing rate of those auditory nerve fibers that are  responsive to the tone. Thus level estimation amounts to estimating the rate parameter whereas detection of changes is akin to  estimating the derivative of that rate.  At the cochlear nucleus the auditory nerve fibers fork and terminate on a variety of units which perform the parallel  extraction of several types of information about the stimulus. Among the types of unit found at this stage are the "on" units  (Pfeiffer, 1966; Moller, 1969), and the "off' units (Gerstein, Butler, & Erulkar, 1968; Evans & Nelson, 1973). The "on" units  respond to the onset of a tone with a short  108 STEPHEN L. BURBECK  burst of spikes returning thereafter to an inactive level despite the continuance of the tone. The "off" units show a  suppression of activity that begins at the onset of the tone, lasts for the duration of the tone, and is followed by a burst  of spikes shortly after the offset. "On" and "off' units can also be found in the superior olivary complex (Goldberg et al.,  1964; Kiang et al., 1973), the inferior colliculus (Erulkar, 1959); Rose et al., 1963; Bock et al., 1972), the medial  geniculate (Galambos, 1952; Aitken & Prain, 1974), and the auditory cortex (Goldstein et al., 1968). They are also found in  the visual pathway, c.f. Enroth-Cugel & Robson, 1966; Hoffman et al., 1972). This suggests that the extraction of information  about changes in intensity is a physiological primitive which should be reflected in psychophysical models. The fact that  "off" responses are seen to originate in single units whose input is the comparatively simple code generated in the cochlea  suggests that it is reasonable to try to model a change detector as a single neuron operating on a very simply encoded  representation of the signal.  Psychophysical Evidence  The earliest psychophysical evidence for change detectors in the auditory system comes from the choice detection data of  MacMillan (1971, 1973) which show a sensitivity to changes in intensity at signal onset and/or offset and a relative  insensitivity to signal duration. Other evidence is supplied by Ahumada et al. (1975) who found that detection of a weak 100  ms signal centered in a 500 ms burst of wide band noise was negatively correlated with the amount of noise energy that  happened to occur just prior to the onset of the tone and lay within a 50 Hz band centered at the signal frequency. This is  counter to the result expected from a level detector but is consistent with a change detector. Jesteadt et al. (1976) report  an auditory analog of the Craik-O'Brien-Cornsweet illusion in vision. If the intensity of a tone is slowly decreased by 10 dB  (the rate can vary from 0.8 to 0.03 dB/ms), is then abruptly increased by 20 dB, and is finally ramped down (at the same rate)  to the original level, one has the illusion that the final level is louder than the initial level. The illusion is reversed if  the slow changes are increases and the abrupt one a compensating decrease. Just as a spatial change detector is thought to  underlie the visual illusion, a temporal change detector is thought to underlie the auditory illusion.  Adaptation experiments by Regan and Tansley (1979) and Tansley and Regan (1979) provide a different sort of evidence for  separate channels for increasing and decreasing intensity. They found that adapting to a tone of decreasing intensity elevated  threshold for a test tone of decreasing intensity but not for a tone of increasing intensity and vice versa. They account for  their data by postulating separate channels for increasing and decreasing intensity. To a first approximation, these channels  appear to operate independently.  Auditory simple reaction time data (Burbeck & Luce, 1982) provide further evidence for both types of detector. These data are  in the form of distributions of simple reaction times to the offset of weak tones masked by wide band noise. Level Detector: Leaky Integrator Model  The primary focus of this paper is the change detector. Rather than model the level detector in detail, we describe it  directly in terms of its hazard function. Prior to the signal change, the hazard function of the level detector is assumed to  be zero. At the change, it rises exponentially to an asymptotic level a, with a time constant of r, i.e.,  h(t) = 0 for t < to,  t,<t,  where to is the time of the change. This sort of behavior could arise from a leaky integrator followed by a threshold detector  as has often been proposed for level detection (e.g., Zwislocki, 1969; Duifhuis, 1973). In the case of offset experiments, the  detector would respond whenever the output of the leaky integrator first fell below an appropriately set threshold. With  stochastic input, such a detector could not have a false alarm hazard rate of zero, but for the present purpose the assumption  is not critical.  Summary of the Model  The complete model for decision latencies involves three submodels: the model for the neural input to the decision process,  the level detector model, and the PIR model. The input is modeled by a Poisson process with a rate parameter it during the  foreperiod and v after the offset. Each event in the Poisson process is taken to be a neural spike that has an inhibitory  impact on the PIR model and also increments the rebound variable. The PIR model "detects" the offset at the first instant in  which the membrane potential exceeds the threshold. The level detector is defined by its hazard function which rises from zero  to an asymptote a with a time constant i. The level and change detectors operate independently and in parallel therefore the  hazard function of the combined detection process is the sum of the change and level detector hazard functions (see Burbeck &  Luce, 1982). They must be constrained, where possible, by consideration of their physiological interpretations. On those grounds four may  be set a priori (Thompson, 1967):  480 29 1-8  112 STEPHEN L. The best information about a pure tone stimulus is confined to a (perhaps small) subset of auditory nerve fibers which  should remain unchanged for tones of the same frequency that differ little in intensity (e.g., 2 dB). Therefore whatever PIR  unit, or set of such units, receives the most useful information in one experimental condition can be assumed to do so in  another condition as long as the stimulus intensity is changed only slightly. This implies that the four as yet unconstrained  PIR parameters should remain fixed despite small changes in stimulus intensity.  Arguments for fixing parameters of the level detector are more speculative since no physiological model has been specified for  it. Yet it is not implausible to assume that its time constant i is, like those of the PIR neuron, independent of small  changes in intensity. Moreover, it turns out that the ability of the model to fit the data is not terribly sensitive to the  choice of i which was therefore fixed at 550 ms for all distributions. The asymptotic hazard rate will, of course, change as a  function of stimulus intensity: more intense stimuli must give rise to larger values of a.  Finally, consider the Poisson model for the encoded auditory stimulus. The postoffset Poisson rate parameter should depend  only on the intensity of the background noise level which is constant for all conditions and on internal noise which is  constant for each subject. Moreover, early work with the simulation of the PIR model showed that the model was relatively  insensitive to the absolute values of ,u and v; what mattered most was the signal to noise ratio u/v. I therefore fixed v at  10 spikes/s. Only p is manipulated freely.  In sum then, we assume that the only parameters which are affected by the intensity of the tone are the foreperiod Poisson  rate parameter h and the level detector asymptotic hazard rate a. All remaining parameters should not be a function of  stimulus intensity. Once the proper values for these intensity independent parameters have been found, the model (if it is  viable) should be able to account for different stimulus conditions by varying only p and a.  The model is confronted with data from three closely linked stimulus conditions. Two of the three are the offset of weak tones  in noise. As was shown in Burbeck and Luce (1982) a 2 dB difference in the stimulus intensity produced surprisingly large and  distinctive changes in the reaction time distributions: the mean and the variance of the distributions were dramatically  smaller for the more intense stimulus, and the hazard functions were markedly more peaked. Nonetheless, by the arguments given  above, the two rather different distributions should be fit by  PHYSIOLOGICALLY MOTIVATED MODEL 113  changing p and a alone (once the proper values for the remaining parameters have been found). The model should also fit a  third distribution obtained with a decrement stimulus wherein the intensity decreases from that of the stronger of the two  tones used in the offset experiments to that of the weaker of the tones (i.e., a 2 dB decrement). In the case of the decrement  data, there is only one free parameter a because each of the Poisson rate parameters will have been estimated by fitting the  model to the corresponding offset distribution.  THE EXPERIMENT  Method  The reaction time paradigm uses weak pure tones in an exponentially distributed random foreperiod design (mean foreperiod of  three seconds). The details of the method and the arguments favoring such a design are given in Burbeck and Luce (1982); only  the differences are noted here. The signals were 3000 Hz tones in wideband noise (No= 10 dB SPL, 0 to 10 KHz). That frequency  was selected because the earlier data gave some hint that the level detector is somewhat less effective at higher frequencies  and our primary interest is in the change detector. The intensity of the tones in the two offset conditions was set to provide  a signal-tonoise ratio (PIN,) of 23 and 25 dB. Therefore in the decrement experiment the intensity was P/No = 25 dB during the  foreperiod and PIN, = 23 dB after the decrement.  Trial by trial feedback and payoff procedures were slightly modified to provide more useful information to the observer.  Feedback information was provided by two lights-hence two bits of information could be given for each trial. Burbeck and Luce  (1982) used these two bits to indicate which of four equal length periods contained the subject's response. But, because  reaction time distributions are quite skewed, responses tended to cluster in the earlier bands. Information is maximized when  the four signals are used equally, so later bands were lengthened by picking a minimum deadline d and a multiplicative factor  a > 1 such that the bands were bounded by d, ad, azd, aid, and a'd. Alpha was adjusted in practice sessions until each  feedback signal was used about equally often. PIR Model Simulation and Parameter Estimation  For each of the three observers the data were fit as follows. The values of a were estimated directly from the slopes of the  tails of the log-survivor functions under the assumption that the level detector is responsible for all of the asymptotic  hazard rate. The remaining parameters are estimated by jointly fitting the two offset distributions. The two Poisson rate  parameters that describe the encoded auditory stimulus prior to the offsets are all that differ for the PIR model in the two  conditions: h, determines the 25 dB distribution and ho determines the 23 dB distribution. The decrement data does not take  part in the PIR and Poisson parameter estimation; the parameters that produce the best joint fit to the offset data are  expected to fit the decrement data without further change.  Parameter estimation was done by computer simulation. For each choice of parameter values the predicted PIR reaction time  distribution was obtained by simulating 500 trials in which the decision latency of the model was determined for each of the  two offset stimuli. Details of the simulation methodology are given in Burbeck (1979). The hazard function of the level  detector was then added to both simulated distributions and the resulting log-survivor functions compared with the data. The  error term to be minimized was the sum of the squared errors from both distributions. Parameters were adjusted manually until  the fit was reasonably good and then an automatic trial and error search process was allowed to run until  PHYSIOLOGICALLY MOTIVATED MODEL 115  further improvement in fit was negligible. It should be noted, however, that in such a complex model, the error surface may  have multiple local minima. The best fit may not have been found.  RESULTS AND DISCUSSION  Table 2 gives the parameters used to fit the data. Figure 2 presents the censored and deconvolved data in log-survivor  function form. The solid lines in that figure show the simulation results from the model. It is obvious that, when the model  is constrained to fit three distributions with a fixed set of decision parameters, the fit to each individual distribution  leaves something to be desired. Chi-squared tests confirm that the model fails to fit the data. Yet the joint fits are not so  bad as to cause us to completely reject the model unless we could be sure that all the a priori parameter assignments and  simplifying assumptions were exact. Certainly the gross qualitative aspects of the data are accounted for, which is a  considerable achievement given the restrictions that have been placed upon the parameters and the required joint fit to the  data. But if the asymptotic detection hazard rate is indeed due to a level detector, then one  might expect to find a simple relationship between that hazard rate and the estimated rate parameter y of the input Poisson  process. The function turns out to be not only monotonic, as would be expected from any reasonable level detector, but nearly linear  with a Pearson r = 0.988. The  asymptotic hazard rate of the level detector was estimated directly from the asymptotic rate of the tail of the reaction time  distributions. This is a straightforward empirical result that depends upon only one assumption: that the change detector  contributes negligibly to long decision latencies. The divergence is worst where the rate parameters are lowest (i.e., for  weak signals). This suggests that the problem is with the level detector which is primarily responsible for detections when  the tone is very weak (Burbeck & Luce, 1982). Thus the divergence may be due to a poor choice of T, the time constant of the  level detector which was rather arbitrarily fixed at 550 ms. It may also be due to the calculation of a from p via the  regression equation obtained from all three subjects inasmuch as the slope for subject SB seems to be larger than the  regression slope. At the high intensity end of the curve we see a different sort of divergence; the model seems to saturate,  i.e., to require an inordinate increase in ft to mimic the effect of a 2 dB increase in intensity. This would place too heavy a burden on the PIR model. Or it could be the case that the larger  decreases in intensity that are involved with the offset of more intense stimuli give rise to momentary undershoots in the  post-offset rate parameter (Kiang, 1965) which the simple Poisson model for the input spike rate is not duplicating. Such an  undershoot would not only aid the change detector, but also allow the level detector to produce more short latency detections.  CONCLUSIONS  The evidence one can bring to bear on the model is mixed. On the one hand, the imperfect fit of the predicted distributions  and of the predicted relationship of MRT to coefficient of variation shows that the model cannot be accepted in its entirety.  One or more of the a priori assumptions or parameter assignments are wrong or else the curve fitting process did not provide  the best possible parameter estimates.  PHYSIOLOGICALLY MOTIVATED MODEL 119  On the other hand, the model does provide fairly good fits to the data particularly in light of the fact that the fit is  obtained with very few free parameters. And the results in Fig. 4 suggest that the behavior of the model remains reasonably  consistent with data from a range of intensity substantially beyond that used in the parameter estimation. To the extent that  the model can be considered to have succeeded, it provides a first link between observed physiological responses to changes in  intensity and observed psychophysical behavior.
