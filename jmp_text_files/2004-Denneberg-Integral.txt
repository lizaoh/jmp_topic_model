In decision making, many classical representations of preferences are cardinal (typically expected utility, or more elaborated models as Choquet expected utility based on monotone measures). They deal with utility functions which are real-valued, and use standard operations of arithmetic such as addition and multiplication. These models have been studied at length and are widely used (see e.g.  Wakker, 1989  for a general survey).

However, it is not always easy nor desirable to deal with cardinal utility functions. A first noticing is that ordinal information is easier to get than cardinal one, and moreover, there are many situations where only order is relevant, cardinals being merely used by tradition and convenience. More fundamentally, the dominant viewpoint in economics, introduced by  Hicks and Allen (1934) , is that utility is related to observable choices (revealed preferences), and any introspective judgments on intensity of preference should be discarded since meaningless. As a consequence, if the only purpose of utility is to explain choices, then utility is ordinal in nature (see a discussion on ordinal vs. cardinal representations in the light of choice-based vs. choiceless utilities in  Abdellaoui, Barrios, and Wakker (2002) ). Another domain where ordinal representation is central is social choice theory (see e.g.  Sen, 1986 ).

A second topic of importance when dealing with scales underlying utility functions is whether they are bipolar or unipolar. Researches in psychology, in particular conducted by Slovic and his followers, have shown that our way of evaluating, scoring, and making decision is guided by  affect , which means “ the specific quality of  “ goodness ”  and  “ badness ”, (1)  experienced as a feeling state  ( with or without consciousness )  and  (2)  demarcating a positive and negative quality of a stimulus ” ( Slovic, Finucane, Peters, & MacGregor, 2002 ). This  bipolar  flavour of the affect, i.e. built on two opposite poles (good/bad, positive/negative) is most often represented by a single bipolar scale, ranging from the most negative to the most positive affect, with a central neutral level which is felt as being neither good nor bad ( Osgood, Suci, & Tannenbaum, 1957 ). There exist in decision theory classical cardinal models that take into account the bipolar nature of human behaviour: these are prospect theory, and the more general cumulative prospect theory (CPT) based on Choquet integral ( Harless & Camerer, 1994 ;  Kahneman & Tversky, 1979 ). Our concern in this paper is to provide mathematical tools able to deal with purely ordinal information, taking into account the possible bipolar nature of scales, so as to keep as much as possible properties and structures of their cardinal counterparts. A preliminary work of the authors along this line can be found in  Denneberg and Grabisch (2001) . In some parts, we will have a more general view, by considering partially ordered ordinal scales (in fact having the structure of a lattice), which are of interest in situations where incomparable levels exist (such as “medium” and “don't know”). This will be achieved in several steps, as described at the end of this section, the final aim being to provide an ordinal counterpart of Choquet integral and symmetric Choquet integral (or more generally of  (1) ), hence a general functional computing the aggregated or average value of a function. This should permit to develop ordinal expected utility and ordinal CPT, as well as other domains of decision making concerned with ordinal information (e.g. multicriteria decision making, social choice). We will see that these new integrals will naturally be interval valued, thus leading to possible incomparabilities between alternatives or acts, hence to incompleteness of the preference relation. This reflects the fact that for a human decision maker, incomparable alternatives do exist ( Aumann, 1962 ).

Of course, there already exist several concepts for computing an expected value (or more generally average, aggregated value) on ordinal scales. The oldest and best known selects a certain quantile, say the median, of a sample as the average value. Next  Fan's (1944)  metric on the space  L 
                      0 ( μ ) of  μ -measurable functions is essentially ordinal. More recently and independently  Sugeno (1974)  developed his integral which employs the same idea as Ky Fan. We will provide a common general framework embedding these three examples. The Sugeno integral, which can be said to be the ordinal counterpart of the Choquet integral, has already served as a basis for a qualitative decision theory, with axioms similar to Savage's ones ( Dubois, Prade, & Sabbadin, 2001 ). Other works related to an ordinal approach of aggregation can be found in  Denneberg (1999)  and  Koshevoy (2002) .

We begin by recalling elementary definitions of lattice theory (see e.g. ( Birkhoff, 1967 ;  Grätzer, 1978 ). A set  L  endowed with a reflexive, antisymmetric and transitive relation ⩽ q  is a  lattice  if for every  x , y ∈ L , a unique least upper bound (denoted  x ∨ y ) and a unique greatest lower bound  x ∧ y  exist. The lattice is said to be  complete  if this property extends to any (possibly uncountable) subset  X  of  L . The top (resp. bottom) of  L  is the greatest (resp. the least) element of  L , and always exist when the lattice is complete. The lattice is said to be  linear  or  linearly ordered  or totally ordered if ⩽ is a total order on  L . The lattice is  distributive  if  x∨( y∧z)=(x∨y)∧(x∨z)  holds for any  x , y , z ∈ L . It is  completely distributive  if distributivity holds for an arbitrary number (possibly uncountable) of arguments replacing  y , z .

Probability measures are monotone and assume only nonnegative values. We maintain this view in our ordinal context. In the cardinal theory of monotone measures there is a hierarchy of important subclasses: supermodular (or convex, 2-monotone) measures ( Denneberg, 1994 ), totally monotone measures (belief functions  Shafer, 1976 ), lower chain measures ( Denneberg, 1999 ), necessity measures ( Dubois & Prade, 1988 ), ( σ -)additive measures and the hierarchy of the respective dualisations. In the purely ordinal context among these only the chain measures and necessity (resp. possibility) measures survive. A continuity property will also be defined in our ordinal environment.
