At the very beginning, Knowledge Space Theory (KST;  Doignon & Falmagne, 1985, 1999; Falmagne & Doignon, 2010 ) was developed under a purely behavioral perspective. KST was especially targeted at establishing formal representations of individual knowledge that could be valid alternatives to the dominant, and perceived as unsatisfactory numerical measure of knowledge. Therefore, in KST the knowledge of an individual is represented by the so called  knowledge state  
                      
                         K 
                       of a student, that is the collection of all the items  
                         q 
                       in a  knowledge domain  
                      
                         Q 
                       he or she proved to master. The collection  
                         K 
                       of all the states that can be observed in the population is the  knowledge structure  and it represents the core deterministic concept of the theory. The answering behavior of an individual, in the first phase of the development of the theory, was considered only from a behavioral point of view, and no cognitive considerations were conducted aimed at explaining the observed responses. The question of establishing which skills underlie the solution behavior of an individual was simply ignored in early KST.

The concept of a skill has been introduced in KST to allow the theory to go beyond a purely behavioral interpretation only at a later stage of its development ( Doignon, 1994; Düntsch & Gediga, 1995; Falmagne, Koppen, Villano, Doignon, & Johannesen, 1990; Heller, Anselmi, Stefanutti, & Robusto, 2017 ). In KST, given a domain  
                         S 
                       of  skills , the  skill assignment  is represented by the so called  skill map . The concept of a skill introduces the possibility to provide a cognitive interpretation to the solution process of items. Each skill

                            S

In recent years a particular kind of knowledge structures received particular attention. It is characterized by two properties called respectively  forward gradedness  and  backward gradedness  ( Spoto, Stefanutti, & Vidotto, 2012, 2013; Stefanutti & Spoto, 2020; Stefanutti, Spoto, & Vidotto, 2018 ), and their relevance is due to the fact that the basic local independence model (BLIM;  Falmagne & Doignon, 1988a, 1988b ) applied to  forward-graded  (FG) or  backward-graded  (BG) structures systematically presents unidentifiability issues ( Heller, 2017; Spoto et al., 2012, 2013; Stefanutti, Heller, Anselmi, & Robusto, 2012; Stefanutti et al., 2018 ).

Spoto et al. (2012)  established a precise connection between FG and BG knowledge structures, and the characteristics of the skill map delineating them. In this way it was possible, by looking at the specific properties of a given skill map, to establish whether the delineated knowledge structure would have been FG and/or BG in some items, and therefore unidentifiable. Drawing upon some of the theoretical results contained in that article, the present theoretical note aims at providing necessary and sufficient conditions for a skill map to delineate a FG and/or BG knowledge structure. The latter is a closure space if the model applied to define states from skills is conjunctive, and it is a knowledge space in the disjunctive case. In the next section, some basic concepts about skill maps are introduced, while the core theoretical results are introduced in Section  3 .

The importance of establishing precise properties of a skill map that lead the delineated knowledge structure to be FG and/or BG is particularly relevant given the identifiability problems of these classes of structures. The present theoretical note provides rules that are easy to check in order to, eventually, adjust a skill map in order to delineate structures which do not present such gradedness. Nonetheless, the present research leaves unsolved some important issues that should be managed by future research.

First of all, in this note we focused on the conjunctive and disjunctive models for delineating, respectively, FG and/or BG knowledge spaces and closure spaces. An important and interesting object matter for future research would be the definition of more general conditions for detecting the properties of skill multi maps and skill functions that delineate FG and/or BG structures via the competency model.

Another interesting perspective for future research is the specific competence structure that generates the performance structure. In the present research we assumed a competence structure coinciding with the powerset on the set  
                         S 
                       of skills. Therefore, we assumed independence among the skills, meaning that any possible subset of  
                         S 
                       can be the competence state of an individual. Nothing has been studied for the case in which precedence relations exist among the skills and, therefore, the competence structure is no longer  
                         
                            
                               2 
                            
                            
                               S 
                            
                         
                      , but a subset of it. Whether the presented conditions stay true in this different situation must be verified. More in detail, we must stress that the powerset  
                         
                            
                               2 
                            
                            
                               S 
                            
                         
                       is both FG and BG in every skill  
                         
                            s 
                            ∈ 
                            S 
                         
                      . This may not be true anymore with a different competence structure, and the effects of this modification must be clarified.

An important result of the present theoretical note is that we established a precise link between the deterministic properties of the skill map and the deterministic properties of the knowledge structure leading to the local unidentifiability of the BLIM. Of course, forward and backward gradedness are not the only causes of unidentifiability of the BLIM. In this respect, the possibility of defining the properties of a skill multimap delineating an unidentifiable knowledge structure (without any particular closure properties) represents a challenging task for future research.
