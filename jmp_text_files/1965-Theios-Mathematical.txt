It  was found that overtraining systematically retards reversal learning. With successive reversals, speed of reversal learning  systematically increases and approaches a limit of one-trial learning. After 220 reversals, one-trial reversals occurred more  than 80 % of the time. The data were compared with quantitative predictions from a number of linear and Markov learning  models. It was possible (a) to reject classes of models which were inadequate and (b) to relate changes in the values of  parameters of the better models to amount of overtraining and number of reversals.  The purpose of the present research is to (a) determine the mathematical structure of reversal learning data and (b) to relate  changes in the values of parameters of specific mathematical models to number of overtraining trials and number of successive  reversals. By considering a number of formally stated models in relation to the data, it is possible to reject classes of  models which are obviously inadequate to describe the learning process as realized in the present experiments. Of particular  interest is the question of whether reversal learning in a simple situation is grossly discrete or approximately continuous. THEORETICAL INTERPRETATION OF THE DATA  The equal-alpha linear operator model (Estes, 1950; Bush and Sternberg, 1959) has often been used to describe the results of  learning experiments. According to the model, the probability of an error on trial n, qn , is equal to a constant factor, a,  times q,_1 . Thus there are two parameters to the model, qi and a. One estimate of qt is the proportion of errors on trial 1  of an experiment. In the present reversal study, all 100 subjects made an error on trial 1 of the reversal. Thus, ql is taken  to equal 1.0 for all groups in the experiment. However, with ql = 1.0 for all groups, the value of a must increase with  overtraining since, empirically, reversal performance decreased with overtraining. With a increasing and qi constant, the  equal-alpha model predicts that errors before the first success, total errors, and trial of the last error should diverge with  overtraining. The fact that the empirical values for these statistics are essentially parallel is sufficient to reject the  equal-alpha model under the assumption that ql is constant and equal to 1.0. As an additional test, estimates of alpha were  determined from the obtained mean total errors, and the expected probability distribution of errors before the first success  was predicted. The difference between the predictions and the data points were large and statistically significant. MARKOV MODELS  A major alternative to the operator-type models is that class of models which can be represented as finite Markov chains. The  simple two-state Markov model with a single transition probability, c (Bower, 1961; 1962), cannot be considered, because it  too predicts diverging curves for F, T, and L when q1 = 1.0. Even if it is assumed that the transition probability varied as a  function of trials, the model would still be inadequate since it predicts that errors cannot occur on any trial subsequent to  the subject's first correct response. In the present experiment. 31 % of the subjects had at least one error following their  first correct response.  One popular class of Markov models is that of the stimulus sampling models in which the number of stimulus patterns is finite  and denumerable (Atkinson and Estes, 1963; Estes, 1959; Suppes and Atkinson, 1960; Theios, 1961, 1963). Models of this class  are inappropriate for the shock escape T-maze situation since they predict that the total number of errors cannot be less than  the number of stimulus patterns. In point of fact, about 10 % of the subjects had only one error, implying the one element  sampling model of Bower (1961) which has already been rejected.  A remaining class of Markov models is that of the discrete performance level models such as those of Bower and Theios (1963)  and Waugh and Smith (1962). In contrast to starting with a general stimulus sampling assumption, the approach used by these  authors is to determine the states and transition possibilities from a logical analysis of the physical and behavioral  properties of the particular situation under study. Consider the model of Bower and Theios (1963) represented in Fig. 2. It is  postulated that at the start of the reversal the subject is in state So in which the probability of a correct response is  zero. On each trial the subject has a fixed probability, c, of leaving the state. If he leaves state So , then either with  probability p he enters an intermediate success state (Ss) in which the probability of a correct response is unity or with  probability q = 1 - p he enters an intermediate error state (SE) in which probability of a correct response is zero. There are at least two possible reasons for the decrease in the parameters c and 0, with overtraining. First, with  overtraining, the subject receives more and more experience with shock, since each trial begins with shock in the start box of  the maze. As a function of experience with shock, the subject may become adapted or less sensitive to the pain induced by the  shock. As a function of overtraining, shock in the previously correct goal box becomes less and less effective in changing  behavior during reversal learning.  An alternative interpretation is that choice behavior and learning to run the maze proceed somewhat independently, in that  running speed continues to increase after choice behavior has asymptoted at 100 %. At the start of the reversal, speed of  running would be directly related to amount of overtraining. In turn, the duration of the shock the subject receives on each  trial would be inversely related to his running speed. Thus, the size of c would be determined by the shock duration. ALTERNATIVE THEORETICAL INTERPRETATION  The fact that the estimates of E = 0.49 and s" = 0.42 are relatively close to each other suggests that the two estimates may  be random samples from a single underlying distribution of probabilities. This state of affairs would imply another  theoretical interpretation of the Markov model. When e = s = 0, Greeno's theorem yields that the proportion of correct  responses on trials between the first success and the last error, P(I), is equal to the parameter p. These conditions could  come about if after leaving the initial error state, the subject entered a state in which he had a temporary memory from which  retrieval of the correct response was not perfect, but occurred only with probability p. On some subsequent trial the subject  could completely learn the correct response with probability 0, and could move from the temporary memory state to a permanent  memory state from which retrieval of the response would be perfect. Assuming this theoretical interpretation of the model and  using the parameter  OVERTRAINING AND SUCCESSIVE REVERSALS 39  estimation scheme outlined earlier, the estimate of B is 0.40, and the four estimates of c are only slightly larger than the  values already estimated. With this memory interpretation of the model, most of the predictions differ from those already  given only in the second or third decimal. To present the new predictions would be redundant and it would be virtually  impossible to differentiate between the two interpretations from considerations of goodness of fit.  SUCCESSIVE REVERSAL LEARNING  A series of experiments was conducted to determine the mathematical course of the changes that take place in the reversal  process as a function of the number of successive reversals which the animal is required to learn. In different experiments  using slightly different procedures rats were given 20, 50, 100, or 220 successive reversals. The 220 reversal experiment  gives a relatively complete picture of the processes involved, and, thus, it will be reported here in detail.  METHOD  Subjects. Three experimentally naive female albino rats of the Holtzman strain were used. They were 90 days of age at the  start of the experiment.  Apparatus. The apparatus was a wooden, single-unit T-maze similar to that used in the overlearning experiment, but having  slightly larger dimensions. The goal boxes and the starting box were 10 in. long, but there was an interior T-shaped choice  section of the maze which had a stem 10 in. in over-all length and a cross-member 15 in. long. The entire maze was 5 in. deep,  and was painted flat grey. Guillotine doors of clear plexiglass separated the goal and the start boxes from the choice area.  The goal and start boxes were covered with lids of 2 in. wire mesh in wood frames, and the interior choice section was covered  with a clear plexiglass lid. The Bird Electronic Inductorium described earlier was used as the shock source, and it was set to  deliver eight spiked waves each sec.  Procedure. Each daily experimental test began with an initial training session in which the rat was run to the side of the  maze opposite to that which he ran on the last reversal session of the previous day. The data from these initial training  sessions were not included in the analyses. Ten reversal sessions were given in succession following the initial session of  each day. At the beginning of any trial within a session, the entire maze was electrified, except one of the goal boxes. The  trial ended when the rat entered the goal box which was not electrified. Whenever a subject accumulated 10 consecutive correct  responses, the shock was switched to the opposite goal box. The intertrial intervals were about 20 sec. The subjects were run  for 20 days for a total of 220 reversals.  RESULTS  Individual response sequences for each rat are coded in Tables 5, 6, and 7 of the Appendix. DISCUSSION  Taken together, the analyses of the overlearning and successive reversal experiments imply a definite structure for the  reversal process in the shock-escape T-maze. First, it appears that there are three discrete levels of performance: An initial  level with the probability of a correct response equal to zero, an intermediate level with the probability of a correct  response equal to a constant between zero and unity, and a terminal level with response probability equal to unity. The fact  that the successive reversal data required the parameter q to be less than the a priori chance value of 2 strongly suggests  that q should be indentified as the probability that the subject temporarily forgets the new correct response and lapses back  into his earlier mode of responding.  In the context of the memory interpretation, the parameters s and e of the Markov model give the probability that the new  correct response is committed to perfect memory from which forgetting is not possible. Within each study, the estimated values  of s and e were invariant with number of overlearning trials and number of successive reversals. However, the estimate of s  was 0.42 in the overlearning study and zero in the successive reversal study. It appears that in the successive reversal study  s become zero rapidly, well within the first 33 reversals. The interpretation of the zero value of s is that during successive  reversals, increases in response probability occurred only following errors.  OVERTRAINING AND SUCCESSIVE REVERSALS 47  The obtained increase in the parameter c with successive reversals indicates that the animal's behavior is being affected more  and more by the initial errors made when a reversal begins. An animal must learn to reverse following an error if he is to  improve his performance with successive reversals. The increase in c can be taken as a description of the development of the  discrimination of the effective stimulus for reversing. In the present study, c had reached its limit of 1 0 by the second  block of 33 reversals.  The remaining point which requires discussion is the emergence and subsequent increases in the tendency for the subject to  make a direct transition from the initial error state to the terminal perfect responding state. This tendency was not present  in the single reversal of the overlearning study or to any large extent in the first block of 33 successive reversals. The  direct-transition parameter, d, rose from a value of 0 to above a value of 0.76 during the 220 reversal sessions. The increase  in the parameter d indicates that the subject learns not to forget which way he is going. In other words, as a result of  repeated reversals the temporary, imperfect memory stage drops out of the learning process. There is at least one obvious  reason for this development. Early in the experiment, the subject will receive a series of long shocks at the start of each  reversal. For a number of trials after the new response is learned, the organism may continue to be in a state of  physiological arousal or stress. Since every trial begins with a shock in the start box, this shock could have some fixed  probability, q, of blocking or interfering with the correct response if the animal is in the aroused condition. An abrupt drop  in the arousal level with time would lead to the perfect performance of the terminal state.  The gradual elimination of the blocking as a function of successive reversals would be a direct result of the animals becoming  adapted to or accustomed to the shock as a result of repeated experience with it. In other words, the initial shock of a  reversal physiologically arouses the organism with probability (I - d), which decreases as a function of experience with shock.  In conclusion it should be remarked that the various theoretical interpretations of the model have value only as heuristic  devices. The interpretations and identifications that were made are those which I have found useful and reasonable.  Undoubtedly, other scientists can give different theoretical interpretations of the model, e.g., neurological interpretations.  The fact remains, however, that whatever theoretical interpretation is used, it must satisfy the mathematical structure of the  discrete performance level Markov model, since that structure so completely describes the reversal process in the shock escape  T-maze.
