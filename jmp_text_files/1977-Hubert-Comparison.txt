BAKER  The University of Wisconsin, Madison, Wisconsin 53706  A permutation procedure is described for statistically comparing a given classification scheme, characterized as a  hierarchically organized collection of subsets, to either a proximity matrix or a second classification scheme defined on the  same basic set of objects. To prevent a bias that may result when an optimization method is used to construct the  classification structures being considered, it is assumed that the desired comparisons involve classifications and/or  proximities that have been derived from separate sources. In addition to an extensive number of examples used to clarify the  major points in the presentation of the inference strategy, several heuristic optimization techniques are also introduced and  illustrated. These latter procedures attempt to "fit" the form of a target classification structure by relabeling the rows and  simultaneously the columns of a given proximity matrix to maximize the correspondence between the fixed target and the  relabeled proximity matrix.  1. INTRODUCTION  In a recent series of articles, a concerned effort has been made to develop a statistical framework for evaluating the results  of a hierarchical cluster analysis, with a special emphasis on the well-known single-link and complete-link methods (see  Baker, 1974; Hubert, 1974; Baker & Hubert, 1975, 1976; Hubert & Baker, 1976, 1977a). Although the discussion in this paper can  be viewed as an additional contribution along these same lines, the techniques pursued will not be limited to any one specific  type of clustering strategy. In particular, the discussion to follow is concerned with two major problems of statistical  evaluation that reoccur continually in the general field of cluster analysis, and especially in the applications of cluster  analysis to psychology and the biological sciences (cf. Sneath & Sokal, 1973, pp. 275-290; Rohlf, 1974):  (A) Given a collection of objects and two different classification schemes for these objects obtained from distinct data sets,  can the two classification schemes be considered similar ? For instance, the classification scheme may be hypothesized a priori from from the  literature, or possibly, found through some cluster analysis of a second data matrix.  For both of these problems the same general statistical procedure may be used since the inference paradigm we suggest depends  only on correlating the corresponding off-diagonal entries in two symmetric square matrices derived from distinct sources.2 In  (A), the matrices are reconstructed from the two given classification schemes; in (B), one matrix defines the empirical  proximity matrix and the second is reconstructed from the classification scheme being evaluated. Once these correlations are  obtained (referred to as cophenetic or matrix correlations in the clustering literature), the additional problem of assessing  statistical significance can be attacked directly through the use of Mantel's generalized permutation test strategy (Mantel,  1967). It should be emphasized that the Baker-Hubert papers cited earlier are concerned with the different inference problem  of comparing a classification scheme, usually obtained with the single-link or the completelink criterion, to the original  proximity matrix from which it was derived. In this literature, the two matrices being compared are not obtained from separate  sources, and consequently, an orientation is required that is very different from that of the present discussion.  By abstracting from several substantive applications available in the psychological literature, three numerical illustrations  will be presented in a later section to clarify the major points of the paper. The first example shows an inappropriate  application of Mantel's strategy in a comparison of two matrices derived from the same source; the second example points out  the consequences of relying on a naive permutation test procedure that ignores the possible "linked" character of the entries  in the matrices being compared; and finally, the third example illustrates a correct application of the generalized  permutation test procedure for the types of analyses labeled (A) and (B). Besides the direct comparison of a proximity matrix  and a classification scheme suggested in (B), the the latter part of the paper also describes several heuristic procedures for  locating "good" classifications of the original object set that fit only the form specified by a given classification scheme.  As will be discussed in the context of a numerical example, optimization strategies of this type may be of interest in an  exploratory analysis of a given proximity matrix, especially in identifying the classification structures that are considered  important in psychological data analysis.  2. NUMERICAL EXAMPLES  EXAMPLE 1. Both of the problems labeled (A) and (B) in the Introduction require that the two matrices being compared originate  from separate sources. The purpose of this restriction is not merely one of a technical convenience since this assumption will  provide the meaning for an associated hypothesis test based on the notion of n! equally likely row and column permutations.  More to the point, whenever the two matrices are forced to conform by some secondary optimization process that already uses  the information contained in one or both of the matrices, the permutation test is essentially irrelevant. Similar difficulties  exist in many other areas of statistics as well; for instance, in using the strategy of stepwise regression, the well-known  normal theory inference technique should not be applied to the final function, since this equation has been selected by a  secondary optimization process guided by the given data set.  As an illustration relevant for our context, consider the proximity measures given in the upper triangular portion of the 10 x  10 matrix of Table I. These integers (ranging from 1 to 45 with larger integers reflecting greater similarities) were randomly  assigned to the 45 object pairs; consequently, these proximities should not reflect any identifiable structure among the ten  objects. The lower triangular portion of the Table I matrix was obtained through a C function defined as in (1) and represents  the results of a hierarchical complete-link cluster analysis for the randomly produced proximity data (see Hubert & Baker,  1976, for a more complete discussion and computational details). However, since the original proximity data were obtained by a random procedure, this correspondence must be attributed solely  to the optimization process and not to any "stable organization" represented in the original proximity measures. EXAMPLE 2. The major justification for the apparent complexity of the variance term for Mantel's permutation test is in its  explicit provision for the possible "linked" character of the entries in the two matrices being compared. For instance, the  entries could correspond to a metric, and thus, the triangle inequality must hold for the values in the  CLASSIFICATION SCHEMES  241  matrix; or possibly, the entries could be correlations, and thus, subject to very strong algebraic dependencies (see Hubert,  1972). A natural question arises as to the differences that could be expected using Mantel's strategy versus a "naive"  permutation test approach that would simply ignore any possible linkage among the entries in the original matrices. In  particular, to generate a final permutation distribution, a naive permutation approach would consider the (z) proximities of  one matrix as fixed and then evaluate 0 for all (2)! (equally likely) reorderings of the entries in the second. OPTIMIZATION PROCEDURES  In comparing a proximity matrix and a hypothesized classification scheme (i.e., the problem we labeled (B) in the  Introduction), it is clear how Mantel's strategy can indicate a nonrandom correspondence whenever the index 0 is sufficiently  extreme. A rather different approach, however, could also be used in conjunction with a preliminary  480 613-6  246 HUBERT AND BAKER  significance test that might provide additional support for the conjectured correspondence between a proximity matrix and a  hypothesized classification scheme. To be more precise, if an appropriate optimization method were actually able to extract  the latter structure from the original proximity matrix, or at least one very similar, then convincing evidence of the type  desired would be provided immediately. In the ideal situation, the hypothesized classification scheme would be a local (or  global) optimum using an appropriate optimization technique. At second best, the global optima or the best local optima  obtained would be close in terms of the index 0 to that found for the hypothesized classification scheme. In short, either of  these two conditions would strengthen dramatically a statement regarding any possible nonrandomness that was initially  suggested by a significance test carried out using Mantel's formulas.  As an introduction to this change in emphasis and to indicate the general type of optimization method we have in mind, assume  that the hypothesized classification scheme is now reinterpreted as a "target," and moreover, the actual optimization routine  attempts to locate the "best" classification in terms of the index 0 satisfying only the form of the target. In the general case and given a particular target and a particular proximity matrix, one representative type of optimization  heuristic would proceed as follows (phrased here as a minimization):  Select a relabeling of the proximity matrix at random and consider the effect of performing some type of (stage-one) local  operation on this initial start. Choose that relabeling constructed by such a local operation that minimizes 0, and then,  using this relabeling, repeat the process until no local operation of the type specified will decrease 0, i.e., until a  stage-one locally optimal result has been constructed. At this point, a second (stage-two) local operation could be  implemented and used in the same manner as stage-one. Finally, the two-stage relabeling could then be used to reenter stage  one, and so on, until a relabeling is obtained that is both stage-one and stage-two locally optimal  Obviously, the number of stages could be increased by using different local operations, and only the final computational costs  should limit the development of an ever more extensive iterative optimization routine. Intuitively, stage one should be  defined in such a way that a reasonably good relabeling is obtained quickly; stage two (as well as other higher-order stages  when implemented) would then be concerned with refining the results constructed by stage one, and thus, could be somewhat more  demanding computationally. AN ALTERNATIvE TWO-STAGE OPTIMIZATION PROCEDURE  It should be apparent that the type of two-stage optimization routine discussed above is capable of extensive variation. To  indicate how this generality can be exploited and also to illustrate what is a powerful heuristic in its own right for fitting  classification targets, we now consider an optimization strategy that still uses pairwise interchanges as a first stage but  replaces the previous three-object circular permutations defining stage two by a different local operation. Specifically,  stage two will now require evaluating the effects of interchanging two subsets of a fixed size K that contain objects  consecutive in terms of the current relabeling. All integers K between 2 and the greatest integer  250 HUBERT AND BAKER  contained in n/2 are considered before that interchange providing the greatest change in the index 0 is carried out. This  particular operation was suggested by the form of the local optima observed with our previous two-stage optimization routine,  since in many instances the local optima appeared capable of further improvement if only groups of consecutive objects in the  relabeling could be interchanged as complete units.  As it turned out, this observation proved correct if qualified slightly. The second stage K subset interchange option may not  do as well as the previous three-object circular permutations when the n is relatively small, say, less than or equal to 10.  For example, even though all the three "best" local optima were still found for the three targets used in generating Table 6,  these "best" local optima given in Table 6 were found less consistently for targets II and III but more consistently for  target IV when the K subset interchange operation was used as a second stage. For n's larger than about 10, however, the  performance of the three-object circular permutation option deteriorates rapidly and when n is around 15 it rarely refines the  results obtainable from the stage one pairwise interchange operation. Conversely, for moderate values of n, the K subset  interchange strategy, used as a second stage, seems to perform very well in fitting a variety of classification targets, and  clearly outperforms the three-object circular local operation. SOME GENERALIZATIONS  There are several extensions or modifications of the ideas presented in the previous sections that suggest additional ways of  applying Mantel's permutation test and/or an associated optimization strategy to other problems of importance in the  comparisons of classifications. For instance, Rand (1971) presents a technique for comparing two partitions that can be  reformulated in terms of a generalized correlation coefficient, and thus, in terms of an index identical in form to ~; e.g.,  see Hubert (1977). Along these same lines, the problem of comparing a single partition to a proximity matrix, or possibly to  another partition, has been discussed explicitly through an analysis-of-variance analog by Hubert and Levin (1976) and Hubert  and Baker (1977b) using Mantel's formulas. Further generalizations that are tied more directly with our notion of a C function  are also possible. As examples, a C function could be defined in terms of the actual "fusing" levels at which clusters are  formed rather than in terms of the arbitrary integers used in (1); or more significantly, a completely different type of C  function could be formulated through, say, an additive tree structure or the associated metrics for characterizing a  classification scheme, e.g., Farris's concept of a cladistic distance function (cf. Rohlf, 1974). It is noteworthy that all of  these generalizations lead to a comparison of two n X n matrices, and consequently, to the concept of a permutation test as  discussed above or to the use of an optimization method with a fixed target.
