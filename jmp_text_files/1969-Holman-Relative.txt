HOLMAN  University of California, Los Angeles, California 90024  Given fairly general conditions describing a two-choice learning process, testable predictions are derived from assumptions  about the relative effectiveness of reward and nonreward in changing response probabilities. Tests of these predictions, using  data from previously-published T-maze experiments with rats, yield the following conclusions. Reward of a response is usually  more effective than nonreward of the opposite response, but is less effective than nonreward of the same response when the  probability of the response is near 1. These facts imply that events that increase response probability are more effective  when response probability is low, while events that decrease response probability are more effective when response probability  is high.  INTRODUCTION  Many mathematical models for two-choice learning state that on each experimental trial, the subject's choice probability is  transformed by an operator that corresponds to the events occurring on that trial but not on previous trials. In simple  position learning by a rat in a T maze, for instance, the relevant experimental events would probably be whether the rat went  left or right, and whether he was rewarded. It is generally assumed that all the operators applied during an experiment have  the same functional form, which presumably reflects the general nature of the learning process. On the other hand, the  operators generally differ with respect to a parameter that reflects the relative effectiveness of the various experimental  events in changing choice probability. Hence, a learning model makes assumptions about two factors: first, the functional form  of the learning process; and second, the relative effects of particular events such as reward and nonreward. Unfortunately,  since experimental data are determined by both factors at once, most attempts to evaluate such models empirically have had  ambiguous results. Although parameter-free comparisons of models are usually difficult, some relatively model-free comparisons of the effects of  reward and nonreward are possible. The present paper derives two methods for comparing reward and nonreward effects, given  fairly weak assumptions about the learning operators. First, during any training period in which the same response is correct  on all trials, it is possible to determine whether reward of one response is more or less effective than nonreward of the  opposite response. Second, during any reversal training period, it is possible to determine whether reward of one response is  more or less effective than nonreward of the same response, at least when the probability of the response is near 1. Moreover,  the results of these two tests can be combined to suggest some inferences about the functional form of the learning operators. Since the identification of the response is arbitrary, the tests based on  Theorem 3 suggest that Tip > T21p and T22P < T12P for all p. Also, the tests based on Theorem 4 suggest that Ti, T11p < p and  therefore T11T12p < p for p near 1, and T21T22p > p and T22T21p > p for p near 0. Given that the operators are monotonic on p,  the first two of the above inequalities can be substituted into the others to show that for i and j equal to 1 or 2, Ti1T72p <  p and T;2Ti,p < p for p near 1, and Ti1T;2p > p and T;2Tilp > p for p near 0.  These inequalities mean that the learning operators are asymmetric, because operators that increase p are more effective when  p is low, and operators that decrease p are more effective when p is high. For instance, in the linear model, the operators  have this property; but in the beta model, the operators are symmetric, because their relative effects do not depend upon p.  Hence, it appears that the linear model is closer to the truth than the beta model, at least in T-maze learning by rats.
