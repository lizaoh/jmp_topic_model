Most of these are expressed as conditions on a family of binary relations defined from  the p(a, b)'s, where a is in the Ath relation to b if p(a, b) > A. The conditions essentially state that this family of  relations is a homogenous or consistent family of semiorders. The conditions are then restated in a more general setting,  starting with a family of binary relations rather than with the data p(a, b) and the particular family of relations defined  from p(a, b). The main result is that almost all the probabilistic consistency conditions studied are equivalent. The logical  relations among all these conditions are described. Finally, some recent work of Tversky-Russo on conditions of probabilistic  consistency on the data p(a, b) is shown to hold in the more general setting of this paper.  1. INTRODUCTION  The notion of semiorder was first introduced by Luce (1956) as a model for an individual's relation of preference. The  relation "I prefer a to b" has been interpreted very generally in the literature on semiorders, and we shall interpret it very  generally here. It can be taken to mean "I think a is louder than b," "I like a better than b," "I think a will do a better  job than b," "I think a is heavier than b," etc. Since Luce's work appeared, there have been several mathematical papers on  the theory of semiorders. Some of the references are Scott and Suppes (1958), Holland (1966a, b), and Roberts (1968, 1970).  It seems quite natural to try to generalize this theory to families of semiorders. In particular, we might be interested in  the connections among several people's relations of preference, or among the relations of preference of a particular  individual at different times. In this paper we study various conditions on a family of binary relations, all of which turn  out to mean more or less that the family is a "homogeneous" or "consistent" family of semiorders. Rather surprisingly, most of  these conditions turn out to be equivalent under certain assumptions. SOME NOTIONS OF PROBABILISTIC CONSISTENCY  There is considerable data in the literature which indicates that a subject's preferences in a (binary) choice situation  change from trial to trial. He exhibits variations in his preferences. He may prefer a to b on one trial and b to a on  another. The word "variations" has deliberately been used here rather than the word "inconsistencies." Although the subject  does not display "absolute consistency," he does display, to paraphrase the very nice terminology of Block and Marschak  (1959), "probabilistic consistency." There have been various mathematical models used to make precise the notion of  probabilistic consistency. The setting is usually the following.
