This paper reports on an experimental study of ultimatum bargaining situations in which an inactive third player is present. The proposer X suggests an allocation (x, y, z) on how to divide a cake between X, Y, and Z. A message m that (partially) reveals this proposal is sent to the responder Y who has to accept or reject it. If Y accepts, each player gets paid as proposed, otherwise each player receives zero. In our experiments the three messages m=(x, y, z), m= y, and m=z were used. The information condition is common knowledge. The proposer's offer is seen to depend on the information condition. Specifically, the lower the information content of the message, the greedier the demand x of the proposer. In the case where m=z, the modal proposal allocates (almost) all of the cake to X. In the other cases, the responder receives slightly more than 1�3 of the cake on average. � 1998 Academic Press 

1. INTRODUCTION 

In the ultimatum game, two players bargain about how to divide a given amount of money, according to a simple protocol. One player, the proposer, offers some portion of the money to the other, the responder. If the responder accepts, he gets what was offered and the proposer gets the rest; if the responder rejects, both players get nothing. The theoretical prediction for this game, based on the assumptions that both players care only about their own monetary payoffs and that

Behavior in the experimental laboratory, however, turns out to be much different from this theoretical prediction: We tend to observe demands of just over 50 0 of the sum; the mode is often a demand of exactly 500, the mean demand is less than 70 0, too greedy demands are rejected (with the mean rejected offer being 20 0) and less than 1 0 of the data is in the neighborhood of the game theoretic prediction. (See Roth (1995) and Camerer 6 Thaler (1995) for recent surveys.) 

Fair divisions are also observed in dictator games. In such a game, the proposer specifies the division and each player gets his allocated share without the responder having to decide. Forsythe et al. (1994) report that also in this game frequently a substantial portion of the money is allocated to responders. While Hoffman et al. (1994, 1996) argued that the giving might be related to the outcome being observed by the experimenter (subjects might be embarrassed to demand too much for fear of appearing greedy to the experimenter), subsequent research has shown that this anonymity-effect, if it exists at all, is only weak. Bolton 6 Zwick (1995) and Bolton et al. (1995) show that the possibility for the responder to punish the proposer (by rejecting the offer) induces the proposer to offer more. In short, proposers make significantly positive offers in both the ultimatum and the dictator game, but they offer more in the former. 

The inconsistency between the theoretical predictions and the empirical results mentioned above has sparked a lively and still ongoing debate about the predictive value of game theory and, more specifically, about the role of fairness considerations in economics. While the assumption that people are motivated only by their own monetary gains seems inappropriate in view of the experimental results, the debate has also shown that issues of fairness are complicated. Are the data best explained by assuming that proposers care for fairness, that they have altruistic motives? Or should we opt for the alternative explanation that proposers are basically selfish but take into account the possibility that at least some responders might be motivated by distributional considerations? Hence, can the proposals be explained as strategic responses to responders' willingness to refuse ``insultingly low'' offers? In short: Do proposers want to be fair, or do they only want to seem fair in order not to provoke the responder? In the former case, why do proposers want to be fair: Are they intrinsically motivated for fair outcomes or do they want to obey to a social norm? In the latter case, why and in which circumstances would the responder be provoked? Secondly, there is the question about learning and experience. Is it true that considerations of fairness are equally important throughout the game, or are they important only at the beginning? Is it true that with enough experience, and if the conditions are sufficiently favorable, behavior converges to the game theoretic prediction that is based on monetary considerations alone? In this case, which conditions are favorable for ``gamesmanship''? 

This paper reports on an experiment that was designed in order to get a better understanding of these issues. Especially, it combines the essential aspects of ultimatum bargaining with those of dictatorship games. In order to explore more thoroughly whether and how fairness considerations influence behavior, we performed ultimatum bargaining experiments with three players, X, Y, and Z,

The overall conclusion that we can draw from these results is that proposers do not have a strong intrinsic motivation for fairness. The fact that most proposers allocate marginal amounts to the dummy (resp. responder) in information condition y (resp. z) shows that proposers do not want to be fair and it suggests that most proposers aim to maximize their own monetary payoffs. Proposers, however, realize that responders will not accept any offer (either because they are insulted by a low share for themselves or because they care about the dummy) and they try to react strategically to the information of the responder by anticipating his reaction. In other words, proposers believe that responders might reject offers which they perceive to be unfair and, therefore, they make offers which appear fair. Uncertainty concerning the beliefs, motives and rationality of the responders induces different accommodating proposals in different information conditions. Experience with the game reduces the uncertainty and this explains why the proposals change through time. In the beginning there are relatively many ``equal splits,'' but these no longer appear when players better understand the incentive structure of the game. The fact that there are conflicts proves that responders must be guided by non-monetary incentives. However, there is not a single rejection that can be clearly attributed to a low share for the dummy. Hence, responders do not seem to care for the dummy. The data (backed by the post-experimental questionnaire) tell us that responders reject when they perceive the ratio y�x to be small, hence, distributional considerations do seem to play a role and responders seem willing to punish proposers who are too greedy. The differences between the cycle mode and the constant mode are quite striking, an issue to which we return in Section 5.

2. EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN 

We recruited altogether 216 undergraduate students from the University of Tilburg to participate in the experiment. They had to register personally and most of them were first or second year students in economics. The students had no background in game theory. The experiment was run on four consecutive evenings, two evenings being devoted to the cycle mode and two devoted to the constant mode. Altogether we had four sessions (groups) that played the cycle mode, and two sessions for each of the constant information conditions, with there being three different information conditions, viz. full, essential and irrelevant. 

After having received general instructions and after having done a preliminary experiment[1] the subject group was split into three subgroups which were then assigned to three different classrooms. Since these lecture rooms were rather large it was easy to exclude any communication between participants. Next, it was randomly decided which role (proposer, responder or dummy) each subgroup would play and instructions for the main experiment were distributed. While all materials were in English, a Dutch translation was read aloud by one of the authors. 

In each of the sessions 1, 2, 3, and 4 devoted to cycle mode 27 subjects participated: 9 persons for each role. They played the ultimatum game for 9 rounds, where a round-robin style matching was used.[2] In each round they bargained for f 12,�which at the time of the experiment was about 86.80. The f 12,�where represented as 120 points and the rules of the game specified that the proposer had to allocate at least 5 points to each player. Hence, each player received at least f 0, 50 (r80.28) in each round if the proposal was accepted. The instructions inform subjects that they play the full information condition (``condition xyz'') in the round 1, 4, and 7, that they play the essential information condition (``condition y'') in the rounds 2, 5, and 8 and that they play the irrelevant information condition (``condition z'') in the rounds 3, 6, and 9. Whereas players X and Y also got a decision form which reminded them of the number of rounds and the changing information conditions, players Z received only a balance sheet to record their payoffs. The Appendix contains a sample of the instruction and decision forms (those for player X ); all other forms are available from the authors upon request. 

Players X also received a communication sheet each round which was used to communicate all the decisions between the players. Let us now look at what the proposer allocates to himself. First of all, there is a clear experience effect: individual proposers dare to make more greedy demands later on in the game and for most proposers the difference is strict. For example x(x, 3)>x(x, 1) for 31 proposers (86 0) and there is only one proposer for which x(x, 3)<x(x, 1). This latter player proposed (110, 5, 5) in the first round; this proposal was rejected and consequently he made more modest demands later on. To abstract from the learning, let us now focus on the last cycle. On average, the proposer then demands 530 of the cake in the full information condition, 600 when information is essential, and 83 0 when the responder has irrelevant information. We see that the proposer is reluctant to demand the entire cake when the responder has relevant information. The modal demand is x=55 when m=xyz and x=75 when m= y. All but 2 proposers demand at least as much when m= y as when m=xyz and 26 (720) demand strictly more in that case. Hence, if it is not so easy to verify whether proposers are greedy, they become more demanding. Comparing m= y to m=z we see that all but 5 proposers demand strictly more in the latter case and that only one demands less. When m=z, the maximal possible demand, x=110, is made by 50 0 of the proposers. (There are only 10 proposers (28 0) that demand less than 100.) 

Over time there is not much variation in the share that is offered to the responder. Of course, his share does depend on the information condition. While on average he gets slightly more than[1] 3[of the case when][ m][=][xyz][ or][ m][=][ y][, he only gets] 8.50 when m=z. In the former two cases, proposers are rather generous: there are only 13 proposals (120) with y(x, t)<40 and only 20 proposals (19 0) with y( y, t)<40. The comparison between these cases is illuminating. While in the first cycle, the responder receives more in the y-mode, in later cycles he gets more in the xyz-mode. The explanation is the following. The proposer would like to maximize his share subject to the constraint that the proposal be accepted. The proposer has only limited information about exactly what proposals will be acceptable for the responder. If Y has limited information, m= y, Y will probably accept as long as y is sufficiently high. If Y has more information, m=xyz, he receives more signals that the offer is ``unfair'' and he has additional arguments to reject the offer, either because Y ``cares for'' Z or because it is now more visible that y is small compared to x. To make the deal acceptable, X has to sweeten it, either by increasing z or by increasing y (thereby bribing Y). The data show that proposers choose to increase y. From the fact that y(z, t) and z( y, t) are small, with over 75 0 of the experienced proposers only offering the minimal =, we can conclude that proposers are not intrinsically motivated by fairness. Yet, even if both y(z, t) and z( y, t) are small, they are significantly different: the hypothesis that y(z, t)=z( y, t) is rejected at the 50 level when tested against the alternative y(z, t)>z( y, t). In fact, the difference increases with experience. Apparently, proposers felt that responders were more powerful than dummies and, therefore, should be given a higher share of the cake. 

Evidence that proposers find condition m=z to be the most puzzling one, is provided by the rather large standard deviations of z (and also of y) in this condition (Table 2). Also compare the different coefficients of determination (R[2] ) in Table 3. The dilemma is that neither a high nor a low value of z can signal to the responder that y will be large. It turns out that y(z, t) and z(z, t) are positively correlated. Nevertheless, responders view high z-values with suspicion, the percentage of rejected proposals does not decrease with z. 

The altogether 324 plays yield only 22 rejections, hence, the percentage of rejected offers is with 6, 79 0 remarkably low. Almost all of the rejections, 18 out of 22, occur in information condition z. Hence, of the 104 plays in this condition, 16.70 result in disagreement. The question remains why responders reject any proposals at all. Here we can offer the following observations. First we know from previous research that some players are indeed willing to punish greedy behavior of others. (See for example Kahneman et al. (1986)). Secondly, responders accumulate quite a considerable amount of money during the x and y conditions of the experiment which may make them more willing to reject marginal amounts in the z condition. They know that proposers make a considerable amount of money during the experiment and they might be concerned about distributional considerations. Hence, they might enjoy punishing proposers who they expect to be greedy. Being informed at the end of the round whether they indeed succeeded in inflicting considerable harm on the proposer (i.e., whether they indeed did ``the right thing''), might very well contribute positively to their inclination to reject proposals in the z condition.

6. CONCLUSION 

The experimental data clearly refute the idea that proposers are intrinsically motivated by considerations of fairness: They only allocate marginal amounts to the dummy and they give little to the responder in information condition m=z. (Also responders do not show concern for the dummy.) On the other hand, the proposer does not demand the maximum possible amount for himself and some responders reject nonnegligible amounts. As the answers to our post-experimental questionnaire show, proposers mainly do not ask for nearly all of the cake since they anticipate that such proposals are more likely to be rejected. The data shows that such expectations are indeed well-founded. Responders might refuse proposals in which y�x is perceived to be small and proposers use the information condition to manipulate the proposal in such a way that it appears to give the responder a fair share. Hence, our main result is that proposers do not want to be fair, but rather want to appear fair, in order to prevent the responder from rejecting the offer. In order to appear fair, proposers react systematically and strategically to the information that responders receive about the proposal. 

To provide a proper perspective on these results, and to enable a general conclusion, we now briefly compare our paper to some related literature. 

Gu�th, Schmittberger, 6 Schwarze (1982) were the first to show that proposers in an ultimatum game do not ask for all of the cake and to raise the question of whether fairness considerations could explain this result. Forsythe et al. (1994) showed that fairness alone cannot explain proposers' willingness to give money, for less is given in dictator games than in ultimatum games. Still, some players do give away substantial amounts of money in the dictator game, so it appears that not all subjects are completely selfish. Hoffman at al. (1994) and Hoffman et al. (1996) argued that this apparent taste for fairness might be explained by the fact that subjects do not want to be recognized as greedy. Hence, observed behavior may not be due to a taste for ``fairness'' but rather to a social concern for what others might think about oneself and for being held in high regard by others. Indeed dictators often demand more for themselves when the individual behavior cannot be observed by the experimenter, but subsequent research has shown that this effect is only minor (Bolton 6 Zwick, 1995). The extreme results in our experiments suggest that our participants were hardly concerned whether or not experimenters will learn what they did: they did not mind to reveal being greedy. 

Bolton 6 Zwick (1995) show that proposers offer more to the responder in the ultimatum game than in the dictator game, from which they conclude that fear of 

243

punishment plays a role. Och 6 Roth (1989) suggest that players' preferences may be such that they are prepared to punish those who treat them unfairly, and Bolton (1991) provides a thorough theoretical and experimental investigation of this assumption. In Bolton's ``comparative'' model, a player cares both about his absolute and relative payoff, specifically, a player prefers more money to less and, if his share is less, he prefers a more equal division. With preferences of this type, a responder might prefer an equal split of nothing to an unfavourable division. Bolton (1991) shows that the resulting model is broadly consistent with the observed experimental regularities. One problem with this model, however, is that the preferences (the players' trade-offs between ``absolute'' and ``relative'' money) are assumed to be common knowledge. If preferences are common knowledge, there cannot be any disagreement according to the model; the observed disagreements refute the common knowledge assumption. 

Harrison 6 McCabe (1996) argue that in general the experimental subjects will not have common knowledge of the rationality, beliefs or motives of the other players. When making their demands, proposers will not know exactly what offers will be acceptable, and by making more information available one can influence proposers' beliefs and, hence, their proposals. In one of their experiments, Harrison 6 McCabe reveal responders' minimal acceptance levels. These turn out to be low and, having this information, proposers increase their demands. The authors conclude that subjects do a very good job at maximizing their expected monetary payoffs, but it clearly remains a puzzle then why people do not behave in this way in the dictator game. 

Allowing for lack of common knowledge, our data are broadly consistent with Bolton's (1991) model of comparative bargaining (see also Bolton 6 Ockenfels, 1997, who further develop this model and explore how it can explain our main observations). If the responder cares about his absolute payoff, y, as well as his relative payoff, y�x, then the various information conditions allow the proposer to manipulate the relative share so as to favourably influence the responder's reaction. Yet, there is also evidence of proposers being influenced by norms of fairness: in early rounds, equal splits are quite frequent. Subjects come to the lab endowed with certain (social) rules of behavior that tell them how they ought to behave and that serve them well in real life. The subjects import these rules into the laboratory and only gradually adjust behavior to what is ``more appropriate'' for the situation at hand. Behavior thus seems determined by various motives; on the one hand there is a desire to behave according to the norm, on the other hand there is the desire to strategically exploit the situation, and which motive is more dominant may be person specific; there is heterogeneity. We refer to Gu�th (1995) for more details concerning this issue. What is clear from our experiment, however, is that, if fairness is a social norm guiding distribution behaviour, it needs monitoring, i.e., it can prevail only in environments where behavior is observable. Furthermore, fairness, as a social norm, seems restricted to those who have strategic influence. The hope that fair outcomes might result from an intrinsic motivation for fairness is not well founded. 

Our important finding, that proposers react systematically and strategically to the information that responders receive about the proposal, was also observed in

Mitzkewitz 6 Nagel (1993). That paper reports results on 2-person ultimatum games with incomplete information. It is common knowledge that the size of the cake is either 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, or 6 (each possibility with probability 1�6), but the exact size is known only to the proposer. Two different games are compared: the offer game��in which the responder only gets to hear what the proposer allocates to him��and the demand game��in which the responder only knows what the proposer demands for himself. Although the subgame perfect equilibrium outcomes of the two game coincide��the proposer should always ask for (almost) the entire cake��actual behavior is markedly different in the two games. In the offer game, the proposer offers an equal split for cake sizes 1 to 4, while he offers 2 for larger cake sizes. In the demand game, proposers ask for the whole cake of sizes 1, 2, and 3, while they demand 3 for large cake sizes. Mitzkewitz 6 Nagel conclude that the responder is likely to reject demands that are visibly unfair and that he will accept offers that he expects to be fair. By using the strategy method (Selten, 1967) the authors are able to show that strategies that build on these ideas are widely used. The responses to our post-experimental questionnaire show that in our game players entertain similar considerations. 

Rapoport 6 Sundali (1996) and Straub 6 Murningham (1993) report similar results. Rapoport 6 Sundali focus on the offer game and they extend the Mitzkewitz�Nagel analysis by (i) allowing a finer grid of offers and (ii) varying the uncertainty that the responder has. They show that the proposer offers a smaller share of the cake when either the cake or the uncertainty about the size is larger. Hence, the proposer exploits the responders' asymmetric information to his advantage. Straub 6 Murningham (1993) compare the case where the cake size is common knowledge to that where only the proposer knows it. They show that the responders' lowest acceptable offer is larger when the cake size is known or when the cake size is larger. The proposer's offer moves in the same direction: The offer is larger when the cake size is known or when the cake is larger. These observations are consistent with ours and can be explained by the theoretical ideas discussed above. 

In spite of all our efforts to avoid effects of repeated strategic interaction, the striking differences between the cycle and the constant information treatment might indicate that participants do not consider each round as an independent decision task. This has been observed in other experiments as well (Bolton et al. (1994)) and it is somewhat questioning the possibilities for theory testing: On the one hand, the game should be played more than once to allow learning to take place, but on the other hand, it should only be played once to test the prediction for the one-shot game. 

No doubt the cycle mode triggers a more thorough understanding of the situation. Participants in the cycle mode experience all three information conditions and therefore react to the opportunities. We have seen that this leads to a stronger dependence on the information condition than can be observed in the constant mode. We conclude from this more generally that decision makers who are confronted with a multiplicity of different decision problems will usually be more willing to exploit the strategic aspects of a given situation as compared to others who mainly envisage the same situation.
