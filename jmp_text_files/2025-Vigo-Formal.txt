Invariance and symmetry principles play a key role in some of the most successful theories of the physical and mathematical sciences. Newtonian Mechanics, Maxwellian Electrodynamics, and both the special and general theories of relativity by Einstein are quintessential examples. Following this tradition, albeit in the domain of psychological phenomena, about a decade ago, a theory of cognition based on invariance and abstract symmetry principles was proposed by the author. Generalized Invariance Structure Theory (GIST;  Vigo, 2013 ,  2015 ) is a mathematical theory of human generalization which attempts to predict the influence of context on a variety of psychological phenomena and whose birth can be traced to attempts at modeling concept formation and categorization behavior. Now that the theory has been extensively tested and proven successful in accounting for the data from a broad range of experiments in different areas of cognitive research with remarkable accuracy (e.g., research on concept learning and categorization ( Vigo, 2013 ;  Vigo et al., 2022 a), choice behavior ( Vigo et al., 2023 ;  Vigo & Doan, 2015 ), and informativeness judgments ( Vigo et al., 2020 ) to name a few areas), it is important to introduce its less known formal aspects. Indeed, such formal excursions often reveal the subtleties necessary to achieve a greater understanding of a theory and an appreciation for its full potential and limits. It is with this general aim in mind that this paper has been written.

The theory is based on the fundamental idea that humans, as well as many species of non-human animals, detect invariance patterns from their environment and the mind itself in order to attain the necessary representations underlying concept formation and related cognitive capacities. These capacities include memory, perception, imagination, language-based reasoning, problem solving, and decision-making. In other words, invariance detection is at the heart of stimulus generalization and simplification as these partake in all aspects of human cognition. While invariance detection (or in a narrower sense, the detection of particular varieties of abstract symmetries) has been proposed as a possible cognitive mechanism underlying perception (e.g., see  Gibson, 1966 ;  Garner, 1963 ,  1970 ;  Pizlo, 2009 ), GIST is the first theory that systematically attempts to account for concept learning and categorization performance in terms of principles based on an original notion of invariance that is more appropriate for modeling these key capacities and, by extension, other cognitive capacities involving human generalization.

Like any other theory, GIST has a set of basic primitives (i.e., core constructs not defined formally). These include  features, dimensions,  and  definability . Intuitively and roughly speaking, I shall define features as attributes of objects; these will be represented numerically on some scale. Dimensions, on the other hand, are more general than features and refer to types of features. In what follows, dimensions will be represented as variables with scaled features as values.

The simplest type of dimension is the binary dimension, so called because it stands for one of two possible values. For example, a color dimension could have as its only two possible values 0 and 1, representing two possible colors such as white and black respectively. However,

We shall call categorical stimuli defined in this specific synthetic fashion  regular .  Shepard, Hovland and Jenkins (1961)  were among the first to use regular categorical stimuli in category learning experiments. While CIT applies only to regular categorical stimuli defined over binary dimensions in the sense mentioned above, GIST, in principle, does not have such a restriction.

Thus, GIST models also apply to  irregular  categorical stimuli under the assumption that the scaling of the dimensional values is meaningful in terms of the scale used to represent the features. There are four main factors that make a categorical stimulus irregular. The first is that it may contain object stimuli defined not on the same set of dimensions, but on different sets of dimensions where object stimuli come exclusively from each set. Second, the categorical stimulus may be a multiset, not a set, thereby allowing for repeated elements. Third, although there may be only one set of dimensions defining the members of the categorical stimulus, dimensions in this single set can vary in terms of the number of dimensional values each may hold. Thus, the set may contain a mixture of binary, ternary, and other  
                         n 
                      -valued dimensions – not just binary ones. Last, each dimension may hold different possible numerical values that are widely incompatible (e.g., some dimensional features may have been scaled with complex numbers for some dimensions and scaled with real numbers for others). Thus, we see that there is a far greater variety of irregular categorical stimuli than regular. Nonetheless, for the sake of simplifying the exposition of key ideas in this paper, I will focus on regular categorical stimuli until the end of the paper when I discuss how GIST handles irregular categorical stimuli when compared to CIT.

In this section, I introduce some basic notation and definitions of the theory. Please note that the style here is not strictly formally axiomatic but formal in a way similar to that also found in non-axiomatic mathematical/theoretical physics and engineering manuscripts. Sometimes this style is referred to as  engineering mathematics , and it is commonly used in papers on mathematical and/or computational modeling. The most famous example of this style is found in Einstein's exposition of the special and general theories of relativity. This approach was chosen in order to make the paper as accessible as possible without sacrificing conceptual precision.

I begin by defining two formal notions of human invariance detection: the first, referred to as a  p -invariance for its perturbation-based underlying mechanism, was proposed in a precursor theory to GIST known as categorical invariance theory (CIT;  Vigo, 2009 ); the other,  s -invariance, so named for its suppression-based underlying mechanism, is at the core of GIST.  P -invariance is defined by an operator referred to as the  logical manifold operator ; however, it applies only to regular categorical stimuli defined by a set of binary dimensions. On the other hand,  s -invariance is defined by an operator referred to as the  structural manifold operator  and it applies to both regular categorical stimuli and to irregular categorical stimuli. The fundamental invariance equivalence principle in GIST is a statement about the relationship between these two formal cognitive mechanisms of invariance detection. 
                         2 
                      
                      
                         2 
                         In  Vigo (2015)  the principle is referred to as the Invariance-Similarity Equivalence Principle.

The principle is important for several reasons among which two are primarily of a methodological character. First, the principle reveals the deeper underpinnings of each theory and mechanism – an important factor when wanting to master each approach. Second, it shows the connection between a natural descriptive way of defining invariance in  p -invariance and one rooted in a plausible cognitive mechanism in  s -invariance. The end result will be that simple similarity assessment is not sufficient to account for category learning and related capacities. As will be seen with respect to  s -invariance in GIST, what seems to be required is invariance structure detection as a construct that gives shape, meaning, and purpose to both similarity and dissimilarity assessment. Indeed, a much more complex mechanism than the simple combination of similarity assessment and attention seen in exemplar theories of categorization characterizes the  s -notion of invariance detection. Accordingly, because  p -invariance is the notion that is most recognizable in universal science, when a link is made between the two via a proof of the principle, it unfolds new perspectives that may help close the theoretical gap between the science of the mind of human observers and the science of physical reality – physics. With these goals in mind, this paper begins with an introduction to each notion.

Remark 2 
                            The components or kernels of a structural manifold (as well as those of a logical manifold) are meant to capture the degree of diagnosticity of each dimension (i.e., how well the dimension predicts category membership). Furthermore, when the components of a structural manifold (aka, the structural kernels) evaluate to values greater than one under a different tau threshold, this is a psychological indicator of the potential existence of illusory invariants. Illusory invariants may account for the perception of optical illusions. Indeed, the Gestalt principles attempt to account for optical illusions by positing how individuals erroneously attempt to maximize invariance and symmetry where it does not quite exist. Thus, it is hypothesized in GIST that an attempt at oversimplifying information results in a distortion of reality of such magnitude that it is responsible for triggering perceptual and cognitive illusions of all types.

As seen in the definitions of the logical manifold operator ( Definition 5 ) which characterizes the process of  p -invariance and of the structural manifold operator ( Definition 6 ) which characterizes the process of  s -invariance,  p -invariance and  s -invariance have much in common. For example, both yield a vector representation (see  Fig. 2 
                         ) whose components are proportion of invariants with respect to each dimension and whose norm stands for the overall degree of invariance of a category. In both notions, the direction of the vector representing the logical or structural manifold serves to differentiate the contextual saliency of a categorical stimulus dimension as a function of the order of its associated component in the logical or structural manifold. Importantly, in GIST, structural manifolds are mathematical descriptions of ideotypes. Ideotypes are core representations proclaimed to underlie other well-known representations, such as rules and prototypes, that have been proposed as key to human generalization behavior.

But how exactly are these two notions of invariance related? The invariance equivalence principle answers this question. In this section three types of models derived from GIST are briefly discussed. These not only highlight the power of the theory but also show how and why logical manifolds are not appropriate for use in these models when compared to structural manifolds. 
                         8 
                      
                      
                         8 
                         Note that GIST models also may be applied to hierarchical categories starting with single object stimuli as categories of multidimensional features and thus, to categories of categories, and to categories of categories of categories, and so on. This suggests the possibility of unifying all perceptual and conceptual phenomena. For the sake of brevity, this aspect of the theory is not discussed here.

GIST applies to both regular and irregular categorical stimuli involving repeating elements. There are potentially multiple levels of repetition in multiset categorical stimuli. More specifically, categorical stimuli with repeating elements may be partitioned into  m  equivalent classes of repeating elements. Thus, if  m  = 1, the categorical stimulus contains a single repeating element, if  m  = 2, it contains two repeating elements, and so on. In contrast, the logical manifold operator produces nonsensical results that stand in stark contrast to those produced by the structural manifold operator.

To further appreciate the absurdity of the result obtained from applying the logical manifold operator on multisets, one need only turn to the law of invariance where a tradeoff between degree of invariance and cardinality is expressed. Under the logical manifold of X, this category would have a degree of difficulty equal to the cardinality of the categorical stimulus. On the other hand, the structural manifold of X assigns a commensurately higher degree of invariance to categories with repeating object stimuli which in turn significantly lowers the raw complexity of the categorical stimulus as measured by its cardinality.

Under certain circumstances, the order of object stimuli presentation may impact concept learning, categorization behavior, and related processes. Another way of thinking about this phenomenon is in terms of overall cognitive workload when attempting to learn a sequence of object stimuli for a fixed period of time that are processed in working memory. In other words, as exemplars of object stimuli accumulate in working memory, these may form different partial categorical stimuli that incrementally are subjected to invariance detection in memory. Nonetheless, as mentioned previously in this section, it is important to recognize that these models can only work with structural manifolds ( s -invariance), not logical manifolds ( p -invariance), because the dynamic incremental character of categorical stimuli described above often involves repeating object stimuli. Indeed, repeating object stimuli in categorical stimuli may often be desired when testing the dynamics of repetition learning.

In  Vigo (2015)  two probabilistic process models of categorization behavior are derived from GIST. These types of models compute the probability of any one object stimulus (or exemplar) of a categorical stimulus being categorized correctly. The first involves using logistic regression on the dimensional variables defining the categorical stimuli, but weighing these variables with the additive inverses of the components or kernel values of the structural manifolds. To explain, recall that these structural manifold components give the degree of redundancy or diagnosticity on an inverse scale of each dimension. Thus, using their additive inverse as coefficients of the dimensional variables in the negative linear exponent of the logistic equation eliminates the need for free parameters and, thus, the possibility of overfitting the data. It also determines the probability of categorizing each object stimulus correctly as a function of invariance structure.

The second approach uses Generalized Representational Information Theory (GRIT;  Vigo, 2012 ), a theory derived from GIST. GRIT and its precursor based on CIT, Representational Information Theory ( Vigo, 2011b ) propose a measure of the degree of informativeness that each subset of a categorical stimulus carries about the categorical stimulus. When the subsets are singletons, the degree of informativeness of each object stimulus can be computed. Once the most informative object is identified, it serves as a frame of reference or prototype for determining the probability of membership in the categorical stimulus using the SoftMax function or  Luce's Choice Axiom (1959) .

These two models appear promising when applied to classification data from current experimental work in progress by the author. However, both models can only work in their full generality when based on structural manifolds because logical manifolds do not apply meaningfully to other than categorical stimuli defined over binary dimensions.

Mathematical descriptions of two cognitive mechanisms for invariance detection were discussed, culminating in a proof of the invariance equivalence principle. Under  p -invariance, observers perturb categorical stimuli as a whole by perturbing their individual object stimuli across each relevant dimension. This process results in the discovery of invariants and their proportion in categorical stimuli. These proportions are assembled as a vector in invariance space (i.e., a logical manifold) where the norm of the vector represents the perceived degree of overall global invariance or logical coherence. This mechanism makes sense when one considers how our perceptual systems distort (perturb) reality to serve the limits of their information processing capacity. This has been observed by perception researchers with respect to the optical illusions that humans experience when attempting to increase the logical coherence or structural homogeneity of juxtaposed or compound stimuli.

In contrast, under  s -invariance, a cognitive mechanism involving partial similarity assessment, dimensional suppression, and rapid attention shifting reveals the relative invariance structure of the categorical stimulus and its conceptual essence. Interestingly, if dimensional suppression is construed as a type of general perturbation across categorical stimuli, the two notions may be reconciled. This reconciliation suggests that maybe both types of invariance detection mechanisms are utilized by humans:  p -invariance for the processing of regular categorical stimuli defined by binary dimensions and  s -invariance for all other types of categorical stimuli.

Although both mechanisms for invariance structure detection have at their root similar vector representations as described in previous sections, differences in their computational tractability by a human observer are undeniable. For one, in the case of  s -invariance, determining the structural manifold of any categorical stimulus takes place in polynomial time, for it reduces to a simple matrix computation per suppressed dimension. However, this is not the case for  p -invariance with respect to

Thus, a conjecture arises: is there a heuristic capable of approximating  s -invariance results on irregular categorical stimuli that is strictly based on the perturbation mechanism underlying  p -invariance? Probably not.  Vigo (2015)  explored that possibility but did not test the proposed solution. This problem may be regarded as the cognitive equivalent of the  P =NP? problem in computational complexity theory.

Only empirical investigations designed to probe into the nature of each invariance detection mechanism may yield the answer as to which may be more plausible in terms of how the brain processes information. For now, given that  s -invariance accomplishes all that the very restricted form of  p -invariance accomplishes and far more, the more tenable mechanism remains the former. Indeed, there is some empirical evidence from eye-tracking experiments where fixation and saccades data point at the cognitive possibility and tractability of  s -invariance structure detection ( Vigo, 2015 ;  Zhao, 2019 ). However, more rigorous empirical research must be conducted to confirm this hypothesis.

In GIST, categorical stimuli are represented by their primal sketches or ideotypes which in turn are described mathematically by structural manifolds. One might say that ideotypes are generated when the structural manifold operator operates on categorical stimuli from the environment and from the mind itself. Ideotypes are precursors to other concept representations. They carry the key structural information needed to form the various well-known concept representations. The distance between any ideotype and the  0  ideotype represents the degree of categorical invariance or categorical logical coherence detected in a categorical stimulus. It, along with a scaling parameter  k , determines the perceived logical coherence of the category structure that the ideotype encodes. The parameter  k  is called an ideotype discrimination parameter because its value indicates the degree of perceived discrimination between a set of ideotypes (and in turn, a set of corresponding structures). This discrimination is a function of the number of dimensions of the ideotype space.

Discrimination in ideotype space refers to the ability to discriminate between relational or structural information and not between object-exemplars as in exemplar models. For the latter models, an increase in dimensionality is consistent with higher discrimination. The opposite is true in GIST, where an increase in dimensionality is consistent with lower discrimination between an ideotype and the zero point in ideotype space (i.e., the  0  ideotype). Thus, the relationship between ideotype discriminability and number of dimensions is an inverse one. To explain, consider two categorical stimuli A and B of different dimensionality but with the same degree of categorical invariance or gestalt homogeneity. Let's say that A is two-dimensional and consists of a black triangle and a black square. On the other hand, B is three-dimensional and consists of a large black triangle and a small black square. Observers generally find A to be more homogeneous than B even though they both have the same unique diagnostic dimension of color. This can be easily explained by the fact that B has more relevant dimensions. In general, all other things being equal, the higher the dimensionality of a categorical stimulus, the less logically coherent or homogeneous it will appear to be, and the closer its ideotype will be to the  0  ideotype. That is, the more difficult it will be to discriminate it from the  0  ideotype.

More specifically, the degree of discrimination in ideotype space (as characterized by the parameter  k ) is inversely proportional to the number of dimensions associated with the ideotypes. In other words, the greater the dimensionality of the ideotype space, the smaller the discrimination parameter will be. On the other hand, when we let  k  be a free parameter, we can also hypothetically determine to what extent the dimensionality of the categorical stimulus (i.e., the number of dimensions that define it) is directly affecting the classification performance of a particular participant. Indeed, for any number of relevant dimensions  D , lower than ideal  D 
                         0 / D  values are consistent with an inability to process (i.e., an inability to detect SKs of) categorical stimuli defined over  D  dimensions. Note that the value  k  is also a function of exposure time: that is, the more time a participant is exposed to a categorical stimulus, the more time the participant has to accurately detect its SKs in spite of its number of dimensions. Thus, the parameter  k  may also be consistent with how long participants are exposed to a particular categorical stimulus. This latter interpretation, along with two formal derivations of the law, are discussed in detail in the book titled  Mathematical Principles of Human Conceptual Behavior  ( Vigo, 2015 ); however, it is still subject to empirical verification.
