JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY: 2, 330-341 (1965)  Habituation of the GSR Component of the Orienting Reflex during Paired-Associate Learning before and after Learning Has Taken  Place  WALTER KINTSCHI  University of Missouri, Columbia, Missouri  The magnitude of the GSR was used as a measure of the strength of the orienting response during paired-associate learning. The  momentary resistance changes which occurred whenever an item was presented were recorded. In Experiment 1 32 subjects learned  to associate nonsense syllables with the responses 1 and 2. The learning data could be described by a two-state Markov model.  The GSR increased in magnitude up to the trial of the last error. After that the response began to habituate. Similar results  were obtained for response latencies. Experiment II was a conventional pairedassociate task with nonsense syllables as  responses. Thirty subjects were run. A threestate Markov model was used to describe the learning data. The GSR was again  correlated with the states of the model: it tended to increase in the initial state, remained at its peak during intermediate  trials, and habituated after learning was completed.  The term orienting reflex (Pavlov, 1928) refers to the respiratory, cardiac, EEG, and galvanic skin responses or to eye or  body movements which are elicited by any new, unknown stimulus. Pavlov distinguished the orienting reflex from two other types  of unconditioned reflexes, the adaptive reflex and the defensive reflex. Two properties of the orienting reflex provide the  criteria for this distinction: first, the nonspecificity of the orienting response, that is, its independence of stimulus  modality; second, the fast habituation of the orienting response. The decrement in the orienting response and its eventual  cessation upon repeated presentations of the same stimulus have been described in detail by Sokolov (1960). Physical identity  of the stimulus is, however, not a necessary condition for the habituation of the orienting response. Luria and Vinogradova  (1959) found habituation to whole stimulus classes, e.g., to words. Unger (1964) recently reported habituation of the  vasoconstrictive response to numbers presented in a series.  Since learning experiments involve repeated stimulus presentations, orienting responses to these stimuli will occur and  eventually habituate. A  two-state Markov model provides a good and detailed description for such tasks (Bower, 1961). Of course, this model deals only  with the response sequence coded in terms of correct and incorrect answers on each trial. It is not concerned with other  measurable properties of behavior which accompany this response sequence, such as the orienting reflex or response latencies. In the second experiment a more complex paired-associate task was studied. The measure of the orienting reflex used  in both experiments was the galvanic skin response.  METHOD  EXPERIMENT I  Subjects. The 44 subjects were drawn from an introductory psychology class. Seven subjects were rejected after the pretask for  lack of measurable galvanic skin responses. Five more subjects were lost because they failed to complete the learning task  within the 50-min experimental session.  Apparatus. A Hunter Cardmaster was employed to present the learning material. GSR was recorded with a Physiograph from the E &  M Instrument Co. A soft lead electrode was placed around the subject's fingertip; a plate electrode on the subject's wrist  served as ground. A constant do current of 201LA was applied to the electrodes and resistance changes were recorded  continuously throughout the experimental session on paper moving with a speed of 5 mm/sec. Basal resistance levels were also  determined. The Hunter Cardmaster was connected to an event marker in such a way that the onset of the anticipation interval  was recorded. Whenever a subject gave his response a Grason-Stadler voice-operated relay produced another deflection of the  event marker. Thus it was possible to relate the GSR to stimulus presentations and the subject's responses. Furthermore,  response latencies could be measured.  Procedure. A 10-15 min pretask was given in order to acquaint the subjects with the experimental procedure and to allow the  basal resistance level to stabilize. A 5-item list of common words paired with numbers was used in the pretask. In the main  experiment subjects learned to associate 12 nonsense syllables with the numbers 1 and 2. The syllables were all of 600 Glaze  association values. The anticipation procedure was used with a fixed 4-sec anticipation interval and a 2-sec inter-item  interval. The duration of the joint presentation of the syllablenumber pairs was experimenter controlled and was usually  around 6 sec. These rather long durations were necessary in order to allow the GSR to return to baseline before the next item  was presented. The inter-trial interval was approximately 30 sec. Items were presented in a different order on each trial. Ten  trials were given.  EXPERIMENT II  Subjects. Thirty-six students served as subjects in Experiment II. Three subjects were excluded after the pretask because of  measurement problems and 3 subjects failed to reach the learning criterion.  332 KINTSCH  Apparatus and Procedure. The same apparatus was used as in Experiment I. The only difference between the two experiments was  in the learning material. In Experiment II subjects learned a 10-item list of two-place numbers paired with nonsense  syllables. The average association value of the nonsense syllables was 47,. The procedure was the same as in the first  experiment.  RESULTS  EXPERIMENT I  The learning data which were obtained in this experiment can be well described by Bower's (1961) all-or-none model of  paired-associate learning. The all-or-none model predicts that no improvement takes place on trials before the last error. The  average error probability on trials before the last error was 0.49 and did not vary significantly from trial to trial, as was  determined by a chi square test (X2 = 4.68, 5 df, N = 479, P > 0.30). When sequences before the last error were Vincentized  into halves, the proportion of errors was found to be 0.50 in the first half and 0.47 in the second half (X2 = 0.53, 1 df, N =  372). EXPERIMENT II  Experiment II was performed partly as a replication and partly in order to further analyze the changes in the orienting  response before learning is completed. Therefore a more complex learning task was chosen for which the simple all-or-none  model is no longer applicable. However, learning in this case can be described as a two-stage all-or-none process (Bower and  Theios, 1964; Kintsch, 1963). The first stage of the process is a transition from an initial state, where the probability of a  correct response is 0, to an intermediate state. Correct responses in the intermediate state occur with a constant  probability. In the second stage an item moves into the learning state. Transitions between the states of the model have fixed  probabilities.  The stationarity of the error proportions on trials before the last error is no longer expected by the two-stage model. The  data confirm this prediction: the Vincentized curve of trials before the last error drops from 0.95 in the first half to 0.65  in the second, X2 = 63.7, 1 df, N = 480. However, the two-stage model does predict stationarity on all trials in the  intermediate stage. The results are shown in the top panel of Fig. 5. The error proportions during the intermediate trials  decreased slightly. This decrease was statistically significant when sequences were Vincentized into halves (X2 = 7.23, 1 df,  N = 50, P < 0.01). Apart from this discrepancy the model fits fairly well. The parameters of the model were estimated from the  total number of errors and the average number of successes which were followed by an error. The learning rate was estimated to  be 0.65 in the first stage and 0.58 in the second  338 KINTSCH  stage. Observed and predicted values for the mean number of errors before the first success were 1.84 and 1.82, respectively.  The mean trial of the last error was predicted as 2.18; observed was 2.21. The numbers of error runs per item was expected to  be 1.14; observed was 1.12. However, both the distribution of the last error and the distribution of total errors differed  significantly from the predicted values, 0.01 < P < 0.05, as determined by the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test. In both cases the  discrepancy arose because more items were always correct after the first presentation than expected. The predicted  distribution of errors before the first success did not deviate significantly from the observed values. On the whole it can be  said that the model fits the data, but not without some major discrepancies.  The structure of the learning data as a two-stage all-or-none process determines the nature of the analysis of the GSR data.  All response sequences were divided into two parts: responses after the last error and responses before the last error. DISCUSSION  The magnitude of the GSR during verbal learning has been shown to depend upon the state of the learning process. The GSR began  to habituate as soon as learning was completed. Between the first presentation of an item and the trial on which the last  error was made for this item the GSR increased in magnitude in Experiment I. In order to analyze this increase further, in  Experiment II the trials before the last error were divided into two parts, an initial string of errors and a mixed sequence of  340 KINTSCH  errors and successes between the first success and the last error. The GSR increased during the initial errors, but not  significantly, and then remained at its peak value during the intermediate trials.  These results are in general agreement with the existing literature. Sokolov (1960) reported a correlation between the  habituation of the orienting reflex and the stabilization of conditioned responses in classical conditioning experiments. In  verbal learning experiments Brown (1937) and Obrist (1950) noted that the GSR follows closely the order of learning in a  serial list. Finesmith (1960) reported a peak of the GSR on the trial of the first success in paired-associate learning.  Thompson and Obrist (1964) reported that changes in the electrocortical activity reach a maximum when syllables are first  being anticipated correctly.  Perhaps it should be pointed out that the results of the present experiments are based upon averages over many subject-item  sequences and therefore do not tell the whole story about the GSR during learning. Many sequences were not as clear as the  ones selected in Figs. 1 and 2. Often a disinhibition of the already habituated orienting reflex was observed when a subject  had made an error on some other item on the same trial, or when a subject became tired or irritated towards the end of the  session. On the other hand some items were learned without any strong GSR at all.  Latency data similar to the ones observed in Experiment I were reported by Millward (1964). Suppes (1964) recorded latencies  in a concept identification experiment with children and found essentially constant latencies for the first two thirds of the  trials before the last error and a slight increase on the last third. After the last error latencies decreased.  The good correspondence between the GSR and the states postulated by the learning model might be taken as a further  verification of the model. Apparently the model does more than provide a formal description of the learning data. Other  psychological processes also correlate with the inferred structure of the learning process. The fact that the orienting  reaction is stronger in the intermediate state than at the very beginning of learning agrees well with the interpretation of  the first stage of the learning process as stimulus encoding. As long as an item is completely new, a moderate orienting  reflex is evoked. This reflex reaches maximum strength after the subject already knows something about the item but recall is  either still incomplete or unreliable. In terms of Sokolov's (1960) neuronal model one could say that a neural representation  has been built up but does not yet coincide perfectly with all the parameters of the stimulation. After an item enters the  learning state habituation sets in.  REFERENCES  BOWER, G. H. Application of a model to paired-associate learning. Psychometrika, 1961, 26, 255-280.  BOWER, G.
