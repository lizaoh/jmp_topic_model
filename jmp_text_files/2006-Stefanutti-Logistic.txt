Models for numerical testing have a relatively long tradition in psychology. They arise in a number of psychometric theories like e.g. classical testing theory or item response theory ( Hambleton & Swaminathan, 1985; Lord & Novick, 1968; Rasch, 1960/1980; Weiss, 1983 ). The nonnumerical approach to psychometrics is more recent. One of the most prominent theories in this field is knowledge space theory ( Albert, 1994; Albert & Lukas, 1998 ;  Doignon & Falmagne, 1985, 1999 ).

In numerical testing a subject is typically characterized by some uni- or multi-dimensional real-valued parameter representing her level or ability. Nonnumerical tests are combinatorial in nature. There is no numeric representation of the characteristics of a subject. In knowledge space theory a subject is characterized by the collection  K  of all problems in a given domain (set of problems) that she is capable of solving. Such collection is called a  knowledge state , and the family  
                         K 
                       of all knowledge states for a given domain  Q  is called a  knowledge structure . A standard assumption is that both the empty set and  Q  are knowledge states.

The approach presented in this paper can be regarded as an attempt to integrate, to some extent, the numerical and nonnumerical fields. The very basic and general idea is that the parameter characterizing an individual (henceforth called  ability  for short) in the numerical approach and the knowledge state characterizing that same individual in the nonnumerical approach are not independent (in the statistical sense). The ability can be regarded as a synthetic and coarse representation of a knowledge state. On the other hand, the latter can be seen as a refinement of the former, which contains more information on the exact situation of an individual with respect to a given domain of knowledge. Thus, given that the ability of a particular individual is known, it makes sense to have some expectation about the knowledge state that characterizes this individual.

Concerning the numerical representations an assumption of uni-dimensionality is made all along the paper but, in principle, the multi-dimensional case is not precluded. The relationship between the ability levels and the knowledge states is worked out on a probabilistic basis. This development essentially amounts to establish a one-to-one correspondence between the ability levels (elements in  
                         R 
                      ) and some family of probability distributions on the collection of knowledge states.

Section 2  is an overview of probabilistic knowledge structures (PKSs) ( Doignon & Falmagne, 1999; Falmagne, 1989; Falmagne & Doignon, 1988b ;  Villano, Falmagne, Johannesen, & Doignon, 1987 ), and provides the theoretical probabilistic background for the model developed in the subsequent sections. In  Section 3  a logistic model is developed for a special class of knowledge structures, called  graded knowledge structures . Then the model is extended to any finite knowledge structure.

In  Section 4  the relationship between a deterministic knowledge structure (DKS) and a PKS is taken into consideration. The concept of  logical independence  in a DKS has been proposed in  Stefanutti (2005) . In the context of independent substructures,  Doignon and Falmagne (1999)  have introduced the concept of stochastic independence in a PKS. The PKSs corresponding to a given DKS are infinitely many (see  Section 4 ), and a correspondence between logical and stochastic independence is not assured by any arbitrary choice of the PKS. One stand out feature of the proposed logistic model is to assure this type of correspondence.

Section 5  deals with parameter estimation in the logistic model. A marginal maximum likelihood (MML) method is developed for the estimation of the parameters of the model. An application example is presented in  Section 6  and some final remarks are given in  Section 7 .

In this section the relationship between a DKS and a PKS is considered. With the term DKS we simply mean any knowledge structure on a set  Q  of items, while PKS refers to a knowledge structure in which some probability distribution  p  on the states is defined. Usually a researcher constructs a DKS through some suitable method (e.g. expert query, cognitive task analysis, derivation from empirical data, or even other methods) and subsequently a probabilistic representation of this model—the PKS—is validated. Since what is constructed and what is validated are two distinct (although related) mathematical objects, it is very important that the relationship between them is as clear, unambiguous and easily interpretable as possible.

To begin with, there exists no unique PKS corresponding to a given DKS. This happens because infinitely many alternative probability distributions can be specified for the same collection of knowledge states. In a PKS there is typically an overabundance of parameters (the probabilities of the knowledge states) which make the model not identifiable. To avoid this problem one can try to reparameterize the model by introducing additional restrictions that reduce the number of free parameters. A LKS can be seen as a set of additional restrictions imposed on a PKS.

Secondly, there is a question about independence (resp., dependence) among the items. Intuitively a knowledge structure is a way to specify the interdependencies among the items in a mathematical form. A typical, although elementary example is “failing item  p  implies failing item  q ”. This assertion can be regarded as a specific assumption that has to be tested against empirical data. Of course there might be other, more sophisticated types of dependence in a knowledge structure involving, for instance subsets of items rather than single items, and different kinds of logical implications (see, in this connection,  Düntsch & Gediga, 1996 ). In the whole they form a collection of assumptions about dependence/independence that one would like to test empirically. However, because of the deterministic nature of such assumptions, they cannot be validated by means of statistical tests. Here comes the connection to PKSs. In a PKS, as in many other probabilistic models, assumptions about dependence/independence between the items are of a probabilistic form. For instance an assumption might require stochastic independence between two or more items or subsets of items; it is typical in item response theory to assume local stochastic independence among the items. Probabilistic assumptions can be tested empirically by means of standard statistical tests.

If a deterministic assumption has to be represented by a probabilistic version in an empirical test, it seems natural to require some correspondence between the former and the latter. If, for instance, the former is an assumption of independence between two or more items or subsets of items, so the latter should be.

Logical independence  in a DKS ( L-independence  for short) is a binary relation  
                         I 
                       between subsets of items, and it is developed around the concept of an  outcome . This last is a pair  
                         〈 
                         S 
                         , 
                         F 
                         〉 
                       of subsets of  Q , where  S  is the collection of all items solved by a student, and  F  contains all items failed by that student.  Stochastic independence  in a PKS (or  S-independence ) has been introduced by  Doignon and Falmagne (1999) . Formal definitions of both types of independence will be given in a moment. A logistic model for knowledge structures has been presented. This model is a special case of the general probabilistic knowledge structure (PKS) of  Falmagne and Doignon (1988b)  and has, as its special case, the simple logistic model of Rasch. It is characterized by four types of parameters: a person parameter  
                         α 
                      , and three parameters for each item: a careless error probability, a lucky guess probability and a difficulty parameter.

The model is firstly developed for graded knowledge structures, and then it is extended to any finite knowledge structure. Probabilistic models in item response theory are often based on the assumption that the probability of solving an item is a function of a person and an item parameter. In the theory of knowledge structures, this assumption would be meaningless. In a knowledge structure items are neither globally nor locally independent. Thus the probability of solving an item may not only depend on the characteristics of that particular item, but also on the characteristics of other items. The question is thus how an item can be ‘isolated’ from the effect of the other items, so that its specific characteristics can be measured. In graded knowledge structures  Axiom 5  is an attempt to answer this question. When an item is ‘isolated’ from the effect of all other relevant items, mastering that item only depends on its difficulty and on the level of the student along a uni-dimensional latent trait.

One stand out feature of logistic knowledge structures (LKSs) is preservation of independence. This feature is not shared by all PKSs. Logical independence is a binary relation between items in a deterministic knowledge structure (DKS). Stochastic independence characterizes the items in a PKS. It has been shown that in a LKS there exists a correspondence between the two forms of independence.

Concerning parameter estimation, a marginal maximum likelihood (MML) method has been developed for the estimation of the item difficulty parameters. To use this method one needs to assume some probability distribution of the  
                         α 
                       parameters in the population of students. In our development we considered the normal distribution  
                         N 
                         ( 
                         0 
                         , 
                         1 
                         ) 
                      , but other distributions might be considered as well; e.g. the normal distribution  
                         N 
                         ( 
                         μ 
                         , 
                         σ 
                         ) 
                       where  
                         μ 
                       and  
                         σ 
                       are estimated from the data, along with the difficulty parameters.

The model has been applied to the data coming from a sample of 500 subjects to whom a test of 40 items on inductive reasoning was administered. For illustrative purposes an application of the model to five of the 40 items has been presented. The choice to restrict the application to a small number of items was imposed by practical considerations. These considerations mainly concern the construction of an initial, hypothetical knowledge structure for the items at issue. The specific content of the single items was unknown to us, and the knowledge structure had to be extracted from the empirical data. The construction process is based on some initial collection of knowledge states which is assumed to be a superset of the true knowledge structure. This initial collection is then refined in a number of steps. With no information on the exact content of the items, it was hard to figure out what this initial knowledge structure should be. The risk was to leave out some crucial knowledge states. It was thus decided to take, as the initial knowledge structure, the power set on  Q . This, however, was feasible only because the cardinality of  Q  was small.

The fit to the data of the initial knowledge structure was already pretty good. The refinement procedure allowed to derive a knowledge structure, containing 14 knowledge states, whose fit was better than that of the initial one. Moreover, in terms of number of knowledge states, this model was ‘simpler’ than the initial one. A substantial difference between the two models is in how they embed assumptions concerning dependence/independence among the items. In the more complex knowledge structure the essential assumption is that each item is L-independent (and thus locally S-independent) of every other item. In the simplest model the items are assumed to be not independent of each other.

A final remark concerns model selection. Besides the likelihood of the model, two additional criteria seem to be relevant: (C1) the number of states should be minimum and (C2) the lucky guess and the careless error probabilities should be minimum. However, for any fixed value of the model likelihood, these two criteria are in contraposition and the best fitting model should be a trade-off between them. How this trade-off could be chosen is not trivial and represents at the moment an open question. A simple, although not totally satisfactory solution has been proposed, and further studies are needed.

The model selection problem arises especially when there is no prior information or knowledge about the interdependencies among the items. In these cases the knowledge structure must be extracted directly from the empirical data. On the other side, in an empirical application, theoretical knowledge about the items could guide the researcher in formulating an hypothetical knowledge structure. This would allow to eliminate a hopefully large number of theoretically implausible models (e.g. models that violate some construct validity) and, in turn, to restrict the collection of competing models. As already observed, it was not possible to follow this route in the example given in this paper.
