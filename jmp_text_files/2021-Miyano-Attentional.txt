The attentional blink (AB) refers to a deficit in the processing of the second stimulus, which is revealed when two target stimuli are presented in a rapid serial visual presentation (RSVP) stream; the correct processing of the first target ( 
                         
                            
                               T 
                            
                            
                               1

                               T

This processing deficit has been widely expected to provide important information about the deployment of attention in the temporal domain, and so many researchers investigated the AB to propose informal or formal models which explain the mechanisms of the AB (e.g.,  Dux & Marois, 2009 , for an extensive review).

Theoretical models explaining the AB are classified into two groups, informal and formal models ( Dux & Marois, 2009 ). An informal model qualitatively describes the processes underlying the AB, and a formal model often referred to as computational model provides the computational frameworks which make it possible to simulate several features of the AB.

Informal models include the inhibition model ( Raymond et al., 1992 ), the interference theory ( Shapiro et al., 1994 ), the two-stage model ( Chun & Potter, 1995 ), and the hybrid model consisting of the two-stage model and the interference theory ( Vogel et al., 1998 ). Each of these models explain the AB by employing its unique mechanism but under the common assumption that the AB reflects limited resources for the processing of the second target. That is, the AB occurs because  
                         
                            
                               T 
                            
                            
                               1 
                            
                         
                       processing results in the insufficient resources for  
                         
                            
                               T 
                            
                            
                               2 
                            
                         
                       processing.

Limited resources models cannot explain the fact that the AB is attenuated or diminished when up to four targets are to be identified within an RSVP stream of distractors, as long as a target is not followed by a distractor. This fact is referred to as the spread of sparing effect (e.g.,  Di Lollo et al., 2005 ). Then, under the idea that the spread of sparing might be explained by attentional control to the task of detecting and identifying targets, several computational models of the AB have been suggested, which include boost and bounce model ( Olvers & Meeter, 2008 ), attention cascade model ( Shih, 2008 ), threaded cognition model ( Taatgen et al., 2009 ), episodic simultaneous type serial token model (eSTST) ( Wyble et al., 2009 ), and perceptual wink model ( Rusconi & Huber, 2018 ) though this model attributes the AB phenomena as the result of the priming effects induced by targets and distractors. Among the formal models, the eSTST model is well known as a computational model which successfully simulate not only the AB but also the related phenomena observed for 3 or four targets stimuli in an RSVP stream, and the perceptual wink model is known to have at least the same simulation ability as the eSTST model, although the perceptual wink model gives a novel account of those AB phenomena based on perceptual dynamics.

In general, the computational models are composed of some information processing components which are physiologically plausible (e.g., eSTST model) or reflect cognitive mechanisms such as sensory processing and working memory (e.g., boost and bounce model, attention cascade model, threaded cognition model). The former models are often referred to as neural network models, and the latter models might be referred to as structural models ( Shih, 2008 ). Neural network models have an advantage in that interactions within and between cognitive components can be described in detail, and the structural models have the merits of employing well established cognitive mechanisms. The computational models, especially the eSTST model and the perceptual wink models are appropriate for the simulation of the AB phenomena, but require some other data for quantitative analysis of the experimental data on the AB like  Fig. 1 , since they have so many parameters to be tuned or estimated. That is, some of the parameters of a computational model are set to the values or estimates obtained from some other data. For example, the perceptual wink model utilizes the estimates obtained from word priming data, and eventually has four free parameters to explain the AB data.

In this paper, first we propose a simple mathematical model of the AB based on a logistic function and a first-order difference system which governs dynamic change of attentional inhibition. This difference system plays a role of securing attentional resources for target processing, and is triggered by targets but not distractors, unlike the input filter of the boost and bounce model which is triggered by both of them. Then, we discuss the maximum likelihood estimation of the parameters included in the model. Numerical examples are given to validate our estimation method. Also we show that our model not only explains some hallmark effects observed in many studies on the AB but also can be used for their mathematical analysis.

Logistic models are commonly used for analysis of count or proportion data (e.g.,  Efron & Hastie, 2016 ). In psychology, logistic models are used for modeling choice behavior (BTL model;  Bradley & Terry, 1952; Luce, 1959 ) such as preference, signal detection, stimulus identification, and so on. The BTL model is one of the most popular models, which describes our probabilistic choice behaviors picking one from given alternatives. In other words, the BTL model is appropriate for modeling the probability of correct response under the existence of some interruptive factors.

In the present paper, we proposed a logistic model of the attentional blink (AB), and showed how it explains some typical effects relating to the AB. Also, by applying the maximum likelihood estimation method to two AB data, we illustrated that our model might be useful for not only mathematical analysis of the model’s behaviors, but also quantitative analysis of the empirical data relating to the AB.

The existing computational models explain the AB by postulating a variety of structural mechanisms which include the mechanism for consolidating targets (e.g., eSTST model), that for filtering out distractors (e.g., boost and bounce model), and a failure to perceive that the second target belongs to the target category (perceptual wink model). In the same manner, our model postulates an inhibition mechanism expressed as a first-order difference system which may be too simple compared to the existing mechanisms. However, without this simple system, mathematical discussions on the AB phenomena may be impossible.

The attentional blink is known to be an effective phenomenon which provides important information about the deployment of attention, and has been studied mostly from experimental or computational points of view. We hope our simple logistic model along with its estimation method gives an alternative approach to investigate various effects of the AB. Especially, our ML estimation method could provide a reliable measure to individual difference studies of the AB, such as the studies on relationship between the magnitude of an individual’s AB and cognitive mechanisms (e.g.,  Dale et al., 2013 ).
