Stochastic Learning Paths in a Knowledge Structure 

JEAN-CLAUDE FALMAGNE 

University of California, Irvine 

This paper presents a stochastic process describing the progress of subjects (for example, students learning a particular field) over a period of time. Typical data involve a fixed sample of subjects tested repeatedly. At the core of the model is a knowledge structure, that is, a possibly large collection Q of items, together with a family of its subsets representing the possible knowledge states. The basic prediction concerns the joint probabilities

The prediction is obtained by analyzing the possible learning paths of the subject through the knowledge structure, mapping out the visits of possible knowledge states at the times of the tests, and integrating over the possible learning rates of the subjects. The learning rate of the subjects, and the times required to master the items along a learning path, are assumed to be distributed gamma. These results elaborate, as a stochastic process, a model proposed earlier by the author. © 1993 Academic Press, Inc. 

INTRODUCTION 

If scholastic education is to be successful, accurate monitoring of the students’ progress must take place. That is, tests or exams must be administered at appropriate intervals. We consider here a situation in which essentially the same test is given repeatedly—for example, every year or every other year—to the same (large) sample of students. For concreteness, we may suppose that the field of study is some part of elementary mathematics. The basic intuition of the theory is that any student progresses through some learning path. As time passes, the student successively masters the items—or the associated concepts—encountered along the learning path. Since learning paths are not necessarily gradations, it may happen that a student will have to acquire several items jointly. (For example, a student in state K has to jointly master items x and y if the state immediately following K in the learning path is Ku {x, y}.) Students may differ from each other by their learning rate, and by the particular learning path followed. We assume that, for a given population of subjects, the learning rate is a random variable, which will be denoted by L. A graphical illustration of a density function for this random variable L is given at the left of Fig. 1, which will be useful for the rest of this section. Consider a student equipped with a learning rate L=A, and suppose that this student, for various accidental reasons, is engaged in the learning path (4). We also suppose that, at the outset, this student is naive. Thus, the first state encountered will be @. Reaching the next state requires mastering the concepts associated with item a. This may or may not be laborious, depending on the learning rate of the student, and the difficulty of the item. In this theory, the time required to reach state {a} is a random variable, the distribution of which depends upon these 2 factors: the item difficulty and the student’s learning rate, represented here by the number /. The item difficulty will be measured by a parameter of the theory. 

The next state encountered along learning path (4) is {a,b}. Again, the time required for a transition from state {a} to state {a,b} is a random variable, the distribution of which depends upon 4 and the difficulty of item 5. And so on. Ifa test takes place, the student will respond according to his or her current state. However, we do not assume that the responses to items contained in the current state will necessarily be correct. Careless errors are always possible. (Anyone can make an error in long division.) Nor do we suppose that the responses to items not contained in the current state will always be false. (Some problems may be solvable

Applying a theory of such a complexity raises a number of issues, which we now address. 

To begin with, some readers may cringe at this plethora of parameters, and wonder whether an application of this theory is a realistic prospect. Actually, as indicated earlier in this paper, the theory has been successfully applied to several sets of data, in the special case of a test given only once (Falmagne ef ail., 1990). Notice that, when a test is administered several times, the number of parameters does not increase, while the number of response categories—te., the number of degrees of freedom in the data—increases exponentially. If the number of learning paths is not prohibitive, or if some Markovian assumptions in the spirit of the last section are satisfied, the complexity of the theory will remain well beneath that of the data to be explained. A second issue concerns the knowlege structure 4 at the core of the theory. For the theory to be applicable, a knowledge structure must be assumed. Methods to obtain such a knowledge structure have been discussed elsewhere (Koppen, 1989; Koppen& Doignon, 1990; Falmagne et al, 1990; Kambouri et al, 1991; Miiller, 1989; Dowling, 1991, in press; Villano, 1991). A reasonable procedure is to start the analysis with a tentative knowledge structure, presumed to contain all the right states, and possibly some subsets of questions which are not states. [f the application of the model proves to be successful, this starting knowledge structure can be progressively refined by assuming that some learning paths have probability zero, thereby eliminating some states.
