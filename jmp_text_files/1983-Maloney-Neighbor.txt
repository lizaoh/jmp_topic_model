Table la gives the coordinates of one sample of points drawn. The  coordinates of the n + 1 points are converted to an n + 1-by-n + 1 symmetric distance matrix (Table lb) between points.  Throughout the paper, all distances are Euclidean unless otherwise stated.  Each row in the distance matrix is converted to a ranking of distances in that row. The diagonal element is ignored. The  smallest distance (the nearest neighbor) becomes 1, and so forth. Each row in the rank matrix (Table lc) is a permutation of  the numbers (1, 2,..., n) with blanks on the diagonals. The output of the first, generating, phase of the simulation is 100  rank matrices and some descriptive information concerning the parameters of the simulation. These rank matrices are the only  information available to the analysis programs.  For each rank matrix, the analysis program computes, for each i, the quantity N(i, n), the number of points (other than i)  which rank i as their nearest neighbor. This quantity is just the number of 1's in the ith column of the rank matrix.  For each rank matrix, then, the program computes the centrality C of the matrix and creates a frequency count, with 100(n + 1)  total counts, of the values of N(i, n) for all i, for all 100 rank matrices. The next process resembles the uniform process on the sphere in that n points in the sample of size n + 1 are iid, generated  from a distribution uniform on the surface of the unit d-sphere. The remaining point is chosen in a special manner: it has  fixed location at the origin, the center of the sphere. Obtained centralities C are shown in Table 10. When d is small most  points on the surface of the sphere are very likely nearer to other points on the sphere than to the point at the origin. The  resulting distribution of N(i, n) is essentially that of points distributed on the unit d-sphere. When d is large, however,  the expected distance between points on the surface of the unit d-sphere becomes large relative to the distance to the origin  (which is always 1). Consequently the central, nonstochastically chosen, point is more and more often selected as nearest  neighbor by points on the surface of the sphere.  The values of mean centrality recorded for d = 100 in Table 10 in all cases reach maximum centrality, (n z + 1)/(n + 1). The  single point at the origin is selected as nearest neighbor by all other points in all 100 replications. V, THE CONSEQUENCES OF MEASUREMENT ERRORS FOR  COMPUTED CENTRALITY  Proximity data in most applications is contaminated by error. This contamination introduces spurious higher dimensions into  any solution obtained and, conceivably, by increasing the effective value of d relative to n, could increase the computed  value of centrality for the data.
