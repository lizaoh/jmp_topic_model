Attention is typically characterized as selective enhancement of the processing of certain stimuli and the suppression of the processing of other surrounding stimuli. A common metaphor for these processes is a moving “spotlight” with variable width and monotonically decreasing intensity (e.g.  Eriksen & St. James, 1986 ;  Laberge, 1983 ;  Eimer, 1997 ). However, there is evidence that attention to stationary targets oscillates over time in a wave-like manner, a form of rhythmic sampling (e.g.,  VanRullen, 2018 ). For example,  Fiebelkorn and Kastner (2019)  review evidence for rhythmic temporal variations in spatial sensitivity for signal detection, which were associated with the phase of theta band oscillations in neural activity occurring at 3 – 8 Hz. in various brain regions. Additionally, rhythmic sampling could shift between spatially separated target regions during the sections of these rhythmic episodes with lessened spatial sensitivity. Evidence for temporally rhythmic attention has also been provided by  Jones (1976 ,  2010 ) and colleagues, who modeled attention to temporally rhythmic stimuli as an entrainment of resonant oscillators (see also  Lakatos et al., 2008 ).

In addition to temporal oscillations of attention, there is also evidence for spatial oscillations. For example, a number of researchers investigating the Eriksen flanker task (e.g.,  Müller et al., 2005 ;  Ahmed & de Fockert, 2012 ;  Lee & Pitt, 2022 ) have found evidence for oscillatory spatial patterns of attention. In one version this task participants are instructed to quickly respond to the direction of a central target arrow that is surrounded by other flanker arrows that may or may not be in the same direction. In another version of the task, participants respond to the identity of a target letter that is surrounded by distractor letters that may be the same or different from the target. These researchers found evidence for a major central lobe of attention and a smaller adjacent secondary lobe of attention with a region of inhibition in between the lobes. This pattern can be interpreted as the difference between a normal shaped distribution of attentional emphasis and a normal shaped distribution of spatial inhibition with a larger standard deviation (e.g.,  Downing, 1988 ;  Lee, 2021 ). However, an alternative interpretation is that spatial attention is inherently wave-like, and that people have some difficulty with top-down control to shape the wave into a narrow region of focused attention. Hence, the secondary lobes occur.

Additional evidence for spatially wave-like attention comes from drivers’ distributions of attention to roadway preview in a simulated driving task ( Jagacinski et al., 2019 ). These experiments inferred drivers’ spatial distributions of attention from their steering movements in response to roadway perturbations. Optimal control theory indicated that the driving task required selective attention to preview close to the vehicle and gradually decreasing attention to preview farther ahead ( Miller, 1976 ). The peaks of the measured spatial attentional distributions varied stochastically such that they had a major lobe for previewed roadway close to the vehicle and a smaller secondary lobe farther away. For both the preview driving task ( Jagacinski et al., 2019 ) and the Eriksen flanker task the ideal attentional distribution is narrow, i.e., to either emphasize preview positions close to the vehicle ( Miller, 1976 ) or to ignore distracting flanker arrows and letters, respectively. However, the measured attentional distributions are much wider than ideal and exhibit distinct secondary lobes indicative of an underlying wave-like process that is difficult to control.

A rapid sequence of visual or auditory events may be perceived as a single continuing pattern or as two or more separate patterns occurring in parallel (e.g.,  Bregman, 1990 ). The latter is often referred to as “streaming.” It occurs when the time between successive stimuli is short and the perceived distance is large, e.g., in visual space or pitch space for auditory stimuli. Klapp ( Klapp et al., 2019 , p. 40) suggested that this perception of multiple parallel patterns may indicate a velocity limitation on the movement of attention, which is one interpretation of the Gestalt grouping principle known as Korte's Third Law (e.g., see  Klapp & Jagacinski, 2011 ). The law indicates that the ratio of distance to the time between successive discrete events determines whether they are perceived as motion of a single object or as separate parallel streams of events.  van Noorden (1975)  and  Jones and Yee (1993)  have similarly discussed a velocity boundary for (change in auditory frequency) / (time interval between events) beyond which continuous attending to a single trajectory changes to a streamed percept.

The present paper follows up on these suggestions and varies the height and timing of a rectangular sequence of lights to test for linear, velocity-like boundaries at which streaming appears or disappears. The slopes of these linear relations are used to estimate a constant velocity of attention in the context of a wave-like model of attention in time and space.

The rectangular motion pattern used for the experiment is shown in  Fig. 1 
                         . A rectangular pattern was previously investigated by  Hock et al. (1993) , who looked for transitions between perceptions of vertical and horizontal movement when diagonal pairs of lights were alternately displayed. In the present experiment, a single small spot of light on a computer display successively appeared in the lower left ( 1 ), upper left ( 2 ), lower right ( 3 ), upper right ( 4 ), and then repeated the pattern multiple times. The dwell time at each corner, Δt, was gradually increased on some trials, and gradually decreased on other trials.

Consider the case of gradual decrease in Δt. The effective velocity of a transition between successively numbered corners can be considered to be the distance between them divided by Δt. In the rectangle shown in  Fig. 1 , the height is 2 units, the width is 1 unit, and the diagonal is 2.2 units long. The effective velocity of the  1 → 2  and  3 → 4  transitions can be considered to be the height of the rectangle divided by the time between the midpoints of the two successive dwell times, which equals 2/Δt. Similarly, the effective velocity of the  2 → 3  and  4 → 1  transitions is 2.2/Δt. One can similarly calculate the effective velocity of the transitions between any of the other pairs of corners, and these are shown in  Fig. 1 . In all, there are 12 transitions that can be thought of as competing for one's attention.

If attention has a limiting velocity, then as Δt is gradually decreased, and the rectangular pattern speeds up, some of the 12 transitions will be too fast for attention to follow them. The competition for attention will be among the slower transitions that are below the limiting velocity. As Δt is decreased even further, only the slowest transitions will be processed within the constraint of the limiting attentional velocity. These are the horizontal transitions at the top and bottom of the rectangle, which have an effective velocity of 1/2Δt. In other words, as the rectangular pattern is gradually sped up, only horizontal motion should be seen, and there should be two parallel motions, one at the top and one at the bottom of the rectangle. At high speeds these two motions are seen as occurring relatively independently, a phenomenon called “streaming” as noted above. Streaming can be considered a way for the attentional system to cope with a very fast movement pattern by treating it as two slower movement patterns separated in space. This percept is very different from the percept at very slow speeds, in which a single dot is perceived to jump from  1  to  2  to  3  to  4  and so on. This latter percept is called “integrated” in that only a single trajectory is perceived rather than two simultaneous trajectories as in the case of the perceived horizontal movements.

In the present experiment, the speed of the rectangular motion pattern was gradually increased or decreased until participants reported that they saw the onset or loss of horizontal motion, respectively. The Δt at this instant will be referred to as the critical dwell time, Δt h *. The subscript refers to rectangle height, h, which is equal to 2 units in  Fig. 1 . The experiment used a set of different heights ranging from 2 to 8 units. The prediction was that a velocity constraint on attention should be manifest as a straight line relation between Δt h * and rectangle height. The slope, the ratio of the change in rectangular height to the change in critical dwell time, should then provide an estimate of the velocity of attention waves.

Participants were 40 college students between the ages of 18 and 35, who participated as part of a research experience portion of an introductory psychology course. All were tested for 20/25 corrected vision. 19 were female, and 21 were male. This research complied with the American Psychological Association Code of Ethics and was approved by the Institutional Review Board at Ohio State University. Informed consent was obtained from each participant.

Participants viewed the rectangular pattern of flashing lights from a distance of six feet (1.8 m). The rectangle had seven different heights corresponding to 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, and 8 times the width. The vertical extent of the tallest rectangle was 2.85° of visual angle, and the width was a constant 0.36° of visual angle for all of the rectangles. The display was updated at 100 Hz.

Participants viewed a black dot against a white background successively move among the four corners of the rectangle in a sequence from lower left to upper left to lower right to upper right, and then repeat. The dwell time at each of the rectangle's corners started at 0.04 s and gradually increased, or started at 0.50 s and gradually decreased on different trials. The dwell time was increased (0.04 s start) or decreased (0.50 s start) by 5% every 3 s. The trials with increasing dwell time had a starting cycle frequency of 1/(0.04 s x 4) = 6.25 cycles/s that gradually decreased. The trials with decreasing dwell time had a starting cycle frequency of 1/(0.50 s x 4) = 0.50 cycles/s that gradually increased. The duration of one cycle of the rectangular pattern is equal to the sum of the dwell times at each of the 4 corners of the rectangle.

Participants had eight practice trials consisting of Increasing and Decreasing Frequencies at heights of 2, 4, 6, and 8 times the width. For trials starting at 6.25 cycles/s and gradually decreasing, they were instructed to report when they no longer perceived only horizontal motion. For trials starting at 0.50 cycles/s and gradually increasing, they were instructed to report when they first started to perceive only horizontal motion. After the practice trials, there was a brief break followed by 7 trials of all Increasing Frequencies for half of the participants and all Decreasing Frequencies for the other half of the participants. For half of the participants in each group the height of the rectangle increased from 2 to 8 times the width across trials, and for the other half of the participants the height of the rectangle decreased from 8 to 2 times the width across trials.

For each participant, a regression line was calculated between the height of the rectangle (2 - 8) and the critical dwell time, Δt h *, at which the transition to or from only horizontal motion occurred. The regression line was fit to the critical dwell time measurements Δt h * at 7 heights per participant, so correlations of  r (5) = 0.67 or more were statistically significant at the 0.05 level, one-tailed. This correlation was significant for 13 of 20 participants with Increasing Frequencies, and for 14 of 20 participants with Decreasing Frequencies. The correlations for these 27 participants formed a dense cluster between 0.67 and.98. The 13 participants with nonsignificant linear correlations had a diffuse distribution of correlations ranging from −0.34 to 0.61.

The inverse slope of the regression line for predicting critical dwell times from rectangle heights was calculated for each of the 27 participants with significant individual correlations. The dimensions of the inverse slope are degrees of visual angle per second, and this measure is believed to be related to attentional velocity. Two of the Decreasing Frequency participants responded very near the beginning of each trial and had unusually high inverse slopes greater than 750°/s. These two outliers were omitted from further statistical analysis. Based on the data of the other 25 participants demonstrating linear trends, the mean of the inverse slopes for individual participants was 93.8°/s (standard deviation = 50.9°/s) for the 12 participants with Decreasing Frequencies, and 19.5°/s (standard deviation = 13.1°/s) for the 13 participants with Increasing Frequencies. This difference is statistically reliable, Welch  t (12.3) = 4.9,  p  < .01. The ratio of these mean boundary slopes is 4.8 to 1. These measures provide evidence of two different velocity boundaries in perceiving the competing motions in the rectangular pattern. Gender differences were not analyzed given the limited sample size.

The present experiment was motivated by  Klapp's et al. (2019)  interpretation of Korte's Third Law that the boundary between streamed and integrated percepts is indicative of a velocity limit on the movement of attention and by similar observations of a velocity boundary for streaming by  van Noorden (1975)  and  Jones and Yee (1993) . A velocity boundary was evidenced in the present experiment by varying the height of the rectangular pattern and finding that the critical dwell time at the integrated - streamed boundary increased in a linear manner for a majority of the participants. In other words, the ratio of the change in rectangular height to the change in critical dwell time was approximately constant as would be expected if attention had a finite velocity.

In measuring the boundary between streamed and integrated percepts, one gets different results depending on whether the stimulus is speeding up or slowing down. Directional effects in making perceptual judgements are sometimes described as hysteresis (e.g.,  Hock et al., 1993 ), which refers to a persistence of an initial perceptual state prior to a transition. However, the pattern of transitions in the present experiment did not show persistence of the initial pattern. Rather there was earlier transition to another pattern depending on the initial perception. For example, when the initial percept was of streamed horizontal motions (far left in  Fig. 2 ), that pattern did not persist to lower frequencies than when the initial percept was of motion of a single dot traversing the corners of the rectangle. Instead, the transition to integrated motion happened at much higher frequencies than the transition to streamed horizontal motions when the initial percept was integrated. This pattern is not an example of hysteresis.

Similar evidence for two transitional boundaries has been found in auditory streaming.  van Noorden (1975 ; see also  Huron, 2016 ) found two boundaries involving change in pitch interval vs. change in time interval that have very different slopes. In agreement with the present experiment, the boundary with the shallower slope corresponds to an upper boundary of a region of integrated perception, and the boundary with the steeper slope corresponds to a lower boundary of a region of streamed perception. The region between the two boundaries could exhibit either streamed or integrated percepts, as exhibited by the influence of van Noorden's instructions to the participants to attempt to maintain either integrated or streamed percepts. Similar ambiguity of the in-between region may be a feature in  Figs. 2 and 3  for the present experiment, in that entering this region from the right or the left results in different percepts, i.e., integrated from the left and streamed from the right. One difference from the present experiment is that van Noorden's data do exhibit a hysteresis effect. Namely, an integrated percept could persist throughout the middle region up to the boundary beyond which streaming is always perceived. A streamed percept could similarly persist throughout the middle region down to the boundary beyond which an integrated percept always occurs. The lack of a hysteresis effect in the present experiment may be due to the different instructions to report a switch from the initial streamed or integrated percept to its opposite. Both data sets find boundaries of an upper region that is always streamed and a lower region that is always integrated. In sum, there is ordinal agreement regarding the existence of three regions and the relative steepness of the two boundaries between regions even though the measurement procedures were different.

The evidence in  Fig. 2  supporting the hypothesis of a velocity constraint on the movement of attention and mounting evidence for wave-like behavior of attention are both consistent with a traveling wave model. Cortical traveling wave phenomena have been observed by multiple researchers (e.g.,  Benucci et al., 2007 ), and the role of attention is an ongoing area of research (e.g.,  Li & Heeger, 2019 ). The present experiment suggests that distributed attention in the context of streaming may be characterized as a traveling wave.

As the height of the rectangle is increased, the value of λ is also increased, which increases  T . In turn the matching value of Δt h * will also increase, which leads to the constant slope of the data in  Fig. 2 . The slope of the linear relation between height and Δt h * (Equations 3 and 4) is therefore an estimate of V A , the velocity of the attentional wave. From the Increasing Frequency data in  Fig. 2 , V A  is estimated at about 14°/s.

An interesting aspect of  Fig. 2  is that the Decreasing Frequency data to the left constitute a streaming boundary with much higher frequencies of perceived movement. Additionally, this boundary has a slope, 70°/s, that is 5.0 times steeper than the slope for the Increasing Frequency data, 14°/s. This mathematically simple integer ratio of the two slopes and the location of the Decreasing Frequency boundary in a region of faster movements can be interpreted as supporting evidence for attentional waves. A wave-based interpretation is that the steeper slope in a region of higher frequencies corresponds to the fifth harmonic of a complex attentional wave, and that this harmonic dominates and facilitates attending to very rapid motion. In other words, the attentional wave may not be a simple sinewave, but a more complex periodic wave with additional harmonic sinusoidal components that are integer multiples of the fundamental frequency. Such harmonics are common in both musical instruments and human speech, e.g.,  Yantis and Abrams (2017) . The 5th harmonic of the attention wave has a five times higher frequency and a five times shorter wavelength than the fundamental. If the wavelength of the fundamental frequency is matched to the height of the rectangle, the 5th harmonic component of the attention wave would have a series of 5 attention peaks between the top and bottom of the rectangle. To maintain successive attention peaks at the top and bottom of the rectangle over time, this harmonic wave only needs to travel one-fifth of the distance between the top and bottom of the rectangle. If the height of the rectangle is doubled, the required change in Δt h * to maintain attention peaks at the top and bottom of the rectangle is therefore only one-fifth as much compared to the prediction for the fundamental frequency, which has the entire height of the rectangle between successive peaks. Therefore, the higher harmonic of the attention wave will be able to track the higher frequency streamed events in the displayed lights despite the constant velocity limitation. Additionally, the Decreasing Frequency tradeoff between height and Δt h * will be five times as steep as the Increasing Frequency tradeoff to the right of  Fig. 2 . A property of this wave-based explanation is that a single attentional wave velocity can account for both slopes if they differ by an integer ratio. This explanation also raises the possibility that there may be additional streaming loci in the region between the two measured straight lines in  Fig. 2  that correspond to the 2nd, 3rd, and 4th harmonics of a fundamental attention wave frequency. If they exist, these loci are probably more difficult to measure with unpracticed participants, and they remain a topic for future research. Selective emphasis of a higher frequency harmonic can be considered an example of nested wave-like attention attuned to matching environmental rhythms as theorized by  Jones (1976) .

The slopes of 70°/s and 14°/s are modeled as arising from participants adjusting the wavelength of their attention waves. Longer wavelengths produce longer cycle times, which are matched by longer Δt h *’s at transitional boundaries. The best fitting linear slopes in  Fig. 3  for the mean Δt h *’s of the participants who did not exhibit linear trends individually are extremely steep, and the linear correlations between h and Δt h * were not statistically significant. Even the quadratic fit to the Increasing Frequency data is very steep. This pattern suggests that these participants may not have adjusted their attentional wavelengths to match the rectangle height. This behavior might be specific to this experiment, but it might indicate an inability to adjust attentional wavelength, λ, in other contexts as well. If so, this behavior might reflect a more general attentional disability. Future research is needed to explore this possibility.

One shortcoming of the present model is that it does not directly address the region to the far left in  Fig. 2  where streaming occurs over a continuous region. When Δt's are very short, the onsets of stimulus lights may be sufficiently frequent that some high but off-peak amounts of attention still give the perception of streaming. A second limitation is that Equations 2 and 3 imply a zero intercept for the graph of height vs. Δt h *. However, the intercepts in  Fig. 2  are non-zero. This result implies that the wave generation process consists of a mixture of processes, at least one of which is not sensitive to the rectangle height and would therefore add a constant to the measured Δt h *. The nature of such processes remains to be determined.

Previous research has found wave-like variation over time in attention to stationary stimuli (e.g.,  VanRullen, 2018 ), which is interpreted as temporal sampling of environmental stimuli.  Fiebelkorn and Kastner (2019)  reviewed evidence of sequential shifting of temporal sampling between different stationary target regions. In contrast, the present traveling wave model posits parallel sampling from multiple locations. If 1/Δt h * is taken as an estimate of the attention wave frequency, the range of frequencies for the Increasing Frequency data in  Fig. 2  is 3.2 – 6.4 Hz for the tallest and shortest rectangles, respectively. Van Rullen's (2018) and  Fiebelkorn and Kastner's (2019)  reviews together provide an attention wave frequency estimate around 3 - 8 Hz, which overlaps the range for the present experiment. The situations under which attention resembles a rhythmically sampling spotlight vs. a traveling wave with widespread parallel sampling may respectively correspond to strategic aspects of focused vs. more distributed attention. In either case, the rhythmic sampling mechanism may be a way for estimating environmental stimuli while reducing the general tendency for constant stimulation to have gradually decaying behavioral effect over time.

Treisman (2006)  and colleagues have demonstrated that distributed attention permits a rapid estimation of the mean size of a set of objects. This performance could be afforded by wave-like attention that is rapidly traveling over a two dimensional surface and affords parallel processing of multiple stimuli. The wavelength λ might be adjusted to permit high sensitivity for performing such a task. In contrast, one would expect a short wavelength plus narrow top-down shaping of the amplitude of the attentional wave to achieve focused attention to details in a small spatial region. The secondary attentional lobes found by  Lee and Pitt (2022)  and other researchers suggest focused attention involves an underlying spatially wave-like process. This interpretation of the attentional “spotlight” suggests commonality between focused and distributed attention in terms of an underlying wave-like structure.

Kaplan's Attentional Restoration Theory (e.g.,  Kaplan & Berman, 2010 ) is supported by the finding that performance of a cognitively demanding task deteriorates over time, but can be restored by a walk through an arboretum or other natural scene. One interpretation of this finding is that many cognitive tasks may require effortful top-down shaping of short wavelength attention to achieve focused attention. In contrast, a walk through an arboretum can be performed with distributed attention involving long wavelength attention and little top-down shaping. This latter mode may require less effort and may be restorative.

Jagacinski et al. (2019)  have proposed that wave-like attention can result from the spatio-temporal interaction of attentional emphasis and surrounding inhibition as described in two partial differential equations that constitute a reaction-diffusion model. That model described a spatial standing wave to capture stochastic variation and secondary lobes of attention to preview in a simulated driving task. In future work, it may be possible to extend that model to describe traveling waves and thereby establish a link to the more basic constructs of attentional emphasis and inhibition.

Attentional velocity limitations may shape perceptual-motor behavior as well.  Jagacinski et al. (2016)  demonstrated that upper body – lower body streaming in drummers, i.e. relative independence in the timing of polyrhymic tapping between hand and foot, was more prevalent than streaming between the two hands at high rates of performance. If one assumes that the cognitive representation of limbs entails a greater cognitive distance between hands and feet than between hand and hand, then this finding can be interpreted as an attentional velocity limitation.  Kim et al. (2011)  similarly found upper body – lower body streaming in the timing of golf shots, which indicates that the previous finding of hand – foot independence for drummers generalizes to other types of movement. These empirical results suggest that a spatially rhythmic mode of attention with separate simultaneous peaks for the upper and lower body will permit better performance than a single narrowly focused spotlight of attention, which may not have sufficient velocity for guiding these tasks. In golf lore, focusing on one aspect of the swing is often reported to result in deterioration of overall performance.

Although not measured in the present experiment, which concentrated on the perception of horizontal movement, when the rectangular light pattern's frequency is very low, it resembles the walking pattern of various four-legged animals. For example,  Pearson (1976)  studied the walking pattern of the cat and found the successive movement of the legs to consist of a pattern of back left, front left, back right, front right. When cats move somewhat faster, a trotting pattern results in which the front left and back right legs move together, and alternate with the back left and front right legs, i.e. a diagonal pattern of coordination. At still faster speeds, a galloping pattern results in which the two front legs move together, and the two back legs move together, i.e. a front-back pattern of coordination. At very slow speeds in the present experiment, the rectangular pattern of lights resembles the cat's walking pattern. If the pattern of lights is sped up somewhat, alternating diagonal pairs of the lights analogous to trotting were perceived by the experimenters. When the lights are sped up even more, the horizontal pattern investigated in the present experiment is perceived, which is analogous to the front legs - back legs grouping that constitutes galloping in the cat. There were also two other perceived patterns of light, a V shaped pattern and a U shaped pattern that did not correspond to the cat coordination patterns, and were subjectively reported by subsets of the experimenters. These various patterns were perceived as a single moving light, unlike the horizontal pattern in which motions between the upper two rectangular corners and between the lower two rectangular corners appear simultaneous and distinct. These observational reports of a progression in perceiving the rectangular lights from a slow walking pattern, to diagonal groupings, to horizontal groupings with increasing speed suggest that attentional velocity limits might also play a role in the progression of limb groupings in the gaits of cats and other animals. Common or at least similar attentional velocity limits may play a role in both perception and in movement control, which are often coupled in skilled behavior.

The present experiment measured attentional velocity at the boundaries between streamed and integrated motion perception. The velocity boundary for slowing down a rapid streamed percept was about five times steeper than the velocity boundary for speeding up a slow integrated percept. These results provide evidence for a traveling wave of distributed attention with a fixed velocity and an adjustable wavelength. Streaming can be considered just one example of spatially rhythmic, wave-like attention. Other spatially rhythmic examples have been found in drivers’ attention to preview ( Jagacinski et al., 2019 ) and in the Eriksen flanker task (e.g.,  Müller et al., 2005 ;  Ahmed & de Fockert, 2012 ;  Lee & Pitt, 2022 ). It remains to be seen if the traveling wave model of attention and the present experimental task will be useful in assessing attentional skill in musical performance, sports, meditation, and/or clinical deficiencies of attention.
