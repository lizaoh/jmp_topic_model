FALMAGNEl'2  University of Brussels, Brussels, Belgium  Several systems of fundamental measurement are available in the literature, which involve two (or more) sets of entities to be  measured simultaneously. Well-known examples are: Guttman scales; additive conjoint measurement; Coombs systems. This paper is  a first step towards a general analysis of such systems for which the name "composite measurement" is proposed. An extension  principle plays a central role; it is used in three proofs and is discussed in general in the concluding section. Three  examples of composite measurement are analyzed in detail: (i) bi-classificatory systems, (ii) bi-quasi-series or Guttman  scales, (iii) bi-semiorders. As far as we know, the analysis of bi-semiorder is entirely new.  I. INTRODUCTION  A basic aspect of some systems of fundamental measurement is that they involve two (or more) sets of noninterchangeable  entities to be measured simultaneously. A series of systems of this kind has been analyzed, in recent years, under the name  "conjoint measurement." Other systems can also be characterized that way, e.g. Guttman scales and Coombs systems. As far as we  know, the distinction between (relational) systems involving one set, versus those involving many sets, has not been  investigated systematically from the point of view of measurement theory, although it is central in Coombs' (1964) theory of  data. The present paper, in which we shall restrict ourselves to (relational) systems involving two sets, is a first step in  that direction. First, let us recall more precisely the systems mentioned above. For the sake of concreteness, each system is  presented in the language of a particular interpretation.  (a) Guttman Scales (see Stouffer, Guttman, Schuman, Lazarsfeld, Star, Clausen, 1950). These three examples differ in many respects. From an experimental standpoint, a striking difference lies in the way the two  sets are introduced: in (a) and (c) one set represent a class of subjects, the other a class of stimuli while in (b) the two  sets are introduced by a factor decomposition of one basic class of stimuli. Formally, these examples differ in the difficulty  of their axiomatic analysis. The axiomatic analysis of (a) is simple when one disregards (as usually in fundamental  measurement theory) the possibility of errors (see Sec. III below). The analysis of (b) is more difficult but is now well  known (see the papers of Luce and Tukey, and Krantz mentioned above). Finally, very little seems to be known about the  axiomatic analysis of (c).  However, in each of these systems, we are dealing with an attribute related to the interaction of two classes of entities.  This contrasts with the more usual cases which involve one homogeneous class of entities related to one another (for a  presentation of these usual cases, see Suppes and Zinnes, 1963). In our opinion, this difference is so basic that it deserves  to be reflected by words. We propose to use the expression composite measurement for any system where relations among two or  more sets are involved;3 the expression standard measurement is proposed for the usual cases involving only one set.  a An alternative choice, made in a previous draft of this paper was "Conjoint Measurement." However, as was pointed out to us  by Professor R. D. Luce, it seems preferable to reserve the expression "conjoint measurement" for systems where a preference  relation is given on a cartesian product. Conjoint measurement is thus a particular case of composite measurement. Let us first remark that sometimes a standard measurement system and a composite measurement system are representable in the  same numerical system. This remark is central in this paper; it can be illustrated as follows. Let us recall that a (finite)  quasi-series is a (finite) set C and a binary relation U on C such that there exists a (numerical) function h on C satisfying  cUc' if and only if h(c) > h(c').  Similarly, one can define a (finite) Guttman scale as two (finite) sets A, B and a binary relation S between A and B such that  there exists two (numerical) functions f, g on A and B satisfying  aSb if and only if f (a) > g(b).  Defined this way, it is clear that quasi-series and Guttman scale share a basic property: they both involve the order relation  on the reals; more precisely, they are both representable in <ff8, >>.  Extending the terminology introduced before, we will say that quasi-series is the standard version of Guttman scale and that  Guttman scale is a composite version of quasi-series (actually, in this simple case, there exists only one composite version;  it is not generally so).  Exploiting this remark, we may classify the already available systems according to (i) the property of being standard or  composite, (ii) the numerical system involved (see Table 1, in Sec. V). Besides giving a nice outlook on the whole theory of  measurement, this classification reveals some gaps. For instance, the composite version of semiorder (Luce, 1956) was, as far  as we know, not analyzed in the literature. A complete discussion of these systems is given in Sec. IV of this paper under the  name bi-semiorder. Although our interest in these systems was raised by theoretical considerations, their practical  significance seems beyond doubt. Another question raised by this classification is: what is the standard version of additive  conjoint measurement? The correct answer is a system which, to the best of our knowledge, has not been studied previously,  although it is close to the infinite difference systems of Suppes and Winet (1955; see also Suppes and Zinnes, 1963, pp.  35-38). The similarities and the differences will be discussed briefly in Sec. V.  Of course, our classification is really useful if the standard and composite systems involving the same numerical system have  some basic connection. A definite connection results from what we have called the extension principle. This principle is used  in all the proofs of the representation theorems given in this paper. Its general statementinvolving some technicalities-is  postponed to Sec. V.
