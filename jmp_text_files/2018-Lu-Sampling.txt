Multilevel analysis has become increasingly popular in educational and psychological assessments (e.g.  Goldstein, 2011; Goldstein & McDonald, 1987; Longford & Muthén, 1992; Muthén, 1989; Raudenbush & Bryk, 2002; Snijders & Bosker, 2012 ). The rapid growth of multilevel item response theory (IRT) modeling has resulted in two realizations. First, the interaction between a person and item is constructed by the IRT model, rather than the traditional aggregate test level performance, assuming all questions contributed equally to the understanding of students’ abilities. Compared with the traditional method, the IRT model provides a more nuanced view of the information for each question provided to a student. Second, in the field of social sciences, a critical issue that is ignored by IRT models concerns unobserved heterogeneity of item responses due to the hierarchical structure of data (e.g., data from students nested within schools/classes or measurements nested within individuals). The multilevel IRT modeling accounts for both population heterogeneity and makes valid inferences from item response data that has a nested or hierarchical structure. The multilevel IRT models for binary response data have also been increasingly used to handle a hierarchical structure (e.g.  Adams, Wilson, & Wu, 1997; Fox, 2010; Fox & Glas, 2001; Kamata, 2001; Maier, 2001; Mislevy & Bock, 1989; Natesan, 2007 ).

A variety of estimation procedures have been proposed to estimate the parameters of multilevel IRT models. The multilevel logistic regression models belong to one class of generalized linear mixed models (GLMMs) (e.g.  Clayton, 1996; Hedeker & Gibbons, 1994; McCulloch & Searle, 2001 ). Marginal maximum likelihood (MML)  (Bock & Aitkin, 1981)  is one of the most important methods for estimating GLMMs. However, the complex dependency structures result in the nesting of integrals in multilevel IRT models, and most estimation equations do not have a closed form. Thus, this approach requires a numerical or Monte Carlo integration or an integral approximation method that includes Laplace approximation  (Raudenbush, Yang, & Yosef, 2000) , adaptive quadrature  (Bock & Schilling, 1997; Rabe-Hesketh, Skrondal, & Pickles, 2002, 2005) , and Monte Carlo integration  (Kuk, 1999; Skaug, 2002) . Therefore, MML leads to a complicated estimation procedure. Moreover, it is difficult to incorporate uncertainty (standard errors) into parameter estimates  (Patz & Junker, 1999) . A fully Bayesian procedureremains straightforward for complex dependency structures in multilevel IRT models and allows for complete uncertainty calculations  (Tsutakawa & Soltys, 1988) . Studies focusing on multilevel models from a Bayesian perspective usually use a probit link function in their formulation  (Albert, 1992; Béguin & Glas, 2001; Fox, 2005; Fox & Glas, 2001; Hoijtink, 2000; Tanner & Wong, 1987) . Also popular in Bayesian multilevel approach studies is the cumulative logit function  (Browne & Draper, 2006; Gilks & Wild, 1992; Zeger & Karim, 1991) .

In this paper, an efficient slice–Gibbs sampling algorithm in a fully Bayesian framework is proposed to estimate multilevel two-parameter logistic models. The slice–Gibbsalgorithm, as the name suggests, can be conceived of an extension of Gibbs algorithm. The sampling process consists of two parts. One part is the slice algorithm  (Bishop, 2006; Damien, Wakefield, & Walker, 1999; Neal, 2003) , which samples the logistic IRT model from the truncated full conditional posterior distribution by introducing the auxiliary variables. The other part is Gibbs algorithm which updates the linear multilevel structure parameters based on the sampled values from IRT model, where the linear multilevel models are assumed to follow normal distributions  (Béguin & Glas, 2001; Fox, 2005, 2010; Fox & Glas, 2001) . Additionally, the Gibbs algorithm part implements sampling by using conjugate prior and greatly increases efficiency  (Albert, 1992; Tanner & Wong, 1987) . The motivation for the approach is manifold. First, using the slice sampling method to estimate the two-parameter logistic model  (Lord, 1980; Tsutakawa, 1984; van der Linden & Hambleton, 1997)  has the advantage of a flexible prior distribution being introduced to obtain samples from the full conditional posterior distributions rather than being restricted to using the conjugate distributions, which is required in Gibbs sampling  (Gelfand & Smith, 1990; Geman & Geman, 1984)  and limited using the normal ogive framework  (Albert, 1992; Béguin & Glas, 2001; Fox, 2005, 2010; Fox & Glas, 2001; Tanner & Wong, 1987) . It allows informative priors andnon-informative priors of the item parameters. Second, the Metropolis–Hasting algorithm  (Chen, Shao, & Ibrahim, 2000; Chib & Greenberg, 1995; Hastings, 1970; Metropolis, Rosenbluth, Rosenbluth, Teller, & Teller, 1953; Tierney, 1994)  depends on the proposal distributions and variances (tuning parameters) and is sensitive to step size. If the step size is too small, the chain will take longer to traverse the target density. If the step size is too large, there will be inefficiencies due to a high rejection rate. However, the slice sampler automatically tunes the step size to match the local shape of the target density and draws the samples with acceptance probability equal to one. Thus, it is easier and more efficient to implement.

The rest of this paper is organized as follows. In Section  2 , the theoretical foundation of the slice–Gibbs sampling algorithm is presented. A general multilevel two-parameter logistic model and model identifiability are presented to explain the binary multilevel data in Section  3 . A detailed slice–Gibbs algorithm is given in Section  4 . In Section  5 , two methods of model assessment are proposed to compare the goodness of fit between the models by using two different parameter structures. In Section  6 , four simulated examples that focus on the performance of parameter recovery, the flexibility and sensitivity of prior distributions for the slice sampler, the results of comparing with the M–H within Gibbs algorithm, and information criteria and cross-validation log-likelihood to assess model fit are given. In Section  7 , the results of model assessment and performance of the slice–Gibbs sampler in practical situations are shown by an empirical example. Finally, some concluding remarks are presented in Section  8 .

A Markov chain based on the slice sampler can be constructed by sampling points alternately from uniform distribution under the density curve and only concerning the horizontal “slice” defined by the current sample points.

The slice sampler shows the better performance, even if we have misspecified priors and proposal distributions or variances in the Metropolis–Hasting (M–H) algorithm. Moreover, more flexible specification of priors can be adopted without using conjugate priors, which is the limitation of Gibbs sampling, and it is not sensitive to the specified priors that will be conducted in the simulation. Furthermore, it avoids retrospective tuning in theM–H algorithm if we do not know how to choose a proper tuning parameter or if no value for the tuning parameter is appropriate. A common approach for model comparison within the Bayesian framework is to compute a Bayes factor  (Gelfand, 1996; Kass & Raftery, 1995) . We select the better model based on the maximization of the marginal likelihood of the data. A serious difficulty in the use of the Bayes factor is its sensitivity to prior distributions. The use of non-informative priors for the parameters in alternative models results in Bayes factors that are not well defined, and it is not possible to integrate out all of the parameters to obtain a closed-form expression of the marginal distribution for high-dimensional models. To avoid these problems, the pseudo-Bayes factor (PsBF) method  (Geisser & Eddy, 1979) , which is based on the cross-validation predictive density, is used to compare the multilevel IRT models.

The complete data log-likelihood of the multilevel IRT model consists of the item response model and the structural multilevel model. Changes in the multilevel part of the model can be tested conditional on the item response part, so relatively small changes in the log-likelihood of the multilevel part can be detected. Multilevel IRT models with different structural multilevel parts but the same item response part are often compared with each other. The effective number of parameters in a multilevel model is often difficult to calculate. Although the nominal number of parameters can be determined directly from the likelihood, the prior distribution imposes additional restrictions on the parameter space and reduces the effective dimension. In the random effect model, the effective number of parameters depends on higher-level variance parameters. When the variance of the random effects approaches zero, all random effects are equal, and the model reduces to a simple linear model with one mean parameter. When the variance goes to infinity, the number of free parameters approaches the number of random effects. We thus use the marginal likelihood to remove “uncertainty”. In the Bayesian framework, another MCMC sampling algorithm is often used for parameter estimation in complex models, namely the M–H within Gibbs algorithm. In this simulation study, we compare the slice–Gibbs sampler with the M–H within Gibbs algorithm from multiple perspectives.

On one hand, it is well known that prior distributions play an important role in the M–H within Gibbs algorithm. When the sample size is not large, the specification of improper priors may lead to inaccurate posterior inference. However, the proposed slice–Gibbs algorithm can still generate satisfactory results even with the misspecification of priors. The slice–Gibbs algorithm is independent of proposal distributions and is only related to the prior distributions, which leads to the estimates of the slice–Gibbs algorithm being identical under cases 3 and 4. In case 3, we use the proper proposal distributions proposed by  Patz and Junker (1999) , although the difference is not obvious for the Bias and RMSE of both algorithms. The average time requirement of one replication for the slice–Gibbs and M–H within Gibbs are approximately 102 s and 160 s, respectively. Thus, the slice–Gibbs sampler is faster and more efficient, especially when a large number of replications is required. Next, we tune the proposal variances in case 4. It is a subjective process that tunes proposal variances to reasonable acceptance probabilities in the M–H within Gibbs algorithm. The acceptance probabilities are approximately 21% for item parameters and 27% for the ability parameters, which are lower than the 32% for item parameters and 48% for the ability parameters in the appropriate case 3. Therefore, more time is required to draw a fixed number of samples. There are slightly larger Bias and RMSE for the item parameters that may not be ignored in accurate item calibration. The results illustrate that retrospective tuning is an issue in using the M–H within Gibbs algorithm. In contrast, the slice–Gibbs algorithm can avoid this retrospective tuning problem.

For example,  Ando (2007)  and  Spiegelhalter et al. (2002)  found that the AIC and the BIC results are not guaranteed to capture the same model.  Azevedo, Fox, and Andrade (2016)  used the AIC, BIC, and DIC to select the optimal covariance structure for the real data set. The AIC and DIC tend to choose the most complex covariance structure model, while the BIC tends to choose the simplest covariance structure  (Azevedo et al., 2016) . Similarly, in this study, the true complex model is chosen by the mAIC and the DIC, and the mBIC chose the simple model. In addition, we are more inclined to choose relatively complex models to fit the data. The failure of the mBIC is likely due to the mBIC itself, as it tends to choose a simple model for any data, or due to the sample size, priors and some other factors. To illustrate the applicability of the multilevel IRT modeling method to large-scale test assessments, we consider a data set of students’ mathematics achievement tests for junior middle schools conducted by Northeast Normal university (NENU) Branch, Collaborative Innovation Center of Assessment toward Basic Education Quality at Beijing Normal University. The analysis of the test data will help us gain a better understanding of the students’ mathematics academic abilities and explore the factors that affect their mathematics abilities. The data set contains a two-stage cluster sample of 2108 students in grade 7 from 16 schools in Changchun, Jilin, China. There are between 121 and 139 students in each school. A stratified sample of schools ensures that all school types and all geographical districts are represented. At the first stage, the sampling population is classified according to district, and schools are selected at random. At the second stage, students in grade 7 are selected at random from the school. The mathematics test consists of 30 dichotomous scored items. In this section, we focus on exploring the differences within and between groups (schools) and constructing the multilevel model which explains these differences with respect to individual mathematical abilities. This paper develops a novel slice–Gibbssampling algorithm to estimate the parameters in the multilevel two-parameter logistic model. It avoids the difficulty of multidimensional numerical integration compared with the marginal maximum likelihood approach. The introduction of strategic auxiliary variables permits to use a flexible prior distribution to obtain samples from the full conditional posterior distribution. As a result, the slice sampler relaxes the restrictions of conjugacy of prior distributions of the usual Gibbs sampling algorithm in binary probit models  (Albert, 1992; Fox & Glas, 2001) , and it is not sensitive to the specification of prior distribution, even though a non-informative prior. Moreover, some major difficulties of the M–H within Gibbs algorithm are overcome in the slice–Gibbs sampler, such as the dependency on the proposal distributions or proposal variances (tuning parameter) and sensitivity to step size in the M–H steps. In certain cases, retrospective tuning in the M–H steps is inefficient and time consuming if we do not know how to choose a good tuning parameter or if no value for the tuning parameter is appropriate. However, the M–H within Gibbs may be a preferred choice if the M–H steps are tuned properly. The slice–Gibbs sampler provides an alternative sampling algorithm to other sampling approaches, such as theM–H within Gibbs. More importantly, the simulation studies show that the slice–Gibbs sampler is faster and more efficient compared to the M–H within Gibbs algorithm in the multilevel 2PL models, even though the performances are similar when both are tuned properly.

Two application issues are addressed in this study. The first is how to construct an appropriate multilevel model or a multilevel IRT-based model to fit the data. Two model comparison methods, the information criterion method and cross-validation log-likelihood method, are used to determine the best-fitting multilevel model or multilevel IRT-based model for simulated or real data. The second is how to explain hierarchical data by using the best-fitting model. The following questions are answered in the framework of students’ achievement analysis. First, controlling for the level-2 predictor gender and the level-3 predictors teacher satisfaction degree and school climate, how will students with high SES scores perform on a mathematics test compared with low SES scores? Second, is the performance identical between males and females when controlling for SES, teacher satisfaction degree and school climate? Third, do the teacher/school effects (covariates) affect individual performance? If so, what are the effects?

Other issues should be investigated in the future. First is whether the slice–Gibbssampling algorithm can be applied to estimate more complicated multilevel IRT models in educational and psychological measurement, such as the partial credit model  (Masters, 1982) , the generalized partial credit model  (Muraki, 1992, 1993) . Second, as many tests intend to measure multiple latent abilities, the multilevel 2PL model can be extended to a multilevel multidimensional logistic model for studying the relations among multiple abilities and covariates in a hierarchical data structure. Third is whether the slice–Gibbs sampler can provide more accurate parameter estimation for the mixture response time model with the multimodality  (Wang & Xu, 2015) . Finally, the performances of informative criteria in different conditions and the practical utility of the two model comparison methods need to be further examined under more general situations.
