Judgments of Frequency and Recency in a Distributed Memory Model 

Bennet Murdock, David Smith, and Juan Bai 

University of Toronto 

TODAM is a theory of distributed associative memory based on the convolution-correlation formalism of A. Borsellino and T. Poggio (1973, Kybernetik, 122, 113�122), and TODAM2 is a revised version which includes context, auto-associations for binding, a dual basis for item information, and mediators for associative information. It can explain some complex interactions (differential forgetting and differential attention) between item and associative information. In this paper we derive the basic expressions for the memory-probe dot product for the 2_2 cases (item and pair study crossed with item and pair probe), and then we apply these expressions to judgments of frequency (JOF) and judgments of recency (JOR). We report an experiment which tests both JOF and JOR for single items with post-cuing to control for encoding strategies and suggest an attenuation factor for repetition to improve the fits. With attenuation, TODAM2 can fit the JOR data, but the JOF fits, while not too bad, consistently predict too much dependence between item and associative information. � 2001 Academic Press 

In judgments of frequency (JOF) subjects estimate how frequently (absolute or relative) an item had been presented in a list, and in judgments of recency (JOR) subjects estimate how recently (absolute or relative) an item had been presented in a list. This paper applies TODAM to JOF and JOR. TODAM is a theory of distributed associative memory based on the convolution�correlation formalism of Borsellino and Poggio (1973). The theory first will be extended to account for JOF, and then this extension will be applied to some JOR data to see if a purely strength-based account can explain them. Finally, we report an experiment that involves both JOF and JOR. Some items were tested for frequency and the other items were tested for recency, and the test type was post-cued. The purpose of the experiment was to test our explanation of the JOR data.

While TODAM can explain a variety of experimental results (Murdock 6 Hockley, 1989), there are two recent findings on the interaction of item and associative information that it clearly cannot explain. The first is the differentialforgetting effect (Hockley, 1991, 1992a). Under comparable conditions, item recognition falls off over lag ranges where pair recognition does not decline. (Lag is the number of items intervening between the study of an item or pair and its test.) Since the forgetting parameter : operates on M, the model predicts parallel forgetting curves for items and pairs. 

The second finding is the differential-attention results (Hockley 6 Cristi, 1996a). In a between-subjects design, both groups of subjects studied short lists of pairs but one group was told to concentrate on remembering the items while the other group was told to concentrate on remembering the pairs. The immediate tests were only on the attended material, but on a surprise final-recognition test both groups were tested on both items and pairs. The item-emphasis group did very badly on pair recognition but the pair-emphasis group did about as well on the item recognition task as the item-emphasis group did. Given the limited-capacity assumption, the decrement in performance for the unattended type should be comparable for both groups.[1]

Discussion 

Many of these ideas have been used before by others. Context drift was part of the stimulus-sampling theory of Estes (1955); separate context elements were used in the MINERVA model of Hintzman (1988); auto-convolution was used for item information in the CHARM model of Metcalfe-Eich (1985); and summing item vectors before autoassociating them was used by Cooper (1973). 

Numerous studies have shown the importance of encoding and retrieval context on memory performance. These studies suggest that the properties of extraneous 

569

stimuli which are present at the time of encoding are encoded along with the item. The present theory embodies a binding between items and context of this form, with the additional assumption that the contextual stimuli are not static but dynamically vary with time. 

In TODAM (Eq. (1)) items are stored by superposition and associations are formed by convolution and then also stored by superposition. In TODAM2 (Eq. (3)) items embedded in context are autoassociated and then stored by superposition. This change is necessary to bind context and items. For item recognition, instead of a simple dot product, in TODAM2��like CHARM��one must correlate the probe with the memory vector and compare the results with the probe. An alternative procedure would be to convolve the probe item with itself and then compare it (by the dot product) with the memory vector. These two methods give identical results; see the proof of the recall-recognition identity in Weber (1988). 

For pair recognition, one would compute the dot product of FG*[2] (or FMG*[2] ) with the memory vector if context was used or, if it were not used, compute the dot product of (f+g)*[2] (or (f+m+g)*[2] ) with the memory vector. In either case, the context would be the context at the time of the test, which would differ from the study context as a function of lag (the number of items intervening between study and test). 

In TODAM2 items are summed before the autoassociation. This change is necessary to provide a dual basis for item recognition [(f+g)[2] =f V f+g V g+ 2(f V g)], and a dual basis is needed to explain the differential-attention data of Hockley and Cristi (1996a). That is, there is an f V f component from F*[2] (the item) and also an f V f component from FG*[2] (or FMG*[2] ) (the pair), so this provides the dual basis.

Explanation 

Why is TODAM2 able to fit these data? For the differential-forgetting results (Fig. 1) the value of the forgetting parameter : is so close to 1 that there is essentially no forgetting over the lag range due to :. However, because the context-drift parameter \ is somewhat lower than : there is a significant change in context over this same range. This context change affects item information, not associative information, because we assume subjects use context on the item tests but not on the associative tests. With the continuous-memory assumption, context in the form of list membership is the only distinguishing factor between old and new items. However, for pairs the situation is different. 

The expected value of the comparison process (the dot product of the probe, e.g., FG*[2] or FMG*[2] , with the memory vector) can be analyzed into four components, pure context (a V a, 2(a V b), and b V b), pure item (f V f, m V m, and g V g), context�item [2(a V f), 2(a V g), 2(a V m), 2(f V b), 2(g V b), and 2(m V b)], and pure associative [2(f V m), 2(f V g), and 2(m V g)]. For pairs, the lures (rearranged pairs) have as strong a pure-context component as the items from intact pairs so context is not a useful cue in pair recognition. Had new-item or mixed lures been used, then context could be useful in pair recognition, so whether or not context will be used in pair recognition depends on the experimental design. 

However, this use of context is not enough to produce the results shown in Fig. 1. Without mediators the level of pair recognition is so low that the curves would never cross. The value of the mediator study probability _ is critical in determining the exact point of intersection. 

The explanation for the differential-attention results is somewhat different. It turns out that not only is there a dual basis for item information, but also the pureitem component is independent of the attention study parameter # and the contextitem component is relatively independent of # (see Murdock, 1997, for details). Consequently, the differential-attention results should hold for a wide range of parameter values.

JOR 

It seems to be generally accepted that JOF's are based on strength, but what about JOR? JOR's could be based on strength, or it could be that something else is involved (Hacker, 1980; Muter, 1979). Just what this might be is not quite clear. Following early studies by Morton (1968) and Peterson (1967; Peterson, Johnson, 6 Coatney, 1969), the standard approach has been to pit frequency against recency. In forced-choice tasks, are items presented more recently correctly selected when the lures or foils are earlier items that had been presented more frequently? The general answer seems to be that frequency has some effect but it does not override recency (see Friedman, 1993, for a recent review; also, see Flexser 6 Bower, 1974, and Hinrichs, 1970, for early models). 

Interpreting these results without an explicit model is difficult because, if JOR is based on strength, how much should it be affected by frequency? We address this question by asking how well TODAM2 can predict JOR when frequency is pitted against recency. The key data come from Hockley (1992b, Exp. 1), where single items (no pairs were used) were presented once, twice, or three times in the first or second half of a list and subjects were given a two-alternative forced-choice JOR where one of the test items came from the first half of the list and the other test item came from the second half of the list.[8] There were three 1P, three 2P, and three 3P items in each half of the list, making 18 tokens in each list half and also a five-item recency buffer. There were nine types in each list half, and since these presentation frequencies were crossed (3_3) there were nine 2-AFC test trials for each list. Presentation and test orders were randomized. The question is how much effect presentation frequency had on JOR. The main results are shown in Fig. 5.

JOF�JOR EXPERIMENT 

This experiment used the continuous-recognition procedure of Shepard and Teghtsoonian (1961) where we varied the presentation frequency of items with post-cued forced-choice comparisons of either JOF or JOR for each item. For the JOF tests subjects had to choose the more frequently presented of the two test items or, for the JOR tests, the more recently presented of the two items. Since subjects did not know at the time of presentation how each item would be tested (i.e., JOF or JOR), they could not attenuate repetitions of JOR items and pay full attention to the repetitions of JOF items because they would not know which was which. 

Consequently, attenuation would impair JOF and have no benefit for JOR (each item was tested for frequency or recency but never for both). On the other hand, if attenuation is a more general learning algorithm then it would be used regardless, unless it was optional.[9] The critical test then is whether the best-fitting value of c is 1 (strategy) or less than 1 (algorithm).

Design. For the experiment, there were 216 different study words. Half of them only appeared in the JOF tests. Among these 108 words, 36 words were presented once, 36 twice, and 36 three times. The other 108 study words only appeared in the JOR tests. Among them, there were also 36 words presented once, 36 presented twice, and 36 three times. The presentations of the study words were random except that no two presentations of the same word could appear consecutively. There were 54 JOF tests and 54 JOR tests randomly inserted into the list. The 54 JOF tests used up all of the 108 study words for JOF tests; the 54 JOR tests exhausted all of the 108 words for JOR tests. The list (study plus test) was 540 items long for each subject. 

In JOR tests, the assigned frequencies of the two test alternatives exhausted all of the nine possible combinations, i.e., the frequency of the correct alternative crossed with the frequency of the incorrect alternative. Within each combination, there were six tests. The position of the correct alternative (either left or right) in the test was balanced, so that it had the same opportunity to be the left word or the right word. In JOF tests, because the test was to select the alternative that had been presented more frequently, the correct alternative would always have the higher frequency so there were only three valid frequency combinations, 2 : 1, 3 : 1, and 3 : 2. There were 12 tests for each of these three combinations. In addition, three kinds of ``null choice'' tests (in which the frequencies of the two alternatives were the same) consisting of six tests each were used so all together there were six frequency combinations, 1 : 1, 2 : 1, 2 : 2, 3 : 1, 3 : 2, and 3 : 3. The relative recency of the last occurrence of the two test alternatives was balanced so that the opportunities for the correct and the incorrect alternative to be more recent within each frequency combination were the same. For JOF or JOR tests the lag of the correct alternative of each test was 10\2, 20\4, or 40\8. The number of tests for each of the lags was the same. The positions of the different occurrences of the repeated words were randomly assigned except that the immediate repetition of a word was not allowed. 

The first six subjects finished the experiment list without any break. Although accuracy was emphasized, the subject was asked to respond both as accurately and as quickly as possible. Accuracy and reaction time were both recorded, but only the accuracy data were analyzed. After the experiment, the subject was asked to fill out a short survey about the experiment. 

For the second six subjects, the experimental list was still 540 items long, but the criterion for the practice list was reduced from 70 0 to 660. Also, every list was divided into two sublists, each having 270 items. This was to decrease any possible fatigue the subjects might experience. Accordingly, for every subject, there were now two half-sessions. Each half-session contained 108 words and 54 tests. These words were chosen randomly to be tested for JOF or JOR. Every word was only tested once. The JOF or JOR determination was random, so there were not exactly the same number of JOF and JOR tests in each half-session. The total number of JOF

and JOR tests was still 54 and all presentation(s) of a certain repeated word and the test involving this word was limited to the same half-session. The second six subjects, therefore, would only deal with lists of half the length that the first six subjects saw. This was why the criterion level was lowered from 70 0 to 660. 

The second six subjects were told about the break in the middle before they began the formal experiment. There was a five-minute break after the first half-session. The subjects were instructed to forget the words they had just studied and try to relax completely. After five minutes, the subject was asked to get back to the screen and get ready for the second half-session. The second half-session began when the subject depressed a key. 

Procedure. Lists varied from subject to subject, and they were generated according to the Shepard and Teghtsoonian (1961) continuous-recognition paradigm as modified by Hockley (1982). The study and test words were intermixed from the beginning to the end of the list, and the study items were always single words. A two-alternative forced choice recognition test was used; the test required the subject to recognize the more recent or the more frequent word from the two alternatives in the test pair. 

The study words were presented one by one in the middle of the screen with a presentation time of 2 s�word. There were two kinds of tests, JOF and JOR tests, both of which followed its last presentation. For JOF tests, the two tested words were presented vertically on the screen, one on top of the other. The subject's task was to select the word which had been presented more times by pressing the upward or downward arrow key according to the position of that word on the screen. To make the meaning of the response key clearer, the two arrow keys were labeled ``up'' and ``down'' in accordance with their directions. 

For JOR tests, the two words were presented horizontally on the screen, side by side. The subject's task was to select the more recent word by pressing the leftward or rightward arrow key. Similarly, the two arrow keys were labeled ``left'' and ``right.'' With this presentation method the appearance of the two-word test itself served as the cue for the test type. 

Subjects were given one or two short practice lists using only 1P and 2P items. Subjects who did not achieve the criterion accuracy (70 0 or 660) were dismissed. The subjects who proceeded were told that a bonus would be added to the payment for better performance in the formal experiment.

Although ad hoc, a reasonable interpretation is that attenuation is a subject-controlled factor or strategy that varies with the nature of the experimental conditions. SUMMARY 

With the addition of context TODAM2 can mimic the general pattern of data in the differential-forgetting effect, the differential-attention effect, context recall, JOF results, and JOR results, all with a reasonable degree of accuracy. Essentially the same parameter values were used throughout and, except to track experimental manipulations, these parameter values did not change with experimental conditions. It is the model that is doing the work, not the parameters. 

With the exception of the recall analysis, all these effects are complex interactions which could not be explained by TODAM. The result (TODAM2) is a more complex model, but it also has greater explanatory power. To improve the fits to the JOR data we assumed an attenuation process namely, that the number of features encoded decreased by a constant proportion with each repetition. The JOF�JOR experiment showed that the best-fitting values of the attenuation proportion (c) were well below 1.0. Attenuation also improved the fits to the JOF data, but the amount of attenuation was much less. 

For JOR, the fits were quite reasonable, and there is nothing in these results to refute the idea that strength could be the basis for JOR. Of course, there are other data which seem to contradict this notion, but these data have not been subject to a formal analysis. For JOF the fits, while not too far off, still show more dependence than they should, though more for item than for pair probes. That is, the effects of pair frequency for an item probe and the effects of item frequency for a pair probe were greater than the model predicted. Perhaps one or more other factors not included in TODAM2 will be needed to account for the discrepancies.
