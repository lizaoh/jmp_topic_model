In one particular period — the mid 1980s — the foundations of two new theories were laid that opened up innovative views and practices for the assessment of knowledge. One is the  theory of knowledge structures  (KST), which was expressly designed as a novel approach to the diagnosis of the cognitive competencies gradually acquired in education, with special attention to the requirements for adaptive and computerized testing ( Doignon & Falmagne, 1985; Falmagne & Doignon, 1988a, 1988b ). The other is the  theory of Bayesian networks  (BNT), which first made its appearance in artificial intelligence studies concerning approximate causal reasoning, and was later applied as a guide for cognitive assessment in education and for probabilistic modeling in several other areas named below ( Lauritzen & Spiegelhalter, 1988; Pearl, 1986, 1988 ). From their beginnings, both theories constantly grew in formal results and techniques for applications. At present, both of them exhibit extensive bodies of literature, formed of theoretical and applicative articles as well as systematic books. Among others, I cite ( Doignon & Falmagne, 1999; Falmagne, Albert, Doble, Eppstein, & Hu, 2013; Falmagne & Doignon, 2011 ) for KST; ( Darwiche, 2009; Kjærulff & Madsen, 2008 ) for BNT in general; and  Almond, Mislevy, Steinberg, Yan, and Williamson (2015)  for BNT as applied to educational assessment.

There are some conspicuous features that are shared by the two theories and that distinguish them from previously established approaches to knowledge assessment. I mention, in particular, the importance of the graphical aspects of their models, their emphasis on the dependence and independence relationships among the various variables involved in the models, the use of concepts from algebra and from combinatorics to establish their mathematical underpinnings, the detailed and explanatory character of their diagnostic reports (organized competence profiles, rather than isolated numerical scores), the probabilistic evaluations that accompany and qualify those reports, and their suitability as platforms for adaptive and computerized testing. Furthermore, both theories, given their prevailing combinatorial character, have drawn special benefit (for applications) from the advancements and accessibility of computational means in recent decades.

The existence of similarities and some differences, and of good reasons for a comparison between the two theories, was noted quite early in their history, for example by  Villano (1992) . Nevertheless, on perusing the literature relating to each theory I could see that references from one school to the other are quite rare and incidental, so that the two theories appear to have been developed independently, in spite of their common practical subject and of the shared features listed above. Specifically, I did not come across any published work that directly compares the two paradigms on selected substantive aspects relative to which a comparison is actually feasible and possibly enlightening. This is precisely the task I undertake in this article. The time for this comparison seems to me ripe for at least two reasons. On the one hand, BNT, born in the field of artificial intelligence, has gradually disclosed its capacity as a framework for knowledge assessment, which was the problem inspiring KST from its very beginning. On the other hand, in the evolution of KST one can note an increasing interest in the probabilistic aspect of its models, which is an aspect of the utmost importance for Bayesian networks as probabilistic graphical models.

Thinking specifically of KST, its versatility and explicit location in the area of knowledge assessment mean that it has been the subject of comparative studies that relate it to other paradigms bearing on the same area. In particular, it has been discussed in conjunction with latent class analysis ( Schrepp, 2005; Ünlü, 2011  ), cognitive diagnostic modeling ( Heller, Stefanutti, Anselmi, & Robusto, 2015 ), and item response theory ( Noventa, Spoto, Heller, & Kelava, 2019; Ünlü, 2007 ). On this account, this present study may then be counted as one further link in an existing chain of comparative analyses, all relating to KST. There is a difference, however, as regards the general aim of my study. I will  not  try to show that certain models of one theory (either KST or BNT), if enriched with further components or strengthened by additional assumptions, are tantamount to models of the other theory. Still less will I try to show that hybrid models can be constructed, or that one of the theories can be subsumed into the other. The purpose of my study is merely descriptive. I will consider corresponding aspects that are visible in both theories and will highlight and discuss similarities and differences between them. In the discussion, the two theories will be kept as separate as are their origins and developments so far.

As stated above, the Bayesian networks (also called belief networks) first came to be discussed under this name in studies of approximate causal reasoning. Their theory, besides inspiring models in that area ( Pearl & Mackenzie, 2018 ) and in the area of educational assessment, has also been applied for probabilistic modeling in cognitive psychology ( Moreira & Wichert, 2018 ), computer vision ( Zhang & Ji, 2011 ), neuroscience ( Bielza & Larrañaga, 2014 ), artificial intelligence in medicine ( Orphanou, Stassopoulou, & Keravnou, 2014 ), biology ( Ni, Baladandayuthapani, Vannucci, & Stingo, 2022 ), ecology ( Landuyt et al., 2013 ), sociology ( Ceriani & Gigliarano, 2020 ), and forensic science ( Dawid & Mortera, 2019 ). Thus, this is an example of a theory that, while having a unitary mathematical setup, has proved to be applicable in various areas of empirical research. This is especially due to its flexibility, which regards both the graphical structure of the models and the form of their local probabilistic components (cf. Sections  3.1 and 4.1  below). Alongside the Markov random fields, the Bayesian networks constitute one of the two main classes of probabilistic graphical models of current interest ( Koller & Friedman, 2009 ). A basic difference between these two classes concerns the graphical structure, which is an undirected graph (i.e., an adjacency relation) in a Markov random field, and a directed graph (i.e., a precedence relation) in a Bayesian network. More precisely, the latter is an acyclic directed graph, often called a DAG, the acronym for directed acyclic graph. Following the example of other authors (e.g.,  Studený, 2005 , p. 46), I prefer to call it an acyclic directed graph, or shortly an acyclic digraph.

Coming back to my specific task, I specify that the basic comparable aspects to be examined are the variables in a model, the set-theoretic relations between the variables, the probability distributions acting on the variables, and the construction of a model in its structural and parametric parts. These four aspects are separately addressed in Sections  2  to  5  of this article. In each section, after introducing the general concept to be discussed, the peculiar shapes it takes in first one and then the other theory are described, and corresponding similarities, differences, and theoretical questions are highlighted. As an  Appendix , I present a table that classifies the main concepts discussed. Although the two theories are about the same practical problem and apply formally similar concepts, they show differences in the terms and symbols used. The expressive system I adopt is as close as possible to the systems available in the respective bodies of literature, but with some unavoidable deviations from one or the other.

A session of knowledge assessment may be conceived as a game event that involves two players, an  examinee  and an  examiner , plus a  questionnaire , such as a sequence of questions or tasks. In the simplified view assumed in this article, all the variables accepted in a model have their origin and location in the first player, the examinee. As regards the second player (the examiner) and the medium of the game (the questionnaire), two basic dichotomies should be considered. The first is that some variables are such that the values they take on in the game become directly and certainly known to the examiner, whereas this does not happen for other variables, so that these latter variables become the target of inference processes (by the examiner). Adopting a lexicon shared by the two theories, I use the adjectives  manifest  and  latent  to refer to variables that are in the former and, respectively, the latter of these epistemic conditions. The second dichotomy is that, among the variables in a model, there are some that occur in direct connection with the questions in the questionnaire, and others that are presumed to be present (in the examinee) independently of the questionnaire. Again adopting the shared lexicon, I use the words  performance  and  competence  to refer to variables that are in the former and, respectively, the latter of these ontological conditions.

For certain purposes, the concept of “set of skills” also enters the discussion of knowledge assessment as addressed by KST (see the next subsection). Because of similarities in their general meaning and theoretical role, I denote such a set by the same symbol  
                            Y 
                          adopted above for the set of proficiencies in a BNT model. Indeed, the skills that are possibly involved in a KST model may be categorized as competence latent variables, in precisely the same way as the proficiencies involved in a BNT model. They are not in one-to-one correspondence with the items in the questionnaire at the basis of the model, and for this reason they cannot be categorized as performance variables. Nevertheless, they may be influential on the abilities directly tested by the questionnaire. They may therefore also become targets of meaningful inferences by the examiner, in the process of knowledge assessment.

KST then puts the emphasis on the distinction and the correspondence between the manifest responses to items and the real latent mastery (or non-mastery) of them, and chooses the latter (under the name of “knowledge state”) as the primary target of inference in knowledge assessment. This choice may be interpreted as a bent of KST towards behavioristic views, that is, a preference for locating the diagnostic targets as closely as possible to the real tasks set for an examinee in a testing session ( Heller, Ünlü and Albert, 2013 , p. 229). The emergence of the “skill” concept within KST may then be judged as a move in the opposite direction, that is, an opening to cognition oriented views, which are characteristic of traditional psychometric approaches to the problem. This opening clearly appeared in the work of  Falmagne, Koppen, Villano, Doignon, & Johannesen  ( 1990 , pp. 207–208), was explained by  Korossy  ( 1999 , pp. 105–106), and characterizes the so-called competence-based (as distinct from performance-based) modeling within KST ( Heller, Augustin, Hockemeyer, Stefanutti and Albert, 2013; Suck, 2011 ). As specifically concerns the subject of our study, it may also be valued as an approach between the two theories, considering that the diagnostic targets of BNT are proficiency profiles, which are related by dependence but not by one-to-one correspondence to the manifest responses in a process of knowledge assessment.

The concept of skill (ability, proficiency, etc.) thus stands out as an important category that plays roles in both theories. It is the subject of plausible assumptions as well as of relevant questions, which will be mentioned in the following sections. One plausible assumption is that distinct skills could be considered that are similar in type but different in  generality , so that the mastery of one skill should be ensured by the mastery of another skill of the same type but wider in generality. For example, the ability to compute (by pencil and paper) the ratio between integer numbers is obviously ensured by the wider ability to compute the ratio between arbitrary numbers (with possible decimals). Another plausible assumption is that skills could be considered that amount to  combinations  of simpler skills, suitably interrelated, so that the mastery of a composite skill should presuppose the mastery of each of its components ( Falmagne et al., 1990 , p. 207;  Millán & Pérez-de-la Cruz, 2002 , pp. 287–288;  Culbertson, 2016 , p. 10).

Another relevant question is about the  distinction  between the abilities individually tested by the items in a questionnaire and the abilities classified as skills in a model, that is (in our notation) the distinction between the variables assigned to set  
                            Y

                            Z

Both theories, in defining and elaborating their models, make use of constructs of set-theoretical and combinatorial character, which I generally refer to as  relations . These include not only relations within the set of variables involved in a model, but also relations within the power set of the set of variables, families of subsets of the set of variables, functions having sets as arguments and as values, and so on. All these are tools used for describing the “structure” of models, as distinct from their numerical “parameters”. The components discussed in this section are exempt from probabilities. The components that involve probabilities are the subjects of the next section.

The concept of d-separation — that is, separation in directed graphs — is indeed quite a complex construct, both in its definition and in its theoretical and practical use. It is, however, a concept of the utmost importance for the whole theory of Bayesian networks. In Section  4.1  we shall see that the fundamental reason for its importance lies in its connection with the concept of conditional independence between random variables (a probabilistic construct, not a graphical one), which itself gives rise to ternary relations between subsets of the total variable in a network.

A prominent concept in KST is that of a  knowledge state . Intuitively, such a state may be regarded as a self-sufficient set of competences, which is well-balanced and gap-free in itself, so that it may exist as such in the cognitive situation of a person. To be “gap-free” here means a relative (not an absolute) completeness, that is, it means that if certain competences are in the set and these cannot psychologically exist without some other competences, then those latter competences must also be in the set. There are some evident similarities between these descriptions. In dealing with the construction of models, both theories draw a distinction between learning or choosing a structure and learning or estimating the parameters taking place in the structure. The former problem is addressed before the latter, and the solution to the former imposes constraints on the shape and admissible solutions to the latter. In the Introduction, we listed some features that are shared by the two theories and that distinguish them from previously established approaches to knowledge assessment in psychometrics and education. The comparative review we have performed confirms the existence of such shared features and better clarifies their meanings. The review also highlights some noticeable differences. We emphasize three of them: a difference in specifying the targets of diagnostic inference, which are competence variables of differing generality in BNT and knowledge states in set-theoretical form in KST; differences between the kinds of mathematical tools used by preference in developing the structural setup of the models, which are concepts from graph theory in BNT and concepts concerning sets of subsets (so-called hypergraphs) and categories of abstract algebra in KST; and differences in the construction, justification, and flexibility of the probabilistic setup of the models. At least in part, such differences can be traced back to differences between the scientific environments in which the two paradigms had their origins, which are artificial intelligence relating to diagnostic processes under uncertainty for BNT, and formal modeling in psychometrics and psychophysics for KST.

Besides the computational side, there are other aspects of the two theories that are not present in our comparative review. I mention two of them. One is the use of models in a dynamic perspective, that is, as means for tracing the evolution of competencies of a student on a course, through repeated testing in successive stages of the learning process. The concepts of “dynamic Bayesian network” in BNT and of “learning path” in KST refer precisely to this important ability of models for knowledge assessment in the two theories ( Reichenberg, 2018 ;  Falmagne & Doignon, 2011 , Chapters 4 and 12). The other aspect is the use of the models as guides for adaptive testing, that is, for a testing session in which at each point the examiner selects the next question on the basis of the responses given thus far by the examinee, in order to optimize the efficiency of the testing procedure. The modular character of the models in both theories, and their focus on dependences and independences between the several variables involved, suggest that those models may indeed be suitable for this use.
