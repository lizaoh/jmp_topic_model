Prospect theory ( Kahneman & Tversky, 1979 ) has become one of the most important descriptive alternatives to expected utility theory. A central element of prospect theory is a weighting function which transforms single probabilities into decision weights. However, this transformation implies direct violations of first-order stochastic dominance (or intransitivities due to the editing phase in prospect theory) which are undesirable from a theoretical point of view. This disadvantage has motivated the development of cumulative prospect theory and variants on it ( Starmer & Sugden, 1989 ;  Luce & Fishburn, 1991,1995 ;  Tversky & Kahneman, 1992 ;  Wakker & Tversky, 1993 ;  Chateauneuf & Wakker, 1999 ;  Luce, 2000 ) which combine the descriptive advantages of prospect theory with the transformation of cumulative probabilities rather than single probabilities. The transformation of cumulative probabilities was introduced in rank-dependent utility theory ( Quiggin, 1982 ) and is consistent with first-order stochastic dominance.

A second central feature of prospect theory and cumulative prospect theory (CPT) is the fact that the value function depends on gains and losses relative to a reference point or status quo and not on final wealth positions as in expected utility theory. This fact, in the following referred to as reference dependence, has been advanced by  Kahneman and Tversky (1979, p. 273)  as a “cornerstone” of prospect theory which may explain why initial wealth has been ignored in the representation of prospect theory and all axiomatizations of CPT. More precisely, reference dependence means that the preference between two lotteries  P  and  Q  may differ from the preference between  P ′ and  Q ′ although  P  and  P ′ as well as  Q  and  Q ′ are identical lotteries in terms of final wealth. The change of preference can be due to a different status quo in both choice situations which implies that  P  and  P ′ as well as  Q  and  Q ′ are different lotteries in terms of gains and losses. However, reference dependence does not imply that initial wealth has no impact on preferences.  Kahneman and Tversky (1979, p. 277)  comment on this issue:

The emphasis on changes as the carriers of value should not be taken to imply that the value of a particular change is independent of initial position. Strictly speaking, value should be treated as a function in two arguments: the asset position that serves as reference point, and the magnitude of the change (positive or negative) from that reference point. An individual's attitude towards money, say, could be described by a book, where each page presents the value function for changes at a particular asset position. Clearly, the value functions described on different pages are not identical: they are likely to become more linear with increases in assets.

Until now, little work has been done to characterize reference dependence in prospect theory and CPT from a theoretical point of view. If we consider a variable status quo and allow the value function to vary with this status quo, reference dependence is obviously a generalization of the pure dependence on final wealth positions. In order to clarify this point, we identify as a first result a preference condition which allows one to discriminate between these two concepts. In the CPT framework this condition implies that the CPT representation reduces to rank-dependent expected utility. This is because reference dependence—with respect to the value and the weighting function—is the only difference between both models. The condition characterizes, therefore, the exact difference between both models in terms of preferences and provides an alternative axiomatization of rank-dependent utility.

All existing axiomatizations of CPT apply only to one particular asset position, that is, to a fixed status quo. This restriction is the starting point of the main analysis of this paper. As a first step an axiomatization of CPT is developed which allows for a variable status quo. Note, however, that we investigate neither the question of which outcome will be the status quo in a given situation nor the question how to measure the momentary location of the status quo empirically. As in most analyses of CPT we assume for convenience that the status quo is given by the current wealth. Due to the variability of the status quo in the present framework, we have to take explicitly into account the possibility that the value and the weighting function can change with increasing status quo which will be referred to as status quo dependence. More precisely, status quo dependence means that the preference between two alternatives can change if the status quo is varied. Unfortunately, there seems to be no empirical evidence reported in the literature in which way these functions will change. According to the quotation of Kahneman and Tversky, it seems plausible that the value function becomes more linear with increasing status quo. Due to the variability of the status quo it is possible to define decreasing, constant, and increasing risk aversion analogously to the expected utility framework. However, besides diminishing marginal utility of wealth, we also have to consider the probabilistic risk attitude for CPT which is determined by the curvature of the weighting function.

Altogether, there are several possible hypotheses which will be analyzed in detail. In particular, this paper provides an axiomatic treatment of how the degree of probabilistic risk aversion, the degree of loss aversion, as well as the marginal value of gains and losses change with increasing status quo.

One contribution of these preference conditions is to provide a tool for empirical research because they allow one to derive experimental designs for testing whether the value and the weighting function change substantially with increasing wealth and, moreover, in which way they change. Such evidence is required in order to apply CPT in situations with variable wealth.

The paper is organized as follows. In  Section 2  a CPT framework with a variable status quo is developed.  Section 3  is devoted to the value function while the weighting function is analyzed in  Section 4 .  Section 5  contains some concluding observations. All proofs are relegated to the appendix.

As already noted, all existing representations of CPT do not pay any attention to the initial wealth of a decision maker. This can be either justified by assuming a constant status quo or by the following axiom which requires that the preferences are independent of the status quo.

Status quo independence : For all  y, y′∈X  we have  P≿ y Q  if and only if  P≿ y′ Q .

Status quo independence is a somewhat extreme assumption since it implies that increasing wealth does not alter the preferences between lotteries at all. Expected utility theory and most other utility theories are characterized in some sense by the opposite extreme, i.e. preferences are determined completely by the final wealth position, irrespective of the formulation in terms of gains and losses. This property corresponds to the following condition:

Apart from the dependence on the reference point discussed above, the value function in prospect theory is mainly characterized by two properties. Firstly, it is S-shaped, i.e. concave for gains and convex for losses, and, secondly, it satisfies “loss aversion”, i.e. it is steeper for losses than for gains. One reason for  Kahneman and Tversky (1979)  to propose an S-shaped value function is their empirically observed “reflection effect” which says that for many individuals preferences in the domain of losses can be described to be nearly the mirror image of their preferences in the domain of gains. The S-shape of the value function in prospect theory and CPT is often referred to as “risk aversion for gains and risk seeking for losses”. However, this statement is rather imprecise since the attitude towards risk depends in CPT not only on the curvature of the value function but also on the curvature of the weighting function. Therefore, we will distinguish between the “probabilistic risk attitude”, determined by the curvature of the weighting function, and the “monetary risk attitude”, determined by the curvature of the value function.

In CPT it is rather difficult to derive a preference condition which guarantees that the value function is concave or convex on the entire domain because this requires, as in the analysis of loss aversion above, a comparison of value differences between positive and negative outcomes. Therefore, the curvature of the value function is analyzed in a first step for gains and losses separately. A decision maker is, consequently, defined to exhibit monetary risk aversion if her value function is concave for gains and also concave for losses. Monetary risk seeking and neutrality are defined analogously:

The starting point of this paper was an axiomatization of CPT with a variable status quo in order to derive a value and a weighting function which both additionally depend on the initial wealth position. This set the stage for characterizing several hypotheses about how these functions change with increasing status quo in terms of preference conditions. Altogether, I hope that the paper provides a comprehensive treatment of a variable status quo in the framework of CPT. Unfortunately, there seems to be no empirical evidence on how individual behavior towards risk changes with increasing status quo. I hope that the present paper will motivate such empirical work and help to identify sensible experimental designs. One involved problem is that experimental manipulations of wealth are likely to be ineffective which means that empirical work has to employ field data. The second major problem is that the status quo is not directly observable. This suggests the need for a theory which allows to identify the status quo in a given situation. Note that this important issue was not treated in the present paper.

The condition says that  w ( p , y )− w ( p + q , y )=− w ( r , y ) implies  w ( p , y ′)− w ( p + q , y ′)⩽− w ( r , y ′) for all  y ′> y  which shows that  w (·, y ′) satisfies  (11)  with ⩽ for  y ′> y  whenever  w (·, y ) satisfies  (10) . The fact that decreasing lower subadditivity is also necessary can be established as follows: Suppose that decreasing lower subadditivity is not satisfied. Then, there must exist  p , q , r  and  y ′> y  such that  w ( p , y )− w ( p + q , y )=− w ( r , y ) and  w ( p , y ′)− w ( p + q , y ′)>− w ( r , y ′). However, in this case  w (·, y ′) does not satisfy  (11)  with ⩽ although  w (·, y ) satisfies  (10) . The results for increasing and constant lower subadditivity as well as all the results for upper subadditivity can now easily be established by an analogous argument. □
