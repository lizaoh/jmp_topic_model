Contextuality (or lack thereof) is a property of systems of random variables. The theory of contextuality, which in psychology has developed from the extension of the theory of selective influences, provides the means to detect this property and, more recently, to measure the degree of (non)contextuality of a system. Among several measures of the degree of contextuality that have been proposed in the literature, I consider here two: the Contextual Fraction (

In psychology, several applications of contextuality analysis are due to the emergence of the quantum cognition field (see e.g.,  Bruza et al., 2009; Bruza, Wang et al., 2015; Busemeyer & Bruza, 2012; Busemeyer & Wang, 2015; Dzhafarov et al., 2017; Khrennikov et al., 2014 ), whereas other applications come from the selective influences literature. In quantum cognition, the search for contextuality has mainly served as means to advocate for the appropriateness of the quantum formalism. For example, in the study of language, the presence of contextuality may witness the lack compositionality of concepts ( Bruza, Kitto et al., 2015 ). As an extension of the theory of selective influences, the aim has been to develop a principled approach to the ubiquitous violations of marginal selectivity in psychological research (e.g.,  Basieva et al., 2019; Cervantes & Dzhafarov, 2017, 2018, 2019; Dzhafarov & Kujala, 2012, 2017b; Dzhafarov, Kujala, Cervantes et al., 2016; Dzhafarov et al., 2015; Zhang & Dzhafarov, 2015 ). Throughout the text, I shall use the data from  Cervantes and Dzhafarov (2018)  to illustrate the defined objects and computations. Their data come from an online experiment in which participants were asked to make two conjoint choices, one of a character and one of a characteristic to describe the character, in accordance with a storyline sketching Hans Christian Andersen’s “The Snow Queen”. The manipulations determined the pair of characters, and the pair of characteristics from which a participant made their choices.

The class of systems known as cyclic has had a prominent role in contextuality research. Cyclic systems are the object of Bell’s theorem ( 1964, 1966 ), the Leggett–Garg theorem ( 1985 ), and Suppes and Zanotti’s theorem ( 1981 ), among many other theoretical results (e.g.,  Araújo et al., 2013; Kujala & Dzhafarov, 2016 ). It is also encountered in most applications which empirically probe for contextuality (e.g.,  Adenier & Khrennikov, 2017; Asano et al., 2014; Cervantes & Dzhafarov, 2018; Dzhafarov et al., 2015; Hensen et al., 2015; Wang & Busemeyer, 2013 ).
