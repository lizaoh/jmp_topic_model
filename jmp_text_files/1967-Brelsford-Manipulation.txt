Stanford University, Stanford, California  In several avoidance conditioning experiments with rats, state occupancy in a Markov model was controlled by experimentally  manipulating the association between the conditioned stimulus and emotional responses. It was possible to establish  identifications between experimental operations and the values of parameters in the model. The results support a memory model  which is based upon the assumption that errors subsequent to the first avoidance response are due to the temporary loss of the  association between the conditioned stimulus and an emotional response rather than to the loss of an association involving the  instrumental running response.  During the past few years several investigators (Bower and Theios, 1964; Theios, 1963; Theios and Brelsford, 1966) have  developed Markovian descriptions for oneway, discrete-trials avoidance conditioning. In general, it has been found that some  type of three- or four-state Markov model provides a satisfactory analog to the conditioning process. Through the use of such  models, the studies cited above have reported accurate predictions with regard to both random variables defined upon the  entire data space and sequential aspects of the data.  These Markov models in abstract form provide an adequate description of avoidanceconditioning data, but a mere description of  the structure of the data is only the first step for psychological theorizing in general. What is important, once the  best-fitting abstract model has been discovered, is to interpret the structure of the model in some particular theoretical  manner by developing coordinating definitions between model events and hypothesized theoretical events in the conditioning  process.  A recent interpretation for the avoidance model was proposed by Theios and Brelsford (1966). They proposed a  temporary-permanent memory interpretation. The memory interpretation involves the assumption that the learning process begins  in the naive state, state N, where the probability of an avoidance response is zero.  1 This article is based upon a dissertation submitted to the Graduate School of the University of Texas in partial fulfillment  of the requirements for the Ph. First, the unconditioned stimulus (Su), in addition to eliciting the instrumental escape response, elicits some type of  emotional response (re) in the organism. A relatively permanent association is formed between se , the stimulus consequences  of re , and the instrumental running response (Rrun) such that se comes to elicit Rrun with a probability of unity.  Simultaneous with this first event, a second event is assumed to be taking place. This event involves the formation of an  association between the conditioned stimulus (Se) and shock-induced re . With probability c, these two events will have  occurred by any given trial. When these two events have occurred the learning process leaves the initial state of the model.  Once the learning process leaves state N errors by the subject are assumed to be due to the failure of the Se to induce re and  not to the failure of the se to elicit Rrun . When the process leaves state N, the association between the Se and re may be  stored in permanent memory, state P, with probability d. In state P correct responses will occur with a probability of unity  since Se always induces re , the stimulus consequences of which are permanently conditioned to Rrun . On the other hand, the  association between Se and re may be stored in temporary memory, state T, with probability (1 - d). As in permanent memory,  avoidance responses will occur with a probability of unity while the Se-re association is stored in temporary memory. However,  while in temporary memory the Se-re association may be forgotten, or if you prefer, may extinguish, with probability q during  the intertrial interval. When this happens the learning process moves to state F, re fails to be elicited by the Se , and an  error occurs due to the absence of the stimulus for Rrun . When the process is in state F, the subject gets shocked, and the  association between Se and re gets re-established with a probability of unity. The association may be stored in permanent  memory with probability e. With probability (1 - e) it is stored in temporary memory. If stored in temporary memory the  association may be forgotten during the intertrial interval with probability q, and the process then moves back to state F  before the start of the next trial. It should be kept  in mind that in each experiment all variables except those directly manipulated were held constant, and thus it will be  legitimate to compare groups from the different experiments.  SUBJECTS  The subjects were 451 experimentally naive male and female albino rats, between 90 and 130 days of age, obtained from the  Josamar Co. of Houston, Texas and from a local supplier. Subjects were housed in group cages and given ad lib food and water.  APPARATUS  The experiment was carried out in a fully automatic shuttle box which has been described elsewhere (cf. Theios and Brelsford,  1966). The S, was the complex consisting of the opening of the guillotine door separating the two compartments of the shuttle  box, the offset of a light in the starting compartment, the onset of a light in the safe compartment, and the introduction of  90-db wide-band white noise into both compartments. The Swas scrambled electric shock delivered by a constant-current  shocker set at 2.5 ma.  PROCEDURE  A one-way avoidance procedure, in which the starting side was randomized across all subjects, was used. The intertrial  interval was 30 sec. All trials began with the subject in the starting compartment. If the subject left the starting  compartment during a trial, he was returned there 5 sec. after the end of the trial. Pretest trials were given in order to  extinguish any initial escape responses to the S, . Pretest trials consisted of the presentation of the S, alone for I 1 sec.  During pretests, shock was never presented, and responses made by the subject had no e ect on the termination of the S, .  Pretest trials were given in a one-way manner, identical to the procedure used in training trials. Each subject was given  pretest trials until he attained a criterion of five successive failures to leave the starting compartment within 5 sec. after  S,. onset. Training began on the trial following the attainment of the pretest criterion.  PARAMETER ESTIMATION AND TEST OF THE ABSTRACT MODEL  In order to measure the goodness-of-fit of the mathematical predictions from the abstract model, a parameter-estimation group  was run Subjects were trained with a one-way avoidance procedure (cf. Theios and Dunaway, 1964). The data from this  24 BRELSFORD  group were compared to quantitative predictions from the model in order to measure the goodness-of-fit of the model. In  addition, the parameters estimated from this group were used in making all predictions about other groups in the various  experiments.  A control group was run in a manner identical to the parameter estimation group, except that the Su was never presented. In  this group the running response merely led to the termination of the Se . This group was run to investigate the possibility  that the Se was a noxious stimulus and that subjects would learn the instrumental running response merely to escape the Se .  If control group subjects perform as predicted by the model, no learning should take place in this group. This prediction of  no learning is equivalent to the assumption that parameter c of the model takes on the value of zero.  METHOD  Subjects. There were 166 subjects in the parameter-estimation group and 20 subjects in the control group.  Procedure. Following the pretest the parameter estimation group was trained in a delayed avoidance procedure (cf. Kimble,  1961) with a 5-sec. interstimulus interval. If the subject remained in the starting compartment for 5 sec. after the Se onset,  he was shocked until he entered the safe compartment. Thus, a criterion of 5 sec. was applied to the probability distribution  of response latencies. All response latencies exceeding the criterion were scored as errors, and all others were scored as  avoidance responses. Each subject was given trials until he attained a criterion of 10 consecutive avoidance responses, at  which time training was ended.  Following the pretest the control group was given acquisition training just as the parameterestimation group, except that no  shock was ever presented on error trials. Each trial began with the onset of So and ended with the subject moving into the  safe compartment or at the end of 11 sec., whichever event came first. If the subject remained in the starting compartment for  the entire trial, he was removed and handled between trials and then replaced in the starting compartment for the next trial.  If the trial ended with the subject in the safe compartment, he was returned to the starting compartment. Each subject was run  until he reached a criterion of 10 consecutive avoidance responses or for 25 trials, whichever event occurred first. Extinction after the first avoidance is thus represented by letting q take on the value  one and e take on the value zero after the process reaches state F. It was the purpose of the present experiment to see  whether or not this assumption concerning extinction would be upheld. Specifically, it was desired to investigate the process  of extinction at theoretically critical points in the learning process.  A group, called the re-extinction group, was trained routinely up to the trial following the first avoidance response. On that  trial the shock was turned off. Subjects under these conditions should absorb in state P or state F. That is, every subject  should generate an indefinitely long string of avoidances or errors. For the subjects who absorbed in an error run,  re-acquisition was begun, and again shock was not given after the trial of the next avoidance. This repeated acquisition and  extinction was continued until all subjects attained a criterion of 10 consecutive avoidance responses. Under these  circumstances the model makes certain predictions about the structure of acquisition and extinction data.  METHOD  Subjects. There were 70 subjects in the re-extinction experiment.  Procedure. Through the trial of the first avoidance response the re-extinction group was run exactly like the  parameter-estimation group. Following the first avoidance response the shock device was turned off, and, using the procedure  from the control group in the parameterestimation experiment, each subject was given trials until he made 10 consecutive  avoidances or 10 consecutive errors. If the subject made 10 consecutive avoidances, his training ended. If the subject made 10  consecutive errors, the shock device was turned on at the trial following the tenth error and would deliver Sif the subject  took longer than 5 sec. to respond. The shock device remained on until the subject made an avoidance response and then was  turned off. Then the extinction process was repeated. The cycle of acquisition and extinction was repeated until all subjects  made 10 consecutive avoidance responses. TESTS OF HYPOTHESES CONCERNING THE r,-s,-R,,, ASSOCIATION  The memory interpretation leads to the assumption that errors following the first avoidance response are never due to the  absence of the sr, Rrun association but are due to the absence of the S,--re association. An experiment was conducted to test  this assumption. The experiment involved three experimental groups and one control group, The three experimental groups were  similar in that they involved attempts to manipulate the re se Rrun association at various stages in the learning process. The  manipulation of re se-Rrun involved the presentation of a very brief shock simultaneously with Se onset on certain trials  following the first avoidance response. The brief shock was designed to elicit the re se , which, according to the model,  should lead to Rrun with a probability of unity. In one group, called the no-error group, the brief shock was introduced on  the trial following the first avoidance. In a second group, called the one-error group, subjects were allowed to make one  error after their first avoidance, and then the brief shock was introduced on the following trial. In the third experimental  group, called the ten-error grout), subjects were allowed to make ten errors after their first avoidance, and then brief shock  was introduced. In the one-error and ten-error groups, no Su shock was presented on the error trials following the first  avoidance response. Once brief shock was initiated for a group, it was continued until the subjects in the group reached a  success or an error criterion. The model predicts that the introduction of the brief shock and its consequent re-se should  cause subjects to make avoidance responses with a probability of unity. DISCUSSION  The present group of experiments has amassed a large amount of data in support of the memory interpretation for the Nlarkov  model for avoidance conditioning. In  44 BRELSFORD  most respects the memory model provides a satisfactory analog for the conditioning process. In addition, it has been  demonstrated that manipulations of certain experimental variables are isomorphic to allowing certain model parameters to take  on predictable values and to forcing transitions to specified states. The manipulations used were the turning oq and later the  turning back on of the Sshock and the application of a 0 1-sec. shock at Se onset during various phases of training.  In only one of the present experiments has the memory model needed additional assumptions to account for the data. That was in  the experiment which involved manipulation of re se through the use of brief shock. In the discussion of the no-error group  data of that experiment, it was pointed out that, since the memory model assumes that errors following the first avoidance are  never due to the extinction of se-Rrun , it must be concluded that brief shock was ineffective in eliciting re Se . However,  the memory model could be modified in such a way that extinction of se-Rrun would be possible. It could be postulated that  when the learning process is in state F and the subject does not respond, if no Su is presented, the se-Rrun association  extinguishes with some probability. Extinction could be represented by the learning process moving to state F' where both the  Se-re association and the se-Rrun association are assumed to be lost. The major di.Ticulty with such a modification is that it  assumes that extinction of se-Rrun occurs on the transition from state F to state F. Yet, the stimulus se is assumed never to  occur in state F. In other words, se-Rrun is assumed to extinguish without the occurrence of se . This assumption makes the  suggested modification of the memory model somewhat unreasonable unless one accepts a decay theory of forgetting. As an  alternative, it might be assumed that both Se and se become associated with Rrun . When no Su shock is presented while the  learning process is in state F, a competing response (Rsit) could become associated with Se . On brief-shock trials following  errors in state F, Se would elicit Rett and se would elicit Rrun . In such cases of response competition, it would be possible  for subjects to fail to reach an avoidance criterion following introduction of brief shock. The assumption that Se becomes  associated with Rrun or Rsit is difficult to make when one considers data obtained when subjects are prevented from making the  escape response on error trials. Theios and Brelsford (1966) found that preventing an escape response has no effect on  performance following the first avoidance. If Se becomes associated with Rrun , trapping would be expected to interfere with  learning on all trials, rather than just those prior to the first avoidance. In any case, the assumption made earlier  concerning the effectiveness of brief shock in eliciting re Se seems to be, at present, the most logical way to account for  the aberrant subjects in the present experiment.  One point at which the memory model appears to be at variance with the data is in the matter of extinction. Once the learning  process is in state P it is assumed that avoidance responses do not extinguish. Even though avoidance responses are extremely  resistant to extinction, it is not unreasonable to demand that a model for  EXPERIMENTAL MANIPULATION OF STATE OCCUPANCY 45  avoidance conditioning should permit the possibility of extinction. Atkinson, Bower, and Crothers (1965) have recently  presented an avoidance model in which extinction may occur. In their model, which is an extension of Theios' (1963) model, it  is proposed that in state P there is some probability that an avoidance response will not be elicited before the termination  of the S, . If this occurs and no shock follows, a competing response of not running may be conditioned. It should be noted  that the concepts employed in the extinction model of Atkinson et al. (1965) could easily be adapted to the memory model.  In conclusion it should be pointed out that the primary value in the memory interpretation for the four-state avoidance model  lies in its ability to stimulate and guide future research. The identifications presented here are those that have proved to  he useful in the past. It is certain, however, that other interpretations will be experimentally fruitful in future work. The  memory interpretation has led to some interesting hypotheses which generally have been supported in the present research. As  such, it would seem worthwhile, at least for the present, to continue using and modifying this interpretation.
