PACKARD  Department of Philosophy, Brigham Young University, Provo, Utah 84601  Various principles about preferences between sets of options are presented and considered with respect to several familiar  decision rules. Necessary and sufficient (or minimally sufficient) preference principles are given for each rule. The paper  concludes with an example of how easy it is to misjudge the reasonableness of preference principles.  Suppose you have to choose between two sets of possible outcomes A and B and some means or another then decides which possible  outcome will occur from the set you select. Which set would you pick? Obviously it depends on the means used to select the  final outcome. For example, if you can make the final choice then you might well use a maximax rule and choose A over B if the  best outcome in A is better than the best outcome in B. But if your arch enemy is to make the final choice, then you might use  a maxi-min rule and choose A over B if the worst outcome in A is better than the worst in B. If you aren't sure how friendly  the means is that makes the final selection, you might use a mixed max-min rule and choose A over B if a weighted average of  the best and the worst outcomes is greater in A than it is in B. If the final selection is done randomly then you might  consider the average utility and choose A over B if the average utility given that A occurs is greater than it is given that B  occurs. Or if you have some other idea of the likelihoods of each outcome's occuring, you might rely on expected utility and  choose A over B if the expected utility given that A occurs is greater than it is for B. A familiar example in which you might  use one of these approaches to choose between two sets of possible outcomes could be deciding to go to the beach or not when  your concern is whether it will be sunny. The possible outcomes would be the following, where g stands for going, -g for not  going, s for it being sunny and -s for not being sunny: (g, s), (g, -s), (-g, s), (-g, -s). You could then choose either to  go, which would be to choose {(g, s), (g, -s)}, or to not go, which would be to choose {(-g, s), (-g, -s)}. But it is nature  that picks the final outcome from the set you select.  This paper defines each of the above decision rules as a preference relation, a relation over non-empty subsets (or events) of  a finite set of possible outcomes. It then presents a list of preference principles some of which have already appeared in the  literature and shows that various of these principles are both necessary and sufficient or just necessary for characterizing  the given preference relations. The paper concludes with an observation of how easy it is to misjudge the plausibility of  preference principles.  295  0022-2496/79/030295-12$02.00/0  Copyright CQ 1979 by Academic Press, Inc.
