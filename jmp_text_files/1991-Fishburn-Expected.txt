They are not trick questions, but neither are they easy. Both concern decision making under  uncertainty and require a little background.  In a typical rendition of decision under uncertainty, a person is to choose a course of action, or act for short, whose  consequence depends on the act selected and on which state in a world of possibilities obtains. Everything valued by the  person is described in the consequences. The world of possibilities lies outside the person's control and consists of a set of  mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive states. Exactly one of these is the correct description of the world, or is the  state that obtains. The person is uncertain about which state obtains, but knows that it cannot be changed by the act selected.  Our questions address the subjective expected utility representation of preference between acts. The answer to Question 1 appears to be nobody. It is true that Tversky (1967) and Richter (1975) (see also Shapiro, 1979, for  n = 2) characterize the partially ordered companion of Model B in terms of abstract polynomial rings, but this does little  more than reformulate the quantitative structure in quasi-qualitative terms whose intuitive content is vanishingly small  except perhaps to a few mathematicians. Their work suggests that the goal of specifying "reasonable" necessary and sufficient  conditions for Model B is unattainable.  Which brings us to the more relevant Question 2. The published literature indicates that its two most common answers are von  Neumann and Morgenstern (1944) and Savage (1954). Both answers are wrong. Subjective probability does not appear in the  representation of von Neumann and Morgenstern. Their axioms apply > to a mixture set, which is a generalization of a convex  set most often interpreted as a set of lotteries or probability measures, and lead to the linear representation x > y p u(x)  >, u(y) with u(~x + (1 - A) y) = Au(x) + (1 - A) u(y) whenever x and y are in the mixture set and 0 5 A < 1. Savage does  axiomatize subjective probability along with utility, but his axioms force the state set to be infinite and imply an  infinite-I correspondent to Model B. It is clear from his remarks about sequences of coin flips (pp. 33 and 38) and small  worlds (see also Shafer, 1986) that he intended his theory to support Model B, but he did not axiomatize that model. Full  accounts of the von Neumann-Morgenstern and Savage theories appear in Fishburn (1970, 1989).  Affirmative answers to Question 2 are hard to find when its conditions are narrowly interpreted. The book has two other high points. The first extends Model B to infinite state spaces and offers a continuous utility  alternative to theories of Savage (1954) and Fishburn (1970, 1982). The second is an alternative approach to a theory of  subjective expected utility with monotone but not necessarily additive subjective probability that was fashioned by David  Schmeidler in the early 1980s to accommodate, in part, the ambiguity problem raised by Ellsberg (1961). The expectation  operation in its representation is done in a cumulative differences manner after Choquet (1953-1954). We now have three  axiomatizations of this representation: Schmeidler (1989) uses a lottery approach, Gilboa (1987) modifies Savage's axioms, and  Wakker follows the topological approach that is used throughout the book. Other  408 BOOK REVIEW  alternatives to the basic subjective expected utility model are reviewed in Fishburn (1988).  In addition to its nearly error-free typography, mathematical rigor, and range of interesting results, Additive  representations of preferences has other pleasing features. It has informative historical summaries, many good examples and  counterexamples, preproof outlines of longer proofs, simplified models of technical concepts, and wonderful footnotes. The  level of mathematics is demanding, but the author provides many helpful guidelines. The book is indispensable for all  researchers who deal with additive measurement, and it will be a valuable text or supplementary text for graduate courses in  mathematical economics, mathematical psychology, and decision theory.
