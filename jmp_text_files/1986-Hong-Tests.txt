In particular, tests that involve a problem structure introduced by Allais (1953) show that  individuals often violate expected utility's linearity assumption (i.e., strong independence axiom). In recent decades, many  theories of choice under uncertainty have been proposed as alternatives to expected utility. This paper describes and tests  weighted utility theory, a generalization of expected utility in which the independence axiom is weakened such that nonlinear  choice behavior, including the Allais paradox, can be accounted for. The tests use a generalization of Allais' problem  structure which demands consistency over a greater number of choice situations. INTRODUCTION  The failure of expected utility theory as a general representation of actual choice behavior under uncertainty is largely  attributable to the theory's assumption that utility (or preference) is linear in the probabilities. Linearity means that, for  a finite lottery A consisting of outcomes xi with probabilities ai, expected utility (EU) is equal to the mathematical  expectation of the outcomes' utilities u(xi), i.e., EU(A) = Y_ u(x,)ai. In axiomatizations of expected utility, linearity is  normally expressed by a "strong" independence axiom: A is preferred to B if and only if a lottery with a # chance of receiving  A (and a 1 -# chance of receiving G) is preferred to a lottery with a # chance of receiving B (and a 1-# chance of receiving  G). THE HILO LOTTERY STRUCTURE  The problem structure employed by Allais (1953) involves embedding two alternatives in otherwise identical lotteries. In the  example shown earlier, the alternatives A (having a 1.00 chance of yielding an intermediate outcome, xi) and B (having a ft  chance of yielding a high outcome, XH, and a 1- /3 chance of yielding a low outcome, XL) were embedded in the lotteries C  (having an a chance of yielding A and a 1- a chance of yielding x,) and D (having an a chance of yielding B and a 1-a chance  of yielding xI), and in the lotteries E (having an a chance of yielding A and a 1-a chance of yielding XL) and F (having an a  chance of yielding B and a 1 -a chance of yielding XL). Note that the only difference between C and E (D and F) is the "other"  outcome, xI or XL.  The HILO lottery structure (see Chew & MacCrimmon, 1979b) is a straightforward generalization of the problem structure  employed by Allais (1953). The structure is specified by two probabilities, a and f, and three outcomes, XL, xt, and xH,  arranged in ascending order of preference. These parameters are combined into four binary (A vs. B) choice situations  (referred to as the O-, I-, L-, and H-situations ), which differ only in terms of the "other" outcome used. In the  O-situation, Ao has a 1.00 chance of yielding xt, while Bo has a f3 chance of yielding XH and a 1 -# chance of yielding XL. TESTS OF WEIGHTED UTILITY 65  The next two sections report empirical tests of weighted utility theory. The objective of the tests is to determine which, if  any, of the three hypotheses best represents actual choice behavior within the HILO lottery structure. The tests involve  comparisons of the implications of the hypotheses against chance and each other.  5. EXPERIMENT I  Method  The subjects were 56 business students at the University of Arizona. They were paid $3 each for their participation, which  took on average approximately 30 min. Each subject responded to a questionnaire which presented three HILO structures, the  parameter values of which are shown in Table 3. These HILO structures were used to assess the robustness of weighted utility  theory over different contexts. Context lb was constructed by subtracting $40 from each outcome in Context la. Context lc was  constructed by "reflecting" the outcomes in Context la at $0.  For each HILO structure, the O-situation was always presented first. However, steps were taken to minimize the effects of  order on the subjects' responses for the other situations. One-half of the subjects were presented with one random order of  the H-, I- and L-situations and one-half of the subjects were presented with another random order of these situations. Also,  one-half of the subjects were presented with one random order of the three HILO structures and one-half of the subjects were  presented with another random order. Finally, one-half of the subjects were presented with each A; alternative followed by the  corresponding B; alternative and onehalf of the subjects were presented with the reverse order for each situation. CONCLUDING REMARKS  The rejection of expected utility theory as a formal representation of actual choice behavior under uncertainty creates a  demand for alternative choice theories and the testing of their implications. This paper has described weighted utility  theory, a generalization of expected utility, and reported two experiments which tested its implications for choice behavior  in the HILO lottery structure. A primary result was that a variation of weighted utility (referred to as the light hypothesis)  consistently outpredicted two other variations (referred to as the heavy and neutral hypotheses) across a variety of contexts.  Since the implications of the neutral hypothesis for any given set of outcomes are the same as those of expected utility, the  light hypothesis of weighted utility also outpredicted expected utility. The light hypothesis was consistent with 55 % of the  observed choice patterns in one experiment and 62% in the other.  Perhaps most interesting are those choice patterns that are consistent with the light hypothesis but violate the strong  independence axiom of expected utility (i.e., AoAIBLAH or BOBIBLAH). These patterns accounted for 33% of the data in the one  experiment and 30% in the other. Subjects whose choices conformed to these patterns behaved as if they required a higher  probability for the mixture of Ao and x; than for the mixture of Bo and x; (i = L, H) to achieve indifference. However, it  should be emphasized that the experiments reported herein were intended to test whether a particular numerical relational  system (weighted utility theory) validly captured a particular empirical relational system (the experimental data). The  testing of information processing hypotheses about behavior consistent with the light hypothesis is left for future research.  Other possibilities for future research include the following. First, although the subjects were compensated for their  participation, pay was not contingent on specific choices. A future experiment which bases pay on actual choices is feasible  but probably would be constrained to use small monetary outcomes. Second, the robustness of the results, especially the  superior predictive ability of the light hypothesis, should be further tested using different outcome and probability  parameter values. Third, an alternative approach to the empirical testing of weighted utility would be to assess an  individual's weight and utility functions in one context and use these to predict his or her choices in other contexts.  Fourth, the rate of violations of the betweenness property ranged from 16 to 32 %, which is sufficiently high to warrant more  research on this issue. Finally, this study's experiments tested only variations of weighted utility, including a test of  certain  72 CHEW AND WALLER  variations of weighted utility against expected utility. Future tests should be designed to distinguish between or among  various alternatives to expected utility, for example, a test of weighted utility theory against prospect or regret theory.
