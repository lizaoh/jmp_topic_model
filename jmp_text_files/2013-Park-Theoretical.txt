The Multinomial Logit Model (MNL) is often used in a variety of situations in economics ( Hausman & McFadden, 1984 ). However, the MNL suffers from the Independence of the Identity of the Alternatives (IIA) property articulated by  Debreu (1960) . The IIA property imposes the restriction that the relative odds of selecting between any two alternatives are independent of the identity of alternatives. Since then, many solutions have been attempted for the IIA property (for examples see  Adamowicz et al., 2008 ).  McFadden (1981)  categorized these solutions into three forms: Probit models based on the work of  Thurstone (1927) , Elimination-By-Aspects (EBA) models proposed by  Tversky (1972a,b) , and Generalized Extreme Value (GEV) models proposed by  McFadden (1978, 1981) . More recently, Batley and Daly (2006)  showed that a special case of the EBA (HEBA) model is equivalent to a special case of the GEV model (Nested Logit). Based on  Batley and Daly’s (2006)  study,  Adamowicz et al. (2008)  pointed out that if GEV and EBA are observationally equivalent, then one cannot know whether individuals make choices according to the compensatory decision rules of GEV or the elimination rules of EBA.

However, the EBA model, explaining the process of a person’s choice when faced with many alternatives that share multiple aspects, has received significant attention because it represents choices in terms of the actual psychological processes that may be taking place in the consumer’s mind. The most common definition of the degrees of freedom includes the number or pieces of information that are free to vary ( Healey, 1990 ;  Jaccard & Becker, 1990 ;  Pagano, 2004 ;  Warner, 2008 ; Wonnacott and Wonnacott 1990). More specifically, the degrees of freedom are commonly defined by the number of independent (of free) observations minus the number of independent (or free) parameters estimated ( Toothaker & Miller, 1996; Walker, 1940 ).

Bamber and van Santen (2000)  stated a counting rule of degrees of freedoms that a model is quantitatively testable if and only if the number of free parameters is fewer than the number of free observations. According to them, the counting rule can be safely applied only to identifiable models. Note that a model is identifiable if the values of parameters in a model can be ascertained from empirical observations. Thus, one cannot expect to use empirical data to ascertain a unique value for the parameter vector in a model if a model is not identifiable. This means that the degrees of freedom (the number of independent observations minus the number of parameters estimated) can also be safely applied to identifiable models.

In the estimation step, the EBA parameters (of which the number satisfies Eq.  (2) ) are estimated from the independent observations (of which the number satisfies Eq.  (3) ). However, the EBA parameters are not determined uniquely without a constraint. In the reparameterization step, thus, Tversky assumed that the sum of all parameters is ‘1’ (the normalizing constraint on EBA parameters) to obtain unique EBA parameters.

In summary,  Batsell et al. (2003)  proposed a linear version of the EBA model and developed a least squares method to estimate the linear version of the EBA model. However, their estimation method has a defect because their estimation method does not utilize the independent observations in the full choice set. We correct the error of their least squares method and conduct a simulation study based on the fixed estimation method in the next section.
