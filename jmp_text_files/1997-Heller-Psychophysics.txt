On the Psychophysics of Binocular Space Perception* 

Ju�rgen Heller 

Universita�t Regensburg, Regensburg, Germany 

Binocular vision gives rise to a perceptual space that is endowed with a rich geometrical structure, which according to Luneburg is a non-Euclidean Riemannian geometry of constant Gaussian curvature. This hypothesis, together with certain psychophysical assumptions, provides a qualitative explanation of classical empirical phenomena. The Luneburg theory has been less successful in quantitatively predicting experimental data, a fact that has sometimes been taken as evidence against the presumed geometrical structure. Some of these shortcomings, however, may also be attributed to psychophysical assumptions that are independent of the geometry of visual space, and that were found to be violated systematically. We propose a generalization of Luneburg's psychophysical mapping that can account for these findings. The foundation of a psychologically significant recoordinatization of physical space is formulated within a conjoint measurement approach. It is shown that psychophysical invariances lead to restrictions on the possible form of the coordinate transformation, but its complete specification is left as an open problem. The proposed approach can at least partially explain some empirical observations that are considered to contradict Luneburg's conception of a psychophysical theory of binocular space perception. ] 1997 Academic Press 

It has rarely been questioned that the world as we see it can be represented by a unitary perceptual space. The socalled visual space is distinguished from other perceptual spaces, like color space, by its compelling phenomenological reality. Moreover, there are no doubts on its spatial character. It thus seems straightforward to embody visual space in a geometrically formulated perceptual theory. A highly sophisticated approach pursuing this objective has been suggested by Luneburg (1947). The Luneburg theory of binocular vision not only provides a geometrical characterization of the internal structure of visual space but also describes its dependence on physical space. As a psychophysical theory it sets out to account for classical empirical phenomena, like the Helmholtz frontal horopter (Helmholtz, 1867) or the visual alleys (Hillebrand, 1902; Blumenfeld, 1913). These phenomena arise under viewing conditions isolating the effect of binocularity by excluding monocular cues and experiential factors. To realize these

Unless otherwise stated these conditions, for which the term primitive visual space has been coined by Roberts and Suppes (1967), apply to all theories and experimental situations discussed in the present paper. 

The frontal horopter results from arranging stimuli in the horizontal plane at eye-level such that they are perceived as lying on a frontoparallel straight line. The resulting physical configurations, however, are not straight at all. At near distances they are curved toward the observer and get increasingly curved in the opposite direction as distance grows. 

The visual alleys are again configurations in the horizontal plane at eye-level and are obtained in the following way. Two stimuli are placed symmetrically to the median line at a fixed position. At various distances nearer to the observer there are presented pairs of stimuli, being movable transversely to the left and the right, that are to be adjusted according to two different criteria. If the pairs of the stimuli are adjusted to match the distance between the fixed points and such that they are again lying symmetrically with respect to the direction straight ahead, then we obtain a distance alley. The so-called parallel alley results from arranging the two rows of stimuli such that they appear to be straight, parallel lines lying symmetrically to the left and the right of the median. It has often been replicated that the settings obtained under the different instructions do not coincide. For most observers, the parallel alley falls inside the distance alley. 

The important achievement of the Luneburg theory of binocular vision is to relate these phenomena to a nonEuclidean geometrical structure of visual space.

There is an impressive body of experimental data testing the empirical adequacy of the Luneburg theory (Hardy, Rand, Rittler, Blank, 6 Boeder, 1953; Zajaczkowska, 1956; Indow, Inoue, 6 Matsushima, 1962a, 1962b, 1963). The theoretical predictions, which result from estimating the free parameters _ and K, were found to be qualitatively in accordance with the data. This is the case for the general form of the Helmholtz frontal horopter as well as the discrepancy between distance and parallel alleys. The exact quantitative predictions, however, are mostly not within experimental error (see the discussion in Indow, 1991). Apparently, only rather coarse, qualitative predictions may be derived from the highly sophisticated theory of Luneburg. This situation is, of course, not completely satisfying. 

A number of experiments have been conducted to investigate whether the empirical failure of individual assumptions of the Luneburg theory is responsible for the indicated problem. In the present paper, we want to focus on postulate (L2), which is logically independent from the geometrical structure of visual space as formulated in (L1). Experimental data, which will be reported in detail below, reveal that the psychophysical assumptions in (L2) are violated in a systematic way. Blank (1978) therefore emphasizes the necessity of generalizing the psychophysical mappings of the Luneburg theory. There is, however, no satisfying theoretical explanation of the data that could serve as a basis for this task. In the sequel, we want to develop such a generalization within a measurement theoretic framework. 

2. PERCEIVED EGOCENTRIC DISTANCE AND DIRECTION 

Except for Foley (1965) there are practically no empirical studies of the locus of constant perceived direction. One reason for this lack of experimental data is the difficulty maintaining single vision with stimuli at different distances being close in direction. While fixating any point of such a configuration, the remaining ones will give rise to double vision very easily, especially under the restricted viewing conditions applied. A second reason may be that at all practical distances the hyperbol$ of Hillebrand nearly perfectly coincide with their asymptotes, which are the radial straight lines y=x tan . emerging from the origin. The a priori plausibility of this trajectory lying between the monocular lines of sight may have prevented researchers from empirically testing its adequacy. 

Even within Foley's setup Luneburg's assumption concerning the hyperbol$ of Hillebrand cannot be called in question, since all stimuli were presented to appear approximately at the same egocentric distance. As a consequence of his data showing a systematic overestimation of perceived direction, Foley suggested modifying the equation in (L3) to %=1.1 } .. 

There are a number of experimental studies of the locus of perceived equidistance (Hardy et al., 1953; Foley, 1966, 1970). In a careful and well-documented study, Foley (1966) presented observers with configurations consisting of five point-like light sources in a dark room. The reference point appeared straight ahead at distances of 120, 220, 320, and 420 cm. The remaining four points with fixed bipolar latitude of .=&24%, &12%, 12%, 24% had to be adjusted to match the egocentric distance to the reference point. The results reveal remarkable and consistent deviations from the Vieth�Mu�ller circle and large interindividual differences, which are exemplified by Fig. 3, showing the data of two observers. The locus of perceived equidistance for Subject 1 is nearly symmetric with respect to the x-axis and shows a tendency toward physical equidistance, as already reported by Hardy et al. (1953). The resulting curves of Subject 10, however, are skewed with the points lying in front of the Vieth�Mu�ller circle in the left half of the visual field and behind it in the right half. Such a skewing occurs more or less in every data set. Moreover, the locus of perceived equidistance cannot be characterized by a single pattern of parallax differences to the corresponding Vieth�Mu�ller circles. The absolute values of these differences decrease with increasing distance. This contradicts the Luneburg assumption that #-shifts as introduced in (L3) preserve perceived equidistance. Foley (1970) has also found non-constant parallax differences using a stereoscopic display, which rules out an explanation by the joint influence of location and illumination cues as discussed in Foley (1966).

6. CONCLUSIONS 

The present paper proposes a generalization of the theory of binocular vision postulated by Luneburg (1947). It is in particular concerned with the psychophysical assumption that the preimages of the perceptual polar coordinates of visual space under the psychophysical mapping are the Vieth�Mu�ller circles and the hyperbol$ of Hillebrand, respectively. There is empirical evidence that the locus of perceived equidistance deviates systematically from the Vieth�Mu�ller circle. As Blank (1978) notes it is necessary to theoretically account for these often replicated empirical findings to render the geometrical structure of visual space empirically testably in a psychophysical setting. 

The theory developed in the present paper has several advantages over previous approaches (Blank, 1978; Foley, 1980). First, it covers both the locus of perceived equidistance as well as the locus of constant perceived direction. These two perceptual attributes are interlocked by a property that has been called duality. This condition, describing the relationship of the preimages of the perceptual coordinates, can be considered the transposition of the orthogonality of the latter in visual space into the physical plane spanned by the bipolar coordinates :, ;. Luneburg (1947) assumed these trajectories to be orthogonal in the :;-plane (see Eq. (2)). From the postulate that the psychophysical mapping of the Cartesian xy-plane into visual space is angle-preserving, Dro�sler (1988) derives the orthogonality of the corresponding trajectories in the xy-plane. Consequently, the orthogonal trajectories to the hyperbol$ of Hillebrand, which are the so-called ovals of Cassini, are suggested as representing the locus of perceived equidistance. Arbitrary coordinate transformations as, for example, from Cartesian into bipolar coordinates or vice versa, are not necessarily angle-preserving. Stating the ``orthogonality condition'' for the preimages of the perceptual coordinates in a certain coordinate system thus 

amounts to qualifying these coordinates as psychologically significant. This is exactly what Luneburg (1947) postulates for #.-coordinates (or equivalently :;-coordinates), and what Dro�sler (1988) implies for Cartesian coordinates. But as a consequence of the large inter-individual differences, which also show up in Fig. 3, there cannot be a single physical coordinate system having this property. The present paper provides the desired psychologically significant recoordinatization by referring to the coordinates 1, 8 as introduced in Eq. (10). The trajectories 1=const. and 8=const. are obviously orthogonal in the plane spanned by f (:) and g( ;), they are generally not orthogonal, but satisfy duality in bipolar coordinates. 

As a second advantage, the development of the theory is guided by measurement theoretic principles providing sufficient conditions that may be tested experimentally without specifying the exact form of the psychophysical mapping. It has nevertheless been shown that a prediction of the locus of perceived equidistance derived for a specific functional form is very close to the data of Foley (1966). A recently conducted experimental test of the Reidemesiter condition, which is one of the key conditions the representation is founded on, will be reported in detail elsewhere (Heller, 1996). The results, however, may unequivocally be considered as supporting the suggested psychophysical theory and, thus, encourage the attempt to completely specify the coordinate transformation (#, .) [ (1, 8). Experimental tests of the geometry of binocular visual space in a psychophysical context, like the test of the assumption of constant Gaussian curvature developed by Eschenburg (1980), can then be carried out. 

There are, however, experimental results that are considered to contradict Luneburg's conception of a psychophysical theory of binocular space perception. Indow et al. (1962a, 1962b, 1963) reported a considerable intraindividual variation of the observer-specific constants K, _ over different sessions within the same experiment, and over different experimental tasks or stimulus configurations (see also Indow, 1991). Within the proposed approach, the variability over time may imply that only a certain form of the functions f, g can be specified, whereas the values of the constants in these functions are showing some fluctuation. This does not necessarily affect the internal structure of visual space. Although with f, g the coordinates 1, 8 are varying, the curvature K characterizing the geometry may remain constant. Concerning the second type of variability it is quite plausible that the presented generalization can at least partially account for the dependence of K upon the stimulus configuration. Presenting some stimuli lying on a Vieth�Mu�ller circle, for example, and assuming that they appear equidistant will be more or less correct (as reflected by the discrepancy between 1, 8 and the modified bipolar coordinates), depending upon the actual physical location of the stimuli. Within the Luneburg theory, all these effect 

42 

JU�RGEN HELLER 

will alter the value of K and will thus be falsly interpreted as changes of the internal structure of visual space. 

Wagner (1985) conducts an experiment outdoors in a large, open field under full-cue conditions (see also Battro, Pierro Netto, 6 Rozestraten, 1976). The Luneburg theory is considered to fail in predicting the obtained data. However, Wagner arrives at this conclusion by applying Luneburg's formulas derived from Eq. (3), which only hold for pointlike stimuli lying in a slanted plane through the eyes in a frameless space. An appropriate formulation of the generalized Luneburg theory for three-dimensional visual space taking into account, for example, the vertical disparities (see Luneburg, 1947; Indow, 1979) that were present in the stimulus configurations, may come to a different result. It would be interesting to compare this approach with an alternative explanation suggested by Wagner (1985) in which visual space is an affine-transformed version of a Euclidean physical space. The characteristic feature of the data of Wagner (1985) that has to be explained is the following. The distances between stimuli oriented in depth are considerably underestimated, compared with those between stimuli in the frontal plane. It has to be mentioned that Foley (1972) obtained similar results for primitive visual space, which are taken to provide evidence against the assumption of constant Gaussian curvature (Suppes et al., 1989; Suppes, 1995). Again, it would be interesting to see whether there is a psychologically significant recoordinatization of physical space that could predict such an effect. 

The above discussion obviously raised more questions than it answered. But we are only beginning to understand which aspects of the reliable empirical findings collected so far are revealing the internal structure of visual space, and which aspects are reflecting properties of the psychophysical function that are not related to the geometry. As a first step it will be quite helpful to reanalyse available data within the suggested theoretical framework. But further experimental, as well as theoretical, work is required to determine the potential and the limits of a psychophysical theory of binocular space perception in the spirit of Luneburg.
