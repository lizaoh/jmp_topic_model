MARLEY  Department of Psychology, McGill University, Montreal, Canada  A probabilistic model for choice, and preference, is introduced that includes (Tversky's) elimination by aspects model, and  the random utility model, as special cases. The model is based on a covert sequential elimination process, the element that is  finally chosen in a simple choice experiment being the eventual lone survivor of the elimination process. The model leads us  to question the usual form of simple choice experiments, in which a subject must (eventually) choose one of the currently  available alternatives, and to suggest that a much more realistic experimental design would allow the subject the no-choice  option, i.e., he may refuse to accept any of the currently available alternatives.  INTRODUCTION  Illustrative examples and counterexamples are often used to motivate new probabilistic models for simple choice, and  preference, experiments. Tversky (1972a, b) took such an approach in presenting his elimination by aspects (EBA) model, and  although this model can handle various counterexamples to earlier models, its range of applicability is still somewhat  constrained. In this introduction, we present an example, the "data" of which are not describable by the elimination by  aspects model, but are compatible with our generalization of the random utility model (Luce and Suppes, 1965). The body of the  paper considers this generalization which includes the EBA model as a special case. The following example is thus an  illustration of the wider applicability of the current model, while not necessarily a compelling counterexample to earlier  models. The first is that it implies regularity (Luce and Suppes, 1965), a very  weak independence assumption. The content of this assumption is that one cannot increase the probability of a subject's  choosing some currently available item by adding further alternatives to the choice set. Although regularity is often a  plausible condition, we present two examples (the first perhaps facetious) where it would not be expected to hold. The first  example concerns a lady in a small town, who wishes to buy a hat. She enters the only hat store in town, and finds two hats, a  and bI , that she likes equally well, and so might be considered equally likely to buy. However, now suppose that the sales  clerk discovers a third hat, b2 , identical to bI . Then the lady may well choose hat a for sure (rather than risk the  possibility of seeing someone wearing a hat just like hers), a result that contradicts regularity. The second example involves  a guest being taken out to dinner, who, in deference to his host, refrains from selecting the most expensive item on the menu.  Clearly, the addition of a single alternative can cause the most expensive dish to become the second most expensive, thereby  increasing its chances of being chosen, again contradicting regularity. These examples, and others (Corbin, 1973), reorient  models of choice towards more concern for contextual and other information that is essentially contained in the set of  available alternatives; we briefly consider such extensions in the discussion section.  The second limitation of the model concerns the nature of the decisions available to the decision maker. In our initial  example, we acted as if the decision maker is compelled to choose exactly one of the current applicants. However, in reality,  we often have the option of refusing to choose, e.g., in the above case, if I wanted to hire a Canadian, and all the  applicants were American, I would not choose one of the available applicants, but would either simply delay my choice, or look  elsewhere. Although the basic model that we describe does not deal with this no-choice option, a natural extension of it does  do so and is discussed toward the end of the paper.  THEORETICAL SUMMARY  In a simple choice experiment, a subject is asked to select one element from a set of available alternatives according to some  specified criterion (preference, loudness, size, etc.). The intuition of this EBA model is as follows: when presented with the available set  A, the subject selects, by a well-specified procedure, some appropriate or desirable "aspect," and rejects those elements of A  that do not possess that "aspect." We note in passing that the choice model (Luce, 1959) is a special case of the EBA model (Tversky,  1972a).  In this paper, we suggest a third model of the transition process, based on a generalization of the random utility model (Luce  and Suppes, 1965). This third model includes  278 CORBIN AND MARLEY  the EBA model as a special case. As a means of introducing the model, we refresh the reader's mind on the definition of a  random utility model.3 We have a master set T, and a random vector U defined on T, such that there is a zero probability of  any two components of the random vector being equal. If A is the available set at a given instance, then the probability of  the subject selecting x E A for further consideration, (i.e., as the "best" element) is given by the probability that the  x-component of the vector U is greater than the y-component of the vector U for all y E A - {x}. Clearly, this model leads to  the selection of an element of A in one step. It is equally clear how to generalize the model to incorporate possible  transitions from the presented set A to some subset B C A, I B I =A 1: simply drop the restriction that there is zero  probability of two (or more) components of the random vector being equal. The revised model thus assumes that there is a  random vector U defined on T such that if A _C T is available for consideration, then the probability that the subject selects  a (nonempty) subset B C A for further consideration is given by the probability that all components of the random vector are  equal for elements in B, which are in turn larger than the components for elements in A - B; these latter components, however,  may be unequal. The probability of this event is denoted QA(B). The process then starts anew, with B the currently available  set, and with resampling of the random vector U, etc., and is repeated until a single element remains.2  We later demonstrate that the EBA-model is equivalent to the special case of the above model in which each component of the  random vector takes on two values only, for example, 0, 1. From this result, it should be apparent why in the EBA model we may  freely talk either of accepting elements with a relevant aspect, or of rejecting elements without a relevant aspect. Also, we  show that the two-valued model is, in fact, more restrictive than the general model, and this result in turn indicates why the  EBA model is more restrictive than it might be.  Tversky (1972b) showed that the EBA model is a random utility model (with zero probability of equality amongst the components  of the random vector). In a parallel proof, we show that the above "generalization" of the random utility model also generates  choice probabilities that are compatible with a random utility model (with zero probability of equality amongst the components  of the random vector). These results should not be interpreted as showing that neither the EBA, or the above, model add  anything beyond our understanding as based on the usual random utility model. Both models add intuitions regarding the process  whereby the choice probabilities may be generated; they talk about (possibly observable) sequential elimination processes;  finally, as we indicate in the discussion, they lead us to consider choice experiments in which refusal to choose any of the  "available" options is itself considered to be an option.  s It makes no difference whether we define the usual random utility model in terms of the choice probabilities P or the  transition probabilities Q, since the model only deals with transitions from sets to single elements. DISCUSSION  We now comment on the two issues raised toward the end of the introduction: the likelihood of the data satisfying regularity,  and the requirement that the subject choose (at least) one of the available alternatives.  RANDOM UTILITY MODELS WITH EQUALITY 291  An important aspect' of the models that we have considered is that, implicitly or explicitly, they assume that the subject is  always aware of the set of potentially available alternatives T, even though, at any given time, the set of actually available  alternatives A is some subset of T. Our counterexamples to regularity rely on a manipulation of the subject's knowledge of the  potentially available set T. For instance, in the hat example, it is crucial that we pose the problem first as a choice  between two distinct hats, and then talk of adding a third hat, identical to one of the first two; for if the lady knew that  the third hat existed in the storeroom, even though it was not currently available for choice (e.g., it was being held for  someone else), then presumably she would always choose the "unique" hat. Thus, regularity tends to fail when the subject  chooses on different occasions with differing knowledge of the potentially available set. The subject can decrease the  possibility of this happening by delaying his choice, and collecting more information-thus we see a subtle interaction between  the conditions under which regularity holds, and the availability of the no-choice option. Before leaving regularity, we  suggest that an "information processing" approach may be appropriate in cases where knowledge about the potentially available  alternatives is manipulated. Thus, suitable formulations of the following "procedures" lead to the choices suggested in our  original examples.  The hat example:  List the potentially available objects.  Delete those objects that occur in two or more "identical" versions. If no element remains, search elsewhere.  If one or more actually available element remains, choose "best."  In the last line, we can specify further procedures, or, perhaps, a stochastic model. The restaurant example:  List the potentially available entrees.  If only one currently available entree, choose it.  Otherwise, delete the most expensive potentially available entree. Choose the "best" of the remaining actually available  entrees.  In the last line, we can again specify further procedures, or, perhaps, a stochastic model.  For further discussion of similar models, see MacCrimmon (1968), who considers how various procedures or decision rules (such  as dominance, satisficing, additive weighting) may be used sequentially in reaching decisions; and Payne and Braunstein  (1971), who present an "information processing" model of people's choices between gambles. It remains for future research to  decide the extent to which such ideas complement current stochastic models. When defining a (transition)  random utility model, we assumed that for A C T,  QA(A) = Pr[Ux = Uy = 0 for all x, y E A] + Pr[Ux = Uy = 1 for all x, y E A].  We commented that this was not the only possible way to assign these terms, especially the first, but that it was nonetheless  necessary to assign this term in such a way that the elimination process would eventually terminate in the choice of a single  element of the available set A. However, interpreting the (Boolean) random vector in terms of the presence, or absence, of  "aspects," it is plausible to suggest that when the aspect currently under consideration is not available (stochastically) in  any of the available alternatives, then the subject refuses to choose one of those alternatives.a For instance, if you offer  me a choice between various beverages, and I am hungry, but not thirsty, I will tell you so, and either have you change the  available set, or seek elsewhere for a snack. We call this the no-choice option. It might be considered purely a delaying  operation, but it is entirely different to the kind of behavior that we might expect to see when all the available  alternatives possess the "aspect" under current investigation. For this reason we propose, for further research, the  distinction between  and  QA(A) = Pr[Ux = Uy = 1 for all x, y E A]  QA( 0 ) = Pr[Ux = Uy = 0 for all x, y E A],  the latter being the theoretical counterpart of the no-choice option.  To our knowledge, little theoretical or empirical work has been done which includes the no choice option. Corbin (1973)  performed a preliminary experiment in which subjects could either choose between two currently available (known) gambles, or  pay a certain amount of money to have a third (currently unknown) gamble added to the available set. A more realistic paradigm  would include the possibility that, when the subject delays his choice, one or more of the currently available options ceases  to be available; by manipulating prior familiarity with the total range of alternatives, one could obtain data concerning  relations between knowledge of what is available, and likelihood of delay.
