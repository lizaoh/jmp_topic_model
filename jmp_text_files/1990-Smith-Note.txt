JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 34, 445-459 (1990)  Theoretical Note  A Note on the Distribution of Response Times for a Random Walk with Gaussian Increments  PHILIP L. SMITH  University of Adelaide  In this note, response time distribution predictions for the two-absorbing-barrier Gaussianincrements random walk in discrete  time are derived. This derivation, which neglects the excess over the absorbing barriers on termination, involves an implicit  limiting operation on the interval between increments to the walk, thereby approximating the discrete time random walk with a  continuous time Wiener diffusion process. Two simple procedures for correcting for the excess over the absorbing barriers are  considered, which increase the barriers by an estimate of the expected value of the excess. These corrections substantially  improve the accuracy of the predictions, as shown by the fit between theoretical and simulated distributions. One implication  of this result is that it is unlikely to be possible to distinguish between discrete and continuous sampling models on the  basis of goodness of fit indices alone. The random walk in discrete time and continuous state space has proven to be of considerable importance in the stochastic  modeling of two-choice discrimination and response time data. It was first considered in this context by Stone (1960) and  Edwards (1965), and subsequently developed in detail by Laming (1968), Link and Heath (1975; Link, 1978a, b ; Heath, 1981) and  Ashby (1983). Most theoretical work to date has emphasized the conditional mean response time and accuracy characteristics of  the process, with a number of different models emerging, distinguished by their assumptions about the form of increments  received by the walk, and constraints upon the processes characterizing different stimuli. Detailed accounts of the  relationship between different models of this class have recently been provided by Townsend and Ashby (1983) and Luce (1986).  Briefly, two classes of models may be distinguished on the basis of.how the random walk is thought to arise in the perceptual  system. In both types of model, the random walk is driven by a noisy stimulus process. In Relative Judgment Theory (RJT) models, observations sampled from a stimulus  process are compared to a sensory referent, generating a differences process, whose successive values provide the increments  to the walk (Link & Heath, 1975). These assumptions are also made by other stochastic choice models, such as the accumulator  model (Vickers, 1970, 1979; Smith & Vickers, 1988).  The purpose of this note is to derive response time distribution predictions for a random walk model with normally distributed  increments. Specifically, it will show that the derivation of response time and accuracy statistics for the model, which  involves neglecting the excess over the absorbing barrier on the terminating step, is equivalent to the approximation of the  discrete time random walk with a continuous time Wiener diffusion process. Two simple techniques for correcting for the excess  over the barrier will then be presented, which considerably improve the accuracy of the resulting approximation.  The normal-increments model is less general than the most general random walk model, in which the distribution of increments  is arbitrary, and which, for the purposes of deriving conditional mean response times, is characterized by three moment  generating function (m.g.f.) parameters per stimulus process (Townsend & Ashby, 1983). Nevertheless, the normal-increments  model is intrinsically of interest because of the natural way in which Gaussian distributions of sensory activity arise in the  psychophysics of discrimination judgments (e.g., Laming, 1985). The previous lack of interest in this model probably results  from the practice of evaluating models primarily at the level of the conditional mean response times. Under these  circumstances, some form of a priori assumptions about the relationship between the parameters of the stimulus process must be  made in order to render the model testable. One such assumption, sometimes made in RJT, is termed the 'unbiasedness'  assumption by Link and Heath, and the `symmetrical stimulus representation' assumption by Luce (1986). Roughly, this states  that the two stimulus processes are mirror images of one another. For the normal increments case, this means that the two  stimuli, S,, and SB, each give rise to a sequence of independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) Gaussian random  variables, where the variances for either sequence are equal and the means are equal but opposite in sign.  When constrained in this way, the normal-increments model predicts that mean response-conditioned times for correct responses  and errors will be equal-a prediction which has received little support from the experimental literature (although for an  important counterexample see Green, Smith, and von Gierke (1983) ). However,  ' This stricture only applies where parameters are being estimated for a single experimental condition. For each condition,  the data yield four conditional mean response times and two response probabilities, which is two fewer data points than the  number of free parameters for the unconstrained model. When parameters are being estimated for a number of experimental  conditions simultaneously, the need to impose constraints on the stimulus representation may optionally be traded off against  constraints elsewhere in the model, e.g., that response criterion values be constant across discriminability levels. The  model for which predictions are derived here is therefore less general than RJT, in that the increments are constrained to be  Gaussian, but more general than the special symmetric subcase in that the unbiasedness assumption need not be made. CORRECTING FOR EXCESS OVER THE BOUNDARY  Vickers and Smith (1985) and Luce (1986) have been critical of random walk models derived from the Wald identity, and present  simulations which show that under conditions where the step increment mean or variance is moderately large, the predictions so  derived may be highly inaccurate. One possible solution to this problem is to assume, a priori, that the decision process is  continuous in time. Under these circumstances, its appropriate representation is as a diffusion process, and the issue of  inaccuracy in the approximation does not arise. However, many interesting decision tasks, such as those investigated by  Vickers, Burt, Smith, and Brown (1985) and Busemeyer (1985b), have an inherently discrete time structure, in which case a  diffusion process representation is inappropriate. One approach to this problem, adopted by Busemeyer (1985a), is to represent  the decision process as a finite-state Markov chain, whose transition matrix is a discrete approximation to the distribution  of the step-increment random variable, Y Such an approach parallels the discrete approximation to the normal-increments  accumulator model  454 PHILIP L. SMITH  employed by Smith and Vickers (1989). While there is no theoretical limit to the accuracy which can be achieved in this way,  in practice, computational factors determine the maximum size of the transition matrix, and hence the accuracy attainable.'`  An alternative approach is to modify the approximation (5) to take account of the excess over the absorbing barrier on the  terminating step. An important implication of this result is that the response time distributions for continuous and discrete  sampling models are well-described by the same functional form. This in turn means that goodness of fit tests, performed at  the level of either the distributions or the marginal statistics, are unlikely to be sufficient in themselves to distinguish  between the two classes of model.  One possible basis for attempting to distinguish between discrete and continuous  458 PHILIP L. SMITH  sampling models is suggested by Eq. (21). Such a test would require an experimental procedure in which the positions of the  absorbing barriers remained constant while the variance of the stimulus representation was systematically altered. Equation  (21) implies that for a discrete process, the values of the absorbing barriers will be overestimated in proportion to Q. Hence  estimates which increase as a linear function of stimulus standard deviation with slope ( would provide evidence for a  discrete process, while estimates which remain constant would provide evidence of the converse. While this procedure provides  a theoretical basis for distinguishing between discrete and continuous models, it relies on the assumption that absorbing  barriers remain constant across stimulus conditions.
