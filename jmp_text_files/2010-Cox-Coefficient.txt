Weber, Shafir, and Blais (2004)  report a meta-analysis of data for animal and human decision making that uses the coefficient of variation (CV) as a measure of “risk sensitivity”. They argue that the CV, defined as the ratio of the standard deviation (SD) to the expected value (EV), is a good predictor of animal and human risk sensitivity.  Weber et al. (2004, pgs. 432–434)  review results from the analysis of animal data in  Shafir (2000)  in which the proportion of animals choosing certain (sure thing) rewards rather than risky rewards is regressed on the CV of risky rewards. For comparison,  Weber et al. (2004, pg. 434)  report results from another regression in which the proportion of sure thing choices is regressed on the EV of risky rewards. They conclude that: “…neither SD nor EV predicts risk sensitivity in isolation. Their ratio, however, in the form of the CV does so very well”.

Weber et al. (2004)  report that a large advantage of the CV over the SD in fitting data occurred with data from environments in which decision makers had to learn about the outcome distribution of different options by trial and error sampling and personal experience. Arguably, such environments might better be described as involving uncertainty rather than risk ( Knight, 1921 ). And different models of decision making might apply in environments characterized by uncertainty than in environments characterized by risk.  Weber et al. (2004, pg. 443) , however, also offered their model as a solution to the implausibility problems for expected utility theory that follow from the concavity calibration critique that applies to theories of decision  under risk  ( Rabin, 2000 ). In response, we ask whether the CV can provide either a credible normative criterion for decision making under risk or a useful descriptive model of decisions under risk in two experiments designed to test its distinctive properties. We also ask whether the CV provides a useful descriptive model of decisions in an experiment designed to test a concavity calibration supposition (with described options) similar to the one discussed by  Rabin (2000) ; this is a direct response to claims made about the CV model by Weber et al.

Consider again the second example of properties of the CV criterion in the preceding section. The reasoning takes the form: (a) if an agent weakly prefers option C to option D then (b) the CV model implies that the agent is more likely to choose option J than option K, between which the only difference is a 1/20 probability of getting 20 in option J and 74 in option K. But what if almost no one were to actually prefer option C to option D? If that were the case, then the example might not be compelling. We here report an experiment with these four options. In the experiment, the payoff numbers represent amounts of money in 2009 US dollars.

Subjects were informed that the experiment included two decision tasks and that a coin would be flipped to determine which task paid money. Subjects were first shown options C and D and asked to choose one option, by circling C or D, or to record indifference (“I don’t care”) by circling I. Subjects were told that if they circled I then the experimenter would choose option A or option B for them. After making their decisions about options C and D, subjects were shown options J and K and asked to choose one option or record indifference.

Before making any decisions, subjects were invited to inspect the coin that would be flipped to select which decision would be paid. They were shown the box containing four white balls and six red balls that would be used to determine the payoffs in option D. Before making their option J and option K decisions, subjects were shown the box containing 19 white balls and one orange ball that would be used to determine payoffs for options J and K. They were informed that, after making their choices, they would be called one at a time to observe the coin flip that would select one of their two decision pages for payoff and that they would themselves each draw one ball from the appropriate box (except for an option C choice). They were informed that they would be invited to inspect the coin and the contents of a box immediately before these “urns” were used to determine an individual’s payoff. Subject instructions and decision pages (or response forms) for experiment 1 are included in the  Appendix .

Experiment 1 included 66 volunteer subjects who were students at Georgia State University. 37 of the subjects chose option C and one chose indifference between options C and D. Recall that the CV criterion predicts that all of these 38 subjects will more likely choose option J than option K, so the majority of them should be observed to choose option J. Data reject this hypothesis. All of these 38 subjects chose option K rather than option J. (In fact, all 66 subjects in the experiment chose option K.) Therefore, the CV model does not provide an empirically valid descriptive model for decisions by subjects in this experiment. In contrast, theories of decision under risk, such as expected utility theory, dual theory of expected utility ( Yaari, 1987 ), and rank-dependent expected utility theory ( Quiggin, 1993 ), are consistent with data for all of the subjects in this experiment. These theories predict that all agents will more likely choose option K rather than option J, regardless of whether they choose option C or option D, because the theories incorporate positive monotonicity of preferences in the reward medium.

In experiment 2, subjects were asked to choose between two options or record indifference on each of six decision pages. One of the six decision pages of each subject was randomly chosen for money payoff by rolling a die in the presence of the subject. Experiment 2 instructions and decision pages (or response forms) are included in the  Appendix . The decision pages were given to each subject in an independently drawn random order. Subjects had access to all six decision pages while making each decision.

Before making their decisions, subjects were invited to inspect the die that would be rolled to select which decision would be paid. They were shown the bingo cage containing balls numbered 1,2,…,10 that would be used to determine the lottery payoffs. They were informed that, after making their choices, they would be called one at a time to observe the die roll that would select one of their six decision pages for payoff and that they would observe selection of a ball from the bingo cage if they had chosen the lottery on the selected decision page. They were informed that they would be invited to inspect the die and the contents of the bingo cage immediately before these “urns” were used to determine an individual’s payoff.

Experiments 1 and 2 were designed to test distinguishing features of the CV model. Referees have suggested that such tests can be viewed as ones “designed to make the theory fail”. Perhaps so, but this is a common characteristic of tests of other theories of decision under risk. For example, tests for Allais paradox and related phenomena are important precisely because they are designed to test implications of the most central distinguishing characteristic of expected utility theory, the independence axiom. But it is nevertheless an interesting question to ask whether data from experiments designed to test other decision theories have implications for the CV model. We conclude that the CV criterion does not provide a credible normative criterion for decision under risk because it is inconsistent with the positive monotonicity of preferences for payoffs. The CV criterion does not provide a useful descriptive model of subjects’ decision making in the risky environment of experiments 1 and 2 designed to test the criterion’s distinctive properties. In contrast, the rank-dependent theory of decision under risk, that incorporates positive monotonicity of preferences for payoffs, is consistent with data from experiments 1 and 2. Finally, the CV criterion does not provide a useful descriptive model for subjects’ decisions in an experiment designed to test a supposition used in concavity calibration propositions for prominent theories of decision under risk. This last test is a direct response to the claim that the CV criterion provides a solution to the problems for plausibility of theories of decision under risk that arise from the concavity calibration critique.
