JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 30, 426-434 (1986)  Majority Rule, Hierarchical Structures, and Democratic Totalitarianism: A Statistical Approach  SERGE GALAM  New York University and Universite Pierre et Marie Curie  An alternative model to the formation of pyramidal structures is presented in the framework of social systems. The population  is assumed to be distributed between two social orientations G and H with respective probabilities po and (1-po). Instead of  starting the hierarchy with a given H-orientation at the top and then going downwards in a deterministic way, the hierarchy is  initiated randomly at the bottom from the surrounding population. Every level is then selected from the one underneath using  the principle of majority rule. The hierarchy is thus self-oriented at the top. It is shown how such self-oriented hierarchies  are always H-oriented provided they have a minimal number of levels which is determined by the value of po. Their stability is  studied against increases in po. An ideal transition to G-selforiented hierarchies is obtained. ci? 1986 Academic Press, Inc.  I. INTRODUCTION  The complexity involved in social behavior makes quantitative modeling rather difficult. Owing to the existance of a large  number of parameters the study of single models is valuable to understand basic features of social behavior (Galam et al.,  1982). Among the rich variety of human collective phenomena political structures are of particular interest. Aimed to locate  and distribute power, political structures determine both stability and efficiency of social organizations. Mathematical  approaches have been developed in political sciences to measure the concentration of power in political systems (Brams, 1968).  One extreme case of political systems is dictatorship. There the power is concentrated at the top of a pyramidal structure and  successively divided towards the bottom (Kemeny & Snell, 1962). Hierarchicallike structures appear indeed to be very common to  social organizations (Lindblom, 1965).  In this paper we propose a new mechanism to build hierarchical structures. The novelty of the approach is to use democratic  rules to get the same concentration of power as in dictatorial systems. Considering the simplest situation of a population  distributed between two social orientations G and H with proportions po and (1 - po) we start the hierarchy from the bottom. In the present investigation we neglect fluctuations (i.e., variance) to consider a simplified model which stresses some basic  mechanics through which supporters of some social orientation are eliminated democratically from the top leadership of  hierarchies.  A rigorous analysis of the stochastic processes involved at each level of the hierarchy requires a more complex study which is  outside the scope of the present paper. DISCUSSION  A general statistical model for hierarchical structures has been presented. Both random and deterministic processes have been  combined resulting in a coherent picture of the organisation of hierarchical structures with respect to a self-orientation in  a fixed social orientation. An ideal picture of democratic totalitarianism has been obtained since majority rule is applied  together with a neutralization of other social orientations at top levels of leadership. The size of the hierarchies obtained  within our approach represents indeed a lower limit to stable social systems.  Generalization to cases with more than two social orientations presents no conceptual difficulty. It just requires a more  complicated set of equations. In addition time should be taken into account since in real social systems elections at various  levels are held only periodically and at different times. Nevertheless, the introduction of such a time dependence would not  change the main features of the model. The generality of the approach should make it applicable to systems in fields as  different as biology and economics.
