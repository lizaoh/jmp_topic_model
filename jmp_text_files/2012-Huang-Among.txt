Traditional theories in judgement and decision making are focused on algebraic properties of utility functions (cf.  Luce, 2000 ). According to the classification in  Marr (1982) , such theories represent the higher and abstract level description of the cognitive system. Due to the abstract nature of such models, it is difficult to connect them to the lower level, physiological findings associated with the human brain. In other words, the macro level theories and the micro level activities are not linked together.

However, recent attempts have been made to develop micro analysis models to explain macro behavior in judgement and decision making, based on a small number of principles of the underlying physical cognition system. The central idea of such models is that the decisions are results of the activation levels in the neural system (cf.  Gold & Shadlen, 2001, 2002  and  Gratton, Coles, Sirevaag, Erickson, & Donchin, 1988 ). So the decision process can be viewed as a dynamic accumulation of the activation of the underlying neural system. When the activation level exceeds a certain threshold, a response is triggered, and a decision is made (cf.  Luce, 1986  and  Smith, 2000 ). According to these principles, human judgement and decision making can be characterized by stochastic processes, which are referred to as sequential sampling process models. Similar to Signal Detection Theory (cf.  Green & Swets, 1966 ), these micro analysis models make the widely held assumption that the sensory and cognitive systems are inherently noisy. Therefore, sequential sampling process models often involve continuous time, continuous state diffusion processes (cf.  Smith, 2000  and  Smith & Ratcliff, 2004 ).

There are several merits of these sequential sampling process models. First, these models link the macro level decision making and the micro level activities of the cognition system. Second, these models are built upon simple components. These components are connected together to produce dynamic and complicated behaviors (cf.  Grossberg, 1988  and  Grossberg & Gutowski, 1987 ). Thus, the decision making process can be viewed as a computational model. For this reason, these models are also called cognitive processing models, and are capable of providing quantitative and real time predictions, based on simulation, or computational formulas of the underlying micro process. Third, the computational models have the potential to explain multiple empirical findings in a systematic way (cf.  Busemeyer & Diederich, 2002 ). Reviews of computational models can be found in  Busemeyer and Johnson (2004)  and  Diederich and Busemeyer (2003) .

Ratcliff and Smith (2004)  suggest two categories of sequential sampling models, depending on the manner in which evidence is accumulated. In one class, the evidence for each choice is accumulated separately and independently, and that choice is picked which reaches the threshold first. This decision rule is referred to as the absolute decision rule, and the models themselves are referred to as accumulator models. The second class of models assumes that there are interactions between evidence for the different choices, and the decision is based on the joint effect (e.g. the difference between choices reaches a threshold in a two-choice task). This class of sequential sampling models is referred to as the random walk model. Decision Field Theory models fall in the latter category. A comparative study of accumulator models and random walk models is presented in  Ratcliff and Smith (2004) . Note that both accumulator models and random walk models can often explain similar empirical findings, and mimic each other. However, depending on the task, and its context, one type of model could be more appropriate. For example, in  Usher and McClelland (2001) , a Leaky, Competing Accumulator (LCA) model is proposed to explain the time-controlled response paradigm, which can reflect the time course of information accumulation. Later in  Usher and McClelland (2004) , nonlinear, asymmetric value functions of gains and losses are incorporated into the LCA model to explain the similarity, contract and compromise effects simultaneously. Although the model of  Roe, Busemeyer, and Townsend (2001)  and the LCA model can explain these effects (similarity, contract and compromise) simultaneously, they depend on different mechanisms. In the former, a linear system with lateral inhibition is used, which allows negative activation levels. In the latter, nonlinear value functions are used, and nonnegative activation levels are always guaranteed. In the context of perception choice, the negative activation level is often undesirable due to the computational burden it presents. In such a setting, the LCA model could be more appropriate.

In this paper, we focus on a class of very successful sequential sampling process models, called Decision Field Theory (DFT; cf.  DeGroot, 1970 ,  Diederich & Busemeyer, 2003  and  Ratcliff & Smith, 2004 ). These models not only embrace the idea of sequential sampling, but also incorporate the theory of lateral inhibition (cf.  Grossberg, 1982  and  McClelland & Rumelhart, 1981 ). They are capable of providing a consistent and unified framework to explain several major empirical findings in the multiattribute, multialternative preferential choice literature, e.g., the similarity effect, the attraction effect, the compromise effect, the loss aversion effects, and also the complicated interactions among these effects. Furthermore, the dynamic nature of the model also implies strong testable predictions concerning the deliberation process and time pressure effects.

The objective of this paper is to compare alternative DFT models in the literature, and establish a unified mathematical framework for these models. This objective is similar in spirit to  Bogacz, Brown, Moehlis, Holmes, and Cohen (2006) , where a group of researchers compare a series of diffusion models for binary choice tasks. They show the precise mathematical conditions under which one model will be equivalent to another. They also explain the diffusion process models from the viewpoint of optimal decision making, and discuss the connections between diffusion models and findings in neurophysiology. Similarly,  Ratcliff and Smith (2004)  compares four diffusion process models (Wiener process, Ornstein–Uhlenbeck process, accumulator and Poisson counter model). Our study is different from the above papers in that we focus on DFT models that can accommodate multiple attributes and multiple alternatives.

The main contribution of this paper is in highlighting the connections between the different DFT models (cf.  Busemeyer & Diederich, 2002 ,  Busemeyer & Townsend, 1993 ,  Diederich, 1997  and  Roe et al., 2001 ), and explaining their mathematical similarities and differences. The underlying formulations reveal significant mathematical disparities, and leads us to present a unified framework. By viewing the alternative DFT approaches within a common framework, we are able to clarify the scope of each. First, we examine the major DFT models and discuss their strengths in explaining multichoice and multiattribute decision making. Then we use a unified mathematical framework to connect different DFT models. Finally, we show the difference between sequentially processing attributes and simultaneously processing attributes. This final contribution highlights the need to recognize that vast differences between psychological applications can be accommodated by the array of DFT models, although no one type fits all.

After a brief introduction to diffusion processes (in Section  2 ), this paper proceeds with three subsequent sections. In Section  3 , we present the mathematical underpinnings of Decision Field Theory and identify similarities and differences among alternative DFT models that have appeared in the literature (cf.  Busemeyer & Diederich, 2002 ,  Busemeyer & Townsend, 1993 ,  Diederich, 1997  and  Roe et al., 2001 ). In Section  4 , we present a mathematical model that provides a common framework for existing DFT models. In this section, we also emphasize differences between sequential and parallel processing in multiattribute decision making. Finally, we conclude the paper in Section  5 .

The basic building block of DFT models is the diffusion process (cf.  Busemeyer & Diederich, 2002 ).  Ratcliff (2001)  presents a broad survey of diffusion processes and their applications in behavioral science, such as signal detection, perception, decision making, categorization, among others. A more detailed introduction of diffusion processes in decision making can be found in  Diederich and Busemeyer (2003) . A further exposition of binary choice diffusion process models can be found in  Smith (2000) , where the decision making process is modeled via a stochastic differential equation. For rigorous mathematical development of the area, the reader can refer to  Arnold (1974) ,  Bhattacharya and Waymire (1990) ,  Durrett (1999) ,  Gardiner (1985)  and  Karlin and Talyor (1975, 1981) . The presentation of this paper is intended to help the reader categorize DFT models by their mathematical properties, and as such we restrict our summary to properties of diffusion processes.

Decision Field Theory is a dynamic preferential choice model based on cognitive and psychological principles. The very first version of this theory appeared in  Townsend and Busemeyer (1989) , which was a deterministic dynamic model. Then it was developed to explain unidimensional binary choice behavior for decision making under uncertainty in  Busemeyer and Townsend (1992, 1993) . The theory was extended to account for binary choices with multiple attributes in  Diederich (1997) . In  Roe et al. (2001) , a multialternative DFT model was proposed and explained as a connectionist network. Surveys of DFT models can be found in  Busemeyer and Diederich (2002)  and  Diederich and Busemeyer (2003) .

Busemeyer and Townsend (1993)  also provide a detailed exposition of the manner in which Model 1 is developed from deterministic Subjective Expected Utility (SEU) theory. This development has seven stages. The first stage is Deterministic Subjective Expected Utility (SEU) Theory, which is an psychology version of the famous Expected Utility Theorem. The idea is that there is a subjective weight for each event. These weights represent the amount of attention given to uncertain events, and are constant across all trials, i.e., deterministic. The valence of an action (or choice) can be represented as the weighted sum of the utilities of the payoffs of this action under uncertain events, where the weights are just the subjective weights. The second stage is the Random SEU Theory. In this stage, the weights are assumed to change across trials, i.e., they are stochastic. The subjective weights (also called attention weights) are continuous random variables. Then the valences and their differences become random variable also. The variance of the difference between two valences is introduced as a new parameter. The third stage is called Sequential SEU Theory. In stage 2, the decision in each trial comes from a sample of a random variable. In stage 3, it is assumed that, within each trial, there are many samples from the same random variable, and these samples are added up (sequentially sampled and accumulated). A new parameter threshold is introduced. For each trial, when the accumulated difference between two choices reaches the threshold, a decision is made. The fourth stage is called Random Walk SEU Theory. To explain the fact that choice probability can reverse its direction when the deliberation time becomes longer, a new parameter is added, which is the initial position of the preference state, and is explained as a bias from past experience. In stage 5, a linear system SEU Theory is developed. To explain the primacy effect and recency effect, a growth-decay rate parameter  
                         s 
                       is added. This parameter is used to represent the influence of the previous preference state. The sixth stage is called Approach-Avoidance Theory, which adds the approach-avoidance parameter  
                         c 
                       to explain the approach-avoidance conflicts. In all the first six stages, discrete time models are used. In the seventh stage Decision Field Theory, the continuous time version of the stage 6 model is derived, which is a diffusion process as defined in  (9) .

While  Busemeyer and Townsend (1993)  show how to construct the DFT model from the random walk SEU theory, their construction is just one viable way of DFT model construction. There is a subtle but key difference between the DFT models and random walk SEU theory. The random walk SEU theory restricts the noise to the weighting function, while the DFT model does not require this. In other words, the noise in the DFT model can arise from either the weighting function or other sources.

An important contribution of DFT is its ability to model multiple response modes such as selling prices and certainty equivalents. For example,  Busemeyer and Goldstein (1992)  and  Johnson and Busemeyer (2005)  use Model 1 to successfully explain preference reversals in multiple response modes. Mathematically speaking, the DFT models used in these papers are the same as Model 1.

To summarize, DFT has evolved from a one-dimensional binary choice model to a multiattribute binary choice model, and finally to a multiattribute and multichoice model. The power and success of DFT is that it can provide fairly accurate quantitative estimation and prediction of human choice behaviors, e.g., choice probability and response time. Note that the above models only describe the deliberating processes. To apply the models, stopping rules must be assigned. There are two stopping rules. One is called the fixed time stopping rule, where the deliberating process stops at a fixed time, and the choice with the largest preference will be chosen. The other is called the optional time stopping rule, where the deliberating process stops whenever the preference of any choice exceeds a threshold. For DFT models, given the stopping time, or threshold, the choice probability and response time (both moments and distribution) can be calculated via closed-form expressions under simplifying assumptions, and numerically for more general cases.

Our analysis of different DFT models shows that, mathematically speaking, there are some inconsistencies between different DFT models. In particular, the differences result from sampling events and attributes separately and jointly; and accumulation of attention sequentially and in parallel. With different assumptions on these issues, one obtains alternative mathematical models. While our attention has been focused on mathematical differences among these models, these differences influence the computation of human choice probability and response time distribution. For illustrative purposes, we present some simulations that demonstrate the differences (see  Figs. 2 and 3 ).

We further extend it to multiple attributes, and multiple choices (corresponding to Models 2 and 4), and compare the forms of sequential sampling and parallel sampling.

Simulation Examples 3 and 4  verify our conclusion that for stochastic processes with the same number of parameters and similar explanation, and similar values of parameters, the observable results could be quite different. Therefore, there is indeed a need to select the adequate stochastic process corresponding to a specific task.

For future research, there is a need to conduct human experiments to test the sequential sampling model and the parallel sampling model, and obtain data to help discriminate one from another in specific situations.  Townsend (1990)  reviewed the historical development of the researches on serial versus parallel information processing, and suggested that serial versus parallel processing could be tested by response time or accuracy data. The authors argue that mathematical models with proper empirical interpretation have the potential to solve the serial versus parallel processing dilemma, or at least demonstrate what cannot work. Later in  Townsend and Wenger (2004) , a more contemporary and comprehensive review of the serial versus parallel information processing dilemma is provided. There the problem of model mimicking is discussed, i.e., psychology models with different assumptions (like serial versus parallel processing) might be able to explain the same set of data, or may provide similar predictions. Nevertheless, there also exist cases where parallel models cannot mimic sequential models, or vice versa. After summarizing the major conceptual issues related to the serial versus parallel dilemma, the authors assert that this dilemma “can be solved using approaches that provide a tight coupling of theory and method”.

Our simulations confirm that sequential processing and parallel processing might lead to different cognitive/psychological conclusions. Moreover, they provide insights on how we should design the human experiments. For instance, one might pose choices (e.g., choose a lens during an eye examination) in which the subject has to weigh multiple attributes simultaneously, and is allowed to control their own response time (rather than the optician controlling the response time). Our simulation results suggest that when a subject has a clear preference between choices (i.e., there exists a negative correlation between

In this paper, we discuss the similarities and differences among different Decision Field Theory (DFT) models in the literature. Our study has revealed certain mathematical differences which can be overcome by the unified framework we propose in Section  4 . Through this analysis, we also emphasize the differences between sequential processing and parallel processing in multiple attribute decision making. We point out that the differences will lead to different stochastic process models, which may lead to different quantitative results. In contrast, our unified framework addresses existing DFT models within one consistent framework. To test our conclusions, we also conduct a simulation study and draw the sample paths of different models. The simulation study shows that different DFT models will incur different levels of variances in the stochastic processes. Such differences will eventually lead to different measurable metrics, e.g., choice probability, response time, etc. This further emphasizes the need for a mathematically strict and uniform DFT framework. However, our unified model is just one way to construct such a framework. There are also other ways to do so. Therefore, our future research will focus on the experimental testing of the unified DFT model, and its variants for the sequential and parallel processing.
