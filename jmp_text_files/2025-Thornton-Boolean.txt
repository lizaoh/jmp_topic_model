Nearly three decades ago, an informational application of the optimal data selection (ODS) model ( Good, 1950 ) was used by  Oaksford and Chater (1994, 1998)  to explain the systematic bias commonly observed in the Wason selection task ( Wason, 1966, 1968 ). In brief summary, the observed phenomenon is as follows. An experimenter puts down four cards showing, typically, the characters A, K, 2 and 7. The subject is asked to decide which cards need to be turned over to determine the truth of a rule which states that cards with a vowel on one side have an even number on the reverse. As it may have an odd number on its underside, the card showing A should clearly be turned over. This is typically recognized. Subjects often say, however, that the 2 card should also be turned over. This is counter-logical. The 2 does not constrain the underside character in any way. The character could be a vowel, in which case the rule is satisfied; or it could be a non-vowel, in which case the rule does not apply. The situation for the 7 is different. Here, the character on the underside is constrained. If it is a non-vowel, the rule does not apply; should it be a vowel, the rule is logically falsified. As  Oaksford & Chater  ( 2003 , p. 292) observe, ‘These two cards are more informative ... relative to the other cards’.

The tendency for subjects to select a logically irrelevant card has long been seen as significant in the study of human reasoning ( Evans, 2002 ). The failure to recognize that, in this scenario, only the A and 7 are relevant suggests that subjects must be reasoning in a way that deviates from, or extends deductive logic. In Oaskford and Chater’s (1994) model, the effect comes to be explained in terms of information gain. It is seen that there is greater uncertainty in the case of the 2 than the 7: only in the latter case is the character on the underside constrained. Turning the 2 reduces uncertainty to a greater degree for this reason. Given reduction of uncertainty has the mathematical effect of gaining information ( Shannon & Weaver, 1949 ), it becomes possible to cast subjects’ behaviour in this situation as an adaptively rational act ( Anderson, 1990 ), in which the goal involves maximization of information.

How well this account explains the evidence has been extensively debated ( Evans & Over, 1996; Laming, 1996; Oaksford & Chater, 2003 ). A substantial literature has emerged dealing with ways in which the evidence varies under different circumstances. If the problem is presented in a conceptually meaningful context, for example, it is more likely to be solved correctly. It is found that people may reason logically in a conceptually meaningful situation, even where they fail to do so in a formally equivalent puzzle ( Evans, 1998 ). The way in which the problem is represented can also make a difference. If it is presented in terms of frequencies, performance may improve ( Hoffrage et al., 2015 ). Changing the order in which the constituents are described can also modulate the effect ( Yearsley & Trueblood, 2018 ).

No attempt has been made to relate these errors to maximization of information gain, but there are reasons to think this can be done. In developing their account,  Oaksford and Chater (1994)  argued it was adaptively rational to maximize information gain, while retaining logic as the normative foundation of reasoning. It was shown that maximizing gain can provide a useful adjunct in this way. As this article shows, the argument can be taken a step further. It is possible to generalize the information-gain model in a way that captures the true/false distinction of Boolean logic. The model can then serve as a normative foundation in itself. It can provide the calculus in which both logical and probabilistic inferences are derived.

Central to this generalization is the use of signed gain. By making gains positive or negative depending on the truth/falsity of the outcome from which they derive, the model’s coverage is increased. With true/false evaluations differentiated in this way, the model can serve two purposes at once. Defining gain in this bilateral way enables both logical and probabilistic forms of inference to be accomplished by maximization.

                       An interesting feature of this adaptation is its ability to overturn the classification of some reasoning errors. The Wason card-selection error becomes normatively valid, for example This inference ceases to be an inherently fallacious deduction. But the same applies to the three errors listed above. With the generalized gain model taken as normative, the DA, AC and CF inferences all become valid deductions. They cease to be classifiable as errors.

It is important to emphasize that this is a purely theoretical demonstration. Whether the result has any explanatory value remains to be determined experimentally. There is a substantial literature dealing with inferential errors made in human reasoning (cf.  Giaretta & Cherubini, 2007 ). Any attempt to explain why errors come to be made must take at least sociological, presentational and psychological factors into account. Any account should be compared against relevant alternatives. The proposal set out here is of a different kind. It does not aim to explain why CF, DA, AC and Wason-selection errors come to be made. It aims to show that, under a generalized information-theoretic normativity, they would not be classified as errors.

The remainder of the article sets out the proposal and theoretical background in more detail. The following section (Section  2 ) presents the generalized information-gain model in formal terms. Section  3  examines its capacity to reproduce probabilistic and logical reasoning in a way that overturns the classification of DA, AC and CF error. Section  4  offers a discussion of implications, and a consideration of potential avenues for further research.

The CF (conjunction-fallacy) error can also be addressed. This features prominently in human judgement of probability. It is known that people often judge compounds (i.e., combinations) in a way that neglects probabilistic normativity. In a typical experiment, a subject might be asked if it is more likely that ‘John is a violinist’ or that ‘John is a violinist and lives in England’? Subjects with no theoretical knowledge of probability often say that the compound state is  more  likely ( Tversky & Kahneman, 1982, 1983 ). This is classified as an erroneous judgement since, according to the laws of probability, the probability of two independent outcomes is the product of their individual probabilities. The probability of the combination cannot exceed the probability of either of the constituents taken individually. This inference is named the  conjunction fallacy  accordingly. 
                            6 
                         
                         
                            6 
                            The tendency to judge compounds in this way is also known as the ‘Linda effect’, in reference to the original example in which the claim ‘Linda is a bank teller and is active in the feminist movement’ was judged more probable than ‘Linda is a bank-teller’ ( Tversky & Kahneman, 1983 , p. 297).

This tendency is also robustly evidenced in human behaviour.  Byrne and Johnson-Laird (2019)  observe that estimates of this kind are reliably observed. They note that, by grossly overestimating the probabilities of compounds, ‘naïve individuals violate the probability calculus in a fundamental way’ ( Byrne & Johnson-Laird, 2019 , p. 4). They conclude that ‘[m]ost people do not know the correct ways to estimate the probabilities of compound assertions ... Their degree of belief in the compound depends on their degrees of belief in its elements’ ( Byrne & Johnson-Laird, 2019 , p. 5).

The potential to overturn this classification of error will be apparent. Unlike probabilities, informational quantities are mathematically summative. Gains can be added together. If we suppose subjects take the probability of an outcome to depend on its evidential support, and for this to be encoded by its information gain, inferences of this kind become legitimate. The perceived probability of a combination is correctly determined by summing the gains of its constituents. Judgements seen as CF errors are valid on this basis.

Whether probability judgements reflect evidential support in this way then becomes an important question. There is some empirical confirmation that this is what occurs in human judgement ( Nelson et al., 2010 ). Experimental corroboration has been produced by  Byrne and Johnson-Laird (2019) , who show that people may equate probability with degree of evidential support; see also  Oaksford and Chater (2020) . Confirmation of a semantic kind also exists. Oaksford and Chater note that ‘the very word “probability” initially referred to the degree to which a statement was supported by the evidence at hand’ ( Oaksford & Chater, 2009 , p. 74).

The framework set out above develops Oaksford and Chater’s (1994) thesis that maximizing information gain can be considered an adaptively rational act. This was originally proposed as a way of explaining logically erroneous judgements in the Wason selection task ( Wason, 1966, 1968 ). It was argued that maximizing information gain could bias logical inference. Interpreting the mechanism as a normative framework might seem to be ruled out by the difficulty of capturing the true/false distinction in the language of information. As has been shown, it is possible to combine gradation of information with logical discrimination of truth, by connecting positive/negative gain to positive/negative states of truth. This is achieved by making information gains positive or negative depending on the truth of the outcome from which they derive. Using signed information in this way, gain maximization becomes normatively general. The mechanism can provide an inferential calculus serving both logical and probabilistic forms of reasoning.

The present proposal blends logical and probabilistic inference together at the level of the evaluative medium. The effect is intrinsically reductionist, in that inferences in logic and probability are reduced to inferences of information. But as integration of logic and probability is involved, it is important to set the proposed framework in the context of other approaches taken to this problem.

A view that has been important historically claims that logic and probability are integrated inherently. It is proposed that they are the same framework, viewed at different levels of abstraction. Probability is conceived to be a generalization of logic in which uncertainty is taken into account. This leads the probability calculus to be cast as a framework in which truth values ‘can range between 0 and 1’ ( Nilsson, 1986 , p. 72) allowing evaluations ‘intermediate between true and false’ ( Nilsson, 1986 , p. 73). On this basis, logic comes to be seen as a reduction of probability. Evans, for instance, asserts that ‘[p]robability theory ... reduces to binary logic when probabilities are set to 1 or 0’ ( Evans, 2012 , p. 6).

An idea still in common currency ( Oaksford & Chater, 2009 , p. 107–8), this is at the heart of early proposals from Benoulli, De Morgan and others (cf.  de Finetti, 1936/1995; Jeffrey, 1983; Ramsey, 1926/1990 ). It is deeply rooted in the literature. In response to comments of Pfiefer and Kleiter (2009), Oaksford and Chater ( Oaksford & Chater, 2009 , p. 107) note that, since  Carnap (1950) , it has been assumed that ‘Bayesian inductive logic  includes  classical logic’ (emphasis added).

The proposal encounters a difficulty, however. Treating probability as a generalization of logic entails identifying  true  with probability 1, and  false  with probability 0. The difficulty is that  false  and probability 0 have different meanings. Whereas  false  represents a state of certainty, probability 0 represents a state of maximum uncertainty. In one case, the outcome is conclusively eliminated; in the other it is not. 
                         7 
                      
                      
                         7 
                         This is not to suggest there are no difficulties in identifying  true  with probability 1. Pfeifer and Kleiter argue that ‘[p]robability 1 and the truth value TRUE are not the same’. ( Pfeifer & Kleiter, 2009 , p. 98).

In some cases, it is possible to ignore this difference in meaning. Whether an outcome is false, or has probability 0 may make no difference in a particular context. Where logical and probabilistic processes are combined, it is not uncommon for Boolean semantics to be violated in this and other ways without adverse effect. Consider the way Boolean variables are used in Bayes nets, for example. In Pearl’s Sprinkler example ( Pearl, 1988 ), there are independent representations for the truth and falsity of each variable. This allows probabilistic reasoning to proceed in a way that differentiates Boolean states. But as the opposition of  true  and  false  is not enforced, outcomes can be simultaneously true and false, contrary to the law of exclusion. A fundamental principle of Boolean semantics is violated. As Pearl shows, intuitively reasonable results can be obtained nevertheless.

In general, the problem remains a concern, however, and considerable efforts have been made to resolve it. Theorists have sought a way of treating logic and probability as one calculus, without obscuring the distinct meaning of  false . Strategies have been explored in which this is captured by exclusion. This is the approach used in Kolmogorov’s axiomatization of probability theory ( Kolmogorov, 1933/1950 ). Relying solely on the equation of  true  with probability 1, the meaning of  false  is defined by implication, i.e., by treating this evaluation as implied for any outcome that is not explicitly true.

This has given rise to several hybrid frameworks, including the  probabilistic logic  of  Nilsson (1986) , and the  probability logic  of  Adams (1998) . In Adams’ (1998) approach, datapoints (or areas) in a space of possibilities encode true outcomes, allowing false outcomes to be represented in terms of excluded (i.e., residual) regions.

Another version of the strategy is seen in the framework of  mental models  ( Goodwin & Johnson-Laird, 2011; Johnson-Laird, 1983; Johnson-Laird et al., 2015 ). (2015)  characterize the approach by saying that ‘mental models represent only what is true in a possibility: what is false is left implicit’ (p. 204). The general principle is again that ‘any conclusion that is not probabilistically certain can be considered to be invalid’ ( Markovits et al., 2012 , p. 1220).

Approaches of this kind do not entirely resolve the problem, however. In all cases, they continue to rely on probability as the calculational medium. The effect of an inference is assumed to involve the updating of probability. Since the distinction between falsity and absence of certainty cannot be captured in terms of probability alone, problems in which this distinction plays a role continue to pose a challenge. In the case of the mental models method, this is recognized. The consequence is seen to be that ‘neglect of falsity ... leads to systematic fallacies’ ( Johnson-Laird et al., 2015 , p. 211). Khemlani and Johnson-Laird point out that the mental models theory ‘leads to fallacious conclusions for certain inferences — those for which it is crucial to represent what is false in a possibility’ ( Khemlani & Johnson-Laird, 2017 , p. 11).

While this way of amalgamating Boolean and probabilistic evaluation may be effective in some contexts, it is inherently problematic, then. There will always be situations in which inferences become obscured. Aside from the cases noted above, contrastive evaluations also give concern. With Boolean and probabilistic representation amalgamated in this way, an outcome that is probable but false, or one that is improbable but true, cannot have any specific evaluation. As such outcomes may be salient inferentially, this is clearly undesirable.

How best to disentangle Boolean and probabilistic evaluation remains an important question, then. One area of work relevant to this is  confirmation theory . For more than a century, theorists working in this field have explored ways of quantifying the degree to which particular evidence confirms or disconfirms a given outcome ( Festa, 2012; Keynes, 1921; Milne, 2014; Nicod, 1924; Tentori et al., 2007 ). Boolean and probabilistic evaluations are kept distinct in this paradigm. Degree of confirmation is seen to be a probabilistic property, i.e., an effect obtained from probabilistic inference. Whether an outcome is confirmed or disconfirmed, on the other hand, is inherently Boolean.

While many confirmation methods have been proposed without agreement being reached, the principle of  final probability incrementality  has been established ( Fitelson, 1999 , p. 363). It is seen that confirmation can be either positive or negative, with equal amounts of oppositely signed confirmation cancelling out ( Keynes, 1921; Milne, 2014 ). By itself, the principle of final probability incrementality changes little. While it disentangles probabilistic from Boolean evaluation, it is not an inferential method. Deductions cannot be chained together as they can in logic and probability. The medium in which confirmation should be quantified also remains uncertain. This is reflected in the applied terminology. Measures of confirmational impact may be said to quantify ‘Bayesian diagnosticity’, ‘interestingness’ ( Glass, 2013 ), ‘evidential support’ ( Tentori et al., 2007 ), ‘increase of firmness’ ( Carnap, 1950 /62), ‘weight of evidence’ ( Good, 1984 ), ‘informativeness’ ( Oaksford & Chater, 2009 , p. 82), ‘degree of factual support’ ( Kemeny & Oppenheim, 1952 ), ‘confirmatory impact’ ( Crupi & Tentori, 2014 , p. 88) and ‘usefulness’ ( Nelson, 2005 ). See also  Festa and Cevolani (2017), Luk (2019) .

The case for adopting a specifically informational measure is emphasized by some, however. It is noted that psychological work on quantification of evidential impact suggests this often involves assessment of information gain.  Oaksford and Chater (2009)  emphasize the importance of Nelson’s work in establishing this connection. As they say,  Nelson ’s ( 2005 ) study ‘provides strong convergent evidence for information gain as the index that most successfully captures people’s intuitions about the relative importance of evidence’ ( Oaksford & Chater, 2009 , p. 78).

An informational measure also promises to be inferentially operational. As informational quantities are inherently epistemic — they are measurements of knowledge — they have the potential to mediate inferential processing. This capacity has been exploited in the past. It is used in  Adams (1975)  method of uncertainty summation, in  Attneave (1954)  analysis of visual perception, in  Thornton (2009)  analysis of representational efficacy, and in Quinlan’s method of decision-tree induction ( Quinlan, 1993 ). The method of  Adams and Levine (1975)  is also relevant. This uses a concept of inferential validity based on quantification of uncertainty (i.e., absence of information). Derived from  Adams (1965) , this framework focuses on  p-validity  ( Adams, 1998 ). According to this principle, an inference is valid just in case the uncertainty of the conclusion is no greater than that of the premises taken in combination.

The difficulty that remains involves the need to distinguish positive from negative support. As the standard, Shannon measure is a strictly positive quantity, this differentiation is difficult to make in terms of information alone. In response, theorists have considered use of non-standard, information measures. An example is Dempster-Schafer theory (DST) ( Dempster, 1967; Schafer, 1976 ). In this framework, evaluations are made in terms of degrees of ‘affirmation’ — broadly equivalent to degrees of confirmation. The principle is, again, that the validity of an outcome increases with its positive confirmation. But the use of a non-standard measure leads to calculational difficulties. Summing degrees of affirmation in DST requires a normalization regime, which can yield paradoxical results ( Dezert et al., 2013 ).

In the present proposal, the standard information definition is retained, allowing mathematical operations (such as summation) to be applied in the usual way. Only the medium is changed. This is generalized to accommodate the Boolean distinction between  true  and  false . Evaluations take the form of information gains, which are made positive or negative depending on the truth of the outcome from which they derive. The confirmation-theoretic principle of final probability incrementality is then upheld. Alternation between confirmation and disconfirmation derives from the Boolean status of the evidence, while cancelling-out of equal and oppositely signed quantities is ensured. At the same time, the medium becomes inferentially operational. The information gain obtained from one inference can supply the evaluation to facilitate another. Evaluations can be chained together as in logic and probability.

This framework offers a new approach to what Johnson-Laird et al. call the ‘long-standing puzzle of how logic and probability fit together’ then ( Johnson-Laird et al., 2015 , p. 201). The result is strictly theoretical, however. How much explanatory value it has is a more complex question. Human reasoning has long been assumed to utilize  both  logic and probability. Its normative foundation has been seen to combine both forms of deduction. As there are many cases where human reasoning deviates away from these formalisms, the explanatory effect is not ideal. DA, AC and CF inferences are cases in point. These have traditionally been seen as errors. If gain maximization is made the normative foundation of human reasoning, all three can be considered valid.

Can this lead to a way of explaining why these inferences are made in human reasoning? An explanation formulated in these terms would have certain advantages. As noted, logic and probability cannot be regarded as one framework, due to the disconnect between  false  and probability 0. Taking  both  to be normative for human reasoning is unparsimonious if they have the potential to be integrated. The principle of Occam’s Razor would seem to favour integration. But several difficulties stand in the way. Inferences of the DA, AC and CF kind are only observed in a statistical sense. Subjects tend to make these inferences under certain, tightly constrained conditions. These particular inferences tend only to be made by subjects  not  schooled in the mathematics of the relevant domain. Someone tutored in use of the probability calculus is less likely to produce the CF inference. Someone schooled in deductive logic is unlikely to produce a DA or AC inference. Another factor is the extreme simplicity of the inferences involved. In each case, the deduction derives one conclusion from a specific condition. This is not the way human reasoning works in day-to-day practice, where complex inferential chains are often involved.

There is a need to evaluate the model in more complex scenarios then. A greater variety of effects need to be examined. Can the model be used to explain base-rate neglect, for example? Might it be of use in accounting for the less-is-better effect ( Hsee, 1998 )? This is seen when the lesser alternative of a proposition is preferred when alternatives are evaluated separately, but not when they are evaluated together. A goal of future work will be to investigate the proposed model as a way of validating seemingly erroneous inferences in a wider range of cases.
