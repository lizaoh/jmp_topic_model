FALMAGNE1  Department of Psychology, New York University  A family of models for confusion matrices is investigated, involving the notion that the data can be explained by two factors:  a stimulus factor and a response bias. Necessary and sufficient conditions are given, and the relations with previous models  are considered (Conjoint Measurement, Quasi-independence, and the so called Fast Guess Model). A new model, the "Simply Biased  Choice Model," is proposed. An application to response data collected in a reaction time situation, in which the stimuli were  presented with constant probabilities, is described in detail. It is shown that a critical prediction of the Fast Guess Theory  must be rejected. Some indications of a "Matching Law" are obtained.  I. INTRODUCTION  We consider here a choice reaction situation in which the correct response to each stimulus has been specified in advance to  the subject. In some instances of such a situation, errors can occur even after extensive training. This may result from the  fact that the stimuli (or the responses) cannot be discriminated with perfect accuracy, from time pressure, forgetting, etc.  The basic data to be considered are the conditional frequency distributions over the classes of responses, given the various  stimuli, i.e., what is commonly called a "confusion matrix". The aims of this paper are: (i) to discuss a two-factor theory  for such data; (ii) to report and analyze, in terms of this theory, the results of a choice reaction time (CRT) experiment.  This introductory section contains a presentation of the theory and a detailed discussion of the rationale for the CRT  experiment.  A. SOME RELATED MODELS  A. Conjoint Measurement  Since in Eq. l, F is an arbitrary function, strictly monotonic in both arguments, only the ordering induced by P on the  Cartesian product R X S is relevant for the theory. A biscalable confusion matrix is thus essentially a case of conjoint  measurement (CM)? In fact, our axioms (BS,') and (BS,') are either part of, or are derivable from, all current CM theories.  There is however an interesting difference between these theories and biscalability: P induces a partial (rather than a total)  ordering on R x S. As a consequence, (BS,) cannot be derived from (BS,)-(BS,). (Clearly, a total order on R x S satisfying  (BS,)-(BS,) also satisfies (BS,).) As far as I know, axiom (BS,) is new. It is thus a candidate for future work in CM.  There is an obvious missing link between biscalability and multiplicativity, namely, a system involving the following  representation:  P(a, x) = F(u(a) v(x)).  A measurement analysis of such a system is more difficult than either biscalability or multiplicativity and will not be given  here. EXPERIMENTAL STUDY  As stated before, the main purpose of the experiment and the data analysis presented here is to check whether the Simple Fast  Guess Model or, more precisely, Eq. 4, holds for error data collected in a "speed" condition, in a situation in which perfect  accuracy can be achieved by the subject in a "no error" condition.  A. Method  Subjects. 6 male subjects, aged 20-30, were employed. They were university research workers or students.  Apparatus. This has been described previously (Falmagne, 1965). The main features will be recalled briefly. The subject faced  a grey vertical panel on which was mounted a Nixie-type cold cathode tube. The subject placed his hands on a keyboard with  three fingers of each hand (index, second finger and ring-finger) resting on keys. The numerals 1-6 could appear on the Nixie.  The delay between each response and the presentation of the next stimulus was about 360 msec.  Design. A 6 x 6 x 6 Latin square design was employed, the variables being subjects, stimulus probabilities, and finger-numeral  combinations. The finger-numeral relation was kept constant throughout the experiment, and was the same for all subjects. Left  ring-finger corresponded to numeral 1, left second finger to numeral 2, etc.  Procedure. The experiment was divided into ten experimental sessions, separated by intervals of one or two days. Each session  consisted of two series of 500 stimuli, with a five minute rest period in between. Each subject, therefore, gave 10,000  responses in the course of the whole experiment. The first session was considered a training period. The stimulus  probabilities were 0.04, 0.08, 0.13, 0.18, 0.25, 0.32, identical for all subjects. The only difference among subjects was thus  the assignment of these probabilities to the stimuli, which was governed by the Latin square design. Notice for further  reference that the same sequence of random events (stimuli) was used for all subjects, in the sense that the stimuli presented  with the same probabilities were  ERROR MATRICES AND ALL-OR-NONE REACTION TIME THEORIES 217  presented on identical trials for all subjects. The desired individual error score was 10,%'. This error score was controlled  during the experiment by the use of two opposite injunctions: "faster!" and "too many errors!", which were communicated to the  subject before each series.  B.
