All of these approaches have been applied to quantum theory. However, also outside the field of quantum theory proper, non-classical probability structures have been applied with great success, in particular in the fields of cognitive sciences ( Aerts, 2009; Aerts & Aerts, 1994; Bruza, Kitto, Nelson, & McEvoy, 2009; Busemeyer, Pothos, Franco, & Trueblood, 2011; Busemeyer, Wang, & Townsend, 2006; Gabora & Aerts, 2002 ), economy and financial models ( Choustova, 2008; Khrennikov, 2010 ), political and social sciences (see, e.g.,   Haven & Khrennikov, 2013  and  Wendt, 2015 ), and information retrieval (see, e.g.   van Rijsbergen, 2013 ). In particular, in the context of risk management, decision making, similarity judgements etc. this formalism has been applied successfully. For general overviews see e.g. the special issue of the Journal of Mathematical Psychology, edited by  Bruza, Busemeyer, and Gabora (2009); Bruza, Kitto et al. (2009) , the online article by  Atmanspacher (2015)  or the book of  Khrennikov (1999) .

Most proponents using quantum methods outside the field of quantum theory do not consider the systems in question as genuine quantum (an exception may be  Wendt, 2015 ), emphasizing, e.g., conceptual similarities between quantum physics and conscious phenomena (e.g.   Aerts & Sozzo, 2015 , and  Filk & von Müller, 2009 ). Therefore, the natural question arises, to which extent systems which can be appropriately described by a classical (or at least non-quantum) formalism can exhibit quantum-like behavior. Actually, despite the huge difference between a classical and a quantum formalism and the many no-go theorems stating that the quantum formalism cannot be reduced to a classical formalism (e.g. the famous proofs of  Gleason, 1957 ,  Jauch & Piron, 1963 ,  von Neumann, 1932 , see also  Bell, 1966 ), there are many possibilities to obtain quantum-like behavior in the context of classical physical systems (see, e.g.,   Atmanspacher, beim Graben, & Filk, 2011  and  beim Graben & Atmanspacher, 2006 ). In almost all cases the main reason for this surprising result is related to a re-definition of the structure of observables:

In classical (e.g. Newtonian) physics, any function of phase space variables (i.e. position and momentum) of particles is called an observable. It is tacitly assumed that the measurement of such an observable (e.g. by measuring the position and the momentum of an object and then calculating the corresponding observable, like energy or angular momentum) does not disturb the system or change its state. If, however, the actual measurements performed on such systems and the obtained results do not correspond to the classical observable structure (if e.g. the concept of measurement is defined in such a way that a measurement changes the state of a system—an example will be given in the last section in the context of neural networks), quantum-like behavior–or, at least, non-classical type of behavior–may occur. Note that “non-classical” is much more general than “quantum-like”: Here, non-classical refers to any violation of Kolmogorov’s axioms while quantum-like refers to probabilities which are obtained as absolute squares of scalar products in some Hilbert space of states (see Section   2.2 ).

In this article I will concentrate on two scenarios where non-classical probabilities and, in some cases, quantum-like behavior are to be expected: (1) pilot wave models and (2) neural networks. Pilot wave models are a generalization of Bohmian mechanics ( Bohm, 1952 ) (see also e.g.,   Cushing, Fine, & Goldstein, 1996 , and  Holland, 1993 ), which makes it obvious that this class of models can exhibit quantum-like behavior. Of course, this raises the question whether Bohmian mechanics is a classical theory or a quantum theory. My viewpoint is that Bohmian mechanics is a  quantum theory  in the sense that it contains Planck’s constant  
                         ħ 
                       and that it describes all quantum phenomena, however, it is based on a  classical ontology  in the sense that all degrees of freedom are objective quantities and “elements of an objective reality” independent of any observer or observation. Furthermore, Bohmian mechanics is a deterministic theory and all probabilistic predictions are the result of an incomplete knowledge and not of an ontological probability. For these reasons I consider the formalism which is used to describe Bohmian mechanics as a classical formalism.

As I have dealt with pilot wave models on other occasions ( Filk, 2012, 2015 ), I will only briefly describe the general structure and instead concentrate on an instructive example (Section   2 ), which not only explains the idea of pilot wave models but which also illustrates how non-classical probabilities can occur in the framework of classical mechanics. Section   3  will give a brief introduction to neural networks. Under certain conditions, neural networks can be considered as special cases of pilot wave models, which justifies the co-occurrence of these two approaches within one article. In Section   4 , I will take a different point of view with respect to neural networks by defining “measurements” as particular processes relating stimuli on input-neurons to the reaction at output-neurons and thus arriving at a different notion of “observable”. This new class of observables shares many features with a quantum formalism, in particular non-commutativity, non-classical probabilities, memory etc.

While Sections   3 and 4  are quite general and rather deal with general principles instead of concrete models, the final Section   5  discusses a special class of neural networks, so-called Hopfield networks, which originally were developed as models for associative memory. For these models parts of the training phase as well as the retrieval phase can be described by a quantum formalism.

The argument is that when we are dealing with a particle and both slits are open, the particle can only proceed through slit 1 OR slit 2 and, therefore, the probability for a particle to hit a particular point on the screen should be the sum of the probabilities of the corresponding probabilities if only slit 1 or slit 2 is open. We know that this is wrong in quantum mechanics and that we have to add the states first and then calculate the probabilities (as the absolute square of the resulting amplitude). The probabilistic rules obtained from this procedure–assigning vectors to states, adding the vectors, and calculating probabilities as absolute squares of scalar products of these vectors–are sometimes called “quantum probability”.

However, in arguing on the basis of an either–or situation, we make two implicit assumptions: (1) We are dealing with a particle which has a definite trajectory which passes through only one of the slits, and (2) the trajectories of this particle do not depend on whether the second slit is open or not. These assumptions lead to probability assignments which contradict quantum phenomena. Assumption (1) is violated in traditional interpretations of quantum theory and assumption (2) is violated in Bohmian mechanics.

In many cases (including the double slit experiment but also in many cognitive experiments) we assign “events” to situations and then we manipulate these situations (with AND or OR operants) which may look plausible but which are not compulsory. In several recent articles Dzhafarov and his collaborators (see e.g.   Dzhafarov, Kujala, Larsson, & Cervantes, 2016  and references therein) emphasize the notion of “contextuality by default” (similar ideas have also been applied to Quantum Theory by  Khrennikov, 2009 ). This approach requires that we have to consider all three probabilities as being obtained in different contexts and the experimental data decide whether or not we can neglect the context. This makes it obvious that essentially everything can be described in terms of “classical” probabilities. However, in some cases we observe particular regularities when comparing different contexts and these regularities can be described by “non-classical” probabilities (shifting the context dependence into the probability calculus).

The fact that in many of the experiments mentioned in the introduction one observes an astonishing agreement of the data with the predictions of a quantum formalism is the non-trivial aspect of this situation. The example of the next section is meant to illustrate the idea of pilot waves and non-classical probabilities in this context.

The following two sections elaborate on the notion of probability in neural networks. Neural networks constitute very illustrative examples for classical systems (in the sense of systems which can be built using simple mechanical or electrical devices) for which the observables can exhibit non-classical behavior. Actually, there are two types of observables which can be defined for neural networks. In this section, I will assume a more conservative viewpoint and consider the activities of the neurons (and, possibly, the weights of the connections) as the phase space (or configuration space) of the model and general functions of these activities as observables. This is in line with treating neural networks as classical dynamical systems. This definition of observables refers to an “ideal scanner situation” in the sense that all neural activities can be measured. Non-classical probabilities will enter because of the assignment of boundary conditions (in this context realized by different input patterns, which constrain the neural activities of the input nodes and thereby influence the neural activity of the whole network) to events. In the next section (Section   4 ), I will use a different set of observables which is related to “input–output relations” and which may be considered as an equivalent of “psychological experiments”.

We now define “events” as particular input-activities. We may be able to define certain probabilities for activities to occur for an event 1 and other probabilities for the activities to occur for an event 2, the probabilities for internal activities for whatever combination we take of these events are, in general, not simply given by the sum or the product of these probabilities. In a similar way, almost all the probability rules mentioned in the introduction are easily violated.

The example of the last section illustrates that the probabilities for internal activities in a neural network for certain inputs may not be (easily) related to the probabilities for internal activities for other inputs. However, this example may appear to be so trivial that one is reluctant to consider it as an example for violations of classical probabilities. It is obvious that for most systems the boundary conditions cannot be related to events for which conjunction or disjunction operants can be defined such that the dynamics of the model satisfies consistent probabilities rules.

We compared the observables considered in the last section (functions of the activities of the neurons) with an ideal scanner situation. The observables of this section rather correspond to “psychological experiments”: We present patterns, questions, or situations to a system and the reaction of the system–e.g. the answers–are the result of such a measurement or observation. Again it is obvious that there is no combination of “events” (in the sense of different input) which necessarily leads to a combination of probabilities which obey Kolmogorov’s axioms. In view of the general remarks of the previous sections it seems to be plausible to find non-Kolmogorovian probabilities in pilot wave models or neural networks  if  we define events and their combinations in the way described. However, as mentioned in the introduction, many experiments in the context of cognitive or social sciences come to the conclusion that their results find the best explanation in a mathematical framework based especially on quantum probabilities. So, why quantum?

In previous articles ( Filk, 2012, 2015 ), I have proposed a general principle, based on pilot wave models in close analogy to Bohmian mechanics, which might explain why the relation between general neuronal dynamics (which acts as a guiding field) and cognitive experience (which is considered a cognitive correlate of a special and more localized neuronal activity being the guided quantity) can have strong similarity to quantum theory. In contrast to these quite general considerations, I will discuss in this section a concrete and well-known example which, at least to a certain extent, realizes this principle. And I will describe the main features of this model in terms of the mathematical language and formalism used in quantum mechanics. These models are known as Hopfield networks or, more general, attractor networks.

I will first briefly review the principles of Hopfield networks (Section   5.1 ) and then describe the training and recovery phase of these networks in relation to the formalism of quantum theory (Section   5.2 ).

Hopfield networks were intensively investigated by John Hopfield around 1980 ( Hopfield, 1982 ) and became a very prominent class of models for associative memory (see, e.g.,   Rojas, 1996 ). From a broad point of view, associative memory is at least a dominant factor in many of the cognitive experiments where quantum-like effects have been found, in particular for various types of similarity judgements.

The idea for Hopfield networks goes back to the seminal book of Donald Hebb  The organization of behavior  which was published in 1949, where he not only proposes what became known as Hebbian learning (often summarized as “what fires together wires together”) but also a neuronal theory for association. Hebb’s idea was that a particular experience activates a certain cell assembly and the connections within this assembly are strengthened due to their simultaneous firing. When later a similar experience activates large parts of this cell assembly, it is very likely that, due to the strengthened connections, also the missing parts of this assembly are activated and thus the old memory retrieved. In this way, a certain stimulus can lead to the association of a similar experience in the past.

This example shows a close relationship between a very simple model for associative memory (the Hopfield networks) and the quantum formalism in terms of linear subspaces (or vectors) and probabilities related to scalar products or projections of these vectors onto other linear subspaces. This relationship is at present under further investigation.

Pilot wave models and neural networks are natural candidates for classical physical systems which show non-Kolmogorovian probability structures. In both cases, the definition of observable and/or the definition of “events” differs from its corresponding counterpart in classical dynamical systems. This is the main reason why these systems often can exhibit a behavior which under certain conditions resemble quantum systems. However, as the example of Chladni patterns illustrates, non-Kolmogorovian probabilities do not necessarily imply quantum probability, however, in most cases where non-Kolmogorivian probabilities are applied, the different contexts under which data have been obtained are put into the structure of the probability calculus. For a special class of neural networks, the so-called Hopfield networks, configurations can be represented as vectors in a Hilbert-space and the probabilities for retrieving certain trained pattern if a given stimulus is presented to the network involve projections of the stimulus vector onto the linear subspaces corresponding to the trained pattern. In this respect Hopfield networks have many features which are closely related to a quantum formalism.
