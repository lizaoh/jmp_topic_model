Texture-Based Methods for Analyzing Elementary Visual Substances 

Charles Chubb 

Department of Cognitive Science, Institute for Mathematical Behavioral Sciences, University of California at Irvine, Irvine California 92697-5100 

Human vision is generally assumed to embody a limited number of lowlevel, spatially parallel image transformations. These transformations define the basic properties vision can sense. Because texture discrimination tasks require rapid, spatially parallel processing, they are ideally suited to investigate these basic transformations. Here it is shown how to use paired comparison texture tasks to functionally analyze such transformations. Tasks are considered in which the observer is presented with two patches of texture, A and B, tiled with micropatterns drawn from a set 0, and is asked to judge which is greater, M(A) or M(B), for some specific texture property M (e.g., M(A) might be the intensity variance of patch A). Performance is modeled by supposing that the observer approximates M(A) and M(B) by noisy, subjective quantities, M�(A) and M�(B), synthesized from the basic transformations resident in human vision. Psychophysical methods are provided for determining the differential impact on M� of the various micropatterns in 0. Specifically, an occurrence in patch A (similarly for patch B) of a given micropattern | # 0 is assumed to contribute an independent, additive random variable X| to M�(A). The mean m(|) of X| gives the average impact on M�(A) of an occurrence of |. It is assumed that the observer judges M(A)>M(B) on a given trial if M�(A)&M�(B)+Y>0, for Y a normal random variable with mean zero and unspecified variance. Simple, efficient techniques are provided for accurately estimating the shape of the function m: 0 � R, which defines the impacts of different micropatterns on M-judgments. These methods yield results that are invariant with respect to all unmeasured model parameters. MATLAB code is supplied for data analysis. � 1999 Academic Press

1.1. Elementary Visual Substances 

This paper builds on the premise that the purpose of the initial stages of visual processing is to measure ``the amounts of various kinds of visual `substances' present

in the image'' (Adelson 6 Bergen, 1991). As Adelson 6 Bergen observe, the lowest levels of visual processing seem not to be concerned so much with locating and labeling ``things'' as with measuring ``stuff.'' 

It is submitted here that the measurement of any kind of elementary visual substance is bound to be rapid and spatially parallel in nature; any sort of basic visual stuff is concomitant to a particular rapid, spatially parallel image transformation resident in human vision. 

It is now a standard supposition that neurons are organized at various levels of visual processing into arrays corresponding to points in the visual field. The component neurons in such an array are assumed all to gauge the same spatially local visual property (the amount of some kind of visual substance), but at different locations in the visual field. Thus, a given neuron in such an array will respond only to stimulation in the restricted retinal region of its receptive field. Simultaneously, however, the other neurons in the array are assumed to be responding analogously to the light stimulating their receptive fields. At any instant, the pattern of responses produced by this spatially organized ensemble of neurons composes what has been called a ``neural image'' (Robson, 1980). 

Such hypothetical neural image arrays provide a plausible means of achieving rapid, spatially parallel image transformations of the sort needed to measure visual substances. To take an example, it has often been suggested that the ensemble of simple cells with receptive fields of a particular scale and orientation may compose a neural image array that linearly filters the retinal image (e.g., Bergen 6 Adelson, 1986; Daugman, 1987; Sutter, Beck, 6 Graham, 1989; Landy 6 Bergen, 1991; Graham, 1989; Malik 6 Perona, 1990; Heeger, 1991; Field, 1987, 1993, 1994; Gallant, Van Essen, 6 Nothdurft, 1995; Sagi, 1995; Olshausen 6 Field, 1996). The impulse response of this (crudely shift-invariant) spatial filter is assumed identical in form to the receptive field of one of the neurons in the array. Thus, the filter achieved by this array is band-pass and orientation-selective. Continuously, in parallel, the neurons in this array are assumed to produce responses that collectively compose a neural, band-pass filtered image of the retinal input. 

Neural image arrays offer the advantage of rapid, spatially parallel computation. However, they are costly to build. Each such array must have one neuron to monitor each local region of the visual field. Typically, many neurons are required. For this reason it is to be expected that few such rapid, parallel, up-front transformations are built into the human visual system. This suggests that there are probably only a few basic visual substances. 

Texture judgment tasks are uniquely well suited to analyze the spatially parallel, up-front transformations resident in human vision (i.e., the basic substances of vision) precisely because such tasks mandate spatially parallel processing by the observer. Suppose an observer is given a brief (e.g., 33 ms) display of two texture patches, A and B, and is asked to judge which is greater, M(A) or M(B), for some physical texture property M. The only way to perform this task is to make use of one or more of the rapid, spatially parallel transformations built into human vision. The display is not available long enough to admit other strategies. Thus, texture tasks isolate the basic substances of vision.

The main purpose of this paper is to describe how to use texture tasks to analyze spatially parallel, visual transformations (i.e., substance-sensing transformations). In particular, the discussion centers on paired comparison texture tasks. As shall be shown, experiments using paired comparison texture tasks are simple to perform and analyze, yet confer remarkable inferential power. 

1.2. Connections to Previous Research 

Histogram contrast analysis (originally described in Chubb, Econopouly, 6 Landy, 1994) is a psychological scaling technique. The primary difference between histogram contrast analysis and other psychological scaling methods can be summarized as follows. Standard scaling methods typically focus on understanding the processes that govern people's conscious comparisons between several objects. By contrast, the methods of histogram contrast analysis are specifically designed to understand the mechanisms by which people aggregate information from objects that are inherently too complicated to admit conscious analysis. 

All (unidimensional or multidimensional) scaling methods seek a mapping f of some set 0 of objects into some (possibly multidimensional) metric space, so that f reflects the empirically assessed psychological relationships between the objects (e.g., Torgerson, 1958; Carroll, 1972; Young, 1972; Bechtel, 1976; Ahumada 6 Null, 1993). In applications of standard scaling methods, observers make judgments directly about the objects in 0. For example, in an experiment aimed at constructing a scale of brightness, 0 might contain lights of different intensities, and the observer might be asked to judge, on a given experimental trial, which of two patches of light (drawn from 0) is more luminant. Such experiments typically yield compressive scale functions, reflecting the fact that observers can detect smaller physical differences between low intensity lights than between high intensity lights. 

In histogram contrast analysis, the objects in 0 are more properly thought of as components which can be aggregated to produce complex objects. The components in 0 are not directly compared. Rather the observer makes judgments about pairs of complex stimuli, each of which comprises a mixture of many components drawn from 0. The scaling function f: 0 � R derived from the data reflects the degree to which the judgments of the observer are differentially influenced by the different types of components available in 0. Thus, for example, in a recent application of histogram contrast analysis to study human visual intensity coding (Nam 6 Chubb, 1996), 0 comprised nine different intensities. On a given experimental trial, the observer was asked to judge which of two patches of texture (each composed of 1800 small squares whose intensities were drawn from 0) had greater total luminance. The experiment yielded a scaling function f reflecting the impact of different intensities on perceived texture luminance. In contrast to the compressive functions typically obtained using more standard, pairwise comparison techniques, the scaling function f was approximately linear over the range of intensities from 0 to 160 cd�m[2] . 

The methods discussed here are a variant of histogram contrast analysis (Chubb et al. 1994). The principal difference between the methods described in the current paper and previous developments is as follows: Chubb et al., 1994, focused on

texture segmentation tasks in which the subject attempted to judge the orientation of a texture-defined boundary; by contrast, the current methods deal with paired comparison texture judgments in which the subject is presented with two texture patches and is asked to judge which of the two has the higher level of a given property (e.g., the higher total luminance). As will be seen, this shift in experimental paradigm leads to simplifications of both model and methods. In addition, several methodological innovations are introduced whose net effect is to greatly increase the efficiency with which data can be collected and analyzed in histogram contrast analysis experiments. 

1.3. Paired Comparison Tasks in General 

In a paired comparison task, on a given trial, the observer is presented with two stimuli, A and B, and is required to judge which is greater, M(A) or M(B), for some property M. M may be a psychological property (e.g., brightness, loudness, pitch, stereoscopic depth, perceived size), or M may be physical (e.g., light intensity, air-pressure wave amplitude, air-pressure wave frequency, retinal disparity, physical size, etc.). 

In the case in which M is a psychological property, it is impossible for the experimenter to give correctness feedback because the correctness of a given response depends on the results of processes internal to the observer (and hidden from the experimenter). The experimenter can only trust that the observer's judgments (1) are stable over trials and (2) accurately reflect the subjective property M. Such experiments can be used to functionally characterize the words that name psychological properties. 

On the other hand, if M measures a physical property, then M(A) can be computed from the stimulus A itself and hence is directly available to the experimenter. In this case, the experimenter can give feedback enabling the observer to use his�her sensory apparatus to synthesize a subjective measure that is as sensitive as possible to the target property M. Such experiments are typically used to constrain computational models of human sensory systems. 

Although the methods to be described here can be used in both psychological (feedback-free) and physical (feedback-driven) paired comparison experiments, for simplicity this paper focuses on feedback-driven experiments. Accordingly, from now on, the reader may assume that M measures a physical property and hence that M(A) can be computed directly by the experimenter for any stimulus A. 

1.4. Paired Texture Comparisons and the Analysis of Basic Visual Substances 

This paper focuses on paired texture comparisons, in particular, on tasks in which the observer is briefly shown two patches, A and B, of texture composed of micropatterns (see Section 2) drawn from a given set 0 and attempts to judge which is greater, M(A) or M(B), for some physically defined texture property M. For example, provided the micropatterns of 0 differ in luminance, one could choose M(A) to be the total luminance of patch A. To take another example, for

a particular micropattern | # 0, one could choose M(A) to be the number of occurrences in A of |. 

To call M a texture property is to imply that the computation of M(A) requires the rapid aggregation of spatially local information from across the patch A. M would not be called a texture property if, for instance, M(A) depended only on the identity of the micropattern painted into the upper-leftmost corner of patch A. 

For a particular micropattern set 0 and a particular physical texture property M, how does the subject judge which of M(A) or M(B) is greater, for texture patches A and B composed of micropatterns of 0? To perform the task, the subject needs to formulate some ``mixture'' M� of basic visual substances such that high quantities of M� in a given texture tend to signal high levels of M. (Of course, there may be constraints on how basic visual substances can be ``mixed.'' Indeed, it may be that they cannot be mixed. In this case, to perform the task, one would have to select M� to be the single basic substance whose level most effectively signaled the level of M.) 

In general, it is not expected that M can be precisely emulated by a substancemixture M�. First, M� is expected to be noisy (whereas M is strictly determinate). More importantly, however, for any given type of micropattern |, occurrences of | in a texture patch need not influence the level of M� in the same way as they influence the level of M. That is, M� is expected to differ not merely randomly, but systematically from M. 

The main purpose of this paper is to show how to efficiently and accurately measure the different amounts by which M� is influenced by the different micropatterns in 0. Sections 2 and 3 elaborate the model and methods in detail. The reader who is more interested in getting a feel for what these methods are good for and how to use them may prefer to jump to Section 4, which presents a simulated experiment.

Patches. The stimulus field is now partitioned into texels: regions the size of the micropatterns in 0. For example, the reader may imagine texels to be small rectangles. Texels are to texture patches as pixels are to images. Just as a pixel can be assigned a color in an image, a texel can be assigned a micropattern in a texture patch. The stimuli to be used in the types of experiments discussed here are composed of ``patches of texture.'' Although in common usage, the word ``patch'' may refer to a connected region of any shape and�or size, again for simplicity, this word shall always be used to refer to a rectangular spatial region, comprising NRows by NCols texels, that has been densely packed with contiguous micropatterns of 0. (These assumptions are made for ease of visualization; the theory and methods to be described require neither dense packing of texels nor rectangularity of patches.) The total number of texels in any patch is N{=NRowsNCols. Any patch should be understood to have the same size and shape as any other patch. It should be noted, however, that different patches may occur together in a single stimulus in different (nonoverlapping) regions of space. The set of texels to which a patch A assigns micropatterns is called the domain of A and is denoted dom(A). For any texel { # dom(A), A({) is the micropattern of 0 assigned by A to texel {. Patches A and B with different domains are said to have the same pattern if they assign identical micropatterns to corresponding locations.

Patch measures. Any real-valued function M of the set of all patches is called a patch measure if M(A) depends only on the pattern of A (and not on A's location in space). Thus, for example, the function L that computes the total luminance L(A) of a given patch A is a patch measure. Note that L(A) aggregates information from all the texels of A and that L(A) is invariant with respect to permutations of the texels of A. By contrast, consider the patch measure H defined as follows: for a given | # 0, H(A)=1 if the micropattern assigned to the upper-leftmost texel in A is |; otherwise H(A)=0. In contrast to L(A), H(A) depends only on the upperleftmost texel of A. Moreover, H(A) depends crucially on the particular arrangement of the micropatterns in A. 

Texture judgments often implicitly involve measures M that require the summation, over the patch, of a ``spatially local'' computation. For example, in attempting to assess whether a rock face will hold your boot without slipping, you make a judgment about the space-average microstructure of the rock face. Implicitly, this judgment requires summation over space of the output of a spatially local operator that gauges, in each local neighborhood, some visual property related to the roughness of the texture. Such specifically textural judgments are epitomized by measures of the following sort (of which L is an example).

3.3. The Plausibility of the Model 

There are two primary issues to be addressed concerning the plausibility of the model. First, it is unlikely that the contributionswill actually be independent. Second, the model assumes that the impact on X|, i from different micropatterns M�(Pq) exerted by a micropattern is independent of the texel to which that micropattern has been assigned. It is more likely, however, that micropatterns at some preferred texel locations exert more influence than micropatterns at others. We discuss each of these two objections in turn. 

3.3.1. Concerning the assumption of independent micropattern contributions. Strictly speaking, the assumptions concerning M�(Pq) (and M�(Pr)) are likely to fail for the following reason: for any given micropattern |, any i=1, 2, ..., q(|), the quantity X|, i , produced by the i th occurrence of | in Pq , is likely to depend systematically on the context of the occurrence (i.e., on the constellation of surrounding micropatterns). 

To take a concrete, hypothetical example illustrating how M�(Pq) might harbor context dependencies, consider an experiment in which 0 comprises small uniform squares of different intensities and the observer's task is to judge which of Pq or Pr has higher total luminance. Plausibly, the impact exerted on perceived total patch luminance by a given texel might be some nonlinear function (e.g., a sigmoidal function) of the difference between that texel's intensity and the average of the intensities within a local neighborhood of that texel (e.g., Shapley 6 Enroth-Cugell, 1984). In this hypothetical scenario, the impact on perceived total patch luminance exerted by an occurrence of a given micropattern is not (as assumed by the model) a random variable spawned by that micropattern in isolation, but is rather a determinate function of the (randomly generated) context in which the micropattern is embedded. 

One might ask, why is this a problem? After all, what difference does it make whether the variability in the X|, i produced by different occurrences of a given micropattern | derives from the variability of contexts in the texture or from intrinsic randomness associated with each occurrence. 

The problem emerges when one reflects that the probability of obtaining a given context C in patch Pq depends on q. To take an extreme example, if the histogram q assigns micropatterns |1 to all but one (randomly chosen) texel of Pq , which receives micropattern |2 , then with probability 1, all of |1 's neighbors in Pq will be of type |1 . Hence (ignoring the differences in context for micropatterns occurring at the edge of the patch), there is no variability in the context in which the micropattern |1 will appear in Pq . On the other hand, if q is a uniform histogram (assigning equal numbers of micropatterns |1 , |2 , ..., |N0), then any context is possible for any micropattern in Pq , and (if N{ is large) all contexts are roughly equal in probability. This means that if the quantity X|1, i produced by an occurrence of |1 varies with context, then the distribution of X|1, i is likely to be very different (with much greater variance) if q is the uniform histogram than if q(|1)=N{&1 and q(|2)=1. 

The model assumes that, for any given micropattern |, the random variable X|, i generated by a texel assigned micropattern | has a distribution that does not

depend on the histogram of the texture patch in which it occurs. The preceding observations show that context effects can easily falsify this assumption. 

Nonetheless, there are good reasons to suppose that, in practice, histogramdependent changes in the distributions F| , | # 0, are negligible. First, if the target measure M is texelwise, then M is itself devoid of contextual dependency; that is, the context in which a micropattern occurs in Pq has no influence on the contribution of that micropattern to M(Pq). Although this by no means guarantees that M� will be free from contextual dependencies, it gives reason to suppose that such dependencies will not predominate in determining M�(Pq). Second, it is typical in histogram contrast analysis experiments to use histograms that differ only slightly from the uniform histogram (only up to levels yielding less than perfectly consistent performance). Thus, the distribution of available contexts differs little across different textures actually used. Together, these observations suggest that the distributions F| are likely to be approximately invariant across all conditions in a histogram contrast analysis experiment in which the target measure M is texelwise.

In measuring m (Section 3.5.2 above), in each condition, the histograms of the FH patches being compared were required to sum to the fixed base histogram h. In theory, it should be possible to measure the M� noise-injection function s using an analogous manipulation: that is, it should be possible keep the difference between the histograms constant and instead vary the sum. In practice this manipulation is rarely feasible. Simulations reveal that even large variations in s(|) across 0 are difficult to detect. Moreover, detection of such differences can only be accomplished by asking the observer to compare textures with histograms, each of which is almost completely dominated by a single type of micropattern. It is questionable whether such stimuli actually require genuine ``texture judgments'' at all. As was noted in Section 3.3, if M� is nontexelwise (i.e., if M� harbors contextual dependencies), then the basic model is likely to fail for such aberrant stimuli. Specifically, the impact function m is likely to be significantly altered for textures in which the distribution of available contexts is highly nonuniform. 

Moreover, for such stimuli, the observer may well have recourse to special, nontexture-based, strategies in making his�her judgment. For example, rather than having to rely on the spatially parallel, up-front transformations resident in human vision, the observer might be able to base his�her judgment on a quasicount of the sparse, salient micropatterns in the two patches to be compared. The potential availability of such strategies makes it doubtful that the texture-based model used to account for performance for stimuli with more evenly distributed histograms can be extended to handle performance for these degenerate stimuli. 

For these reasons, the issue of estimating the M� noise-injection function will be considered no further in this paper. The simulation in Section 4 will focus exclusively on estimation of the M� impact function.

5. FINAL REMARKS 

The rapid, spatially parallel, up-front image transformations resident in the visual system define the fundamental substances from which the visible world is constructed. Texture tasks using brief displays are ideally suited to isolate such rapid, spatially parallel transformations. 

This paper has focused on what can be learned from a single paired comparison experiment using texture patches composed of micropatterns drawn from a given set 0. Given the expense in building spatially parallel neural arrays, it is likely that human vision is equipped to sense only a small number of basic types of visual stuff. This suggests that in a task in which the subject is required to discern which of two texture patches, A or B, has the greater level of a given property, M, the subject may have to base his�her judgments on a subjective property M� that only crudely approximates M. Methods have been provided for functionally characterizing the subjective property M� used to estimate M. 

It is hoped that clever applications of these techniques may enable vision researchers to (i) compose a table of the elementary substances that human vision is equipped to sense and to (ii) determine the rules according to which the spatially parallel transformations used to sense these substances can be combined�adapted to sense other (nonbasic) sorts of stuff.
