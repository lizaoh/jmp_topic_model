The analysis of  queueing problems usually prescribes that some costs should be associated with the time people spend waiting to be served.  These are usually considered to be of an economic nature, though the existence of other costs of a psychological nature is  accepted. It is generally accepted that after having to wait for a certain amount of time, anxiety and stress start to build  up in an individual, due both to the sense of waste and the uncertainty involved in a waiting situation. This work provides a  theoretical basis for analysing this building up process as it occurs during the waiting period. It is proved that, under very  general conditions, the resulting psychological stress accumulated during the process is a marginal increasing function of the  waiting time, and some strategies to minimise it are developed. The results obtained give theoretical support to the usual  assumptions underlying the development of systems to provide "real time" information to people in waiting situations. This is  especially important in the operation of public transportation systems. INTRODUCTION  A recurrent problem in economics and engineering is the one concerned with the cost of waiting in queueing systems, which can  be described as follows. An individual requires to be served by a system which may not be immediately available to him. If  this is the case the individual will have to wait and there will be some costs associated with the waiting. These costs have  traditionally been treated, especially in the operational research context, as costs of an economic nature (Hillier &  Liebermann, 1974, Chap. 10). There are other costs, of a psychological nature, involved in the process of waiting, though  their existence has not been fully explained. There is evidence that time in idleness, and particularly waiting in idleness,  is perceived as longer than the real time (Loehlin, 1959). It has also been proved, especially in the transportation field,  that people value savings in waiting time much higher (about three times) than in in-vehicle travel time. (Bruzelius, 1979, p.  152). This seems to imply that a psychological reaction occurs when the individual faces a waiting situation. We claim that this psycholgical reaction is explained by the results that we present  next. We will postulate the existence of a subjective cost (disutility) for waiting, which the individual associates to  different levels of waiting time. During the waiting process the individual is conscious about this cost. This anticipation of  a loss is anxiety provoking and as such can be considered as an agent for psychological stress (Cofer & Appley, 1964, p. 443).  Its severity as stressor, or intensity for the building up of stress at any moment in the process will be then represented by  the conditional expected loss perceived by the individual at that particular moment. This intensity and the duration of  exposure to the situation will be responsible for the psychological stress and its resulting effects of anxiety and uneasiness  Therefore, we can see the existance of a cost of psychological nature resulting from being exposed for a certain time to a  waiting situation. We will name this cost the psychological cost of waiting, and will define it as the psychological stress  accumulated during the waiting period, where the rate of acumulation at a particular moment is represented by the severity, or  intensity, of the stressing agent at that moment.  We will prove that this stress intensity increases during the waiting process, and consequently that the psychological cost of  waiting is a marginal increasing function of waiting time. We will also prove that just before the individual is served the  stress intensity is larger than it would be if the individual knew he was just about to be served.  Finally, we will prove some results related to the value of giving the individual information during the process about the  real time he is going to be served. In particular, we will prove that any delay (or prematurity) in providing this information  is the cause of unnecessary psychological cost on the individual. Moreover, this additional psychological cost is an  increasing function of the amount of delay (or "earliness") in providing the information. In the analysis we will consider  both Bayesian and non-Bayesian behaviour of the individual during the waiting period. We will show that the pattern of results  is the same for both approaches, though the derivation of detailed results is made only for the latter. CONCLUDING REMARKS  The results we have presented give a theoretical explanation to the claims generally made in the sense that people dislike  waiting situations, particularly when uncertainty about the waiting time is involved. These claims are present in the  development of systems to provide "real time" information in many queueing situations. For example, in recent studies and  experiments aiming the development and implementation of systems to provide "real time" information to passengers waiting at  bus stops, it has been claimed, regarding the benefits that would justify such systems, that "the stress of the waiting  situation can be reduced, with a possible reduction in the perceived time, if some realistic guide to length of time required  to wait is given," (Warman, 1981, p. 150). This assumption is theoretically supported by the results we are reporting in this  work.  In other contexts the value of providing "real time" information is implicitly assumed. For example, it is assumed that people  dislike not knowing the length of the queue ahead of them in a waiting situation because it is related in some way to the time  they will have to wait. Consequently in some systems like those where individuals are served in a "first come, first served"  basis following a number assigned at arrival (for example, the meat section in a supermarket, or the shoes section in a  department store), information is constantly displayed regarding the number that is being served. This allows a customer to  know the length of the queue ahead of him and consequently have a feeling for the time he will have to wait. It is assumed  that people feel more comfortable this way.  Another example of displaying information which is not specifically the time the individual will have to wait, but could be  indirectly associated to it, is the case of elevators. In this case an illuminated (or arrowed) number gives real time  information of "how far" the elevator is, which, as in the case of "queue ahead," is associated with the time to wait. Most  people would agree that there is greater discomfort with elevators that do not provide this information. And again, the  implicit assumption here is the disliking for uncertainty about the time to wait.  As final examples of situations where the principles discussed in this work apply, we will mention the displaying of "real  time" information about flight schedules at airports or about train schedules in some rapid transit and railroad stations. The  common feature in these and the previous examples is the assumption that by merely informing the individual sometime in  advance about the time he will be served, the psychological stress and anxiety associated to waiting is reduced. The present  study provides theoretical support to this assumption and provides insights for the design of appropriate policies in this  regard.  The above comments provide some empirical backup to the theory we have  THE PSYCHOLOGICAL COST OF WAITING 101  developed in this work. Formal empirical testing, however, is recommended. An ideal test should call for the continuous  monitoring of some physiological and/or psychological stress-related reactions during a waiting situation. These reactions  should be measurable in some way, and their changes during the process should be large enough as to be detected by the  measurement instruments. In this regard we must make some considerations. First, obtaining adequate measures of the variables  describing stress is a major problem since they "either cannot be measured at all or can be measured only approximately"  (Ivancevich and Matteson, 1980, p. 52). Second, the stressing situations we are concerned with last for a few minutes or at  most for a few hours. It is unlikely, therefore, that they provoke significant stress reactions unless they are of severe  intensity (Ivancevich and Matteson, 1980, p.77). In our context a severe intensity of the stressor is represented by a high  expected disutility (as given by the anticipated level of waiting). Unless this disutility goes beyond the threshold where the  individual starts to perceive a threat to his "well-being", it is unlikely that we observe anxiety-related responses. This  would be the case, for example, when the individual may be late for an important appointment. Another example, and a more  crude situation leading to high expected disutility for waiting would be the one of an individual who finds out that his house  is burning, calls for the firemen and must wait for their arrival. In this case, the subjective cost (disutility) for waiting  is influenced by the loss due to the uncontrolled fire. The individual may perceive this disutility function H(w) as (rapidly)  increasing with the waiting time. In fact, the amount of loss from an uncontrolled fire may be assumed to be exponentially  growing with burning time (Shpilberg, 1977).  In addition to the severity in the stressing situation, we must have an appropriate setting for the waiting in the sense that  measurement could be continuously performed, and that no other stressors besides waiting had significant effects during the  experiments. Thus we should be able to isolate the changes, if any, in stress reactions due to the waiting itself.  There are several variables mediating in the process, and influencing the levels of reactions. With regard to variables  related to the individual we have, besides his disutility function which we have just mentioned, his attitude to waiting, his  beliefs about the levels of waiting in terms of his subjective probability distribution for the waiting time, and his chronic  propensity to anxiety. As for variables external to the individual we have, for example, the level of comfort in the waiting  environement. In the ideal setting for the test none of these variables should change significantly during the experiment.  Assuming we have obtained a series of measurements for a particular variable related to stress, whether physiological like  blood pressure, heart beat, etc., or psychological like an index of (instantaneous) state of anxiety as the one recommended by  Spielberger (1979), we may perform statistical tests for the changes of time in the level of those variables. In particular,  it would be of interest to compare the level of the variables before and after giving information about the time to be served.  In summary, the success of an empirical test of the nature we have described will  102 EDGAR ELiAS OSUNA  depend on how well we satisfy the following conditions: (a) the individual has a "high" disutility function (subjective cost  for waiting) at the time of the experiment; (b) there are no other stressing factors in the situation; (c) continuous  measurement can be performed on one or more stress-related variables; (d) changes in mediating variables influencing the  levels of reactions should not be significant during the experiment.  SUMMARY  In the preceding sections of this work we have tried to explain the psychological stress normally associated to a waiting  situation. We found that this psychological reaction can be described as the result of a building up process starting at the  beginning of the waiting period, and sometimes even before.  A detailed analysis of the process provided support for the hypothesis that the resulting stress could be explained by the  sense of waste the individual associates to the time spent in waiting, and by the uncertainty he may have about the time he  will still have to wait. Under these hypothesis we proved that the psychological stress the individual accumulates during his  waiting, which we view as a psychological cost for waiting, is a marginal increasing function of the time he has been waiting.  Furthermore, we also proved that the total psychological cost for the whole waiting period could be cut down by providing the  individual with real time information about when he was going to be served; some optimal policies for doing it were derived.  We found that there will always be a time during the waiting period when it would be worthwhile to inform the individual about  the time he will be served, and any delay (or prematurity) in providing him with this information would be the cause of  additional, avoidable, psychological cost. Moreover, this additional psychological cost increases as the delay (or  "earliness") in providing the information increases.  These results are seen as a theoretical explanation to the claims generally made in the sense that people dislike waiting,  particularly when there is uncertainty about the time to wait. An empirical testing, however, is recommended, and the study  provides some considerations about the desired characteristics and conditions for an appropriate test.  The basic assumptions for the study were quite general which allows for the applicability of the results in a wide variety of  queueing systems and waiting situations. The basic postulate was the existence of a subjective cost (disutility) for waiting,  that the individual associates with different levels of waiting time. We also postulated the existence of a subjective  probability distribution for the time the individual will have to wait, based on his previous experience and information about  the system. The analysis was made considering both Bayesian and nonBayesian behaviour of the individual during the waiting  process. The same pattern of results was found in both approaches, though the derivation of detailed results was restricted to  the latter.
