INTRODUCTION  Under what conditions is it possible to map objects or stimuli into real intervals so that one object's interval is completely  to the left of another object's interval on the line, if, and only if, the first object is perceived to possess less of some  specified property than the second object? This question is motivated by measurement-theoretic aspects of perceived or  measured orders when a sensory or instrumental threshold prevents the detection of "small differences" in the relevant  property between distinct objects. The operative threshold need not be uniform, i.e., it may or may not conform to the notion  of a constant just-noticeable-difference (jnd).  A general answer to the opening question is given by Fishburn (1970a) when the set of objects is countable. A similar solution  was independently derived by Mirkin (1972) for finite sets, following the earlier finite-set solution for the semiorder  specialization (constant jnd) presented by Scott and Suppes (1958) and Scott (1964).  The purpose of the present paper is to explore two forms ointerval representations for sets of arbitrary cardinality. After  describing these forms we shall discuss interval orders and semiorders, which form the basis of our analysis, and indicate the  results cited above. As noted by Luce (1956), Krantz (1967), and others, a semiorder can be viewed as a relation which characterizes a perceived or  measured ordering of elements (stimuli, bodies, alternatives) according to some property (loudness, mass, preference) when a  sensory or instrumental threshold prevents the detection of "small differences" in the relevant property between distinct  elements. The same might be said of interval orders. In the case of a semiorder the threshold is uniform (constant jnd) in the  sense of P3: if you perceive z as louder than y and y as louder than x then, since z and x are separated by at least two jnd  intervals, you will detect a difference in loudness between either z and w or x and w (possibly both). On the other hand,  interval orders allow the discriminability range to vary from element to element, as might be the case when a noise source  affects elements differently. If z is perceived to be louder than y, and y is perceived to be louder than x, and if x, y and z  are pure tones but w is impure or "fuzzy" then the subject might not detect a significant intensity between either x and w or  z and w.
