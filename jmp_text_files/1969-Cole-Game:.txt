JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY: 6, 470-486 (1969)  Shell Game: A Three-Choice, Contingent Procedure for Probability Learning'  MICHAEL COLE AND LEO KELLER  University of California, Irvine, California 92664  Five groups composed of 24 college students each were run in a three-choice probability learning experiment in which  information was given only concerning correct responses. Different groups received different assignments of reinforcement  probabilities to the three choices. Major findings of this investigation were (i) asymptotic response probability approximated  probability matching if reinforcement probabilities summed to unity, and (ii) for all reinforcement schedules, statistics of  the data were well described by the linear or n-element models of statistical learning theory using the assumption that  following incorrect responses, response probability was redistributed in a constant ratio of the probabilities of the  reinforcements associated with the remaining alternatives.  A central problem in developing a theory of behavior for situations involving uncertain outcomes is how to describe the  changes in choice probabilities resulting from each kind of trial outcome. In the two-choice probability learning experiment,  the usual procedure is to inform the subject of the correct alternative following each response (Estes and Straughan, 1954).  However, with adult subjects the same effect is obtained if the subject is only informed following correct responses, since  the symmetry of the two-choice situation leads to the natural assumption that if one response is not correct, the alternative  response must be correct (Burke, Estes, and Hellyer, 1954).  Some investigators have sought to extend the analysis of the two-choice situation to include "blank trials" where the subject  is instructed to interpret a nonreinforced outcome as a "no information trial." (Atkinson, 1956; Greeno, 1962.) In order to circumvent the symmetry of the two-choice experiment, a  three-choice situation was used.  Under the present procedure, the subject was informed of the exact trial outcome on those trials when he correctly chose the  experimenter-defined correct alternative. However, when the subject chose incorrectly no information was given concerning  which of the two remaining alternatives was correct. We have termed this a "threechoice, contingent" procedure because each  trial outcome is determined jointly by the experimenter-determined probability of a given alternative being called correct  (7ri) and the subject's response (Ai).  The three-choice contingent situation raises several interesting empirical questions. With respect to the mean learning curves  we would like to know what relation obtains between the response probabilities and the probabilities that the events actually  occur. A related problem is to determine the way in which the choice probability is affected by different relations among the  7T values for the various alternatives.  Analysis of the sequential data should provide evidence concerning the effects of various types of trial outcomes. Of  particular interest in this regard are the following questions: (i) What is the effect of an incorrect response ? Does it  leave response probabilities unchanged, and if not, how are these probabilities redistributed among the remaining alternatives  ? (ii) Does the effect of an incorrect (or correct) response depend on rr ? (iii) What are the effects of runs of correct and  incorrect responses ? In the cases of runs of correct responses, we have a new situation in which to examine the recency  function (Jarvik, 1951) for runs of a particular event. The nature of our experiment also permits another type of "recency"  function, e.g., the determination of response probabilities following various runs of incorrect responses.  The theoretical task will be to test various assumptions about the effects of the different kinds of trial outcomes and the  way in which choice behavior changes as a function of practice.  None of the three-choice contingent experiments so far reported (Detambel, 1955; Gardner, 1957; Neimark, 1956) provide  sufficient data to answer the empirical and theoretical questions posed above. In addition to lack of information about the  distribution of response probabilities across the two less frequently reinforced alternatives, the range of 7T values  investigated has been limited, and the series of trials typically short. Furthermore, no sequential aspects of the data have  been reported. If no  response occurred in the required 3 sets, a tone sounded, indicating to the subject that he must respond more quickly. One means of representing the three-choice, noncontingent experiment is to assume that responses become  associated with whole patterns of stimulation provided by the experimental situation. This is the representation provided by  the N-element pattern model (Atkinson and Estes, 1963). By making changes in the assumptions of the model which render it  appropriate for application to the present experiment, we arrive at the following set of axioms:  SAMPLING AXIOMS  S1. Exactly one pattern (element) is sampled on each trial.  S2. Given the set of N patterns available for sampling on a trial, the probability of sampling a given pattern is 1 /N,  independent of the trial number and the preceding events.  480 COLE AND KELLER  RESPONSE Axiom  R1. On any trial that response is made to which the sampled pattern is conditioned.  CONDITIONING Axioms  Cl. On every trial each pattern is conditioned to exactly one response. At the beginning of the experiment, the probability  that a pattern is conditioned to any response is 1 /r, where r is the number of alternatives.  C2. If an element conditioned to Ai is sampled and Ai is reinforced, the element remains conditioned to Ai .  C3a. If an element conditioned to Ai is sampled and Ai is incorrect, it becomes conditioned to another response with  probability c.  C3b. If response Ai is incorrect and counter-conditioning is effective, then the element becomes conditioned to the remaining  response alternatives in the ratio of their 7T values; i.e., if Ai is incorrect, the sampled element becomes conditioned to A;  with probability c7r;/('r; + Trx) and to Ak with probability cvkl ('r; + ak).  C4. Patterns that are not sampled on a trial do not change their conditioning state on that trial.  C5. The probability, c, that a sampled pattern will be conditioned to a reinforced response is independent of the trial number  and the preceding events.  The conditioning assumptions are all straightforward and follow the original pattern model except for C3b which is specific to  the present situation. The trial outcome in question occurs when a response is made, is incorrect, and conditioning occurs.  Since there are two other possible responses, we need to specify how the conditioning is divided between them. We assumed a  relative frequency hypothesis which states that conditioning would occur to the other two responses in proportion to the  probabilities that those responses would themselves lead to a correct outcome. This process is only approximate in the early  trials when the subject knows little about the relative frequency of the events but should improve over trials as the subject  "learns" the Tr values associated with the various alternatives. This is admittedly an ad hoc assumption, but it proved  successful whereas several other plausible alternatives did not. In addition to the linear and pattern models, various members of the family of scanning models (Bower, 1959; Cole, 1965;  Estes, 1960) were applied to the data from the present experiment. These "two-process" models have the virtue of predicting  overshooting, such as we observed in Group 2, at the cost of introducing an extra parameter. The set of models studied  involved various assumptions concerning orienting and approaching as given in Bower, (1959). The technique used was to  generate Monte Carlo data with a wide range of parameter values and compare the resulting features with the actual data.  However, none of the two-process models we applied to the data provided adequate fits to the sequential data. Invariably the  trial-to-trial changes in response probability predicted by reasonable choices of parameters were too small (a difficulty also  encountered by Cole, 1965). Despite their apparent promise, additional work on the two-process models is required to resolve  these difficulties.  DISCUSSION  In general, both the pattern and linear models, incorporating a special assumption about nonreinforced trials, provide a  respectable fit to these data. Nevertheless,  THREE-CHOICE PROBABILITY LEARNING 485  these models encounter difficulties. First, these models, employing the particular weighting assumptions used, cannot predict  the overshooting which is observed mainly in Group 2. Although alternative weighting assumptions may be found which more  closely approximate the observed terminal levels, the variance in the data is sufficient to make it worthwhile to replicate  this overshooting before revising the model, especially in view of the successful predictions for the other groups in the  experiment. A second difficulty involves the lack of invariance of parameters across groups which was evident for both the  learning curves and the sequential statistics. A third difficulty was that the parameters estimated from the learning curves  were far smaller than those which best describe the sequential statistics; this has been a recurring point of difficulty with  the linear and N-element models (for instance, Friedman et al., 1964). The only group not encountering this difficulty is  Group 5, where a B of .003 provides the best fit to both the learning curves and the sequential data.  The recency curves based on correct and incorrect responses instead of the usual noncontingent events represent an interesting  and perplexing result for theoretical interpretation. If the present results are replicable, it seems that both "positive" and  "negative recency" are both simultaneously present in this three-choice contingent situation. There is "positive recency"  since the probability of repeating a response increases following repeated reinforced occurrences of that response. There may  also be "negative recency" since the probability of repeating an incorrect response increases following increasingly long runs  of nonreinforcement of that response. At present, we tend to attribute this apparent paradox to biases produced by subject  The major theoretical question pursued in this study was to determine how response probabilities are redistributed on trials  when an incorrect prediction response occurs. Despite its deficiencies, the reasonable fit provided by the linear and pattern  models lend support to our assumption about nonreinforced trials: reinforcement redistributes probability in a constant ratio  of the probabilities of the reinforcements associated with the remaining alternatives.
