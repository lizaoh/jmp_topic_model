INTRODUCTION  Many sorts of psychological data reflect responses to pairs of stimuli. Common  examples are ratings of the similarity of one stimulus to another, and probabilities with  which one stimulus is chosen over another. To represent such data, let X be a finite set  with elements x, y, etc., and let p be a real-valued function on X x X. Cobmbs (1964)  and Shepard (1972a) divide these sorts of data into two classes: proximity data, which  are supposed to be symmetric in the sense that p(xy) = p(yx); and dominance data,  which are supposed to be antisymmetric in the sense that p(xy) is a strictly decreasing  function of p(yx). For instance, similarity ratings are proximity data because usually  instructions enforce symmetry, while choice probabilities are dominance data because  usually p(xy) = 1 - p(yx).  There are important types of pairwise data, however, that do not satisfy either symmetry  or antisymmetry. For instance, although confusion probabilities are generally treated as  proximity data, they are rarely exactly symmetric, since usually the probabilities of  identifying x as y and y as x are not exactly the same. Similarly, time order errors may  cause violations of antisymmetry in psychophysical choice probabilities. These deviations  from the ideal have been treated in two different ways in the literature.  One possibility, which has been pursued mainly with proximity data, is to consider the  symmetric and asymmetric aspects of the data to be inseparable parts of the same  fundamental process. For instance, the multidimensional unfolding model of Coombs  (I 964) generalizes the usual multidimensional scaling model to associate each stimulus x  with two points Xand xin a metric space, such that p(xy) is strictly increasing on the  1 This work was supported by a Visiting Research Fellowship at Educational Testing Service, by  Grants GB-42218 from the National Science Foundation and 1 R03 MH 29174-01 from the  National Institute of Mental Health, and by computer time from the UCLA Office of Academic  Computing. Tversky (1977) has discussed models that  express p(xy) as a function of sets of attributes attached to x and y; these models predict  symmetry only in special cases. Cunningham (1978) and Harshman (1978) have introduced  asymmetric models based on free trees and factor analysis, respectively.  The other possibility is to consider the symmetric and antisymmetric aspects of the data  to reflect different processes that can be distinguished by appropriate analysis. The  simplest example of this approach is the common tendency to estimate symmetric  distances as the mean of p(xy) and p(yx), or antisymmetric choice probabilities as the  mean of p(xy) and 1 - p(yx). This procedure thus attributes any deviations from  symmetry or antisymmetry, respectively, to random error. When the p(xy) are proba-  bilities, signal detection theory (Green & Swets, 1966) and Lute (1959, 1963) choice  theory propose a more sophisticated analysis. These models include both stimulus  parameters, which are symmetric when the p(xy) are confusion probabilities and anti-  symmetric when the p(xy) are preference probabilities, and also bias parameters, which  account for deviations from symmetry or antisymmetry. For proximity data, Bishop,  Fienberg, and Holland (1975, Chap. 8) and Tobler (1979) d escribe models that predict the  asymmetries in p(xy) from two functions, one on x and the other on y, that may or may  not be assumed identical to each other. Constantine and Gower (1978) Gower (1977),  and Tobler (1976) describe even more complex models that include multidimensional  representations for the asymmetric as well as the symmetric parts of the data.  The present paper follows this second approach, but replaces the specific functional  assumptions of earlier models with more general assumptions of monotonicity. A series of  models will be explored, which represent the data as a monotonic combination of a  symmetric function on pairs of stimuli, and a bias function on individual stimuli. The  models make no prior assumptions about the symmetric function; but all the models  assume that the bias function is one-dimensional, and they impose increasingly strong  additional conditions on the bias function. Some of these conditions are natural generaliza-  tions of assumptions that have already been investigated in models that use a one-  dimensional utility function to describe purely antisymmetric dominance data. THE SCALING PROGRAM  A computer program has been written to estimate from data the symmetric and bias  functions specified by each of the monotonic models in turn. The entries on the diagonal  of the data matrix, such as p(xx), can be optionally either included or ignored in the  analysis. Ties in the data values are not counted, and ties in the scale values are not allowed.  Since the weak bias and scalable bias models reduce to the weak utility and simple  scalability models, respectively, when the data are antisymmetric, the program can also  be used to fit the latter models to dominance data.  The program seeks ordinal scale values that minimize the number of violations of each  model. The minimization uses a version of the iterative algorithm described by Slater  (1961), for the weak utility model and by Holman (1978) for multidimensional scaling  with a monotonic distance function. At each iteration, the computer determines the  change in number of violations that would occur if each stimulus (for the bias function),  or each pair of stimuli (for the symmetric function), were moved to each other ordinal  position on the scale, holding all the other scale values constant; then it ranks the  moves according to the quotient of the reduction in number of violations divided by the  number of ordinal positions moved; and finally it makes the highest-ranking moves until  the total number of positions moved reaches a predetermined limit, called the step size.  If an iteration fails to reduce the number of violations, the previous scale values are  retained and the step size is reduced. Iterations continue until either no moves are  found that reduce the number of violations, or a predetermined number of iterations  is reached, or the step size is reduced below 1.  This procedure shares with other iterative algorithms the problem that it may converge  to a local rather than a global minimum in number of violations. As the program was  being developed, however, it was repeatedly tested on the same sets of data with different  starting configurations and slightly different version of the algorithm. Although the final  configurations were not always the same, the differences were small, both in scale values  and in number of violations.  For the weak bias model, the program estimates a bias function b that minimizes  violations of (1). Two starting configurations are used, in order to increase the likelihood of  finding a global minimum. They correlated the different symmetric  scales derived from an olfactory confusion matrix with a symmetric matrix of similarity  ratings for the same stimuli; the correlation was highest for the row bias model. This  technique has the limitation of requiring independent symmetric matrices for the same  stimuli.  Another method can be used to choose between the models based only upon their own  symmetric scales. The symmetric scales can be fit to a model for symmetric proximities,  such as a multidimensional space or a system of clusters. If one scale shows a superior fit,?12 ERIC W. A similar  independence of bias from symmetric configuration was found by Jones et al. (1978) in  olfactory confusions.  In conclusion, the present results suggest that asymmetries in proximity data are not  just an embarrassment to be ignored or discarded. Instead, asymmetries can be sum-  marized by a one-dimensional scale that is both reliable and interpretable. This dimension  is mathematically independent of the structure of the symmetric part of the data.
