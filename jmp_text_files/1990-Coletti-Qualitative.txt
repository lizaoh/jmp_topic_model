INTRODUCTION  At the basis of probability theory, de Finetti (1930) puts the so called "Principle of coherence," thereby identifying the  quanitative theory of subjective probability with the theory of coherent previsions.  The profound meaning of the coherence condition (see Definition 3) is emphasized by de Finetti's words, "a prevision-function  p is coherent if its use cannot lead to an inadmissible decision (i.e., a decision such that a different possible one would  have certainly led to better results, whatever happened)" (de Finetti, 1975).  Moreover, such a way of formulating probability theory provides a more general axiomatic system than that of Kolmogorov; in  fact, it coincides with Kolmogorov's system when the family of events is an algebra and countable additivity holds. Yet it  also allows us to take into account families of events which are not closed with respect to Boolean operations and, most of  all, we need not fix a priori, once and for all, the family of events.  The extension theorem relative to coherent previsions allows us to enrich the family of events painlessly at any moment, as  needed. Note that in a qualitative framework, it is important to consider as distinct from the impossible event all the possible ones;  also those which, due to the "inadequacy" of the set of real numbers, must be represented by zero probability.  For this qualitative probability, axioms of coherence, positive coherence, and strong coherence are introduced and extension  theorems, as in the quantitative case, are proved.  The qualitative theory gives the possibility of expressing all that can legitimately be said by arguing solely in terms of  relations between events about which we have a precisely stated opinion. In fact the extension theorems permit us to proceed  to put all the events we wish (one after the other) in relation, preserving coherence. When an infinite number of events are  considered the conditions of coherence still refer to finite subsets.  The qualitative conditions of coherence are necessary and sufficient for the representation of a qualitative probability by a  coherent numerical probability (in the sense of de Finetti ).  In particular, if the family of events is a Boolean algebra and the relation ~ is connected, our coherence condition is  equivalent to Wakker's condition (Wakker, 1981), while the strong coherence condition is equivalent to Scott's condition  (Scott, 1964), for finite sets of events.  The condition of coherence does not ensure the existence of a strictly positive probability which realizes ;~, not even on a  finite set of events (see Example 1 in Section 3). To obtain this result, we introduce the condition of positive coherence,  which is intermediate between coherence and strong coherence. It turns out that positive coherence is a necessary and  sufficient condition for the existence of strictly positive coherent probability which almost realizes :~ on any finite set of  events.  Positive coherence is also a sufficient condition for the existence of a coherent conditional probability (in the sense of de  Finetti (1975), Holzer (1985), and Regazzini (1983)) which almost realizes ~, in a possibly infinite set of events.  The idea of using conditional probability rests essentially on the following remark. Since R is Archimedean, any (real)  probability representing a qualitative probability on a "sufficiently rich" set of events will necessarily assign value zero  to most events: thus, in the passage from order to numbers, a great deal of information is lost. But conditional probability  is a more accurate tool for distinguishing among different "degree of belief" when they are given a null numerical probability.  Example 3 in Section 3 shows that the existence of a probability which almost realizes the qualitative probability { does not  secure the existence of a conditional probability which almost realizes :~. Moreover, this latter condition does not imply  positive coherence, as Example 2 in Section 3 shows.  COHERENT QUALITATIVE PROBABILITY 299  2.
