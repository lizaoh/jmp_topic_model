At one end of this continuum is the kernel solution. A new solution, called the competitive  bargaining set, constitutes the other extreme. It incorporates the concept of a multiobjection, which is based on the idea  that threats may be perceived and considered simultaneously even though their simultaneous implementation is impossible.  For the simple homogeneous majority games [n - 2, 1, 1,..., 1] the competitive bargaining set (1) yields unique outcomes for  each coalition structure, (2) coincides with von Neumann and Morgenstern's main simple solution for certain coalition  structures, and (3) converges to Shapley value as n - cc when the grand coalition forms.  Mathematical and experimental implications of the new solution are discussed briefly. DISCUSSION  An analysis of the assumptions underlying the bargaining set and the kernel has led to the concept of multiobjection, which  captures the essence of the power gap between the Apex and Base positions, i.e., the Apex's ability to multiobject vs the  Base's limitation to a single objection. The resulting competitive bargaining set yields a unique solution for apex games  located at one extreme of the continuum of solutions prescribed by the bargaining set while the other extreme point is the  kernel solution.  Within the continuum of solutions prescribed by the bargaining set the actual disbursement of payoffs for a given coalition  may depend on the game environment. The game environment consists of some ill defined variables which are psychologically  important though presently not incorporated in the game theoretic formulation. Included among these variables are the  determinateness to form a coalition, the degree of desired stability, and the degree of competitiveness. All of these  variables, which cannot be assumed to be mutually independent, may be expected to vary from one player to another and to  depend upon the structure of the characteristic function and the type of communication allowed. Extending Davis and Maschler's  argument we suspect that as the degree of determinateness to form coalitions gets stronger, the degree of competitiveness is  reduced, or the stability requirements are relaxed, the results will approach the kernel's predictions. And conversely, as the  players become  COOPERATIVE n-PERSON GAMES 285  more competitive or there is a desire for stronger stability the outcomes of the bargaining set will approach the p.c.'s  prescribed by the competitive bargaining set.  The above hypothesis regarding the bargaining set and its two extremes is consistent with findings in several coalition  formation experiments. Outcomes in experiments related to apex and other n-person games when n = 4 (Maschler, 1965) and n ---  5 (Selten and Schuster, 1968, 1970; Maschler's Jerusalem Conference gamme4) were distributed over the entire set of solutions  comprising M(i) with an average very close to its middle. Other outcomes for n = 4 and n = 5 (Horowitz and Rapoport, 1972)  were distributed mainly in one half of M('), with an average very close to Hl". The discrepancy between the central tendencies  of the final outcomes of these studies may be very likely attributed to the differences among the studies in the form of  communication. Whereas Maschler as well as Selten and Schuster allowed free, face-to-face negotiations, the  computer-controlled study of Horowitz and Rapoport restricted the communication to a small prescribed set of formal messages  without allowing players to see or hear one another. Face-to-face contact allows the communication of intentions, gestures,  and emotions, thus, probably enhancing the salience of social norms of equity, decreasing competitiveness, and increasing the  determinateness to form a coalition. The lack of personal contact, on the other hand, facilitates competitive tendencies and  strengthens the desire for a stronger stability.  It is revealing to compare the competitive bargaining set Hl to von Neumann and Morgenstern's main simple solution. Although  both models have been shown to yield the same payoff vectors, they differ from each other in two ways. First, while the  competitive bargaining set yields for each winning coalition in the apex game a unique payoff vector, the main simple solution  is only one of a large number of solutions, each of which contains an infinite number of imputations. Besides considerations  of simplicity, there is no apparent psychological justification for the special status of the main simple solution. A second,  not less important, distinction between the two theories concerns the development of a dynamic theory for the bargaining  process. Stearns (1967) and Horowitz and Rapoport (1972) showed convergence schemes leading to the bargaining sets. These  dynamic interpretations of the bargaining set models can be tested by a proper analysis of the bargaining process. No such  analvsis seems feasible for the static theory of von Neumann and Morgenstern.  Outstanding problems subject to future investigations are of two types: mathematical and experimental. From the mathematical  point of view the first problem is that of existence.
