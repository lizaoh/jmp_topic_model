A series of papers by Falmagne and his colleagues have developed a class of models describing the evolution of preferences or choices as a continuous time stochastic process, namely a real-time random walk on a set of states which are typically order relations of some kind ( Doignon & Falmagne, 1997; Falmagne, 1996, 1997; Falmagne & Doignon, 1997; Falmagne & Ovchinnikov, 2002; Falmagne, Regenwetter, & Grofman, 1997; Regenwetter, 1997 ). We refer to the models in this collection as  random walk models  in line with the use of the term in the literature on random walks on graphs or hyperplane arrangements (e.g.  Aldous & Fill, 2004; Billera, Brown, & Diaconis, 1999; Diaconis & Brown, 1998; Woess, 2000 ).  Regenwetter, Falmagne, and Grofman (1999)  have successfully applied a special case of such models to attitudinal panel data from the 1992 US presidential election.  Böckenholt (2002)  gave a good statistical account of the same data using a Thurstonian random utility model (as well as some nested submodels). We refer to the models in Böckenholt's collection as  Thurstonian models . Both classes of models are real-time stochastic processes. The key difference is that the “random walk models” concern a random walk on a discrete set of states (with the transitions occurring in real time), while the “Thurstonian models” describe the evolution in terms of multivariate normal random utilities that are indexed by time.

The idea of representing the attitudes of individuals with respect to a set of alternatives—such as candidates in an election—by order relations is common in choice theory. In the random walk models, a transition between two neighboring states (thus, from one order relation to another) is attributed to the occurrence of some unobservable ‘token’ of information delivered randomly by the environment in the form of advertisements, conversations, news items, to name a few possibilities. Such tokens are in principle unobservable because it is practically infeasible to track any given individual over an extended period of time and record every piece of information that s/he has been exposed to. Even if we could overcome this hurdle, it would remain unclear how we could possibly ascertain the effect of any given piece of information on the respondent, once we have noted that the respondent has been exposed to it. For example, two individuals may be affected differently by the same TV spot.

Our solution to this puzzle consists in modelling the flow of information that a particular population of respondents is exposed to by an implicit stochastic process of pointwise token-events, whose parameters must be estimated from the empirical data. The data typically take the form of the respondents’ stated preferences at two or more time points. The models are testable because their hypotheses about the evolution of preferences are specific and the relevant attitudinal panel data are quite rich.

An elaborate empirical investigation along these lines ( Regenwetter et al., 1999 ) has focused on the preferences of potential voters in the 1992 presidential election in which three major candidates were opposing each other, namely George Bush, Bill Clinton and Ross Perot. In that analysis, preferences were modeled as (strict) weak orders on the set of candidates: Bush, Clinton, Perot. (A strict weak order is a ranking with possible ties. We drop ‘strict’ from now on.) In the cited paper, the researchers applied a particular random walk model for weak orders to 1992 National Election Study (NES) panel data ( Miller, Kinder, Rosenstone, & NES , 1993 ). The fit of the stochastic model to these data was quite satisfactory (yielding nonsignificant chi-squares). Nevertheless, a close look at the fit (and recent attempts to replicate such analyses on other data) reveals a systematic and interesting deficiency of the weak-order random walk model, namely that it underestimates the number of respondents expressing identical weak orders at two distinct time points. A close look at Böckenholt's fit of his Thurstonian model to the same 1992 data reveals that his model suffers from the same deficiency. This motivates the new developments presented here. We provide a generalization of the weak-order random walk model which explains this phenomenon by assuming that a potential voter may, occasionally, ‘tune out’ of the campaign and become insensitive to the events in the campaign. At such times, the voter's (random walk) state is frozen, and it remains frozen until the occurrence of specific event that induces the voter to ‘tune in’ to the campaign again. We also test this new model on the same 1992 NES panel data that have been used in the previous work ( Böckenholt, 2002; Regenwetter et al., 1999 ).

The core notion of this paper—the collection of weak orders with the possible transitions between them—is a special case of the concept of a medium. A  medium  is a certain kind of semigroup of transformations on an appropriately chosen set of states, constrained by strong axioms (cf.  Falmagne, 1997; Falmagne & Ovchinnikov, 2002 ). There are many examples of media, both in everyday life and in mathematics. A simple intuitive example is offered by a jigsaw puzzle. Here, the states of the medium are all the partial solutions of the puzzle in which the pieces are interconnected (i.e., there are no loose pieces). Note that the ‘empty puzzle’ and the completed puzzle are also regarded as states. The transformations consist in either attaching a piece to the current state or in removing a piece from the current state (with the constraint that the remaining pieces must remain interconnected), forming thus a new state in all cases (see  Falmagne, 1997  or  Falmagne & Ovchinnikov, 2002  for details on that semigroup).

We begin by reviewing the main concepts of the weak-order random walk model used by  Falmagne et al. (1997)  and  Regenwetter et al. (1999) . The weak-order random walk model uses a medium on the collection of weak orders over a finite set. We refer the reader to the original papers for more detailed motivation and explanations. The model assumes that the current opinion of each respondent in an opinion poll can be appropriately represented by a weak order, that is, by a ranking of the three candidates, with possible ties. As indicated in our introductory paragraph,  Regenwetter et al. (1999)  have successfully applied the weak-order random walk model to 1992 National Election Study panel data ( Miller et al., 1993 ), which are based on the responses of the individuals in a panel with two successive polls. Not only did the model fit well, but it was also able to account for interesting features in the data. For instance, on the average, the amount of information flow (which is quantified as the number of “tokens” in the model) concerning Perot was estimated to be far greater than that for the two other candidates, suggesting that the opinions regarding Perot were very volatile during the campaign. This mirrors the general consensus about the 1992 campaign in the political science literature, that used many different sources of data and methods of analysis.

However, a closer examination of the fit also indicates that, in some cases, the best-fitting predictions systematically underestimate the number of respondents whose responses at the two polls yield identical weak orders. In other words, the model systematically underestimates the frequencies on the diagonal of the data matrix. (By  data matrix , we mean the contingency table consisting of the joint frequencies of all possible weak orders at the two time points.) To take care of the deficiency, we introduce in the following section a generalization of the weak-order random walk model.

Our discussion above suggests that at least some potential voters are more consistent across successive polls than what either the weak-order random walk model or the Thurstonian model predict. To account for this effect, which is slight, it seems reasonable to search for a minor alteration of the weak-order random walk model, rather than for an alternative to it.

A tempting notion is that some individuals may become momentarily impervious to all matters related to the campaign. At some point, they may ‘tune out,’ either for some personal reason or as a consequence of some external event related to the campaign. For example, they may be dismayed by its negative tone and by the personal attacks on the candidates. When this happens, we assume that their opinions of the candidates become temporarily ‘frozen.’ The appeal of this idea lies in the fact that it is capable of implementation in the general framework of the weak-order random walk model. As a first result of our analysis, note that the data from two time points are not independent, based on a test of (quasi-)independence performed separately for each of the three samples of constituents. The TIO model is custom designed to accommodate this observation.

Note in passing that the fact that we set the Poisson rates equal across constituencies does not mean that the respondents receive the same tokens. Rather, the make-up of the token stream is reflected by the token probabilities, which we do not set equal between constituencies. Following a suggestion of our Associate Editor, we briefly consider another extension of the weak-order model, that could accommodate the lack of movement very naturally. In the literature, some researchers have studied so-called “Mover–Stayer” versions of Markov chains to model heterogeneous populations (see, for example,  Blumen, Kogan, & McCarthy, 1955; Goodman, 1961; Spilerman, 1972 ). In the context of the current study, this model is tantamount to saying that some respondents might have fixed attitudes and never move. That is, they never engage in transitions between preference states during the campaign, or in other words, they do not participate in the stochastic processes we have outlined above. The “stayers” in a Mover–Stayer model should be among those whose ratings of Bush, Clinton and Perot across different polls correspond to identical weak orders. Thus, the Mover–Stayer model may also improve the fit on the diagonals.

The Mover–Stayer extension of the weak-order random walk model that we consider here has 39 extra parameters, in addition to the weak-order model parameters, namely, for each constituency, 12 parameters for the stationary distributions of the stayers, and 1 parameter representing the proportion of stayers. The results show a dramatic improvement of fit on the diagonals. We would also like to suggest that even though the Mover–Stayer version of the weak-order random walk model can remedy the underestimation problem on the diagonals, the TIO model is nonetheless conceptually more appealing. It captures the idea that some respondents might change their minds about being a “stayer” or a “mover” during the campaign—an alteration that fits nicely within the broader framework of media theory.

We plan to compare the Mover–Stayer model and the TIO model more comprehensively, as well as consider other model selection issues, in the near future. This topic is beyond the scope of the present paper.

In this paper, we introduce a new real-time random walk model, the tune in-and-out model, and we discuss this model's application to a presidential election survey. We report a substantial overall improvement of the TIO model over the original weak-order random walk model, as well as over Böckenholt's Thurstonian model, on the 1992 national election study panel data. In particular, it gives a much more accurate account of the number of respondents who did not change their order of preferences for Bush, Clinton and Perot across the two polls. Also, more importantly, this model gives a precise formalization of a mechanism often invoked to explain the occasional lack of involvement of potential voters in an election.

We now briefly review some of the open questions that our analysis has raised. In follow-up work, we intend to carry out a more systematic analysis of the TIO model and its variants and nested submodels by systematically choosing and comparing various possible parameter constraints. Such a systematic comparison is key to a deeper understanding of the critical features underlying the tune-in and -out transitions. For example, in an earlier section we have mentioned a submodel which assumes that the tune-in and -out activities only occur after the first poll. This model has the appealing feature of ‘forcing’ more tune-in and -out movements among the live and frozen states and it may reveal some interesting interplay among these states. Thus, in spite of the borderline fit of that submodel reported earlier, a more detailed analysis along this line may shed an important light on the TIO mechanism. Moreover, we plan to investigate the validity of the TIO model by applying it to other data sets, such as 1996 national election panel data.

A second interesting question is to check whether the TIO model is distinguishable from a more classical kind of model, namely, a random walk with absorption, which states that at a certain point in the campaign, respondents make up their mind how they will vote and their preferences remain fixed after that point. This is similar to one version of the TIO model in which the tune-in token probabilities are set to be zero. However, setting the tune-in tokens equal to zero means that we are no longer considering a medium, and the stochastic process is no longer a random walk. Actually, in the current context with two time points, setting the tune-in probabilities to be zero probably would not affect the fit very much. This is because (i) the estimates of the tune-in probabilities are comparatively much smaller than those of other token probabilities; (ii) the estimates of the tune-out probabilities are smaller than those of the tune-in probabilities. The latter fact means that there are few respondents moving to the frozen states in the first place. Thus, whether the tune-in probabilities are positive or zero probably would not have a substantial impact on the final estimation. That is, the two models would probably make similar predictions and we would probably require multiple time points to pin the models against each other.

Another open issue concerns the test statistic used throughout the analysis. In this paper we adopt  Böckenholt (2002)  methodology by using the log-likelihood estimate for the goodness-of-fit and likelihood ratio tests. However, the fact that there are many cells with zero (observed) frequencies causes some estimation problems. Finding a more appropriate test statistic for this type of data remains a challenging task. One possible option for dealing with this problem in future work may be a pseudo-Bayes approach (see, e.g.,  Bishop, Fienberg, & Holland, 1975 , Chapter 12).
