Knowledge Space Theory (KST,  Doignon & Falmagne, 1985, 1999; Falmagne & Doignon, 2011; Falmagne, Koppen, Villano, Doignon, & Johannesen, 1990 ) is a fundamental approach to the assessment of knowledge in mathematical psychology. In contrast to the widespread approach of Item Response Theory (IRT; e.g.,  Fischer & Molenaar, 2012; Lord & Novick, 2008; Rasch, 1960 ), KST does not represent individuals’ ability as a continuous latent variable that underlies responses to items of varying difficulty, but rather by discrete latent classes which are called  knowledge states . The concept of a knowledge state is central in KST and corresponds to the set of items that an individual can solve within a domain of knowledge. A collection of knowledge states that also contains the empty set and the domain is called a  knowledge structure , and formalizes the intuitive notion of the set of possible states during a learning process.

A typical example is provided by a mathematical assessment

Each item can be thought of as a problem that requires the student to master a specific concept or notion. If one envisages items as instances of problems, drawn from a domain of problems covering some field of knowledge to a sufficient extent (in this case, school algebra), then one can exploit relations between these notions. For example, based on the pattern of responses to a subset of problems, one can predict accuracy of future responses to other problems that require more complex (or simpler) notions. Moreover, one can use these relations to design efficient assessment procedures. In fact, a main goal of KST is to develop methods of querying that allow to uncover the latent knowledge state of an individual.

Formally, KST was developed as an axiomatic theory based on set theory and combinatorics, in which hierarchies between items are modeled through partial orders, quasi orders, and surmise functions. Essentially, a knowledge structure captures the different and possible solution strategies to a set of items. To link such a deterministic, set-theoretic structure to empirical data, probabilistic models have been proposed. These models account for noisy response behavior by modeling the transitions from latent knowledge states to observable response patterns, for instance, by assuming that students sometimes correctly guess when not mastering an item or sometimes fail even when mastering it. In addition, probabilistic models have been proposed to model the discrete probability distribution over the set of possible knowledge states as a result of some learning process.

In the present paper, we are concerned with the link between probabilistic models of KST and Multinomial Processing Tree (MPT) models, a prominent class of models proposed by Bill Batchelder and David Riefer in a series of classic papers (e.g.,  Batchelder & Riefer, 1980, 1986, 1988, 1990; Riefer & Batchelder, 1988 ). Traditionally, MPT models are mainly applied in cognitive and social psychology to disentangle different latent processes that are assumed to contribute to observable behavior. Usually, MPT models aim at testing some substantive theory of interest and are tailored to specific experimental paradigms such as old–new recognition tests (for reviews, see  Batchelder & Riefer, 1999; Erdfelder et al., 2009 ). More generally, however, MPT models can also be interpreted and used as an encompassing class of statistical models that subsumes various types of multinomial models assuming a finite number of discrete latent states (e.g., latent-class models or log-linear analysis;  Erdfelder, 2000 ).

We extend the well-known links between the class of MPT models and other multinomial models by showing that MPT models may also serve as an encompassing framework for yet another model class in mathematical psychology, namely, probabilistic KST models. This is due to the fact that both MPT and probabilistic KST models are multinomial models for discrete data and assume that the observed frequencies can be modeled as a mixture distribution over a finite number of latent states. Essentially, the model equations of both MPT and KST models are derived from a trivial application of the law of total probability as the sum over all possible combinations of latent states that result in a specific response pattern. In MPT models, the latent states usually refer to different branches or cognitive processes, whereas in KST, the latent states refer to different knowledge states, represented by specific subsets of items that have been mastered by an individual.

Two developments in KST are particularly relevant in connection to MPT modeling: descriptive languages and Markov processes. First,  descriptive languages  refer to a list of words that compactly describes the elements of a knowledge structure ( Degreef, Doignon, Ducamp, & Falmagne, 1986 ). Extending this concept,  assessment languages  are specific descriptive languages that also specify an algorithmic procedure that allows to uncover the latent knowledge state of an individual ( Degreef et al., 1986 ). Such descriptive languages can easily be organized in sequential decision trees. Similarly, the application of discrete and continuous Markov processes (see, e.g.,  Falmagne, 1989a; Falmagne & Doignon, 2011; Falmagne et al., 1990 ) to represent the progression of students along a stochastic learning path, or to uncover their knowledge states, is easily amenable to a representation in terms of sequential decision trees. Our work builds on previous work by  Erdfelder (2000)  who showed that descriptive and assessment languages as well as stochastic learning processes can be modeled within the MPT framework. However, whereas these developments in KST focus on  tree-based procedures  to uncover the latent state of an individual by administering an optimal subset of items, or to model the progression of an individual along some learning path, in the present context, the focus is shifted toward providing a  tree-based representation  of fundamental KST models.

More precisely, we are interested in linking MPT models to standard probabilistic models of KST: the Basic Local Independence Model (BLIM) and the Simple Learning Model (SLM). Whereas the BLIM merely defines a probabilistic state-response mapping from knowledge states to response patterns, the SLM in addition assumes that the state probabilities can be factorized in terms of the probabilities of the single items. A preliminary connection between MPT models and the BLIM, based on estimating the parameters of the model, was shown by  Wickelmaier (2016) . The rationale behind focusing on these two model classes is that, recently, a connection has been highlighted between generalized versions of the BLIM and SLM on the one hand and fundamental dichotomous and polytomous IRT models on the other hand ( Noventa, Spoto, Heller, & Kelava, 2019 ). Establishing such a link across different psychometric models is important because it allows to merge different traditions and unifies different frameworks, for instance, by complementing the information provided by continuous latent traits with the information provided by discrete knowledge structures. More precisely, the generalized version of the BLIM has been shown to be an equivalent reparameterization of a  
                         4 
                      -parameter IRT model under the assumption of (strong) local independence ( Noventa et al., 2019 ). Moreover, the generalized version of the SLM has been shown to be a generalization of polytomous IRT models and to provide a generalization of the (strong) local independence assumption in IRT. These connections suggest that the BLIM and the SLM are central for investigating violations of local independence in IRT. As such, reframing these fundamental models of KST within the context of MPT models may allow researchers to test different structures of local (in)dependence.

Moreover, recent developments in model selection for MPT models might complement already existing methods for constructing, testing, and uncovering knowledge structures. Broadly speaking, one can identify three main categories of methods for building knowledge structures: query to experts, skill map construction, and data-driven approaches. Especially the latter might also be of interest to researchers testing violations of local independence in IRT. Common data-driven approaches for uncovering latent knowledge structures include Boolean analysis of response patterns to dichotomous items (see, e.g.,  Buggenhaut & Degreef, 1987; Sargin & Ünlü, 2009; Schrepp, 1999 ), tracing back knowledge structures through their skill maps (see, e.g.,  Spoto, Stefanutti, & Vidotto, 2016 ), or clustering methods (see, e.g.,  de Chiusole, Stefanutti, & Spoto, 2017 ). For more information on such methods for extracting knowledge structures from data, the reader is referred to  Falmagne et al. (2013) . Currently, however, most of the available methods for uncovering knowledge structures are based on the BLIM or on more general models, but to our knowledge, there are no methods based on the SLM. Given the importance of the SLM for testing local independence in IRT, it is thus useful to extend these existing methods using methods and techniques for MPT models.

The paper is structured as follows. First, we introduce the definition and formal notation of KST and MPT models. Second, we highlight how the SLM allows to generalize the concept of (strong) local stochastic independence in IRT models. Third, we prove that standard probabilistic models of KST like BLIM and SLM can be represented by specific MPT models. Fourth, we discuss the benefits and practical limitations of the application of MPT models in KST and possible directions for future research.

Multinomial Processing Tree models assume that frequencies of a finite number of observable categories follow a finite mixture distribution with each mixture probability referring to a sequence of latent cognitive states. The probability of such a sequence (i.e., of a processing branch) is the product of all conditional probabilities of the corresponding latent events. Moreover, different branches can lead to the same observable category (see, e.g.  Hu & Batchelder, 1994 ). As discussed in Section  2.3 ,  Theorem 2  appears to be of particular relevance for IRT models since the logic of the SLM provides a way to account for violations of the assumption of local independence between the items of a test. It should be stressed that the SLM ignores the latent trait that typically characterizes IRT models. A more rigorous treatment requires to model every probability  
                            
                               
                                  g 
                               
                               
                                  q 
                               
                            
                          using the IRT decomposition into person skill and item difficulty (e.g., via a Rasch model). However, for the purpose of the present manuscript, this layer of complexity is not necessary since we focused on showing that local dependence between items (as modeled by KST) can equivalently be modeled via MPT methods. IRT person parameters and item difficulties can optionally be added to refine the model, for instance, by means of hierarchical MPT models which assume a latent-trait structure ( Klauer, 2010; Matzke, Dolan, Batchelder, & Wagenmakers, 2015 ).

We showed that standard probabilistic models in KST can be recast as special cases of MPT models ( Batchelder & Riefer, 1999 ). This is the case because both KST and MPT models are equivalent to latent class models which assume that observed response patterns follow a mixture distribution over a finite number of latent states ( Hu & Batchelder, 1994; Schrepp, 2005; Ünlü, 2011 ). In previous work,  Erdfelder (2000)  already pointed out a connection between MPT models and specific KST models (i.e., assessment languages and stochastic learning processes) that aims at selecting an optimal subset of items for assessment or modeling individual learning paths. However, more complex and ambitious learning theories for such purposes (e.g.,  Falmagne, 1989a, 1989b; Falmagne & Doignon, 2011 ) cannot be cast as MPT models due to the presence of task difficulty parameters and individual parameters for learning rates.

In the present article, we extended previous work on the connection between MPT and KST models ( Erdfelder, 2000; Wickelmaier, 2016 ) in two ways: First, we provided a simple formal proof of the representability of the fundamental KST model, namely the Basic Local Independence Model (BLIM) in  (3) , as an MPT model. Second, we showed that the Simple Learning Model (SLM) in  (7)  —a specific case of the BLIM in which the state probabilities are further constrained (leading to a smaller number of parameters)— is also equivalent to an MPT model. Establishing such a link is especially relevant for IRT modeling because the SLM allows to generalize the standard local independence assumption in IRT to situations with local dependence between the items ( Noventa et al., 2019 ). Moreover, motivated by the close link between the SLM and IRT, the supplementary material presents a simple simulation study showing that MPT methods can successfully be applied to discriminate between different dependence relations between the items when local independence is violated. In the simulation, we generated and fitted data based on five different knowledge structures for four items (including the power set for which the IRT assumption of local independence is satisfied). The results showed that standard methods for model selection of MPT models (e.g., the Bayes factor and the BIC) resulted in high recovery rates even for small sample sizes (i.e.,  
                         
                            N 
                            = 
                            100 
                         
                      ). However, since this simulation used a very small number of items and knowledge states, it can only be considered as an initial proof of concept. To assess the applicability of MPT software and the recovery rate of MPT model-selection methods in realistic scenarios, more complex simulation studies are required using knowledge structures with thousands of states as typically encountered in KST.

The close link between the different model classes may not only provide theoretical and practical benefits for MPT and KST researchers, but also for IRT practitioners. For example, the statistical machinery and software for MPT modeling is well developed, which may in turn facilitate the statistical analysis of KST and IRT models as illustrated in the supplementary material. Since MPT models can easily be specified via the standard EQN syntax ( Moshagen, 2010 ), it is straightforward to perform structural analyses of models (e.g., identifiability checks) as well as statistical analyses (e.g., model fitting and selection). Moreover, it is straightforward to test equality as well as order constraints on the probability parameters for lucky guesses and careless errors ( Knapp & Batchelder, 2004; Stefanutti & Robusto, 2009 ). However, when using the SLM for the purpose of IRT modeling ( Noventa et al., 2019 ), the item probabilities  
                         
                            
                               g 
                            
                            
                               q 
                            
                         
                       are usually equipped with a logistic link function as well as item-difficulty and person-trait parameters. Notably, such a latent-trait extension has also been developed for MPT models ( Klauer, 2010; Matzke et al., 2015 ) and implemented in user-friendly software ( Heck, Arnold, & Arnold, 2018 ). Hence, IRT researchers may rely on the SLM to account for dependencies between items (e.g., if some items can only be solved whenever another item has already been solved), while accounting for interindividual differences in performance via the latent-trait MPT approach. The formulation of the SLM as an MPT model might facilitate the analysis of dependencies between items, for instance, by means of a data-driven search for the most plausible knowledge structure.

MPT modelers might also gain advantages from the connection of MPT with KST models. In fact, KST models represent a flexible model class with several connections to other assessment theories in psychometrics. For instance, KST has not only been linked to latent class models ( Schrepp, 2005; Ünlü, 2011 ), but also to other important frameworks such as Formal Concept Analysis ( Rusch & Wille, 1996 ), Cognitive Diagnostic Models (CDM;  Heller et al., 2015 ) and both non-parametric ( Ünlü, 2007 ) and parametric IRT ( Noventa et al., 2019; Stefanutti, 2006 ). This in turn shows that MPT models might be relevant for other applications in psychometrics (for an application to response styles, see  Plieninger & Heck, 2018 ). More generally, and in line with the topic of the present special issue, the connection of mathematical models in cognitive psychology (MPT), mathematical psychology (KST), and psychometrics (CDM and IRT) was at the core of Bill Batchelder’s proposal of  cognitive psychometrics  ( Batchelder, 2010; Riefer, Knapp, Batchelder, Bamber, & Manifold, 2002 ). However, whereas the present work focused on the formal link between MPT and KST models (and indirectly, the CDM), future work may connect the KST framework to substantive MPT models with psychologically meaningful parameters (e.g., parameters measuring memory performance or response bias).

Another benefit of linking MPT and KST models that may be explored in future work concerns the identifiability of parameters, an important issue both for MPT models (e.g.,  Batchelder & Riefer, 1990; Meiser, 2014; Moshagen, 2010 ) and for probabilistic KST models such as the BLIM (e.g.,  Heller, 2017; Spoto et al., 2012, 2013; Stefanutti et al., 2012, 2018 ). Given that standard KST models can be represented as MPT models, it remains to be seen if and how the literature on the identifiability of knowledge structures applies to the identifiability of MPT models and vice versa. A complete transfer of the identifiability issue would imply that solutions developed within one framework (e.g., imposing order constraints on MPT parameters;  Erdfelder & Moshagen, 2013 ) may be useful in the other.

Finally, there are important limitations that need to be considered. The present manuscript focused merely on the theoretical connection between the KST and MPT frameworks. From a practical perspective, a limitation of applying MPT methods for KST research concerns the way how MPT models are usually specified in software packages. In the common EQN file format, MPT models are specified by defining the model equations for all branches separately ( Moshagen, 2010 ). For the power set of four items, the SLM model thus requires

                       rows of code to specify all possible combinations of the

As a remedy, we developed wrapper functions that translate probabilistic knowledge structures to EQN equations automatically (see supplementary material), thereby facilitating model fitting and comparison of KST models with MPT software. However, for large numbers of items and/or knowledge states, the representation of KST models by MPT models may become impracticable since all possibilities of mapping the latent knowledge states to specific response patterns need to be represented explicitly in the model file. Although this might be more problematic within KST settings in which large domains are associated to structures with hundreds or thousands of states, the application of MPT models appears to be perfectly feasible in IRT contexts when considering smaller numbers of items.
