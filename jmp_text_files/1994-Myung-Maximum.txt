Maximum Entropy Interpretation of Decision Bound and Context Models of Categorization 

IN JAE MyuNG* 

Ohio State University 

Maximum entropy inference is a method for inferring an unknown probability distribution from a set of moments of that distribution in such a way that all information contained in the set is maximally utilized. This article presents a maximum entropy interpretation of the decision bound models and the context model of categorization. For the decision bound models it is shown that several forms of decision bound can be derived as maximum entropy solutions based on relatively limited information on category structure. For the context model it is shown that a maximum entropy inference model is asymptotically equivalent to the context model under some restrictive conditions on the category exemplar distribution and similarity parameters of the model. The maximum entropy inference model, however, does not require the storage of exemplars in memory as does the context model. Maximum entropy inference also provides a theoretical justification for similarity rules of the context model, namely, the multiplicative similarity rule for binary features and the Gaussian—Euclidean- and the Exponential-city-block-similarity rules for continuous features. © 1994 Academic Press, Inc. 

The decision bound models of categorization (Ashby & Maddox, 1990, 1992, 1993) assume that the subject divides the perceptual space into distinct category regions and that, for a new stimulus, the subject determines in which region of the perceptual space the stimulus representation belongs and emits the associated response. Various forms of decision bound, which include the optimal, linear, and general quadratic decision bounds, were investigated, and recent studies (Ashby & Maddox, 1992; Maddox & Ashby, 1993; Ashby & Lee, 1991) have shown that the decision bound models provide an accurate quantitative description of human categorization performance. The models, however, make no assumption about how the subject computes a decision bound from the perceptual experience. It is assumed that somehow the subject learns to use a particular decision bound, providing no detail of how this might happen. 

On the other hand, the context model (Medin & Schaffer, 1978; Nosofsky, 1986) assumes that a category decision is based on a similarity computation between a probe item and stored exemplars. The model makes specific assumptions about how similarity should be computed. The original model (Medin and Schaffer, 1978} adopted a multiplicative similarity rule for stimuli with binary features.

Nosofsky (1986) generalized the model to continuous feature dimensions by introducing the multidimensional scaling (MDS) approach. Although the context model provides quantitative fits to categorization data, a good account of data does not necessarily justify the underlying assumptions. What assumptions of the model are relevant and irrelevant in accounting for categorization performance? What is the theoretical justification for the multiplicative and the MDS similarity rules? There has been little theoretical work on the justification or foundations for the assumptions of the context model. Marley (1992) showed that Luce’s choice rule (Luce, 1963), assumed by the context model, can be given an interpretation in terms of a multidimensional Poisson process. The Poisson approach also gives a natural interpretation of the similarity measures in terms of interactions between sensory and memory states although “there is nothing in its structure as given that requires such similarity functions” (as assumed by the context model) (Marley, 1992, p. 315). This article investigates theoretical foundations of decision bound models and the context model and seeks interpretations for the assumptions of the two models. For the decision bound models, I show that several forms of decision bound can be derived as an optimal maximum entropy inference based on relatively limited information on the category structure. For the context model, I show that maximum entropy inference provides an alternative interpretation of the model regarding the memory storage assumption. Maximum entropy inference also provides a theoretical justification for the similarity rules of the context model, namely, the multiplicative similarity rule for binary features and the MDS similarity rules for continuous features. 

The article is organized as follows. First, the basic ideas of maximum entropy inference are introduced and some useful theoretical results relevant to the present investigation are derived. Then, decision bound models are briefly reviewed, followed by maximum entropy interpretations of the models and implications. Next, the context model is briefly reviewed, and its assumptions are critically examined. This is followed by maximum entropy interpretations of the model and implications. Finally, the last section of summary and conclusions follows. 

MAXIMUM ENTROPY INFERENCE 

The principle of maximum entropy (Jaynes, 1957, 1978; Tribus, 1969) is an inference method used when information about an unknown probability distribution is provided in the form of the moments of that distribution. An infinite number of different probability distributions satisfy a given, finite set of moments. The inference problem is to find a probability distribution that satisfies a set of moments and is in some sense the best estimate. By virtue of an axiomatic proof, it is shown below that there is a reasonable method for estimating probabilities from moments. This method is known by several names including minimum relative entropy inference (Johnson & Shore, 1982; Shore & Johnson, 1980). Since minimum cross or

relative entropy inferences (Kuliback, 1959} are equivaient to maximum entropy inference when no prior probability is available, the latter term (maximum entropy inference) is used in the present article. 

Shore and Johnson have shown, based on five consistency axioms, that maximum entropy inference is an optimal method for estimating a probability distribution when information is given in terms of moments. The five axioms—with explanations—are as follows (adapted from Levy, 1989). 

1. Uniqueness. The inference method must produce a single probability distribution for a given set of moments. 

2. Null Information. The inferred probability distribution for a given set of moments should not be changed by adding the “null” constraint that is satisfied by any probability distribution. For example, the requirement that the sum of probabilities be equal to one is a null constraint. 

3. Invariance. The same probability distribution must be obtained independent of the choice of coordinate system, for example, whether the rectangular coordinate system or the polar coordinate system is used. 

4. System Independence. For n sets of moments that represent n independent systems of events, the inferred probability distribution must be given in a multiplication of n probability distributions each of which is inferred based on each set of moments. 

5. Subset Independence. The inferred probability distribution should be the same regardless of whether the inference method is separately applied to disjoint subspaces of system states to obtain conditional probability densities, which are then combined to form the full system density, or is directly applied to the full space of system states. 

Shore and Johnson (1980) showed that if a value to be maximized in an inference procedure satisfies these five axioms, then it is the Shannon information measure (Shannon, 1948). (Information and entropy can be used interchangeably.)

The notion of entropy maximization may provide a useful theoretical tool for understanding human cognitive performance as well as a statistical tool for data analysis. In this article, this maximum entropy hypothesis is a major theoretical thrust in deriving a probability desirable for inferring category structure.

First, the notation is defined. A real-valued vector y=(),,..., x) denotes an exemplar made up of N features. Unless stated otherwise, y is assumed to be a random vector. A real-valued, constant vector x = (x,,..., X,) denotes a new stimulus pattern to be categorized. The letter C denotes the category label. 

For simplicity, a two-category case with two features (NV = 2) is considered in this article; the generalization to a multicategory case is straightforward. Thus the letter C is A or B, and the exemplar vectors y and x are two-dimensional. 

A categorization problem can be defined as an inference of the most likely category for a given stimulus x. Let us assume that, to solve the problem, the subject estimates the probability or likelihood that the stimulus belongs to a particular category; i.e., P(C |x). According to Bayes rule, this probability can be computed from the prior probability P(C) and the conditional probability P(y =x | C), which

However, the context model seems to produce better fits than the prototype model to human categorization probability data. The context model has been successful in accounting for human categorization performance revealed in a variety of experimental settings (e.g., Busemeyer et al, 1984; Medin er al., 1982; Nosofsky, 1991). At least three properties of the context model seem crucial for describing categorization data. 

The first property of the context model has to do with the exemplar storage assumption. For instance, Nosofsky (1991) compared predictions from the prototype model and the context model based on the multiplicative similarity rule. The context model consistently outperformed the prototype model (11 out of 12 experiments for binary feature dimensions, and 18 out of 19 experiments for continuous features dimensions). However, the superiority of the context model over the prototype model does not necessarily support the validity of the assumption of the storage of individual exemplars. This exemplar storage assumption has never been tested empirically. Furthermore, it is an unrealistic assumption. For example, it is hard to imagine that a 70-year-old fisherman would remember every instance of fish that he has seen when attempting to categorize an object as a fish. The fact that the context model provides a better account of the data than the prototype model gives only indirect support for this exemplar hypothesis. It could be the case that the exemplar storage assumption is sufficient but not necessary for accounting for the probability data. It would then be safe to say that some mechanism that reflects frequency information of exemplar presentations is critical in accounting for the observed categorization probabilities. Examples for such mechanisms would be the storage and utilization of the category means and the category base rates, or of the category means and the category variabilities. Interestingly, there have been some experimental findings (e.g., Ashby & Maddox, 1992; Fried & Holyoak, 1984; Park & Hastie, 1987; Posner & Keele, 1968) that people are indeed sensitive to the variability of category exemplars.

A second important property of the context model is the multidimensional scaling rule for computing the similarity 4(x, y) for continuous features. Numerous studies, in particular those by Nosofsky, have shown that the MDS approach combined with Luce’s choice rule describes categorization performance remarkably well. However, there is a caveat. This result does not necessarily mean that people have the natural notion of similarity that is computed using the MDS rule. The only thing known for sure is that the MDS similarity rule describes categorization probabilities with reasonable accuracy. 

A third property of the context model is the multiplicative rule for computing the similarity 4(x, y) for binary features. An advantage of the multiplicative rule over the additive rule is that the context model with the multiplicative rule can be sensitive to the correlational structure of features while the same model with the additive rule cannot handle correlations. (This is shown rigorously in Appendix D.) Indeed, the data suggest that categorization decisions seem to be influenced by correlations among features (e.g., see Medinet al, 1982; Ashby & Maddox, 1990, 1992). 

In sum, we have identified three properties of the context model that seem essential for accounting for observed categorization probabilities. They are a mechanism that reflects exemplar frequency, the MDS similarity rule for continuous features, and the multiplicative similarity rule for binary features. The present investigation shows that maximum entropy inference based on a minimal set of assumptions not only furnishes the above three essential properties but also almost recovers the entire context model for both binary and continuous features. However, the derivation does not require the exemplar storage assumption as does the context model. Also important, the investigation provides a theoretical justification for the multiplicative similarity rule and the MDS similarity rule.

SUMMARY AND CONCLUSIONS 

The goal of this article has been to understand the theoretical foundations of two categorization models, decision bound models and the context model. The present investigation provided theoretical interpretations of the major assumptions of these models in terms of maximum entropy inference. 

For the decision bound models, it is shown that many forms of decision bound assumed by the models can be derived as maximum entropy inference solutions based on limited information on category structure, such as category means, variances, and base rates. Proposition1 showed that maximum entropy inference implies the decision bound of the general quadratic classifier without requiring the assumption of perfect memory. The maximum entropy inferred decision bound is reduced to the particular decision bound in Eq. (19), which becomes the optimal decision bound for normally distributed stimuli, assuming that the subject estimates the category structure without error. Proposition 2 shows that maximum entropy inference can even produce a piecewise linear decision bound under an appropriate storage assumption. The piecewise linear decision bound is not part of the decision bounds originally proposed by the decision bound models. The piecewise linear decision bound, however, as shown in Fig. 1, seems to provide a good approximation to the optimal decision bound. In short, maximum entropy inference provides a plausible mechanism that computes various forms of decision bound without the assumption of perfect memory or the normality assumption on the distribution of exemplar stimuli. 

For the context model, the main result is a demonstration that the maximum entropy inference model based on fewer storage assumptions than the context model reproduces both the Gaussian—Euclidean MDS version of the context model for continuous features (Proposition 3) and the multiplicative similarity version of the context model for binary features (Proposition 4) as a limit approximation under some conditions of the category exemplar distribution and similarity parameters of the context model. 

The maximum entropy inference model, however, does not require the storage of individual exemplars in memory as does the context model. An immediate implication of this important result is that one does not have to assume the storage of individual exemplars. The maximum entropy inference model assumes that the category means and correlations among features for binary features (plus the category variances for continuous features) and the category base rates only are stored in memory and that categorization decisions are made probabilistically by 

CATEGORIZATION AND MAXIMUM ENTROPY INFERENCE 

359 

optimally utilizing the stored information. It is shown, then, that the model describes the observed probability data equally as well as the context model, in particular, for normally distributed stimuli. The present result does not necessarily imply that the exemplar storage assumption is invalid. The context model with this assumption may still be a correct description of human category learning. However, according to the present investigation, unless the storage assumption is unequivocally supported from rigorous experimental tests, a good account of probability data by the context model should not be interpreted as validating evidence for that assumption. 

Note that an assumption on memory storage similar to that of the maximum entropy inference model was made in the prototype model (Posner & Keele, 1968; Reed, 1972). Perhaps the reason the prototype model fails to account for data is that information about the category base rates and category variances is neglected and furthermore the stored statistics are not optimally used in the prototype model. 

The present investigation provides a theoretical justification for some of the assumptions of the context model, namely, the multiplicative similarity rule for binary features and the Gaussian—Euclidean MDS and Exponential-city-block MDS similarity rules for continuous features. No one has really shown where these rules come from, except by saying that models with these rules tend to fit the data well. According to maximum entropy inference, these similarity rules are the forms that maximally utilize the information contained in a given set of stored moments. There is a caveat against a possible misinterpretation of this result. That is, the justification for the similarity sules is applied only to the end form of the computation, but not necessarily to the category judgment process based on similarity computation as assumed by the context model. In fact, no assumption of similarity computation was made in the present derivation of categorization probability. Instead, it was assumed that people would maximally utilize information contained in a set of stored moments when they make category judgments.
