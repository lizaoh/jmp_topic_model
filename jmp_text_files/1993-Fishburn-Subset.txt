Independence axioms based on subset structures are examined within three theories of decision under risk when outcomes are subsets of items. The theories are the linear utility theory of von Neumann and Morgenstern, weighted linear utility theory, and skew symmetric bilinear utility theory. The effects of the independence axioms on subset utilities include several additive and multiplicative decompositions. | © 1993 Academic Press, Inc. 

1. INTRODUCTION 

This paper continues a study (Fishburn, 1992a) of subset preference in the spirit of earlier work in Churchman and Ackoff (1954), Fishburn (1972) and Barbera, Sonnenschein, and Zhou (1991). We focus here on subset outcomes in decision under risk in the contexts of linear utility theory (von Neumann and Morgenstern, 1944) and more flexible nonlinear utility theories (Machina, 1982, 1987; Chew, 1983; Fishburn, 1982, 1988). Our particular emphasis is the effects of independence axioms on subset utility representations. 

We are motivated by two observations. First, choice outcomes very often consist of collections of discrete items that can be combined and rearranged in various ways. A few examples are committees, packages of new car options, courses to offer next year, and menus of research proposals. It is natural to formulate such outcomes as subsets of individual items and to consider subsets as the basic units for preference or evaluation. It is also tempting, largely for convenience, to evaluate only the individual items and then to extrapolate their values by an algebraic process, such as addition, to obtain values of subsets. However, such a process is liable to significant distortion because of value interdependencies within subsets

The independence conditions we discuss either deny or only partially account for such interdependencies, and more imaginative interdependent models, such as the balance model of Farquhar and Rao (1976), are needed for their fuller consideration. On the other hand, our axioms show precisely what is being assumed when specialized decompositiens of subset utilities are used in risky decision formulations with subset outcomes. Moreover, it is our hope that the limited treatment offered here will stimulate both the development of interdependent models and empirical research on choice behavior that will enhance our understanding of preference patterns in subset evaluation and choice. Our second observation is that surprisingly little of the extensive literature of economic consumption theory, multiattribute utility, decision under risk and uncertainty, time-dependent preferences, and social choice theory (for example, Arrow, 1963; Chipman, Hurwicz, Richter, and Sonnenschein, 1971; Debreu, 1959; Fishburn, 1970, 1973, 1988; Keeney and Raiffa, 1976; Krantz, Luce, Suppes and Tversky, 1971; Luce and Raiffa, 1957; Savage, 1954; Wakker, 1989) deals with subset outcomes. However, subset structures are prominent in this literature. Examples include algebras of subsets for subjective probability (Savage, 1954; Kraft, Pratt, and Seidenberg, 1959; Fine, 1973; Chateauneuf, 1985; Fishburn, 1986) and event ambiguity (Elisberg, 1961; Einhorn and Hogarth, 1986; Fishburn, 1993), binaryattributes formulations of multiattribute preference and choice (Debreu, 1960; Fishburn, 1970; Krantz et al, 1971; Tversky, 1972; Wakker, 1989), and vector space formulations that accommodate subset algebras along with other decisiontheoretic structures (Fishburn, 1992b). 

It is therefore natural to borrow ideas from other topics for our discussion of subset outcomes, and we do this freely. At the same time, the ubiquity of subset outcomes and the combinatorial possibilities for their items support a nonderivative treatment of subset preference. We approach the subject in the latter spirit but mention related. ideas from other areas. 

The next section presents the axioms and representation theorems of our study. Proofs appear in later sections.

6. DISCUSSION 

In this paper we have extended an analysis of subset outcomes in decision under risk begun in Fishburn (1972, 1992a). It was shown how special independence conditions for gambles with subset outcomes simplify utility functions on subsets in linear utility theory and two versions of nonlinear utility theory. 

Although the resultant forms greatly ease the task of utility assessment, they are inappropriate in situations that have significant preferential interdependencies among items. It is our hope that the present work will encourage further development of viable models for subset preference in the face of interdependencies.
