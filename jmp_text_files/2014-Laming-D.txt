There are two lights, left and right, mounted on a screen. Below each light is a key. A third light in the centre of the screen announces the beginning of a trial. The participant’s task is to predict, by pressing one or other of the two keys, which of the two outcome lights, left or right, will be lit on that trial. Once the prediction has been registered, one of the two outcome lights is switched on and the experiment proceeds to the next trial.

In the experiment by   Suppes and Atkinson (1960, Ch. 10; see Fig. 1)  the choice of outcome, left or right, was programmed in a random sequence, independent of the participant’s predictions (non-contingent reinforcement), with one of the lights, say the right-hand one, chosen 60% of the time. This kind of reinforcement schedule attracts particular attention because the proportion of predictions frequently evolves to match the proportion of outcomes (probability matching).

Three groups of 30 participants each made 240 successive predictions.  Fig. 1 
                       shows the proportions of ‘right’ responses (actually, whichever response was correct 60% of the time) in successive blocks of 20 trials. The participants were instructed to be correct as often as possible, so that the optimum strategy was to identify the more frequent outcome and choose that outcome on every trial. But, in fact, the participants converged on 60% ‘right’ responses (probability matching)

Suppes and Atkinson applied  Estes’ (1959)  multi-element stimulus-sampling model to their data. This is equivalent to an urn model as follows:

There is an urn containing some number,

If the prediction, whichever it is, proves correct, the ball is returned to the urn, so that the probabilities of prediction on the next trial, ‘left’ or ‘right’, are unchanged. The history of binary prediction (or probability learning) experiments has been reviewed by   Estes (1964) . The idea originated with   Humphreys (1939)  as a human analogue to classical conditioning, and the chief focus of Estes’ review is comparison with other paradigms of learning. The view I advance here is that a binary prediction experiment realises a stochastic process of choice responses that often converges to a distribution that matches the distribution of outcomes. The parameters of the stochastic process do not change during the experiment; the process merely evolves, and so does not require the participants to learn anything. Estes’ review includes both the studies mentioned above, but does not mention the problem of the approach to asymptote. This paper offers a solution to that problem.

Signal detection experiments provide an interesting comparator, because performance depends on the relative probabilities of signal and noise, and the proportion of “Yes” responses tends to match the proportion of signal trials ( Dorfman, 1969; Thomas & Legge, 1970 ; see also  Laming, 2001 , for the numerical data)—‘probability matching’, as in  Fig. 1 . Probability matching is not optimum ( Green, 1960 ) and, of itself, implies some interaction between successive trials to converge on a pattern of responding that matches the feedback.

The hypothesis I investigate here is that the trial-to-trial interactions in binary prediction are of the same nature as those in signal detection; that is, they may be modelled in the same way, saving only that in binary prediction

Fig. 3 
                          displays some well-known data from  Tanner et al. (1956) ; see also   Green and Swets (1966, pp. 88–89.) . The observer was asked to detect a brief tone (1000 Hz for 100 ms) in a background of Gaussian noise. Each trial consisted of a single presentation in which the signal might, or might not, be present in the noise. The observer’s response was followed by feedback, so that the observer always knew when he/she had made a mistake. The different data points (filled circles) in  Fig. 3  plot the proportions of false-positives and correct detections in random sequences in which the signal was presented with probabilities 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7 and 0.9 respectively. Increasing the frequency of signals increases the observer’s readiness to report a detection; that is, missed detections are traded for false-positives. The operating characteristic (OC) is a trading relationship between detections (when a signal is presented) and false-positives (given noise alone). It is copied from the theory of signal detectability ( Peterson, Birdsall, & Fox, 1954 ) and is determined by the detectability of the signal against the noise.

The choice of operating point by the observer equates to probability matching, in which the frequency of “Yes” approximates the frequency of signal presentations. The observer was encouraged to maximise the number of correct responses and was rewarded accordingly ( Green & Swets, 1966 , p. 90). The open circles in  Fig. 3  show the optimum placements of the operating points, that yield the maximum proportion of correct responses. These placements depend on signal probability. The optimum occurs where the tangent to the OC (broken lines in  Fig. 3 ) has a gradient equal to the ratio of signal probability/noise probability; i.e., 9, 2.33, 1, 0.43 and 0.11. A small displacement along the OC then produces changes in the proportions of correct detections and correct negatives that are exactly balanced by the different probabilities of signal and noise. But the actual choice of operating point is much more conservative than this optimum ( Green, 1960 ).

Thomas and Legge  ( 1970 ; also   Dorfman, 1969 ) pointed out that the empirical operating points in  Fig. 3  correspond approximately to probability matching. Probability matching is accomplished when there are equal numbers of errors of each kind, that is, when the probabilities of error are inversely proportional to the probabilities of the stimuli (i.e. to

Fig. 4 
                          shows how probability matching is achieved.  Tanner et al. (1970)  asked their observers to distinguish between two levels of a 1 kHz tone, presented for 100 ms, without any noise background. One tone was set at 70 dB (the ‘signal’) and the other (noise) at some lesser level, adjusted to permit about 70% correct responses. There were three different presentation schedules with the louder tone presented at random with probabilities 0.2, 0.5 and 0.8.  Fig. 4  reproduces the data from the 0.5 schedule.

Tanner et al. (1970)  picked out those trials in which the subject had missed a real signal, or reported a signal that was not there, and looked at what happened on the  following  trial. The left-hand diagram presents the data from an initial 9800 trials, aggregated from seven observers on which they received no feedback, not knowing when they had been correct and when wrong. It can be seen that the trade-off between missed detections and false-positives fluctuates, with errors occurring chiefly at the extremes of those fluctuations. The centre diagram presents the next 9800 trials on which the observers received the usual knowledge of results. Comparing the two diagrams (the data points for trials following an error are reproduced in red from the left-hand diagram, for comparison, and connected by arrows to the data points in the centre diagram), it can be seen that the knowledge that one has just made a mistake shifts the trade-off for the next trial in such a way as to make a repetition of that particular mistake less likely; but that change simultaneously increases the likelihood of the opposite kind of error.

This effect of knowledge of results appears even more clearly in the right-hand diagram, which summarises performance from a final 24,500 trials, again aggregated from seven observers, without feedback. (The data points for trials following an error are again reproduced in red from the middle diagram.) It is clear that being told one has just made a mistake induces a large shift in the trade-off for the next trial. Equilibrium is achieved when the numbers of errors of each sort (missed detections and false-positives) are approximately equal. But if there are equal numbers of errors of each sort, the total number of reported detections must match the number of signal presentations. The equilibrium result is probability matching.

Fig. 5 
                          shows the data for the final 24,500 trials per signal probability from all three presentation schedules. Ordinarily, a large proportion of signal trials drives performance towards the upper right hand corner of the diagram (cf.  Fig. 3 ). Here the relationship between signal probability and location lies in the opposite direction, a phenomenon termed ‘probability contrast’ by   Parducci and Sandusky (1965) , showing that the adjustment to signal probability in  Fig. 3  is dependent on feedback.

Suppose, as a Null hypothesis that, in the absence of feedback, signal detection performance is independent of signal probability, the parameters being the same. So, if signal probability is 0.8, one would expect to get about 80% “Yes” reports (detections and false-positives). But the central tendency of judgment ( Hollingworth, 1909; Laming, 2004 , pp. 141–149) will act to reduce that proportion. A reduced proportion of “Yes” reports would transpose into reduced probabilities of detections and false-positives when the signal probability is 0.8, as in  Fig. 5 . When the signal probability is 0.2, on the other hand, the central tendency of judgment acts in the opposite direction.

The operating points following a correct rejection (noise) and a missed detection (i.e. the response “No”) are biased towards the bottom-left, while those following a correct detection and a false positive (the response “Yes”) are biased towards the top-right. This is consequent on sampling variability; briefly, “Yes” is more likely when the operating point drifts down the scale in  Fig. 2 –this equates to the top-right in  Fig. 5 –and conversely.

The principal thesis of this paper is that the fluctuations in performance at asymptote in binary prediction are of the same psychological nature, that is, may be accommodated by a model of the same structure, as the fluctuations at asymptote in signal detection, save only that in binary prediction there is no signal. It is unclear how much this matters, because probability matching is far from invariable in binary prediction. One possibility is that some participants discover which is the more frequent outcome and then predict that outcome on every trial, while others remain captured by a process that asymptotes with probability matching.   Rakow, Newell, and Zougkou (2010)  used the three-choice variant of the prediction task introduced by   Tversky and Edwards (1966)  in which the participant could either observe an outcome, but not predict, or predict and receive an ultimate payoff, but without any feedback. They reported percentages ranging from 7.1 to 26.8 for choosing the more frequent outcome on every trial, increasing with the disparity in probability and with experience in the experiment. But, setting aside those participants who correctly identified the more frequent outcome, the distribution of predictions made is spread much more widely than the distribution of outcomes actually observed (Rakow, Newell and Zougkou,  Figs. 1 and 2 ; in the three-choice variant these two quantities are disjoint). So the fact of probability matching admits some doubt.

The study of fluctuations in signal-detection performance has a substantial history.   Parducci and Sandusky (1965)  reported a signal-detection experiment using brief flashes of one of two lights, left and right (the same stimulus set-up as in Suppes and Atkinson, above, but two lights very close together), but without feedback.   Sandusky (1971)  compared two models, one in which the two distributions in  Fig. 6  are sectioned into three categories by two fixed criteria, with observations falling between the criteria leading to a repetition (or alternation) of the response on the preceding trial, and a second model in which the distributions are generated by differencing relative to the input on the preceding trial. Tanner, Rauk and Atkinson anticipated thereby two influential ideas in sensory judgment: first the comparison of each trial with its predecessor ( Laming, 1984; Stewart, Brown, & Chater, 2005 ) and the positive correlation, as much as 0.8, between successive log magnitude estimates ( Baird, Green, & Luce, 1980; Laming, 1984 ).

Binary prediction was originally devised by   Humphreys (1939)  as a human analogue to classical conditioning, and   Estes (1964)  was chiefly concerned to compare it with other paradigms of learning. But binary prediction has been modelled here as a stochastic process of choice responses that often converges to match the distribution of outcomes. The exponential parameter has been variously estimated to be 0.025 ( Fig. 1 ), 0.023 and 0.019 ( Fig. 11 ), and 0.026 ( Table 4 ). It may be that the coincidence of estimates from  Fig. 1  and from the signal-detection analysis ( Figs. 5 and 8 ) is pure chance; but it may also be that both experiments are looking at the same underlying process and that 0.025 (or thereabouts) per trial is a value of some generality.

Participants in the binary prediction experiments were instructed to be correct as often as possible with their predictions. Their optimum strategy was to identify the more frequent outcome and choose that outcome on every trial. Very few participants did this. Suppes and Atkinson’s participants were led to believe that they were playing a game with a partner and that the outcome on each trial (actually non-contingent) depended on their joint responses. Given that mindset, it is perhaps understandable that their participants had difficulty. However, the performance of the two reward groups in  Fig. 10  might be held to give the lie to this argument.

The signal detection model advanced here is a Markov model of first order on the underlying variables. It asymptotes on probability matching ( Norman, 1974 ). That means that some extraneous input is required for participants to identify the more frequent outcome and do anything other than match the outcome frequency.   Rakow et al. (2010)  find that the more frequent outcome (“maximising” strategy) is identified more readily with a greater difference between the frequencies of the two outcomes and with increasing experience at binary prediction.

Binary prediction has been modelled here as signal-detection without the signal. Shifts in predictive frequency conditional on events on the preceding trial are analogous to the shifts in the signal-detection operating point in  Fig. 8 , likewise conditional on events on the preceding trial. This enables binary prediction to be related to a much broader class of experimental tasks, and the details of the model to be justified with reference to existing signal-detection experiments. It also presents the binary prediction experiment as a stochastic process that converges to a dynamic equilibrium. The parameters of the process do not change during the experiment; it merely evolves. ‘Learning’ now means simply the convergence of decision criteria to that equilibrium and there is no need for the participants otherwise to have any knowledge of the nature of the outcome sequence. If the quarter of the participants in the Friedman et al. study be disregarded, and probably also some of those in the Suppes and Atkinson reward groups, most participants did not learn what the outcome frequencies were. In fact, probability matching has the contrary implication that people are mostly unable to learn about relative frequencies, not, that is, without paper and pencil and formal statistical analysis of the data.
