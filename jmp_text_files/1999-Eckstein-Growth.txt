A Dynamic Model of Cognitive Growth in a Population: Spatial Tasks and Conservation 

Shulamith Graus Eckstein 

Technion, Israel Institute of Technology 

A dynamic model of cognitive growth is developed that is applicable to cross-sectional studies of growth in a population, and that predicts the distribution of scores as a function of age. This model modifies the theory of P. van Geert (1991, Psychological Review, 98, 3�53) for the cognitive growth of an individual under limited resources, by taking into account the effect of schooling or training. The modified theory provides an explanation for the puzzling effect reported by C. J. Brainerd (1977, Psychological Bulletin, 84, 919�939), that the gain in scores obtained in concept training experiments is independent of the initial state of the learner. In developing the modified theory, it was found that the assumption of delayed feedback is unnecessary. To apply the model to populations, it was assumed that the ability parameters that appear in the growth function are distributed normally in the population. A distribution function G(s; t) was derived that predicts the distributions of scores s at age t in terms of a small number of parameters. Although the abilities are assumed to be distributed normally, the score distributions are often bimodal. The model was applied to the studies of H. Thomas and A. Lohaus (1993, Monographs of the Society for Research in Child Development, 58, 1�169) on the growth of the concepts of verticality and horizontality, and to the study of P. M. Bentler (1970, Perceptual and Motor Skills, 31, 855�859) on the growth of the concept of conservation. In these applications the score distributions are clearly bimodal, but there is no evidence of abrupt transitions or catastrophes. This shows that the presence of a ``catastrophe flag'' (bimodal distributions) does not necessarily imply the presence of a catastrophe. � 1999 Academic Press 

The purpose of this paper is to develop a dynamic model of cognitive growth that can be applied to cross-sectional studies of growth in a population, and that can predict the distribution of scores as a function of age. Some years ago, van Geert (1991) presented a dynamic model that describes cognitive growth of an individual under limited resources. In this model, growth is governed by the logistic equation and is an explicit function of time. The model is appropriate for longitudinal studies

Such a study was carried out by Thomas and Lohaus (TL) (1993) on the growth of the concepts of horizontality and verticality among children and adolescents. The score distributions were often bimodal, so TL assumed that the ability to perform spatial tasks is distributed bimodally in the population and constructed a mathematical model based upon this assumption. The parameters of the model were plotted as a function of age, but the theoretical model did not contain age as a variable, and therefore could not describe the functional dependence of growth upon age. Indeed, Thomas and Lohaus (1993, p. 33) express the belief that ``rarely is there theory to guide the choice of a suitable parametric family for fitting a growth function.'' 

Yet such a theory does exist: it is the cognitive growth model of van Geert (1991). His model describes the cognitive growth of an individual. In this paper, we will use a modified version of van Geert's model to investigate two basic questions. First, can a model of individual growth be applied to a population and used to describe score distributions? In the following, we shall show how the model can be used to derive a function G(s; t) which gives the distribution of scores, s, at each age, t. 

The second question is whether it is necessary to assume that abilities are distributed bimodally in order to account for bimodal score distributions. A bimodal distribution of a given characteristic occurs when there are two subpopulations with qualitatively different characteristics. But is the converse true? That is, does a bimodal distribution of scores imply two qualitatively different subpopulations? We argue that it does not. There are two possible scenarios to interpret a bimodal score distribution for subjects at a given age. In one scenario, the population is composed of two latent classes of qualitatively different abilities. In the other scenario, there is only one class, and abilities are distributed along a continuum in the population, in a unimodal fashion. But the age at which maximum growth takes place and the rate of growth are different for different individuals. If the variance in these quantities is sufficiently large, then a considerable number of people will have acquired the competence to perform a certain task at a given age, while a considerable number of others will not have. In this scenario, as in the first scenario, scores cluster about two different levels, but the underlying theory of the distribution of abilities in the population is quite different. 

Thomas and Lohaus have chosen the first scenario, as far as spatial tasks are concerned, for in their model there are only two values of the ability parameter, ``success probability,'' in the entire sample, and they partition all subjects into ``poor performers'' and ``good performers.'' They also propose that their model be used as a general methodological framework, implying that partitioning into latent classes should be used for other cognitive tasks as well. 

In this paper, we choose the second scenario, and show that it is unnecessary to partition populations into qualitatively different classes in order to account for bimodal score distributions. The basic assumption of the paper is that the parameters that describe individual abilities are distributed unimodally in the population. We approximate the unimodal distribution by a normal distribution, which

COGNITIVE GROWTH IN AN INDIVIDUAL 

The main variable of interest in the model was called the growth level, L, by van Geert (1991). This variable represents the cognitive level of an individual in some specific domain. One example of growth level given by van Geert is the number of words in a child's passive vocabulary. Another example is the proportion of sentences in which a particular grammatical rule is properly used. Other examples of growth level, used in this paper, are the number of correct answers in assessment instruments that measure subjects' understanding of horizontality and verticality. 

What other variables should appear in the model beside growth level? We are interested in the changes in cognitive growth as a function of age, so an obvious variable is the age (or time), t. Many other variables are clearly of great importance. Some of these are physical, such as state of health; and others are affective, such as motivation level. Affective variables were investigated in a seminal series of papers by Dweck and her collaborators (Dweck, 1986; Dweck 6 Leggett, 1988; Elliot 6 Dweck, 1988; Bempechat, London, 6 Dweck, 1991), who found that goals are central determinants of individual achievement patterns. Dweck and associates show that cognitive growth, as well as affective and behavioral features of achievement patterns, follows directly from different goals. 

Undoubtedly, environmental factors, such as schooling, also affect cognitive growth. It is not easy to disentangle effects of schooling and age, but recently there have been notable advances on this problem. Bisanz, Morrison, and Dunn (1995) used a very ingenious experimental design, the cutoff design, to disentangle the effects of age and schooling upon children in kindergarten and Grade 1. They found that performance on conservation of number improves as a function of age, but not as a function of schooling. Contrariwise, performance on mental arithmetic improved with schooling rather than with age.

This model is very similar to the model of van Geert, except that he neglected to include the external driving force, E. Van Geert assumed that the effects of external influences are all contained with the constant K, which he called the carrying capacity. He did not include an external driving force because he assumed that cognitive growth is autocatalytic, that is, that it runs by itself. But then he was faced with the problem that without an external driving force, it is impossible to grow from level 0 to any finite level. This is not the case when there is an external driving force. We will call the first term in the right hand side of Eq. (4) the ``autocatalytic force,'' and the second term will be called the ``external force.'' 

BEHAVIOR OF THE MODEL 

To investigate the behavior of the model, we study graphs of the growth level for various parameter values. There are four independent parameters in the model, r, E, K, and T. Three of these parameters are fundamental, and would surely appear in any theory of growth. These are r, which gives the magnitude of the autocatalytic force; E, which gives the magnitude of the external driving force; and K, the carrying capacity, which is the maximum growth level in the absence of an external driving force. The fourth parameter, T, gives information about the functional form of the external force; that is, it tells us about the scheduling of training. This parameter should be different for different schedules. In our simple model (Eq. (5)) it is the time when the external force terminates. The Appendix describes the process of fitting the solution of L(t) in the time period when E=0 to the solution in the time period when E is non-zero.

Is Cognitive Growth Abrupt or Continuous? 

A long-standing controversy about cognitive growth is whether it is abrupt or continuous (for a brief review, see Brainerd, 1993). What does our model imply with respect to this controversy? If we look at Fig. 1 or Fig. 2, we see that the growth level increases and then levels off, so that it appears as if the learner has gone from one stage to another. But is the increase abrupt or continuous? This really depends upon the parameter r. Recall that the time scale of these graphs is in units of 1�r. If r is sufficiently large, growth would appear to be abrupt. If it is sufficiently small, it would appear to be continuous. 

Clearly, our model is unable to answer the question whether cognitive growth is abrupt or continuous, because it is consistent with either abrupt or continuous behavior (and all intermediate types, too) depending upon the value of r. On the other hand, it gives us a quantitative measure of the degree of abruptness, namely the value of r. It is much more informative to characterize the behavior of growth by a numerical parameter than to characterize it by a dichotomy��either abrupt or continuous. 

Training versus Development: The Significance of E 

Until now, we have considered cases in which the external force is not very large compared with the autocatalytic term. Let us now consider the opposite case, namely that the external force is very large compared with the autocatalytic term. This circumstance might be expected to occur when the effect of training is very large. For example, consider the learning of conservation concepts. Children who are nonconservers are conventionally classified as Stage I; children who consistently conserve are classified as Stage III; and children who do not respond consistently are classified at Stage II. Children who are in Stage I become conservers spontaneously on a time scale of the order of a few years. DYNAMIC MODELS OF COGNITIVE GROWTH OF POPULATIONS 

As in the case of Kail's research, studies of cognitive growth are frequently crosssectional in nature, and the data is often presented in terms of sample means. 

46

As noted above, in some cases it may be reasonable to compare the model directly with mean growth levels. But, as Estes (1960) showed, this is not always the case. In this paper, we will not be concerned with mean scores, because we wish to put the model to a severe test. We will not be satisfied with predicting mean scores as a function of age; rather we intend to predict the entire score distribution as a function of age. In the cases that we will discuss, the score distributions are bimodal, so that mean scores are not very informative about individual abilities, and are therefore of minor interest. Can the model describe bimodal score distributions? To study this question, it is clearly necessary to use a model that is explicitly designed for populations. 

To gain some perspective, we begin by discussing some current models of cognitive growth in populations. Many empirical studies of cognitive growth do not measure a variable representing growth level, but rather categorize subjects into distinct cognitive stages. In these studies, an ordinal scale is used to characterize cognitive growth, rather an interval scale, as in van Geert's model. For example, Shayer and his collaborators (Shayer, Ku�chemann, 6 Wylam, 1976; Shayer 6 Wylam, 1978) investigated the distribution of Piagetian stages of thinking in a sample of 10,000 children between the ages 9 and 14. Similar investigations, on a smaller scale, were carried out by Lawson and Renner (1974) and Shemesh, Eckstein, and Lazarowitz (1992). The appropriate model to describe this kind of study is a model of population dynamics, based upon systems of differential equations, similar to those used in population ecology. Eckstein and Shemesh (1992) developed mathematical models of this kind. They assumed that a population can be separated into groups at different cognitive stages, and their models described cognitive development as transitions from one stage to another. Eckstein and Shemesh (1993) also used their model to describe the development of children's conceptions of motion. Recent developments in this field are models by Preece and Read (1995) and by Eckstein and Kozhevnikov (1997). 

Van Geert's model is a much more detailed description of cognitive growth than the models discussed above. Rather than characterizing cognitive levels by broad categories, it assumes that the growth level of an individual can be measured on an interval scale and it describes the changes in the growth level as a function of time. As we have already noted, we will apply the model to populations and use it to describe not merely mean behavior, but rather the distribution of scores obtained by subjects as a function of age. 

APPLICATION OF THE MODEL TO POPULATIONS 

In the model of this paper, each individual's abilities (for a particular learning task) are characterized by two parameters, the carrying capacity, K, and r, the fractional rate of increase of growth level. These parameters are undoubtedly dependent upon external influences to some extent; but in addition, there are parameters (E and T ) which are entirely due to external influences. The growth level, L(t) is explicitly dependent upon these parameters: L=L(t; r, K, E, T ).

How does it happen, in the model of this paper, that an unimodal ability distribution results in bimodal score distributions? The key reason for the bimodality is the saturation of L. At younger ages, children's growth is much less than their carrying capacity, and therefore the distributions are unimodal, peaked at the lowest score. At older ages, many are close to their maximum growth level, so the score distribution may again be unimodal, but peaked at the highest score. But at intermediate ages, there are some children with a low growth level, and others with maximum growth level; and this leads to bimodal distributions. 

DISCUSSION 

A model of van Geert (1991) for cognitive growth of an individual has been modified by adding a term to take into account external forces, and has been applied to populations. The parameters of the model describe two separate cognitive abilities for a given task, namely the fractional rate of increase in growth, and the maximum growth level. Two additional parameters refer to the magnitude of the external driving forces and to its schedule. 

In this model, each individual acquires competence in a given task according to an S-shaped learning curve determined by her own abilities. There is only one class in the population, among whom ability parameters are distributed in a continuous and unimodal fashion. Despite the unimodal character of the ability distributions, the theory of this paper predicts score distributions that are often bimodal. This picture contrasts with that of TL, who assumed that spatial abilities are distributed bimodally, and that populations can be partitioned into two discrete latent classes that are qualitatively different: ``good performers'' and ``poor performers.'' To predict score distributions, TL required 3 parameters at each of 10 age levels. In contrast, the model of this paper requires only 4 parameters for all age levels. A function G(s; t) of score, s, and age, t, was derived that gives the distribution of scores as a function of age, and the function was fit to the score distributions of the spatial tasks. There are very good quantitative fits to the data on the water-level task for both boys and girls, and on the van task for boys. The fit was only qualitative on the van task for girls. The growth of spatial concepts is not abrupt, but appears to continue throughout the age range considered. 

The hypothesis that the concepts of horizontality and verticality are acquired synchronously was tested by fitting a theoretical score distribution simultaneously to both empirical score distributions for boys. The simultaneous best fit to both score distributions for boys was found to be marginally acceptable, so that the hypothesis of synchronous acquisition of spatial concepts cannot be rejected. It was not possible to fit both distributions for girls, because of the poor fit to the van task distribution for girls. 

Comparison of the score distributions for boys and girls showed that the differences between them are not statistically significant. The empirical distributions were

combined, so that boys and girls were considered as one group. A calculated distribution was found which gave a marginal fit to the combined data for boys and girls on the water-level task. It was not possible to fit the combined data on the van task, because of the poor fit to the van task distribution for girls. 

There was a strikingly good qualitative fit of the model of this paper to the conservation data of Bentler (1970), but the quantitative fit was poor. This may be due to the fact that the scale of the scores in this case was ordinal rather than interval, and it is suggested that in this case, the model should be refined further. The theoretical distribution is strongly bimodal, as observed empirically. 

Van der Maas and Molenaar (1992) pointed out that bimodal distributions are catastrophe flags: that is, if a catastrophe, i.e., a discontinuous transition, takes place, then the resulting distributions will be bimodal. They therefore interpret the distributions found by Bentler (1970) as strong evidence for discontinuous stagewise development. However, in this paper, we have seen that continuous models of development also result in bimodal distributions. This is certainly the case for the spatial tasks, for which the underlying growth curves (Figs. 10 and 12) are clearly continuous and slowly varying. In the case of the conservation tasks, the growth curves are more abrupt (Fig. 14), but still they are not discontinuous. 

Is cognitive development continuous or abrupt? This is a central question of cognitive development, and has a long history of controversy. The model of this paper suggests that it is not very useful to analyze development in terms of dichotomies��abrupt or continuous development��because growth functions lie on a continuum, with continuous functions at one end and abrupt functions at the other. It is proposed that growth be characterized quantitatively, by measuring the fractional rate of increase r. We suggest that this is a more fruitful procedure than a characterization of growth as either stagelike or continuous. 

The average times required for growth in the cases that we have examined are rather long��being of the order of 2 years for conservation tasks and even longer for the spatial tasks. These long times for growth lead us to a puzzling situation. As we have already noted, a great deal of research has shown that when children receive training on Piagetian concepts, their improvement in performance is abrupt (Brainerd, 1979, 1981, 1982). This result, that cognitive growth is abrupt, is supported by the excellent fit to the experimental data of a model using absorbing Markov chains. The time scale of the cognitive growth in the training experiments is hours, and this is incompatible with the rise times of years that we have calculated. 

Actually, the long times calculated for growth of conservation concepts is not really irreconcilable with abrupt growth. This is because the tasks in Bentler's assessment instrument were of varying difficulty, and it may be that children acquire each of the concepts needed for performing a given task in an abrupt fashion, but that each concept is acquired at a different age. In that case the time needed to acquire all the concepts would appear to be long��perhaps years. However, this explanation is not valid for the long rise times on the spatial tasks, since the test items were all of comparable difficulty. 

On the other hand, the raw data of the spatial tasks seem to rule out abrupt growth. Suppose that the spontaneous growth of an ability is abrupt, say on 

66

the scale of days. Then the probability of testing subjects exactly during their acquisition period would be very small. So, at any given time, the great majority of subjects would either have acquired the concept or not acquired it. The score distributions would then have two peaks, one at zero, and one at maximum score, with virtually no intermediate scores. The score distributions for the conservation tasks are rather like this (see Fig. 11), but this is not the case for the spatial tasks: On the contrary, the scores on the spatial tasks for the great majority of subjects were of intermediate value. 

Of course, it may be that there is no contradiction between the presumed abrupt growth under conditions of training and the long rise times calculated in this paper. It may be that there would not be abrupt growth of spatial concepts even under conditions of training. But it does not seem reasonable to suppose that it is impossible to train this particular ability in an abrupt fashion. 

Brainerd (1993) speculated that ``it is quite conceivable that acquisition might be abrupt when children receive focused training on a concept but continuous when improvements arise from the desultory learning experiences and gradual maturational changes.'' The results of this paper appear to corroborate this speculation. 

FUTURE OUTLOOK 

Two kinds of simplifications are involved in the development and application of a mathematical model. As a matter of fact, one kind of simplification is really the purpose of modelling. The model replaces a complex world by a schematic representation which omits most of the variables of the system. If the model is successful, it throws in relief the essential aspects of the system, those aspects which may be the major determinants of the phenomena observed. The other kind of simplification is technical and is due to difficulties of computation. Both kinds of simplification have been used in this research. We have used an extraordinarily simple model of cognitive development, namely Eq. (4). Yet, despite its simplicity, we have seen that this model is capable of describing the richness of real-life phenomena. It not only describes score distributions at one age level, but it allows us to follow these score distributions from one age to another as the functional form of the distribution changes drastically. 

What have we learned from this model? Basically that it is enough to postulate two causes for cognitive growth, namely the autocatalytic force and the external driving force, in order to be able to predict a great variety of behavior. This is not to say that other variables are not important. They may, in fact, be crucial. But the autocatalytic force and the external force certainly exist; and in themselves they are sufficient to explain many phenomena. 

To enable us to carry out the calculations, several computational approximations have been made. It is to be expected that even better results will be obtained with improved computational techniques. It is to be hoped that more realistic models that take into account the effect of other variables will also be developed and that these models will give us more insight into the developmental process.
