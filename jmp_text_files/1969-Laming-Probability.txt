LAMING  University of Cambridge, Cambridge, England  Both choice-reaction time (CRT) and probability of error are known to depend (a) on signal probability, and (b) on the  preceding sequence of signals. These two effects, (a) and (b), are shown to require a common explanation. A review of  Bertelson's idea of "preparation," which is a putative explanation of (b), is followed by a mathematical model for two-choice  experiments, based on an information theory of continuous response sets. The subject has an information store of fixed  capacity, whose contents are updated after each trial of the experiment; moreover, the store is divided between information  about signal frequency and information about the frequencies of repetitions and alternations. Expectancy (or subjective  probability) is determined, in part, by the contents of the store on each trial; CRT and the probability of error are thus  related to the preceding sequence of signals by an underlying probability learning process. A quantitative account is given of  some previous results. Certain admissible variants of the model and its possible application to probability learning  experiments are discussed.  It has been known at least since Hyman (1953) that choice-reaction time (CRT) depends on signal probability as well as on the  number of alternatives. In a two-choice experiment with unequal signal probabilities, the more frequent signal receives a  faster response and the less frequent signal a slower response relative to the equiprobable case. This will be called the  signal probability effect. Explanations of this effect have usually assumed that the subject knows the signal probabilities  without saying how this knowledge is acquired. Nonetheless, for present  convenience this group of effects will be referred to as the signal sequence effect. Since both signal probability and  sequence effects are related to the stochastic structure of the signal sequence, they must be, at least in part, confounded  (cf. Kornblum, 1967). Moreover, Falmagne has attempted to explain the signal probability effect entirely in terms of the  signal sequence effect.  There is, therefore, an important theoretical question associated with these two sets of experimental results: Are the signal  probability and sequence effects two distinct classes of phenomena, although experimentally confounded, or are they one and  the same as Falmagne has asserted ? Recently Laming has shown, within the generality of his experiments, that these two  effects are essentially one and the same in that the variation of CRT and probability of error with signal probability in a  twochoice experiment may be accounted for by an invariant system of sequential constraints operating on a signal sequence of  variable stochastic characteristics. Interest has therefore shifted to the mechanism underlying these effects-in short, how  the subject learns about signal probabilities.  The present paper presents a descriptive model for the signal sequence effect. It is a post hoc model, developed only after a  careful scrutiny of the same experimental results it purports to represent. It may be thought of as a set of considered  conclusions drawn from the experimental data. To understand why this particular model has been proposed it is necessary to  have some idea what the empirical phenomena look like. Although there are many reports of the signal probability effect and  not a few of the sequence effect, these reports usually consist of the analysis of experimental data for comparison with some  particular theory and fail to give a comprehensive picture of what is going on in the CR experiment. The present paper is  concerned exclusively with two-choice experiments, in which context it is relatively easy to form a coherent picture of the  phenomena in question. Therefore, we begin by looking at the two sets of empirical phenomena in detail and at the way in which  they have been shown to require a common explanation. The very nature of the experimental data suggests some of the components  of the model which follows. Finally the data will be reexamined in the light of the model, to see how accurately they are  represented. However, the label "subjective  probability" needs some justification, and this is most easily accomplished by reference to Laming's information theory of  CRTs.  In Laming's theory (Laming, 1968, pp. 30-39) the subject in a CRT experiment is thought of as sampling a stream of  information. The information stream is stochastically stationary and its properties depend on the signal presented, so that  the signals can be distinguished in this way. On the other hand any particular pattern of information might arise from either  signal (albeit much more likely from one signal than from the other) so that discrimination is never perfect, but continually  improves with time. The subject continues to sample the information stream until he reaches a preassigned level of confidence  about the identity of the signal; at this point of time he initiates the appropriate response. The RT is therefore related to  the length of information stream sampled, and hence to the information about the signal contained in the response. This  process, as a means of making a decision, is maximally efficient, because the average information collected during the  decision process can never be less than the average amount contained in the response, and the average information collected is  assumed to take this minimum value. Because the information stream is stochastically stationary, information is collected at a  uniform average rate, which may be different for different signals. This means that mean RT (the mean over both correct and  incorrect responses to a given signal) is a linear function of the average information about the signal contained in the  response. The Signal Sequence Effect  Figure 3 shows the signal sequence effect in Experiment 3. Each signal in the experiment is classified according to the exact  number of consecutive SAS or SBs preceding it. Mean CRTs and PEs are averaged over all 24 subjects. During a run of SAS the  mean RT to SA and the PE both progressively decrease, while the PE when SB is presented increases. The mean RT to SB however  shows no consistent change, whereas one might have expected it to increase during a run of SAS. A complementary set of  relations hold following a run of SBs. (The continuous curves in Fig. 3 will be discussed later during the evaluation of the  model.) Experiments 1 and 2 yield results very similar to Fig. 3, with the sequence effect somewhat greater in magnitude. But  in those experiments the signal sequence effect is confounded with the probability effect (assuming these two effects to be  distinct), while in Experiment 3 there is no probability effect at all.  At first sight the data in Fig. 3 appear to confirm the finding of Bertelson (1961) and Leonard et al. (1966) that repetitions  are faster than alternations, and to disagree with Hyman (1953, 2-choice condition), Welford (1959) and Williams (1966), who  all found alternations faster than repetitions. But note that the RT to SA and the PE are both greater after the sequence ...  BA than after ... AB, and the complementary relation holds for SB . So, considering these eight data points alone, there is  agreement with Hyman, Welford, and Williams and disagreement with Bertelson! Clearly the signal sequence effect is more  complicated than any of these earlier writers realized. Neither the simple comparison of CRTs to new and repeated signals  (Bertelson, 1963; Williams, 1966) nor the graphical presentation of mean RT during particular runs of signals (Hyman, Fig. 2;  Bertelson, 1961; Hale, 1967; Fig. 3 here) afford an adequate basis for reliable conclusions about the nature of the effect.  Therefore the ultimate discussion here will be concerned only with the quantitative results in Laming (Ch. 8).  Nonetheless, Fig. 3 affords a valuable insight into the nature of the signal sequence effect. For every run of signals  classified in Fig. 3 it can be seen that the greater mean RT is paired with the greater PE and conversely. This is  qualitatively the same relation between the two mean RTs and the two PEs as obtains in Figs. 1 and 2. It appears that Fig. 3  might be capable of being mapped onto Fig. 1 or 2 by assigning a suitable pair of signal probabilities to each run of signals.  If this can be done, then  the signal probability effect and the signal sequence effect are one and the same, and subjective probability is some function  of the preceding sequence of signals. Of course, this is an intuition and not an experimental result; it needs to be checked  by quantitative analysis.  Regression Analysis of the Signal Sequence Effect. Laming (pp. 95-99 and 159-162) has developed a technique for the  quantitative analysis of local variations in CRT and the possible occurrence of errors. The relations between each of these  variables and preceding events in the experiment-signals, responses, reaction times, and combinations of these events-are  represented in two regression equations, in which each coefficient measures the influence of a particular preceding event. The  regression coefficients are estimated by the usual method of least squares treating signals and responses as Boolean  variables, which can take only the values 0 and 1. This analytical technique has the following desirable properties:  1. It yields a quantitative measure of the local variation in CRT and PE associated with each preceding event (e.g., see  Laming, Figs. 8.2 and 8.3, pp. 104-105). The regression coefficients are asymptotically independent as the number of terms in  the regression equation increases, although in practice there may be a little confounding.  2. Each regression coefficient may be tested independently for significance; alternatively a set of independent confidence  intervals may be obtained (see Figs. 4-9 here).  3. It is possible to determine which coefficients (if any) vary under the range of conditions studied in the experiment (see  Laming, Tables 8.1 and 8.2, pp. 99-100).  4. It is possible to determine whether the variation of CRT and number of errors occurring in the experiment can be accounted  for by an invariant system of sequential constraints operating under the variable conditions of the experiment (Laming, p. 98).  When Experiments 1 and 2 were analyzed in this way, it was found that the variation in CRT and numbers of errors with signal  probability could be entirely accounted for by an invariant system of sequential constraints operating on the variable signal  sequences, with just one reservation. In Experiment 2 there was a marked effect on the error scores of the order in which the  subject experienced the test series (p. 56); it was necessary to represent this order explicitly in the regression equation  applied to Experiment 2 (p. 101). This analysis therefore confirms the conjecture that the signal probability effect and the  signal sequence effect are essentially one and the same phenomenon measured in different ways. The sequence effect appears in  the microstructure of the experiment; the probability effect is its macroscopic counterpart. It now remains to develop a model  for the signal sequence effect, bearing in mind that order effects between successive experimental series must also be  represented.  CHOICE-REACTION EXPERIMENTS 89  MODELS FOR THE SIGNAL SEQUENCE EFFECT  Two distinct classes of explanation have been advanced for the signal sequence effect. The interpretation outlined above is  essentially an explanation in terms of expectancy. After a given sequence of signals, the subject responds more quickly to one  signal than the other because he thinks the one more likely to be presented on the next trial than the other. Bertelson, on  the other hand, has suggested that the sequence effect is due to an interaction in real time between successive  stimulusresponse cycles. Both types of explanation have their peculiar advantages. An explanation in terms of expectancy can  account readily for sequential relations between nonadjacent stimulus-response cycles; an interaction in real time between  successive CR processes requires that the magnitude (and possibly the character) of the sequence effect should depend on the  intertial interval. Bertelson's theory will be discussed first.  Bertelson's Concept of "Preparation"  By preparation Bertelson (1961, p.93) meant "any process leading to a gain of time on a subsequent action." In a two-choice  experiment Bertelson found that a sequence of signals with 75% alternations (condition ALT) took longer than a sequence with  75% repetitions (condition REP); moreover, the difference in time was greater with a short intertial interval (ITI; mean ITI  ranged from 105 to 159 msec in different sessions) than with a long ITI (635-669 msec). Bertelson concluded that after each  response the subject prepared for the following signal. Preparation took a measurable time and (presumably) consisted of part  of the reaction process being performed in advance of the signal; it is therefore an integral part of the CR process. It was  implicitly assumed that the subject could prepare for one signal or the other, but not for both (cf. Falmagne, 1965, p. 81),  and usually prepared for the preponderant event (in this case a repetition or alternation). Herein lay a possible explanation  of the signal sequence effect.  Bertelson explicitly rejected an explanation entirely in terms of expectancy (on the grounds that the phenomenon was related  to the ITI) or an explanation in terms of learning during previous blocks of trials (since the superiority of condition REP  appeared in the very first block). Since subjects usually prepare for the preponderant event, expectancy is certainly  involved, and since the subjects were told nothing about the stochastic structure of the signal series in advance, the  expectancy must be derived from a short-term learning process, short relative to the 50 trials comprising a block.  Bertelson was particularly concerned to explain differences due to the ITI. His main conclusion was that "it takes less (time)  to prepare for a repetition of the choice which has just been made than for another choice, so that the time available for  preparation becomes a critical factor." So, if the ITI is short, the signal sometimes arrives before preparation for an  alternation is complete; therefore, for those trials  on which the subject's expectation is fulfilled, alternations take longer than repetitions at short ITI and also longer than  alternations at long ITI. However, there are some difficulties: (a) In conditions REP and RAND (i.e., a sequence with 50%  alternations) Bertelson's short ITI group appear to respond faster to repetitions than do the long ITI group (Table IV, p.  96). (b) In view of (a), preparation for a repetition must be completed before the end of the short ITI, whereas preparation  for an alternation must usually exceed this time and sometimes also outlast the long ITI (because the difference between  conditions REP and ALT persists at long ITI). (c) There appear to be interactions between nonadjacent stimulus-response cycles  (pp. 99f); this is not explained.  Laming (1968, Experiment 5) studied this problem in greater detail, comparing ITIs of 4096, 512, 68, 8 and 1 msec. It appeared  that responses to repetitions were slightly faster than responses to alternations (compare Laming's Figs. 8.5a and 8.5b, p.  109); certainly this is so at the three shorter ITIs, though the difference is small. But the data are dominated by other,  much larger effects. When the ITI is short (64 msec or less) the RT to a signal following an alternation (rather than the RT  to the alternation), and especially the RT following a run of alternations, is much increased. Using very short ITIs Laming  found increases in RT of the order of 100 msec. Here evidently is the explanation of Bertelson's primary observation that  condition REP is faster than condition ALT. When the ITI is long, on the other hand, the principal effect is that apparent in  Fig. 3 here, which has been tentatively interpreted in terms of subjective probability or expectancy. It may be, therefore,  that the difference in RT to repetitions and alternations per se can be entirely explained in these terms, without invoking  Such an explanation of Bertelson's repetition effect requires that when SB, say, has been presented, the subject is led to  think SB more frequent than S,4 . Laming (pp. 102-106) has argued that this is the case, and it seems a sufficient explanation  of Falmagne's (1965, Table 12, p. 113) results. Welford (1959, p. 197), on the other hand, found alternations faster than  repetitions; but in that experiment the subjects had a priori reason to expect the signals to alternate. Williams (1966)  repeated Welford's result in circumstances in which a priori expectancy is not a plausible explanation. Williams concluded  that she obtained the contrary result to Bertelson because she used a very long ITI (12-15 sec).  Subjective probability is one factor determining the amount of information required to make the response, and this information  is represented by the expression in brackets in (1). So any experimental factor which affects the coefficient B in (1) should  affect mean RTs to repetitions and alternations in proportion. (B in Eq. 1 is the gradient of RT as a function of information  and is the inverse of the mean rate of flow of information.) Signal-response compatibility appears to be such a factor.  Bertelson (1963) studied the interaction of signal-response compatibility with the repetition effect. At the outset he  considered two possible ideas, (a) that reactions to  CHOICE-REACTION EXPERIMENTS 91  repeated signals were organized through a different mechanism to alternations and were not influenced by signal-response  relations, and (b) that repetitions were faster than alternations because of a facilitative aftereffect which was independent  of signalresponse relations. In the event neither hypothesis was confirmed. But the structure of the mean RTs in Bertelson's  (1963, p. 480) Table 1 is such that stimulus-response compatibility can be represented exactly by the coefficient B in (1),  and the fundamental difference between alternations and repetitions can be interpreted in terms which mean that (in this  experiment) more information is required for an alternated response than for a repeated one. Put another way, the subject is  expecting a repetition rather than an alternation. Consequently, Bertelson obtained a significant result from his analysis of  variance and covariance (Table 2, p. 480) only because he used an additive instead of a multiplicative statistical model to  predict RTs to alternations from RTs to repetitions. Bertelson, Collyn, De Blander, and Lessire (1967) found that advance  information reduced the difference between RTs to repetitions and alternations. It seems plausible that, when the subject is  given advance information about the signal to be presented on the following trial, he should rely less on information about  signal frequency afforded by preceding events.  It follows, then, that the justification of "preparation," as Bertelson has conceived it, must depend solely on the  interaction of the repetition effect with ITI. The repetition effect certainly decreases as ITI is increased, but the rate of  change is not so great as is often supposed. Bertelson is sometimes alleged to have shown in his 1961 paper that the  repetition effect existed at the short ITI, but was absent at the long ITI. If this were indeed the case, it would be good  evidence in support of an explanation based on preparation. But Bertelson's 1961 paper is misleading here. Bertelson used  different subjects for the two ITIs. He showed that repetitions were faster than alternations for the short ITI group and  failed to find a significant difference for the long ITI group, but he did not compare the two groups directly. It appears  from Bertelson's scatter diagrams (Fig. 1, p. 97) that there was in fact no significant difference between the two groups in  the difference between median RTs to repetitions and alternations (using a Kolmogorov-Smirnov 2-sample 2-tailed test; Siegel  1956, pp. 127-136). For evidence of the change in the repetition effect with ITI we must rely solely on Bertelson and Renkin  (1966) and Hale (1967).  Bertelson and Renkin achieved a more sensitive result than Bertelson (1961) because each subject was tested at every ITI.  Their Fig. 1 suggests that the difference in RT between repetitions and alternations decreases more slowly with ITI and  continues to decrease over a wider range of ITIs than had hitherto been supposed; moreover, the rate of decrease is less when  each data point comes from a different series of signals with constant ITI than when the ITIs are varied randomly within one  signal series. Hale obtained similar results and showed that if the ITI was sufficiently increased, alternations became faster  than repetitions (cf. Williams, 1966). Now Hale's Fig. 1 (p. 137) shows that with an ITI of 2 sec the mean RT is greater at the  92  LAMING  second position in a run of the same signal than at the first or third or any subsequent position. This would occur, for  example, if the subjects were looking for alternating sequences of signals and doing so more insistently at long ITIs. This  looking for alternations may be the essential change in the sequential structure of CRT data as the ITI is increased.  The results of Bertelson and Renkin, and of Hale, indicate a complex interaction between the signal sequence effect and ITI.  It may be that the subject processes the signal series differently when he has more time in which to do so. I think that such  an explanation of this interaction could be grafted onto a pure expectancy model with as great a facility as that with which  Bertelson combined expectancy with an interaction in real time in his original paper (1961).  Falmagne (1965) has used "preparation" in a subtly different way. Preparation, in Falmagne's model, is akin to expectancy and  selective attention, and is no longer identified as a process occurring in real time. States of preparation at successive  trials form a Markov chain. Transitions between the states depend on the signal presented, and this affords a better account  of the signal sequence effect than was possible with Bertelson's original conception. Falmagne's work has recently been  reviewed by Laming (1968, pp. 20-22 and 111).  The data discussed in the remainder of this paper come from experiments which employed ITIs of 1.5 and about 2.5 sec. The  complications of the interaction between the signal sequence effect and ITI are therefore largely evaded, but the conclusions  must be read with a corresponding restriction on their generality.  The Information Theory of Continuous Response Sets  The signal sequence effect has been interpreted tentatively in terms of a subjective probability that is not fixed, but varies  from trial to trial. At the same time subjective probability is obviously related to the objective signal frequencies;  previous work suggests that it is approximately a geometric moving average over the preceding sequence of signals (see Laming,  pp. 102-106). This means that at the beginning of each trial of the experiment the subject makes an implicit response, being  the selection of an appropriate value of subjective probability for that trial. The response is selected from the continuous  set (0, 1) (though possibly only a subset, possibly only a finite number of discrete points represents the admissible response  set) and the selection process depends on what information the subject has about signal probability. The idea of sensory  information in a signal detection experiment determining the choice between "Signal" and "No Signal" is already well known.  The requirement here is for a generalization of this idea to the choice from a continuous response set. The information theory of continuous  response sets is therefore concerned with relating the acquisition of discrete units of information, in the course of  responding to the preceding signals, to the choice of a subjective probability in preparation for the present trial. Meanwhile, a measure of  the usefulness and applicability of the model may be obtained by comparison with Laming's (1968, Ch. 8) results. Laming  CHOICE-REACTION EXPERIMENTS 97  looked at the signal sequence effect by means of a multiple regression analysis, which estimated effects contingent on  particular signals, rather than looking at effects contingent on particular preceding sequences of signals, as in Fig. 3. A  reason was given for this: "At present very little is known, factually, about the sequential relations within a  choice-reaction experiment. So instead of proposing a hypothetical model with which to compare the data, as Falmagne has done,  it seemed better to undertake, in the first place, a comprehensive analysis of the data, without any theoretical bias, in  order to discover the pattern of sequential relations that actually exists" (Laming, p. 95). In the light of that analysis,  the present model for subjective probability has been developed, and the model is now going to be compared with the original  experimental results. This obviously needs careful justification.  1. The model for subjective probability is a post hoc descriptive model. It stands or falls in the first place according to  the accuracy with which it represents the original data. Obviously it needs to have predictive validity for experimental  situations other than the original situations, but this predictive validity is not at present under test.  2. It might appear at first sight that a more objective or stringent evaluation could be achieved by comparing the model with  aspects of the data other than those considered in the original analysis (e.g., with the data as shown in Fig. 3). This is not  necessarily so; it depends on the assumptions underlying the model. If it is hypothesized that the subject perceives the  preceding sequence as a Gestalt (so that, in principle, two very similar preceding sequences might have quite dissimilar  effects), then the model must be compared with observed effects contingent on particular preceding sequences. For example,  Gambino and Myers (1967) identified the preceding run of reinforcing events in a probability learning experiment as the  critical stimulus influencing the subject's choice of response-as the Gestalt perceived by the subject. Therefore the Gambino  and Myers model should be evaluated by comparison with data analysed in the manner of Fig. 3, since for that model such data  are measures of the hypothesized primary effects. But the model that has been put forward here states that the total effect of  the preceding sequence on a given trial is the sum of independent contributions from each of the preceding signals and from  each consecutive pair of signals. This model should therefore be compared with estimates of the individual contributions, and  these estimates are obtained directly from Laming's regression analysis. The data in Fig. 3 correspond to partial sums of the  hypothesized independent contributions and do not therefore afford so immediate an assessment of the assumptions underlying  the model.  3. Finally it may be argued that because other writers (e.g., Falmagne, 1965) have evaluated their models by deriving  predictions for the sort of sequential statistics shown in Fig. 3, the same should be done here, to facilitate  cross-comparisons. Accordingly, the initial evaluation of the model for subjective probability will be achieved by comparison  with Laming's estimated regression coefficients; this com  48o16/=-7  parison leads to a crude goodness-of-fit statistic. DISCUSSION  The model that has been presented here gives a good fit to the first-order coefficients of (12) and (13) and a passable  representation of the sequential statistics in Fig. 3, but it fails to fit the second-order coefficients. This failure is  evidently due to inaccuracies in (1) and (2), regarded as empirical equations. But there are two reasons why these conclusions  should remain tentative at the present time.  The first reason is the unusual method of testing the model. At present only an exploratory analysis of the sequential  constraints in the data is available. This analysis (described in Laming, 1968, App. C.2) was carried out to discover what  sort of sequential constraints existed in the data, and the present model was constructed in the light of those findings.  Obviously a rigorous analysis is required, designed explicitly to test this model, involving point estimates of the parameters  and familiar significance tests. It is certain that such an analysis will yield a fit to the data at least as good as that  already obtained. But it will be a long and tedious project, and it presents certain technical problems connected with  nonlinear regression equations. Moreover, the model here concerns only one of several classes of sequential constraints  distinguished by Laming (Ch. 8), and it is not certain that these different classes of constraints can be analysed  independently of each other. It seems to me better to wait for an understanding of the microstructure of these choice-reaction  experiments as a whole before attempting a definitive analysis of any one part. Meanwhile these interim results merit  publication as they stand.  The second reason why only tentative conclusions are possible at present concerns the a2 and b2 coefficients. These  coefficients were adequately explained only by supposing the subject to consider the frequency of alternations of the signal,  and this  hypothesis was supported empirically only by the evidence in Laming (Fig. 8.4, p. 107). Variants of the Model  The essence of the model, mathematically speaking, is that subjective probability relating to the signal to be presented on a  given trial can be represented as the sum of two geometric moving averages over preceding events in the experiment, one moving  average taken over preceding signals, the other over the preceding sequence of repetitions and alternations. Statements about  the storage and utilization of information are of the nature of a psychological interpretation of the mathematical structure.  Substantially the same mathematical structure is consistent with interpretations other than the one advanced here, and this  particular psychological wrapping for the mathematical goods was chosen because it seemed, on grounds largely unconnected with  the experimental results, to be more appealing and intuitively more plausible than the alternatives considered. Applications to Probability Learning Experiments  The phenomenon that has been studied here in the context of choice-reaction experiments is obviously an example of probability  learning, and therefore the model presented in this paper might well be useful in the analysis of probability learning  experiments. Indeed, some of these experiments (e.g., Friedman, Burke, Cole, Keller, Millward, and Estes, 1964) are but  choice-reaction experiments in which the response has to be made before the signal is presented. But it is not obvious how the  model for subjective probability should be used. In a choice-reaction experiment the subject is capable of very low error  rates and therefore needs to decide a priori not whether SA or Sa will be presented, but to select the correct value of  relative likelihood. This selection process is described by the model. Given the (subjective) relative likelihoods of Sa and  SB the subject in a probability learning experiment might choose the subjectively more probable alternative; alternatively he  might choose between SA and SB according to their subjective relative likelihoods. It will be interesting to consider both  these possible applications of the model in combination with two of its variants. There is a superficial resemblance between the treatment of subjective probability proposed here and the "runs" model of  Gambino and Myers (1967). The subject in a probability learning experiment is assumed to build up a distribution of length of  run of the same reinforcing event. There is some inaccuracy in the subjective distribution of run length due to the subject's  limitations in the integration of information. Moreover Gambino and Myers find it necessary to introduce a subjective  distribution of length of alternating sequences as well. The superficial similarity to my own ideas is obvious, although the  two models are not mathematically related.  However, the potential usefulness of these ideas in the study of probability learning is somewhat compromised by the results  of an extended probability learning experiment by Friedman, Carterette, and Anderson (1968). These authors ran 10 subjects for  5 weeks under a 50 : 50 reinforcement schedule. The sequential characteristics of the response sequence from a given subject  showed significant changes from  one week to the next, and even greater differences were apparent between one subject and another. Indeed, as Friedman et al.  remark, "After five weeks in the experiment it could hardly be said that the subjects had reached a stable state." In  comparison, Laming's sequential analysis of RTs and errors shows remarkable consistency across Experiments 1, 2, and 3, which  employed different groups of subjects, and hence the probability learning in these experiments appears very stable. Any theory  of probability learning must account for the relative lack of stable equilibrium in the subjects' behaviour, and this presents  a difficult problem.  In each generic type of experiment there is trial-by-trial feedback; in the probability learning experiment this is the  reinforcing event, and in the CR experiment it includes, among other things, the identification of the signal. The model put  forward here and other models proposed for probability learning concern the way in which this feedback is processed; they  specify strategies for the integration of the information contained in the feedback. Now if the subjects in either type of  experiment consistently follow a particular strategy, then a model of this type is practicable, and Laming's regression  analysis provides a direct, albeit tedious, technique for discovering what the strategy is. But a particular strategy will be  consistently followed only if it is consistently successful, and there must therefore be some sort of long-term feedback,  which discriminates among strategies. In the CR experiment the long-term feedback seems to cause the subjects to adopt one  particular type of strategy (although there are quantitative changes in strategy as the ITI is increased, and these changes  might be attributable to changes in long-term feedback); this is the essential condition for stable sequential behaviour. But  in the experiment of Friedman et al. the reinforcement schedule was a completely random binary sequence, so that any strategy  was as effective as any other; hence the lack of stability.  In the study of probability learning it is necessary to identify the range of possible strategies and the long-term feedback,  and show how the latter discriminates among the former. It may be that subjects in probability learning experiments oscillate  among long-term strategies much as subjects in CR experiments oscillate, trial-by-trial, among possible values of subjective  probability. Friedman et al. (1964, p. 291) comment that the linear model does not allow for sufficient variability in  asymptotic response frequencies. This failing of the linear model may be inherent in the relative lack of stability of the  probability learning experiment, rather than in the process represented by a linear operator per se. Evidently the problem of  long term feedback and strategy selection does not arise with CR experiments, where subjects appear to follow a uniform  strategy, and it is sufficient merely to describe the equilibrium short-term strategy.  In this paper only data from two-choice experiments have been considered. It is a fair requirement that the model should be  capable of generalization to multichoice experiments. This is indeed possible, in principle, but the analysis of multichoice  sequential constraints is very tedious, because there are so many possible interrelations to consider, and this is the  principal difficulty in the further development of the model.
