On Order Invariant Aggregation Functionals 

Sergei Ovchinnikov and Alexander Dukhovny 

San Francisco State University 

This paper deals with aggregation functionals defined on arbitrary sets of ordinal numerical values in the framework of representational measurement theory. Our basic assumption is that such a functional represents a meaningful relation between variables; i.e., it is invariant under actions from an appropriate automorphism group. We prove that an aggregation functional is continuous, idempotent, and invariant if and only if it can be represented - in the form of the Choquet integral with respect to a monotonic [0, 1] valued set function. We also establish a polynomial representation for these functionals. � 2001 Elsevier Science (USA) 

1. INTRODUCTION 

The term ``aggregate'' has a number of different meanings depending on the particular environment in which it is used. For instance, one may view a social ordering of alternative social states as representing an aggregate of individual orderings for these states. This is how the concept of a social welfare function is introduced (Arrow, 1963). In this paper, we are concerned with the process of aggregation of individual numerical values and call this process an ``aggregation function.'' 

Suppose first that we have a finite set of variables x1 , x2 , ..., xn that assume values in a bounded interval J on the real line. There are, basically, two approaches to the so-called ``aggregation problem.'' First, aggregation functionals can be introduced explicitly like, say, the class of weighted arithmetic means. Then various properties of these functionals can be established justifying the name ``aggregation'' in a particular application framework. Second, a class of aggregation functionals can be defined axiomatically by means of their properties. Then an attempt could be made to find a general formula for such aggregation functionals. A well-known example is the axiomatization of the weighted arithmetic mean (see Acze�l, 1966, Theorem 3 in Section 5.3).
