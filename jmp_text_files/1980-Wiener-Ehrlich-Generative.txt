The purpose of this paper  is to illustrate these ideas with an experiment in which subjects were asked to rate the similarity between stimulus pairs.  The stimulus materials consisted of the permutations of a 4-item pattern with the properties of a dihedral group which insured  the existence of sets of elementary transformations. Three analytic techniques were used to determine the generator set of  transformations abstracted by subjects. The first analysis consisted of a monotonic regression between dissimilarity ratings  and the number of elementary generators of a given permutation. The residual variance of this monotone regression, suitably  normalized, was used as a quantitative goodness-of-fit measure. For the stochastic analysis, cumulative distributions of  dissimilarity ratings were obtained for permutations requiring one, two, or three generators. The idea was that permutations  requiring fewer generators should be associated with distributions of lower dissimilarity values (higher similarity scores) as  compared to permutations predicted to be transformationally more complex. The final analysis, a multidimensional scaling of  dissimilarity ratings, converted subjects' ratings into spatial structures to determine whether individual subjects' ratings  exhibited the predicted spatial arrangement. The monotone regression and stochastic analyses abstracted similar generator sets  for individual subjects, some of which provided perfect fits to the data. Other  stimulus components are derived when needed by applying transformational operators to the basic components. The psychological  importance of such representation systems has become evident in recent years. First, generative representations are  reductionistic in the sense that the total stimulus set is represented by some appropriately reduced subpart and the set of  transformations needed to generate the complete set. It makes little sense to assume that every possible stimulus in some  potentially infinite set is stored explicitly since there may be too many stimuli for this to be feasible. In contrast, a  generative representation provides a finite description of an infinite class of objects any one of which is generated by  applying the transformations to the set of primitive components.  Second, a generative representation can explain a subject's ability to identify and construct stimuli that he has never seen  before. For example, even if a subject has experienced only a portion of all letter strings which conform to the rules of a  simple grammar, he may recognize and recall grammatically consistent strings to which he has not been exposed (Braine, 1965a,  1965b; Segal & Halwes, 1965, 1966; Smith, 1966, 1967; Reber, 1967). Thus, generative representations are creative in that they  permit generalizations to new stimuli.  For every pair of stimuli (a, b) in a stimulus set, there are two relations: aRb and bRa. One can conceive of these relations  as transformations which when applied to the first stimulus will yield the second. The many transformations so defined can be  reduced to some elemental set of primitive relations from which other transformations can be derived by appropriately  compounding them (Shaw & Wilson, 1975; Imai, 1977). The subset of transformations, G, of the complete set of transformations,  T, is a generator set if all the other transformations in T can be expressed as compositions of G's members. For example,  Franks & Bransford (1971) used a generator set composed of three transformations to derive their transformation system. In the  Kolers & Perkins experiments (1969, 1975), there were 28 sets of three generators whose composition yielded the eight  transformations studied. Since, in general, there may be several different generator sets of the same system, we shall  designate different generator sets as G, G', G", etc.  In this paper, we shall consider a transformation system called the Geometric System, GS, which contains the seven rotation  and reflection transformations plus the identity transformation. Psychologically, GS would appear to be a compelling  transformation system because subjects partition patterns into equivalence classes on the basis of rotation and reflection  operators (Garner & Clement, 1963; Handel & Garner, 1965; Lappin, 1971). In summary, we suggest that three basic assumptions are necessary to formulate a theory of generative representations:  (1) A subject will abstract transformational relationships from a set of stimuli presented to him.  (2) A subject will "reduce" the set of abstracted transformations to an elemental set in order to eliminate redundancy. (This  elemental set must generate the total transformation set.)  (3) A subject's perception of the similarity of two stimulus patterns is a monotonic function of the number of transformations  required to transform one stimulus into another. Thus, a generator set imposes a metric on the set of stimuli. The fact that  different generator sets can exist for a transformation system means that different distance metrics can be defined.  GENERATIVE REPRESENTATION SYSTEMS 225  ANALYTIC TECHNIQUES  A subsidiary purpose of this paper is to provide a demonstration of three analytical techniques that can be used to fit  generator models to subjects' dissimilarity data. We require that these techniques discover the same underlying structure  despite wide variations in the absolute numerical magnitude of the similarity response. Also the same underlying structure  should be obtained by these techniques from different ways of collecting similarity estimates: judgments, confusions or  reaction times (Shepard, 1974). Finally, the techniques should be insensitive to random error subjects make in producing their  similarity responses. The central feature of these techniques is that they analyze only the order relationships in a  individual subject's data and are therefore invariant under any monotone preserving transformation of the data. Hence, all  three techniques judge the satisfactoriness of a proposed generator model in terms of how well the theoretical distance values  enter into a monoonc relationship with the experimental similarities. The three techniques will be illustrated with synthetic  data generated from a single hypothetical subject who follows Gg, perfectly.  Monotone regression. Consider an experimental task in which subjects judge the similarity of stimulus pairs on a 5-point scale  (I = maximally similar, 5 = maximally dissimilar). If a subject abstracted the generators Raa and H, and if there were no  indeterminancy in the translation of ratings to the transformational steps of G,,,, then he might respond with a similarity  judgment of "1" to 90rotations and horizontal reflections, with a "3" to 180rotations and diagonal reflections, and with a  "5" to 270rotations and vertical reflections. To perform a monotone regression, we must find a sequence of numbers that are  (a) monotonically related to the data and (b) as "nearly equal" to the theoretical distance values as possible, i.e., the sum  of the squared deviations is a minimum (Kruskal, 1964a, 1964b; Kruskal & Carroll, 1969). The numbers 1, 2, and 3 are  monotonically related to the dissimilarity scores 1, 3, and 5, and are as close as possible in magnitude to the theoretical  distance values (see row 1 to Table 1). In this case, the data are perfectly monotonically related to the distances specified  by G (the sum of the squared deviations is 0) since whenever one dissimilarity score is smaller than another, the  theoretical scores satisfy the same relationship.  Stochastic greatness. The notion of stochastic greatness is best explained graphically. On the abscissa we plot dissimilarity  values such as ratings 1-5, where 1 = maximally similar and 5 = maximally dissimilar. For each pair of stimuli a subject sees,  a given generator model will predict a certain number of transformations required to change one stimulus into another. Thus,  for G,,,, with Rao and H, stimulus ABCD is related to stimulus DABC by an Rsrotation, or one transformation. On the other  hand, stimulus ABCD is related to BADC by a vertical reflection which requires three transformations with G9i (see Table 1).  If we take all stimulus pairs which require the same number of transformations and cumulate the ratings a subject gives for  these pairs, we obtain a function showing the probability of a rating of value X or lower. This function is generated  separately for each set of stimuli which have the same predicted number of transformations. If subjects in fact perceive the  similarities of stimulus pairs according to the model,  226) WIENER-EHRLICH, BART, AND MILLWARD  they will rate stimuli low (i.e., similar) on the average when the theory predicts few transformations. Thus, for any  similarity value, X, the transformationally simpler stimulus pairs will have a higher probability of receiving a rating equal  to that value or lower as compared to stimulus pairs predicted to be transformationally more complex. Therefore, curves  generated from stimulus pairs with few predicted transformations should lie on top of curves generated from pairs with many  predicted transformations. Note such predicted curves can occur even when a subject rates a single stimulus pair with few  predicted transformations higher (less similar) than one with many predicted transformations. But, stochastically, the ratings  for pairs with few transformations must be lower than the ratings for pairs with many. AN APPLICATION  The psychological processes involved in representing complex stimuli are no doubt very elaborate. Therefore, we expect  different internal representations as a function of subjects, stimulus formats and practice effects. These three major  variables were manipulated to test generative theory and the ability of the three analytic techniques to measure underlying  structure. Since a variety ogenerators exist, different subjects could abstract different transformations as generators.  Thus, one independent variable was individual differences. A second independent variable was pattern format. As mentioned  previously, the saliency and ease of using formally equivalent transformations in the linear and quadrant formats are  different. Therefore, we expect that different formats will lead to the abstraction of different generator sets. A third  variable was practice. The task used in the present experiment requires subjects to make dissimilarity judgments between pairs  of stimulus patterns. Since the similarity judgment task involved 204 pattern pairs presented in succession, changes in  internal structure with repeated presentations of stimulus patterns is highly likely.  Method  Subjects. Twenty-four Brown University undergraduate and/or graduate students were paid $5 each for participation in the  experiment. Subjects were assigned in a counterbalanced order to one of two perceptual formats-the linear format vs. the  quadrant format. There were eight females and four males in the linear format condition, and six females and six males in the  quadrant format condition.  Stimuli. The stimuli consisted of a set of 24 patterns composed of different arrangements of four colored squares: red,  yellow, white, and blue. We will let A, B, C, D  228 WIENER-EHRLICH, BART, AND MILLWARD  correspond to the four colored squares. The patterns were constructed according to either a linear format or a quadrant  format: the canonical arrangement for the quadrant format has AB for row 1 and DC for row 2, i.e., D ~, and in ABCD for the  linear format. The format of the patterns remained constant over trials for a single subject.  For the similarity judgment task, 204 cardboard cards were used. Each card was divided into two halves each containing a  different one of the 24 patterns. Thus, each pattern pair on a card exemplified one of the 23 nonnull permutations.  Design. The 204 cards were presented in three blocks of 68 each. The 24 different patterns were first divided into three  subsets of eight patterns, where the patterns within a given subset exemplified only geometric permutations. (See cubes 1-3 in  Fig. 1.) Each of these subsets was used in a different block of 68 trials. For the eight patterns that are members of the same  subset, there are (z) = 28 different pairs. Although the transformational relationship within a pattern pair is symmetric for  H, V, Di , D, , and R,,, permutations (i.e., if a pair of patterns X-Y is related by either H, V, D, , D21 or R180, then the  pair of patterns Y-X is related by the same permutation), that is not the case for pattern pairs related by either R9o or R2,o  . Eight of the 28 pattern pairs were related by either R9o or R2,u . The symmetric counterparts of those pairs were then added  to the set of 28 pairs to make 36 pattern pairs exemplifying geometric permutations. These 36 pattern pairs thus represent  nine geometric permutations, each permutation repeating four times. To generate the 32 cards with nongeometric permutations,  pairs must be composed from different geometric subsets. For each 68-trial block, two patterns from the block's subset of  geometrically related patterns were combined with everyone of the 16 patterns of the two unused geometric subsets. In 68  trials, then, all 24 stimuli were used, all geometric permutations were presented four times including the inverses of R9o and  R211 (36 presentations), and the 16 nongeometric permutations were used twice. Across blocks of 68 trials a different subset  of geometrically related patterns was used but the above pattern was repeated.  Since the geometric transformations form a group structure, they can be handled in isolation because they form a separate  system. Consequently, in the present paper, only data on the geometric permutations will be reported. For an analysis of  generative performance on the 16 non-geometric permutations, see Wiener-Ehrlich, Bart, & Millward (1978).  Procedure. Each subject was run individually in a session that lasted for three hours. Subjects were presented with a stack of  cards containing pairs of stimulus patterns. For each pattern pair, they were required to indicate on a 5-point scale, the  degree of dissimilarity between two stimulus patterns (1 = highly similar, 5 = highly dissimilar). The Permutation x Format interaction is to be expected since stimulus format  differences should cause subjects to see the pairs differently. The fact that Blocks X Format and Blocks X Permutation were  not significant is encouraging since it implies that although there are changes over blocks, the pattern of results does not  change greatly. We must be less sanguine about the fact that subjects do seem to change their pattern of responding over  blocks.  To test the general theory being presented here, then, it is best to deal with each block of data separately and to use only  individual subjects. Which generator sets are abstracted? Do subjects tend to use generator sets with few generators ? Which  generator sets tend to be abstracted for a given stimulus format ? We now turn to an examination of the various generator  models. The better the model, the more separate the curves. We can measure how separated the curves are by using a Wilcoxon rank-sum  test on pairs of consecutive curves, P, vs. P2 , and P2 vs. P, . Discussion  Generative representation theory assumes that a subject abstracts transformations from the stimuli presented and reduces them  to a set of elemental relations which are used to make similarity judgments. The number of operations required to change one  stimulus into another is a measure of the similarity of the two stimuli. For example, if generator set Ggll is abstracted,  then all stimuli related by any one of the geometric permutations are equivalently similar, while if Ggl is abstracted, then  the number of operations involving only two permutations yield the similarity measure. The experiment presented here gives  empirical support for this theory: Different analytical techniques converge on the same generator sets for individual subjects  while different generator sets are derived as a function of experimental manipulations.  Differences in Internal Representations  Individual differences in generative representations. As the data from the monotone regression and stochastic greatness  analyses indicate, the best fitting set of elementary transformations varied across subjects. One subject employed a maximally  redundant generator set, Ggll , 14 subjects employed one of the minimal generator sets, Ggl Gg5 , while three subjects could  be described by any one of several generator sets. Furthermore, even among subjects who employed the same generator set, there  were variations in the coding of transformations. For example, subjects 2, 3, 6, 8 and 11 employed Ggl on the final block of  the similarity task. Subjects 3, 6, and 11, however, performed rotations in both clockwise and counterclockwise directions  since they processed a 270clockwise rotation as a single 90counterclockwise rotation. Subjects 2 and 8 also processed  rotations  GENERATIVE REPRESENTATION SYSTEMS 243  in both clockwise and counterclockwise directions, but they scanned a 90clockwise rotation as a 270counterclockwise  rotation. Thus, subjects vary in the generative representation systems they abstract.  Experimental manipulations. Two experimental variables, practice and pattern format were varied. Both the monotone regression  analysis and analysis of variance indicated a change in the pattern of similarity responses over three block of trials. The  goodness-of-fit analysis of all generator set models computed separately for each subject on each block showed that (a) 16 out  of 24 subjects employed the same GS generator set on blocks 2 and 3, (b) for the eight remaining subjects, performance on the  third block yielded the best fitting value, and (c) GS generator sets provided perfect fits to the data for 16 out of 24  subjects on the final block. These findings suggest an interpretation of the practice effect as a movement toward a stabilized  set of generator transformations.  Configural format influenced the abstraction process by determining which rotation and reflection permutations were regarded  as elemental. Subjects in the linear format condition tended to consider the horizontal reflection (simple reversal, ABCD -->  DCBA) as elemental, whereas subjects in the quadrant format condition tended to regard the vertical reflection as elemental (D  c --* c D). Considering only the generator sets that provided the best fit on block three (see Table 4), nine out of 12  subjects in the linear format condition employed some generator set in which the horizontal reflection, H = ABCD -> DCBA was  coded as a generator and the vertical reflection V = ABCD ---> BADC as a complex permutation (e.g., G91 = (R9o, H) or Gg$ =  (R,,, H, D)). In contrast, eight out of 12 subjects in the quadrant format condition employ a generator set that coded the  vertical reflection as a generator and the horizontal reflection as a composite transformation (e.g., G, = (R,,, V) or Gag =  (R,,, V, D)).  Several comments about the different generator sets for the different pattern formats should be made. First, the fact that  vertical and not horizontal transformations tended to be used as generators (for the quadrant format condition) is  characteristic of bilateral symmetry perception-vertical symmetry is perceptually more prominent than horizontal symmetry  (Chipman, 1977; Chipman & Mendelson, 1979). Second, pattern format appears to influence which transformations are salient and  thus coded as generators, and not the actual generative process itself. Third, it should be noted that the tendency to  decompose relations into basic or elemental ones has been observed under different format conditions for a variety of stimulus  sets exhibiting different algebraic structuresblack and white triangular stimuli illustrating the group I D3 I of symmetries  of an equilateral triangle (Fraser, 1976), configurations of colored geometric shapes exemplifying the group I D4 j of  symmetries of a square (Franks et al. 1971), and with geometrically transformed text illustrating a I C I -{- I C I -}- ~ C21  group structure (Kolers et al. 1969; 1975).  Convergence of Analytical Techniques  The monotone regression and stochastic greatness analyses yielded similar estimates of a subject's generators (that is, of the  metric imposed on the set of stimulus items) and so are converging techniques. In contrast, in the scaling analysis, the  finding that the  244 WIENER-EHRLICH, BART, AND MILLWARD  generators yielded unequal cognitive distances, produced less than perfect spatial structures for some subjects.  There are two different interpretations of the scaling results. First, the finding that for some subjects, portions of the  scaling configuration appeared to be stretched or reduced, means that such subjects used different ratings for different  generators presumably because they consider the generators to be of unequal psychological effect. This may explain why we  obtained less than "perfect" cubes for some subjects in the scaling analysis. Thus, the scaling solutions could be construed  as reflecting systematically varying weights that accrue to different generators.  However, the same transformation should always lead to the same distance, and that did not always occur. Thus, an alternative  explanation, more consonant with this fact, is that there is substantial noise in subjects' responses. Consider the following  arguments for this interpretation. First, the monotone regression and stochastic greatness analyses determine the goodness of  the models over multiple ratings of the permutations and so the ratings should be more stable than in the scaling analysis. In  the monotone regression, each permutation occurs in multiple stimulus presentations, so ratings are averaged over several  stimulus pairs. In the stochastic greatness analysis, permutations with the same number of generators are combined, and both  the cumulative distributions and rank order statistics are based on these pooled ratings. In contrast, the multidimensional  scaling technique uses single ratings of the 28 stimulus pairs to produce a spatial analysis of the eight geometrically  related stimuli. Thus, the somewhat imperfect figures in Fig. 4 may be due to the greater instability of ratings in the  multidimensional scaling analysis.  A second reason for considering noisy data as the cause of a poor fit using multidimensional scaling is that even within a  given generator, there is considerable variability in the magnitude of a generator's effect. If we look at the spatial  representations for subjects 3 and 11, we can see that a generator's effect appears to vary depending on the particular  stimuli involved. For example, for subject 11, the horizontal reflection is more dissimilar for stimuli 4 and 5 than for 8 and  2. Since even the same generator did not consistently produce the same effect across different single pairs, it would appear  that multidimensional scaling is more susceptible to uncertainty in the response generation process than the other techniques.  In summary, we interpret the multidimensional scaling results as suggesting that even where there is an underlying structure,  as in our generator models, techniques that treat subjects' variations too strictly can distort the results. We conclude that  the results of out three analytic techniques generally support a generative model as an approximation to the structure  subjects might be using to interrelate stimuli.  In conclusion, our major emphasis has been to provide a more precise formulation of the acquisition of transformations in  terms of generative theory and to offer a methodology that would enable us to distinguish among different generator models. We  believe that our analytical techniques and the experimental task of rating the dissimilarity of stimulus items are of general  use in studies on generative representations. They allow the experimenter to investigate a variety of metrics that can be  imposed on a set of stimuli and do not require the averaging of data across subjects.
