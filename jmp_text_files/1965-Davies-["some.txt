THE VALUE OF m  When the perceptual maze is used as a psychological test item, the subject has to attempt to link the maximum possible number  of dots, m. In designing a maze, the experimenter has free choice of R and of D. The actual allocation of the dots is done  randomly by a computer and the experimenter finally has the choice of accepting the resulting maze as useful, or not. Clearly,  it is of interest to know what value of A he may expect with values of R and of D. If the dots happened to be laid along the  side of the maze, m will equal D or R, whichever is smaller. Alternatively, if the dots are arranged in adjacent rows,  starting at the base, m will be a small number (with R = 20 and D = 60, for example, m = 3). Such a special arrangement of  dots is of course as probable as any other but the number of such arrangements compared to the total number of ways of  arranging the dots on lattice points is very small. There are around 1056 ways of arranging 60 dots over a 20 rank maze. If  the dots are randomly distributed, any intermediate value of rA is possible though central values will be more probable than  extremes. It is obvious that m depends on D. It is not clear at first sight whether it should depend on R. Thus we consider  the question, given D and R, what is the mean value of m ? DISCUSSION  This paper has been concerned with investigating the properties of an assembly of dots in a maze of triangular outline when  operated on by simple rules. The analysis could easily be extended to dots placed in the rectangular mazes also used by  Elithorn. The Perceptual Maze presents the experimental psychologist with a tool of great  380 DAVIES AND DAVIES  power and flexibility. The rules are so simple that all but the least intelligent of subjects can readily grasp them. A series  of mazes can be constructed from the trivial, in finely graded steps to one of whatever difficulty may be required. The maze  possesses the property which few if any other tests possess, that of being precisely specified by a few simple quantitatively  controllable parameters. At most this consists in knowing the rank of the maze and the location of each dot but a maze of  desired properties can to a great extent be designed by fixing the rank and the number of dots alone. Thus given some value of  D, we may calculate m (Section 4); from this and a value of R, and via Um (Section 3), a mean value for D,, the "calculated"  difficulty, can be found (Section 1), which appears closely related to a subject's actual performance on the maze.
