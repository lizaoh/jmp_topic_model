JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL, PSYCHOLOGY 18, 140-176 (1978)  Studies on the Balancing, the Minimal Balancing, and the Minimum Balancing Processes for Social Groups with Planar and  Nonplanar Graph Structures  OSAMU KATAI AND SOUSUKE IWAI  Department of Precision Mechanics, Faculty of Engineering, Kyoto University, Kyoto, Japan  Balancing processes of social groups which transform an unbalanced group to a balanced one by changing the interpersonal  relations in the group are characterized in terms of signed graphs. The graphs are given by categorizing the (interpersonal)  relations within groups as positive or negative. A balanced state of a group is defined as one in which all the circuits of  the graph have a positive sign, and the other cases are called unbalanced states. First, we show that a balancing process for  a signed graph (changing the signs of its lines) is decomposed into that for each of the block components of the graph.  Second, we classify signed graphs as planar and nonplanar. For the planar cases, we define the dual graph of a planar signed  graph, and the balancing processes are characterized by specified sets of lines of the graph. According to the  characterization, effective algorithms to derive the minimal and the minimum balancing processes are proposed. For the  nonplanar cases, the balancing processes are characterized in terms of adjacency matrices of nonplanar signed graphs by  introducing the notion of sign vectors. The characterization results in effective algorithms to derive the minimal and the  minimum balancing processes. These discussions are also extended to the case where interpersonal relations have their own  directions.  1. INTRODUCTION  A pioneer work on the mathematical description of human behavior and its analysis was done by Lewin (1951). Reflecting on the  field theoretical approach in physics, he explained the behavior of individuals as charged particles in an electromagnetic  field and established a theoretical framework called field theory in psychology.  Harary and Norman (1953), using graph theory, extended the notion of life space introduced by Lewin and offered a way to  describe the interpersonal relations in social groups. This method was applied to the analysis of power structures in groups  by Cartwright (1959) and French (1956). Also, by Cartwright and Harary (1956), the method was applied to classify the  interpersonal relations in a group into either balanced or unbalanced states according to Heider's theory concerning the  cognitive balance of individuals.  Heider (1946) considered a cognitive field of a person which contains a perceived person and an impersonal object. These three  entities have some relations with each other and each relation is categorized as positive or nagative; for example, agree or  disagree, like or dislike. A balanced state of the cognitive fields is defined as all three relations being positive, or one  of them being positive and the other two negative. The other cases are called unbalanced states; there is tension and strain to become a balanced state.  Cartwright and Harary (1956) extended Heider's theory to deal with balance and unbalance of groups of people. They employed  the notion of a signed graph to represent the interpersonal relations of social groups. One of the main results, referred to  as the Structure Theorem, states that a balanced group is either a highly cohesive group in which all members like each other  or else consists of two highly cohesive subgroups which dislike each other. Their study is important for the comprehension of  dynamics of social groups. It especially explains under what conditions strain or conflicts occur in social groups and also  gives valuable suggestions on how to resolve the strain or conflicts. Conflict resolution is regarded as a balancing process,  that is, a process which transforms an unbalanced group to a balanced one by changing the interpersonal relations. These  processes were studied by Abelson and Rosenberg (1958), Harary (1959), and Flament (1963). They also introduced the notions of  minimal and minimum balancing processes. A minimal balancing process is a balancing process in which any proper subprocess is  no longer a balancing process. A minimal balancing process is called a minimum balancing process iff the number of changes of  relations during the process is minimum.  In this paper, we study the characterization problems for balancing, minimal balancing, and minimum balancing processes. First  of all, we note that a general signed graph can be decomposed into its block components. We show that these balancing  processes can be decomposed into those for each block component of the graph. Next, we classify signed graphs into planar and  nonplanar graphs. For the planar cases, we define the dual graph of a planar signed graph. The balancing, the minimal  balancing, and the minimum balancing processes of a planar signed graph are characterized by special sets of lines of the  corresponding dual graph. According to the characterization, we propose an effective algorithm to derive these balancing  processes. For the nonplanar cases, the balancing processes are characterized in terms of the adjacency matrix of a signed  graph by introducing the notion of sign vectors. Also, introducing a binary relation on the set of the sign vectors, the  characterization of the minimal balancing processes and an algorithm to derive them are given. In this framework, the minimum  balancing processes of a signed graph are characterized by a quadratic form containing the sign vectors and the adjacency  matrix of the signed graph. The characterization is directly related to the centroid method in factor analysis techniques. The  iterative algorithm to find the optimum factor loadings in the centroid method is used to derive the minimum balancing  processes of the nonplanar cases. Moreover, we discuss the balancing problems for the groups in which the interpersonal  relations have their own directions.  2. BASIC NOTIONS IN GRAPH THEORY AND THE DEFINITION OF SOCIAL BALANCE  The following definitions are mainly from Harary (1969) and Ore (1962).  A graph G is a pair (L, V), where V is a finite, nonempty set of vertices (points) and L is a set of unordered pairs of  distinct vertices. G can be identified by the subset L of  V (D V, that is, the set of unordered pairs of distinct elements of V. Each pair x = {u, v; (x = uv) of vertices is called a  line of G and x is said to join u and v. The vertices u and v are said to be adjacent and the vertex u and the line x are said  to be incident with each other. A connected sequence of lines is said to be a path when no vertices appear more than once. A  closed path is called a circuit. A signed graph (s-graph) S is a graph such that each line has a positive value + or a  negative value - attached. We sometimes denote -!,and - by -}-1 and -1, respectively. We denote by G(S) the graph G obtained  from an s-graph S by eliminating the signs of lines of S.  A social group with interpersonal relations naturally corresponds to an s-graph when each relation is categorized as positive,  negative, or nonexistent. CHARACTERIZATION OF THE BALANCING PROCESSES FOR GENERAL SIGNED GRAPHS  In an unbalanced group, some members feel unpleasantness or tension and strain to restore a balanced state. For example, in  Fig. Id, the member v2 feels unpleasantness because member v3 is friendly with his friend v4 and also hostile with his enemy  2'i , but the direct relation of v2 to v3 is a hostile one. If the direct relation becomes a friendly one according to the  popular wisdom "The friends of my friends are my friends, and the enemies of my enemies are also my friends," then there is no  strain in the cognitive field of v2 , and the resultant s-graph is the same as Fig. I a. However, if the relation between v2  and v3 cannot be changed, then the member vl is an enemy's enemy, so the relation of v2 to vl becomes friendly. Also if the  relation between v2, and v4 becomes negative according to the wisdom "The friends of my enemies are my enemies," then the  resultant s-graph is the same as Fig. lb and is also balanced. The above procedure is directly related to the method introduced by Abelson and Rosenberg (1958) used to search for the number  c(L*). They regarded the number as the complexity of unbalance. Their method is also based on the adjacency matrices and  employs certain rewriting rules (transformations) directly acting on the adjacency matrices. On the other hand, in our  procedure, the sign vectors play the same role as the transformations of adjacency matrices and the adjacency matrices are not  rewritten, so that the procedure becomes much simpler. CONSIDERATIONS ON DIRECTIONS OF RELATIONS AND WEAKEND NOTIONS OF BALANCE  In the previous sections, we treated only the social groups in which the directions of interpersonal relations can be  disregarded or, in other words, all the interpersonal relations are reciprocally symmetric.  When the directions of relations must be taken into account, social groups are represented by signed directed graphs (signed  digraphs). The definition of balance in this case, is given as all the semicycles having positive signs. A semicylce is a  sequence of directed lines which constitute a circuit when the directions of the lines are disregarded and its sign is defined  in the same way as the nondirected case (for details, refer to Harary, Norman, & Cartwright (1965)). Hence, there is no  essential difference between directed and nondirected cases, and all the theorems and lemmas and the corollary remain valid  except for some slight modifications in Sections 4 and 5. Therefore, our treatment of balancing problems is also effective by  disregarding the directions of interpersonal relations.  The only modification in Section 4 is the definition (construction) of the dual graph as follows. In a planar signed digraph  S, if there exist two lines joining the same pair of vertices, then we regard the region encircled by the two lines as a face  of S. Recent studies on social balance yield some weakened notions of balance such as local balance, cycle balance, limited balance,  and path balance (for details, refer to Harary et al. (1965)). As they have shown, the notions of local balance and cycle  balance can be reduced to the usual balance. Hence, we can treat the balancing problems prescribed by these weakened notions  by similar methods. However, limited balance and path balance cannot be reduced to the usual notion of balance, therefore we  must search for another way to approach the problems.  CONCLUSIONS  A social group with interpersonal relations naturally corresponds to a signed graph when each relation is categorized as  positive or negative.  From the above viewpoint, we have discussed how the balancing, the minimal balancing and the minimum balancing processes of  social groups are characterized in terms of the signed graphs. The method we used consists otwo parts in accordance with the  classification of signed graphs based upon their planarity.  If a group corresponds to a planar signed graph, then the balancing processes are characterized by the set of vertices with a  negative sign in the dual graph of the original signed graph, shown by Theorem 2. The balancing processes of a nonplanar  signed graph are characterized by the adjacency matrix of the graph, shown by Theorem 5.  From the above results, the minimal and the minimum balancing processes were characterized by Theorems 3 and 4 for the planar  case and by Lemma 9 and Theorem 10 for the nonplanar case, respectively.  For both cases, we have proposed an effective procedure to derive the minimal and the minimum balancing processes for  arbitrarily given signed graphs as shown in Sections 4B, 5C, and 5E. The grouping process in the algorithm to derive the  minimum balancing  processes for the nonplanar case is strictly related to the theory of opinion change under power structures studied by French  (1956).  Flament proposed c(L*) (cf. (46)) as a degree of unbalance of a group, that is, the value indicated by (45) can be regarded as  a measure of balance. Therefore, a group having an adjacency matrix with a simple structure in the sense of centroid factor  analysis is more balanced than a group with a complicated adjacency matrix.  In Section 6, we have shown that our methods are applicable to the groups with directed interpersonal relations. Also, we  discussed the case where the definition of balance is modified.
