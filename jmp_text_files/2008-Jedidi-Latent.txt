People use lexicographic rules when they value some things so highly that they are not willing to make tradeoffs. For example, one might believe that no cost is too high to save the life of a sick child, or that nothing can ever justify human cloning. Such protected values appertain to deontological ethics — the rightness or wrongness of actions, rather than their consequences (see  Baron and Spranca (1997) ,  Fiske and Tetlock (1997)  and  Tetlock, Kristel, Elson, Green, and Lerner (2000) ).

In some situations, people may not explicitly set out to avoid tradeoffs, yet their choices suggest the use of a lexicographic rule. Research suggests that we cope with emotion-laden choices by avoiding tradeoffs among attributes ( Drolet & Luce, 2004 ). We also simplify complex choices, or too many choices, by using lexicographic rules.  Perry (1991)  examines the selection of cases by the justices of the US Supreme Court and reports that these strongly suggest the use of a lexicographic rule. Most of us have so many choices when buying a product — say a car or a cell phone — that we simplify the decision by lexicographically screening the alternatives. Related evidence is provided by  Kohli and Jedidi (2007)  and  Yee, Dahan, Hauser, and Orlin (2007) , who report that a substantial fraction of their subjects used lexicographic rules to evaluate personal computers and smart phones, respectively.

People can use lexicographic rules even when tradeoffs are not difficult to make. Experiments by  Tversky, Sattah, and Slovic (1988)  suggest that people use lexicographic rules although their alternatives are described using only two attributes.  Slovic (1975)  reports that people use lexicographic rules to break ties among equally-valued alternatives, much like the rules we use to break ties in sporting events — tie-breakers in tennis, extra innings in baseball, overtime in football and soccer. Other research concerning the use of lexicographic rules in decision making is described by  Buchanan (1994) ,  Colman and Stirk (1999) ,  Ford, Schmitt, Schechtman, Hults, and Doherty (1989) ,  Gonzalez-Vallejo, Bonazzi, and Shapiro (1996) ,  Jensen (1990) , and  Westenberg and Koele (1994) . Finally, recent research suggests that people use lexicographic rules not only for evaluating alternatives but also for forming perceptions; for details, see  Broder (2000) ,  Gigerenzer, Hoffrage, and Kleinbölting (1991)  and  Martignon and Hoffrage (1999, 2002) .

Collectively, these various lines of research suggest the significance of lexicographic rules and indicate that models for their representation and inference can be useful in the study of decision making. This result is known at least since it was brought to notice by  Debreu (1954) . Lexicographic preference relations over discrete attributes can be represented using directed graphs. The vertices of the graph represent attribute levels, and the directed edges represent the preference ordering of the levels across attributes. This representation is a special case of CP-Nets, which also allow for the conditioning of preferences, for example on the levels of other attributes; see, e.g.,  Boutilier, Brafman, Domshlak, Hoos, and Poole (2004a,b) . Standard lexicographic models do not consider such preference dependence; but they can be represented by linear models in which the parameter values are suitably constrained. A linear model is thus general enough to represent lexicographic preferences, but its parameter values no longer have the usual metric properties.  Kohli and Jedidi (2007)  obtain the necessary and sufficient conditions under which a linear utility function over discrete attributes represents lexicographic preferences.  Kohli (1999)  shows that a number system in which the radix changes from one digit to another is sufficient for representing lexicographic rules.  Martignon and Hoffrage (1999, 2002)  describe a related representation of lexicographic structures over binary cues. Both  Kohli and Jedidi (2007)  and  Yee et al. (2007)  describe algorithms for inferring lexicographic rules over discrete attributes using ordinal data.

Objective and contribution.  The purpose of this paper is to describe a method for inferring lexicographic rules for each of a number of unobserved groups of subjects using data from choice experiments. The main difficulty in using such data is that the number of observations is typically insufficient for estimating individual- level preference functions. The extent of the difficulty increases for problems in which the alternatives are described using a large numbers of attributes and attribute levels. Pooling data across subjects allows inference of choice rules, and the simultaneous clustering restricts the pooling to subjects with similar choice rules. We assume that the sample is drawn from a population in which individuals are partitioned into subgroups, that all members of a subgroup have the same lexicographic preference structure, and that the subgroups have different preference structures. The observed preferences of individuals in a subgroup may differ from the underlying lexicographic preference structure of the subgroup due to an unanalyzed random process.

We use a non-parametric approach to rule inference, maximizing a goodness-of-fit that is equivalent to Kendall’s tau measure of association between input and predicted preferences. The associated inference problem is NP- Hard ( Schmitt & Martignon, 1999 ). Polynomial-time algorithms are thus unlikely to be found for solving the problem. We use a greedy heuristic to identify both clusters and the associated lexicographic rules. The algorithm extends the method proposed by  Kohli and Jedidi (2007)  to allow the inference of latent clusters, and allows the use of choice instead of preference data for the inference of lexicographic rules. These inferred rules reflect a combination of individual uncertainty, response error and within-cluster heterogeneity.

Nominal attributes and indifference among attribute levels.  The assumption of an  a priori  preference ordering over attribute levels is appropriate for ordered attributes like computer memory, but not for nominal attributes like brand name and color. In addition, it is possible for a subject to be indifferent among two or more levels of an attribute — for example, between two colors of a car, or two wireless service providers. Subjects may also have minimum cutoffs on an ordered attribute — for example, all hard disks of 1 GB or less may not be acceptable. Similarly, they might obtain no additional value once an ordered attribute reaches a certain threshold — for example, a person might consider all personal computers with more than 512 MB of random access memory as being equivalent. The following inference procedure allows for preferences over nominal attributes and indifference among attribute levels.

We approach the inference of lexicographic rules in a way similar to  Krantz, Luce, Suppes, and Tversky (1971) , who observe that many measurement problems reduce the search for solutions to a set of linear inequalities.  Lehner and Noma (1980)  and  McClelland and Coombs (1975)  note that the uniqueness of solutions to such systems of linear inequalities is stronger than an ordinal scale but weaker than an interval scale. This is also the case for lexicographic preferences. We seek a solution to a system of linear inequalities arising from the comparison of selected and unselected items in each choice set for which we obtain data. The parameter space is constrained to ensure that only those parameterizations representing lexicographic preferences over a set of attributes are evaluated.

An alternative selected from a choice set can be considered to be implicitly preferred to each of the remaining alternatives in the set, provided we assume transitivity and independence of irrelevant alternatives. We use the term  binary pair  to refer to a pair of alternatives in a choice set, one of which (by definition) is the alternative chosen by a subject. Similarly, we use the term  binary preference  to refer to the implicit preference for the chosen alternative in a binary pair. The simplest measure of fit between the actual and predicted binary preferences is the number of concordant pairs — that is, the number of binary pairs for which the actual and predicted preferences are the same. We seek an attribute ordering that maximizes this measure; it is equivalent to maximizing Kendall’s tau.

                       Typically, we are not likely to collect enough data from a person to estimate individual-level choice models. We therefore examine methods for inferring cluster-level lexicographic choice rules. The clusters are  a priori  unknown. We assume that each person deterministically belongs to a single cluster and that there is one, common, lexicographic rule for each subject in a cluster.

We propose an approximation algorithm for obtaining solutions to the problem of inferring cluster-level lexicographic rules. The proposed algorithm has two steps. In the first step, subjects are assigned to a pre- specified number of clusters with arbitrary cluster memberships, which might be obtained from a finite-mixture, multinomial logit model. In the second step, we use the greedy algorithm described below to obtain a lexicographic rule for each cluster. We then re-assign each subject to a cluster (rule) that correctly predicts his/her choices in the maximum number of cases. We iterate between the two steps — assignment of subjects to clusters, and selection of a lexicographic rule for each cluster — until there is no further improvement in the overall value of the Kendall’s tau across clusters. We implement the algorithm for various numbers of clusters, and select the smallest number of clusters beyond which the improvement in the fit is minimal. This two-step procedure is similar to the E–M algorithm, except that we use the Kendall’s tau value, instead of a likelihood value, as the criterion.

A single run of the above algorithm produces a rule for a given cluster of subjects. We use the algorithm to infer a rule separately for each cluster. We then re-assign each subject to the “best” of these rules (i.e., we assign the subject to the cluster that produces the maximum value of Kendall’s tau for the subject). We iterate between the assignment of subjects to clusters, and the selection of a lexicographic rule for each cluster, until there is no further improvement in the value of Kendall’s tau across clusters. We repeat the procedure for different numbers of clusters. We choose the number of clusters beyond which the marginal improvement in the fit value is minimal.

It is possible to begin the algorithm with a random partition of the subjects into clusters. However, this can result in similar rules being obtained at the first step of the greedy algorithm, and the iteration between rules and clusters can converge to a local optimum. Either or both of two approaches can be used to overcome this problem. First, one can implement the algorithm multiple times using different (not necessarily random) partitions of subjects, and then select the solution with the best value for the objective function. For example, one can use the results of a finite-mixture multinomial logit model to guide the initial clustering of subjects. Second, one can begin with as many different and arbitrary lexicographic rules as the number of clusters under consideration. Assigning each subject to the best of these rules produces an initial clustering of the subjects, after which the greedy heuristic can be used to identify a new set of lexicographic rules. In the application reported in the next section, we use both 10 restarts using each of these methods, and then select the best solution across the 20 runs of the greedy algorithm.

To qualify for the study, a respondent had to be (i) over 18 years old; and (ii) either an owner, or considering the purchase, of a mid-size TV set in the next 12 months. Each respondent received a $4.00 incentive for participation in the study, which was sponsored by Sawtooth Software.

Each subject was first told about the following six attributes and attribute levels that were used to describe the television sets: (1) brand name (JVC, RCA, Sony); (2) screen size (25 ″ ,26 ″ ,27 ″ ); (3) sound quality (mono, stereo, surround); (4) channel blockout (yes, no); (5) picture-in-picture (yes, no); and (6) price ($300, $350, $400, $450). The subject was then presented a sequence of eighteen customized choice sets using an interactive, PC-based program that randomized the presentation of the choice sets and recorded his or her response.

We used this cross-validation study to also examine the stability of the cluster memberships and parameter estimates across the hundred runs. The percent of times subjects are classified to the same cluster (average hit rate) is 92% for the lexicographic model and 91% for the MNL choice model. To assess the stability of the eight-cluster solution, we computed the fraction of the one hundred runs in which the most important attribute is the same for the full sample and the validation sample. We repeated this analysis for the second- and third-most important attributes. Across clusters, we found a perfect match for the most and second-most important attributes, and an 86% match for the third-most important attribute. For example, for cluster one, we found that sound quality is the most important attribute (see  Table 1 ) in both analyses, using the full dataset and the 100 randomly generated validation datasets. For comparison, we used the magnitude of the range of the MNL coefficients within a particular attribute to determine its order of importance. Using this measure, we found a 93%, 71%, and 69% match, respectively, for the most, second-most, and third most important attribute. These results suggest that the lexicographic rules we obtained are at least, if not more, robust than the measures of attribute importance we obtain from a MNL model.

A number of psychological studies emphasize the possibility that in some domains, people may have exclusively lexicographic preferences. In other domains, people are known to use lexicographic rules to simplify complex choices, or to efficiently evaluate a large set of alternatives. The purpose of this paper is to propose a novel form of lexicographic preference structure and to describe a method for its inference using choice data. The proposed model assumes that the sample is drawn from a population in which individuals are partitioned into subgroups. All members of a subgroup have the same lexicographic preference structure. Different subgroups have different preference structures. Due to an unanalyzed random process, the observed preferences of individuals in a subgroup may differ from the underlying lexicographic preference structure of the subgroup. The present paper presents a method for inferring the subgroups and their respective lexicographic preference structures from choice data. The inference problem is NP-Hard. We therefore use a (greedy) heuristic for its solution. We describe an application of the proposed method using data from a choice experiment involving television sets. We find that the proposed model predicts as well as a finite-mixture, multinomial logit model, both for estimation and holdout data. This suggests that the data are consistent with the use of lexicographic rules by subjects, because a lexicographic model is obtained as a special case of a linear model.

The present research should advance the study of group preference structures, especially where researchers have hypothesized lexicographic preference structures. Alternatively, if the objective is to disprove hypothesized lexicographic preference structures, one will need a best-fitting lexicographic model be estimated and compared to the fits of alternative, non-lexicographic models. The present research suggests how such a lexicographic model might be estimated for unobserved clusters of subjects.

An important area for future research is the development of an error model for lexicographic preferences. The use of a standard error model, as for example in logit or probit models, is not appropriate because these require that the unobserved utility function has cardinal (interval-scaled) properties. In contrast, a linear-model representation of lexicographic rules allows a larger set of transformations than are permitted for cardinal utility functions. Another area of research is to examine if and when people switch to and from lexicographic and compensatory models. As  Luce (1978)  observes, it is possible that for an attribute like price, a person might use a lexicographic rule to order alternatives that are widely different on an attribute; but for smaller ranges of the attribute, he might use a compensatory strategy. Part of the problem in modeling such processes is that the measurement scale for such preference structures is not simply ordinal (as it is when preferences are lexicographic) or cardinal, but some combination of the two. It should be useful to examine how one might represent such preferences, and infer their use from choices or stated preferences.
