WANG  Center for Consumer Product Technology, National Bureau of Standards  A suggestion for the prioritization of alternatives using the Perron-Frobenius right eigenvector of a pairwise comparison  matrix has recently been made by T. Saaty. We note that use of the left eigenvectors is equally justified (as long as order is  reversed). The purpose of this note is to point out that the resultant prioritizations may disagree even when the comparisons  are "nearly consistent."  A scheme for prioritizing alternatives based on pairwise comparisons (and, based perhaps also on a hierarchy) has recently  been suggested by T. Saaty (1977). This approach accepts an n-by-n positive "reciprocal" (a;i = 11 ai;) matrix A = (aii) and  produces, using the Perron-Frobenius right eigenvector of A, an ordering of {1, 2,..., n}. The entries, ai; , are interpreted  as pairwise, ratio comparisons among a set of alternatives numbered 1, 2,..., n. If the comparisons are "consistent" (aikaki =  ai9), this method simply reproduces the obvious order, and, if not, an ordering is produced by an aggregation process embedded  in the eigenvector which is not fully understood mathematically. This is the basic step in a process which may ultimately  amalgamate orderings (using the cardinal values of the eigenvector components) in a hierarchy of the intermediate goals in  which the alternatives are scrutinized. The Saaty proposal is one of a class of intriguing and promising eigenvector-based  methods for prioritization as a management tool, under study by the authors (at the National Bureau of Standards). It is the  goal of this note to point out one difficulty (and mathematically arbitrary aspect) of this particular method of which  potential users should be aware. Of course, the problem of prioritization under inconsistent comparisons is a delicate one;  furthermore, the difficulty observed here is not the only one associated with the application of this or of other ranking  schemes. One, perhaps obvious, conclusion to be drawn from these comments is the following. In general, one should not presume a single  ordering to result from input data (unless there is an "obvious" one which would result from any reasonable scheme). Rather, a  range of possible orderings allowed by widely accepted axioms should be sought.  REFERENCE  SAATY, THomAs L. A scaling method for priorities in hierarchical structures.
