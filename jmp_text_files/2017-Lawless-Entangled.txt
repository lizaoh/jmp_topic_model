We will argue that interdependence is much more than the mutual dependence common to social science ( Jones, 1998 , pp. 29–30) or the mutual information in information theory (signals;  Conant, 1976 ). Interdependent states are bistable, entangling speakers and listeners so that once interrupted produce a loss of information from both the perspective of the speaker and listener; e.g., teachers are uncertain whether their message has been understood by students, while students are uncertain whether they have understood the message sent.

If quantum-like effects exist in the social world, we have concluded that they are expressed as interdependence ( Wang & Busemeyer, 2016 , p. 147). If true, although interdependence is difficult to grasp, we should see its effects at multiple social levels. We were not the first to propose this idea. After discovering that measurement at the atomic level of one factor complementary to another affected both, known as the measurement problem,  Bohr (1955)  proposed that measuring a social factor complementary with another generalized the measurement problem.

Much has been written over the years about interdependence (e.g.,  Lewin, 1951 ;  Von Neumann & Morgenstern, 1953 ), often aimed at social choice ( Luce & Raiffa, 1967 ); e.g.,   Hardin (2003)  has found strategic choice too complex to be apprehended, which may be why the true nature of interdependence has been hidden from scientists. Most laboratory studies with interdependence as the focus result in “bewildering complexities” ( Jones, 1998 , p. 33), leaving mathematicians or physicists lost at sea. While we draw a parallel between interdependence as a phenomenon and quantum theory in its level of difficulty, we agree with  McNeill (1981) , “Human society is, however, more complicated than atoms and molecules …”. Even still, despite great success with the quantum theory, after more than a century since its discovery, the meaning of quantum theory remains as controversial as in 1927 ( Krips, 2013 ). The reverse appears to be true for the social sciences; although meaning seems to flow easily from social science, we agree with  Hardin (2003, p. 3)  that the meaning so derived can be indeterminate, the result of a convergence process by individuals ( Adelson, 2000 ) and social systems that excludes contradictory evidence ( Darley & Gross, 2000 ). Moreover, for decades, while validation had seemed to be straightforward in the social sciences, replication is an issue today ( Nosek, 2015 ).

Why is interdependence important? From  Wendt (2015) : “humans live in highly interdependent societies (p. 150) …[where they form] organized, structured totalities in which parts and whole are dynamically interdependent …” (p. 134). Most scientists attacking the problem of interdependence focus on it from an individual’s perspective; e.g., in classical science, Darwin and Einstein relied on the work of others for their extraordinary advances, yet the common belief exists that an individual is sufficient to advance science and overcome the resistance of peers (e.g.,  Kurzweil, 2013 , p. 23–24). Focusing on the individual ignores how humans interdependently determine social reality. Instead of quantum-like cognitive models focused on the individual (e.g.,  Wang & Busemeyer, 2016 ), we have found that studying the effects of teams observing and in action on the field solving problems which individuals working alone cannot solve has wide social application ( Ahdieh, 2009 ). If quantum-like effects occur among humans, we speculate that the effects on teams might be more easily displayed than at the level of the individual ( Lawless & Sofge, 2016b ).

If interactions process information ( Cooke & Hilton, 2015 , Ch. 3, p. 6); if humans are cognitively capable of interacting with others; if the cognitive capacity of interactants is sufficient to process the information arising during interaction; but, unlike classical social science, if interaction creates bistable information in that the information to act (e.g., giving a lecture) is different from the information observed (e.g., listening to a lecture), uncertainty is created from alternative interpretations of social reality. Interactions occur in states of dynamic interdependence (the turn-taking exchange of bistability between interactants) to create a measurement problem for participants and observers that accounts for the incompleteness associated with self-reports. We hypothesize that for insiders in a state of dynamic interdependence, ceteris paribus, cognition is sufficient to process the information emitted during an interaction, but insufficient to capture information about the interaction, the cause of irreproducibility; for observers outside of an interaction (e.g., a scientist, playwright, marriage counselor), dynamic information is inaccessible. If interdependent information in the interaction is bistable and when extracted is incomplete, irreproducible and uncertain, our assumption conceptually constrains teams. To grasp the social reality associated with an issue under uncertainty, interdependence spontaneously creates a debate among observers to reduce uncertainty, entangling undecided observers with both sides of an argument until deciding, interfering with the creation of knowledge, contradicting social scientists who believe they understand interdependence when they cannot.

Interdependence results in bistability; incompleteness; and uncertainty ( Lawless & Sofge, 2016b ), organizing this paper: Under bistability we place the measurement problem, game theory, and predictions; under incompleteness we place self-reports, irreproducibility, multitasking by teams, degrees of freedom and boundary maintenance; and under uncertainty we place responsiveness and teams, reducing uncertainty with debate, conflict, and social reality. But the entangled nature of interdependence makes our divisions arbitrary: bistability and irreproducibility foster uncertainty, uncertainty reduces predictability, increasing uncertainty, etc.

We generalize Bohr to account for the existence of bistable interpretations of the same social phenomena like  Fig. 1  ( Lawless, Sofge, Chaudron, & Bartheye, 2016 ). As an example of social bistability, contrast the two opposing views in the same quote ( AP, 2015 ):  
                            An Indian Army spokesman …said that Pakistani soldiers fired without any provocation and that the fighting was continuing. [However, the] Pakistani Army accused India of “unprovoked” firing and added that “Pakistani troops befittingly responded”.

From  Tal (2015, Sect. 8.2) , “theory and measurement are interdependent”. New measurement practices follow new theory ( Kuhn, 1961 , pp. 161). Establishing a measurement problem in social science would indicate the need for a new practice in measurements for social science.

From a classical social science perspective, it does not matter whether measurement occurs before or after a phenomena is disturbed, but it does for quantum phenomena ( Wheeler & Zurek, 1983 ). For Shannon information theory, measurement states are separable.

Einstein claimed ( Krips, 2013 ),  
                               when we observe objects under ideal conditions we are seeing things …as they exist independently of being perceived.

In attacking the Copenhagen interpretation of the quantum,  Einstein, Podolski, and Rosen (1935)  also claimed,  
                               Any serious consideration of a physical theory must take into account the distinction between the objective reality, which is independent of any theory, and the physical concepts with which the theory operates.

In contrast, the “measurement problem …concerns the apparent impossibility of an objective measurement of quantum systems” ( Wendt, 2015 , p. 67). Quantum mechanics was discovered to account for anomalies in measurement at the quantum level. At that level, small objects are disturbed when measured, preventing, for example, simultaneous measurement of complementary factors (e.g., location and velocity).

Most physicists abide by Bohr’s interpretation as  Bell (1987, p. 189)  noted,  
                               despite more than seventy years of interpreting QM and resolving the measurement problem, the Bohr interpretation …remains …for the average physicist.

After his success with quantum complementarity,  Bohr (1955)  applied his theory to social states to conclude that action and observation were psychologically complementary, and inter-tribal differences were sociologically complementary (e.g., language; culture). Measurement disturbs social states (e.g., observing workers makes them perform better, known as the Hawthorne effect, from  Roethlisberger & Dickson, 1939 ), causing interference and correlations that can be modeled with Von Neumann’s theory of entropy ( Wang & Busemeyer, 2016 ).

However, two game-theory players provide a model of bistability that washes out when the games are actually played. With our quantum-like models ( Khrennikov, 2015 ), we explore why.

A measurement problem does not exist in game theory ( Lawless et al., 2016 ). Why?  Wendt (2015)  criticized games: “classical game theory is …[as] if agents are fully separable …[with] exchanges between …independent actors (p. 134)”.

Lewin (1951)  noted that the new theory of games introduced a mathematical model of interdependence.  Jones (1998, p. 33)  found that the study of group dynamics with games held  unrealized  promise for social psychology:  
                               most of our life-important decisions occur in contexts of social interdependence.

Rand and Nowak (2013, p. 413)  concluded “The population does best if individuals cooperate …”. But for cooperation to arise, a mechanism is needed. In the laboratory, their mechanism to favor cooperation over competition is the Prisoner’s Dilemma Game, a:  
                               game involving two players …. If both players cooperate, they earn more than if both defect. However, the highest payoff is earned by a defector whose partner cooperates, whereas the lowest payoff is earned by a cooperator whose partner defects. It is individually optimal to defect …but socially optimal to cooperate. (p. 413).

But  Rand and Nowak (2013)  hoped that the “evidence of mechanisms for the evolution of cooperation in laboratory experiments …[can be found in] real-world field settings” (p. 422).  Lawless (2016a,b)  disagreed: in the business world, cooperation can be construed as collusion (e.g., see the recent US lawsuit against ATandT, in  Kendall & Flint, 2016 ).

Instead of cooperation,  Smith (1776/1977)  argued that the pursuit of self-interest among market participants in the real world produced mutual benefits, a position that has been criticized as morally impoverished. To counter this criticism,  Sugden (2015)  proposed “a concept of intentional cooperation for mutual benefit” (p. 143). Using games in the laboratory, Sugden suggested that “market transactions can express intentions for mutually beneficial cooperation” (p. 164).

Luce and Raiffa (1967)  wrote that game “theory assumes rational players” (p. 5) who make choices to match their preferences (p. 50), an assumption Raiffa later abandoned ( Langer, 2016 ).  Kelley (1992)  sought to link these self-reported preferences of subjects to the actual choices they made while actually playing Prisoner Dilemma Games, but failed. Kelley, supported by  Hardin (2003, p. 8) , concluded that individuals in a game did not choose as they self-reported.

Bacharach (2006, pp. 44–45) , however, raises another issue about games:  
                               game theory is a theory of rational choice and so should deliver solutions which accord with, not actual behaviour, but …our intuitions about what is rational.

But  Kahneman (2002)  concluded that while the intuition to make a rational choice may be correct (p. 483), it suffers from biases, errors and illusions, and it handles uncertainty and competing alternatives poorly (p. 453). In quantum states, the measurement problem means “intuition is dangerous” ( Gershenfeld, 2000 , p. 253); and  Simon (1978, p. 367)  concluded that “assumptions of perfect rationality …do not even remotely describe the processes that human beings use for making decisions in complex situations”.

Worse, with minor changes in the configuration of choices offered to players, countering the classical approach,  Press and Dyson (2012)  linked the Prisoner’s Dilemma Game to extortion:  
                               [Supposedly] there exists no simple ultimatum strategy whereby one player can enforce a unilateral claim to an unfair share of rewards.

Further, by manipulating feedback to players of repeated Prisoner’s Dilemma Games,  Lawless (1992)  found that competitive feedback made players more competitive while cooperative feedback made players more cooperative. New participants in a no-feedback (no interdependence) condition matched the individual differences predicted by self-reports. Combining these effects produced a significant interaction between feedback (cooperation or competition) and individual differences (“cooperative” or “competitive”), making choices “nonlinear”.

By generalizing complementarity to social matters,  Bohr (1955)  concluded that humans were either observers or actors ( Wang & Busemeyer, 2016 , p. 147). But when Bohr brought his reservations about the static nature of game theory to  Von Neumann and Morgenstern (1953, p. 148) , they complained that if Bohr was correct, how to proceed was “inconceivable”.

Bistability is an instance of mind-body dualism, where “ideas cannot be reduced to material conditions” ( Wendt, 2015 , p. 5).  Wendt (2016)  argues that individuals are non-separable, superposing with other humans into a quantum-like entanglement similar to the atomic level. But when observers shift from bistability to a separable state to self-report, information about their interdependent states become incomplete and irreproducible ( Lawless, 2016a,b ).

Bacharach (2006, p. 12)  assumes preferences help (p. 84) a “group identification process where an individual engages in team reasoning with respect to a particular group if and only if he identifies with that group”. For Bacharach, once each member of a group has identified with that “group as a unit of agency, each of them recognizes the same team utility function as their common objective” ( Sugden, 2015 , p. 153).

But  Hardin (1982)  proposed that “Public choice …postulates that people act in their individual interests in making collective decisions …[however, Hardin tested] three constructs of modern political economy–public goods, the Prisoner’s Dilemma, and game theory …to find out why people choose to act together in situations that the theorists find hopeless.

Using games,  Hardin (2003, p. 1)  concluded that “indeterminancy …in virtually all social contexts …has been hidden from view or ignored in manifold theories”, but the only path that establishes strategic determinancy is when theorists ignore or block indeterminancy from view (p. 6), leading him to reject ordinalist (Pareto), utilitarian (Bentham) and cardinalist (Coase) theories.

The ordinal revolution in public choice theory, per  Hardin (2003, p. 4) , was itself overthrown by  Arrow’s (1950) . Impossibility Theorem, discovered by Arrow while seeking a solution to the aggregation of ordinal preferences. Arrow showed it was impossible to aggregate ordinal preferences rationally, but aggregation could occur by voting (e.g.,  Luce & Raiffa, 1967 , p. 333).

In general, no rational or intuitive person likes bistability, but it helps to explain innovation. Innovation requires a team multitasking in a state of interdependence to solve a complex problem in competition against an equally capable team, forming a bistable competitors ( Lawless, 2016a,b ). Unable to solve problems without risking self-organized competitors that may threaten social turmoil and political control, authoritarian regimes prefer instead to steal the solutions elsewhere. General M. Hayden, the former Central Intelligence Agency (CIA) and National Security Administration (NSA) chief, stated that the Chinese stole millions of records from federal employees for the innovativeness that has so far eluded China. He told his Chinese counterparts: “You can’t get your game to the next level by just stealing our stuff. You’re going to have to innovate” ( Baker, 2015 ).

The historian  McNeill (1981)  wrote that “…efforts to make human conduct intelligible and predictable have never met with much success”.

From a theoretical perspective, interdependence interferes with predictions about behavior in the future. Why? Humans learn at an early age that there are two sides to every story, an awareness of the incompleteness resulting by hearing only one side of a story (e.g., alcoholic denial; in  Probst, Manthey, Martinez, & Rehm, 2015 ; deception in war; lying as a social grace, in  Spence, 2004 ). For the most difficult social issues faced by a local community, when free to speak, citizens tend to compete with each other by debating their issues in a marketplace of ideas ( Holmes, 1919 ):  
                               the best test of truth is the power of the thought to get itself accepted in the competition of the market.

When a competition center that exists to test ideas becomes stable (Martyushev, 2013), we label these stable centers of competition as Nash equilibria (NE; e.g., debates by Republicans and Democrats). When prolonged, these interactions over time produce nonlinear limit cycles ( Lawless et al., 2016 ); e.g., predator–prey cycles; in  May (1973, p. 90) . During a limit cycle, predictions are likely to be erroneous ( Lawless et al., 2016 ).

We first discovered limit cycles during the campaign between Clinton and Obama in January–February 2008 for the Democratic Nomination to the Presidency ( Lawless et al., 2011 ). During that time prolonged interdependence, measurements (polls) failed to predict Obama as the eventual nominee.

What about network scientists? While rejecting subjective data, predictions by physical network scientists focus on a narrow range of physical behaviors (e.g., mobile phone calls, web searches, citations; in  Barabási, 2012 ;  Newman, Barabasi, & Watts, 2009 ). In contrast, (subjective) social network scientists have found subjective distance to be more predictive of happiness than geographic distance ( Christakis & Fowler, 2013 , pp. 43–45). But, these two domains in the same field of research do not converse, a problem our new direction in research addresses.

Making social situations irreproducible, measuring a state of bistability produces incomplete information between observation and action or inter-tribal disagreements.

From  McNeill (1981) , social scientists believe “…truth resides in faithful transcription of recorded words and deeds”. Reflecting its cultural myths, to model how its best teams worked, Google searched for the “perfect” team by interviewing the very best of Google’s teams, only to be disappointed by discovering nothing new ( Duhigg, 2016 ).

Subjective self-reports and interviews about behavior, while ignoring actual behavior, reflects the belief by social scientists that the truth about an individual is what the individual claims ( Funder, 2013 , p. 24),  
                               The easiest way to find out about somebody’s personality is to go straight to the source for her own opinion.

Yet  Funder (2013, pp. 92–93)  concludes that correlations for personality seldom reached 0.40, leaving unexplained variance of at least 80%. If pervasive, this incompleteness means that social truth and meaning can never be objectively determined.

Diener (2016)  found that self-reports of well-being and self-esteem were highly correlated to each other and to academic and job successes (p. 558). Disputing Diener with a thirty-year meta-analysis of self-esteem compared to objective measures “Given the often misleading nature of self-reports”,  Baumeister and colleagues (2005)  found only negligible correlations.

In a more egregious example,  McConnell and Leibold (2001)  claimed that the self-reported Implicit Association Test (IAT) strongly predicted implicit racial bias.  Blanton and colleagues (2009)  reanalyzed these race IAT scores to reveal a pattern of behavior consistent with a pro-Black bias rather than the anti-Black bias claimed. What is a team? From  Cooke and Hilton (2015, Ch. 1, p. 3) , a team consists of:  
                               …two or more individuals with different roles and responsibilities, who interact socially and interdependently within an organizational system to perform tasks and accomplish common goals.

Teams are interdependent phenomena. But in Shannon’s information theory, for the purpose of statistical inference, the information in data elements must be i.i.d. (independent and identically distributed random variables), unable to account for social effects; except that, mutual or transmitted information is information interdependently shared between sender and receiver (e.g.,  Conant, 1976 ). Similarly, in social psychology, interdependence is supposed to be statistically removed from experimental data to increase replicability and validity ( Kenny, Kashy, & Bolger, 1998 , p. 235); consequently, less attention has been given in classical social science to interdependence as a phenomenon. While  Cooke and Hilton’s (2015)  broad-based review of teams is an exception that repeatedly associates interdependence with the best performing teams, still, they do not study the phenomenon directly.

We have known for over two decades that individuals are poor at multitasking ( Wickens, 1992 ), e.g., texting while driving is as dangerous as inebriation ( Strayer, Drews, & Crouch, 2006 ). In contrast, a team that multitasks can solve problems that individuals working alone cannot, a significant discovery in the evolution of humans ( Ambrose, 2001 ). Multitasking by the perfect team occurs when independent, responsive individuals occupy roles that do not overlap (e.g., in baseball, a catcher, pitcher, and short-stop are mutually exclusive positions; in  Lawless, 2016a,b ). Responsiveness among independent teammates helps a team to multitask. With interdependence, a team converts a group of individuals into a configuration that multitasks to solve a problem better than the same individuals working independently. But when they step away from performing their roles, information from dynamic interdependence (skills) becomes incomplete.

Not any team can be thrown together to solve a problem; e.g., a pickup baseball team is unlikely to be able to compete against the New York Yankees. Contradicting  Simon (1992)  and  Bandura (1971) , it may be that for the best teams, personal cognitive beliefs (e.g., ideology, religion, politics) are less important than the physical skills members contribute to their team; e.g., as an anecdote, Hank Greenberg, despite his religious beliefs, became one of the greatest sluggers in baseball history, the first Jewish superstar in major league baseball. In support, Cummings (2015) found that the impact of different disciplines on the success of collaboration among scientific teams had a significantly negative impact.

In our quantum-like model ( Lawless, 2016a,b ), the perfect team, its independent agents interdependently multitasking perfectly, expend the least effort possible on team structure to generate least entropy production (LEP; from  Nicolis & Prigogine, 1989 ), allowing a team to expend most of its available energy on the problems the team was designed to solve, thereby generating maximum entropy production (MEP; e.g., sports teams; science), providing entertainment, or driving social change. In fact, “evolution (progress) in Nature demonstrates …maximization of the entropy production” ( Martyushev, 2013 , p. 1152).

Degrees of freedom . Contributing to the loss of information inside and outside of a team that is successful, highly interdependent teams have fewer degrees of freedom ( Lawless, 2016a,b ). From  Cooke and Hilton (2015, Ch. 3, p. 13) :  
                               …team cohesion is positively related to team effectiveness and that the relationship is moderated by task interdependence such that the cohesion-effectiveness relationship is stronger when team members are more interdependent.

Einstein first proposed fewer degrees of freedom for quantum entanglement ( Stone, 2015 , pp. 238–9). Fewer degrees of freedom means that a team has begun to act as a single unit ( Lawless, 2016a,b ), why information about its success becomes elusive to inside and outside observers, and why a coach for a team has value. For teams, we have discovered that as interdependence increases, it becomes ever more difficult to establish objective information (i.e., incompleteness). This reduction in the degrees of freedom may generalize to become an accurate gauge of not only human team fitness, but also to measure and model the performance of teams in general.

Traditionally, teams have been organized around a division of labor, overlooking the benefits of interdependence harnessed by a team that multitasks to solve a problem a collection of individuals cannot. In contrast, our model is based on the entropy of interdependence ( Lawless, 2016a,b ), reducing degrees of freedom ( dof ) like quantum entanglement. The reduction in  dof  is more or less invisible to insiders and outsiders. When a team, composed of independent individuals, approaches perfection, it enters into a state where the members are highly interdependent with each other ( Cummings, 2015 ), observers sensing that the team is acting as a single unit, like a crystal ( Lawless, 2016a,b ). But the reasons why are opaque to inside and outside observers. This result for highly interdependent states once measured lead to incompleteness, a loss of complete information from the two sides of an interaction as seen moment to moment from an actor and an observer; e.g., the different perspectives of employees and employers.

Boundary maintenance : From  Lewin (1951, p. 319) , a good team is when “the whole is more than the sum of its parts …[and its] boundaries are stronger than any inner boundaries” (p. 319). A team’s physical boundaries are important ( Sundstrom, de Meuse, & Futrell, 1990 ). Boundary maintenance by a team informs us how a team protects itself: to keep a team’s communications clear as members coordinate their responsiveness by reducing external destructive interference; to keep a team physically bound together ( Cooke & Hilton, 2015 , Ch. 3); and to protect a team’s internal signals from discovery (e.g., the coach of the New England Patriots professional football team was fined for videotaping the signals of his opponents; in  Paine, 2014 ). Still, too much emphasis on a team’s or firm’s defenses reduces its ability to function.

The uncertainty from interdependence makes social objects responsive; e.g.,  Carroll (2016) ,  
                            The presence of …the wolves of Yellowstone, not only helped to “keep the world green” …they maintained elk at healthier population levels while scaring away the coyotes that preyed on smaller species.

The information to act (e.g., a political speech) is different from the information to observe (e.g., observation by an undecided voter), creating uncertainty. To best grasp the social reality associated with an issue, dynamic uncertainty spontaneously creates a debate between entangled humans to reduce uncertainty, with information weighed by undecided observers until sufficient for decisions, where uncertainty interferes with the creation of knowledge, contradicting the practice of classical social science that accepts self-reports at face value.

How humans determine social reality is critical to our thesis. Unless claims survive a challenge, courts routinely reject the claims made by individual witnesses ( Albright & Rakoff, 2014 ); children ( Battin & Ceci, 2003 ); even experts.

Uncertainty surrounds complex issues. To solve them, free humans routinely prepare a highly competent team to address complex problems in competition against an equally capable team, forming a Nash equilibrium (NE), the term borrowed from games. At a NE, uncertainty motivates competition between the two teams to unearth information, judge, decide, innovate, entertain or reduce errors ( Lawless, 2016a,b ); uncertainty reflects a lack of information about which of the two interdependent teams has the best grasp of social reality; the eventual loser, plagued by doubts, attempts to transform itself; e.g., after Republicans lost the White House in 2012 ( Edsall, 2013 ),  
                            …a Republican task force released a …diagnosis of the party’s many liabilities: its ideological rigidity, its preference for the rich over workers, its alienation of minorities, its reactionary social policies and its institutionalized repression of dissent and innovation.

A stable Nash equilibrium (NE) reflects an ongoing state of dynamic uncertainty. While traditional views hold that information processing occurs inside of teams ( Cooke & Hilton, 2015 , Ch. 3), we argue the function of an NE is to reduce uncertainty before and after decisions; e.g., the Library of Congress, petitioned to remove the term “illegal aliens” from its subject headings, agreed, only to be attacked by Republicans, “the first time Congress has intervened with the routine relabeling …the library does every year” ( Aguilera, 2016 ).

Neutrals . We had once held that individual behavior and cognition were a curiosity for the theory of teams because what is learned about individuals transforms unpredictably during states of interdependence ( Kelley, 1992 ). But the only way to determine the winner of two competitive but equally competent teams competing to reduce uncertainty requires neutral individuals to be freely able to chose the best judge of social reality. In the aggregate, we conclude that free individuals, as neutrals, determine social outcomes and decisions for teams, firms and organizations, an opening for quantum-like social models ( Lawless et al., 2016 ).

We operationalize neutrals as individuals responsive to the uncertainty created by the demands of a Nash equilibrium prior to making a decision; e.g., a judge deliberating without a conflict of interest or party affiliation; undecided jury members deciding a case; an audience member in a theater waiting to be entertained; an investor indifferent to the firms studied while searching for opportunity; an undecided independent voter weighing which politician to support even in the face of bistable criticism. Neutrals are non-partisans, open to both sides of an argument, holding no conflicts of interests, but freely engaged in determining an outcome.

Neutrals sort through the noise (uncertainty) to find the signal for the path that improves social welfare. In our model, bias precludes ideologues from openly processing information about opposing camps; however, ideologues entangle bistable neutrals in a state of superposition with both sides that form patterns of constructive and destructive interference (quantum) that, once measured, produce limit cycles ( Lawless et al., 2016 ).

In the study of conflict, traditional theory promotes the normative value of cooperation. For example, in addition to  Judd and McClelland (1998, p. 196)  and  Rand and Nowak (2013)  conclude “…a group with intransitive preferences …is likely to have a great deal of conflict among group members”. But Judd and McClellan failed to heed  Arrow’s (1950)  finding that it is mathematically impossible to obtain transitive results with three or more ranked preferences, rendering their conclusion moot (see also  Luce & Raiffa, 1967 , p. 339). Without conflict, how are disruptive innovations found; how does a country weigh the decision to defend itself; and how does an old business transform itself? These questions have not been satisfactorily answered with traditional models (e.g.,  Pfeffer & Fong, 2005 ).

Hackman (2011)  concluded that resolving internal conflict by teammates, not by leaders, helps teams; e.g., at Amazon, the most valuable retailer in the USA, ( Kantor & Streitfeld, 2015 ),  
                               workers are encouraged to tear apart one another’s ideas in meetings …Losers leave or are fired in annual cullings of the staff—“purposeful Darwinism” ….

Resolving uncertainty with dissensus may not be effective when no signals of a competitor with the best grasp of social reality exist, possibly causing conflict ( Kirk, 2003 ). Alternatively,  Chagnon (1998)  found that striking first in real-world conflicts adds uncertainty.

Wissner-Gross and Freer (2013)  found that intelligent choices maximize entropy production (MEP); e.g., a maximum search of the space to solve scientific problems. Intelligence helps to avoid “excluded” volumes. Maximizing interdependence among all teammates improves team performance ( Cummings, 2015 ), suggesting team size as a factor.

Censorship affects mutual information (interdependence) by increasing the size of the excluded spaces that block paths for teams seeking MEP. Censorship reduces interdependence, ending interference in Von Neumann’s entropy that makes it different from Shannon’s. Censorship reduces the opportunities for teams to exploit interdependence to maximize MEP. With the discovery of the mismanagement of nuclear wastes by DOE at its SRS site in 1983 ( Lawless et al., 2014 ), we sought to determine why DOE’s world-class scientists made costly waste management mistakes over decades (DOE’s cleanup may well exceed $200 billion at SRS and Hanford alone, its two major waste sites). To model mismanagement, traditional behaviorism and game theory did not get us to the root of the problem, the collapse of competitive interdependence in the field by the managers in charge of DOE’s scientists. Both paradigms were unable to address the warnings of  Lilienthal (1963) , the first Chair of the Atomic Energy Commission, that nuclear scientists funded by management could be corrupted into supporting, correctly as it turned out, the many false claims made by DOE to the US Congress that the environment was fully protected, and that DOE’s plant workers and the public were safe.

In this section, we first concentrate on mergers. Our thesis from  Bohr (1955) ,  Lewin (1951)  and  Smith (1776)  is that interdependence is a resource best exploited by humans when fewer excluded volumes exist ( Wissner-Gross & Freer, 2013 ), suggesting that the free movement of labor and capital helps right-sized teams to seek MEP.

Team size determines its division of a problem’s labors into smaller parts such that “more hands make light work” ( Cooke & Hilton, 2015 , Ch. 1, p. 13). However, Cooke and Hilton conclude the optimal size of a team is unknown. Excessive size may be an impediment to effective collaboration. The larger the group, the greater the likelihood of social loafing and the more effort required to coordinate activities, increasing Shannon entropy, reducing effectiveness.

We hypothesize that a correctly sized team maintains a state of interdependence among all of its members; where interdependence means responsiveness, a team should be constituted of the minimum number of teammates that allows the team to perform its mission.

As businesses grow larger naturally or with mergers, they become more stable ( Lawless et al., 2010 ). Mergers reduce a team’s vulnerability to market perturbations. Churches merge ( Tomberlin, 2015 ). Families merge ( Smith, 2016 ). Newspapers merge ( Clark, 2015 ). Cities merge ( Editorial, 2015 ). Schools merge (e.g.,  Maher, 2015 ). Mergers may reduce competition in stable markets (e.g.,  Levine-Weinberg, 2016 ); or declining markets (e.g.,  Freedberg, 2015 ). Mergers can increase competition against superior competitors (e.g.,   Merle & Johnson, 2016 ). And mergers can open markets to new competitors ( Carew, Raice, & Henning, 2016 ).

For our purposes, mergers can also improve a team’s fitness in a market ( Andrade & Stafford, 1999 ). As an example of two firms fitting together ( R&O, 2016c ),  
                               History shows that mergers can frequently cause job losses as the firms eliminate supposedly redundant positions and consolidate operations …. 
                             When two groups merge, they “cut costs” by reducing redundancy (e.g.,   Herman, 2015 ); similarly, when a crystal forms, it gives off energy. When groups are poorly run, or when a new merger fails, spinoffs occur (e.g.,   Mattioli, Benoit, & Fitzgerald, 2016 ), increasing entropy and energy. This similarity between mergers and crystals in the way that teams form and break apart follows the second law of thermodynamics.

Hackman ( Coutu, 2009 ) concluded that the job of leadership is to nourish a team’s competitiveness by, if necessary, brutally replacing poorly fitted team members with teammates more skilled at making contributions to the team. Even still,  Kotter (2007)  concluded that only about 30% of businesses are successful in transforming their teams. It can be done. During World War II, Henry J. Kaiser used “Competition among Kaiser teams to produce the most ships at the fastest pace …” ( Cushing, 2014 ).

A key assumption underlying the formation of interdisciplinary science teams is that the inclusion of individuals with diverse perspectives will lead to breakthroughs that might not be achieved by more homogeneous groups (e.g.,  Fiore, 2008 ). But  Cummings (2015)  found that diversity negatively impacted the productivity of science teams. We speculate that the adverse impact of interdisciplinary teams is an example of redundancy impeding the interdependence that spontaneously arises in teams.

First, the vast majority of business mergers fail ( Christensen, Alton, Rising, & Waldeck, 2011 ) because their fit does not improve market competitiveness ( Andrade & Stafford, 1999 ). However, we have found a similarity between merger formation by reducing extra workers compared to crystals that release energy when forming, qualitatively simulating the second law of thermodynamics for the way that teams form (releasing energy) and break apart (requiring energy).

Second, for relative entropy, based on the results that compare, for example, PetroChina’s employee counts (548,000), we see that Exxon’s significantly fewer employees (82,000) to produce about the same amount of oil is more efficient. Compared to PetroChina, teams at Exxon appear to be fully exposed to the vicissitudes of the marketplace and the public, a clear sign of interdependence. The same cannot be said of redundancy at PetroChina, with its many times greater number of employees, indicating inefficiency.

If supported, our finding also contradicts subjective social network theory; e.g., ( Centola & Macy, 2007 , p. 716): “As redundancy increases, the network becomes more efficient …” Instead, we have found that redundancy subverts interdependence, making teams less efficient; e.g., for the lack of “economic efficiency” in China ( Denyer, 2016 ). As yet another example, Global Firepower ( GF, 2016 ) has ranked the military productivity of Iran, an autocratic theocracy with 545,000 front-line troops, at 21st, much less effective than the military productivity of Israel, with 160,000 front-line troops, ranked 16th, a constitutional democratic republic. Redundancy in Iran suggests an impediment to interdependence and a potential source of corruption.

Compared to free-market economies, we have found that authoritarian regimes and isolated bureaucracies are unable to reach the levels of MEP by having improperly sized teams unable to solve the problems that they have deemed important ( Lawless, 2016a,b ). We suspect that redundancy breaks down the effects of interdependencies (responsiveness) on teams, converting some teammates into featherbedders.

Setting decisions aside, letting LEP represent the ground state for the structure of a team, one aspect of emotion may be represented by a team’s excited state. For example, by spending the minimum energy on the structure of its relationship, the perfect husband–wife team is largely able to devote the balance of its available energy to address the problems it faces in life. By way of contrast, a husband–wife team getting divorced is expending most of its available energy on ripping apart the structure of its team, leaving little energy to solve the problems that they encounter in life. This thinking has led us to conclude that a computational model of team emotion for teams based on first principles is possible.

The study of the individual by social and psychological scientists has produced misleading information for society ( Ahdieh, 2009 ), namely, that the value of cooperation supposedly outweighs competition (e.g.,  Axelrod, 1984 , p. 7), which we have contradicted (cooperation between business competitors can be construed as collusion). Instead, the fundamental limits of worldviews constrained by opposing checks and balances organize social systems around competition to control social elements better than can the cooperation enforced by authoritarians, bureaucracies and monopolies (e.g., corruption in China; see  Talley & Peker, 2015 ; for competition’s improvement of DOE’s prior governance, see  Lawless et al., 2014 ).

Quantum-like models based on interdependence reinforce the value of free market and liberal republican processes that constrain the power of social leaders (e.g., governing with checks and balances; the practice of competitive science; and the adversarial prosecution of evidence in the courts) while restricting the speculated value of enforced cooperation (e.g., consensus-seeking; socialism; collectivism). In addition, metrics based on the quantum-like mathematical physics of teams may improve social welfare by reducing human error ( Mittu et al., 2016 ).

Interdependence forms a Shannon channel to communicate, coordinate, or block interaction ( Conant, 1976 ). But interdependence is more. It is responsiveness ( Carroll, 2016 ); humans can either observe an actor in action or be the actor that acts ( Bohr, 1955 ). Moreover, information extracted from the interaction is bistable, uncertain and irreproducible.

An alternative interpretation: Losing cognitive bandwidth in an interaction might explain incompleteness. For the average person engaged in a taxing conversation (e.g., with Kissinger, the political scientist, or Milton Freeman, the economist), it likely would be a struggle to keep up. Compare that with being immersed in conservation with a long-time colleague who says something with which the listener disagrees; while speaking, instead of listening, the listener could rehearse what to say when the listener’s turn comes up, reducing the ability to listen, accounting for incompleteness.

But, based on interdependence, we have also challenged  Tetlock and Gardner’s (2015)  claim that practice makes for better forecasts, an erroneous recommendation for humans and teams in states of interdependence. Neither Tetlock–Gardner nor game theorists have recognized that interdependence creates a measurement problem that interferes with their models ( Lawless et al., 2016 ). Our model recognizes that the rational recovery of meaning from interdependent situations is not possible, accounting directly for the long-standing difficulty with modeling the mathematical physics of teams ( Lawless, 2016a,b ); in addition to how often polls fail to predict competitive races, witness how hard it is for a jury to make its decision when the defense and prosecuting attorneys are equally competent ( Freer & Perdue, 1996 ).

Our equations capture the bistability in social settings arising between social consensus and dissensus (e.g., political, judicial, scientific). For social dynamics, we claim that the movement of neutrals interdependent with both sides, like quantum entanglement, allows neutrals to process both sides of an issue. Neutrals not only moderate conflict, they can decide elections, the competition for them generating non-linear limit cycles that interfere with rational forecasts ( Lawless et al., 2016 ).

Also, guided by the mathematical physics for our theory of teams, we are heartened by our research results for team redundancy, an encouragement for future research into a metric able to measure the performance of teams at different levels of performance at vastly different scales (e.g., scientific teams, bureaucracies, mergers). From it, the unchecked inefficiencies of illiberal governance, such as the autocracy in China or Cuba, preclude the levels of MEP attained by free-market economies, impeded by the redundancy in the much greater size of teams in autocracies addressing targeted problems. By right-sizing teams, the MEP directed at solving problems in a society able to freely self-organize its labor and capital is denser ( Lawless, 2016a,b ).

For teams, structure is critical to success. For a perfect team, the less entropy produced by the structure of a team, the more entropy that can be generated for its mission, producing maximum entropy ( Lawless, 2016a,b ). What is now better known is the characteristic of right-sized teams. We suspect that a phase shift occurs as team size shifts from undersized to right-sized as the last member is added. Based on  Landers and Pirozzolo (1990) , mergers and our right-sizing results, we speculate entropy reduces with the last, well-fitted addition to a team, paralleling what happens in a crystal.

We also suspect that emotions may serve to confirm our hunch about the second law of thermodynamics in the formation and dissolution of teams. By applying set-point theory to groups ( Brickman, Coates, & Janoff-Bulman, 1978 ), the better and more stable a team performs, the harder it is to understand why; this accounts for the finding by  Zajonc (1998, p. 619)  that expertise is otherwise non-obvious beyond the physical display of skills Not so for divorce; e.g., regarding Brexit ( Erlanger & Bilefsky, 2016 ):  
                         With British politics in turmoil, there were already clear indications on Saturday of a tense and bickering divorce from the European Union …The sense of shock was …acute in London …which reacted …with anger, disappointment and even tears.

To summarize, as forecasted by  Wendt (2015, p. 34) , our quantum-like model of interdependence has discovered several novel solutions for perfect team performance, dismal team performance, and the right size of teams, heretofore, an open problem ( Cooke & Hilton, 2015 , Ch. 1, p. 13). Hopefully in the future, it will lead us to a new model of emotion. Our novel findings advance the science of teams, provide a metric of team performance, and offer a general model of interdependence. For  Wendt (2015) , the mind-body problem is about “how to understand the relationship between mental states, which are subjective, and brain states, which are objective (p. 14)”. However,  Pinker (2007)  warned that if scientists are able to solve the mystery of the mind-body problem, it may “undermine the notion that we are free agents responsible for our choices”.

While interdependence is traditionally described as mutual dependence (e.g.,  Jones, 1998 , pp. 29–30), in competition against opponents its entangled nature becomes the strategic choices that cause social indeterminacy ( Hardin, 2003 ). But interdependence is much more. If we treat the flow of information between social objects as complementary ( Bohr, 1955 ), bistability arises between the subjective self and its physical actor; team members; and tribes, causing incomplete interpretations, irreproducible interactions and uncertain outcomes, casting doubt on Pinker’s warning Interdependence allows teams to multitask but not individuals ( Lawless, 2016a,b ). If bistable emotions display the stickiness, length and valence of interactions then as  Zajonc (1998, p. 619)  has found emotions and self-reports should and do correlate poorly; and that deaf subjects were more emotional to spoken texts than normal subjects, indicating that skill is less emotional than its lack; e.g., compare calm experts to emotional novices ( Landers & Pirozzolo, 1990 ). Instead of the interaction as mystery ( Jones, 1998 ), we have shown that excess entropy indicates a team’s failure (e.g., Samsung’s  Note  7; in  Sang-Hun, 2016 ); that the diffusion of entropy can mask a team’s malignant size (e.g., the control of teams by China’s Communist Party; in  Buckley, 2016 ); that the reduction of entropy signifies a successfully assembled team; and that a team’s success in competition is met by the absence of entropy (e.g., Apple; in  Rosoff, 2016 ). With our quantum-like models, the mind-body problem in the form of interdependence is the primary social resource that free humans exploit to innovate, evolve and improve social welfare.

For future research on teams and the second law of thermodynamics, we hypothesize that the least redundancy to achieve a full state of interdependence maximizes productivity.
