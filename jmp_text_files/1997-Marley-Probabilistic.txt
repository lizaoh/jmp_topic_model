Probabilistic Choice as a Consequence of Nonlinear (Sub) Optimization* 

A. A. J. Marley 

Department of Psychology, McGill University 

Several researchers have convincingly argued that much probabilistic choice could be the result of (attempted) maximization of some utility function that is nonlinear in the choice probabilities. Others researchers have developed descriptive utility theories that are nonlinear in event probabilities. The present work combines these two viewpoints and motivates utility theories that can be nonlinear in either one or both of choice and event probabilities. The presented theories are based on the idea that most choice situations involve multiple incompatible goals which have to somehow be combined in reaching a decision. In particular, I assume that the decision maker is faced with a vector optimization problem which is converted to a scalar optimization problem, the latter being solved to yield a noninferior solution to the original vector optimization problem. I show that in certain special cases this approach yields the same utility representations as certain other recent nonlinear utility theories. ] 1997 Academic Press 

1. INTRODUCTION 

Consider two objects of choice A and B, where you prefer A to B according to some criterion. Now suppose that you are offered a mixture of A and B where A occurs with probability p, B with probability 1&p not equal to 0 or 1. Will you ever prefer the mixture of A and B to each of A and B separately? I suggest in this paper, as do various data, that you might, even though it is also possible to give what appear to be plausible reasons why you should not. I first introduce notation in which to formulate the above ideas, then present arguments as to why one should never prefer the mixture to its components, then present counter-arguments in support of choosing the mixture over its components. The body of the chapter then develops theories in which such mixtures may reasonably be preferred to each of their components.

COMPOUND-BETWEENNESS: 

If A o B then Ao ( p, A; 1&p, B) o B for all p # (0,1). 

Why is this plausible? One argument is that if the event with probability p occurs, then you will receive A, whereas if the event with probability 1&p occurs you will receive B which is less preferred than A. Thus, provided p # (0, 1), it seems ``logical'' that you should prefer A to the p-mixture of A and B, which in turn should be preferred to receiving B for sure. However, when A and B are lotteries��discrete probability distributions over money outcomes��people do not always satisfy this condition (Camerer and Ho, 1994). The ``violations'' when the lotteries are presented in the above compound form are significantly less than when they are presented in a reduced form��see Camerer and Ho (1994) and the other literature that they discuss for details. Nonetheless, in attempts to generalize classical expected utility theory in directions that are more descriptive of human data, consideration is often given to weakening betweenness conditions similar to that just presented (Camerer and Ho, 1991; Chew, Epstein and Segal, 1991; Segal, 1994). 

In the above discussion, I implicitly assumed that the probability p in the p-mixture ( p, A: 1&p, B) was fixed ��with no discussion of by whom. But now consider the possibility that p can be varied and, in fact, selected by you, the chooser. Again I ask whether, when you prefer A to B, there will ever be a case where for some p # (0, 1) you will prefer the p mixture ( p, A; 1&p, B) to each of A and B. A logic similar to that already presented suggests that you should never prefer the p-mixture (Segal, 1994). Whichever randomization technique you use, with probability p it selects outcome A, which fits your preference for A over B; with probability 1&p it selects B, which you prefer less than A. Thus it appears that when the latter event occurs, you should (if possible) override it and continue to choose A. So according to this ``logic'' you will never prefer ( p, A; 1&p, B) to each of A and B. Nonetheless, people do appear

Thus people violate betweenness axioms for mixtures and they choose probabilistically. So there is at least a need for descriptive theories that can fit these results. In this paper, I discuss such theories, but also show that such behavior might also make normative sense in certain cases. 

Consider the following motivating example. You are single and frequently eat in restaurants. You have eclectic tastes that include a desire for spicy, subtle, rich entre�es, and a diversity of desserts. Now various of these tastes can be satisfied by Indian, Italian, or French foods, but no single style of food simultaneously satisfies all your tastes. Thus you might solve your problem by visiting different types of restaurants on different occasions��that is, you will prefer a mixture to any single component of the mixture. But now suppose you are asked (forced) to state a preference order over the actual restaurants. Focusing on just your selection of the ``best'' restaurant, you may interpret this task in one of several ways: which restaurant would I like to go to next, given my recent history of restaurant visits; which restaurant, if I had to, would I choose to go to every time I eat out; which restaurant would I (or do I) go to the most frequently. Note that the ``basic'' preference order over the restaurants is in this interpretation derivative, and perhaps more suspect in terms of its reliability, than the actual choices in the mixture. (Segal, 1994, discusses similar issues of interpretation). 

In summary, the probabilistic mixture allows you to come closer to satisfying a pattern of multiple goals or criteria than you could by deterministically selecting one of the options (all the time). The above example assumed choices among certain outcomes��that is, the quality of the various restaurants, or the tastes that they satisfy, did not vary over visits. Clearly, including such variability ``simply'' adds to the complexity of the situation and the arguments for probabilistic choice continue to hold. 

However, how about choices between lotteries over amounts of money? Are there similar arguments that would lead to probabilistic choice? I believe that there are, and they are discussed in a preliminary manner in this chapter. The basic intuition in this case is that there might again be multiple criteria or goals that you set yourself when choosing repeatedly amongst a set of lotteries: you may wish to maximize your probability of receiving more than some fixed amount; you may wish to gain as much on average as possible; you may wish to minimize your probability of ``ruin'' defined in terms of loosing more than a specified amount. As in the restaurant example, there may be no single lottery in the available set that allows you to simultaneously achieve all of these goals, but a suitable 

``mixture'' might bring you ``closer'' to simultaneously achieving all of the goals than does the deterministic choice of the same lottery every time. Also, as in the restaurant example, there will be many possible interpretations of any ordering you give over the individual lotteries (Segal, 1994). Finally, the above focused on why you might prefer a mixture of several lotteries when that mixture is determined by your own choices. It should be quite clear, and is discussed later, that similar reasons might lead you to select a mixture of two lotteries over either of its components when that choice is presented to you. That is, returning to the initial example, there are clear reasons why your choices might not satisfy compound betweenness. 

In many choice situations there are ideal or referent levels that can be thought as the goals in that situation. These goals may be entirely determined by the context��for instance, when choosing between the members of a set of lotteries, you might (unrealistically) set the goal of always receiving the maximum amount that is possible in the selected lottery. Or the goals may be set independent of the context��for instance, you may set the goal of making a million dollars in the next year. In either case, it is reasonable to assume that it is the goals, and possibly one's current ``distance'' from those goals, that is driving the choice behavior. Most of the formulations that I present in this chapter are in terms of one very simple type of goal. This is not an essential aspect of the present approach, but it does allow me to make my main points easily, and to relate the present results to certain earlier work in nonlinear utility theory. I briefly discuss other cases��with a more general goal structure��in the section on lotteries.

It will be of interest to study in detail the relations between these, and other, proposals. In particular, the above proposed representations are, in general, nonlinear in either or both of the event and choice probabilities. Such nonlinear utility representations, especially those that are nonlinear in the event probabilities, have received extensive treatment recently as descriptive theories compatible with numerous phenomena such as the Allais paradox, preference reversals, and non-transitive choice (Camerer and Ho, 1994; Fishburn, 1988; Luce and von Winterfeldt, 1994). And, of course, Machina (1985) used nonlinear utility theories to defend the position that probabilistic choice can be optimal. The approach of this paper can motivate a large class of nonlinear utility theories that appear to be quite different in form to previous suggestions, with the exception of Fishburn's SSB utility theory. Much further work is required to develop this class of theories and to study their applicability to various empirical phenomena. 

Finally, all of the above proposals vis-a-vis lotteries were formulated for the case where there is some set of ideals or referents to which current options are compared. This was done simply for ease of exposition and to show the relation to Fishburn's SSB model. However, it is not a necessary aspect of the general model, which can be formulated for any set of goals or criteria. For instance, the goals might be: to simultaneously maximize the expected return and to minimize the variance of the return; to simultaneously maximize the expected gain and to minimize the expected loss; to simultaneously maximize the probability of gain and to minimize the probability of loss. A nonlinear utility function may then arise when the relevantvectoroptimizationproblem is converted to an appropriate scalar optimization problem. 

6. SUMMARY 

I have proposed that in many decision making situations ��both under certainty and under uncertainty or risk��the decision maker would like to simultaneously achieve multiple goals. These goals are frequently incompatible and therefore the best the decision maker can do is to ensure that the choices lead to noninferior solutions. A standard way to find noninferior solutions is to convert the original vector optimization problem to a scalar optimization problem. Such conversions lead to the construction of utility functions that are often naturally nonlinear in event or choice probabilities. These nonlinear utility functions have the potential to explain numerous empirical phenomena such as intransitive and probabilistic choice.
