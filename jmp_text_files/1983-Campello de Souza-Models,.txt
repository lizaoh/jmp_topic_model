INTRODUCTION  Several probabilistic models have been suggested as descriptions of choice behavior, the intention being to encompass the  psychological descriptive aspects and the normative-rationality aspects of the preference patterns of decision makers. The  basic idea is to define concepts of preference patterns based on these models which capture the natural difficulties the  decision maker has in perceiving value differences between the alternatives available to him, his inabilities to compare the  alternatives, and his vacillations in considering these or those aspects of the alternatives as more important. This will then  explain the fluctuations in choice behavior responsible for the great varieties of attitudes of the individuals or groups  involved in real-world decision making problems. We are referring specifically to fluctuations in choice behavior that are not  products of learning or adaptation. The important references here are Luce and Suppes (1965), Block and Marschak (1960), Luce  (1959), Marschak (1960), Manski (1977), and Tversky (1972a,b).  The probabilities which describe choice behavior are sometimes considered to be generated by some utility model, which is the  traditional way to represent preference patterns. The connection between probabilistic choice behavior and utility is based  upon a postulated existence of randomness in the determination of subjective values, or in the decision rule.  In this article we analyze certain properties of the probabilistic binary choice models. We use a result due to Dushnik and  Miller (1941) put in the framework of graph theory, which permits us two things: first, an interpretation of probabilistic  choice behavior in terms of complete and partial orders; and second, a guideline to answer important questions in the  literature. MIXED MODELS  There are some sources of errors affecting the choice probabilities that cannot be modelled by strong constant utility. We  have to be able to distinguish between strength of preference and clarity of perception of preference. As explained in Tversky  (1972a), choice probabilities reflect the utilities of the alternatives and also the difficulty in comparing them. So either a  large difference in value or an easy comparison can produce an extreme choice probability (i.e., close to 0 or 1). But the  scale values of the alternatives cannot capture their comparability and so we must reject simple scalability. The substitution  of one alternative for an equivalent one (in terms of utility) does not necessarily preserve choice probability. The  substitution may affect the comparability among the alternatives, in which case there will be an influence on the choice  probabilities.  We are then led to consider the existence of another probabilistic mechanism which is responsible for the vacillation of the  decision maker about the relevant attributes or aspects of the alternatives. It will be the source of what we will call the  large scale errors. The Elimination by Aspects Model of Tversky is an example of a model for large scale errors. It has to do  with the difficulty the decision maker has in selecting relevant attributes or aspects in a multiattribute choice setting.  There is a correction on the strong constant utility model (see Halff, 1976) which takes into account the comparability of the  alternatives, but in a very specific (deterministic) way, which does not consider the vacillation of the people about which  aspects of the alternatives they think are more important, and this vacillation clearly has a strong influence when one is  comparing alternatives. COMPLETE ORDER BASIS OF A PARTIAL ORDER  In most of the axiomatizations of the notion of preference there is always a rather demanding axiom; the preferences must  define a complete ordering on the choice set. This is really quite a strong requirement. It is easy to see that there are  situations in which there is no basis for comparison between some two alternatives. So it is more natural to ask only for the  existence of a partial order on the set of alternatives. That is, only the basic (rational) notion of transitivity is imposed.  It has a normative character, but it is hard to dispute its rationality. The fact that people often violate this axiom  (transitivity) does not imply any dilemma. One should not confuse rationality with perfection. CONCLUSIONS  One can give psychological interpretations to several of those probabilistic models for binary choice behavior. The mixed  models can be interpreted in terms of complete and partial orders. They embody the two main sources of error (or probabilistic  mechanisms) occurring in choice behavior, namely, the small and large scale errors.  The Mixed Model will certainly not generate the whole set T; in Theorem 6.1 we presented a point in T which is not in SST, and  therefore not in SCU, and so is outside the set of all convex combinations of SCU points. In fact the point is an extreme  point of T.  Even though the binary random utility models represent a very broad class of situations in choice behavior, our results  suggest that we can look for other representations based on the normative-rationality constraints expressed by the moderate  stochastic transitivity condition and the triangle inequality condition. The representations can include, in principle, more  influences than the random utility models can. Indeed we can make a slight modification on the Mixed Model and consider it as  a convex combination of MST models. In this case, based on the result of Theorem 7.1, we can state that it is possible to  generate a mixed model which cannot be a random utility model. All we have to do is to consider several points satisfying  moderate stochastic transitivity and not belonging to RU like the one presented in the proof of Theorem 7.1 (i.e., with  several s's, 0 < e < 1) and take a convex combination of all of them. The resulting point will of course be a mixed model  point which is not in RU.  We do not know if we can generate the whole set T with this modified mixed model, but we do know that we can generate more  than the RU model. The problem of estimation of all those models is still open.  480/27/2-6  ZOO FERNANDO MENEZES CAMPELLO DE SOUZA  ACKNOWLEDGMENTS  The author would like to express his gratitude to Professor Terrence L.
