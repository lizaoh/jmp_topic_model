The principles of rational choice are often violated and a number of psychological theories and mathematical models have been proposed to accommodate these violations or context effects ( Busemeyer, Barkan, Mehta, & Chaturvedi, 2007; Roe, Busemeyer, & Townsend, 2001; Rooderkerk, Van Heerde, & Bijmolt, 2011; Tversky & Simonson, 1993; Usher & McClelland, 2004 ). Some representative examples of context effects are the similarity effect ( Tversky, 1972a ), the attraction effect ( Huber et al., 1982 ), and the compromise effect ( Simonson, 1989 ). The majority of models for the context effects assume a geometric structure for the data being studied, whereby the alternatives are represented as points in a continuous multidimensional space ( Shepard, 1980 ). The present study will take the feature-based approach whereby objects are described in terms of their common and distinctive features ( Tversky, 1972a, 1977 ). In particular, this study will focus on the attraction effect whereby the choice probability of the dominating alternative is increased when an asymmetrically dominated alternative is introduced into the choice set ( Huber et al., 1982 ).

The attraction effect is a systematic violation of not only the IIA principle but also the similarity effect and another important consistency principle called regularity ( Rieskamp et al., 2006 ). The regularity principle asserts that the addition of an option to a choice set should never increase the probability of selecting an option from the original set. The traditional EBA is unable to represent the attraction effect because it abides by regularity and the basic principle of economics that inefficient, dominated alternative should not affect choice ( Luce, 1977; Tversky, 1972b ). The present study shows that if the traditional EBA model ( Tversky, 1972a ) or the elimination-by-tree (EBT) model ( Tversky & Sattath, 1979 ) is modified to incorporate higher preference for the features shared by more alternatives, the modified model can represent various other context effects such as the attraction and compromise effects.

The behavioral foundation for the model modification is the prototypicality effect whereby the most representative or prototypical alternative of the category or the choice set gains a significant advantage in preference ( Carpenter & Nakamoto, 1989; Gordon & Holyoak, 1983; Loken & Ward, 1990; Martindale & Moore, 1988; Nedungadi, 1990; Veryzer & Hutchinson, 1998; Whitfield & Slatter, 1979 ). The prototypicality is the degree to which an object is representative of a category. An object’s prototypicality depends on its family resemblance, where family resemblance is the degree to which a category member has attributes in common with other category members ( Barsalou, 1985; Rosch & Mervis, 1975; Smith, Shoben, & Rips, 1974; Tversky, 1977 ). Thus the more an alternative shares its features with others, the greater its preference becomes. This study defines the prototypicality effect operationally as an increase in the perceived attractiveness of a feature when the number of alternatives sharing the feature is increased. When the less attractive alternative is completely dominated by the more attractive one, the increased preference for the two alternatives is used for increasing the choice probability of only the dominating alternative. Thus the prototypicality effect combined with the asymmetric similarity effect produces the attraction effect. The GEBA model implies that the asymmetrically dominated alternative, while not being able to take any share, makes the dominating alternative (B) more prototypical and thus more attractive than the competing alternative (A). Dominating a close, competing alternative is a way of transforming the harmful impact of the competing alternative into a beneficial one. Having a higher utility than close competing alternatives is a prerequisite for being a greater beneficiary of the prototypicality effect. The analytical results have similar implications to the studies of  Carpenter and Nakamoto (1989)  and  Keller (1998)  suggesting that a successfully differentiated, highly attractive alternative may further benefit from promoting features on which it matches the competition.

The prototypicality effect helps the more similar option, apparently contradicting the similarity effect. The versatility of the GEBA model lies in its ability to represent not only the similarity effect but also the opposite of the similarity effect. A positive correlation between an option’s prototypicality and its preference can be attributed to several factors. Prototypical items are more preferred because of their high frequency of exposure and familiarity ( Gordon & Holyoak, 1983; Rosch & Mervis, 1975; Zajonc, 1968 ), the ease of learning and classification ( Posner & Keele, 1968; Rosch, 1973 ), and their being coded by mental representations capable of greater activation ( Martindale & Moore, 1988 ). The ability of a prototypical object to represent the category as a whole may have some information value in and of itself ( Rosch, 1978 ). People may think that choosing a prototypical option is more justifiable or less likely to give rise to regret ( Bell, 1982; Shafir, Simonson, & Tversky, 1993; Simonson, 1989 ). In addition, people’s loss aversive tendency can also be an explanation for the higher preferences for prototypical options ( Simonson, 1989; Tversky & Simonson, 1993 ). In this regard, the prototypicality-based account does not contradict the traditional explanations that the attraction and compromise effects can be attributed to the loss aversion effect, high justifiability of prototypical options, or people’ tendency for minimizing maximum loss (regret). Considering that the leaky, competing accumulator (LCA) model ( Usher & McClelland, 2004 ) explains the attraction/compromise effects with loss aversion, there is a theoretical connection between LCA and GEBA model. As to the similarity effect, the structure of the GEBA (or EBA) model reflects the concept of the ‘distance-dependent’ negative lateral inhibition of the multialternative decision field theory (MDFT) ( Roe et al., 2001 ).

An alternative’s perceived category membership can complicate the people’s perception of a prototypical option. When there are three alternatives in the choice set (two similar alternatives and one dissimilar alternative), the natural grouping of the two similar alternatives and the prototypicality effect can cause a strong preferential bias against the lone alternative ( Glazer, Kahn, & Moore, 1991 ). However, if an alternative is not a prototypical example of the category but a strongly discrepant one, it can be perceived as belonging to a separate subcategory ( Sujan & Bettman, 1989 ). Such a ‘subtyped’ alternative can be perceived as prototypical in the new subcategory. Thus, in order for an alternative to become a beneficiary of the prototypicality effect, it should either dominate the competing alternatives in the category or create a new subcategory ( Trout & Ries, 1972 ).

This study succeeds the insights contained in the previous models including MDFT and LCA model, and proposes a simpler one based on one of the earliest models for the contexts effects. The strength of the MDFT and LCA is that they are dynamic and capable of explaining the effects of deliberation time on contexts effects. However, the advantage of the GEBA model is that it is much simpler to compute the choice probabilities as compared to those for the MDFT and LCA cases. The behavioral foundation for the model modification is that the number and the weight of shared features determine the prototypicality of an option, and people show higher preference for the most prototypical option. The modified EBA model increases the attractiveness of each feature by an amount proportional to the number of alternatives sharing the feature, and thus predicts higher choice probabilities for the options with more shared features. The analyses based on GEBA imply that the interactions of the similarity and the prototypicality effects can give rise to the various context effects. The different viewpoints of psychology and economics coexist in the GEBA model in that the model can represent the contextual variations of choice probabilities while maintaining the utility values fixed within the model structure. The feature-based representations, such as the GEBA, seem particularly appropriate for conceptual stimuli, such as people or countries, that appear to be characterized by a large number of discrete features ( Tversky & Hutchinson, 1986 ).

In order to understand people’s choice behavior, one should understand not only the effect of alternatives’ utilities, but also the positive and negative effects of close competing alternatives. The GEBA model reflects the truism of choice that the introduction of a similar alternative can be either detrimental ( Tversky, 1972a ) or beneficial ( Huber et al., 1982 ) to the existing one. If the introduced alternative happens to be dominated by or sufficiently inferior to the close alternative, the detrimental effect disappears and the remaining beneficial effect gives rise to the attraction effect. The theoretical modeling of the context effects with the GEBA model enables us to make clear conceptual distinctions among the similarity effect, the reverse-similarity effect (with and without the attraction effect), and the attraction effect (with and without the asymmetric dominance).
