Measurement of connectivity has been one  major concern (Flament, 1963). Assessment of observed statistics has been aided by the application of the probability theory  of random graphs to compute expected statistics and their distributions. Psychological structures have been described  indirectly in terms of similarity matrices (Shepard, 1974), which after appropriate coding also can be represented as  (undirected) graphs whose connectivity can be assessed by a similar use of random graph theory (Ling, 1973, 1975)1 Shepard's  and Ling's descriptions aim beyond the piecing together of pairwise ties; they aim to capture something of the overall  structure, in the form of a multidimensional scaling (McFarland & Brown, 1973) or a hierarchy of clusters.  This article is concerned with the statistical assessment of a procedure for representing an array of sociometric graphs by an  array of theoretically interesting graphs known as a "blockmodel" (Boorman & White, 1976; Breiger, 1976; Breiger, Boorman, and  Arabie, 1975; Heil and White, 1976; Schwartz, 1977; and White, Boorman, and Breiger, 1976). When a given array of empirically  obtained sociometric graphs can be "fit" by a given blockmodel, substantive conclusions are possible. Unfortunately, as with  the other structural models in the social sciences, a fit to a blockmodel may arise by chance alone. Consequently, in this  paper we focus on obtaining statistics related to the probability that a fit to a given blockmodel might occur by chance. Such  statistics are useful in assessing the significance of any given blockmodel fit to actual data.2  t The interpretation of the probability space of random graphs is then more problematic (Ling, 1973, p. 160). A sociometric graph is a directed graph where each vertex has the same  outdegree, with slings (self-choices) and parallel directed edges forbidden. It is efficient to represent each graph by a  binary matrix with a constant row-sum. Sociometric choices on several different criteria-for example, like, dislike, and  help-are represented by an ordered array of such binary matrices, with vertices in the same order within each matrix. A  blockmodel is an ordered array of image graphs on a smaller number of vertices called blocks; again edges are directed and are  binary, but slings are allowed.  HOMOMORPHISMS FROM MULTIPLE GRAPHS ONTO BLOCKMODELS  A blockmodel analysis takes an empirical array of t sociometric graphs and attempts to map the array homomorphically onto the  blockmodel. The homomorphism is a mapping h from the n vertices of the array onto the b blocks of the blockmodel that  preserves the following graph structure: for all 1 < k < t, 1 < e, f < b, block e has a directed edge to block f in the kth  image graph if and only if there are vertices 1 < i, j < n, with h(i) = e and h(j) = f such that i has a directed edge to j in  the kth sociometric graph. If such an h can be found, the array is said to be fit to the blockmodel.  If an h can be found that satisfies the conditions for a homomorphism, the n vertices can be partitioned into b cells where  the b cells are the inverse images under h of each of the b blocks. In practice, the search for a blockmodel fit involves  fording all b-cell partitions of the n vertices, which satisfy the homomorphism conditions. There is an algorithm called  BLOCKER (Heil and White, 1975) to carry out this search; below it is used to find the observed fits in a Monte Carlo run. A  fit is of substantive interest only if each cell in the partition contains at least some minimum number of vertices, call it  m, with m > 1.  In this paper we assume that the data array is drawn at random with equal probabilities from a universe of such arrays.
