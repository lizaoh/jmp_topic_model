Our thoughts, memories and behavior are guided by actively maintained internal representations. In the visual domain, visual short-term memory (VSTM) is the structure that allows us to simultaneously maintain multiple visual representations when problem-solving or attempting to remember several objects, people or places. It is also the structure that allows us to actively store representations that guide our visual attention and eye movements when we interact with the environment ( Luck & Vogel, 2013 ). These properties of VSTM capacity make it fundamental to most everyday function. Despite its fundamental nature, there is active theoretical debate regarding how to construe the structure of VSTM. According to traditional theories, VSTM consists of a single memory store, which is commonly thought to be limited in capacity to 3–4 items. Both of these claims have been challenged, however. More specifically, there is active disagreement regarding how information is represented in VSTM, i.e., via item ‘slots’ (e.g.,  Cowan et al., 2001 ) or as a continuous resource (e.g.,  Bays & Husain, 2008; Wilken & Ma, 2004 ). There is also debate regarding whether VSTM consists of a single store or two separable stores (e.g.,  Makovski, 2012; Sligte, Scholte, & Lamme, 2008 ). Although much computational work has examined how information is stored in VSTM, few studies have investigated the dimensionality of VSTM. This is the aim of the current study. Specifically, we directly examined through the application of state-trace analysis, a method specifically designed to test the dimensionality of psychological constructs, whether VSTM is best conceived of as a single or multi-component structure.

In recent work,  Sligte et al. (2008) ; see also,  Landman, Spekreijse, and Lamme (2003)  and  van Moorselaar, Olivers, Theeuwes, Lamme, and Sligte (2015)  argued that our experience of a detailed visual environment is grounded on representations stored in a distinct and previously unknown type of VSTM. Specifically, the authors proposed that an initial, high-capacity VSTM store precedes a more robust and limited capacity VSTM store. In contrast to iconic memory, representations in this hypothetical initial stage of VSTM purportedly persist for up to 4 s, whereas information in iconic memory persists for 0.5 to 2 s, depending on luminance conditions (e.g.,  Sperling, 1960 ). In contrast to representations held in durable VSTM, representations in this new type of VSTM are easily masked by new visual input. Due to this difference, this new form of VSTM has been called  fragile VSTM  ( Sligte et al., 2008 ). The proposal that there is a second, high-capacity form of VSTM has faced criticism (e.g.,  Makovski, 2012; Matsukura & Hollingworth, 2011 ) because it conflicts with long-standing models of VSTM as a unitary system with a limited capacity.

Evidence for a fragile, high-capacity VSTM was found using a variant of a change-detection paradigm. In this task, participants were briefly presented with two arrays of items (a memory array and a test array) separated by a blank interval. In the  retro-cue  condition, one of the memory array items was cued after the offset of the memory array and before the onset of the test array, and participants were asked to report whether a feature of the cued item changed across the arrays. Performance in this condition was compared to a  post-cue  condition in which the target item was cued after the second array was presented, that is, after the first array was masked by the test array.  Sligte et al. (2008, Experiment 1)  reported that in the retro-cue condition participants could remember the equivalent of 16 different items. In contrast, in the post-cue condition participants remembered the equivalent of only 4 items, showing performance within the limits of traditional VSTM capacity.

Subsequent experiments have cast doubt on the existence of fragile, high-capacity VSTM. For example, when aspects of array configuration that may facilitate chunking of array items are controlled for, evidence for only a 5–7 item capacity under retro-cue conditions has been found (e.g., Sligte et al., Experiment 3; see also,  Matsukura & Hollingworth, 2011 ). In addition,  Matsukura and Hollingworth (2011)  pointed out that a lack of control in training between retro- and post-cue conditions could explain why estimates of capacity were larger in the retro-cue condition than in the post-cue condition. Specifically, Sligte et al. allowed participants to continue training in the retro-cue and post-cue tasks separately if participants “indicated that they could have attained higher performance” (pg. 8). Matsukura and Hollingworth examined practice effects on retro-cue performance and showed that estimates of capacity increased from four to approximately six items with more training. Effects of practice cannot account for all effects of retro-cueing, however, because its benefits on memory have been replicated even under conditions where practice was controlled for (e.g.,  Pertzov, Bays, Joseph, & Husain, 2013 ). It is also important to note that explanations for the retro-cue advantage have been proposed that do not rely on the existence of fragile VSTM; for example, presentation of a retro-cue may allow for more attentional processing of the target item prior to the presentation of the test array (e.g.,  Makovski, 2012; Matsukura & Hollingworth, 2011 ). This interpretation is consistent with the view that the precision with which any single item is coded in VSTM is influenced by how much attentional processing is allotted towards that item. Increased attentional processing will improve the precision of a stored representation, and accordingly, may inflate estimates of capacity that do not discriminate between the number of items that are stored and their resolution (for alternatives to traditional discrete-slot models see:  Zhang & Luck, 2008 ). Critically, this view does not require positing a second dimension in VSTM. Rather, it assumes that an identical latent variable is accessed in the retro- and post-cue conditions to varying degrees.

A direct method for assessing the structure of psychological constructs is through the application of state-trace analysis ( Bamber, 1979 ). State-trace analysis is based on the minimal assumption that levels of performance on two dependent variables as a result of change(s) in (an) independent variable(s) should be monotonically related if they rely on the same latent variable. In the current study, the  dependent variable  is a measure of performance on the change detection task in the retro-cue and post-cue condition. We measured performance on each VSTM task using the difference between hits and false alarm rates (the  difference score ), a measure based on the mainstream discrete-slot model of VSTM ( Cowan et al., 2005; Rouder, Morey, Morey, & Cowan, 2011 ), and the signal detection measure  d’,  a measure based on the continuous resource model of VSTM. We conducted a separate state-trace analysis with each measure because each makes different assumptions about how representations are stored in VSTM, and thus may lead to different conclusions regarding how a manipulation affects performance. Relatedly, under some conditions measures based on the discrete-slot and signal detection models are not monotonically related to one another. For instance, the raw difference between a hit rate of .85 and a false alarm rate of .41 is .44, which yields a  d’  of 1.26, whereas the raw difference between a hit rate of .9 and a false alarm rate of .5 is .4, which yields a  d’  of 1.28. Therefore, our analyses provide insight into whether conclusions about dimensionality hold regardless of what assumptions are made about how information is represented in VSTM and whether they are consistent across different transformations of the data.

As noted, the  first independent variable  was practice, which has been shown to affect performance on VSTM tasks ( Matsukura & Hollingworth, 2011; Xu et al., 2018 ). In particular, participants completed two separate sessions, approximately 24 hours apart. We used performance in each session to measure practice effects. The second independent variable was memory set size, which is also known to affect performance in a monotonic fashion. We used five different set sizes to ensure a wide range of points in the outcome space.

Based on the logic of state-trace analysis, if VSTM is a unidimensional structure (that is, if fragile VSTM and durable VSTM are actually the same thing), then the data for each of our memory measures should fall on a single, monotonically increasing curve in the state-trace plot. Failure to find this would support the hypothesis that fragile VSTM and durable VSTM are actually separate components of the human memory system. In both cases, the two manipulations, that is, memory load and practice, produce a monotonic change in performance on the retro- and post-cue versions of the VSTM task. Likewise, there is a main effect of task-type, that is, performance on the retro-cue task is better than performance on the post-cue task. Participants were from the University of Illinois community and participated in exchange for $16 ($8 per session). All participants reported normal or corrected-to-normal vision.

The experimental program was written in Matlab, using Psychophysics Toolbox extensions ( Brainard, 1997; Pelli, 1997 ). Stimuli were presented on a Samsung 2013 LED monitor with screen resolution of 1920 by 1200 pixels and a refresh rate of 60 Hz. Stimuli were presented on a black background.

The experimental procedure ( Fig. 3 ) was similar to a procedure used by others for these tasks ( Makovski, 2012; Matsukura & Hollingworth, 2011; Sligte et al., 2008 ). All stimuli were shown in white on a black background. At the beginning of each trial a white fixation cross (1ºby 1º) was presented for 1000 ms. Next, participants were shown 4, 5, 6, 7 or 8 letters for 250 ms. We used consonants to minimize the use of chunking strategies by participants. On a given trial each letter was sampled randomly without replacement (i.e., each letter could only be shown in one location on a given trial) from the following set: ‘B’, ‘C’, ‘D’, ‘F’, ‘G’, ‘H’, ‘J’, ‘K’, ‘L’, ‘M’, ‘N’, ‘S’, ‘Q’, ‘R’, S’, ‘V’, ‘X’, and ‘Z’. Each letter (2ºby 2º) was presented randomly at one of eight possible locations, placed equidistantly around the circumference of an invisible circle centered in the middle of the screen with a radius of 5.2º. In the retro-cue condition the memory array was presented for 250 ms and was followed by a 1000 ms retention interval, after which a cue was presented for 500 ms; the test array was then presented 500 ms after cue offset. In the post-cue condition the memory array was presented for 250 ms and after a 900 ms blank interval the test array was presented; the cue was presented 100 ms after the onset of the test array until participants made a response. Thus, in both conditions the cue was presented 1000 ms after the offset of the memory display. The cue was a thin line (2.5ºby 1º) that pointed from the center of the screen towards the location of one of the stimuli. The test array remained visible until participants made a response. On half of the trials, the cued letter changed from what was in the memory array, and on half of the trials it remained the same. Non-cued letters always remained the same across the two arrays. Participants were instructed to respond by pressing an arrow key whether they perceived a change or not (left arrow key = ‘change’, right arrow key = ‘no change’). In order to minimize differences in practice across conditions, retro- and post-cue conditions were presented in blocks of 20 trials each. The order of the blocks was counterbalanced across participants. All participants completed two experimental sessions. Each session consisted of 380 experimental trials. Participants completed 20 training trials (10 retro-cue and 10 post-cue trials) at the beginning of the first experimental session. There were a total of 40 condition combinations (change (2) x cue type (2) x session (2) x set size (5)). There were an equal number of trials in each experimental condition and each cell had 19 observations.

The raw data are available online. Before reporting the results of our state-trace analysis, we report the results of confirmatory analyses to assess whether each of our three manipulations of set size, session, and cue-type (retro versus post) had the expected effects on performance. We assessed performance using a measure based on the discrete-slot model (hits minus false alarms) as well as a measure based on the signal detection model ( d’ ) of VSTM capacity. We assessed both measures of performance because each reflects different assumptions regarding how information is stored in VSTM and may lead to different conclusions regarding how a given manipulation affects people’s ability to store information in VSTM ( Swets, 1986 ). Including these two measures permitted us to verify that our results were robust across these different metrics. Prior studies have shown that  K  capacity estimates (i.e., hits minus false alarms multiplied by the number of to-be-remembered items) are typically constant as a function of set size (e.g.,  Cowan et al., 2001; Matsukura & Hollingworth, 2011; Sligte et al., 2008 ), which means they cannot be used in the state trace to assess monotonicity. For this reason, we used an estimate based on the discrete-slot model that is unweighted by set size in our primary analyses, which was simply the difference between hit and false alarm rates (for an identical analysis, see  Sense, Morey, Prince, Heathcote, & Morey, 2017 ). We will refer to this measure as the  difference score . It is important to note that Cowan’s  K  estimate of capacity and the difference score are based on the same latent variable model ( Swets, 1986 ). Thus, in cases where we collapsed across set sizes to make contrasts, such as for comparisons across experimental sessions and cue-type conditions, a significant effect on the difference score would also reflect a significant effect on Cowan’s  K  estimates of memory capacity.

For the critical test, we applied state-trace analysis to directly assess the dimensionality of VSTM. We used the  STACMR  package for Matlab ( Dunn & Kalish, 2018 ), which assesses monotonicity via conjoint monotonic regression. Conjoint monotonic regression is a statistical procedure that obtains a best-fitting prediction to observed data under the constraint that two or more dependent variables are monotonically related (see  Dunn & Kalish, 2018  for more technical details on this procedure). When fitting this model, we made two basic predictions about how set size and practice should affect performance. Specifically, we predicted that performance should not increase with increasing set size, and that it should not decrease with increasing practice. We used both of these predictions to form an initial partial order to constrain our model, which may help increase statistical power ( Dunn & Kalish, 2018 ). The purpose of this study was to examine the structure of VSTM. More specifically, we tested the novel view that there are two separable types of VSTM: a fragile form of VSTM that is high in capacity, and a more durable form of VSTM that is lower in capacity. To examine this view, we leveraged state-trace analysis, an analytic approach specifically designed to assess the dimensionality of psychological constructs. The advantage of using state-trace analysis is that it eschews the problem of making strong assumptions about how changes in a latent psychological variable translate to changes in the dependent variable. The minimal assumption made in state-trace analysis is that levels of performance on two dependent variables as a result of changes in an independent variable should be monotonically related if they rely on the same latent variable. We applied state-trace analysis using two different transformations of the data as the dependent variable: hits minus false alarms and  d’ . We used both metrics because each rests on different assumptions about processing and may have led to different conclusions regarding what effects a manipulation has on processing ( Swets, 1986 ). Our results were consistent across both measures. Specifically, we found no evidence for violations of monotonicity, and accordingly, evidence for a unidimensional model of VSTM.

Our results and conclusion are consistent with other work that shows a close relationship between attentional processing and VSTM (e.g.,  Bays & Taylor, 2018; Gazzaley & Nobre, 2012 ). In particular, this work suggests that representations in memory can be of variable resolution. Thus, the benefit of retro-cueing likely reflects the advantage of allotting greater attentional processing towards the cued item while it is held in memory (e.g.,  Bays & Taylor, 2018; Makovski, Sussman, & Jiang, 2008; Matsukura & Hollingworth, 2011 ). Redeploying attention towards a representation in VSTM increases its precision ( Pertzov et al., 2013 ), and may also protect it from interference produced by the onset of new visual input, such as the onset of the test array (e.g.,  Makovski & Jiang, 2007 ). Our state-trace results complement this line of work, demonstrating that it is un-parsimonious to assume multiple VSTM stores.

We are aware that Sligte and colleagues have tried to provide additional support for the existence of fragile VSTM by showing that manipulations of attention have different effects on retro-cue vs. post-cue performance ( Vandenbroucke, Sligte, & Lamme, 2011 ) and that performance in retro-cue vs. post-cue conditions may rely on different neural substrates ( Sligte, Scholte, & Lamme, 2009; Sligte, Wokke, Tesselaar, Scholte, & Lamme, 2011; Vandenbroucke, Sligte, de Vries, Cohen, & Lamme, 2015 ). Differences in neural processes do not necessarily imply functionally distinct systems, however (e.g.,  Henson, 2006; Sherry & Schacter, 1987 ), and dissociations are neither necessary nor sufficient for demonstrating the existence of multiple latent variables ( Dunn, 2003; Dunn & Kirsner, 1988; Loftus, 1978; Newell & Dunn, 2008 ). State-trace analysis is not subject to these limitations and provides a much sounder and more powerful approach for addressing questions of dimensionality in data.

One limitation of our experiment is that it assessed only part of the outcome space, corresponding to relatively high levels of performance (accuracy levels ranging from 69% to 93%). Therefore, in principle it is possible we are assessing a state-trace that is truly generated by two latent variables in part of the outcome space where violations of monotonicity are not evident, and that different results might be obtained if performance were closer to chance. We cannot think of an a priori reason for why violations of non-monotonicity would not be evident in our experiment, however. Within the logic of state-trace analysis a multidimensional model can predict a much larger range of points in the outcome space. The fact that our data all fall on a single monotonic function favors the more parsimonious inference that a single latent variable is driving performance on both the retro- and post-cue tasks, which is also in line with theories of how attention interacts with performance on VSTM tasks. Nevertheless, it may be fruitful for researchers to apply state-trace analysis with different independent variables and/or an even larger range of levels of the independent variables used here (e.g., memory set size) to assess a wider range of performance.

Together, our results suggest that commonly used measures of VSTM capacity, such as Cowan’s K, may lead to untenable conclusions (for a similar view see  Makovski, 2012 ). This is because  K  estimates of capacity are based on the discrete-slot model, which assumes that each to-be-remembered item is stored in an all-or-none fashion. Unlike alternative measures, such as those based on signal detection theory,  K  capacity estimates cannot capture variability in the fidelity of memory representations because the model assumes that memory representations are stored with complete fidelity or not at all. Therefore, conditions that affect the precision of memory representations, such as the presentation of a retro-cue, may be interpreted as if they affect item capacity, or in the current context, as if they probe a separate memory store. Our results suggest that this interpretation is unsubstantiated and that the use of discrete-slot measures of capacity such as Cowan’s  K  can lead to unwarranted conclusions.
