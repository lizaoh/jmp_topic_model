At the beginning of his influential paper Thomas Schelling ( 1973 ) observed that many hockey players do not choose to wear helmets if they are free to decide for a variety of reasons including vanity, intimidating the opponents and avoid limitations in seeing and hearing. However, the majority of them judges crazy not to wear the helmet because without it they risk serious injuries. This is an example of decision making where the consequences of a choice have an effect not only on the individual personal gain but also on the other’s payoff. Schelling employs respectively the terms internality and externality to refer to these two different kinds of choice’s consequences. In particular, internality is the effect of one individuals’ choice on their own payoff while externality is the advantageous/adverse effect on others’ payoffs. In his work, he focused on binary choice with externalities: as Schelling wrote, there are countless real world examples of this class of decision, such as boycotting a country or a product, voting a non-proliferation treaty or getting vaccinated. However, what all these situations have in common is the interdependence of choices among individuals.

Schelling’s modeling of binary choice with externalities, which extended classic prisoner’s dilemma, in the subsequent decades, has influenced a wide number of studies in different fields, namely economics ( Boorman, 1975; Brock & Durlauf, 2001; Potts et al., 2008; Runge, 1986; Spence, 1974 ), social psychology ( Colman, 2003; Komorita & Parks, 1994 ), sociology ( Watts & Dodds, 2017 ), networks ( Boccaletti et al., 2006; Marwell et al., 1988 ), biology ( Dodds & Watts, 2004, 2005 ), marketing ( Pfeffer et al., 2014; Watts & Dodds, 2007 ) and political science ( Axelrod, 1980, 1981; Ostrom, 2003 ) to cite a few.

In this paper, following Bischi and Merlone ( 2009 ), we focus on the time evolution of binary choice with externalities: indeed, in many real-world applications people make decisions repeatedly but in the original work of Schelling no dynamic models are explicitly described. Given the importance of the Schelling model, in this paper we investigate the dynamics of binary choice when switching choices is determined by a cognitive process in which the current response of each agent is influenced by the previous response. Such kind of effects are commonly observed in empirical studies within psychology: the quantum cognition approach is a recent framework that has proven to be able to model and predict these contextual effects.

The attempt of modeling binary decisions requires to choose a formal computational description of human probabilistic reasoning. Traditionally, cognitive science has employed classic (Bayesian) probability theory not only as a normative criterion (i.e., identifying an optimal decision) or a prescriptive criterion (i.e., how a rational agent should make decisions) but also as a formal framework to develop probabilistic models of cognition. This perspective has showed its great potentiality to describe and predict high-level cognition phenomena including human inductive learning and generalization ( Tenenbaum et al., 2006 ), and causal inference ( Griffiths & Tenenbaum, 2005; Steyvers et al., 2003; Tenenbaum et al., 2007 ).

In line with this traditional view, even the majority of game theory studies has employed classic probability theory to model how an agent makes decisions. When considering binary choices ( Schelling, 1973 ), the mathematical modelization proposed by Bischi and Merlone ( 2009 ) is based on agents’ switching propensities which can be interpreted in terms of classical probabilities. However, since the

These findings are primarily concerned with the influence of context on judgments (such as order effects of questions see Hogarth & Einhorn,  1992 ; Schwarz & Sudman,   2012 ) and violations of the law of total probability. Contextual effects characterize not only probabilistic reasoning but they are ubiquitous in psychology ( Basieva et al., 2019; Cervantes & Dzhafarov, 2018; Gronchi & Provenzi, 2017; Gronchi & Strambini, 2017; Pierguidi et al., 2016; Righi et al., 2015 ): indeed, perceptive and cognitive processes never happen in isolation and the circumstances strongly affect the final result.

In sum, classic probability has played a conflicting role: on the one hand, given its flexibility and popularity, it has been used as a toolbox for modeling perceptual and cognitive processes ( Oaksford & Chater, 2007 ). On the other hand, several lines of research have emphasized the systematic violations of the basic principles of the same theory from a behavioral point of view ( Kahneman & Frederick, 2005 ). However, in the last fifteen years several authors ( Busemeyer & Bruza, 2012; Busemeyer et al., 2006; Pothos & Busemeyer, 2013; Wang et al., 2013 ) have proposed an alternative approach for modeling cognitive processes based on quantum probability theory (i.e., the mathematical foundations of quantum theory), generally called the  quantum cognition approach . Analogously to the classical approach, quantum probability theory is a formal framework for assigning probabilities to events ( Hughes, 1989; Isham, 2001; Pothos & Busemeyer, 2013 ). Differently from classic probability theory, which is based on Kolmogorovian axioms and it is usually represented through Venn diagrams, at least for trivial examples, in quantum probability theory a Hilbert space ( Kantorovitch & Akilov, 1982 ) is defined and possible outcomes are represented as subspaces (see  2.2.1  for details).

Binary decisions in the Schelling model are based on externalities which means that an agent choice affects the choice of other agents. For example, following one of the many examples of Schelling, the choice of Alice to ride a bicycle to work is not only affected by her own gain (e.g., do physical activity, saving her gas money) but also from the behavior of other people (if none of her colleagues or neighbors take the bike, Alice may feel uncomfortable being the only one who rides a bicycle to go to work).

The models based on the Schelling approach can be considered population games. In fact, population games describe simultaneous strategic interactions exhibiting three properties: (1) each environment contains a large number of agents capable of making independent decisions; (2) each agent is small, meaning that the choices made by an individual have only a minor impact on other agents’ outcomes; (3) agents are anonymous: an agent’s outcome from the interaction depends on his own strategy and the distribution of others’ strategies, without requiring the individuation of the opponents. Of course, the behavior of the population is based on how the agents are modeled. As far as we know, previous literature developed models taking into account agents whose behavior is grounded on classical probability: the most studied case is that of the so-called impulsive agents which switch strategies even when the difference between payoffs is extremely small ( Bischi et al., 2009; Bischi & Merlone, 2009 ).

Impulsive agents are characterized by a value called propensity. If the choice made is not satisfactory (in other terms agents who choose the other option had got a better payoff), they tend to switch behavior toward the other option according to the propensity value. Thinking about Alice in terms of an impulsive agent means that if she is not satisfied with her choice not to take the bike (as compared to the opposite choice of a group of colleagues and neighbors), she will resolve to change her decision according to her sensitivity to other’s behavior (the propensity value). The propensity does not change in time and the Schelling approach usually assumes that this value is the same for all agents. As a consequence, the way in which impulsive agents are modeled assumes that human behavior is the expression of a stable disposition (with the additional assumption that the agents share the same disposition): this relates to the idea that the actions of an individual are driven by beliefs and attitudes but the converse is not true ( Kvam et al., 2014 ). However, this modeling approach contradicts well-known account of human behavior such self-perception theory ( Bem, 1967, 1972 ) and empirical observations ( Bem & McConnell, 1970; Freedman & Fraser, 1966; Salancik & Conway, 1975; Sherman, 1980 ).

                            As Kvam, Busemeyer and Lambert–Mogiliansky ( 2014 ) noted, self-perception theory does not necessitate a non-classical framework. However, its postulates are consistent with quantum principles. 
                          Going back to the example, let us consider another individual, Bob, together with Alice: again, they have to decide whether to commute by bicycle or not. We assume that they are indistinguishable in terms of measurable parameters (such as individual psychological differences, as seen before) and that they share the same belief about the decision of taking the bike. In this case, the classical (impulsive) model predicts that Alice and Bob will make the same choice. However, psychological theory and empirical observations suggest that also the previous actions of the two actors could affect their choices. In other terms, even under these narrow assumption, if Alice has previously made a sequence of choices (for example, in the last two days she has been cycling to work) that is different from Bob’s sequence (two days ago he did not ride the bike and yesterday he did), albeit they are indistinguishable with regard to individual differences, this could lead to a different choice about the decision to be made today. This is in line with the constructive view in psychological research ( Feldman & Lynch, 1988; Schwarz, 2007; Shafer & Tversky, 1985 ): the observed behavior (choices, decisions) does not merely reveal preferences stored in memory. Instead, preferences, attitudes, beliefs are constructed in the elicitation process based on contextual factors including the framing of the problem, the method of elicitation and even past decisions ( Ariely et al., 2003; Tversky & Kahneman, 2000 ). Indeed, past decisions can alter preferences ( Ariely & Norton, 2008; Sharot et al., 2010 ). This is consistent with the fundamental principle of quantum theory according to which the operation of measurement creates and records the property of the system simultaneously. So, quantum cognition ( Pothos & Busemeyer, 2013; Wang et al., 2013 ) is an ideal approach for improving the way of modeling agents within the Schelling framework.

From an empirical point of view, the attempt of introducing the quantum formalism is also motivated by the evidence collected in the Prisoner’s Dilemma game. For example, Kvam, Busemeyer and Lambert–Mogiliansky ( 2014 ) had participants to play after a promise made to a complete stranger to cooperate in the game (no relation with the other player and without consequences for the outcome of the game). Under this condition, they found that participants exhibited higher rates of cooperation compared to the control group and that the quantum model is an effective way to capture this effect ( Kvam et al., 2014; Mogiliansky et al., 2009 ). Not only this result suggests how the constructive view of preferences also concerns specific experimental settings such as the Prisoner’s Dilemma game, but also shows how the quantum formalism is relevant to Schelling model which generalizes the Prisoner’s Dilemma ( Schelling, 1973 ).

In the next section, we will describe in more detail the behavior of an agent modeled within the quantum cognition framework in the Schelling setting.

The key distinction between compatible and incompatible events is a quantum theory concept that has been exploited in the cognitive domain in order to capture the constructive aspect of preferences. Compatibility means that it is possible to specify a joint probability distribution for all outcome combinations (in this case the prediction of the classic and the quantum approach overlap). In the case of incompatibility, it is impossible to ascertain the outcomes of all questions at the same time.

In this paper, we have investigated the behavior of agents modeled with the quantum cognition framework in binary choice games with externalities. Binary choices with externalities were first described by Schelling where the consequences of the choices of an individual are affected by the actions of other agents. Previous works on this topic have mainly considered impulsive agents 
                         4 
                      
                      
                         4 
                         Actually, as common in economics, some contributions consider evolutionary dynamics (Bischi & Merlone,  2017 ; Bischi et al.  2018 ) as well, yet they are outside the scope of this paper. 
                      , against which we have compared the quantum agents. The dynamics with impulsive agents is always characterized by convergence to a fixed point or to periodic cycles of any period ( Bischi et al., 2009 ).

In the last years, the quantum cognition framework has proven to be very effective in modeling several aspects of human mind and behavior: for the purposes of our case, quantum cognition is able to capture the constructive view in psychological research (i.e., the idea according to which “information about attitudes that have been previously measured may be retrieved with some additional influences from the current context, and this context affects the construction process of subsequent judgments”,  Busemeyer & Bruza, 2012 , p. 115) an aspect neglected by previous modeling attempt based on classical agents.

We have considered two different kinds of agent: quantum agents that are either context-insensitive (compatible decisions according to the quantum cognition terminology) or context-sensitive (incompatible decisions). In this work, the context-sensitivity is referred to the influence of the current choice on future choices: in other terms the case where preferences are constructed according to the actions made.

As expected, when the quantum agent is context-insensitive, the observed behavior is a particular case of the classical (impulsive) agent. This is in line with previously remarks about quantum cognition framework where in case of compatible events predictions are equivalent to those of the classical framework (see for example Pothos & Busemeyer,  2013 ): in quantum probability theory, when two events are compatible, the resulting probability values reduce to those computed by classic probability theory. Of course, the actual added-value of the quantum framework is its effectiveness in modeling contextual effects, in this case the internal (mental) context generated by the first decision influences the subsequent decisions.

From a mathematical point of view, we observed that, although the context-insensitive dynamics can be obtained as a special case of impulsive agents, it cannot be obtained as a special case of the context-sensitive model dynamics. This is in line with the idea that contextual effects can be modeled in terms of incompatible questions, one of the main claim of the quantum cognition framework ( Pothos & Busemeyer, 2013 ). Furthermore, considering the sensitivity to the previous question, parameter  
                         β 
                       enriches the dynamics and introduces multiple attractors with non trivial basins, a peculiar behavior not seen in previous literature with impulsive agent. In this case, the outcome depends on initial conditions, so that with all things being equal (including relevant parameters together with the belief of the individual and the influence of the past decision on the current choice) the emergence of nonconnected basins causes a loss of predictability about the system evolution as discussed in  Betz (2006) .

Keeping into account that our modeling effort is based on the proportion of choices observed in a population of individuals, we have observed that even under strict assumptions (the initial belief and the contextual influence of the previous action on the current action are exactly the same in all individual of the population), in the quantum cognition version of the Schelling model, different past decisions yield different trajectories.

Taking the example mentioned in the introduction, let us refer again to Alice and Bob when we assumed they are indistinguishable in terms of measurable parameters (for example, measures about individual psychological differences). Also, they have the same belief about a particular decision problem and the effect of the current decision on future decisions is the same. However, if Alice has previously made a sequence of choices (she commuted by bicycle in the past two days) that is different from Bob’s (who two days ago did not commute by bicycle and yesterday did) in the current decisional problem there are no means to predict the choices of Alice and Bob (even if they have identical payoffs given that the last decision of both individuals was the same) because of the sensitivity with respect to small changes of initial conditions.

This observation exploits a novel potentiality of the quantum framework: in our analysis only the quantum agents with incompatible choices exhibit behaviors with such an unpredictability property. Albeit a long-standing debate about the unpredictability of human behavior ( Kareev, 1992; Lavazza, 2019; Libet, 1999; Neuringer, 1986; Neuringer & Voss, 1993; Radder & Meynen, 2013; Saigle et al., 2018; Ward & West, 1998 ), the complexity of human cognition and the brain is usually referred as the reason why the development of models able to explain and predict behavior is considered an extremely difficult task. Of course, scientific psychology at least implicitly assume that it is possible to isolate variables that allow to predict human behavior in some extent. Indeed, the literature about reasoning and decision making have detected many variables that affect human behavior in a predictable way (net of individual differences) such as time constraint in dual process theory or framing effects (e.g., the famous Asian disease problem). Such predictions emerge in terms of sample of individuals, not at single level person. Indeed, it is basically not possible to a-priori predict what a single individual will choose. Our results suggest that, assuming a model of cognition based on the quantum framework, the unpredictability of human behavior stands on the choices made in the past. Indeed, it could be assumed that the current belief state of individual is based on his or her past choices. However, it is virtually impossible to know the whole history of choices carried out by an individual in the past and, according to our model, this leads to the unpredictability of individual behaviors.

Albeit the large influence of the Schelling model on several fields, there is a lack of research aimed at checking the prediction of the model as it is difficult to reproduce in a laboratory the assumptions necessary for a population game ( Sandholm, 2010 ). Despite the difficulty to find specific literature in order to compare the two models’ predictions with actual human behavior data, it is possible to make some general remarks with respect to empirical findings. The classical version of the Schelling model with impulsive agents predicts that the behavior is based only on the payoff and the proportion of people who make a certain choice. In the case of the model with quantum agents, contextual factors (and, specifically, past actions) affect indeed the current choice and show the unpredictability inherent to path dependence ( Vergne & Durand, 2010 ). Empirical observations generally support the predictions of the model with quantum agents compared to the model with classical agents. For example, Salganik, Dodds and Watts ( 2006 ) created an artificial “music market” where people had to evaluate the preferred songs and eventually download them. The authors manipulated the information associated to other’s people choice about preference ratings and number of downloads. They observed that as the strength of social influence increased, the unpredictability of success of the songs increases too. This confirms the role of social influence in terms of contextuality but the behavior of people cannot be predicted only on the basis of other’s behavior. It should be noted that this experiment does not reproduce accurately the Schelling model. However, it represents evidence supporting the idea that predictions of the quantum models are closer to actual behavior than classical models are. 
                         5 
                      
                      
                         5 
                         See also Golman, Jain and Saraf ( 2019 ) for theoretical insights about the limits of classical model to predict the emergence of complex and unpredictable cultural trends. 
                       Moreover, to cite a few examples made by Schelling, data related to vaccination ( Cohen et al., 2013 ), illegal bicycle parking ( Fukuda & Morichi, 2007 ), helmet usage in hockey ( Wise & Scott, 2012 ) suggest that actual decisions made by people depend not only on the behavior of others and the payoffs of the options, but also on contextual factors that could explain the wide fluctuations observed in actual behavior.

Schelling examines interdependence of choices between individual when they are assumed to be identically situated both statically and dynamically and applies his analyses to social dilemmas relevant to policy makers. Extending his approach considering more sophisticated decision making models may provide a better understanding of collective choice dynamics.

Furthermore, it would be interesting to introduce heterogeneity. Some efforts to introduce heterogeneity in binary choices lead to interesting results ( Dal Forno & Merlone, 2019 ), yet the analysis becomes much more complicated and will be the subject of further work. Another possible approach would be considering ABM (agent-based modeling) as it makes possible to endorse the heterogeneity of agents ( Mignot & Vignes, 2020 ).

This work has showed how quantum agents may exhibit unpredictable behavior in certain conditions as illustrated in the last example ( Fig. 8 ), where impulsive agents models, at least in this situation, fails to capture the complexity of human behavior. This confirms the potentiality of quantum cognition in modeling human behavior.
