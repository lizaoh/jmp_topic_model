MURDOCK  University of Toronto  According to TODAM, a distributed memory model based on convolution and correlation, item and associative information are  stored and retrieved from a common memory vector. The model also gives a possible representation of forgetting, encoding,  attentional, and habituation processes. The main purpose of the present paper is to present the necessary derivations so the  model can be applied to item recognition, pair recognition, and cued recall in a single-trial or list-learning paradigm.  Applications to the list-strength effect, item- and pair-recognition, and paired-associative learning are briefly discussed.  1992 Academic Press, Inc.  The distinction between item and associative information is basic to the understanding of human memory. Item information is  information that enables us to recognize familiar objects or events ("items"), while associative information enables us to  relate ("associate") two different items or events. We know the face is familiar, but we cannot recall the name. Some of the  early evidence in support of this distinction was presented in Murdock (1974).  In this paper I will first describe how item and associative information are represented in TODAM, a theory of distributed  associative memory based on convolution and correlation of random vectors. This theory, an extension of the CADAM model of  Liepa (1977), has been described in detail elsewhere (Murdock, 1982, 1989) so only a brief review will be given here. In the  major part of the paper, I will present the derivations for the moments of the resemblance distributions necessary to apply  the model to item recognition and pair recognition and recall. These results are necessary if one is to apply the model to  recall and recognition data. Weber (1988) has presented a detailed analysis of the single-trial case; here we specify the  encoding and attentional processes and extend the analysis to the multitrial case. Finally I will briefly discuss some of the  applications of the model to experimental data. Representation  Items (e.g., A and B) are represented by independent random vectors (e.g., f and g) which are vectors whose elements are  random variables. Technically it would be desirable to have them infinite dimensional (Schonemann, 1987) but here we restrict  the analysis to the finite case where the dimensionality is denoted by N. Following Anderson (1972, 1973), it is assumed that  these random variables are drawn from a normal distribution with mean zero and variance P/N where P, the power of the vector f  (P=Y; f(i) f(i), i= 1, N, where f(i) denotes the ith element of f), is assumed to be 1.0.  Retrieval  Recognition involves a comparison process whereby the probe item vector (say f) is compared with the memory vector (M) by  means of the dot product. The result of this comparison process (f - M) is fed into a decision process, but the decision  process is not considered here. Recall involves a generation process and a deblurring process. For generation, the probe item  (say f) is correlated with the memory vector (M) and the retrived information, denoted by g', is an approximation to g. For  recall, g' must be mapped into g by deblurring, but the deblurring process is not considered here either.  How can predictions about recognition and recall be derived if we do not specify the decision and deblurring processes? For  recognition, we can make ordinal predictions for a yes-no procedure just knowing the "strength" (i.e., dot product) values,  and a decision model compatible with TODAM is available (Hockley & Murdock, 1987). For forced-choice recognition, we can use  the standard assumptions of signal-detection theory (Green & Swets, 1966) as a criterion-free model. For recall, even though  we do not model the deblurring process we can characterize its probability of success. Consequently we can make detailed  predictions about  recall if we have the moments of the resemblance distribution even though we do not specify what the process is. However, in both cases (i.e., recall and item  recognition), the means and variances of the resemblance distributions must be given in terms of the parameters of the model.  Model Parameters  TODAM has a number of parameters to fit the model to data. These parameters are an attempt to quantify certain cognitive  processes within the framework of a  distributed memory model. Given the richness and diversity of these cognitive processes, these parameters may turn out to be  grossly inadequate. On the other hand, for some applications the model is overdetermined (Murdock, 1989) and we may have too  many parameters, not too few. In any event, each parameter can be associated with an established cognitive process, and this  account at least includes some of the complexities necessary for a quantitative accounting of recall and recognition data.  There are six parameters in the model: N, a, p, ~, 0, and p. The first parameter, N is the dimensionality of the item vector  space. It characterizes the type of stimulus material used in the experiment. Recognition memory is better for pictures than  words (Shepard, 1967) so the value of N would be larger for pictures than for words.  The second parameter, a, is a forgetting parameter. For 0.0 < a < 1.0, there is less forgetting as a approaches 1.0. The third  parameter, p, is an encoding parameter. Each element in an item vector is encoded (i.e., added to the memory vector) with  probability p or not encoded (i.e., is not added to the memory vector) with probability 1- p. We can have separate encoding  probabilities for the A and B items of a pair; these are denoted p and q in Eq. (1). Since the convolution term is pf * qg,  probability fluctuations affect item and associative information in the same way. Poorly encoded items (i.e., items with many  missing features) will be poorly (i.e., sparsely) associated; well-encoded items (i.e., items with few missing features) will  be well (i.e., densely) associated.  These encoding probabilities are functions of the presentation duration (Murdock, 1989) so strictly speaking we should write  p(t) and q(t). For the most part this dependence is not needed here so to simplify the notation we will write p and q instead.  It we plot d' as a function of trials, as the encoding probabilities increase the learning curve has a higher intercept but a  lower slope. This is detailed in Murdock (1989) or Murdock and Lamon (1988) for item-recognition learning but it is also true  for paired-associative learning as well. Our general aim is to develop expressions for item and pair recognition and recall in terms of  the parameters of the model.  Item and Associative Tests  For an item recognition test, a single item (old or new) is compared to the memory vector and the comparison is effected by  the dot product. Associative tests can be either recall or recognition. In recall the probe item f is correlated with the  memory and the retrieved information is g' (an approximation to g). Recall probability is then assumed to depend on the  resemblance of g' to g (Murdock, 1989), and this resemblance is measured by the dot product g - g'. Recall probability is the  probability that a sample drawn at random from the old-item distribution will be within specified tolerance limits and closer  to the ideal (PZ) than any competitors; see, e.g., Murdock (1989) for details.  Associative or pair recognition of the pairs A - B, C - D, ... can be tested by intact (or "old") probes such as A - B or C -  D, rearranged probles such as A - D or C - B, mixed pairs such as A - X or X - B, or new pairs such as X - Y (Humphreys, Pike,  Bain, & Tehan, 1989). It is assumed that whatever probe is used in pair recognition, the two probe items (say A and B) are  convolved and the convolution is compared to the memory vector (Murdock, 1989). In terms of the theoretical analysis, for an  intact probe we would want the expectation and variance of (f * g) - M where M contained f * g.  It turns out that recall and pair recognition have features in common since for recall  g.g'=g.{f#(f*g)}=(g*f)-(f*g)=(f*g)4*g)  (Appendix) and for pair recognition we also have  (f*g).{(f*g)+...}=(f*g).(f*g)+....  This analysis implies that there are parallels between pair recognition and intra-list intrusions. In particular, intra-list  intrusions in recall are similar to a rearranged probe test in pair recognition.  Specifically, suppose M contains the pairs f * g and u * v. Suppose we consider the intra-list intrusion v where f is used as  the recall cue. Then  v.v'=v.(f # M)=(v*f)-M =(f*v).(f*g)+(f* v) .(u*v)+ .  This is exactly the same expression that we would get for the dot product of f * v with the memory vector if the associative  recognition test consisted of the rearranged test pair f and v.  This parallel between recall and pair recognition simplifies the derivations because we can use some of the same results for  both. (This parallel also holds between extra-list intrusions in recall and mixed pairs in associative recognition where one  probe item is old and the other proble item is new, and between a nonlist probe in recall and a new probe in associative  recognition.)  This parallel also provides a potential method of isolating factors specific to the deblurring process. Because the retrieval  information in recall and pair recognition is the same, but recall involves an additional deblurring process, experimental  manipulations that affect associative recall and recognition differently must be localized in the deblurring process. As an  example, there is evidence that the A - B, A - D paradigm produces retroactive inhibition (RI) in recall but not in  recognition (Dyne, Humphreys, Bain, & Pike, 1990). By this analysis, the RI effect in recall would be due to an inhibition in  the deblurring of B following the A - D interpolated learning. Presentation-Order Assumption  In all the derivations we have assumed a constant order of presentation on all study trials. This enabled us to write  a, (r)=aL-I+(R-r)L  and then by using q where n = aLIO we could factor out c where C = AO R-' and partition the forgetting and encoding parameters  so a varied with serial position and q varied with repetition. We cannot do this quite so neatly if a random presentation  order is used, and, of course, in practice a random presentation order generally is used.  One can work through the derivations without this parameter simplification, and the results are different for the p  (within-repetitions) and the p2 (between-repetitions) terms. For the former, it makes no difference whether a constant or  varied presentation order is used. The reason is very simple. We can still factor out an a(R-r)L term, and on each trial each  item has an aL-r(r) value where 1(r) varies with r (trial). Alternatively, for any given set of data on  item or pair recognition or recall one can estimate the best-fitting parameter values. Strictly speaking the derivations  require the same presentation order of the L items on all R trials but the simulation results shown in Table 4 suggest they  may be a good approximation for a randomized presentation order.  In the calculations, for the mean one should use Eq. (5a) or (5b) for an itemrecognition test or Eq. (8) or (8a) for a  pair-recognition test or recall. Use Eq. As an example, Ratcliff, Clark, and Shiffrin (1990) have studied the list-strength effect using a pure-mixed list comparison  in which strength was manipulated using either presentation time or number of presentations. In the pure lists all items were  either "strong" or "weak" but half and half in the mixed list. Their major result was  that the ratio of the ratios (R = A/B where A is the strong-to-weak ratio in the mixed list and B the strong-to-weak ratio in  the pure list) was essentially unity across seven different experiments. They argue (Shiffrin, Ratcliff, & Clark, 1990) this  is a surprising result and counter to all the current global matching models, of which TODAM is one.  To apply the more complete version of TODAM presented here to the liststrength effect, one would have to consider the  procedural details. With one exception their presentation format was paired associates, and in the mixed-list condition the  presentation of strong and weak items was blocked. Since paired associates were used we would assume item and associative  information were added to the memory vector, and we would require the parameter of the exponential function relating  presentation time to encoding probability (p). In the repetition experiment all study trials preceded the test trial, so Eq.  (6) would apply directly.  As a first approximation, we could disregard output interference and assume all items had the same average position in the  test list. While this is presumably true in the experiments, in the model this neglects variability in the test position. It  would be straightforward to predict means and variances (and hence d') for strong and weak items in pure and mixed lists when  presentation time was varied; when repetition was varied the predictions would depend on whether the same presentation order  was used on each trial.  However, it is always possible to mimic the actual experimental presentation order with the model. One simply keeps track of  the order on an item-by-item basis, and you compute the variance after every single presentation. That is, you use Eq. (1) to  form strength vectors for the item and associative components, you update these strength vectors after every presentation, and  you compute the requisite variances concurrently. This is a much more tedious approach, but it means that in principle the  model can be applied to any ordering or any appropriate experimental paradigm. While you lose the advantages of the  closed-form expressions, you gain in the general applicability of the model.  In a different experimental paradigm, Humphreys et al. tested intact, rearranged, mixed, and new probes. According to the  analysis presented here, the false-alarm rate for rearranged, mixed, and new pairs should be quite similar-and more similar as  list length increases-because all of them have an expected value of zero and the variance differences would be relatively  slight because the covariance increment for rearranged or mixed pairs is not very large compared to the new-item variance. (In  this context, by "new-item" variance we mean Var[(f * g) - (u * v)] where u and v are different from f and g.)  Since they used a confidence-rating procedure, I used the EPCROC program of Ogilvie and Creelman (1968) to estimate the d'  values for the three different conditions. In each case I used the intact-pairs condition as the old-item distribution, and  the rearranged-, mixed-, and new-probe conditions as the new-item distributions. The d' values were 1.46, 1.63, and 1.78,  respectively, so the new-item condition provided the easiest discrimination and the rearranged-pairs condition the hardest  discrimination.  This analysis suggests that item information may have played a role as well. When both members of the probe are new, not only  does the associative information not match but neither does the item information. In the mixed-pair condition, one item  matches and in the rearranged-pair condition both items match, even though in neither case does the associative information  match. There were also slope differences; they were 1.0, .53, and .35 for rearranged, mixed, and new probes, respectively.  Thus, the variance of the signal distribution was not (statistically) different from the variance of the noise distribution  when the rearranged probes were used as the noise distribution, but the variance was different (larger) when mixed or new  probes were used. A detailed explanation would require including the item information in the information used in the decision  process, and perhaps other factors as well (e.g., Humphreys, Bain, & Pike, 1989).  Hockley (1991) used a continuous recognition memory task in which pairs of items were presented interspersed with item- or  pair-recognition tests. The item tests were old-A, old-B, or new single-item problem, and the associative tests were intact or  rearranged pairs. Over lags 2-20 the d' for pair recognition was statistically constant but the d' for item recognition  dropped significantly.  Although I have not explored the full parameter space, so far I have had no luck in fitting these data with the version of  TODAM presented here. Item and pair recognition can both drop or both stay flat, but it is their interaction which is more  difficult to explain. One possibility is adding context cues to the item vectors, but this idea needs to be further explored.  Finally, these analyses can be used to apply TODAM to the substantial body of list-learning data that we have. This includes  such data as mean learning curves, learning-to-learn effects, the length-difficulty relation, total-time effects, and  eventually even transfer and interference effects. Many of these topics were discussed in Murdock (1989), but the analysis  presented here will allow us to investigate more fully the operation of various cognitive processes in item and associative  learning.
