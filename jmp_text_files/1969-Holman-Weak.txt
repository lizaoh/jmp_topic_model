HOLMAN  University of California, Los Angeles, California 90024  Two types of extensive measurement are defined: strong, in which two objects with the same scale value must be indifferent;  and weak, in which two objects may have the same scale value without being indifferent. Both types of measurement lead to  ratio scales; and for both types, axioms are provided that are weaker than previous axiom systems. For strong extensive  measurement, the usual positivity and solvability assumptions are eliminated. For weak extensive measurement, positivity,  solvability, and commutativity are all eliminated, and the Archimedean assumption is weakened but cannot be entirely dispensed  with.  INTRODUCTION  Most systems for measurement attach numbers to objects in such a way that the objects satisfy a particular empirical relation  (such as "heavier than" or "preferred to") if and only if their attached numbers satisfy a particular numerical relation (such  as "greater than"). This perfect correspondence between empirical and numerical relations can of course never be exactly  realized in practice because of experimental errors; hence, it might be possible to weaken the correspondence required by the  theory without sacrificing any experimentally important properties of the measurement. The present paper defines a weakened  version of extensive measurement, which possesses the same additivity and uniqueness properties as the conventional strong  type of extensive measurement, but which can be derived from a much less restrictive set of axioms. The present results also  permit strong extensive measurement to be derived from weaker axioms than usual. It is hoped that these findings may suggest  ways of weakening the conditions for other forms of measurement, such as the conjoint measurement of Luce and Tukey (1964),  that are closely related to extensive measurement. Otherwise, the two types of measurement  have the same properties. Moreover, the uniqueness of the function f is the same in both cases: if f and g are any two  functions satisfying the conditions of strong or weak extensive measurement, then f = ag for some positive constant a. This  uniqueness has been established by Marley (1967, Lemma 1) for strong extensive measurement in a slightly different context,  and it can easily be verified that Marley's proof does not depend upon his particular context or upon the difference between  strong and weak extensive measurement. In other words, both types of measurement define ratio scales. Furthermore, whenever  both types of measurement are possible, the resulting scales will be the same except for the arbitrary unit. Axiom 1 requires that > be a weak order; Axiom 2  requires that o be associative. These axioms are stronger than Axioms S1 and S2; but this difference vanishes in context,  because Suppes (1951, Theorems 1, 4, and 5) immediately derives Axioms 1 and 2 from the rest of his axioms.  Axiom 3 imposes a monotonicity requirement that is usually weaker than Axiom S3, depending upon context. Suppes (1951, Theorem  2) shows that if a > a and Axiom S3 holds, then the elements of A are commutative as well as monotonic. On the other hand,  Theorem 1 above indicates that commutativity does not necessarily follow from Axioms 1-3 and 5: although the scale values of  ab and ba must be the same, weak extensive measurement does not imply that these elements must necessarily be indifferent.  Theorem 2, however, indicates that commutativity does hold if Axioms 4 and 6 are added.  Axiom S5 requires that the elements of A be unbounded and strictly positive. Axiom 4, on the other hand, requires only that A  be unbounded and that the sign of each element be consistent in the sense that the element must be either an identity, or  strictly positive, or strictly negative. The assumption of unboundedness, which is retained in the present axioms, is  eliminated in the axioms of Luce and Marley (1969),  EXTENSIVE MEASUREMENT 289  at the price of retaining most of Suppes and Zinnes' other axioms and slightly changing the definition of strong extensive  measurement.  Axiom S6 is an Archimedean axiom. Axiom 5 weakens this assumption by accommodating nonpositive elements and requiring that  only one element possess the Archimedean property. Axiom 5 also includes an unboundedness assumption by the use of strict  inequalities. This axiom is automatically satisfied if A is unbounded and finitely generated, because x can be chosen to be  the largest (or smallest) element in the generating set. The axiom is also satisfied by most of the lexicographically ordered  sets in which strong extensive measurement is not possible, although Fuchs (1963, pp. 171-172) discusses some unbounded sets  in which even Axiom 5 does not hold.  Finally, Axiom 6 amounts to a requirement that A contain no anomalous pairs as defined by Alimov (1950) or Fuchs (1963, p.  162). This axiom serves as an Archimedean axiom for strong extensive measurement, and also replaces Axiom S4, which imposes a  requirement of solvability or natural ordering. At least in the context of the previous axioms, Axiom 6 is definitely the  weaker, because Axiom 6 is necessary for strong extensive measurement according to Theorem 2, while Axiom S4 clearly is not.  Roberts and Luce (1968) state an alternative Archimedean axiom that concisely combines Axiom 6 and Axiom 4 without  unboundedness.  In summary, then, Theorem 2 generalizes the hypotheses of Suppes and Zinnes by eliminating the assumption of positivity and  replacing the Archimedean and solvability axioms with a prohibition of anomalous pairs. The assumptions that remain are  necessary as well as sufficient for strong extensive measurement. Theorem 1 further generalizes these hypotheses by  eliminating the assumptions of commutativity and consistency of sign, and replacing the prohibition of anomalous pairs by a  weak Archimedean axiom. Even for weak extensive measurement, however, the Archimedean axiom cannot be entirely eliminated: if  Axiom 5 did not hold, it would always be possible to find an element with an infinite scale value. Axioms 1-3 are not strictly  necessary for weak extensive measurement, because such measurement does not restrict the ordering of elements with the same  scale value. In weak extensive measurement, elements that have the same scale value without being indifferent must either be  non-Archimedean (if their scale values are 0) or must form anomalous pairs (if their scale values are not 0), while in strong  extensive measurement, such elements are prohibited by the axioms. This fact sums up the difference between the two types of  measurement, and suggests that this difference would be very hard to detect in practice. The methods and results described by Fuchs (1963, Ch. 11) for such semigroups are extensively used in this paper.  Also, of course, the present results are applicable to ordered semigroups.
