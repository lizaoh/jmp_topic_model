Many kinds of processing could conceivably go on concurrently, but investigations have focused on only a few, such as concurrent encoding processes (e.g.,  Egeth & Dagenbach, 1991 ;  Pashler & Johnston, 1989 ), or, more controversially, concurrent response selection processes (e.g.,  Greenwald, 1972 ;  Meyer & Kieras, 1997a, b ;  Van Selst, Ruthruff, & Johnston, 1999 ). The difficulty is not so much in conceiving of processes that are likely to be concurrent; but in demonstrating concurrency. Here we use the method of selectively influencing process durations to show that production of a time interval is concurrent with a visual search.

In a time production task, a participant attempts to produce a target time interval by, e.g., pressing a button and then pressing it again when the target interval is judged to have elapsed. The use of selective influence, explained below, depends on the investigator's ability to find experimental factors each of which changes the duration of a single process, leaving other processes unchanged. This is difficult if processes share resources or send cross-talk to each other (e.g.,  Navon & Miller, 1987 ; Townsend & Thomas, 1994). At first it may seem that such concerns are irrelevant for the seemingly passive process of producing a time interval. However, the duration of a produced interval almost always increases or decreases as the load in a secondary task increases ( Brown, 1997 ;  Fortin & Breton, 1995 ;  Fortin & Massé, 1999 ). An increase is called  interference . In a review of over 80 experiments on time judgment (including time production),  Brown (1997)  found only nine experiments in which another simultaneous task did not produce interference. Of the nine, two were experiments on visual search by  Fortin, Rousseau, Bourque, and Kirouac (1993, Experiments 3 and 4) , and in one of these (Experiment 4) the duration of the produced interval actually decreased as load in the secondary task increased. Although the decrease is in the opposite direction from the interference usually found, it is an indication that an experimental factor influencing search may also have effects on time production.

Because the visual search task of  Fortin et al. (1993)  producing neither an increase nor a decrease in simultaneous time production is so unusual, it is worth investigating it further. In a visual search task, a participant searches for a target among distractors in a visual display. The experiment used circles, some with an additional vertical line, as stimuli. In the visual search literature, this particular task is often considered to be high in demand for attention but low in demand for short-term memory.  Fortin et al. (1993)  attribute its unusual behavior to its low short-term memory demand.

In the timing literature, interference is commonly interpreted by saying that during time production the accumulation of temporal information (pulses from a timer) is interrupted by simultaneous processing in the other task ( Gibbon, Church, & Meck, 1984 ;  Thomas & Weaver, 1975 ;  Zakay & Block, 1996 ). Interference is usually taken as evidence that the time production requires resources also needed by the other task, such as attention or short-term memory.

Some visual search tasks are thought to require attention, for example, searching for a conjunction of features like color and shape ( Treisman & Gelade, 1980 ). Because time production is also considered to require attention, the need to somehow share attention could lead to interference when visual search and time production are carried out at the same time (e.g.,  Coull, Vidal, Nazarian, & Macar, 2004 ). Therefore, it is noteworthy that in Experiments 3 and 4 of  Fortin et al. (1993) , the two different visual search tasks that did not produce interference as predicted by an accumulation interpretation are each considered to require attention.

In Experiment 4, the produced intervals shortened as search set size increased.  Fortin et al. (1993)  explain this with the hypothesis that a filled-duration illusion occurred with the visual displays. A filled-duration illusion occurs when an interval filled with a stimulus presentation is judged longer than an empty interval of the same duration. More generally, intervals may be judged as longer when they contain larger amounts of information, for example, more discrete elements or more stimulus motions ( Brown, 1995 ;  Buffardi, 1971 ;  Fraisse, 1963 ;  Thomas & Brown, 1974 ). The source of the illusion is not well understood, but a possibility is that events occurring in the filled-duration send irrelevant signals to the temporal information accumulator, and these irrelevant signals get counted. If the participant is timing the filled-duration, it will be judged as extra long. But suppose the participant is not using the accumulator to time the filled-duration, but to produce a time interval. In that case, the irrelevant signals from the filled-duration make the accumulator reach its criterion sooner than it ordinarily would, resulting in a shortened produced interval. In the dual task literature, such effects are often called cross-talk.

A new experiment on visual search and time production, closely following Experiment 3 of  Fortin et al. (1993) , was carried out. The visual task used in the present experiment is identical to one designed in  Treisman and Souther's (1985)  study on asymmetries in visual search (Treisman & Souther, Experiment 1). That it requires attention is agreed (see, e.g.,  Palmer, 1995 ;  Wolfe, 1998 ), that it uses memory is not (e.g.,  Horowitz & Wolfe, 1998 ;  Klein, 1988 ).

There were three reasons for doing the present experiment. First, the earlier experiment of  Fortin et al. (1993)  produced rarely found results, so it is worth testing that visual search can be carried out concurrently with time production using a different methodology than was used earlier. Because the earlier result leads us to expect concurrency, this also serves as a test of the selective influence methodology. Second, it is worth checking for evidence of cross-talk, and if evidence of it is found, determining whether it jeopardizes the tests for concurrent processing. Third, in the earlier experiments of  Fortin et al. (1993)  the visual display was presented near the beginning of the produced interval. Here, the visual search occurs near the end of the produced interval. Rather complicated events are sometimes hypothesized to occur at the end of a process (e.g.,  Chun & Wolfe, 1996 ;  Lejeune, 1998 ); see for example  Logan and Burkell (1986) , who discuss early and late bottlenecks. It is worth seeing whether there is any disruption produced by two processes completing at about the same time. In particular, because the participant must receive information from two different processes in order to decide when to respond, there may be more potential for cross-talk.

In the present study, factors are manipulated with the intention of selectively influencing certain processes in both the visual search and the time production tasks. Because the task is nearly identical to that in Experiment 3 of  Fortin et al.'s (1993)  study, it was predicted that visual search and time reproduction will be performed concurrently. Because there was evidence in Experiment 4 of Fortin et al.'s study that duration of visual search and time production might also be negatively correlated under some conditions, signs of a negative correlation were sought here.

The following sections are organized in two parts. The first part reports an experiment designed to determine whether time production and visual search were carried out concurrently in a process arrangement called a critical path network. The second part examines in detail predictions based on selective influence. Simulations demonstrate effects of manipulating factors on mean response times and on cumulative distribution functions (cdfs). Despite our design based on an earlier experiment not producing cross-talk, we found violations of selective influence in our data. An alternative assumption is that a factor influences the duration of more than one process through, e.g., cross-talk. This can lead to the violations, as shown by simulations. The data indicate that the violations found may be overcome by averaging over levels of irrelevant factors. Diagnostic tests demonstrating concurrency are then carried out.

In the experiment a time interval was presented to a participant, who then started to reproduce the interval with a keypress. After a variable delay following the keypress, a visual display was presented. When the participant judged that the time interval to be reproduced had elapsed and determined whether the target was present or absent in the visual display, a second keypress ended the trial.

Response time  is defined as the time between the keypress starting the interval reproduction and the (single) keypress ending the trial (see  Fig. 1 
                      ). Response time is the time needed to perform both tasks, temporal and visual. Consequently, the delay between the two keypresses includes the time necessary for temporal processing alone before presentation of the visual display, and a period of time for which temporal and visual processing are executed simultaneously. If temporal processing ends first, there should also be some time of visual processing alone. If visual search ends first, the participant has to wait until the end of the subjective target duration to execute the second keypress. Response time is thus an index of both temporal and visual processing times, and should therefore not be considered as a simple time interval reproduction.

The main activities in the task are presented in  Fig. 2A 
                       in a bar chart and in  Fig. 2B  in a critical path network. (More information about critical path networks is given in a later section.) The participant begins the reproduction of the time interval with a button press at  o . After a stimulus onset asynchrony (SOA), the visual display appears. The participant then searches the display, selects a response, and prepares the response. The processes involved in searching the display could all be represented individually, but as the predicted results would be the same, for simplicity, all the search processing is labelled Search. After the response has been selected and after the interval to be reproduced is over, the response is made with a button press at  r .

In the experiment, processing times were influenced by varying the duration of the interval to be reproduced in the time reproduction, and by varying display size and target presence/absence in the visual search. Also varied was the SOA. This is the time from the participant's button press to present a blank screen and start the reproduction of the interval until the presentation of the visual display. In the model, when the duration of the interval to be reproduced is increased, the duration of the interval the participant produces is prolonged; when the size of the display increases, the search process is prolonged; when the target is absent, rather than present, the search is prolonged; and, of course, when the interval between the start of time interval reproduction and the visual display is increased, the SOA is prolonged.

We note for choosing SOA values that the task was devised so temporal processing would be terminated first in some proportion of trials, and visual processing would be terminated first in other trials. The SOA values were chosen taking into account the time needed to search the display estimated from data in Experiment 3 of  Fortin et al. (1993) , in which the same visual search task was used in comparable conditions of practice and number of experimental trials. Another parameter considered was the durations to be reproduced in the present experiment, which were 2400, 3000, and 3600 ms. The same two values of SOA were used with each of the three target intervals. The two SOA values of 2200 and 2400 ms were judged likely to lead to a substantial proportion of trials in which visual search ended first and a substantial proportion of trials in which temporal processing ended first. For example, the longer SOA, 2400 ms, was equal to the shortest target interval, which ensured that time reproduction was finished before the search task in some trials.

Participants : Sixty-seven students from Purdue University, 40 men and 27 women (mean age, 19.5; SD , 2.09; range, 17–32 years old) volunteered to take part in the experiment in exchange for course credit. They were all naive to the purpose of the experiment.

Apparatus : A PC-compatible computer running MEL (Micro Experimental Laboratory) software-controlled stimulus and feedback presentation. Participants were seated in front of a monitor about 50 cm from the screen. Instructions, visual stimuli and feedback for the search task were presented on a computer screen. Intervals were recorded to the nearest millisecond. The space bar was used to make the instructions appear on the screen. The “0”, “1”, “2”, and “3” keys of the numerical keypad of the computer were used to start blocks and produce responses. The visual display was presented on the computer screen. Participants were tested individually in quiet rooms.

Stimuli : Displays were composed of circles of ∼1 cm diameter. Locations of targets (circles) and distractors (circles with an intersecting vertical line) in the visual display were randomly selected. The midpoint of the vertical line in a distractor intersected the circle at the lowest point of its circumference. The stimuli were white, presented on a black background screen.

Procedure : Three experimental sessions included five 36-trial blocks. The first block was a practice block and was therefore not included in the analyses. Participants were tested in three sessions. With very few exceptions, these were on three successive days.

As illustrated in  Fig. 1 , an experimental trial started with a fixation cross, which disappeared when the participant pressed the “0” key. This keypress immediately triggered the first tone (1200 Hz, 20 ms). When the target interval had elapsed after the first tone, a second identical tone was presented. There was a silent 500 ms delay after the second tone, after which the word “Reproduction” appeared in the center of the screen, indicating to the participant that the interval could be reproduced when ready. The reproduction was started by pressing the “2” key, which made the word “Reproduction” disappear on the screen. The screen remained empty during a delay, the SOA. After the SOA, the visual display appeared on the screen, and the display remained on until the participant respond by pressing the “1” or the “3” key to indicate the presence or absence of the target, respectively. Feedback “correct” or “error” was presented for 500 ms to inform the participant whether the response in visual search was correct or not. The feedback was followed by the next trial, which started again with the fixation cross. The task was to execute the second keypress when both tasks were finished, that is, when the participant judged that the target interval had elapsed  and  that the response to the visual task was decided.

The target interval to be reproduced (2400, 3000, or 3600 ms), the SOA (2200 or 2400 ms), display size (1, 6, or 12), and target presence or absence varied randomly from trial to trial, with the constraint that all combinations of factor levels were presented once in each 36-trial block. Participants were asked to remove their watches before the experimental session.

Data from 432 experimental trials were collected from each participant. Trials in which a wrong response was given to the visual search task were not included in the analyses; these were 3.06% of the original trials. Outliers, defined as response times for a combination of factor levels three standard deviations longer or shorter than mean response time for that combination were then removed; these were 1.11% of the remaining data. Results from analyses of variance on mean response times and errors are presented here. Later, after the model is described in more detail, we present results of the selective influence analysis.

Overall, the typical features of performance in time reproduction and visual search were obtained with mean response times. RTs increased with increasing display size, and were longer when the target was absent, as usually observed in visual search experiments. These factors also interacted, and the slope of response time as a function of display size in the target present condition was about half the slope in the target absent condition (16.0 versus 31.7 ms). These results confirm that participants were performing the two tasks in accordance with the instructions.

The critical path network introduced earlier for the experimental task is in  Fig. 2 . In it, the interval preceding the visual display (the SOA) and the visual search go on sequentially, but are concurrent with the reproduction of the time interval.

Processes in a critical path network are represented by vertices. The SOA is represented by a vertex as well, as are instantaneous events such as button presses. Arrows do not represent processes, rather an arrow from a vertex  a  to a vertex  b  indicates that the process represented by vertex  a  must be completed before the process indicated by vertex  b  can start. In that case, we say process  a immediately precedes  process  b.  A process can start when and only when all its immediate predecessors have finished. If there is a path from  a  to  b  going along the arrows in their proper directions, then  a precedes b . In that case, process  a  must be completed before process  b  can start. If either  a  precedes  b  or  b  precedes  a , we say  a  and  b  are  sequential . If there is no path from vertex  a  to vertex  b  following arrows in their proper directions, and no such path from  b  to  a , then we say processes  a  and  b  are  concurrent . Note that two processes are said to be concurrent if it is possible for them to be executed simultaneously, but it may happen that one is completed before the other starts.

The duration of a process is a nonnegative random variable. The duration of a path is the sum of the durations of the processes on the path. In a critical path network, all the processes must be completed for the task to be completed. On a particular trial, all the processes on the path with longest duration must be completed, and when they are completed the task is completed. Therefore, the time to complete the task is the sum of the durations of the processes on the longest path through the network, which is called the  critical path . For more on critical path networks, see e.g.,  Elmaghraby (1977) ,  Fisher and Glasser (1996) , and  Wenger (1999) .

A key idea in discovering how the unobservable mental processes in a task are organized is the notion of experimental factors that selectively influence different processes, proposed in the classic paper by  Sternberg (1969) . See  Sternberg (1998)  for a recent discussion. An experimental factor is said to selectively influence a certain process if it changes the duration of that process and leaves the durations of other processes unchanged. For example,  Sternberg (1969)  proposed that increasing the size of a set to be searched through prolongs the process of comparing the elements with the target, but does not change the durations of other processes, such as those concerned with the response.

To illustrate the predictions, and to examine potential violations due to cross-talk, simulations of the network in  Fig. 2  were carried out with Excel software. The patterns we predict would occur for almost any marginal distributions of process durations. As simulations require specific distributions, the durations of the processes were composed from mutually independent gamma distributions, with exponential and normal distributions as special cases (see, e.g.,  Fisher & Goldstein, 1983 ;  Goldstein & Fisher, 1991, 1992 ;  Luce, 1986 ;  Townsend & Ashby, 1983 ). In our simulations, we changed the durations of the interval, SOA and search. Our purpose is to illustrate the qualitative effects of selectively influencing one pair of sequential processes, SOA and search, and the effects of selectively influencing one pair of concurrent processes, interval and search. (Another pair of concurrent processes, SOA and search, could be illustrated, but would be redundant.) Simulation details are in  Appendix A . Results of the simulations show what the diagnostic patterns derived in previous work look like, and demonstrate that under more or less plausible circumstances the patterns are distinctive. Later, simulations will examine effects of violations of certain assumptions. The simulations are not an attempt to quantitatively fit our later data.

Cumulative distribution functions : Recently, qualitative predictions have been derived for cdfs, to augment predictions for means ( Nozawa, 1992 ;  Schweickert & Giorgini, 1999 ;  Schweickert, Giorgini, & Dzhafarov, 2000 ;  Townsend & Fific, 2004 ;  Townsend & Nozawa, 1995 ). In important work on interdependent process durations,  Dzhafarov (2003)  formulated a meaningful way for an experimental factor to selectively influence a process, even though the duration of that process is correlated with the durations of other processes. This leads to predictions derived for cdfs produced in critical path networks with correlated process durations ( Dzhafarov, Schweickert, & Sung, 2004 ). The form taken by correlations between mental process durations is not well understood, but there is considerable evidence that durations are sometimes not independent (e.g.,  Miller, 1982 ). Process durations cannot be correlated in arbitrary ways, if it is possible for factors to selectively influence the processes. The gist of the assumption made by  Dzhafarov (2003)  is that statistical dependencies between process durations arise only through a set of extraneous random variables. Given a fixed value for each extraneous random variable, the process durations are mutually statistically independent. The predictions described here all can be derived using that assumption.

The first prediction is that when the level of a factor is changed, the cumulative distributions for the response times satisfy stochastic dominance (called the Long RT Property by  Sternberg, 1973 ). A particular assumption relevant here is that when an experimental factor selectively influences a process, the cdfs for the process durations at the various levels of the factor satisfy stochastic dominance. Consider the duration of a process under two conditions that differ only in that the level of a factor selectively influencing the process is changed from one condition to the other. S tochastic dominance  is satisfied if for every time  t , the cdf for the process duration under one of the conditions lies on or above the cdf for the other condition (see, e.g.,  Shaked & Shanthikumar, 1994 ;  Townsend & Schweickert, 1989 ). Stochastic dominance for response times is defined in the analogous way. Several cases of stochastic dominance can be seen in the cdfs in  Fig. 3 
                         . The four cdfs in the figure arise from two levels of SOA crossed with two levels of display size. The cdf for the condition with both factors at their lower levels lies above the other three cdfs. It sometimes touches other cdfs, but it never crosses them. Similarly, the cdf for the condition with both factors at their higher levels lies on or below the other three cdfs, sometimes touching them, but never crossing them. Finally, the two cdfs for the conditions with one factor at its higher level and the other factor at its lower level lie in between the other two, sometimes touching, but never crossing, one of the other two. (The prediction of stochastic dominance does not require that the middle two cumulative distributions do not cross each other.)

Values of this expression are graphed in  Fig. 4  (bottom). It can be seen that the values start at 0, become positive, and then become negative. The negative area exceeds the positive area in  Fig. 4  (bottom). The fourth predicted pattern is more general: When sequential processes are selectively influenced, for any time  t , the summed positive and negative areas bounded by the cdf interaction contrast from  t  to infinity is negative (or zero).

If the above-predicted patterns do not occur in the data, one can safely conclude that no critical path network without a Wheatstone bridge exists in which the experimental factors selectively influence the processes ( Dzhafarovc et al., 2004 ;  Schweickert, 1978 ;  Schweickert et al., 1992 ;  Schweickert & Townsend, 1989 ).

A potential problem for the predictions is that a factor that changes the duration of a process may change the mean and the variance of the process duration in a way that violates stochastic dominance. We turn to the new question of effects of violations of certain assumptions.

Potential problem with variance decrease : There is considerable evidence that when a target is present, a visual search stops when a target is found (e.g.,  van Zandt & Townsend, 1993 ). Then response time variance may be lower on target absent trials than on target present trials ( Townsend & Ashby, 1983 ). One way this could happen is the following. Suppose on target present trials the search is self-terminating, that is, search stops as soon as the target is found. Then the search stops sometimes after one item has completed processing, and sometimes after several items have completed. Suppose on target absent trials, the search is exhaustive, that is, search always stops after processing of all items has completed. Then the mean will be longer on target absent trials, but the variance may be smaller. Such smaller variances for absence trials, with larger means, have been found in search tasks ( Schneider & Shiffrin, 1977 ;  Shiffrin & Schneider, 1977 ). Self-terminating and exhaustive search are discussed here only for purposes of an example, our response time analysis does not depend on assumptions about them. We note that although such smaller variances are possible, they may not occur very often, see  Ward & McClelland (1989) , and also note that evidence has been found that exhaustive processing does not always occur on absence trials (e. g.,  Bricolo, Gianesini, Fanini, Bundesen, & Chelazzi, 2002 ;  Cousineau & Shiffrin, 2004 ).

A decrease in variance is not itself contrary to our assumptions. But it could lead to a violation of stochastic dominance.  Fig. 5 
                          (top) illustrates hypothetical density functions for target present and target absent search durations. Note that the upper tails of the densities cross in such a way that the density for target present durations lies above that for target absent durations. It follows that at large values of  t , the cdf for target present durations will lie below that for target absent durations, violating the stochastic dominance we assumed.

The mean interaction contrasts were all positive ( Table 6 ). In summary, in the simulations there were nine combinations of factor levels for factors influencing concurrent processes. Of the corresponding 36 pairs of cdfs, only four had appreciable violations of stochastic dominance, the rest did not. Of the corresponding nine cdf interaction contrasts, only two had appreciable violations of nonnegativity, the rest did not. For the six combinations of factors influencing sequential processes, there were no appreciable violations of stochastic dominance or of interaction contrast predictions, although we have no reason to suspect that such would always be the case. The upshot is that assumption violations of the kind considered lead to results contrary to predictions for some process duration parameter values but not for others. The simulations suggest that to look for violations of stochastic dominance for process durations, the ideal conditions are when the mean response times are close together, so the distribution functions are close. An unconstrained negative correlation between two process durations particularly undermines the assumptions, because a higher factor level increases the variance while drawing the means closer. On the positive side, when mean response times are not close to each other in different conditions, the patterns predicted for selective influence tend to occur, despite the assumption violations. The simulations here support the reasonable rule of thumb that a factor which mostly influences one process, but somewhat influences a second process, tends to behave as if it selectively influences the first process. The simulations also demonstrate that it is important to have several diagnostic tests because one or two tests alone may miss something.

We now return to the results of the experiment. We (a) examine selective influence through a detailed analysis of cdfs in conditions where means are close, and (b) examine process arrangement through an overall analysis of interaction contrasts of mean response times and of cdfs of response times.

The alternative hypotheses discussed earlier can lead to violations of stochastic dominance in the upper and lower tails of the distributions. Violations are most likely to be found when the means are close, bringing the distributions close. There are six cases in which the means for two conditions are within 30 ms of each other ( Table 1 ), and these were examined.

The empirical cdf estimated at one bin depends on the frequencies accumulated at lower bins, making tests at several different bins hard to interpret. Hence, we tested histograms. Each pair of distributions to be tested was compared at four bins in the lower tails and four bins in the upper tails. For each pair, the percentiles for .05, .10, .15, and .20 in the lower tail and .80. .85, .90 and .95 in the upper tail were found for the distribution in the more difficult condition. These percentiles were then used as cutoffs for both distributions. If stochastic dominance is satisfied, for the four bins in the lower tail, the frequency for the less difficult condition is predicted to be greater than or equal to that for the more difficult condition. For the four bins in the upper tails, the direction of the prediction is reversed. All tests are one-tailed.

It is striking that all but one significant difference is in the lower tails, where violations would be expected if there is a negative correlation between search duration and reproduced interval duration. The one significant test at the upper tails may indicate a real effect, but it provides little information to work with, so we will not pursue it.

For the hypothesis of correlated search and interval durations, the six pairs of distributions are tested in four places in the lower tails, so there are 24 tests altogether. We would expect a few to be significant by chance, but five are significant. As an approximation, if we suppose the tests are independent, then with tests at the .05 level, the probability of five or more significant tests when the null hypothesis is true is less than .01. (We note that one  z  just exceeds the critical value, but the probability of four or more tests being significant is still less than .05.) The hypothesis of stochastic dominance at the lower tails is rejected, suggesting that there is a negative dependence between visual search and time reproduction. As we discuss below, this type of negative dependence is to be expected if a filled-duration illusion occurs because of cross-talk from the visual display (or visual search) contributing to the timing of the reproduced interval.

The negative dependence creates a problem for the assumption that display size and target presence/absence selectively influence the visual search. We cannot eliminate the problem, but fortunately we are able to overcome the observed violations of stochastic dominance by taking advantage of an extremely useful property. (Whether this overcomes violations for the unobserved process durations, we do not know, of course.)

The useful property is based on pooling. Suppose stochastic dominance is satisfied when we change the level of a certain relevant factor, at a given level of an irrelevant factor. But suppose for another level of the irrelevant factor, stochastic dominance is not satisfied when the level of the relevant factor is changed. If we pool over the levels of the irrelevant factor, the resulting cdf is a mixture of two distributions. That is, for level  i  of the relevant factor, the resulting cdf is  F i

Consider a time  t  at which stochastic dominance is violated for one component. If at this time  t  the magnitude of the change is bigger for the component that does satisfy stochastic dominance, the result is that the mixture distribution itself satisfies stochastic dominance at time  t . The upshot is that even if stochastic dominance is violated for individual cells in a design, one may be able to produce stochastic dominance by pooling over levels of irrelevant factors.

A further advantage of pooling is that interaction contrasts are variable, so pooling over irrelevant factors increases the precision of the estimates. The predictions are about the signs of the interaction contrasts, and they are distribution free. Consequently, averaging over levels of irrelevant factors does not alter the predicted patterns for a pair of factors.

Fortunately for our analysis, stochastic dominance is obtained for the response time distributions when we pool over levels of irrelevant factors.  Fig. 7

Pooling allows us to use the effects of factors to make inferences about the process arrangement, to which we now turn. For simplicity in what follows, we will speak of a factor as selectively influencing a certain process, when in fact it may be having small additional influences on other processes.

Display size and target presence/absence : Before we turn to pairs of factors which we think selectively influence two processes, we consider two factors which we think influence the same process. In  Table 8E , the interaction contrasts for display size and target presence/absence are positive. At first, this may seem to indicate that these two factors selectively influence two different sequential processes not on opposite sides of a Wheatstone bridge. However, in addition to the patterns mentioned in the introduction, when two factors selectively influence two sequential processes, the values of the mean interaction contrasts converge as the levels of the factors increase ( Schweickert, 1978 ;  Schweickert et al., 1992 ;  Schweickert & Wang, 1993 ). Here the effect of target presence/absence increases as display size increases. This result is found in many studies, indicating that the interaction contrasts typically diverge as display size increases. Consequently, display size and target presence/absence do not selectively influence two different processes. We consider both these factors to influence the search.

Sequential processes : The nonnegative interaction contrast between SOA and target presence/absence (for response times averaged over the other factors) supports the hypothesis that SOA and the search are sequential processes. As further support for this hypothesis, the interaction contrasts for SOA and display size (for response times averaged over the other factors) are also nonnegative, with the one exception of −37 ms (see D and F in  Table 8 ). The SOA precedes the display, so these factors would be expected to influence sequential processes. We cannot explain the exception and we suspect it is a fluke.

Concurrent processes : Mean interaction contrasts for the time interval factor paired with one of the other factors are all negative or zero but for one negligible exception of +1 ms (see A, B, and C in  Table 8 ). This clearly supports the hypothesis that each pair of factors selectively influences two concurrent processes. That is, the produced time interval and the SOA are concurrent, as are the produced time interval and the search. In short, the interaction contrasts of means indicate that the SOA and search are sequential, and both of these processes are concurrent with the produced interval.

One of the main goals of this experiment was to verify whether searching for a visual target can be performed concurrently with timing in our visual search and time reproduction task. The interaction contrasts for means indicate such concurrency. To bolster this conclusion, we examined interaction contrasts of cdfs. We pooled over levels of irrelevant factors. For a given level of one relevant factor and a given level of another relevant factor, cdfs were formed for each participant by collecting response times into 210 bins (ignoring levels of the other factors). The individual cdfs were then combined to make average cdfs. At a particular bin, the average cdf is the sum of the individual cdfs, divided by the number of participants. Note that a qualitative prediction true for each individual is true for the average; for example, if for some variable a positive value is predicted for every individual, then a positive value is predicted for the average.

Display size and target presence/absence : As discussed earlier, display size and target presence/absence both influence the search.  Fig. 7  (top) shows cdfs for display sizes 1 and 6 with targets present and absent. We pooled over levels of factors other than display size and target presence/absence. For understanding the cdfs for these two factors, two facts are important. First, the effect of target presence/absence is larger for larger display sizes. Second, for  m < n  a display of size  n  has a display of size  m  as a part, so that whatever must be done with a small display must also be done with a larger display. Small displays are finished before large displays.

The consequences can be seen in the cdfs of  Fig. 7  (top), for display sizes 1 and 6 with targets present and absent. At small times  t , some display size 1 trials have finished, but few display size 6 trials have finished. At small times  t , the two cdfs for display size 6 are nearly equal. Hence, at small times  t , the effect of target presence/absence for display size 1 dominates. The result is a small positive cdf interaction contrast at small times  t  ( Fig. 8 
                         ).

At large times  t , almost all display size 1 trials have finished and the participant is waiting for the interval to finish. For display size 1, the target absent cdf is nearly equal to the target present cdf (because the end of the interval determines the response time), so the display size 1 cdfs do not contribute to the cdf interaction contrast. However, at long times  t , some, but not all, display size 6 trials will have finished. Hence, at large times  t , the effect of target presence/absence for display size 6 dominates. The result is a large negative cdf interaction contrast at large times  t . (Of course, at very large times  t  all trials have finished, and the cdf interaction contrast is 0.)

At any time  t , the interaction contrast  IC ( t ) is the sum of a positive contribution from display size 1 and a negative contribution from display size 6. The area bounded by the interaction contrast function has a small positive region from  t =0 until some time  t = t ′, followed by a larger negative region after  t ′. Consequently, the net area bounded by  IC ( t ) from any time  t  to infinity is negative (or zero).

The patterns described for the cdf interaction contrast for display size and target presence/absence are the same as those predicted for two factors selectively influencing two sequential processes. Without additional analysis, one could be misled. However, our earlier analysis of the interaction contrasts for means ruled out the possibility that display size and target presence/absence selectively influence two sequential processes, and we accept the usual interpretation that display size and target presence/absence both influence the search. This demonstrates the importance of having more than one diagnostic test available.

Sequential processes : If we average over target present and target absent trials, then SOA and display size are expected to selectively influence two different sequential processes (namely, SOA and search). Likewise, if we average over display sizes, then SOA and target presence/absence are also expected to selectively influence the sequential processes of SOA and search. Representative cdfs for two levels of SOA and two levels of display size are in  Fig. 7  (middle). Note that our predictions are qualitative and distribution free, so if a prediction is true for each participant, it will be true for the average over participants.

When we pool over levels of irrelevant factors, stochastic dominance is satisfied as predicted ( Dzhafarov et al., 2004 ;  Schweickert & Giorgini, 1999 ;  Schweickert et al., 2000 ). Stochastic dominance is demonstrated in all other conditions as well.

Further predictions are that interaction contrasts for SOA and display size, and for SOA and target presence/absence will start at zero, may become positive, and change sign, from positive to negative. The signs may continue to alternate, but the total area under the interaction contrast curve will be non-positive. The net area bounded by the interaction contrast calculated from any point  t  to positive infinity is always non-positive.

In summary, there is a violation of stochastic dominance for response time cdfs of the form to be expected if there is cross-talk between the visual search and time reproduction. Averaging over levels of irrelevant factors removed the violation. It is quite likely that on some trials the visual search process and the time reproduction completed at about the same time, but there is no evidence that this produced a disruption. Predicted patterns in means and cdfs for response times supported overall the common sense hypothesis that the SOA preceded the search, although one combination of factor levels violated the prediction. Predicted patterns also supported the hypothesis that time reproduction went on concurrently with the SOA and, of more importance, concurrently and without interference with the visual search.

Overall, the results support a critical path network in which the reproduction of the time interval goes on concurrently with the visual search. The simplest such network consistent with the data is shown in  Fig. 2 . Evidence for concurrent visual search and time reproduction was found in earlier experiments by  Fortin et al. (1993) , although in those experiments the search task occurred nearer the beginning of the produced interval, rather than towards the end, as here.

Display size and target presence/absence produce patterns in interaction contrasts for response time means and cdfs like the patterns predicted for two factors selectively influencing two sequential processes. However, a further prediction from that hypothesis fails in our data and those in the literature, namely that the interaction contrasts converge as display sizes increase. Our interpretation is the usual one, that display size and target presence/absence both influence the search.

Some violations of stochastic dominance for response time cdfs can be explained if cross-talk occurs in this task. All but one of the violations of stochastic dominance are where one would expect them if search and reproduced time interval are negatively dependent. This would be expected if participants judge that more time elapsed when a larger display was presented.

This relationship, temporal overestimation of stimuli or events containing larger amounts of information, is generally referred to as a filled-duration illusion ( Fraisse, 1963 ). For example, when participants were asked to judge the duration of a 40 or 80 ms visual field that was either blank or filled with three letters, filled fields were judged longer than blank fields ( Thomas & Weaver, 1975 ). This finding was explained by assuming that time estimates are based on the output of two processors, a timer and a visual processor. A similar distinction was made in a more recent attentional model of time estimation that was inspired by Thomas and Weaver's work ( Zakay, 1989 ).

In these models, attention is shared between two processors. The output of the timer is monotonic in time,  t . The other processor, in this case a visual processor, produces an output that is received by the timer and can be used for temporal judgement, although this may not be its primary function. For example, perhaps the visual processor produces a signal when each item is completed. This output increases as display size increases. When the display is larger, the timer has information that more time has passed than actually has passed. Hence the participant stops the reproduced interval earlier.

Note that in the task here, visual search would influence time judgment for a very small portion of the total response time because visual search occurs near the end of the trial in our task. In fact, the reproduced interval is empty for most of its duration, a part which would be judged essentially from the output of the timer.

If visual search was carried out and completed during the reproduced time interval, and a response was made to indicate when the time interval ends, the result of a filled interval illusion would be a shortening of reproduced intervals with increasing set size. A shortening was actually observed in a previous experiment, in which a slightly different visual search task (searching for a conjunction of features) was interpolated in time production ( Fortin et al., 1993 , Experiment 4). In the present experiment, on some trials the time interval ended last while on other trials the visual search ended last. If a filled interval illusion is occurring, then increasing display size has two opposite effects, increasing the visual search duration while decreasing the time interval. Whether response times increase or decrease as display size increases depends on the balance between these competing effects. In our situation, mean response times increased as display size increased. However, the cumulative distribution analyses suggest that a negative dependency occurred with our visual search and time reproduction task, as in Fortin et al.'s Experiment 4.

The search task of  Fortin et al. (1993)  Experiment 3 is nearly identical to that used here. Fortin et al. did not find evidence of cross-talk. If the tasks are so similar, is there any reason to think there would be cross-talk here, but not in the earlier experiment? In the paradigm of Fortin et al. the visual search task was presented early in the reproduced interval. The search was always completed well before the response was made. Hence, to determine when to respond, the visual search could be ignored. Here, the search could end after the interval, so the participant must attend to the search for information about when to respond.

Cross-talk would be produced in this situation if the search does not send a single piece of information to indicate when it is over, but instead sends several pieces of information to indicate when parts of it, such as individual items, are over. A variant of this possibility is that the search is ended based on the time devoted to it, rather than on items completed. Because timing an interval is a necessary part of our task, participants may be inclined to use a timing strategy for the search task as well (even though it may not be the most accurate strategy available). If such is the case, and if a single timer is serving both tasks, information about time spent processing of the display will be sent to the timer, becoming a source of cross-talk. Our data suggest that cross-talk is occurring, but do not inform us about the mechanism producing it.

A visual search task identical to that in Experiment 3 of  Fortin et al.'s (1993)  was carried out with time reproduction. In the present study, the search task was performed near the end of interval reproduction rather than at the beginning in order to apply a different method of analysis for this task, selective influence for task networks. The method is based on factors selectively influencing processes in a critical path network. The results lead again to the conclusion that timing can go on concurrently with a visual search task that demands attention, and that there may have been a negative dependency between reproduced time and visual display size. These findings converge remarkably with results obtained in the previous experiments where visual search was interpolated in time interval production ( Fortin et al., 1993 ). The conclusions of the present study offer clear support to these previous results. They also demonstrate that selective influence is useful for examining process arrangements not only with reaction time tasks but also with time interval reproduction.

In the simulations, durations of the visual search task were formed by adding two components. One had an exponential distribution with mean 25, 300, or 600 ms, for display sizes 1, 6, and 12, respectively. The other component had a gamma distribution with mean  αβ =100 ms and parameter  β =100/16. An exponential distribution for searching the display could arise, e.g., when the target is present, and the search is self-terminating, with processing time for the target decreasing with the number of elements in the display (see  Townsend & Ashby, 1983, p. 213 ). The sum of exponential and gamma random variables is similar to the sum of exponential and normal random variables (the ex-Gaussian) used as an approximation for response times by  Ratcliff (1978) , see  Luce (1986)  for more details. In the simulations, the duration of the produced interval had a normal distribution with mean of 2400, 3000, or 3600 ms, depending on the condition; for each condition the variance equalled the mean. (As the normal distribution is a limiting case of a gamma distribution, these normal distributions are almost exactly the same as gamma distributions with  α  equal to 2400, 3000 or 3600 ms and parameter  β =1 in each case.) The duration of the SOA was a fixed number, 2200 or 2400 ms. Ten thousand simulated trials were generated for each combination of factor levels.

A Wheatstone bridge is a relatively complex structure, so for parsimony one would not pursue the possibility without a good suggestion in the data that one is present. Logically, we can rule out the possibility that the SOA and the produced interval are sequential processes, in a Wheatstone bridge or otherwise, because the SOA and the produced interval start at the same point.

For a given pair of factors, data that include some cdf interaction contrasts that are completely positive and some having a negative section following a positive section could be an indication of a Wheatstone bridge (see  Fig. 11  of  Schweickert & Giorgini, 1999 ). For the produced interval and search, the negative sections of the cdf interaction contrasts are small and unsystematic. One of the largest negative sections of a cdf interaction contrast involving the target interval and the search occurs with the factors of target interval and target presence/absence in  IC 3 in Fig. 11B. But the entire negative area bounded by  IC 3 is on the order of 1 ms, and this could easily occur by chance. The data give no reason to pursue a Wheatstone bridge.
