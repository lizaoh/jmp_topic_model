COWAN  Department of Psychology, Kansas State University, Manhattan, Kansas 66506  Impossible figures were regarded neither as new perceptual phenomena, nor as examples of known phenomena such as illusions.  They were seen as geometric anomalies, not psychological ones, which serve a heuristic function in the study of perception.  With this in mind, a topological analysis was undertaken with the anticipation of the following goals: (a) A systematic way  (algorithm) of generating these figures should be evident; (b) simple properties of impossible figures which may not have been  previously understood should be discovered. Both of these conditions were met.  Impossible figures have occupied a peculiar niche in the psychology of perception. Most everyone agrees that they are curious  perceptual phenomena, but most investigators treat them only in a descriptive way (e.g., Gregory, 1970). Those who have  studied Escher's graphics (1971) analytically have had much to say about his tesselations (figure-ground packing) but nothing  about his impossible figure artistry (see Coxeter, 1961, and Escher, 1971). Hochberg (1968) has suggested some ingenious ways  of studying impossible figures but his theoretical treatment of them is sparse.  Our knowledge of impossible figures is such that even the most fundamental questions regarding them remain unanswered. In fact  one might ask if these figures are the proper study of the psychologist or the geometer (or both). To be sure, they can serve  as a device for the psychologist to aid in the study of other behaviors, but do they represent some perceptual phenomenon in  their own right ? Penrose and Penrose (1958) claim they do, and they classify impossible figures as illusions. However, they  are probably not illusions, since illusions are figures which are physically possible but erroneously perceived. Our  perceptions of impossible figures can be quite veridical but these figures, as drawn, are physically unreal.  One reason for our ignorance, perhaps, is that there is no known systematic way of generating these figures. Hence, most  impossible figures remain outside our experience. The act of drawing a figure is one of reproducing what one has seen before  either through experience or imagination. The set of unknown impossible figures has by definition never been subject to our  experience, and not all of us possess the imagination of an Escher. Even with Escher's fertile vision it is doubtful that he  could anticipate every variety of these figures. If an algorithm for their generation were  190  Copyright 1974 by Academic Press, Inc. BRAID REPRESENTATION OF CORNERED TORUSES  The braid representation of corners and their combinations has been treated informally in the preceding paragraphs. In this  section a more formal treatment will be given, and some rules of composition will be derived.  The set of structural elements include creases, edges, strips, strip lines, corners, and sides. These have already been  introduced, and their formal definitions will be listed without comment.  DEFINITION. A torus crease is a surface line which follows a closed cross cut and not a loop cut line.  DEFINITION. A torus edge is a surface line which follows a closed loop cut and not a cross cut line.  DEFINITION. A strip is the surface area bounded by any two designated edges.  DEFINITION. An edge line which can unambiguously represent one and only one designated strip is a strip line.  DEFINITION. Any cross cut section of a torus which contains crease lines on a single cross cut line is a corner element.  Different corner elements are distinguishable by their pattern of crease lines.  DEFINITION. Any cross cut section of a torus which contains no crease line is a side element.  DEFINITION. The set of corner and side elements is called the set of geometric elements.  There are two important ways of stipulating strip line location. These are given in the following definitions.  DEFINITION. The location of strip lines around a cross cut line on a three dimensional representation of a torus will  determine strip line level.  IMPOSSIBLE FIGURES 199  DEFINITION. The outside to inside location of strip lines on a two dimensional representation of a torus will determine strip  line position.  It is interesting to note that the differences between these two definitions are based on differences in dimensional  representation. This distinction makes it possible to represent the geometric elements by braids, since the first definition  establishes the underpass-overpass pattern, and the second defines the positions of the lines on the braid frame. This braid  representation will be stated as the single axiom of this system.  Axiom OF REPRESENTATION. Each distinguishable element within a given class of geometric elements (corners, sides) can be  represented by a unique set of braids formed by the weaving pattern of the strip lines of that element. The axiom and definitions above establish that the geometric elements can be represented by braids, but nothing has been said  about how corner (or geometric) elements can be joined. The fact that these elements are represented by braids suggests that  corner elements are combinable only if their braids are combinable. Since Artin's theorem states that braids form a groupoid  under given conditions of combination, it is necessary here to explicitly state what these conditions are for the braids in  the braid table. It was pointed out earlier that strip lines remain level invariant as they are reflected around a corner,  only their positions can change (see Fig. 2). This characteristic will determine the rules for braid combination, and the  concept of composition of geometric elements (and their braids) can now be defined.  DEFINITION. Two braids representing two geometric elements are said to be L-composable if, after joining, their strip line  levels are unaltered. GENERATION OF IMPOSSIBLE FIGURES  Impossible torus figures have at least one side that changes its level. In Fig. lb, for example, the strip line drops from top  to side as it goes from x to y. This warrants further exploration, and Fig. 7c will be used as an example in the discussion  that follows.  There is a tendency to perceive Fig. 7c as three segments lying along x, y, and z coordinates in three-space. If Fig. 7c were  truly three dimensional, and the segments were forced into a common plane, the middle segment would be twisted in the manner  shown in the upper drawing. However, the three segments in Fig. 7c can be made to lie on a common plane by perceptual means  alone (i.e., without the aid of physical distortion). The Z corner to the right of the figure can be seen as lying flat on the  two dimensional surface of the page. In this position the t strip at this corner is seen face up and parallel to the surface  of the page. The t strip at the N corner to the left of the figure can also be perceived as face upward and parallel to the  page. This implies that the N corner and the Z corner both lie in the same plane as the page at least when viewed  independently. Can the two corners be seen as coplanar when regarded as a unit ? The answer is a qualified "yes" if the  remainder of the torus is drawn to suggest it. One way to keep the segment attached to the second of the two corners from  perceptually jutting out of the plane of the first corner is to complete the torus with elements which unambiguously occupy  the plane of the first corner. Figures 7e and f are two such completed toruses using the elements in Fig. 7c and b,  respectively. The reason for restricting the analysis to figures in two space now seems apparent. The impossibility of these  figures implies the attempt to force all segments into a single plane by perceptual means.  Now compare Fig. 7c with Fig. 2c. There is a surprising equivalence between these corner and side elements that might be  explained as follows. The top strip of Fig. 2c occupies an inside position at the origin but an outside position at the  terminus. If this corner is straightened, while at the same time preserving this top strip characteristic, then the braid form  is preserved, and the real twisted segment depicted at the top of Fig. 7c is created. But the strip variations of the real  twisted strip are equivalent to those of the "perceptually" twisted strip below it; thus a braid from Z-1 (specifically braid  Zii) can be used to represent either the corner element in Fig. 2c or the "impossible" side element of Fig. 7c. More importantly, the results of Theorem 2 are applicable to the side as well as the corner braids either  independently (if they are L-composable) or joined. Parts 2 and 3 establish that the torus figure is an impossible figure by definition.  The following corollary suggests the most direct way in which an impossible figure can be formed.  COROLLARY. If a closing product of n (where n _>- 3) corners is selected so that, at least once in the selection of corners,  the direction of choice violates the arrows of L-composition, then an impossible figure will be created, but the unselected  side elements cannot be omitted from the braid equation.  The side elements can be omitted from the selection of elements for the geometric construction, since the sides are  indistinguishable in their geometric form. They must be included in the braid form, however, because at least one of the side  braids is from Z or Z-1, and, if omitted, the two corner braids would not be L-composable by the preceding lemma. ROTATIONS, INVERSES, EVERSES AND OBVERSES  The cyclic rotation of the toruses has an uninteresting effect on the visible appearance of the figures; the step-wise  rotation of the braids past fixed geometric corners and sides does not. Such a rotation carries the side braids to the corners  then back to the sides again. In the case of the possible figures (with included sides) the side to corner rotation is likely  to lead to an impossible figure. Because the sides of possible figures are always represented by M* or N* braids, the one-step  braid rotation will include no Z or Z-1 corners. These possible-impossible figure pairs created by rotation may possess  interesting, and as yet undiscovered, interrelationships. The theory reflects the  characteristics of both possible and impossible figures, and new impossible figures can be generated as a result. Furthermore,  previously unknown features of impossible figures, such as the representations of their opposite sides, can now be determined.  The theory can at least generalize to figures of n sides and figures whose corners are indented.  At the beginning of this paper the question was asked if the study of impossible figures was properly within the domain of  geometry or psychology. The approach here was clearly from the standpoint of geometry. It is evident that some of the  "impossible" figures produced here are psychologically more impossible than others; the psychologist might well disagree with  the geometer about the definition of figure impossibility.  Yet the development here has provided the psychologist with a generative procedure by which large sets of impossible figures  can be produced for him to study, and this might be of some use to those interested in perception and cognition. Shepard and  his collaborators (Shepard & Chipman, 1970; Shepard & Metzler, 1971; Shepard & Feng, 1972) have studied the problem of mental  manipulation of geometric relationships. Now that the reverse side of an impossible figure can be determined, we might ask if  it is possible to rotate an impossible figure mentally and correctly reconstruct the opposing side.  The analysis suggested here might also shed some light on the general question of  IMPOSSIBLE FIGURES 211  figure reversal. A Neckar cube might be considered as a torus with a hole of zero dimensions. The reversal of such a cube can  be seen as the passage of the inside points to the outside-much like sphere eversion (see Philips, 1966). In the present  context this problem becomes one of finding and studying everse transformations.  The use of braid theory has opened the way to other generalizations regarding impossible figures. For instance, the figures  discussed here all have square cross cuts. Presumably, figures with n-sided cross cuts could come under braid analysis.  Prismatic cross cut figures and ribbon figures, for example, would be represented by three string and two string braids  respectively. The problem reduces to one of determining the set of corner elements and their braid representations.  Finally, I would like to add a note about the formal development of the theory. The careful reader might have wondered about  the strict adherence to level invariance since this made it impossible to deal with the strip lines resulting from the  original dissection shown in Fig. 1. The strip line in Fig. lb, which traces the path taken by the strip in Fig. lc, clearly  changes both level and position. Could another system of analysis based on position invariance be developed? Would this come  closer to dealing directly with the stripline configurations in Fig. lb? We might also ask if some more general theory, such  as the theory of knots, (Crowell & Fox, 1963), might be even better suited for this problem.  REFERENCES  ALBERS, E. Zeichnungen.
