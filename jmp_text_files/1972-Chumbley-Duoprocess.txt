CHUMBLEY  University of Massachusetts, Amherst, Massachusetts 01002  A theory of concept learning which separates the learning process into attribute identification and paired-associate learning  stages is proposed and applied in detail to a 4-choice conjunctive task. The quantitative predictions of the theory were found  to be as good as a model by Trabasso and Bower (1964) who viewed learning in terms of one-dimensional subproblems. The  duoprocess model has two advantages over the subproblem learning theory. It has more plausible process assumptions with  reference to the 4-choice conjunctive task and its process assumptions are general enough to permit application of the model  to a variety of concept learning tasks.  Within the last decade it has been demonstrated that hypothesis testing theories of concept learning can quite adequately  describe the data from simple two-choice concept identification and discrimination learning tasks. These theories (Bower and  Trabasso, 1964; Chumbley, 1969; Erickson, 1968; Levine, 1970; Restle, 1962; Trabasso and Bower, 1968) have not been applied  with any degree of success to concept learning paradigms involving either solutions based on more than one stimulus dimension  or solutions involving learning to sort the stimuli into more than two categories. The two possible exceptions to this  statement are contained in papers by Trabasso and Bower (1964) and Bower and Trabasso (1964). In the former paper it was  assumed that subjects test hypotheses about the solutions to subproblems. A theory based on this assumption was presented and  applied to a four-choice conjunctive concept learning task. Very good fits to the data were obtained but it will be shown that  the theory assumes two psychological processes of questionable plausability and it cannot be applied to other paradigms. The  other theory proposed by Bower and Trabasso (1964), a duoprocess theory, was presented in an appendix to a paper devoted  primarily to simple two-choice concept identification. It was designed specifically to handle data they had obtained in a  problem which required learning how to sort stimuli with four-valued dimensions into two or four categories. The purpose of the present paper  is to present a theory of complex concept learning which is identical in spirit and quite similar in form to the Bower and  Trabasso duoprocess theory. It will be argued that the duoprocess theory considered here provides quite adequate fits to the  data used to support the Trabasso and Bower subproblem learning theory, is more compatible with current theory and data than  is their subproblem theory, and has the important advantage of being applicable to a wide variety of concept learning  situations.  The duoprocess theory assumes that in any concept identification problem there are potentially two main stages, an attribute  identification stage and a paired-associate learning stage. This assumption has been proposed by several other investigators  (Bourne, Dodd, Guy and Justesen, 1968; Haygood and Bourne, 1965; Richardson and Bergum, 1954; Trabasso and Bower, 1964), but  only Bower and Trabasso (1964) carried the suggestion through to an explicit model of concept identification. The duoprocess  theory asserts that the subject in a concept identification problem first tests hypotheses about which dimension(s) or  attribute(s) are relevant. Once he has solved this part of the problem by finding that, e.g., orange circles and only orange  circles require the response "one," he learns a short paired-associate list whose stimuli are the other combinations of the  values of the relevant dimensions, in this example, orange triangles, blue circles, and blue triangles, and whose responses  are the available responses other than the response "one." In the simple two-choice problem where the solution depends upon  only one binary-valued dimension, the paired-associate stage does not exist since the subject, having found a correct  hypothesis for one response, can be correct in using the other responses by a process of elimination. In more complex  problems, however, the paired-associate stage may comprise a substantial part of the learning task. The present paper will  concern itself with these more complex paradigms since there is an abundance of theoretical literature covering the simpler  concept learning and discrimination learning paradigms. Because the Trabasso and Bower (1964) data set is probably the most  complete published set and their subproblem learning theory provides a good basis for comparing the fit of theory to data, the  special case of the duoprocess theory for their task will be examined first.  Application of the Theory to Four-Choice Conjunctive Problems  During the attribute identification stage of learning, it is assumed that the subject determines which pair of stimulus  dimensions is relevant by testing hypotheses, one hypothesis at a time. Each time he forms a hypothesis, he randomly selects a  pair of stimulus dimensions, and uses the stimulus and reinforcement of the current trial to form a locally consistent  hypothesis. He then assumes this hypothesis is correct, memorizes it carefully, and starts to learn the remaining three-item  paired-associate list. If he has not selected the relevant attributes in forming his hypothesis, he will  A DUOPROCESS THEORY OF CONCEPT LEARNING 19  eventually make an infirming error. An infirming error is the result of one of the following two events:  (a) the hypothesis stimulus pattern is present and the correct response is other than the hypothesis response;  (b) the correct response is the hypothesis response, and the hypothesis stimulus pattern is not present.  When a infirming error occurs, he will start over again with a new hypothesis and a new paired-associate list. Errors which  are not the result of one of these two events, i.e., noninfirming errors, do not occasion the selection of a new hypothesis.  It is assumed that the subject's reaction to noninfirming errors is the belief that he has not yet learned the  paired-associate list. Of course, if he has chosen the relevant stimulus dimensions, he will make no further errors with the  hypothesis response and will eventually learn to make correct responses to the other combinations of relevant dimension values. In the Trabasso and Bower (1964) model, the subjects solve a four-choice conjunctive concept identification problem by solving  the two logical subproblems which combine to form the total problem. For the problem in Table 1, one can define two  subproblems based on color and form. The color subproblem would be: orange objects are either a "one" or a "two" and blue  objects are either a "three" or a "four." The subjects work concurrently and independently on the two subproblems. When both  are solved the subject has solved the total problem. The probability the subject makes a response which is correct with  respect to both subproblems is the product of the probability correct on the color subproblem and the probability correct on  the form subproblem. This multiplication rule follows from the assumption of subproblem independence.  Trabasso and Bower conducted an ingenious test of the independence assumption before utilizing it in their theory. Three  groups of 40 subjects each were run in an experiment. Group C subjects were told that form was a relevant dimension and were  given the solution to the form subproblem. They then proceeded to learn the color subproblem under the otherwise normal  four-choice concept identification procedure. Group F subjects were likewise told that color was relevant and learned only the  form subproblem. Subjects in Group CF learned both subproblems knowing only that there were two relevant dimensions and the  nature of the rule to be used in combining them. Using the data from these three groups of subjects, Trabasso and Bower then  demonstrated that the learning curve for Group CF could be predicted very accurately from the learning curve for Groups C and  F by using the multiplication rule. This was done for two experiments and, they were even able to predict the learning curves  for Groups C and F by examining subproblem learning in Group CF.  Application of the duoprocess theory to the task presented to Groups C and F requires no modification of the basic premises of  the theory but some changes in the details of specification. Most heories of simple concept learning make a distinction between the amount of learning that occurs on error trials and the  amount which occurs on trials where a correct response is made. A common, but not universal assumption, is that resampling  occurs only on error trials. Theories incorporating this assumption (Bower and Trabasso, 1964; Trabasso and Bower, 1968) have  been very successful in handling a variety of data from simple two-choice problems. The subproblem learning theory proposed by  Trabasso and Bower incorporates the assumption that subjects in  A DUOPROCESS THEORY OF CONCEPT LEARNING 29  Groups C and F were solving their subproblems in the same way as subjects in a normal two-choice problem. However, the  subproblem learning theory proposed makes no distinction between correct and error trials. It can be shown that if the  subproblem theory is modified to assume resampling only on errors, the ability to predict the statistics for Group CF by using  parameter values obtained from Groups C and F is lost. Thus, if we are to accept the subproblem learning theory, we must also  accept the conlusion that the underlying processes in simple concept identification and those in complex concept  identification are markedly different. The duoprocess theory, on the other hand, has as a special case the Bower and Trabasso  (1964) learning-on-errors theory. It can also be pointed out that other theories of simple concept identification could be  utilized in describing the attribute identification stage of learning. In particular, all such theories which have the Bower  and Trabasso theory as a special case will do quite nicely.  A second problem with the subproblem theory is the multiplication rule. While applying this rule to response probabilities  leads to predictions in agreement with at least some data, the rationale underlying its use does not seem appropriate for  applying it to a learning process based on hypothesis testing. Trabasso and Bower assume that the subject tests hypotheses  about the subproblem solutions with the hypotheses selected at random and with replacement. This means that a subject could,  therefore, theoretically have solved the color subproblem and be testing a color hypothesis for the second subproblem. Since  color is a salient cue, this would theoretically occur with a fairly high probability. This prediction of the Trabasso and  Bower theory is almost certainly wrong but is a necessary consequence of assuming the subject learns two independent  subproblems. The duoprocess theory, of course, neither implies nor utilizes the subproblem independence assumption in any form  but can predict data which seem to support the multiplication rule.  Application of the Duoprocess Theory in other Situations  One of the advantages of conceptually separating the complex concept learning task into attribute identification and  paired-associate stages was quite nicely demonstrated by a model proposed by Bower and Trabasso (1964). They took full  advantage of the separation of stages to handle the data from a concept identification task in which each of the three  stimulus dimensions had four values. The model, which was applied with partial success to two data sets and then never  mentioned again, differs appreciably in the conceptualization of the attribute identification stage from the model proposed in  this paper. The primary reason the Bower and Trabasso duoprocess theory does not fit the data of the two-choice group is their assumption  of how the subject behaves after he has learned three stimulus-response assignments. In the two-choice group, there were two  responses each paired with two values of the relevant dimension. Bower and Trabasso assumed that the subject would not notice  this fact and that P(C I, Z') would be equal to 1/2 throughout learning. This choice makes possible a convenient estimator for  0. The P(C I U) would be 1/2 until three stimulus-response assignments had been learned and then be 1.0. In other words, the  assumption was made that the subject would not notice the way the stimuli were mapped on to the response.  One basic modification of the duoprocess theory was also made in determining that i = 1/4 for the two-choice group. In cases  where the number of available responses is less than the number of stimulus patterns to be discriminated, the second of the  two conditions specified above as sufficient for infirming a hypothesis might be dropped. The first condition must still be  retained but a hypothesis might not be infirmed if the correct response is the hypothesis response and the hypothesis stimulus  pattern is not present. In fact, a subject behaving as the duoprocess theory specified must drop the second condition to learn  the two-choice task.  Testing quantitative predictions from the duoprocess theory with existing data  32 CHUMBLEY  from tasks other than those described above is complicated by some of the experimental materials and procedures which have  been used. The applications presented were facilitated by the fact that the subjects either received detailed information  about the structure of the problem through instructions or the structure was relatively obvious given the stimulus  characteristics and the number of response alternatives available. Tasks of this type have been appropriately labeled  attribute identification tasks by Haygood and Bourne (1965). In other types of tasks, rule learning or identification and  complete learning, the basic problems the subject encounters can be attributed to difficulty in learning how to characterize  the stimulus patterns being presented. Haygood and Bourne (1965) have proposed that what a subject learns is a truth table  strategy. This strategy involves learning to sort the stimuli into four classes, TT, TF, FT, and FF, depending upon whether or  not a given relevant attribute is present. The duoprocess theory in its present stage of development does not specify how a  subject learns the truth table strategy. However, given a subject with a truth table strategy, the duoprocess theory should be  applicable to his behavior. In fact, the Trabasso and Bower (1964) experiments involved just this situation. It is not yet  known whether the duoprocess theory can handle the data generated by a subject using the truth table strategy in rule learning  or complete learning. Acquisition of the data for tests of the theory in these situations is now in progress.
