Maximum Likelihood Estimation of Factor and Ideal Point Models for Paired Comparison Data 

Rung-Ching Tsai and Ulf Bo�ckenholt 

University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign 

Many paired comparison data sets reported in the literature are obtained in a multiple judgment setting where each judge compares all possible item pairs one at a time. Despite the repeated measures structure of the data, typically the multiple judgments are analyzed under the strong assumption of independence. This disregard for dependencies in the paired comparison judgments is a serious model misspecification that may lead to incorrect statistical and substantive conclusions. Building on Takane's (1987, Cognition and Communication, 20, 45�62) analysis of covariance structures models, we present a variant of the Monte Carlo expectation maximization (MCEM) algorithm for estimating probabilistic paired comparison models of multiple paired comparison judgments. The MCEM algorithm is straightforward to implement and converges quickly even when the paired comparison data are sparse. A detailed analysis of a paired comparison experiment illustrates the usefulness of this approach for the interpretation of similarity and individual difference effects in preference data. � 2001 Elsevier Science 

Key Words: maximum likelihood estimation; wandering ideal point model; paired comparison data. 

1. INTRODUCTION 

The method of paired comparisons (PCs) is an attractive data collection tool for three reasons. First, by asking for a comparison of two items at a time, the PC method imposes minimal constraints on the response behavior of a judge (Bock 6 Jones, 1968). Second, internal consistency checks are available which allow for the identification of judges who are systematically inconsistent in their judgments (Kendall, 1975; van Acker, 1990). Third, paired comparison data provide a rich source of information about individual differences and similarity relationships in the evaluation of the choice options. Interestingly, the last point has received little attention in the literature. Despite the availability of theoretical proposals for the

Instead, paired comparison data are typically aggregated over judges and then analyzed under the strong assumption that the paired comparison judgments are independent. For example, although Rumelhart and Greeno (1971) obtained their paired comparison data by asking respondents to compare all item pairs, their data were analyzed numerous times in the literature without taking into account the repeated-measures structure (Bo�ckenholt 6 Gaul, 1986; Carroll 6 De Soete, 1991; Mellers 6 Biagini, 1994; Takane, 1980; Edgell 6 Geisler, 1980). Not surprisingly, rather different interpretations of the data were reported, all of which are likely to be inconclusive since they neglected dependencies in the data introduced by the multiple pairwise judgments (Bo�ckenholt 6 Dillon, 1997). 

The purpose of this paper is to present maximum likelihood methods for estimating and testing multiple judgment data within a Thurstonian framework (Thurstone, 1927). Our approach builds on Takane's (1987) analysis of covariance structures (ACOVS) models because they allow for a separate treatment of withinand between-judge sources of variability. We present factor-analytic and ideal-point representations and show that these representations are indistinguishable on the basis of paired comparison data. In addition, we discuss several special cases, some of which have been proposed previously in the literature. The main part of the paper focuses on the Monte Carlo expectation maximization (MCEM) algorithm (Chan 6 Kuk, 1997; McCulloch, 1994, 1997; Meng 6 Schilling, 1996; Wei 6 Tanner, 1990; Yu, Lam, 6 Lo, 1998) as a general approach for estimating individual difference effects in paired comparison data. Although it has been noted in the literature that estimation of random effects is difficult when the number of items exceeds three (Bo�ckenholt 6 Dillon, 1997; Takane, 1987), it is shown here that the MCEM algorithm provides an attractive solution to this problem that is straightforward to implement. 

The remainder of this paper is structured as follows. First, we discuss and compare factor-analytic and ideal-point paired comparison models. Next, we give details on the MCEM algorithm for parameter estimation and model testing. Next, we present an application similar to the Rumelhart and Greeno (1971) study to illustrate the estimation and interpretation of both similarity and individual difference effects.

Let %#(+, 4, 9) and Z be n_R matrices of the latent factor scores. In this section we describe the maximum likelihood estimation of % by the Monte Carlo EM algorithm (Tanner, 1996). The high dimensionality of the joint distribution of y=( y12 , y13 , ..., y(J&1) J)$ prevents direct numerical calculations (except when J=3). To tackle this problem, we apply the EM algorithm in which [Y, Z, $, W] is treated as the complete data, whereas the observed data W is regarded as the incomplete data. 

In general, there are two steps involved in an EM algorithm, the expectation (E) and the maximization (M) steps. The E-step consists of computing either the expected log-augmented posterior or the conditional expectations of the sufficient statistics required to obtain the maximum likelihood estimates in the M-step. The M-step finds estimates to maximize some objective function such as the likelihood of the complete data based on the previous set of parameters. By iterating the E and M steps for a sufficiently large number of times, the obtained sequences of the parameter estimates converge to the maximizer of the likelihood of the observations.

Convergence 

A standard criterion for assessing convergence of an EM algorithm is to examine whether the relative changes in the parameter values from successive iterations are smaller than a predetermined constant such as 0.0001. This approach is not possible in the context of the MCEM algorithm because the use of simulations in the E-step introduces additional Monte Carlo error, and consequently instead of converging to a unique value, the convergent sequence fluctuates within a small neighborhood of the maximum likelihood estimate. Despite this seemingly undesirable feature of the procedure, Chan and Ledolter (1995) showed that, for suitable starting values close to an isolated local maximizer of the likelihood and with a sufficiently large Monte Carlo sample size M, the MCEM sequence converges to the maximizer with high probability. 

Common practices in monitoring the convergence of MCEM are plotting %[(][k][)] , 2L(%[(][k][)] , %[(][k][+1)] ) (i.e., the difference in the log-likelihoods of the observations for two adjacent estimates), or %[(][k][)] &%�(i.e., the deviation of the estimates to the MLE) against the iteration number k. After a certain number of iterations, the sequence should appear to have stabilized but fluctuate randomly about % or 0 for the first and the latter two sequences, respectively. After such a point, the sequences can then either be terminated or be continued with larger M to reduce the variability of the fluctuation (Chan 6 Ledolter, 1995; Chan 6 Kuk, 1997; McCulloch, 1994, 1997). 

Although the Monte Carlo error can be reduced by increasing the size of M, there is a trade-off between the accuracy of the approximations of the conditional expectations in the E-step and of the computational time. As a result, it is inefficient to start with a large M. Another possibility is to directly implement an automated updating scheme for M (Booth 6 Hobert, 1999) in the MCEM algorithm.

Model Testing 

Large sample tests of fit are available based on the likelihood-ratio statistic (G[2] ) and�or Pearson's goodness-of-fit test statistic (X[2] ). Asymptotically, if a paired comparison model provides an adequate description of the data, then both statistics follow a /[2] -distribution with (2[(][ J] 2[)] &m&1) degrees of freedom, where m refers to the number of parameters to be estimated. The likelihood-ratio test is most useful when the number of items to be compared is small. Otherwise, only a small subset of the possible paired comparison patterns may be observed. In this case, it is doubtful that the test statistics will follow approximately a /[2] -distribution. However, useful information about a model fit may be obtained by inspecting standardized differences between the observed and fitted paired comparison probabilities for subsets (e.g., all triplets) of the items. Although these residuals are not independent, a careful inspection of their direction and size is useful in identifying a systematic misfit. 

Nested paired comparison models can be compared by computing twice the difference between their log-likelihood values. This difference is asymptotically distributed as a /[2] -statistic with the degrees of freedom equal to the difference between the numbers of parameters in the unrestricted and restricted models. Although computationally intensive, we compute values of the maximized log-likelihood functions using the GHK smooth recursive simulator (Geweke, 1989; Hajivassiliou, 1993; Keane, 1994) which has been shown to provide accurate approximations of highdimensional normal probabilities.

6. CONCLUSION 

This paper presented the MCEM algorithm as a general approach to estimating parameters of factor-analytic paired comparison models within a Thurstonian framework. As shown in the application, analyses of marginal paired comparisons obtained in a multiple judgment experiment may lead to incorrect conclusions about similarity effects. Fortunately, the MCEM approach provides a straightforward solution to this problem by facilitating the fit of the paired comparison model to the joint distribution of the[J][(][J][&1] 2[)] responses. Although not emphasized here, we note that only minor modifications are necessary to allow for incomplete paired comparisons and the consideration of covariates. 

Since the MCEM algorithm is still a relatively new technique, there is a need for further research and applications of this procedure. Open issues include more welldefined methods for assessing convergence of the algorithm, diagnostic checks, and specification tests. However, despite this long list of future work, we expect many more applications of the rich and flexible class of factor-analytic Thurstonian models in the future. Clearly, the MCEM approach appears to be a promising
