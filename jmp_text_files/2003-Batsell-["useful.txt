The publication of Tversky's  elimination by aspects  (EBA) model in 1972 ( Tversky, 1972a,b ) represented significant opportunities for advancement in the field of choice modeling. As is widely known, the EBA model generalized the models of  Luce (1959)  and  Restle (1961)  and is capable of modeling choice data that suffered from both the similarity problem and the dominance problem ( Tversky, 1972b ). EBA represented a significant step forward both in terms of mathematical modeling of choice, as well as insight into the psychological aspects of choice.

Despite its potential importance, however, there has not emerged a method, simple or otherwise, for estimating the parameters of EBA. In his original paper,  Tversky (1972b, p. 293)  had to make the assumption, which in his case may have been reasonable, that 2 of the 6 parameters in the 3-alternative case were zero, and fix the scale by setting a third equal to one, in order to use STEPIT ( Chandler, 1969 ) to estimate the remaining three parameters for the model. General application of EBA in routine situations would be greatly hindered if one had to make a priori assumptions that some, or many, of the parameters are zero. And applying search routines to problems of even modest numbers of alternatives, say  N =6, would require a search among as many as 61 parameters.

In later work, citing the large number of parameters required by EBA,  Tversky and Sattath (1979)  pursue and estimate tree models. Tree models, however, are not as general as EBA, and presuppose that the alternatives for which choices are being modeled can be described in terms of a (rather restrictive) hierarchical tree structure; they also require the assumption that choice follows an equally restrictive hierarchical process. Motivated by the belief that the more general and psychologically rich EBA model would offer significant advances in choice modeling, this paper demonstrates a heretofore unknown mathematical result that renders the EBA model very easy to estimate. In doing so, we also demonstrate that the number of free parameters in EBA is fewer than 2

                      −3, which is the number described by Tversky in his original work.

This paper begins with a brief restatement of EBA. Following this restatement, we then prove that simple differences in probabilities across choice sets are linearly related to the parameters in the EBA model. This linear relationship means that once the appropriate probability differences are calculated, parameters in the EBA model can be easily estimated with widely available least-squares routines. In introducing the parameter estimation approach the paper also addresses the number-of-free-parameters issue which first surfaced in applying the estimation approach. Finally, the parameter estimation approach is demonstrated using actual data from the repeated choices of one individual.

Most models of choice behavior are specified in terms of, and are estimated using, the probabilities of choice for objects in sets. Every model, however, has an implicit (or explicit) assumption about how the choice probabilities for objects in offered sets change as objects are inserted into, or removed from, those sets. For example, the Luce model is based on the assumption that new shares are always distributed in proportion to old shares. Compared to the Luce model, the EBA model allows a much wider range of potential shifts in choice probabilities from set to set. The way in which choice probabilities change from set to set can thus be used to discriminate between the Luce model and the EBA model. Also, as we later prove, the changes in choice probabilities from set to set are directly related to the scale values in the EBA model.

Note that there is valuable information in the probability differences that can be used to infer certain properties of the data. First, when object 1 is added to the set with only 2 and 3, they both begin with the same shares of 0.5 and object 1 pulls the same proportion from both, i.e. 40% [((0.5−0.3)/0.5)]. This is what one would expect in choice data if the Luce axiom were true. In the case of adding object 2 to the set with 1 and 3, it pulls nothing from object 1 and all of its resulting share of 0.3 from object 2. This result is inconsistent with a Luce-based model but can be explained with Tversky's EBA model. That is, relative to objects 1 and 3, object 2 has no unique features and shares everything it has with object 3; that is, 2 and 3 are clones. The result upon adding 3 to {1,2} is consistent with this analysis. The point of this discussion is that there exists important diagnostic information in the probability differences.

In this section we demonstrate the estimation of EBA on real, as opposed to hypothetical, data. The data resulted from the repeated choices of one individual. The subject, a student volunteer, had a regular habit of taking a study break each weeknight. During the break he would proceed to a row of vending machines in the basement of his dorm and purchase a snack—usually one of the following products—Yodels (1), Twinkies (2), Devil Dogs (3), Milky Way (4), and Three Musketeers (5). Over a period of twelve evenings, he was asked to modify his behavior in the following way. On each evening he was provided with a list of  all  26  possible subsets of the  5  products —all subsets containing 2 or more of the 5 products. Before proceeding to the vending area, he indicated, for each of the 26 subsets, the snack in that subset he would most like to buy that evening. In making his choice from each subset he was asked to imagine that the vending machines were out of all snacks but those listed in the set. Note that, across the 12 nights, the subject made a total of 312 choices.

Finally, he was provided with 12 random numbers (1 for each evening) between 1 and 26. After making his choices from each of the 26 subsets, the random number for the evening was used to designate a single subset from the 26. To enhance the importance of the a priori choice from each subset, the subject agreed to actually purchase the snack he had chosen from the subset indicated by the random number for the evening.

The 12 repeated choices across each of the 26 subsets were used to estimate the proportion of choices for each object in each set—a total of 75 proportions. The probability difference scores were calculated using  Eq. (13)  and these values form the 70 observations of our dependent variable. The appropriate probability differences from  Eq. (18)  form the independent variables and weighted least squares was used to estimate the EBA scales. The weights used were the inverse of the variance of the proportions that defined our dependent variable. The scale values were constrained to be between 0 and 1 (inclusive) and sum to 1. As might be expected, a large number of the 30 potential EBA scales were not significantly different from 0. The pattern of substitutability in the choice shares, and thus the EBA scales, reflect the fact that Yodels, Twinkies and Devil Dogs are three cake-like products with fillings and icings, and Milky Way and Three Musketeers are candy bars. This makes perfect sense in terms of relative substitutability of the five products. That the pattern of the shift in choice shares across the sets could result in two EBA scales with such face validity is encouraging and rather remarkable. Note that we did not prespecify which scale values or feature overlaps to estimate. These simply resulted from which EBA scales must have been operative based on the pattern of the probability differences. The second row of  Table 3  shows the 5 estimated scales for the Luce model.

The statistical tests make clear the EBA model fits significantly better than Luce. Moreover, the face validity of the resulting scales add credibility to the result. Thus, it is possible, from the patterns of the changes in the probabilities alone, to discern what combination of feature overlaps exist in the perception of the subject, and the relative weight given to those features in making choices.

                         t 
                      −3, the number of proper nonempty subsets of  T  minus an arbitrary unit of measurement (1972b, p. 290). For  t =3 this would imply 5 free parameters but actually it is 4, one less than 5. In addition to an arbitrary unit of measurement there is a very subtle relationship between the parameters that renders an EBA solution to be non-unique. Appendix A proves the number of free parameters for  t =3 is 2 
                         t 
                      − t −1 which is 4.  Appendix B  offers results from hundreds of numerical experiments which suggests the number of free EBA parameters for  t  objects in general is 2 
                         t 
                      − t −1.

This paper describes a simple method for estimating the parameters in Tversky's  elimination by aspects  model. Included also is the first estimation of EBA parameters that did not require artificially setting some parameters to zero. Finally, the paper corrects an error in the specification of the number of free parameters in the EBA model. Rendering the EBA model easy to estimate should greatly facilitate its application across many contexts.
