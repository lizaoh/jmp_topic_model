JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY: 4, 48-71 1967)  Dynamic Programming Models for Multistage Decision-Making Tasks'  AMNON RAPOPORT'  University of North Carolina, Chapel Hill, North Carolina  The dynamic programing approach for solving multistage decision-making problems is presented. A general formulation is given  followed by models of deterministic, stochastic, and adaptive versions of a particular multistage decision-making problem.  Seventeen subjects participated in a computer-controlled multistage decision-making task, intended for testing the adaptive  model. Group and individual decisions are analyzed in terms of both an adaptive and a nonadaptive model. Extensions of the  adaptive model, by adding different environmental and personal constraints, are outlined briefly.  A multistage decision-making process is one with many stages, either discrete or continuous, involving sequential decisions.  Restricting ourselves to the discrete case, a multistage decision-making process can be briefly characterized as follows. At  the beginning of stage k a decision maker (DM) obtains information about the present state of a system in which he is  operating, and makes a decision. The state of the system then changes to give a new state at the beginning of stage k + 1. The  new state depends on the previous state, on DM's decision in stage k, and on some stochastic variable. The objective of DM is  to make a sequence of decisions so as to maximize some criterion function for the entire process. The criterion function  usually depends on the states of the system at the different stages, and on the sequence of decisions. If the criterion  function is maximized the decisions are referred to as optimal.  Dynamic programing (Bellman, 1957, 1961) is an iterative technique for finding optimal decisions for multistage  decision-making processes. Dynamic programing is "a new approach based on the use of functional equations and the principle of  optimality, with one eye on the potentialities of the burgeoning field of digital computers" (Bellman and Dreyfus, 1962, p.  vii). Although digital computers are necessary for solving most dynamic programing problems, the method provides no general  all-purpose algorithm. Each problem formulated in dynamic programing terms poses its own difficulties, and optimizing  techniques must be sought in order to develop a numerical or analytical solution.  1 This paper was written while the author was Assistant Research Psychologist at the Mental Health Research Institute,  University of Michigan. The paper will extend  the dynamic programing approach to a class of problems dealing with human multistage decisions. The purpose is not to  prescribe normative decisions for human multistage decision-making, but to investigate the possibility of using the dynamic  programing approach to describe these processes. We shall try to demonstrate the advantages of the new approach in generating  adequate descriptions of these processes in complex decision-making tasks, the treatment of which has been almost nonexistent  in the psychological literature.  DEFINITION OF TERMS  1. Stage. A stage is an arbitrary unit in time or space into which the process is divided. The stage represents a mathematical  device for quantizing continuous variables such as time or space. Frequently, the quantization is not only a mathematical  tool, but also a natural device because many processes developing in time and space are most conveniently measured in discrete  periods.  2. State. The state of the system is given by a vector of one or more elements. We restrict ourselves here to a vector with  only one element, xk. , k = n, n - 1, where n denotes the number of stages in the entire process.  3. Decision. The decisions are made by DXI. They are often constrained by lower and upper limits. We shall denote DM's  decision in stage k by y7,,, where yk E Y, and Y denotes the admissible set of decisions.  4. Transformation. In the multistage decision-making process, stage k transforms its input state xk to an output state Xk_1 ,  in a way dependent on Yk . This transformation (or transition function) will be denoted by Tk. , where xk 1 -- T&,, ,Yk).  S. Criterion function. When there is an optimization problem, there should be also some criterion by which the different  decisions might be evaluated. The criterion function, or the objective function, usually depends on the sequence of state  variables and on the sequence of decisions for the whole process. It is customary to refer to it as a profit function or a  cost function.  6. Policy. A vector of decisions, y', that satisfies the constraints will be referred to as an admissible policy. A policy  that maximizes the criterion function, when it is a profit function, or minimizes it, when it is a cost function, will be  referred to as an optimal policy.  7. Principle of optimality. The principle states: "An optimal policy has the property that whatever the initial state and the  initial decisions are, the remaining  ,t80!4!1 -4  50 RAPOPORT  decisions must constitute an optimal policy with regard to the state resulting from the first decision" (Bellman, 1957). The  principle guarantees that the process will have the following independence of path property: After any number of decisions,  say n - k, the effect of the remaining k stages of the decision process upon the total return will depend only upon xk and the  subsequent decisions (Bellman, 1961).  The above terminology will be used for formulating multistage decision-making processes. We shall emphasize the special  attractiveness of the dynamic programing approach, which offers a way for solving different classes of multistage  decisionmaking processes that can be classified as deterministic, stochastic, and adaptive, dealing with sequential choice  behavior under conditions of (subjective) certainty, risk, and uncertainty, respectively. For each of these cases a general  formulation will be given, followed by a model for a particular decision-making task. One of the models will be tested later. THE ADAPTIVE CASE  In earlier sections we confined ourselves to decision-making processes for which the state of the system at each stage, the  transformation law, the criterion function, and the probability distribution of the stochastic variable were assumed to be  known. We would like to relax one of these assumptions. In particular, we would like to consider decision-making processes in  which DM suffers from an initial lack of knowledge of some aspects of the decision making situation but is able to "learn"  about the structure of the situation as time goes on.  There are several aspects of the decision-making situation that might be initially unknown. For example, DM might have an  imperfect knowledge of the exact form of the transformation law, or DM might have only a partial knowledge of the state  DYNAMIC PROGRAMMING MODELS FOR MULTISTAGE DECISION-MAKING TASKS 57  of the system at each stage k. A question of a particular interest (Freimer, 1959) is that of developing a model for cases  where the probability distribution of the stochastic variable is initially unknown but is learned as the process unfolds.  Models applied to human learning of probability distributions have been tested by Rapoport [1964 (a) and (b)], showing a  promising degree of success.  What we want, then, are learning or self-adaptive models, designed to track changes in the behavior of the stochastic process  (see Bellman, 1961; Bellman and Dreyfus, 1962; Roberts, 1964). We want to construct models for decision-making processes,  where the history of the process is important to DM and is explicitly considered by him in order to generate a prediction for  the future behavior of the system. Bayesian decision theory (Raifia and Schlaifer, 1961), may provide these models.  Consider the same stochastic multistage decision-making process described in the previous section. DISCI !SSI()N  One of the main purposes of the present study is the comparison of the adaptive and nonadaptive models. The difference between  the two models lies in one assumption, namely, that DM learns the mean of h(e) in a way specified by Bayesian decision theory.  A test of this assumption was obtained by comparing both the adaptive model and the nonadaptive model to the observed  decisions. The results suggested a reason-  68 RAPOPORT  able correspondence between the predicted optimal decisions of either of the models and the median decisions. In addition, the  decisions predicted by the two models provided upper and lower bounds for many of the median decisions in the last problems.  Another way of saying it is that most DMs were "conservative" (Edwards and Phillips, 1964). The magnitude of the revision of  their subjective probability distributions was less than the correct amount prescribed by the Bayesian part of the adaptive  model. Adaptive behavior was also found in the analysis of individual decisions. Most of DMs adapted to the changing states of  the system and exhibited learning of P,, together with consistent tendencies toward either undercontrol or overcontrol of  the system.  The confirmation of the dynamic programming models should not be taken, however, to be conclusive. Both the adaptive model and  the nonadaptive model are dynamic programming models. They differ in only one assumption. We have compared these models to one  another but have said nothing about comparing the class of dynamic programing models to an alternative class. Since dynamic  programing models will usually be rather complex and will not provide analytical solutions, more simple classes of models  should be tried to fit the data. For fitting the present data, we have considered linear models of the Bush-Mosteller type and  Markov models, but they do not seem to be appropriate.  It has been shown above that the dynamic programing approach provides a uniform way for treating deterministic, stochastic,  and adaptive versions of multistage decisionmaking processes. When the dynamic programing approach yields an analytical  solution, the transition from a model for the deterministic case to models for the stochastic and adaptive cases involves few  changes. The main advantage of this way of constructing models is its ability to provide a direct test of the effects of  introducing an additional element into the decision-making process. To demonstrate this advantage, Rapoport (1966) compared a  stochastic version of the Reader's Control Problem to a deterministic version of the same task studied by Ray (1963). The  comparison showed the price paid, in terms of DM's efficiency in performing the task, for the introduction of a stochastic  variable into the system.  It is noted that psychological models, in general, have not provided a uniform way for treating deterministic, stochastic, and  adaptive processes. The introduction of a stochastic variable into the theory has generally necessitated the construction of  an alternative model, one derived from a completely different set of assumptions. An example is given by the deterministic and  stochastic models suggested to explain human decision processes in a Prisoner's Dilemma game (Rapoport and Chamah, 1965). A  detailed comparison between such alternative models, is, in general, rather difficult, as it involves comparison of the  complete sets of assumptions. It becomes very difficult, if possible at all, to find out which assumption is responsible for  the relative superiority of one model over the other.  Another advantage of the dynamic programing approach is the flexibility in handling  DYNAMIC PROGRAMMING MODELS FOR MULTISTAGE DECISION-MAKING TASKS 69  progressive relaxations or additions of assumptions about the nature of the decisionmaking situation and about the subjective  characteristics of DM. As Shuford has noted, when DM is faced with a decision-making situation, "there may be, and probably  are, constraints operating in the situation which invalidate the optimality of the experimenter-derived strictly optimal  strategy" (Shuford, 1964, p. 129). One set of constraints, usually known to both the experimenter and the subject, has to do  with progressive degradations of the actual environment. In the Reader's Control Problem this is accomplished by introducing  stochastic variables into the system, by providing only partial information about the value of xk , by eliminating knowledge  of the duration of the task, by changing the parameters, and by further degradations, whose effect on the optimal solution  might be interesting to study.  Another set of constraints, which may be called personal constraints, is related to the subject's perception of the  decision-making task (or misperception, from the experimenter's point of view), the costs associated with employing a policy  yielding better decisions (cost of thinking), etc. It is usually very difficult to specify these constraints. Empirical  investigations, and a detailed structural analysis of the task are needed in order to find out the personal constraints.  In the Reader's Control Problem several personal constraints might be included. As Ray (1964) has suggested, DM will probably  not consider all possible alternative decisions in making his decision, especially when the range of alternative decisions is  very large. Rather, DM will generally consider a small self-selected subset of alternative decisions-his region of decisions.  Thus one constraint might involve the narrowing of the range of alternative decisions into a personal region of decisions,  which might depend on personal variables such as perceptual biases, overall strategies, etc.  Another personal constraint might have to do with the tendency of most DMs to either undercontrol or overcontrol the system.  Tendencies of this kind, whenever found, were rather general. DMs who undercontrolled or overcontrolled the system in the last  problem exhibited the same tendency in previous problems. There might be several ways of including this constraint in the  model. One of them is by assuming a prior distribution, h(P,)*, which is not uniform, i.e., an V: Nn .  A third constraint might have to do with some specific subjective hypotheses concerning the sequence of the stochastic  variables. It is noted that the adaptive model completely ignores the order of the stochastic variables en, en-1 ,..., el .  The history of the stochastic process is completely specified by the statistics r and s, assuming that the Bernoulli variables  are independent. However, in spite of the fact that DMs were instructed as to the independence of the Bernoulli variables,  many of them exhibited behavior inconsistent with this assumption. An interrogation of DMs, which followed the experiment,  indicated that many of them were constantly involved in developing and testing very definite hypotheses concerning the  sequence of the Bernoulli variables, especially in the beginning of the task. Some DMs employed  70  RAPOPORT  positive recency hypotheses, while others employed negative recency hypotheses. There is a lot of evidence showing the same  effects in other decision-making tasks such as probability-learning experiments.  A fourth constraint might have to do with what one may call DM's ability to project into the future. People's ability to  calculate or even to consider the future states of a system effected continuously by their decisions is probably severely  limited. Evidence exists (Rapoport, 1966) that for some DMs this limit does not exceed very few stages. We should, then, be  able to construct a model, a constrained optimal model (Shuford, 1964), which will take this constraint into consideration.  One way of doing this is given in Rapoport (1966), who suggested a suboptimal model for either the stochastic or the adaptive  case. In the suboptimal model Mk_1 in (38) or in (45) is replaced by Mj_1 , where j is a constant and j G n. The variable j is  an individual parameter, which might be interpreted as DM's ability to foresee the effect of his present decision on the  future j stages of a multistage decision-making process. The same idea of constructing a suboptimal policy by optimizing only  j steps into the future has been proposed by Matheson (1964).  Several difficulties may be involved in the inclusion of the above-mentioned constraints in models for the Reader's Control  Problem. One grave difficulty consists in attempting to formulate these constraints in a precise mathematical form. Even more  important, the inclusion of these constraints in a model will usually imply the substitution of analytical solutions, in the  few cases they can be found, by numerical solutions. The price, however, might still not be too high. The availability of  highspeed computers and highly developed search techniques might make the construction and utilization of numerical  optimization techniques quite efficient.
