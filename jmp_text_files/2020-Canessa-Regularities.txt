Simon famously advocated the use of differential equations to study phenomena in social sciences in general, and in psychology in particular (e.g.,  Simon, 1952, 1964 ), especially when considering their dynamics. It is our understanding that this recommendation never caught on, particularly in psychology. Given that all data collected in psychological experiments are collected in time, one cannot but wonder why this is the case. We speculate that lack of adherence to Simon’s desideratum may stem from psychologists’ reliance on a research strategy that focuses on independent and dependent variables, and on seeking to establish causal relations. In contrast, functional relations seem to blur that distinction. Relating variables in functional equations seems too close to the dreaded circular definitions that psychologists are trained to steer away from. Putting these concerns in parenthesis, and because we truly believe in Simon’s prescription, in the current work we develop a mathematical model of the Property Listing Task (PLT), precisely because PLT data are sequentially produced. This model consists of a set of interrelated equations that describe the course of property listing in a PLT, aided by a very minimal set of assumptions.

It is generally accepted that concepts can be described by properties (e.g.,  Schyns, Goldstone, & Thibaut, 1998 ) and their respective frequency distributions ( Ashby & Alfonso-Reese, 1995; Griffiths, Sanborn, Canini, & Navarro, 2008 ). These properties are treated as binary (i.e., either present or absent). Though binary properties do not always reflect well the underlying semantic structure ( Goldstone, 1994; Tversky & Hutchinson, 1986 ), they are a common practice when studying semantic structure for linguistically coded concepts. When researchers want to study Semantic Memory (SM) for linguistically coded concepts, they often use the Property Listing Task (PLT). In the PLT, people are asked to produce semantic content for a given concept (e.g., for DOG, people may produce “has four legs”). This content needs to be coded into property types by grouping tokens that differ only superficially into a single type (e.g., “has four legs” and “is a quadruped” might be coded as “four legs”). The PLT is widely used across psychology (in cognitive psychology, e.g.,  Wu & Barsalou, 2009 ; in marketing, e.g.,  Hough & Ferraris, 2010 ; in social psychology, e.g.,  Walker & Hennig, 2004 ; in neuropsychology, e.g.,  Perri, Zannino, Caltagirone, & Carlesimo, 2012 ).

In Conceptual Properties Norming studies (CPNs), PLT data are collected for a large set of concepts across many subjects (e.g.,  Devereux, Tyler, Geertzen, & Randall, 2014; Kremer & Baroni, 2011; Lenci, Baroni, Cazzolli, & Marotta, 2013; McRae, Cree, Seidenberg, & Mcnorgan, 2005; Montefinese, Ambrosini, Fairfield, & Mammarella, 2013; Vivas, Vivas, Comesaña, García Coni, & Vorano, 2017 ). These norms are matrices with frequency distributions of property types for a large number of concepts. CPNs provide information about underlying conceptual structure for a representative individual (e.g.,  McNorgan, Kotack, Meehan, & McRae, 2007 ), and may be used to test theories about the nature of concepts and conceptual content (e.g.,  Cree & McRae, 2003; Vigliocco, Vinson, Lewis, & Garrett, 2004; Wu & Barsalou, 2009 ) and as a source of control variables for experiments (e.g.,  McRae, Cree, Westmacott, & Sa, 1999 ).

Though the PLT and ensuing CPNs are important across the field, there is no published model for the PLT. The few things we know about the PLT do not amount to a model (e.g.,  Ashcraft, 1978 , found that there tends to be more intersubjective agreement for concepts’ typical rather than atypical properties), which is why we believe that the field would benefit from having one. Furthermore, note, from our discussion above, that the PLT’s relevance in the field relates to its static properties (e.g., what it can tell us about the structure of semantic memory), not to its dynamics.

In the current work, and building on some previously observed regularities in PLT data ( Chaigneau, Canessa, Barra, & Lagos, 2018 ), we expand on those analyses, develop a mathematical description of the observed patterns in PLT data and validate them by analyzing three different CPNs (two published and one homegrown). The mathematical model consists of a set of interrelated equations that describe the listing process, aided by a very minimal set of assumptions.

To avoid confusions regarding what our model is, we find it useful taking time in explaining what our model is not. At the most general level, our model is not a model of cognitive processing mechanisms. Although we speculate about cognitive mechanisms that might give origin to the regularities we observe in CPN data, and use them to ground and develop our mathematical description, the focus of the current work is not to test hypothesis about those mechanisms. Rather, our aim is to show that those regularities accurately describe the PLT, and to discuss how they may advance our understanding and analysis of PLT data. Our strategy is to postulate and validate a set of functional relations between observable variables which, inasmuch as they allow describing our data, place constraints in the ways in which those data need to be interpreted and on theories seeking to explain PLT data.

At a more particular level, our model is not a model of the process of free listing from Long Term Memory (LTM) (reviewed in  Wixted & Rohrer, 1994 ; and more recently by  Hockley, 2008; Osth & Dennis, 2015 ). In the Free Listing Task (FLT), subjects are required to report items from memory (sometimes recognition is used instead of recall), where the set to be retrieved is typically limited to a small group of items. In some studies, participants learn a list of items and are then asked to retrieve them from memory. In some other studies, participants are asked to retrieve items from a set that is presumably already in memory (e.g., names of American presidents, types of animals) ( Hills, Todd, & Jones, 2015; Wixted & Rohrer, 1994 ). In FLT models, memory search is viewed as a kind of sampling process ( Wixted & Rohrer, 1994 ), where retrieval probability is a function of associative strength between the retrieval cue and items in the search set ( Hills et al., 2015; Hockley, 2008 ).

Another task that is similar to the PLT is the Free Association Task (FAT; e.g.,  De Deyne, Navarro, Perfors, Brysbaert, & Storms, 2019; Maki, 2007 ). In the FAT, people are asked to list the first word or few words that come to mind in response to a provided cue word. Compared to the PLT, the FAT is less restricted, and things that are valid associative responses might not be valid property responses (e.g., producing “Checkers” in response to CHESS). Just as occurs with the FLT, the FAT assumes that retrieval probability is a function of associative strength between the retrieval cue and items in the search set, and that associative strength arises from experience ( Maki, 2007 ). Thus, both the FLT and the FAT assume that memory search is performed over a limited, though possibly very large set.

The most important difference between the FLT and the FAT on the one side, and the PLT on the other side, is that it is questionable that conceptual properties are restricted to a limited set. Though, e.g., the FRUITS category may well be a limited set in memory (i.e., apple, orange, and so on), the conceptual properties of FRUITS could be unlimited. Properties for concrete concepts like CHERRY include things like categories to which the object belongs (e.g., “is a fruit”), parts (e.g., “has a stem”), other perceptual features (e.g., “is small”), and thematic relations (e.g., “eaten in pies”) ( McRae, 2004 ), but are not limited in the same way the members of a category are. Many other properties are possible, e.g., “they are a source of fructose” or even “I like them”. This means that the list of conceptual properties for any concept may well be open-ended (cf.,  De Deyne et al., 2019 ). This is even more obvious for abstract concepts, which are difficult to ground in concrete perceptual experience (cf.,  Barsalou, Santos, Simmons, & Wilson, 2008; Paivio, 1986; Wiemer-Hastings & Xu, 2005 ), have different senses and are more dependent on context ( Borghi et al., 2017; Borghi & Cimatti, 2009; Hoffman, Lambon Ralph, & Rogers, 2013 ). The possibility of unlimited conceptual properties makes it difficult, in both practical and theoretical terms, to use models developed to explain memory list phenomena (e.g., search of associative memory, SAM, and its descendant models,  Kimball, Bjork, Bjork, & Smith, 2008; Raaijmakers & Shiffrin, 1980; Sirotin, Kimball, & Kahana, 2005 ; and other closely related models,  Polyn, Norman, & Kahana, 2009 ) to model the PLT listing process.

Related to the issue of unlimited number of conceptual properties, there is the issue of intersubjective variability. We know that there is a large variability in properties produced in the PLT across participants ( Chaigneau et al., 2018 ). This is to be expected if conceptual properties are not a finite set, and current models are ill-equipped to handle it. Of course, an easy way out would be to assume that learning experiences vary across people. If learning experiences differ, it would not be surprising that listed properties also differ. However, that assumption commits such a theory to conceiving the PLT as a search process from a more or less static memory. It is unclear that this is the case, and at least theoretically, the listing process could be a much more dynamic one (cf.,  Barsalou, 1999 ).

Thus, in the current work we remain agnostic regardingwhether properties are finite or infinite, if the PLT draws from a finite memory store, what makes properties more or less available for listing, and what is it that leads subjects to stop listing in the PLT. As an alternative to the strategy of modeling cognitive mechanisms, and as already discussed above, we develop a mathematical model that consists of a set of interrelated equations that aim at describing the PLT listing process, where many different causes could account for the observed data.

By necessity, we cannot at this stage of our discussion fully explain what our model could allow for. Though full details will be provided at the end of this paper, we find it useful to present here a brief preview. We grant, as the reader will soon notice, that the relations we formalize in our equations are simple, or even self-evident. However, as we will show shortly, our equations are notably successful in describing the PLT listing process. Importantly, though the equations themselves are of interest, it is also their implications and more removed extensions that carry import. At the level of the equations themselves, our model uncovers empirical regularities that could be used as benchmarks to evaluate mechanistic models of the LTM querying process in tasks like the PLT. A more removed extension is the following. As we will also discuss in the following sections, our model provides novel measures of the richness of semantic structures ( Yap, Pexman, Wellsby, Hargreaves, & Huff, 2012 ) underlying property listing performance. This could allow us in future work to compare conceptual structure richness in different groups of people (e.g., different social groups) or different concepts (e.g., abstract versus concrete concepts), and to link these measures to measures that are thought to reflect cognitive processing (e.g., reaction times, brain activity).

Given that we will use those variables (s,  k ,  v  and  m ) to develop the mathematical description of PLT data, we suggest keeping  Table 1  in sight, which provides a summary. In our description, we assume that during the course of listing in a PLT, participants list properties that describe a concept, but at different rates. We assume that the rate of property generation decreases with listing order. Different mechanisms could predict changes in listing rates (this is probably not an exhaustive list). One mechanism depends on differences in availability. Differences in availability could result in rate changes because highly available properties, which tend to be produced at the beginning of the list, may result in higher production rates than less available properties, which tend to be produced at the end of the list, and are presumably harder to recall. A second mechanism depends on the interaction between semantic memory (SM) and control processes. Listing properties requires managing access to SM under proactive interference (PI) conditions. Proactive interference is the interference produced by content already recalled, on the retrieval of content not yet accessed from SM ( Conway & Engle, 1994; Kane & Engle, 2000; Wickens, Born, & Allen, 1963; Wickens, Moody, & Dow, 1981 ). In FLTs, as subjects list items from memory, memory performance decreases over successive trials ( Keppel & Underwood, 1962 ). This phenomenon is called PI buildup. It is well known that this process is influenced by the semantic field from which content is being retrieved, because changes in semantic field increase performance (known as release from PI; e.g.,  Wickens, Dalezman, & Eggemeier, 1976 ). The PI mechanism suggests that the rate of listing in the PLT will decrease with listing order. Yet another mechanism that predicts changes in listing rates is monitoring for intrusions and repetitions. As subjects continue listing, intrusions and repetitions become increasingly likely, such that attentional resources are taxed and listing properties becomes​ harder.

To summarize, Eqs.  (6) and (8)  assume that availability should decrease approximately monotonically during the listing course of a PLT, which means, according to our arguments, that properties that are not repeated in other lists (i.e., those that differ among participants) should be reported at a later stage of the PLT. This trend is what makes the curve of  k  (total number of different listed properties) vs.  s  (average cumulative number of listed properties) non-linear (i.e., quadratic), as Eqs.  (7) and (11)  show. Although we have observed this regularity in previous analyses of CPN data ( Chaigneau et al., 2018 ), those analyses were performed across a large group of concepts, not for individual concepts, as we do in the current work.

The reader should consider here that the  k  vs.  s  trend does not necessarily have to be non-linear. Think of a concept that has only low availability properties. To see if our mathematical description of regularities in PLT data holds, we resorted to two published CPNs ( Lenci et al., 2013; Vivas et al., 2017 ), plus a small norming study of our own, which we had performed for a previous study not directly connected to the current work. Thus, our data spanned 3 countries and 2 languages (Italian and 2 Spanish dialects), with 958 participants producing properties for 497 abstract and concrete concepts and verbs. We briefly describe each of these CPNs in turn.

The  Lenci et al. (2013)  norms report properties for 70 concepts (50 abstract and concrete nouns, and 20 verbs). Subjects (N  
                            = 
                          48) were 22 congenitally blind and 26 sighted Italian participants. The Lenci et al. data are freely available on the web. To avoid introducing further complexities in our analyses, in the current work we used data only from the sighted group.

The  Vivas et al. (2017)  norms collected properties for 400 concepts (living and nonliving objects). Participants were Argentinian (N  
                            = 
                          810), all Spanish speakers. The Vivas et al. data were kindly made available to us by the authors.

Finally, in our own norms (hereafter dubbed the  Chilean norms ) we collected data for abstract concepts. Each participant (N  
                            = 
                          100, all Chilean Spanish speakers), was asked to produce properties for 10 randomly chosen concepts taken from a total list of 27 abstract concepts. For each concept, participants were required to list things (words or short phrases) that would allow someone else to correctly guess the concept. Using this procedure, for each of the 27 concepts we obtained properties from 38 participants on average (min  
                            = 
                          22, max  
                            = 
                          53), resulting in a total of 5457 token responses. These token responses were coded as follows. During a first coding phase, a trained coder classified the 5457 responses as valid or invalid. Valid responses provided conceptual content (e.g., producing “money” for the concept HAPPINESS). Invalid responses were cue repetitions (e.g., producing “deciding what to put on my nightstand” as a response for the concept DECISION), property repetitions, and metacognitive (e.g., “this is hard”) or off-task comments. During a second coding phase, the coder grouped the 4941 remaining valid responses into 729 response types.

To estimate the reliability of the coding process of the Chilean norms, a second coder received the 729 codes, and proceeded to independently recode the 4941 valid responses. Following  Hallgren ’s ( 2012 ) recommendation, we computed Cohen’s kappa ( Cohen, 1960 ) as a reliability estimate. The advantage of using kappa instead of simply using the percentage of agreement, as is often done ( Bolognesi, Pilgram, & van den Heerik, 2016 ), is that kappa corrects for chance. Coding produced a kappa of .76, which is considered a substantial agreement ( Landis & Koch, 1977 ), suggesting that our subsequent analyses were not unduly affected by unreliability concerns.

Given that our description of the regularities observed in PLT data is limited to a few variables, it will most probably not account for all the variance in the data. That is, other variables currently not considered should also help explain the observed data (e.g., changes in semantic memory retrieval strategy, PLT’s procedures and instructions). Thus, to assess our description’s soundness, we used regression analyses (i.e., explained variances will tell us how well our equations account for the data). For each data set (i.e., Lenci et al. CPN, 400 concepts for Vivas et al. CPN and 27 concepts for Chilean CPN). This was done following the same procedures outlined in the idealized PLT example discussed in Section  2 . Another concern regarding the current work is the relatively weaker support found for equations involving availability ( v ). As already discussed, this could stem from limited precision in our measurement, from errors carried over from other measurements, and from subjects using strategies to produce properties from semantic memory (SM). Regarding this last point, we know that people produce clusters of items in the FLT ( Polyn et al., 2009; Wixted & Rohrer, 1994 ), and that these clusters may stem from strategic search processes ( Hills et al., 2015 ). We believe that similar strategies can be observed in the PLT. As an illustration,  Fig. 3  shows data from one subject in the Vivas et al. CPN, while producing properties for the concept AIRPLANE. It is easy to see in  Fig. 3  that while availability is in general decreasing as property listing proceeds (Eq.  (4) ), this is not a monotonic trend. Intuitively, we think  Fig. 3  is showing changes in strategy—marked by the occasional interruptions of the decreasing trend, which allow continued access to SM for report. Presumably, the participant thought of “transportation over long distances” as one cluster, then “perceptual properties of wings” as another, “other uses of airplanes”, and so forth. Our current equations are not able to capture non-monotonic decrements such as these, which may account in part for the relatively weaker support for Eqs.  (4)–(6) . In the near future, we aim at further refining our work to incorporate strategic processing and clustering.

Finally, and although we found support for our equations, one could hypothesize that other expressions might also fit the data. To dispel that doubt,  Appendix B  theoretically discusses other possible non-linear functions, and statistically tests our Eq.  (3)  vs. a hyperbolic alternative, and our full model vs. a full set of six linear equations that may be regarded as a null model. To foreshadow,  Appendix B  shows that our model not only fits the data better than other possible expressions, but that it is also more internally consistent than its alternatives.

Given our promising results, we believe our equations can be used to study concepts in several different ways.

A potential use for our model is to discriminate between different types of concepts, abstract and concrete concepts being a prime example. Much literature describes abstract concepts as more variable and dependent on context, leading to more sparse and less rich structures in semantic memory, while concrete concepts are less variable and less dependent on context, showing also less sparse and richer semantic structures ( Recchia & Jones, 2012 ). Though space considerations preclude us from providing more details regarding the following hypothesis, we hope it is clear why we believe that our equations’ coefficients will be able to accurately discriminate between concrete and abstract concepts. Our model predicts that abstract concepts are associated with harder to produce lists of properties that are less available for listing, and exhibit more intersubjective variability across the duration of the listing process. Those features should reflect in the relative value of some equations’ coefficients when comparing concrete and abstract concepts. For example, Eq.  (3) , which relates  s  (average cumulative number of listed properties) to listing order ( m ), could have a smaller

This pattern should occur because, if high availability properties tend to come early in lists for concrete concepts, the corresponding high availability means that these properties tend to be the same across subjects (i.e., more subjects will produce them). It then follows that the total number of different properties listed for a concept across participants ( k ), should increase slower relative to the average cumulative number of listed properties ( s ) for concrete than for abstract concepts. In other words, subjects should start producing different properties earlier, in relative terms, for abstract than for concrete concepts.

A similar discrimination could be achieved between different populations that use a putatively same concept. In contrast to research that attempts to characterize groups depending on their similarity in semantic content (e.g.,  Danowski, 2010 ), we believe that our model could be used to describe intergroup differences based not on the semantic content per se, but on the processing characteristics of putatively the same concepts. Two examples may make this point clearer. If people develop concepts across the lifespan, it is likely that children learning abstract concepts will show different patterns during listing than adults, though conceptual content could be similar. Similarly, it might be possible to assess differences in the production of conceptual content between sighted and blinded individuals. Given that concrete concepts are mainly grounded on perceptual content, and that blinded people are less exposed to it than sighted ones, that could homogenize properties more in sighted than in blinded subjects. If so, coefficient

Further uses of the current mathematical model are possible. As a final illustration, consider variables such as a concept’s familiarity, frequency and age of acquisition, all of which imply a greater exposure to a given concept. Taking their effects on a one by one basis, those variables should all produce longer property lists and also properties that are more available (e.g., properties that are more available and lists that are longer for DOG than for OSTRICH). However, our model shows that these variables are related in the PLT. Other things being equal, larger

This suggests that the pattern one would expect to find is that more exposure would lead to longer lists but also to a faster decrease in property availability, which may also lead to a lower average availability. This pattern would be consistent with the fan effect in memory retrieval ( Anderson, 1974; Anderson & Reder, 1999 ).

A caveat before closing relates to the use of listing order in our equations. The reader may have noted that our equations containing  m  could also be expressed in terms of true time. Unfortunately, CPNs do not supply Reaction Time (RT) data. In fact, we did not find CPNs which provided the necessary raw data to calculate our variables and also reported RTs. That is why we use  m  in the current work. The disadvantage of using listing order is that  m  assumes equal RT intervals throughout the listing process and across subjects and concepts, thus making us unable to gauge the precise duration of temporal gaps and total duration. This works against us being able to detect the kinds of non-linearities expected during the PLT listing process. This is something we are currently working on. Though measuring RT could introduce noise in our measurements, it should allow us to test whether mechanisms such as availability, interference and monitoring produce their hypothesized effects; something that listing order data do not allow. If these processes operate as we hypothesize, time elapsed between responses should compress or expand depending on the interplay between the abovementioned mechanisms.

Computational simulations of PLTs using  m  (listing order) and RT (including noisy processes) performed in our lab, suggest that using RT should allow us to more clearly see the logarithmic and non-linear trends described by the model, relative to using  m  data. Our preliminary assessment is that, regarding Eqs.  (3), (5) and (14) , because we do see a logarithmic trend using  m , then we should see it even more clearly using RT, given that RT can better reflect the expansion and contraction of time between listed properties. The same can be said regarding Eqs.  (4) and (13) . Given that  m  assumes equal temporal spacing of listed properties across concepts and participants in a PLT, this tends to linearize the relation between availability ( v ) and  s  (average cumulative number of listed properties), and between  k  (total number of different listed properties) and  s . Hence, RTs should show even better those non-linear relations. Of course, these are all things that await for future work.

On closing, we are optimistic that our current and future work on the PLT will allow a better understanding of concepts in general and of CPNs in particular. Understanding the PLT’s listing process may lead to being able to meaningfully compare CPNs across laboratories, countries, languages and cultures, something that researchers are currently unable to do. Although much work has yet to be done to accomplish such goals, we think that the relations found in PLT’s observed variables offer a solid initial research ground. Given that the relations may be generalizable to any PLT, differences in the equations coefficients may be used to study the idiosyncratic behavior among PLT’s and/or to control for those idiosyncrasies when comparing PLT’s results.

Summarizing this appendix’s results, we may say that, from an empirical viewpoint, our model as a whole receives stronger support than its counterparts. The comparison between the hyperbolic and logarithmic  s  vs.  m  relation (i.e., Eq.  (B.1)  vs. Eq.  (3) ) shows good support for our model. In the same vein, the omnibus test corresponding to the statistical contrast between our model as a whole and its naïve linear counterpart favors our model. On the other hand, at the level of each individual equation, the empirical comparisons between our model and the naïve linear one, show a better fit for our model in four out of six equations. Nevertheless, note that empirical support for our model is consistent across the three different CPNs, whereas support for the linear model is not. Furthermore, also note that the relatively weaker empirical support for our model is associated with Eqs.  (4) and (6) , both including availability ( v ). Remember that in Section  3.3  we discussed that data did not exactly follow the hyperbolic monotonic trend in  v  that we postulated (e.g., see  Fig. 3 ), for reasons already explained. That approximation in Eqs.  (4) and (6)  imposes an upper limit to the amount of explained variance we can get in a regression, thus lowering the  
                            
                               
                                  AAR 
                               
                               
                                  2 
                               
                            
                          of the corresponding equations.

On the other hand, from a theoretical point of view, recall that our model consists of a set of interrelated and logically/mathematically derived equations, which allows having an integral description of the PLT’s listing process. Furthermore, having a theoretically grounded description reduces the risk of overfitting the regression equations and finding spurious relations among variables. In contrast, the alternative linear model does not deliver an integral and grounded description of the listing process. Most notably, the linear model cannot explain the crucial asymptotic listing process (Eqs.  (3) and (14)  vs. Eqs. (3a) and (14a)), nor the quadratic relation between  k  and  s  (Eq.  (13)  vs. Eq. (13a)).
