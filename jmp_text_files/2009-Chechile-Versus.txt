An important issue in the application of mathematical models in psychology concerns the question of whether to fit a model separately to each individual and then average the results over the subjects as opposed to fitting the model once to the pooled data for the group. A number of investigators have demonstrated problems in fitting a nonlinear model on the basis of grouped data when the data span a set of separate experimental conditions ( Ashby, Maddox, & Lee, 1994; Estes, 1956; Sigler, 1987 ). In those multiple-condition studies, it was possible for a nonlinear model to hold for each individual, but not to be a good fit for the grouped data. However,  Chechile (2009)  has also provided a demonstration, in terms of a multinomial processing tree (MPT) model, that a more accurate estimate of an important model parameter is obtained from the data pooled across the subjects. This result occurred for a model based on a single condition, and it has stimulated the present research to examine more thoroughly the averaging versus pooling issue for single-condition models.

Typically, experiments have a number of replication test trials,

Does it matter if a mathematical model is first fit to the individuals and then averaged as opposed to pooling the observations across individuals before fitting the model? This question has been dealt with in statistics for the case of the random-effects analysis of variance model. General linear statistical models have also considered the random-effects case for categorical data (see ( Agresti, 2002; Agresti, Booth, Hobert, & Caffo, 2000 )). Yet, in mathematical psychology, there has been extensive use of psychological theories for modeling categorical data as opposed to using a generic statistical model. A particularly important example of this approach is multinomial processing tree (MPT) models ( Batchelder & Riefer, 1999; Chechile, 1998, 2004, 2007 ). With the MPT approach, probability trees are used to represent latent psychological processes, such as memory storage and guessing, and the trees are used to describe how the underlying processes result in responses in the various categories of the multinomial data structure. MPT models are generally nonlinear, so the question of averaging individual fits versus pooling data is still an open question.

Because the issue of averaging versus pooling might be dependent on the MPT model itself, this problem is addressed by examining a number of representative MPT models. The models are described in the next section. In a later section, a series of Monte Carlo simulations are examined to assess the relative accuracy of model fits for each of the models. In a subsequent section, another series of Monte Carlo simulations examine the bias and power of various statistical procedures for detecting condition differences. Also the exploration of the issue of pooling versus the averaging of individual estimates in the context of MPT model leads to a general condition that predicts when differences are expected. Finally, all the results are discussed and compared to other approaches for modeling categorical data.

In the  Appendix , two methods for point estimation are provided for each of the four models. One method is the standard maximum likelihood estimate (MLE), and the other method is population-parameter mapping (PPM). The MLE is based on finding the joint set of MPT parameter values that maximize  (1) . The PPM method is a procedure that involves sampling from the Bayesian posterior distribution. The PPM method is described in detail in  Chechile (1998, 2004) . PPM method is a modified Bayesian procedure based on repeatedly sampling vectors from the joint posterior distribution in terms of the  
                         ϕ 
                       parameters. Each vector of  
                         ϕ 
                       values is then mapped to a corresponding vector of the  
                         θ 
                       parameters associated with the MPT model. The mean of the successfully mapped  
                         θ 
                       values is the point estimate for the parameter. It is beyond the scope of this paper to elaborate much more on the PPM method. PPM estimates are examined here along with the MLE values because  Chechile (2004)  showed that there are occasions where the PPM estimates are more accurate than the MLE. It is also possible that the issue of averaging estimates versus pooling might yield different conclusions for the MLE and PPM estimates. Yet, in this paper, the relative merits of the MLE and PPM estimates are not the central focus.

The estimation of the parameters of a MPT model can be done in a number of ways. One method is to fit the model to each individual and obtain a mean estimate of these estimates for each condition. At the other extreme, one can pool the frequencies across subjects and fit the model once for each experimental condition. In general these two methods do not result in the same estimate; although there is now a simple test via  Theorem 4  to determine in advance if the two approaches will result in the same or a different value. Given a difference between the two approaches, the focus in this paper has been on the relative merit of these two approaches. However, there is also a third approach that is between these two extremes, i.e., the utilization of hierarchical multinomial models. See  Klauer (2006)  and  Stahl and Klauer (2007)  for a discussion of the hierarchical approach to MPT modeling. With the hierarchical approach the researcher pre-specifies the number  
                         C 
                       of subgroups of subjects within the condition. If the researcher specifies  
                         C 
                         = 
                         
                            
                               n 
                            
                            
                               g 
                            
                         
                      , then the individual fits would result, and if the researcher sets  
                         C 
                         = 
                         1 
                      , then the pooling method would result. As a general characterization, however, the number for  
                         C 
                       suggested by the developers of this procedure is usually a small number greater than 1. This recommendation makes the fits closer to the pooling method except that there is a possibility for detecting divergent subgroups. In this paper, the problem of subject heterogeneity has been ignored. In clinical applications of MPT modeling, subject heterogeneity cannot be disregarded ( Chechile, 2009 ). However, in an experimental context, researchers usually work fairly hard to achieve a homogeneous subject population. Consequently, the focus here is on the question of either individual model fits versus a single fit with pooled data. The key finding of this paper is that pooling generally results in more accurate parameter estimates; although for a specific atypical data set it is possible that the averaging method results in more accurate estimates.

As discussed previously, the implicit assumption behind averaging individual point estimates is that each subject is weighted equally. But, on reflection, there is not a good reason for weighting each individual equally for MPT models that have conditional parameters that are operative to a different degree across the various individuals in the group. In Section  4.4  examples are provided that illustrate how the pooling method is effectively equal to a weighted mean of the subjects as opposed to an arithmetic mean.

The conclusions arrived at in this paper have come from an analysis of a few MPT models that vary by being either saturated or unsaturated and by having outcomes that are realized by either unique tree pathways or realized by multiple pathways. The pooling advantage varies with the model and statistical estimator. However, one should be careful not to form judgments about the models themselves. For example Models A and B differ in that there is less bias for detecting condition differences for Model B relative to Model A. Although that is an interesting statistical property, there is no reason to argue that Model B is a better model than Model A. The structure of the MPT model depends on the underlying psychological processes. Although the models have different values for the pooling advantage, percentage bias, and power, the researcher is advised to use the tree model that best measures the psychological processes of interest. Once the model is specified, then the accuracy of the estimates can be discovered. With sufficient sample size the problem of error in the estimates can be greatly reduced. However, there is no way to improve a model if the tree model mismatches the experimental task or if the latent parameters of the model are not valid measures of fundamental psychological processes of interest.
