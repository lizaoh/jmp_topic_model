Cognitive neuroscience and cognitive modeling pursue a common goal, which is to identify the various mechanisms that give rise to observable behavior. It is therefore not surprising that their combination has become an extremely productive research agenda. For example, model-based functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI;  O’Doherty, Hampton, & Kim, 2007 ) has been applied to link activation in the brain’s reward circuitry, including the ventral striatum and the ventromedial prefrontal cortex, to the parametric encoding of reward expectations and reward prediction errors ( Bartra, McGuire, & Kable, 2013; Clithero & Rangel, 2014; Garrison, Erdeniz, & Done, 2013 ). Expected values and prediction errors are particularly suitable for neuroimaging data analysis, because they vary on a trial-by-trial basis when using reinforcement learning (RL) paradigms. The predictions of an RL model–that has been fitted to the choice data–are simply regressed against the fMRI data, revealing which brain areas track the development of expected values or prediction errors over the course of the experiment (e.g.,   Daw, O’Doherty, Dayan, Seymour, & Dolan, 2006 ;  Gläscher, Hampton, & O’Doherty, 2009 ;  Gluth, Rieskamp, & Büchel, 2014 ;  O’Doherty et al., 2004  and  Pessiglione et al., 2008 ).

Importantly, a person will perceive an identical decision situation differently after receiving feedback, which leads to learning and consequently to different behavior across trials. In contrast to learning situations, however, many psychological experiments use static environments, and potential changes in cognitive processes from trial to trial are not accounted for. Even an RL model does not predict any trial-by-trial variability, as long as no learning takes place (e.g., if the feedback is fully predictable). In the worst case, some models make deterministic predictions and do not allow accounting for any form of variability. Traditional models of decision making under uncertainty, including expected utility theory ( Von Neumann & Morgenstern, 1947 ), prospect theory ( Kahneman & Tversky, 1979 ), but also some choice heuristics (e.g.,   Brandstätter, Gigerenzer, & Hertwig, 2006 ), belong to this category. Applying a naïve error theory allows formulating choice predictions in terms of a probability distribution ( Hey, 1995; Loomes & Sugden, 1995; McFadden, 2001; Rieskamp, 2008 ). Beyond that, sequential sampling models of decision making assume that stochastic sampling of evidence underlies the decision process, which allows probabilistic predictions of response times ( Busemeyer & Townsend, 1993; Smith & Ratcliff, 2004 ). However, even probabilistic choice models make identical probabilistic predictions for identical choice situations, so that if the probability of choosing option A over alternative option B is predicted to be, say, 60% in one trial, it will also be 60% when the same situation is encountered a few trials later. That the subject might actually choose A in the first but B in the second situation (instead of B and then A, or A in both trials, or B in both trials) has to be attributed to unsystematic noise the model cannot explain.

More precisely, when model parameters are assumed to be fixed for specific conditions or persons, variability in behavior is difficult to explain ( Lee & Wagenmakers, 2014; Lewandowsky & Farrell, 2011 ). In sequential sampling models of decision making, for instance, a single decision threshold parameter value is estimated that reflects the amount of evidence required to make a choice ( Bogacz, Brown, Moehlis, Holmes, & Cohen, 2006; Smith & Ratcliff, 2004 ). This parameter might vary across different conditions, for example, when either speed or accuracy is emphasized. But within a single condition the threshold is assumed to be fixed, and its value is usually estimated based on hundreds or even thousands of trials (e.g.,   Ratcliff & Rouder, 1998 ). Naturally, the behavior varies across these trials, and the sequential sampling model “explains” this by the variability of the stochastic process of sampling information. However, we argue that the variability in behavior across trials could also be due and explained by the variability of model parameters such as a decision threshold (see also  Craigmile, Peruggia, & Van Zandt, 2010  and  Zandbelt, Purcell, Palmeri, Logan, & Schall, 2014 ). Moreover, behavioral variability should in principle be reflected in variability of the recorded brain data (see also  Glimcher, 2005 ). We suggest that the variability of behavior and the neural system could be matched to each other and might offer important insights about the neurocognitive system and the models trying to explain it.

Indeed, recent studies in model-based cognitive neuroscience have tried to exploit this trial-by-trial variability by first contrasting a model’s average predictions with the participants’ actual behavior in single trials (i.e., response times [RTs] and/or choices) and then using this prediction–observation discrepancy to inform the analysis of neuroimaging data ( Gluth, Rieskamp, & Büchel, 2012; Gluth, Sommer, Rieskamp, & Büchel, 2015; van Maanen et al., 2011 ). The central aim of this review and tutorial is to present a generalization of this approach to illustrate its applicability to (almost) any form of cognitive model and (almost) any form of neural or physiological data.

The rest of the article is structured as follows: In Section   2 , we summarize neuroimaging studies of human decision making that captured trial-by-trial deviations from model predictions to analyze the brain data as outlined above; in Section   3 , we provide a general formalization of this approach rooted in Bayesian theory, as well as an accompanying example to facilitate understanding; in Section   4  we discuss and exemplify parameter recovery simulations as a precondition for the successful application of our technique; in Section   5  we specify further requirements and recommendations for models and neuroimaging data; Section   6  provides a comparison with alternative ways to test for trial-by-trial effects; finally, Section   7  concludes with a discussion of potential results that can be obtained with this technique and their implications for further developments of theories in cognitive neuroscience.

Whereas reinforcement learning studies exploited the fact that many learning components such as reward expectations, prediction errors, but also learning rates and various forms of uncertainty are naturally changing ( Behrens, Woolrich, Walton, & Rushworth, 2007; Boll, Gamer, Gluth, Finsterbusch, & Büchel, 2013; Krugel, Biele, Mohr, Li, & Heekeren, 2009; McGuire, Nassar, Gold, & Kable, 2014; Payzan-LeNestour, Dunne, Bossaerts, & O’Doherty, 2013 ), models of decision making (including sequential sampling models) face the problem that predictions often remain constant from trial to trial.

                       In fact, observations are assumed to be generated by the same probability distribution (e.g., a Wiener diffusion process) and to be mutually independent; that is, they are independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) random variables ( Luce, 1986 ). Accordingly, early model-based fMRI studies on perceptual decision making restricted the association of cognitive modeling and brain data to correlations across subject ( Forstmann et al., 2008 ). In particular, Forstmann and colleagues used the linear ballistic accumulator (LBA) model ( Brown & Heathcote, 2008 ) to model choices and RTs in a random dot motion task with different conditions that prioritized speed, accuracy, or neither. The LBA was estimated so as to allow specifying the level of response caution (i.e., the distance between the decision threshold and the upper end of the starting point distribution) in speed and accuracy conditions separately. As expected, subjects were less cautious in the speed condition. At the time when people were instructed to respond quickly or accurately in the upcoming trial, the authors recorded the fMRI signal in the pre-supplementary motor area (pre-SMA) and the caudate nucleus (CN). They reported a negative correlation between the difference in response caution in the speed and accuracy conditions and the difference in fMRI signal in pre-SMA and CN. Their conclusion was that a higher increase in baseline activity in the pre-SMA and CN mediated particularly fast decisions in the speed condition.

Forstmann et al.’s (2008)  results clearly indicate an involvement of the pre-SMA and CN in response caution. However, if we consider the possibility that the decision threshold does not stay constant across trials within a specific condition, then the study leaves open whether these brain structures could also be important for threshold adjustments on a trial-by-trial level. van Maanen et al. (2011)  have recently addressed the idea of trial-by-trial adjustments of response caution in perceptual decision making ( Fig. 1 
                      (A)). The authors extended the LBA model to allow for single-trial variability of the starting point (and the drift rate). To do this, they first fitted the standard LBA to the entire RT distribution and then derived the maximum likelihood estimates of starting points for each trial based on the trial-specific RTs. In other words, they asked: “Keeping all other parameters fixed (to the values derived from LBA), what is the most likely parameter value for the starting point in trial  
                         t 
                       given the RT in  
                         t 
                      ?” Intuitively, faster RTs should indicate higher starting points, but RTs are also affected by other parameters (i.e., drift rate, decision threshold, non-decision time). The single-trial starting point estimates were then correlated with single-trial estimates of the fMRI hemodynamic response as derived from an independent component analysis approach (see  Eichele et al., 2008 ). van Maanen et al. found that the trial-by-trial signal of an independent component comprising the pre-SMA was linked to the trial-wise estimates of starting points. Interestingly, a component that included the CN was also related to starting points but in a different way from the pre-SMA (only in speed-emphasis trials that directly followed accuracy-emphasis trials), illustrating how single-trial analyses can provide new insights into brain–behavior relationships. The single-trial LBA by van Maanen et al. was also used in combination with EEG data to link variability in response caution with the amplitude of the contingent negative variation ( Boehm, van Maanen, Forstmann, & van Rijn, 2014 ) as well as with fMRI data to link variability in the drift rate with tuning curves in the primary visual cortex ( Ho et al., 2012 ).

Further evidence for a trial-wise association between response caution and activation in the pre-SMA and CN comes from an fMRI study on the emergence of value-based decisions ( Gluth et al., 2012 ) ( Fig. 1 (B)). In this study, participants had to decide whether to take or reject a financial offer of unknown value based on sequentially revealed probabilistic cues. A sequential sampling model was developed to predict which decision was made and how many cues were sampled. One step in the analysis of the fMRI data addressed whether the trial-wise discrepancy between the number of sampled cues predicted by the best model and the actual number of sampled cues by the participants was forecasted by fMRI signals at the onset of each trial. The authors found a network of brain areas including the pre-SMA and CN to be related to this discrepancy estimate, meaning that when activity in the pre-SMA and CN was high at the beginning of a trial, subjects tended to sample fewer cues (i.e., behaved less cautiously) than predicted by the model. Importantly, the discrepancy does not reflect merely the number of sampled cues, because the model’s predictions varied from trial to trial based on what information was conveyed by the respective sequences of cues.

Of course, mapping trial-by-trial fluctuations in behavior with variability in parameter values of cognitive models is not restricted to a specific process such as response caution in sequential sampling models but can be generalized to various processes of other models. For instance, in a recent study,  Gluth et al. (2015)  again used the discrepancy between average model predictions and single-trial behavior to infer the strength of episodic memory on the basis of memory-guided decisions ( Fig. 1 (C)). While lying in the MR scanner, their participants first had to learn associations between different food snacks and different positions on the screen (encoding phase). Later, two locations were highlighted, and participants had to recall the associated snacks and to choose the snack they preferred (decision phase).  Gluth et al. (2015)  modeled decisions by means of a sequential sampling model tailored to the mnemonic demands of the task. More specifically, the model included a memory parameter to account for interindividual differences in the ability to recall the snacks in the decision phase. Critically, this parameter reflects a person’s average memory capacity over all decision trials and does not tell whether a particular snack has been remembered or forgotten. On the other hand, observing that a person does not choose a very good snack (on several occasions) indicates that the subject might have forgotten about it. The authors followed this reasoning and used a Bayesian approach to derive the posterior probability that a snack

(2015)  found that their snack-specific estimate of memory strength was correlated with activation in the right hippocampus in the encoding phase, showing that we can make (trial-by-trial) inferences about related cognitive processes such as memory or attention on the basis of observing and modeling decisions.

In this section, we introduce a general approach for capturing trial-by-trial variability by assuming variability of the values of model parameters that represent specific mechanisms of the model. Our method is most similar to the approach we followed in  Gluth et al. (2015) , in which we determined trial-by-trial posterior estimates of model parameters using a Bayesian approach. For better comprehensibility, we supplement the general formalization with a concrete example taken from  Gluth et al. (2012) . As described above, in this study trial-by-trial estimates of response caution were based on the probability that a choice has been made until the time point at which it was actually made (see middle panel of  Fig. 1 (B)). As a practical example, we now apply this general framework to derive trial-by-trial estimates of the decision threshold of the sequential sampling model proposed by  Gluth et al. (2012) . As stated above, this model predicts how many pieces of information a subject samples before buying or rejecting an offer.  Fig. 2 
                      (A) shows an example trial of the experiment. In each trial, the subject could sample up to six cues to decide whether to accept or to reject a stock offer. Depending on the performance of the stock the subject received a positive or negative payoff. Possible cue values were +2, +1, −1, and −2, where positive (negative) values increased the probability that accepting the current offer leads to a gain (loss) of money. Cues appeared/disappeared consecutively after 2–4 s until a decision was made, and acquiring a cue led to a fixed amount of information costs. If a choice was made before presenting the last cue, the remaining cues were not revealed. Participants were not informed about whether their choice was correct or incorrect. Generation of cue sequences was randomized. The sequential sampling model tracked the development of evidence for/against accepting the offer by means of the log-evidence. However, we assumed that this estimate of evidence was affected by Gaussian noise, with the amount of noise being a free parameter. The model predicted a choice when an upper (accept) or a lower (reject) decision threshold was crossed. The model included a parameter to account for the decrease of the decision threshold with time (which improved the model fit strongly), so that the decision threshold actually comprises two components, the initial threshold and the decrease. Here, we are interested in the initial threshold and in deriving trial-by-trial estimates for it (so we keep the decrease of the threshold and the noise parameter constant).

In our example, we focus on a single trial (#103) of a real subject (#24) from the study who behaved close to the group average with respect to how many cues were sampled (group average: 4.16 ± 0.70  SD ; Subject 24: 4.25). The cues from 1 to 6 in this trial were +2, −2, −2, +1, +1, and +1, so after suggesting accepting the offer at Cue 1 the evidence switched to rejecting at Cues 2 and 3 and then turned back slowly to accepting at Cue 6 ( Fig. 2 (A)). The subject rejected the offer after seeing the first three cues. To illustrate the estimation of trial-by-trial variability, we imagine that this trial was repeated two times later in the experiment, but now the subject did not reject after three cues but accepted the offer after one observed cue or after six observed cues. We now examine how the posterior estimate of the threshold changes across these three trials.

In fact, the correlation of thresholds and choice points for this subject is .58, so there is a substantial dependency but also sufficient unexplained variance (implying that thresholds and choice points may be differently related to the neuroimaging data). This is reasonable because choosing early or late in this task heavily depends on how congruent the cues are: If the cues are all positive or all negative, the subject might decide early even with a relatively high threshold; if consecutive cues are constantly canceling out, the subject might decide late even with a rather low threshold. Looking at the time course is also helpful to see whether there is enough variance in itself (if not, one could try out a smaller step size) as well as to detect outliers and slow drifts in the development of the parameter. Outliers could distort the neuroimaging analysis, and slow drifts are usually not measurable with fMRI or EEG due to high-pass filters and baseline corrections.

Fluctuations in behavior are not necessarily due to systematic changes in the neurocognitive system but might just be random noise. For instance, if you generate simulations of a sequential sampling model with a noisy evidence accumulation process, you will observe variability in response times and accuracy rates even without parameter variability. Apart from the more general question of whether parameter values are always variable (which we discuss in Section   7 ), this raises the issue of parameter recovery, or in our case of “parameter variability recovery”: Given that there is variability in the parameter of interest, does this variability produce such (fluctuating) behavior that we are able to capture it with our method? We think that researchers who seek to apply our method must conduct parameter recovery tests (preferably before data collection) to evaluate whether it is possible to identify the variability in parameter values that might cause variability in behavior. Thus a recovery study is able to evaluate whether a cognitive model and a planned experiment are suited to identify the data generating trial-by-trial variability.

Here, we again use the model and experiment from  Gluth et al. (2012)  to exemplify this parameter variability recovery test. In a first step, we generate variability in the threshold parameter from trial to trial, simulate the model, and apply our technique to test whether this variability can be recovered. We then keep the threshold parameter constant but generate variability in another parameter, the noise parameter of the accumulation process, to examine to what extent this “noise” variability tends to be misattributed to “threshold” variability. Finally, we generate variability in both parameters to investigate potential interactions between them (for example, it could be that variability in the threshold can only be recovered as long as variability in the noise parameter is low). For all recovery analyses, we took the model parameters of all 26 subjects from Gluth et al., added trial-wise variability (i.e., Gaussian noise) to the respective parameter(s), generated synthetic data for the 120 trials of each participant, fitted the model to these data, and applied the technique described in Section   3  to estimate trial-by-trial variability in the decision threshold. By using the parameters of all subjects we ensured having reasonable and reasonably different parameter sets (cf.  van Ravenzwaaij & Oberauer, 2009 ). The critical test is the strength of the correlation between true and estimated trial-by-trial parameter values ( van Maanen et al., 2011 ). To investigate how the amount of variability affects the recovery performance, we simulated 10 different levels of variability: the standard deviation of the Gaussian distribution, which was used to create the trial-by-trial variability in a parameter, ranged from 10% to 100% of the standard deviation of this parameter across subjects. We ran 100 simulations per subject and variability level.

When we do not induce variability in the threshold but in the noise parameter, this variability is picked up by our technique to some extent in form of a small negative correlation between the estimated value of the threshold parameter and the data-generating noise parameter ( Fig. 5 (B)). This is due to the fact that simulations with high noise in the decision variable tend to generate decisions that are very fast, which is then attributed to a low decision threshold (note that this might not have been the case, if we had modeled variability in the noise parameter, too). Importantly, the correlation seems to be so small that significant correlations with neuroimaging data are not to be expected. Thus, it is unlikely that (potential) brain signals reflecting variability in the noise parameter could be misattributed to variability in the threshold parameter.

The small negative correlation might further suggest that recovery of threshold variability could be hampered by variability in the noise parameter. However, when variability is induced in both parameters, the correlation between true and estimated threshold parameters appears to be unaffected, with the average correlations (for high variability levels) being again above 0.6 ( Fig. 5 (C)). The same is true for the negative correlation between the noise parameter and the estimated thresholds. In summary, our method of estimating trial-by-trial variability in model parameters tends to underestimate the variability on an absolute scale but recovers the (relative) differences from trial to trial. Thus, it seems to be suited for correlational analyses with neuroimaging data. However, generalizing these results to other models and tasks is very limited, and researchers must run separate recovery analyses for their own work.

In addition to running parameter recovery tests, some more requirements with respect to both behavioral and brain data and the cognitive model should be fulfilled, if one wants to apply our approach successfully. Ideally, the researcher should take care of these arrangements before conducting the study, because at least some of them are irreversible. First, the experimental paradigm should allow researchers to track behavioral measures during fMRI or EEG recordings for every trial. This seems trivial, but especially in fMRI studies behavior is often recorded in just a small portion of trials (e.g.,   Hindi Attar, Finckh, & Büchel, 2012 ) or only after scanning (e.g.,  Singer et al., 2004 ). Similarly, the neuroimaging set-up must allow capturing signal differences on a trial-by-trial level, so positron emission tomography or fMRI block designs are not suitable for our approach. The same is true for analyses that remove trial-specific variability such as event-related potentials of EEG.

Besides identifying these requirements, we also give some recommendations to facilitate the application and increase its efficiency. Our first piece of advice is that a rich set of behavioral data is helpful when one wants to explain variability across trials: If model predictions are based not only on choices but also on response times (such as in sequential sampling models), there are more opportunities to observe trial-by-trial fluctuations in behavior. This does not necessarily imply more trial-by-trial variability in parameters, but given that such variability does exist, the likelihood of capturing it is increased by including response times in the analyses. In perceptual decision making, for instance, people often achieve very high accuracy rates of above .9, which means that for about 90% of the trials parameter variability could not be estimated, if a model takes only accuracy into account. Alternatively, one could arrive at more precise predictions of variability by combining multiple choices to specify the posterior distribution. For example, in  Gluth et al. (2015)  the probability of successful encoding of a single item was based on three observations in the decision phase ( Fig. 1 (B)).

Second, a Bayesian modeling approach ( Lee & Wagenmakers, 2014 ) is highly recommended because it directly addresses and solves several practical issues of our approach: It forces the modeler to specify the range of possible parameter values beforehand (though this is usually also done for maximum likelihood estimation), and it can directly provide posterior distributions of model parameters - similar to the prior distributions generated in our approach ( Fig. 2 (A)). In fact, our practical example above used an approximation to the prior distribution of the threshold parameter that suffers from two shortcomings. First, the likelihood of threshold values is calculated while keeping all other parameters constant (at the values taken from the initial estimation). A hierarchical Bayesian modeling approach can handle this more elegantly by simultaneously drawing entire parameter sets during the sampling process (but note that hierarchical modeling is also possible with maximum likelihood estimation; e.g.,   Farrell & Ludwig, 2008 ). This might even alleviate the problem of the reduced range of parameter variability, that we observed in our recovery analyses ( Fig. 5 (A)): This reduction results from the very narrow prior distribution ( Fig. 3 (A)), which in turn is determined by keeping all other parameters constant. Second, we followed a grid-search algorithm with a specific step size for trying out different parameter values, which runs the risk of being too imprecise for capturing variability on the one hand and of requiring much computation time on the other. This becomes irrelevant with directly estimating the parameter distribution via Bayesian modeling (though this difficulty could also be reduced by applying more efficient optimization algorithms).

Intriguingly, Bayesian modeling could be used to estimate trial-by-trial variability in a model parameter in a single step: The hierarchical Bayesian model could assume one parameter value for every trial that is drawn from a higher subject-level distribution (just as subject-specific parameter values can be drawn from higher group-level distributions with this technique;  Kruschke, 2010  and  Lee & Wagenmakers, 2014 ). This would even allow testing whether the increased model complexity that comes with assuming trial-specific parameter estimates is warranted, for instance, by comparing the trial-unspecific and the trial-specific model by means of the deviance information criterion ( Spiegelhalter, Best, Carlin, & van der Linde, 2002, 2014 ). Stated differently, one could test whether assuming trial-by-trial variability in a model parameter leads to a substantial improvement of model fit (that outweighs the loss of parsimony).

Our final recommendation pertains to the quality of the cognitive model itself. The model should provide a very good description of the data, so that remaining variability can be clearly attributed to specific components of it and not just to its general insufficiency. Our approach is only optimal within the limits of the underlying model. If the model itself makes little sense, then attributing behavioral fluctuations to one of its components cannot be very meaningful, either. Hence, it is advisable to subject multiple models to a rigorous model comparison procedure before extracting trial-by-trial predictions on the basis of the winning model.

How is our approach related to the studies and techniques discussed in Section   2  and to alternative approaches? As stated above, the approach is basically a generalization of the method used in  Gluth et al. (2015) , where memory-based choices were used to derive the posterior probability that a choice option was remembered. It is also similar to the methods used in the two other studies ( Gluth et al., 2012; van Maanen et al., 2011 )—but with some notable differences. Compared to  Gluth et al. (2012) , the new framework has a stronger theoretical foundation, as it provides trial-by-trial estimates for distinct model components. In  Gluth et al. (2012) , the discrepancy between model predictions and observations (in terms of too early or too late predictions) was used to approximate fluctuations in the initial decision threshold. However, these very general model predictions are also affected by other parameters (i.e., the decrease of thresholds with time, the standard deviation of the drift rate). Our new approach allows isolating single model components (by holding the rest of the model constant), which also enables a comparison of the respective contributions of different parameters to variability in the data (one simply has to repeat the analysis outlined in Section   3  for other parameters). In  van Maanen et al. (2011) , maximum likelihood estimates for single-trial parameters (LBA’s start points and drift rates) were derived analytically, which is desirable but often infeasible to derive. More importantly, van Maanen et al. did not implement priors for parameter values to constrain these maximum likelihood estimates. This step is not mandatory to get trial-by-trial predictions, but it helps avoid that some single-trial estimates are very extreme or equally likely (e.g., see the blue lines in  Fig. 3 (B)).

Two conceptually related approaches were proposed by  Ratcliff, Philiastides, and Sajda (2009)  and by  Ide, Shenoy, Yu, and Li (2013) , respectively. Using data from a perceptual decision making task, Ratcliff et al. linked variability in the drift rate of the drift diffusion model (DDM) to an EEG component for which single-trial estimates could be obtained. Trials were split on the basis of the amplitude of the EEG component, and the DDM was estimated for each half of the trials separately. The authors were able to show that the drift rate was higher for high-amplitude compared to low-amplitude trials even within single conditions of perceptual noise. The main difference to our approach is that the neural data was used to inform the behavioral modeling, whereas our approach relies on directly extracting variability via behavioral modeling and then informing the neuroimaging analysis. Ide et al. used a dynamic belief updating model to estimate trial-by-trial changes in the expectancy of stop signals in a stop-signal detection task and to relate this to the neural encoding of expectancy and surprise signals in the anterior cingulate cortex (see also  Harlé et al., 2015 , who relates this model to substance abuse in adolescents). In contrast to our approach, the actual behavior of the subjects did not inform the estimates of belief updating, but the model simply assumed that all subjects updated their expectancy in the same manner.

Another different approach to relating trial-by-trial variability in cognitive and neural processes was proposed by Michael Frank and colleagues ( Cavanagh et al., 2011; Cavanagh, Wiecki, Kochar, & Frank, 2014; Frank et al., 2015; Wiecki et al., 2013 ). Their technique is based on implementing trial-by-trial changes in neuroimaging data, such as variable peaks in the hemodynamic response function of fMRI data or trial-specific power estimates taken from EEG time–frequency analyses, in hierarchical Bayesian modeling of Ratcliff’s drift diffusion model ( Ratcliff, 1978; Wiecki et al., 2013 ). For example,  Cavanagh et al. (2011)  showed that the decision threshold can be affected by fluctuations in the EEG power of theta-band frequencies (4–8 Hz) at frontal electrodes, presumably reflecting an adaptive conflict-control mechanism mediated by interactions of the medial prefrontal cortex with the subthalamic nucleus. Practically, the supposedly relevant part of the neuroimaging data is first extracted on a trial-by-trial basis and then implemented in the hierarchical drift diffusion model using a regression approach. Posterior probability estimates of the regression coefficients (that relate the brain data with the model) and model comparisons determine whether adding the neural term to the model is warranted. The central difference from our approach is that the brain signal has to be extracted first to implement it in the model that predicts behavior (instead of first extracting trial-by-trial predictions from the behavioral model to predict neuroimaging data, as in our case). Hence, this method requires much more specific hypotheses about which brain signals could be related to trial-by-trial changes in cognitive processes: The researcher must have a good idea about which kind of signal (e.g., theta-band power) in which brain region (e.g., medial prefrontal cortex) could be linked to which component (e.g., decision threshold) of the cognitive model. Exploring the entire space of possible brain–behavior relationships with this technique would be far too time consuming. Furthermore, the software tool provided by the research group ( Wiecki et al., 2013 ) is restricted to the drift diffusion model (and the LBA).

A similar but more advanced method of combining neuroimaging and behavioral data in a Bayesian modeling framework was introduced by  Turner et al. (2013) . Their idea was to model both behavioral and brain data sets simultaneously with hierarchical Bayesian modeling to restrict parameter estimation on both the “neural model” and the “behavioral model”. A central difference from Frank’s approach is that one does not have to specify which brain region might be related to which model parameter in advance. Instead, an entire set of areas (potentially covering the entire brain) can be used simultaneously, and their relationship to the model is computed for every parameter. In addition, the joint modeling of brain and behavioral data makes it statistically reciprocal; that is, the neural data can constrain parameter estimations of the behavioral model and vice versa. This method has recently been applied to improve predictions of Ratcliff’s drift diffusion model for a perceptual decision-making task by means of adding information from an fMRI data set ( Turner et al., 2015 ). The approach is as general as ours and has the advantage of statistical reciprocity, but its complexity might discourage many cognitive neuroscientists from using it for their models and data sets. In contrast, our method can be realized quite easily by simply restructuring formulas that are already part of the cognitive model. In other words, everyone who has managed to set up and fit a cognitive model should be able to go this one step further and apply our technique.

In this section, we present three scenarios of how trial-by-trial variability in behavior might be explained by variability in model parameters and related to neuroimaging data. We also discuss implications for theoretical developments in cognitive neuroscience. We try to keep the discussion at a high level of generalizability but we complement it with real examples from the literature. All scenarios are based on the (methodologically simple but straightforward) idea that the trial-by-trial parameter variability, derived by applying our technique, is used as a predictor in a general linear model (GLM) of the neuroimaging data analysis. The GLM is particularly popular in fMRI; here, trial-by-trial parameter variability could be implemented as a parametric modulator: It predicts how the hemodynamic response of the fMRI blood–oxygen-level-dependent signal is modulated as a function of the parameter variability at specific time points in the experiment (for more information, see  Büchel et al., 1998 ;  Büchel, Wise, Mummery, Poline, & Friston, 1996 ;  O’Doherty et al., 2007 ). These specific time points could, for instance, refer to when decisions are made or when subjects are instructed to emphasize speed or accuracy in the upcoming trial. There are, of course, many other possible ways of linking parameter variability to neuroimaging data (see also  Turner, Forstmann, Love, Palmeri, & van Maanen, 2016 ), but an in-depth discussion is beyond the scope of this article.

As outlined in Section   4 , behavior is always variable because the neurocognitive system is always affected by “random noise”. Applying our technique to a randomly fluctuating behavioral measure will attribute these fluctuations to the parameter of interest. Yet, it is possible that this purported parameter variability does not exist. To give an example, neurophysiological studies in monkeys suggest that the evidence accumulation process, reflected in the gradual increase of firing rates in cortical areas such as the lateral intraparietal cortex and the frontal eye fields, stops at a stereotyped level that is independent of response times and task difficulty ( Hanes & Schall, 1996; Roitman & Shadlen, 2002 ). This has led to the proposal that adaptations in response caution are mediated by a change in the start point of accumulation rather than by an adjustment of the decision threshold itself ( Bogacz, Wagenmakers, Forstmann, & Nieuwenhuis, 2010 ; but see  Simen, 2012 ). Consequently, looking for a neural correlate of response caution variability at the time when the choice is made (i.e., when the decision variable crosses the threshold) might not reveal any significant results. Instead, the more promising time window to look at is when the decision process starts, or even earlier, when people are instructed or incentivized to behave more or less cautiously in the upcoming trial ( Boehm et al., 2014; Forstmann et al., 2008; Gluth et al., 2012; Gluth, Rieskamp, & Büchel, 2013; Ivanoff, Branning, & Marois, 2008; van Veen, Krug, & Carter, 2008 ). Hence, not finding a relationship between trial-by-trial variability in cognitive model components and neuroimaging data might support or even promote theoretical developments in cognitive neuroscience.

Another reason for not finding an effect of trial-by-trial variability in the neuroimaging data could be the low signal-to-noise ratio of the latter. Both fMRI and EEG analyses require many repetitions of trials to separate the faint hemodynamic or electrical signal from all the differences sources of random fluctuations, including movements, physiological artifacts such as heart beat and respiration, or artifacts of the measurement tools ( Huettel, Song, & McCarthy, 2014; Luck, 2014 ). This might raise skepticism about whether these techniques are in any form suited to capture the trial-by-trial changes which we are addressing here. However, we would argue that there is no reason to believe that the low signal-to-noise ratio of neuroimaging tools should be more problematic for investigating parametric effects as compared to simpler comparisons of experimental conditions. In fact, it seems more likely that simple contrasts such as “task vs. rest” or “emotional vs. neutral” in an fMRI study are systematically confounded by heart rate or respiration. Having said this, we would recommend that researchers seeking to apply our methods should consider (and maybe even test) whether the targeted trial-wise variations in model parameters might be contaminated by (regular variations in) physiological artifacts.

We have introduced a general, comparatively simple technique to capture trial-by-trial variability in cognitive model parameters (and similar components) to explain trial-by-trial variability in behavior and link it to the analysis of neuroimaging data. Our approach relies on deriving probability estimates for different parameter values over all trials as well as for each individual trial and then combining these estimates by means of Bayes’s rule to obtain the most likely posterior estimate in every trial. Crucially, the method can be applied to (almost) any type of mathematical model as well as to a broad range of neuroimaging data and analyses (but especially to event-related fMRI data and time–frequency EEG analyses). In addition, the approach can also be used in combination with physiological measures such as skin conductance, electromyography, heart rate, eye tracking, or pupil dilation. We hope to encourage other researchers in cognitive and systems neuroscience to employ our technique to tighten the links between their neuroimaging data and their favorite cognitive models to allow for theory advancement.
