Humans have remarkable abilities to reason under uncertainty: we can make reasonable predictions about whether it will rain tomorrow or decide whether to buy a stock based on market analysis. The predominant framework for reasoning under uncertainty is classical probability theory,

Other notable empirical deviations from classical probability theory include the unpacking effects ( Tversky & Koehler, 1994 ), probability identity violations ( Costello & Watts, 2014 ), and normality identity violations ( Huang, Busemeyer, Ebelt, & Pothos, 2024a ). These probability judgment errors are systematic, occurring not just as outliers in a few individuals but consistently across a substantial portion of individuals, as well as in the mean of aggregated judgments. Consequently, they cannot be attributed to Gaussian random noise in probability judgments or random effects, as these would cancel out in the aggregated data.

The mixed findings create an important gap that needs resolution: how can probabilistic accounts of human cognition be so effective, despite our consistent tendency to commit fallacies in probabilistic reasoning? Research related to this question has inspired two Nobel prizes in the field (Herbert Simon in 1978 and Daniel Kahneman in 2002) and has inspired a range of important computational and mathematical models in the field. The goal of this tutorial is to provide an introduction to these models and the specific reasoning fallacies each model seeks to address. A key strength of this tutorial is its focus on the precise and concise mathematical formulations of the models, offering readers the tools to accurately reimplement them.

We aim to present this tutorial to cognitive scientists and psychologists interested in computational and mathematical modeling of cognition, as well as those exploring human probabilistic reasoning. The sections on quantum models and models that incorporates probability judgment with choice and response time will require some basic knowledge of linear algebra, set theory, and differential equations. However, we strive to minimize the required prior knowledge and present these topics in the most accessible way possible. We believe that studying human probabilistic reasoning is valuable for cognitive science and decision-making research, given the broad applications of probabilistic models in cognition and their relevance to human rationality. Practically, probabilistic reasoning is crucial in fields like military and medical decision-making, as well as in daily aspects such as weather forecasting and stock market predictions. A mathematical understanding of the biases we exhibit in probabilistic reasoning can significantly improve how we manage these situations under uncertainty.

These violations led  Tversky and Kahneman (1973, 1983)  to suggest that our way of dealing with uncertainty does not strictly follow traditional probability rules. Instead, we use mental shortcuts learned from our everyday experiences, which are often called  heuristics , to make decisions. For example, because we hear about heart disease and cancer more often, we are more likely to remember them and overestimate how common they are, leading to the subadditivity effect.  Tversky and Koehler (1994)  call this the availability heuristic, which explains unpacking effects. Similarly,  Tversky and Kahneman (1983)  talk about the representativeness heuristic, which helps explain the conjunction fallacy. The second section of this tutorial will look into these heuristics.

Heuristics have been criticized for their lack of clarity and formal mathematics, leading to skepticism about how well they can predict quantitative outcomes or be applied broadly ( Costello & Watts, 2014; Fantino, Kulik, Stolarz-Fantino, & Wright, 1997; Gigerenzer, 1996; Tentori, Crupi, & Russo, 2013 ). In reaction to these criticisms,  averaging models  have been developed as a more mathematically solid approach ( Birnbaum, Anderson, & Hynan, 1990; Fantino et al., 1997; Nilsson, 2008; Nilsson, Winman, Juslin, & Hansson, 2009; Yates & Carlson, 1986 ). These models suggest that the probability of two events happening together (conjunction probability) is actually the weighted average of the probabilities of each event happening on its own.

In addition to averaging models and heuristics, there is been interests in exploring alternative explanations for probabilistic errors that do not rely on probability at all. A notable contribution here is the  inductive confirmation theory  ( Crupi, Fitelson, & Tentori, 2008; Tentori et al., 2013 ). This theory, as detailed in  Tentori et al. (2013) , shows that people are more prone to making conjunction fallacies when there is strong inductive support between the two events in the conjunction, than when the actual probabilities of the conjunctions increase. Yet, such declining rate of conjunction fallacies with rising probabilities pertains to only objective probabilities, which are based on the statistical reality, not subjective probabilities, which come from personal beliefs. This distinction matters because what people believe can often differ significantly from the statistical reality. For example, while objectively a Swiss individual is more likely to drive than ski, it does not rule out a subjective belief that suggests the opposite. Both the averaging models and the inductive confirmation theory will be further explored in the third section.

One main criticism of heuristic models, averaging models, and the inductive confirmation theory is that they focus too narrowly on certain types of errors in probability judgment, without providing a broad explanation for a wide range of such errors. The first significant attempt to offer a comprehensive theory for many different probabilistic fallacies are the  quantum probability models  ( Busemeyer & Bruza, 2012; Busemeyer et al., 2011 ). These models propose that the same principles underlying quantum mechanics can also explain why people make certain errors in probability reasoning.  Pothos and Busemeyer (2009)  showed how the quantum probability model could account for the conjunction fallacy seen in the Linda problem.  Busemeyer et al. (2011)  highlight that the model’s applicability extends beyond the conjunction fallacy, shedding light on unpacking effects, disjunction fallacies, order effects, and disjunction effects within a cohesive theoretical framework. The quantum probability model will be delved into in the fourth section.

A similar approach to the Probability Plus Noise model is the  Bayesian Sampler  model ( Chater et al., 2020; Sanborn & Chater, 2016; Zhu et al., 2020 ). This model falls under a broader category of models that attribute human biases to a form of Bayesian rationality that is constrained by resources ( Lieder & Griffiths, 2020 ). Unlike the Probability Plus Noise model, which attributes errors in probability judgment to a flawed sampling process, the Bayesian Sampler posits that the sampling process itself is reliable. Instead, it suggests that errors in probability judgment arise from a biased Bayesian prior. According to  Zhu et al. (2020) , the Bayesian Sampler is capable of generating predictions that align with those of the Probability Plus Noise model for marginal, conjunction, and disjunction probabilities. Moreover, it provides more accurate predictions for conditional probabilities than the Probability Plus Noise model. Consequently, the Bayesian Sampler is considered to perform at least as well as, if not better than, the Probability Plus Noise model in predicting human probability judgment errors.

While the Quantum Sequential Sampler is considered a cutting-edge model for understanding probabilistic fallacies, it is not without its limitations. For example, the Quantum Sequential Sampler encounters difficulties in addressing  double conjunction fallacies  in averaged judgments. This challenge extends to all models that uses subjective probabilities to explain probabilistic fallacies, including both the Probability Plus Noise model and the Bayesian Sampler. Double conjunction fallacies occur when the perceived probability of a conjunction is rated higher than the probabilities of its individual components. Despite empirical support for the presence of this fallacy ( Crupi, Elia, Aprà, & Tentori, 2018; Yates & Carlson, 1986 ), it remains a phenomenon in need of further exploration ( Busemeyer et al., 2011; Huang et al., 2023 ).

Heuristics are mental shortcuts that allow individuals to quickly make decisions and form conclusions with minimal cognitive effort ( Kahneman, 2011; Kahneman, Slovic, & Tversky, 1982 ). These strategies are particularly useful in situations where time and information are limited. From a historical perspective, heuristics are important to probabilistic fallacies, as they are intimately links with the discovery of these fallacies ( Gigerenzer, 1996; Tversky & Kahneman, 1983; Tversky & Shafir, 1992 ). This section will explore two prominent examples of heuristics associated with probabilistic fallacies: the  representativeness  and  availability  heuristics ( Kahneman et al., 1982; Tversky & Kahneman, 1973 ), which are linked to two of the most significant probabilistic fallacies: the conjunction fallacy and unpacking effects.

It is important to note that this section does not cover all possible heuristics related to probabilistic fallacies. We focus on representativeness and availability because they are closely linked to the original discoveries of the conjunction fallacy and the unpacking effect and serve to provide a historical overview of debates surrounding the heuristics and biases program.

Representativeness describes how closely a member matches its categorical prototype or typical examples of the category (see  Fig. 2 ). For instance, a mockingbird is usually viewed as a more representative member of a bird than an ostrich, because of its greater similarity to what is conventionally expected of birds. In many occasions, representativeness can be quantified by similarity measures, but this is not always the case ( Tversky & Kahneman, 1983 ). For instance, even though whales are typically viewed as more representative of mammals than platypuses, they actually are less similar compared with typical mammals (like cats or dogs) than platypuses, considering the terrestrial nature of these typical mammals. In scenarios where quantifying representativeness directly poses challenges, empirical studies usually resort to assessing the perceived representativeness by asking participants to directly evaluate verbally how representative a member is of its category ( Kahneman et al., 1982; Tversky & Kahneman, 1983 ).

Tversky and Kahneman (1983)  posited that the representativeness heuristic – where decisions are made based on the representativeness of a member of its category, rather than the actual probability of belonging to that category – explains the conjunction fallacy. This is because, representativeness of a conjunction can be higher than the representativeness of one of the marginals.

Consider the Linda problem in  Example 1 . In  Tversky and Kahneman (1973) , Linda is introduced as follows:  
                            Linda is 31 years old, single, outspoken and very bright. She majored in philosophy. As a student, she was deeply concerned with issues of discrimination and social justice, and also participated in anti-nuclear demonstrations. 
                          This depiction frames Linda as a committed social activist, a profile that closely matches common perceptions of a feminist, given the frequent association between feminists and social activism. In contrast, this characterization does not align with typical perceptions of bank tellers, who are often thought of as more conservative and less involved in political activism. To explain the conjunction fallacy,  Tversky and Kahneman (1983)  argued that the “feminist” label enhances Linda’s representativeness, and thus Linda align closely more with the combined category of a feminist bank teller than just a bank teller.

Through empirical testing,  Tversky and Kahneman (1983)  confirmed that people indeed judge the representativeness of Linda of the conjunction to be higher than that of the bank teller alone. This finding implies that, at least in terms of correlation, representativeness appears to have a linkage with human probability judgment.

Availability heuristic is the mental shortcut based on availability: how easily an example come to mind ( Tversky & Kahneman, 1973 ). The heuristics is predicated on the notion that if an event or example can be easily recalled from memory, it is judged to be more common or likely to occur (see  Fig. 2 ). To quantify availability, studies have linked retrieval strength and content accuracy to it ( Higgins, King, & Mavin, 1982; Kahneman et al., 1982 ). Nonetheless, there are instances where the predictions of the availability heuristic deviate from those of retrieval strength ( Schwarz et al., 1991; Schwarz & Vaughn, 2002 ).

The availability heursitics can be applied to explain the conjunction fallacy: when a particular member of a category is more easily remembered, it is often also considered more representative or typical of that category ( Tversky & Kahneman, 1983 ). For example, in an experiment where participants are asked to evaluate the frequency of words ending in “- - - - - - n -” versus those ending in “- - - - - i n g” within the same text, logically, every word in the latter category also fits the criteria of the former, meaning the frequency of the “- - - - - i n g” words cannot exceed that of the “- - - - - - n -” words. Yet,  Tversky and Kahneman (1983)  identified a conjunction fallacy where participants perceived the “- - - - - i n g” ending words to be more common. From the standpoint of representativeness heuristics, this conjunction fallacy occurs because words ending in “ing” are seen as more representative of English words. This perception of representativeness is closely linked to availability: words ending in “ing” are simpler to recall than words with “n” as the second but last letter, demonstrating how availability influences judgments of probability and representativeness.

In addition to explaining the conjunction fallacy, the availability heuristic provides valuable insight into another notable probabilistic fallacy: the subadditive unpacking effect ( Smelser, Baltes, et al., 2001 ). The availability heuristic suggests that subadditivity effects arise because specific instances or “unpacked” events are more readily retrievable from memory than their broader, more abstract “packed” counterparts, leading to an overestimation of the probability of the sum of “unpacked” events compared to the “packed event”. Consider  Example 2  from the introduction. From our daily experience, heart diseases and cancers are very commonly mentioned as causes of death and thus they easily remembered. On the other hand, the more general category of “natural causes” is more abstract and harder to recall. Therefore, the availability heuristic suggests that this ease of recall can cause the combined probability judgment for heart diseases and cancers to be higher than that for natural causes.

Furthermore, the availability heuristic could also shed light on superadditivity in unpacking effect ( Sloman et al., 2004 ). This is because introducing less common examples within a category, like deaths from the Hanta virus, could diminish the memorability of these unpacked events because of their rarity, thereby influencing the collective probability estimate for these events.

Heuristic models are deeply rooted in the identification of probabilistic fallacies. Despite their foundational role, these models encountered substantial critique and challenges from the outset.

One major issue with heuristic models is their inherent vagueness. For instance, the question “how representative is Linda as a bank teller?” can be interpreted variably by different individuals, highlighting the ambiguity surrounding the term “representative”. Although there have been attempts to quantify these ambiguous concepts within heuristics, such efforts have consistently fallen short of capturing all their nuances.

This ambiguity directly impacts another significant concern: the models’ predictive limitations.  Gigerenzer (1994, 1996)  argue that many heuristics offer merely post-hoc explanations for specific fallacies without addressing their occurrence in broader contexts. Thus, the link between people’s interpretations of terms like “representativeness” and the fallacies themselves may be correlative rather than causative.

The strongest critique of heuristic models comes from  Costello and Watts (2014, 2018) , who demonstrate that people’s probability judgments adhere to various invariant quantitative patterns. While heuristic models might qualitatively suggest these patterns, they falter in making precise quantitative predictions without making addition of new assumptions ( Nilsson, Juslin, & Winman, 2016 ). The Probability Plus Noise model proposed by  Costello and Watts (2014)  will be examined in detail later as we delve into this critique.

Lastly, heuristic models are criticized for their specificity to certain contexts, especially those involving prior information or descriptions, known as the M-A paradigm. An alternative, the A-B paradigm, directly asks participants for probability judgments (e.g., “what is the probability that it will rain tomorrow?” vs “it will rain and it will be cloudy tomorrow?”). In such scenarios, the concept of representativeness becomes less relevant, but clearly people can make probability judgments about these events.

To address the lack of quantitative predictions of heuristic models, an alternative framework was introduced: the averaging models. Initially, averaging models focused primarily on the conjunction fallacy, but later versions broadened their application to include a wider range of fallacies ( Einhorn & Hogarth, 1985 ). These models, inspired by empirical evidence of averaging in human perceptions ( Anderson, 1981 ), suggest that probability judgments of conjunctions are derived from weighted averages of the probabilities of the individual conjuncts.

Averaging models can be generally divided into two primary categories based on their operational rules for combining probabilities. The first category utilizes additive combination rules, where the probabilities of individual events are summed in a weighted fashion to estimate the conjunction’s probability ( Einhorn & Hogarth, 1985; Fantino et al., 1997; Nilsson et al., 2009; Wyer, 1976; Yates & Carlson, 1986 ). The second category adopts multiplicative combination rules, proposing that the probabilities of individual events are multiplied together to ascertain the probability of the conjunction ( Abelson, Leddo, & Gross, 1987; Birnbaum et al., 1990 ). In the following section, we will explore these two approaches in greater detail.

Averaging models and the Inductive Confirmation (IC) theory have undeniably shed light on specific probabilistic fallacies, such as the conjunction fallacy, offering a quantitative elucidation to the otherwise ambiguous heuristic models. Yet, they are not without their own set of theoretical constraints.

Firstly, a significant shortcoming of the averaging models and IC is their narrow focus on the conjunction fallacy. Although  Einhorn and Hogarth (1985)  and  Nilsson et al. (2009)  introduced more encompassing models that tackled multiple fallacies, none of the averaging models can provide a comprehensive framework to account for the entire spectrum of observed probabilistic fallacies.

Secondly, even though averaging models are quantitative in nature, they do not necessarily depict a cognitive process leading to these fallacies. Their primary focus remains on finding a numerical rule where the judged probability of conjunction is greater than that of the conjuncts, rather than delving into the cognitive mechanisms at play.

Finally, and most importantly, these averaging models are limited to accounting for the mean of the data and lack a theory of error.  Birnbaum et al. (1990)  attempted to address this by adding Gaussian random error to the averaging model’s predictions, but this approach falls short in adequately capturing the broad individual differences observed in judgment predictions. Similarly,  Nilsson et al. (2009) ’s model also only focused on predicting the means rather than the distribution of judgments.

In closing, the insights provided by averaging models, IC, and heuristics on specific fallacies could potentially be consolidated into a unified process model that predicts the quantitative magnitude of all probability judgments. Next, we will delve into how the quantum probability model serves as a robust framework that clarifies the underlying intuitions of heuristics and reveals that the averaging process seen in averaging models may stem from a phenomenon referred to as quantum interference.

Quantum probability models embark on an ambitious journey to provide unified accounts of probabilistic fallacies and make quantitative predictions of probability judgments ( Busemeyer et al., 2011; Pothos & Busemeyer, 2009 ). These models postulate that mathematical principles from quantum theory can offer a robust framework for understanding cognitive science phenomena ( Busemeyer & Bruza, 2012 ). While quantum models employ the mathematical axioms of quantum theory, they do not necessarily posit contradict with the success of Bayesian cognition ( Huang et al., 2023; Pothos & Busemeyer, 2022 ). As we will see, classical probability theory can be viewed as a special case of quantum probability theory.

Unlike classical probability theory, which is based on set theory, quantum probability theory is built on the algebra of projection matrices. A key difference between the two is that matrix operations, such as multiplication, may not commute, while set operations, such as intersections and unions, are inherently commutative. As we will explore later in this section, this non-commutativity enables the quantum model to account for various probabilistic fallacies. Since this tutorial focuses on the specific quantum probability model for human probability judgment errors, a detailed comparison between classical and quantum probability frameworks is beyond the scope. For a thorough conceptual comparison, we encourage readers to refer to  Pothos and Busemeyer (2022) .

As another note, we recognize that many readers may not be familiar with quantum probability models or the concepts discussed in this section. To assist with this, we have included an  Appendix  that offers more detailed explanations of these concepts. If at any point you encounter unfamiliar terms or ideas, please refer to the  Appendix  at the end of the manuscript for clarification.

Finally, the non-commutativity property of quantum probability theory is deeply connected with another behavioral effect: the order effects ( Busemeyer & Bruza, 2012; Trueblood & Busemeyer, 2011; Wang & Busemeyer, 2013 ). (2011)  propose that participants often assess the more probable event first, a strategy that can lead to the emergence of the conjunction fallacy. This “more likely first” rule has also been incorporated into the latest advancements in Quantum Sequential Sampler models ( Huang et al., 2023 ).

While quantum modeling have been praised for its comprehensive approach to explaining many probabilistic fallacies under the same framework, it is not without its critics. Several compelling arguments challenge the model’s scope in explaining probabilistic fallacies.

First, to explain the conjunction fallacy, quantum models require the assumption that events  
                            A 
                          and  
                            B 
                          are incompatible, which inherently suggests the potential for an order effect when considering events  
                            A 
                          and  
                            B 
                         . However, an experiment by  Boyer-Kassem et al. (2016)  about the Linda problem ( Example 1 ) revealed that the conjunction fallacy occurred without a corresponding order effect. Conversely, another study within the same work by  Boyer-Kassem et al. (2016) , along with further evidence from  Yearsley and Trueblood (2018) , indicates a notable correlation between order effects and the conjunction fallacy at times. These mixed results highlight the complexity of the relationship between these phenomena and the need for more comprehensive research.

Another notable limitation of the quantum model is its failure to account for the double conjunction fallacy, where the probability attributed to a conjunction surpasses that of each individual constituent event. While various studies have documented instances of the double conjunction fallacy ( Crupi et al., 2018; Huang et al., 2023 ), these findings primarily demonstrate that such fallacies can occur sporadically rather than in averaged judgments. Moreover, the challenge of explaining the double conjunction fallacy in averaged judgments is not just a problem for the quantum model, but also for even more recent approaches to probability judgments, including the Bayesian Sampler and the Probability Plus Noise model.

A particularly compelling critique of the quantum probability model is offered by  Costello and Watts (2016b) , which highlights the model’s difficulty in accounting for the numerous empirical violations of probability identities (see  Table 1 ). These discrepancies are thought to stem from systematic cognitive noise ( Costello & Watts, 2014 ), a factor that the quantum model’s rigid axiomatic framework struggles to incorporate effectively. In response to this challenge, the Quantum Sequential Sampler model ( Huang et al., 2023 ) has been developed as an innovative approach that successfully integrates cognitive noise into the quantum probability framework. This model, which represents a significant advancement in the field, will be discussed in more detail later in this tutorial.

Finally, a significant limitation impeding the advancement of quantum probability models is the lack of accessible software and tools that enable researchers to easily implement these models. To date, only a few efforts have been made to develop software packages specifically for quantum cognition ( Pothukuchi et al., 2023 ), and much more work is needed to create a user-friendly package that allows cognitive science researchers to readily apply quantum models.

Probability identities are combinations of probabilities that are expected to sum to zero under classical probability theory. They are first identified by  Costello and Watts (2014)  and later expanded in  Costello and Watts (2016a) .  Table 1  summarized all 16 of these identities. As discussed, the biggest challenge facing the quantum probability model is its inability to account for empirical violations of probability identities. However, the Bayesian Sampler model encounters many of the same challenges as the Probability Plus Noise model. Like the Probability Plus Noise model, the Bayesian Sampler model necessitates additional assumptions about conjunctions and disjunctions that are difficult to test empirically to predict conjunction and disjunction fallacies. Additionally, neither model can predict any conjunction fallacy where the probability of the conjunction exceeds 0.5. Furthermore, both models fail to predict a subadditivity effect greater than 1 for two mutually exclusive events. These challenges indicate that although the Bayesian Sampler model offers improvements, further research is essential to fully comprehend human probability judgments.

Recently, several models have emerged that aim to integrate human probability judgments with decision-making under uncertainty. In this tutorial, we highlight two of the most prominent approaches: the Autocorrelated Bayesian Sampler and the Quantum Sequential Sampler. This integration is significant as it represents the first attempt to unify probability judgments and choice probabilities — two measures that are seemingly related but have not been directly linked in previous models. Besides, these models can also predict response times of choices, an important component of choice dynamics ( Ratcliff & McKoon, 2008 ).

The Autocorrelated Bayesian Sampler enhances the original Bayesian Sampler from  Zhu et al. (2020)  in two significant ways. First, it extends the model to predict choice, confidence, and response time in decision making. Their simulation results show that the original Bayesian Sampler with a direct sampling process cannot account for various important patterns in choices and response times. Second, it replaces the original model’s direct sampling with independent samples with a Monte Carlo Markov Chain (MCMC) method that uses autocorrelated samples. We will briefly discuss here how the Autocorrelated Bayesian Sampler predicts choice and response time, and examine a few enhancements it introduces in predicting probability judgments. For a deeper dive into the model’s broader applications, please see  Zhu et al. (2023) .

To model choice and response time, a necessary consideration is stopping condition of the sampling process. To predict probability judgments, both the autocorrelated and the original Bayesian Sampler assume that sampling stops at a predetermined fixed sample size. However, this stopping condition may not adequately capture choice and response time, particularly when sample numbers are evenly distributed across available options ( Zhu et al., 2023 ). As a solution, the Autocorrelated Bayesian Sampler utilizes a different stopping condition known as the “max–minus-next rule”. This rule asserts that individuals calculate the difference in sample counts between the two most probable options, and cease sampling once this difference surpasses a specified threshold. For instance, in binary decisions between choices A and B, should the samples favoring A exceed those for B by a set threshold (e.g., 10 samples), the sampling ends, and it is predicted that the participant will opt for A over B.

Regarding probability judgments, the Quantum Sequential Sampler outperforms all existing models, including the Autocorrelated Bayesian Sampler. This superiority stems from its ability to more accurately predict both probability identities and mean judgments quantitatively, as well as its capability to explain many normality identities that other models struggle with qualitatively ( Huang et al., 2024b ). Despite these strengths, the Quantum Sequential Sampler is not without its challenges. Beyond the previously mentioned

Concerning choice and response time, both the Quantum Sequential Sampler and Bayesian Sampler models have yet to be tested against empirical data to verify their consistency with actual results. Although both models demonstrate the ability to simulate a drift diffusion model – the gold standard in modeling choice and response time – they employ fewer parameters than the drift diffusion model in predicting these metrics. Therefore, it is crucial to fit both models to empirical data to determine their true effectiveness. Additionally, the traditional drift diffusion model has predominantly focused on the dynamics of preference choices (e.g., “Do you prefer Paris over Madrid?”) ( Ratcliff, 2006; Ratcliff & Smith, 2004 ), rather than on prediction polls (e.g., “Who will win the 2028 US presidential election?”) ( Atanasov et al., 2017; Erikson & Wlezien, 2012 ). In contrast, models like the Autocorrelated Bayesian Sampler and the Quantum Sequential Sampler are more apt for modeling decisions in prediction polls rather than preferences. This is because prediction polls are more closely related to probability judgments, as both involve forecasting future uncertain events. It remains unclear how well the drift diffusion model performs in predicting both choice and response time in the context of prediction polls, and whether models of probability judgment might offer more accurate insights for such scenarios. To the best of the author’s knowledge, no studies have yet measured response times in prediction polls.

Finally, there has been criticism of the quantum sequential sampler regarding their potentially higher complexity, which may grant them greater flexibility in fitting data. To address these concerns,  Huang et al. (2024b)  conducted a generalization test, fitting the model to only a portion of the data and testing it on the remaining, unfitted data. The results of this test indicate that the Quantum Sequential Sampler not only provides a better fit to the data compared to classical models like the Bayesian Sampler but also demonstrates superior generalizability. Additionally, some studies have compared quantum and classical probability models using Bayesian analysis, showing that the quantum model continues to outperform its classical counterpart even when penalizing for complexity ( Busemeyer, Kvam, & Pleskac, 2019 ).  Trueblood et al. (2017)  further argued that quantum probability models require a less complex mathematical structure, as fully compatible classical probability spaces demand higher-dimensional vector representations, whereas incompatible quantum probability spaces can be represented in lower-dimensional vector spaces.

In our concluding remarks, we briefly highlight three significant future directions for this line of research. We strongly urge researchers to explore these areas, either by testing the models discussed in this tutorial against these challenges or by developing new models to address the unresolved issues identified in existing frameworks.

In previous discussions, we explored how models of probability judgment could be adapted to account for choice and response time. However, existing models struggle to accurately predict the response time distribution associated with probability judgments. To clarify, while these models can adapt to predict the response time of choices (e.g., “Will it be rainy tomorrow?”), they fall short in predicting the response time for probability judgments (e.g., “What is the probability that it will rain tomorrow?”). This limitation stems from the models’ assumption that the sampling process for probability judgments stops at a fixed sample size, but the mechanism determining this fixed sample size remains unclear. That said, more works are needed to clarify the stopping conditions for probability judgments in order to predict response times of probability judgments.

In  Huang et al. (2024a, 2024b) , it was discovered that binary complementarity is violated (the sum exceeds 1) when participants are asked to assess the probability of Trump winning the 2020 election in state A, versus Biden winning in the same state. This finding contrasts with many previous studies where binary complementarity is commonly observed ( Budescu, Wallsten, & Au, 1997; Tversky & Koehler, 1994 ). However, besides ( Huang et al., 2024a, 2024b ), there is robust evidence suggesting binary complementarity can be violated both superadditively ( Macchi, Osherson, & Krantz, 1999 ) and subadditively ( Epping & Busemeyer, 2023 ). According to  Huang et al. (2024a, 2024b) , a key reason for the discrepancy is their methodology: instead of framing questions as “Trump versus not Trump”, they compared Trump directly with another typical candidate, Biden, making it less apparent that their probabilities should be complementary. If this explanation holds, similar effects might be observed in other domains, such as weather or sports forecasting, when the term “not” is avoided. Consequently, a broader experiment replicating the findings of  Huang et al. (2024a, 2024b)  is warranted. Furthermore, such studies should not only replicate their findings but also explore the range of sums for two complementary events to understand better under what conditions binary complementarity violations occur.

The double conjunction fallacy, where the probability of a conjunction exceeds that of both of its individual marginals, remains a puzzle for recent comprehensive models of human probability judgments including Bayesian Sampler, Probability Plus Noise model, and quantum probabilistic models. Empirical evidence regarding this fallacy is mixed. While studies such as those by  Crupi et al. (2018), Fisk and Pidgeon (1998)  and  Huang et al. (2024b)  confirm its occurrence, others, like  Busemeyer et al. (2011)  and  Wyer (1976) , cast doubt on its frequency. Given these mixed results, further investigation is necessary to explore the prevalence of the double conjunction fallacy more thoroughly, which will help in rigorously testing these existing models.

Despite the limitations of current comprehensive models in accounting for the fallacy, specialized models designed for the conjunction fallacy, such as the inductive confirmation account by  Tentori et al. (2013)  and the averaging model by  Wyer (1976) , provide potential explanations for the double conjunction fallacy, albeit within a narrower scope. Establishing robust evidence for the double conjunction fallacy could strongly support these specific heuristics or averaging models. This would also underscore the necessity to incorporate elements of these heuristic models into broader, more comprehensive frameworks, enhancing their explanatory power.

A key challenge for existing models of probability judgments is their inability to predict, a-priori, which events are more likely to result in conjunction and disjunction fallacies. Inductive confirmation theory shows potential in addressing this gap by providing confirmation measures that might forecast conjunction fallacies in advance. However, this approach remains underexplored, and the specific thresholds in confirmation measures that could lead to a conjunction fallacy are not yet clearly defined. Furthermore, the same approach fails to forecast the disjunction fallacy.

Beyond inductive confirmation, no current models attempt to forecast conjunction and disjunction fallacies. Quantum models, for example, explain these fallacies through event incompatibility but lack a mechanism to determine, a-priori, which events are likely to be incompatible. So far, incompatibility has been associated primarily with familiarity, as previously discussed ( Trueblood et al., 2017 ). Likewise, models such as the Bayesian Sampler and the Probability Plus Noise Model address these fallacies post hoc, relying on assumptions about sampling processes that do not offer predictive capacity.

Future research should investigate other event properties that might correlate with the likelihood of conjunction and disjunction fallacies, and incorporating them into the computational models.

A promising future direction involves exploring how existing models of probability judgments and decision-making under uncertainty can be integrated with other cognitive processes, such as memory and attention ( Jones & Love, 2011 ). For instance, sampling-based models suggest that sample sizes and cognitive noise might relate to working memory, though they have not yet thoroughly explained these connections in light of empirical findings. Further research is needed to define these connections clearly and to enable predictions regarding memory and response time effects.

At present, the quantum model is the only framework that attempts to link measures of attention and memory with decision-making under uncertainty. For instance,  Rosendahl, Bizyaeva, and Cohen (2020)  introduces a quantum model that integrates arousal with decision-making under uncertainty. However, this model has yet to be tested on empirical data.
