The availability of multisensory signals is often beneficial when making perceptual decisions ( Alais, Newell, & Mamassian, 2010; Chandrasekaran, 2017; Stein & Stanford, 2008; Ursino, Cuppini, & Magosso, 2014 ). To study benefits in the speed of responses, research has extensively used the redundant signals paradigm with its three basic conditions ( Ashby & Townsend, 1986; Diederich & Colonius, 2004a; Giray & Ulrich, 1993; Hershenson, 1962; Kinchla, 1974; Miller, 1982, 1986; Todd, 1912 ); see dark grey box in  Fig. 1 (a). The long-standing and highly replicated finding is the  redundant signals effect  (RSE): responses to redundant signals are faster than responses to unisensory signals (for recent examples, see  Crosse, Foxe, & Molholm, 2019; Innes & Otto, 2019; Lunn, Sjoblom, Ward, Soto-Faraco, & Forster, 2019; Murray et al., 2018; Van der Stoep, Van der Stigchel, Van Engelen, Biesbroek, & Nijboer, 2019 ).

Two competing model architectures were considered most frequently to account for the RSE: race models and pooling models. As championed by  Raab (1962) , race models assume that the two targets are processed by parallel decision units and that a response on a given trial is triggered by the faster of the two units (the term “race model” reflects the analogy to a horse race). If corresponding decision times follow random distributions that overlap, race models predict faster and less variable reaction times (RTs) due to statistical facilitation. The mechanism is that a slower decision in one unit can be compensated for by a faster in the other. Alternatively, pooling models assume that sensory evidence for both targets is combined to satisfy the threshold of a single decision unit ( Miller, 1982 ). As two targets provide more sensory evidence than one, pooling models predict a speed-up of RTs by summing the evidence. The mechanism is somehow similar to the speed-up of RTs as expected when, for example, the volume of a sound or the contrast of a visual stimulus is increased. At this point, it is critical to highlight that we use the term pooling model to avoid ambiguity ( Otto & Mamassian, 2017 ). To describe models with a single decision unit, the alternative term coactivaion model has been used rather consistently (e.g.,  Blurton, Greenlee, & Gondan, 2014; Colonius & Diederich, 2017; Diederich, 1994; Mordkoff & Yantis, 1991; Schwarz & Ischebeck, 1994; Townsend & Wenger, 2004; Ulrich & Giray, 1986; Yang, Altieri, & Little, 2018; Zehetleitner, Krummenacher, & Müller, 2009 ; for a recent review, see  Colonius & Diederich, 2018 ). However,  Miller (2016) , who coined the term in 1982, clarified that the term should not be used to describe basic model architecture.

For multisensory and cognitive research, it is a central theme to understand how sensory evidence is combined and, therefore, to discriminate between the competing model architectures. A frequently applied approach checks if race models can be ruled out, which uses the convenient feature that race models directly predict the RSE on the level of RT distributions. Inequality 1 is also known as Miller’s bound, which corresponds to the race model prediction assuming a maximal negative correlation between decision times to auditory and visual signals ( Colonius, 1990 ; a negative correlation implies that observing a fast response to one signal would make it more likely to observe a slow response to the other). The handy feature of Miller’s bound is that it relates RTs in redundant and single signal conditions (the left-hand term is given by the empirical CDF in the redundant signals condition, the right-hand term is given by the sum of the empirical CDFs in the single signal conditions;  Fig. 1 b, top panel). If Miller’s bound is violated at any time  t , the mainstream view is that race models cannot explain the RSE and that the alternative class of pooling models needs to be involved instead (for recent reviews of the approach, see  Gondan & Minakata, 2016; Otto & Mamassian, 2017 ). As violations of Miller’s bound replicate well with multisensory signals, pooling models have dominated the research area in the last decades.

A critical issue with Miller’s bound is that the test makes the so-called context invariance assumption (e.g.,  Ashby & Townsend, 1986; Colonius, 1990; Luce, 1986; Miller, 2016; Otto & Mamassian, 2017 ). To make exact predictions, Miller’s bound should be computed based on the processing times of the two parallel decision units on presentation of redundant signals ( Fig. 1 b, bottom panel). Unfortunately, it is difficult to extract these times empirically as it is difficult to know if a response on presentation of redundant signals was triggered by one or the other decision unit ( Luce, 1986 ).                        By assuming context invariance, empirical RTs as measured in the single signal conditions can be used to compute Miller’s bound as stated in Inequality 1. At this point, it is therefore crucial to realize that any study that has used Miller’s bound, knowingly or not, has tested race models under the assumption of context invariance.

Is it important to consider context invariance? Violations of Miller’s bound are typically used as evidence to reject race models as an explanation of the RSE, which seems like a valid deductive argument: If race models explain the RSE, then Miller’s bound provides an upper limit. Empirical evidence demonstrates that Miller’s bound is violated (denying the consequent). Therefore, race models cannot explain the RSE. The problem with the argument is that the first premise is incomplete. The correct conditional statement reads: If race models explain the RSE and if context invariance holds, then Miller’s bound provides an upper limit. As the evidence stays the same, it can only be concluded that the race model and the context invariance are not both true. It is therefore crucial to realize that violations of Miller’s bound cannot be used to rule out all race models, that is, a cognitive architecture that assumes two parallel decision units (e.g.,  Gondan & Minakata, 2016; Otto & Mamassian, 2010, 2017; Yang et al., 2018 ). A valid alternative is that the context invariance assumption may be wrong.

Given that Miller’s bound is frequently used in research on multisensory RTs, an important question is whether or not the research field is aware of the context invariance assumption. As mentioned above, the assumption is considered in early theoretical work (e.g.,  Ashby & Townsend, 1986; Colonius, 1990; Luce, 1986 ), but it is only implicit in  Miller (1982)  and it is not mentioned in a frequently cited methods paper promoting the test ( Ulrich, Miller, & Schröter, 2007 ). Given that  Miller (1982)  used the terms separate activation and race model synonymously, the add-on “separate” may imply that context invariance is an inherent feature of this model class, but this link is rarely made explicit ( Miller, 2016 ). To evaluate the current understanding in the field, we systematically checked recent empirical studies that used the test. For this review, we searched the Web of Science (Clarivate Analytics) for articles published between 2016 and 2018 that had cited either  Miller (1982)  or  Raab (1962) . Using this method, we identified 84 studies that tested the RSE empirically. Among these, 50 studies used Miller’s bound,

Given the lack of consideration, it would be vital for the field to know if context invariance can be reasonably assumed, in which case the rejection of race models could still stand. Unfortunately, as pointed out by  Luce (1986) , testing the context invariance assumption directly is difficult. The reason is that on presentation of redundant signals, it is difficult to know whether a response was triggered by one or the other decision unit. Hence, it is difficult to access the left-hand term of Eq.  (2) . As an alternative approach, we propose that it is possible to check context invariance more generally, and critically, in empirically testable scenarios. If context invariance holds in such scenarios, one might argue that it is reasonably safe to assume context invariance to be also true when testing race models with Miller’s bound. On the other hand, if context invariance does not hold more generally, such findings would show that the assumption, as used in the race model test, must be considered with more care.

To derive conditions that allow testing for context invariance broadly, we extended the original redundant signals paradigm by using the same signal compositions but with three types of task instructions ( Fig. 1 a). As in the original paradigm, we included the typical  A 
                      
                         ∪ 
                      
                      V -instruction and asked participants to respond on presentation of either an auditory or a visual signal. Hence, responses were required in three trial types ( A- ,  V-,  and  AV -trials). No response was required on catch trials. In an additional  A -instruction, we asked participants to respond only on presentation of an auditory signal. Hence, responses were required in unisensory auditory and in multisensory trials ( A-  and  AV -trials). No response was required on unisensory visual trials ( V- trials) and on catch trials. Likewise, in an additional  V -instruction, participants were instructed to respond only on presentation of a visual signal ( V-  and  AV -trials, but not on  A- trials and catch trials). Using this design, two types of context influence can be tested, which we coin  stimulus context  and  instruction context .

As first scenario, the role of stimulus context is tested by analysing responses under unisensory instructions ( Fig. 1 d). For example, under the  A- instruction, we measured performance in both uni- and multisensory trials ( A-  and  AV- trials). The difference between these conditions is that multisensory trials contain a task-irrelevant stimulus (here the visual stimulus). Analogously, the role of stimulus context can be tested under the  V- instruction. If performance is generally invariant to the stimulus context, performance in uni- and multisensory trials is expected to remain unchanged.

As second scenario, the role of instruction context is tested by analysing responses on presentation of unisensory targets ( Fig. 1 e). For example, on  A- trials, we measured performance under both uni- and multisensory instructions ( A-  and  A 
                      
                         ∪ 
                      
                      V -instructions). The difference between these conditions is that under multisensory instructions, participants are asked to respond also to the second unisensory signal (here the visual stimulus). Critically, on analysed trials, the second signal was not present, hence the physical stimulation in both conditions is the same. Analogously, the role of instruction context can be tested on presentation of  V- trials. If performance is generally invariant to the instruction context, performance under uni- and multisensory instructions is expected to remain unchanged.

With these two scenarios, we aim to evaluate the plausibility of the context invariance assumption (Eq.  (2) ). To explore the role of stimulus and instruction context broadly, we tested performance in a simple detection task (as introduced so far) and analogously in a go/no-go tasks. A difference between the two tasks lies in the number of transient signal onsets. In the detection task, A- and V-trials present transient signal onsets in only one modality. In contrast, AV-trials have transient signal onsets in both modalities. Hence, the number of transients is changing. In the go/no-go task, all trials have two transient signal onsets, one in each modality. Hence, the number of transients is here constant. As transient signal onsets are considered to contribute to violations of Miller’s bound ( Innes & Otto, 2019 ), we speculate that changes in context might influence these two tasks differently. Consequently, we investigated each scenario using a task (detection vs. go/no-go) and sensory modality (audition vs. vision) factorial design. If performance is constant across changing contexts, our results would provide at least some assurance that assuming context invariance is unproblematic when testing race models with Miller’s bound. On the contrary, if performance is not constant, our results would provide a warning message that the context invariance assumption must be considered with more care.

44 participants took part in the study (10 males, 34 females; aged between 18–34 years). All had self-reported normal or corrected-to-normal vision and normal hearing. Participants gave informed consent before the experiment. The study was approved by the  University Teaching and Research Ethics Committee  (UTREC; approval code: PS12613).

The study was carried out in a dimly lit and sound-attenuated room. We controlled the experiment using MATLAB (MathWorks) and the Psychophysics Toolbox extensions ( Brainard, 1997 ;  Kleiner, Brainard, & Pelli, 2007 ; Pelli, 1997 ). Auditory stimuli were delivered to both ears using Sennheiser HD-280 Pro headphones with a sampling frequency of 44.1 kHz. We calibrated the auditory volume using a Bruel and Kjær sound level metre (Type 2250 light) equipped with an artificial ear adaptor (Type 4153). Visual stimuli were presented using a Dell UltraSharp U2713 monitor (resolution: 1920*1080 pixel; refresh rate: 60 Hz). The viewing distance was 57 cm as supported by a headstand. We collected responses using a hand-held pushbutton (custom-made) connected to an RTBox V6 ( Li, Liang, Kleiner, & Lu, 2010 ).

We employed both a detection and a go/no-go task. In the detection task, a target signal was either present or not. Participants were instructed to respond when they detected a target signal. In the go/no-go task, a signal was presented on each trial. If a target signal was presented, participants were instructed to respond. Nontarget signals did not require a response.

For each task, we defined targets using three instructions ( Fig. 1 a). In A 
                            ∪ 
                         V-instruction blocks, participants responded to any auditory or visual target signal. In  A -instruction blocks, participants responded only to auditory target signals. In  V -instruction blocks, participants were asked to respond only to visual target signals.

Within instruction blocks, we presented four different combinations of signals randomly interleaved ( Fig. 1 a). In the detection task, these were an auditory signal only ( A -trials), a visual signal only ( V -trials), both an auditory and a visual signal simultaneously ( AV -trials), and no signal at all (catch trials). In the go/no-go task, these were signals with an auditory target and a visual nontarget ( A -trials), signals with a visual target and an auditory nontarget ( V -trials), signals with both an auditory and a visual targets ( AV -trials), and signals with both an auditory and a visual nontargets (catch trials).

The RSE has been observed basically with any combination of salient multisensory signals. For consistency, we here use a stimulus design similar to previous studies from our group ( Innes & Otto, 2019; Otto, Dassy, & Mamassian, 2013; Otto & Mamassian, 2012 ).

Auditory simulation . All auditory stimuli were generated anew on each trial using Gaussian noise (i.e., a sequence of normally distributed random numbers at a sample rate of 44.1 kHz). Auditory detection and go-signals were noise tones filtered using a 2nd order Butterworth filter with cut-off frequencies of 1000 Hz and 1500 Hz. Auditory nontarget signals were noise tones filtered using a 2nd order Butterworth filter with cut-off frequencies of 250 Hz to 750 Hz. All three types of signals were presented at 55.5 dB SPL. In addition, they were embedded in auditory background noise, which was bandpass filtered using a 2nd order Butterworth filter with cut-off frequencies of 200 Hz and 1550 Hz. We presented the background noise at 55 dB SPL.

Visual simulation . Visual background stimulation consisted of 1500 dots presented on a uniform grey screen. Each dot had the size of a pixel and was randomly either black or white. Stimulus timing . Trials started with the presentation of the audio-visual background and a white fixation dot at the centre of the screen. This foreperiod was composed of a minimum duration of 750 ms and a random component that was sampled from an exponential distribution with a mean of 500 ms. Then, a response period of 1000 ms started. On signal trials, signals were presented throughout the response period. Auditory signals included a squared-sine ramp onset and a squared-cosine ramp offset, each of 10 ms duration. On catch trials in the detection task, stimulation continued as during the foreperiod. After a response or at the end of the response period, all audio-visual simulation stopped. Following false alarms and misses, a feedback screen with a red dot as fixation appeared for 750 ms. A new trial started after the presentation of a uniform grey screen for 500 ms. Auditory and visual events were calibrated for synchrony using the RTbox.

Responses made during the response period in target trials (i.e., containing a target according to instruction) were considered valid. If no response was made within the response period, the trial was counted as a miss. Any response made during the foreperiod or during the response period on catch trials was considered a false alarm. Participants were encouraged to avoid false alarm and misses. Following a false alarm or a miss, a corresponding trial was repeated by adding it back to the remaining trial schedule and re-shuffling the presentation order.

Trials were presented in blocks of at least 60 trials (more trials were added following errors, see above). To ensure that the response rate is kept constant at around 50% ( Gordon, 1967; Näätänen, 1972 ), each block contained 30 target trials ( Table 1 ). Each block lasted about 3–4 min.

Each participant performed both the detection and the go/no-go task. The order of tasks was counterbalanced across participants. For each task, four  A- , four  V-  and six  A 
                         
                            ∪ 
                         
                         V -instruction blocks were presented. This procedure ensured that for each target condition and instruction block, 60 valid responses were collected. For example, each  A -instruction block contains 15  A -trials and 15  AV -trials ( Table 1 ). Presenting four  A -instruction blocks thus yields 60  A -trials and 60  AV -trials per participant. Instruction blocks were presented in a pseudo-random order. Including breaks, the experiment took around 2.5 h to complete. In total, we presented 76,278 trials and collected 36,957 correct responses on signal and go trials.

The extended paradigm allows computing Miller’s bounds with three different pairs of unisensory CDFs ( Fig. 5 a). First, we used  A - and  V -trials, both tested in  A 
                         
                            ∪ 
                         
                         V -instructions ( Fig. 5 , dark red). These conditions share the same instruction with the predicted redundant signals condition but differ in the presented stimulus. We refer to this computation as the stimulus-different pair. This option is typically tested in RSE studies. Second, we used  A -trials in  A -instructions and  V -trials in  V -instructions ( Fig. 5 , light red). These conditions differ to the predicted redundant signals condition in both stimulus and instruction, which we refer to as the both-different pair. Third, we used  AV -trials tested in  A - and  V -instructions ( Fig. 5 , orange). These conditions share the same stimulus context as the predicted redundant signals condition but differ in instruction. Consequently, we refer to this computation as the instruction-different pair. We computed Miller’s bound with each of the three pairs. Hence, this allowed us to check RTs on  AV -trials tested under the  A 
                         
                            ∪ 
                         
                         V -instruction for potential violations of the race model inequality in three different ways.

For statistical analysis, we performed all tests in IBM SPSS Statistics 22. When analysing the effects of stimulus context, we used a repeated-measures analysis of variance (ANOVA) with factors  task  (detection, go/no-go) and  instruction  ( A ,  V ). When analysing the effects of instruction context, we used an ANOVA with factors  task  (detection, go/no-go) and  trial  ( A ,  V ). For both, we specifically looked at the intercept term in the ANOVA outcome as a significant intercept can be directly interpreted as a violation of stimulus and instruction context variance, respectively. We further performed a correlation analysis across conditions to investigate the relationship between performance changes due to instruction context and history costs (for both speed and accuracy). When analysing violations of Miller’s bound, we used an ANOVA with factors  task  (detection, go/no-go) and  unisensory RT pair  (stimulus-different, both-different, instruction-different). When performing ANOVAs, we applied Greenhouse-Geisser corrections whenever sphericity was violated. The alpha level for all statistical tests was 5%.

In summary, predictions of multisensory RTs depend on the context of the unisensory RTs that are fed to the model. In this example, violations of Miller’s bound vary with the unisensory pair type, which can eventually lead to fundamentally different conclusions. In the extreme, the conclusion can flip from rejecting race models when using one pair to not rejecting race models when using another pair. Hence, it is essential to consider the influence of context.

This study emphasizesthe critical role of the context invariance assumption, which is frequently made in multisensory modelling. The assumption states that processing of parallel decision units is invariant to the experimental context, i.e., whether a single or a redundant signal is presented (Eq.  (2) ). It is crucial to consider context invariance carefully, as its violation can lead to a categorically different interpretation of multisensory benefits, for example whether the basic architecture of race models is rejected, or not. While the assumption was thoroughly discussed in early theoretical work (e.g.,  Ashby & Townsend, 1986; Colonius, 1990; Luce, 1986 ), it is silently assumed in 84% of recent empirical contributions as reviewed in the introduction. Unfortunately, it is difficult to test for context invariance empirically ( Luce, 1986 ). To tackle this issue, we assessed context invariance indirectly in two similar scenarios that can be tested: stimulus and instruction context. If performance is generally constant when the experimental context is changing, our results would provide at least some assurance that it is reasonably safe to assume context invariance. In contrast, if performance is variant across changing contexts, our results would highlight that the context invariance assumption is likely to be violated and should therefore be considered more cautiously.

As first scenario, we investigated performance under unisensory instructions when the stimulus context is changing ( Fig. 1 d). For example, for responses under  A -instructions, we compared performance in  A - and  AV -trials. The stimulus context is changing as the latter presented also a task-irrelevant visual stimulus. We found a trade-off between speed and accuracy, responses in multisensory trials were faster while sensitivity decreased ( Fig. 2 ). The message from the first experiment is that performance can be variant when the stimulus context is changing.

Our findings are not unique, performance changes across different stimulus contexts have frequently been reported in research on selective attention in multisensory perception. For example,  Talsma, Doty, and Woldorff (2006)  instructed participants to attend to the visual modality. The authors found that responses were slower and less accurate for audio-visual compared to visual objects. As another example,  Mozolic, Hugenschmidt, Peiffer and Laurienti (2008)  asked participants to attend to stimuli in one modality. Here, responses were faster and more accurate for audio-visual compared to visual stimuli. Finally, several studies on warning signals have reported performance differences in visual tasks with and without task-irrelevant auditory warning signals (e.g.,  Van der Burg, Cass, Olivers, Theeuwes, & Alais, 2010; Gleiss & Kayser, 2013; Kim, Peters, & Shams, 2012; Lippert, Logothetis, & Kayser, 2007; Matusz, Wallace, & Murray, 2017; Murata, Kuroda, & Karwowski, 2017 ). Given this broad range of observations, it seems rather difficult to insist that performance in unisensory tasks is invariant to stimulus context.

To understand the observed speed-accuracy trade-off in our experiment, we noted that the change in sensitivity was largely due to an increased false alarm rate with task-irrelevant signals. It is therefore possible that the speed-up of responses on AV-trials was caused by fast but false responses to the task-irrelevant stimuli (e.g., the speed-up of RTs on  AV -trials under  A -instructions could be caused by fast false alarms to the visual stimulus component). To check this possibility, we compared genuine unisensory RTs (e.g., responses on A-trials under A-instructions) to the timing of false alarms in unisensory trials that did not require a response (e.g., on V-trials under A-instructions). However, we found these false alarms to be rather slower than genuine RTs. Thus, the observed speed-up of RTs is not directly due to false alarms to task-irrelevant signals. Some other change in processing is required to explain the change of performance.

On hypothesis is temporal preparation in that an earlier presented and/or faster processed signal in one modality facilitates processing of a signal in another modality ( Los & Van der Burg, 2013 ). For example, if the task-irrelevant component on AV-trials (e.g., the visual component under A-instructions) is processed faster than the task-relevant component (e.g., the auditory component under A-instructions), the former could facilitate processing of the task-relevant signal through an effective preparation period. In contrast, if it is processed slower, no facilitation is expected. Interestingly in this regard, we found RTs in the go/no-go task to be faster on V- compared to A-trials. Consequently, the temporal preparation hypothesis would predict a larger speed-up on AV-trials under A- compared to V-instructions. In contrast, in the detection task, we found no difference in RTs. Consequently, the speed-up is not expected to differ either. Our results are in accordance with these predictions ( Fig. 2 b). Hence, the violation of stimulus context invariance could be explained by the temporal preparation hypothesis, which not necessarily requires integration or pooling of sensory evidence.

As second scenario, we investigated responses to unisensory trials when the instruction context is changing ( Fig. 1 e). For example, for responses to  A -trials, we compared performance under  A - and  A 
                         
                            ∪ 
                         
                         V -instructions. The instruction context is changing as the latter requires to attend also to a second stimulus (which is however only present on other trials). We found that performance under  A 
                         
                            ∪ 
                         
                         V -instructions was slower and had higher miss rates than that under unisensory instructions (except for V-trials in the detection task; see  Fig. 3 ). The message from the second scenario is that performance with unisensory signals can be variant when the instruction context is changing.

Our findings are not unique here either, performance changes under different instructions have frequently been reported in studies exploring the influence of selective attention in multisensory perception. For example,  Talsma et al. (2006)  recorded responses to visual objects. They found responses were slower and with more errors under the instruction to attend to both audition and vision compared to attending to vision only. In addition, they found a decrease in the P1 component of the ERP signal across the two attentional instructions. As another example,  Mozolic, Joyner et al. (2008)  adopted a similar task and found decreased sensitivity to auditory targets under an audio-visual than an auditory attention task. They related this to the suppression of visual cortex activity when participants were instructed to attend to audition only. Given these congruent findings, it seems rather difficult to insist on the assumption of instruction context invariance on presentation of unisensory signals.

To understand the performance changes in our experiment, we explored the link to sequential effects. When analysed as a function of trial history, performance is typically better when a modality is repeated (e.g., when a  V -trial is preceded by a  V -trial) than when modalities are switched (e.g., when a  V -trial is preceded by an  A -trial), which can be expressed as trial history costs (for examples in RSE studies, see  Gondan, Lange, Rösler, & Röder, 2004; Gondan, Vorberg, & Greenlee, 2007; Innes & Otto, 2019; Miller, 1982; Otto & Mamassian, 2012 ). The question is whether sequential effects account for the performance changes as observed with different instructions ( Fig. 3 ). Interestingly, we found that the change in history costs basically mirrored the performance changes when the instruction context is changed ( Fig. 4 ). The changes in sequential effects can be understood from an attentional perspective. Under unisensory instructions, participants can attend to only one modality, which allows to suppress processing in the other ( Mozolic, Hugenschmidt et al., 2008; Mozolic, Joyner et al., 2008 ). For example, A-instructions allow participants to focus on audition and to suppress any visual signal. In this case, modality switch costs should be low because there is actually no need to switch attention between modalities. In contrast,  A 
                         
                            ∪ 
                         
                         V -instructions ask participants to divide attention across audition and vision ( Spence, Nicholls, & Driver, 2001 ). In this case, modality switch costs should be higher because attention needs constantly to switch as signals are presented in different modalities. This analysis suggests that the violation of instruction context invariance is at least partly due to changes in sequential effects.

An interesting observation is that responses to auditory stimuli are more affected by sequential effects than responses to visual stimuli. It may be tempting to speculate here about a potential link to the Colavita visual dominance effect. When instructed to respond to auditory and visual signals with separate behavioural responses, the observation is that participants respond more frequently only to the visual component when both stimuli are presented ( Colavita, 1974; Sinnett, Spence, & Soto-Faraco, 2007 ). As this paradigm also requires that the two signals are presented in a random order, future research may investigate if sequential dependency contributes to the Colavita effect.

As both stimulus and instruction context invariance are violated, our results demonstrate that context invariance cannot generally assumed to be true. To illustrate the importance of the assumption when interpreting multisensory RTs ( AV -trials under  A 
                         
                            ∪ 
                         
                         V -instructions in our experiment), we tested for violations of Miller’s bound, which is widely used to reject race models (see the many follow-up studies of ( Miller, 1982 )). Critically, our experimental design allows deriving Miller’s bounds using three different pairs of unisensory RTs, which differ in stimulus context, instruction context, or both (see Section  2.6  and  Fig. 5 a). If context invariance would be generally true, it should not matter which pair is used to compute the bound. However, this is not what we found. Violations of Miller’s bound varied when computed with different unisensory pairs, with violations being largest with the most frequently used instruction-different pair ( Fig. 5 b). In contrast, no significant violations occurred with the both-different and the stimulus-different pairs in the go/no-go task. If the role of context is not considered, these differences result in conflicting conclusions. While one test seems to suggest that race models must be rejected, the other would see exactly the same behavioural data to be in agreement with race models.

Unfortunately, current research practice basically neglects contextual effects. As reviewed in the introduction, 84% of the RSE studies published between 2016 and 2018 seem to be unaware of the context invariance assumption (at least it is not mentioned). What is more, if it is considered, the importance of the assumption is rather downplayed. For example, without naming the assumption explicitly,  Chandrasekaran (2017)  simply argued that when ‘appropriate care is taken to arrange experimental conditions’, race models can be rejected using Miller’s test. However, the approach of how to appropriately arrange experimental conditions is left vague. Alternatively, race models that allow for context variance are criticized as being ‘unfalsifiable’ ( Miller, 2016 ). However, we think that this criticism applies more urgently to the large body of studies that reject race models without providing an alternative model that allows for testable predictions ( Otto & Mamassian, 2017 ). Simply stating that the RSE is due to ‘multisensory integration’ does not provide explanation, unless the integration mechanism is explicitly specified by a testable model. The key point made by this study is that any model of the RSE, whether using a race or pooling architecture, needs to consider contextual effects.

To understand multisensory interactions, it may be particularly interesting to compare conditions that do and do not violate Miller’s bound. In this respect, as discussed above, no significant violations occurred with the both-different and stimulus-different pairs in the go/no-go task ( Fig. 5 b). In contrast, violations occurred with the corresponding pairs in the detection task. Using what they called a comparative approach,  Innes and Otto (2019)  discussed a link between violations and transient stimulus onsets and a similar explanation could apply here. As presented in the introduction, single and redundant signal conditions differ in the number of onset transients in the detection task (one vs. two transients). On the other hand, the number of onset transients is constant in the go/no-go task (there are two transients in both single and redundant conditions because the former also present a nontarget signal). Using a similar logic, it is further interesting to note that violations in both tasks are largest when the instruction-different pair is used to compute Miller’s bound ( Fig. 5 b). As these conditions are heavily affected by trial history effects ( Fig. 4 ), this finding highlights that any modelling approach, be it with a race or pooling architecture, should seriously consider the contribution of sequential dependency to the RSE.

To advance research on multisensory processing, we argue that the basic race model architecture, despite being frequently rejected, is actually very helpful to examine contextual effects. A first issue is that the basic race model architecture perfectly matches the task demands of the redundant signals paradigm ( Otto & Mamassian, 2017 ). Intriguingly then, the basic race model architecture, for example in form of the independent race model ( Raab, 1962 ), directly allows for predictions on the level of RT distributions. These predictions can be used as benchmark performance. The quest is then not necessarily to reject the basic race model architecture but rather to understand deviances from the benchmark, which can be interpreted as the influence of context as a valid alternative. This approach was possibly first formulated by Townsend and co-workers by quantifying the deviance from the independent race model in terms of processing capacity, which led to  systems factorial technology  as a diagnostic tool (e.g.,  Little, Altieri, Fific, & Yang, 2017; Townsend & Nozawa, 1997; Townsend & Wenger, 2004; Wenger & Townsend, 2000; Yang et al., 2018 ). Instead of discriminating between processing architectures (e.g., pooling vs. racing), this approach classifies performance with respect to workload capacity (e.g., limited vs. super-capacity using the independent race model as baseline; for a recent application to study the effect of ageing, see ( Yang et al., 2019 )). In some sense elaborating on this approach,  Otto and Mamassian (2012)  explicitly modelled a violation of the context invariance assumption by adding noise as a single interaction parameter. This work not only demonstrated that the RSE can be explained with a race model despite violations of Miller’s bound, but also allows to systematically study deviances from the benchmark using the model as a tool in a comparative approach ( Innes & Otto, 2019 ). In the aggregate, we are convinced that both approaches, systems factorial technology and explicit modelling using the race architecture, will be very helpful to understand the processing of multisensory signals when tested with the redundant signals paradigm.

To advance multisensory modelling, we think that huge progress can be made by comparing different accounts of the RSE. As a first step in this endeavour, it is important to be clear about definitions. For example in the process of model development, it is crucial to decide about the cognitive architecture that is involved when making multisensory decisions ( Otto & Mamassian, 2017 ). Is multisensory evidence pooled in a single decision unit, or is it a race between two parallel decision units coupled by an OR gate? Unfortunately, definitions are not always clear with respect to this partition of the model space. For example, violations of Miller’s inequality are considered as evidence for coactivation ( Miller, 1982 ). In the following decades, the term “coactivation” has been used rather consistently, at least with some connotation, to describe models with a single decision unit (e.g.,  Blurton et al., 2014; Colonius & Diederich, 2017; Diederich, 1994; Miller, 1982; Mordkoff & Yantis, 1991; Schwarz & Ischebeck, 1994; Townsend & Wenger, 2004; Ulrich & Giray, 1986; Yang et al., 2018; Zehetleitner et al., 2009 ). Only recently,  Miller (2016) , who originally coined the term in 1982, clarified that the term should not be used to describe basic model architecture, which is the reason why we use the term “pooling” to explicitly describe models with a single decision unit. With clarified definitions at hand, as a next key step, it is of course of major interest to directly compare pooling and racing models and to trigger in multisensory research a true competition: Which model explains the data better?
