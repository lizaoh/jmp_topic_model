A commonly held belief amongst economists is that humans are, or should be, risk averse. Introspection reveals this to be quite plausible in many situations, although risk-seeking behaviour has often been observed in experimental studies of human choice. In Expected Utility Theory (EUT) ( von Neumann & Morgenstern, 1947 ) risk aversion is a necessary conclusion if people face diminishing marginal utility, an assumption for which there is strong intuitive appeal. This has enhanced the appeal of risk aversion as the default assumption of human behaviour. Whether it is reasonable to associate human risk attitude entirely with the propensity to appreciate each unit of consumption by slightly less as the total amount increases, has been questioned in a number of papers over the last decades (see  Davies, 2006 ;  Dyer & Sarin, 1982 ;  Wakker, 1994 ;  Yaari, 1987 ), but in most economic uses the degree of risk aversion continues to be identified solely with the curvature of the value function. Surely an attitude to risk involves more than diminishing marginal returns?

In recent decades, the simple framework of EUT has been found to provide a poor description of actual behaviour in risky situations and has undergone a number of revisions that attempt to remedy this descriptive failure. Amongst these remedies, which have not yet been completely successful in the quest for descriptive adequacy, have been the introduction of utility seen in terms of changes from a reference point rather than in terms of absolute wealth levels, and the related notion of Loss Aversion ( Kahneman & Tversky, 1979 ). These changes reflect greater psychological sophistication in the way in which utilities are deemed to be attached to monetary outcomes. With the addition of a reference point, the curvature of the value function no longer represents diminishing marginal returns. However, the most commonly observed pattern of concavity for gains and convexity for losses may be interpreted psychologically as diminishing sensitivity to values away from the reference point. A further significant shift was the recognition that individuals may distort objective or subjective probabilities when making decisions due to their attention being disproportionately directed to the outcomes in the tails of the distribution of outcomes. This resulted in the suite of Rank-Dependent Utility (RDU) models, of which the first was described by  Quiggin (1982) . These elements are combined in the framework of Cumulative Prospect Theory (CPT) ( Tversky & Kahneman, 1992 ).

These more sophisticated and empirically accurate models of human choice break the strict link between risk attitude and diminishing marginal returns. Diminishing sensitivity from a reference point, loss aversion, and non-linear probability distortions all influence overall risk attitude, but the precise degree is determined by a complex interaction of a number of independent sources. This paper provides a theoretical analysis of these components of risk attitude and their interaction.

Whilst the literature is full of empirical tests of CPT, as well as applications, there are very few theoretical investigations. However, there does exist some previous literature investigating risk attitude from sources other than utility curvature.  Yaari's (1987)  dual theory of choice has a linear value function and hence risk neutrality from this source, but permits risk attitude due to decision weights. Yaari shows convexity of the decision weighting function in this framework to be necessary and sufficient for aversion to mean-preserving increases in risk. Risk attitudes due to decision weighting, but with linear utility have also been investigated in  Chateauneuf (1988) ,  Roell (1987) , and  Wakker (1990) . Like traditional approaches based on marginal utility, all these analyses only permit a single source of risk aversion.

Turning to analyses that do not restrict either utility or decision weights to be linear,  Chew, Karni, and Safra (1987)  use aversion to mean-preserving increases in risk to characterise the concavity of utility and convexity of decision weights jointly.  Chateauneuf and Cohen (1994)  also examine utility and decision weights together, but use a weaker definition of risk aversion, i.e., that a lottery is not preferred to its Expected Value (EV). They find that neither concave utility nor convexity of the probability transformation is implied in this case.  Wakker (1994)  separates the effects of risk attitude into that due to riskless marginal utility, and that caused by probabilistic risk aversion from RDU decision weights. He shows that the value function is concave (convex) if and only if preferences show decreasing (increasing) marginal utility. Independently of marginal utility, a convex decision weighting function implies probabilistic risk aversion such that a decision maker will give less value to a probability mixture of two distributions than to the midpoint of the RDU valuations of these distributions.

These analyses have included the effects of decision weights, but not that of the reference point, which is necessary for differential attitudes to gains and losses and loss aversion.  Schmidt and Zank (2004)  allow for decision weights, value function curvature and the reference point, but rather than examine the effects of these on risk attitude, they study the conditions required to ensure that a version of loss aversion, which they term strong loss aversion, is satisfied.  Baucells and Heukamp (2004)  go beyond RDU and define extended notions of stochastic dominance which characterise which distributions will be dominated for an individual who faces all three sources of risk attitude in CPT. In addition to including loss aversion, and unlike previous analyses, they allow both the value and decision weighting functions to have both convex and concave segments, which the weight of evidence suggests is the most common case. Thus, Baucells and Heukamp's analysis is similar in spirit to this paper in that they examine risk aversion whilst allowing all components of CPT to vary independently. However, they do not explicitly examine how the combination of these effects influences the overall risk attitude of the decision maker.

In this paper we examine the risk premium of a gamble, traditionally defined as the amount by which the EV of the gamble exceeds the certainty equivalent value, as a useful way of characterising the degree of risk aversion. The risk premium is the certain amount an individual would forego in order to avoid the risk inherent in the gamble. In EUT, the risk premium has a particularly simple approximation.  Arrow (1965)  and  Pratt (1964)  showed that, approximately, the risk premium depends only on the variance of the lottery, and on the “coefficient of absolute risk aversion” which reflects the shape of the decision maker's value function. More fundamentally, the risk premium is always positive if and only if the value function is concave.

The effect of the probability weighting component of CPT on the risk premium has already been partially investigated by  Levy and Levy (2002) , although their application of CPT is flawed in places. Building on  Hilton (1988)  who considers the effect of RDU decision weights on risk premia, this paper adds to the existing literature a new theoretical analysis of risk premia in CPT. This analysis examines how total risk attitude is affected by each of the behavioural components in the full framework of CPT when they operate simultaneously and crucially, interact with each other.

In  Section 2 , using CPT we provide general solutions for two risk premia. The  standard risk premium  reveals the true amount of risk attitude relative to the objective EV, if the distribution is objectively given as in EUT, or the risk attitude relative to beliefs for Subjective Expected Utility ( Savage, 1954 ). The  behavioural risk premium  indicates the risk attitude that would be revealed by the decision maker's behaviour, where the EV against which the subject's evaluation is measured includes distortions of probability. The difference between these two concepts we term the  attitudinal premium .

In  Section 3  we follow  Pratt's (1964)  methodology for obtaining approximate local solutions for the risk premium. We analyse first, in  Section 3.1 , the situation in which there is no re-weighting of probabilities, and discuss the effect on this premium of loss aversion and the shape of the value functions. The first, curvature, component of the solution is analogous to the traditional Pratt–Arrow condition of concavity of the value function, but in our more complex behavioural model involves the curvature of gains and losses separately. The second component governs the first order effect of loss aversion on the risk premium. The Pratt–Arrow risk premium is shown to be a special case of the CPT risk premium. For completeness we provide a characterisation for local risk aversion away from the reference point, and show that this collapses to the standard Pratt–Arrow risk premium.

In  Section 3.2 , we introduce the full CPT model with decision weights at the reference point. We provide approximate results for both the standard and behavioural risk premia. The introduction of probability distortions changes the two components mentioned above such that they are reliant on the shape of the decision weighting function at the reference point. We provide conditions required to guarantee risk aversion in the full CPT model, and show how these conditions are altered by the asymmetry of the underlying distributions. In  Section 3.3  we provide solutions for the risk premium away from the reference point.

Section 4  provides an example illustrating the role of the components of the risk premium in a financial context, where we make specific assumptions regarding the parametric forms of the value and probability weighting functions.  Section 5  concludes.

                         Köbberling and Wakker motivate their index of loss aversion by pointing out that this measure allows us to define loss aversion independently of the unit of payment. This is not true of the most common version of CPT ( Tversky & Kahneman, 1992 ). Both these indices allow loss aversion to be neatly separated from the attribution of basic utility to gains and losses. In particular Köbberling and Wakker show that these measures allow us to characterise individuals with identical preferences for all gains-only and loss-only prospects whilst differing in their preferences for mixed prospects. However, in the class of measures that have this property, only the ratio of slopes at the origin is independent of the unit chosen for payment. Our analysis will provide another reason for this measure: the ratio arises naturally in the approximate solutions for the risk premium, and choosing this as our definition of loss aversion enables us to simply and elegantly express the effects of loss aversion on the risk premium.

We have shown that the standard characterisation of risk attitude as being driven by the curvature of the value function glosses over many nuances of the potential behavioural origins of risk aversion. When replacing EUT with a more behaviourally sophisticated model of decision making, we see that risk attitude may arise in complex ways from the interaction of a number of distinct psychological or psychophysical reactions. Whilst utility curvature still plays an important role in risk attitude, it becomes important to distinguish between the gain and loss domains when considering the effect of curvature. The most common empirical result of concave reactions to gains and convex reactions to losses (which may be theoretically defended by an appeal to diminishing sensitivity) means that curvature for gains and for losses have quite distinct, and opposite, roles in determining overall risk aversion. In addition, risk attitude is affected by two other behavioural components: loss aversion, and probability weighting. Both these concepts have effects on the risk premium that are distinct from the traditional effect of utility curvature.

This paper has provided both general and approximate local solutions for the risk premium that show the effect of each behavioural component. We have provided the conditions on the behavioural components that are required to ensure local risk aversion. These conditions are the behavioural analogues of the traditional Pratt–Arrow condition of concave utility. The more sophisticated behavioural models allow us to decompose risk measurement more meaningfully than has hitherto been possible, and also to be more precise in our analysis of the effects of a given change in behaviour.
