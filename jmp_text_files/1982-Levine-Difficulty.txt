INTRODUCTION    use of periodic functions, called the "periodic procedure," was introduced in an earlier article (Levine, 1975) to make  practical the use of certain physical measurement ideas in psychology. This paper reports a successful attempt to apply the  periodic procedure to an important psychological measurement problem, the measurement of aptitude test item difficulties.  ficulties of items on a subtest of the College Board's Scholastic Aptitude Test were measured with both the periodic procedure  and with LOGIST, a welldeveloped alternative procedure. A close agreement was found between the two methods. This close  agreement is particularly striking because different samples (from the same test administration) were used by the two methods.  eement provides support in favor of both methods. On the one hand, since the periodic procedure is distribution free, the  close agreement shows that the stronger assumptions underlying LOGIST are not a source of error in connection with its  application to SAT (see also Lord, 1970). On the other hand, the close agreement indicates that the weaker assumptions  supporting the periodic procedure are strong enough to determine SAT item difficulties, and that the new method is reasonably  safe against the unanticipated difficulties that often beset new methods.  iodic procedure exemplifies a systematic approach to measurement, characterized by a use of general functional equations and  group theoretical methods in place of specific distribution assumptions (Levine, 1970, 1972, 1973, 1975). A great deal of work  remains to be done before the new methods can be regarded as fully comparable to the better-developed, widely used  "optimization methods," i.e., to the methods which, like LOGIST, work by optimizing an index of goodness of fit for a model  making a specific distribution assumption.
