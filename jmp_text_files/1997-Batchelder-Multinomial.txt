A general processing tree (GPT) model family (e.g., X. Hu H W. H. Batchelder, 1994, Psychometrika 59, 21�47) is developed for measuring latent cognitive processes that may occur when subjects classify factorially defined stimuli. The GPT family for a two-factor, four-category design was shown to generalize Massaro and colleagues' fuzzy logic model of perception (FLMP) for this design. In fact, members of the family nest the GPT version of FLMP. The model family is based on the idea that the manifest categorical response depends on the joint outcomes of two dichotomous, latent processes. Closed-form expressions for maximum likelihood parameter estimates as well as their asymptotic variances are provided for all but one member of the model family. Members of the GPT family relax FLMP's processing assumptions in various ways, so these models can provide insight into the nature of processing in cases where FLMP is found to fail. Members of the model family were fitted to data from a speech perception experiment, and it was found that FLMP failed to fit the data, but two other models in the family provided an excellent account of the data. Finally, it is shown how to generalize the approach to different numbers of factors and categories. ] 1997 Academic Press 

INTRODUCTION 

Factorial categorization experiments are employed widely in several domains of experimental psychology, including speech perception, pattern recognition, decision making, and social perception, e.g., Anderson (1990), Ashby (1992), Cohen and Massaro (1992), and Massaro (1987). In a typical experiment, each factor corresponds to a physical dimension of the stimulus, and it has several ordered levels. Stimuli are constructed by joining all possible combinations of factor levels, one from each factor, and the experimeter presents each stimulus, possibly several times, to the subject for classification into exactly one of several available mutually exclusive categories. example, in a speech perception experiment involving one visual and one acoustic factor, the factors might be ``degree of lip closure'' and ``degree to which second and third formant transitions are rising,'' and the subject's task would be to classify each stimulus as �ba� or �da�, as in Massaro and Cohen (1983). In another example, oval letter-like stimuli might vary on obliqueness of a straight line and the closeness of a gap in the oval, and the subject's task would be to classify each stimulus as a ``G'' or a ``Q,'' as in Massaro and Hary (1986). In such experiments, the researcher might be interested in whether or not each factor contributes independently in the subject's classification decision, and in determining the psychophysical relationship between the levels of each factor and the subject's response. One approach is to work with an appropriate family of mathematical models that postulate parameters that reflect the cognitive processes underlying a subject's behavior in the classification task. 

Currently, there are many mathematical approaches to modeling factorial categorization experiments, and several are described in the chapters of Ashby (1992). In particular, Cohen and Massaro (1992) describe a Bayesian probability model, a Gaussian multidimensional model, a signal detection model, and a feedforward connectionist model, and they relate each model to the fuzzy logic model of perception, or FLMP (e.g., Massaro, 1987, 1989; Oden 6 Massaro, 1978; Massaro 6 Friedman, 1990). 

The purpose of this article is to describe a new family of factorial categorization models that are instances of the general processing tree (GPT) model family described in Batchelder and Riefer (1986, 1990), Riefer and Batchelder (1988), and Hu and Batchelder (1994). In particular, we focus on FLMP for a two-factor, four-category paradigm, and we show that the probabilistic structure of FLMP for this design is a GPT model. Moreover, when FLMP is viewed in this way, many other GPT models that are generalizations of the FLMP structure can be developed. These models can capture possible information integration

The Two-Factor, Four-Category FLMP 

In Crowther, Batchelder, and Hu (1995), we discussed and analyzed FLMP from a measurement-theoretic perspective. While we expressed concerns with Massaro and colleagues' interpretation of FLMP parameters as fuzzy truth values, we acknowledged the established success of FLMP in fitting data. In particular, we showed that FLMP for a two-factor, two-response paradigm could be reparameterized into a simple logit model with no interaction effect (Crowther et al., 1995, Eq. (27)) and, in turn, that it was equivalent in functional form to the well-known Rasch (1960) item-response model of test theory. 

Further, we discussed FLMP for the two-factor, fourcategory paradigm and showed that it could be formulated as a simple GPT model (Crowther et al. 1995). The stimuli, then, are the I_J members of the Cartesian product, A_B, with members Sij=(Ai , Bj), 1�i�I, 1� j�J.

GENERALIZATIONS OF FLMP 

One interpretation of the GPT formulations of Fig. 1 is that the response to a stimulus (Ai , Bj) depends on the outcome of two independent, dichotomous latent processes, one controlled exclusively by factor A and one controlled by factor B, which we denote as the A-process and the B- process, respectively. Of course, this interpretation does not imply that subjects are aware of, and able to report, the outcome of these latent processes. Figure 2 represents two ways of writing FLMP as a GPT model, where the latent processes are represented. Unlike Massaro and colleagues' interpretation of FLMP parameters, the parameters in these formulations are interpreted as the probabilities of the outcomes of the latent processes. Let :1 and :2 denote the outcomes of the A-process, and ;1 and ;2 denote the outcomes of the B-process. Then the trees in Fig. 2 imply that response T1 occurs if the latent process outcomes are (:1 , ;1), T2 if (:1 , ;2), T3 if (:2 , ;1), and T4 if (:2 , ;2). We postpone giving an explicit interpretation of the latent processes until our example; however, in terms of Massaro (1987), one could interpret them as indicating which of two binary features is matched to the stimulus. In the Conclusion, we discuss the differences between these interpretations of FLMP. Clearly, our interpretation differs from Massaro and colleagues' interpretation of FLMP at the latent process level and in terms of the parameters. Since both tree models in Fig. 2 lead to statistically equivalent models, we see that for FLMP the order of these latent processes does not affect the response probabilities. 

It is possible to construct other, more general models in GPT form that postulate that the response to a stimulus depends on two dichotomous latent processes. FLMP can be generalized in two main directions: (1) let the probability of one of the latent processes depend on the outcome of the other latent process; and (2) retain process independence but allow the probabilities of the latent processes to depend on both components of the stimulus. Other generalizations include combining these properties in various ways. The next two subsections develop these models.

AN EXAMPLE 

To demonstrate the GPT approach for modeling factorial categorization data, we employ data gathered in a two-factor, four-category speech perception experiment reported in 

Crowther (1993). The vowel �a� (as in ``pot'') typically is longer than �7� (as in ``putt'') (e.g., House, 1961), and the offset frequency of the first formant of the vowel (F1 offset) tends to be lower when preceding voiced stop consonants (�g�) than voiceless stops (�k�) (e.g., Summers, 1987). Vowel duration (e.g., Raphael, 1972) and F1 offset frequency (Fischer 6 Ohde, 1990; Wolf, 1978) are potent voicing cues in perception, and although studied less extensively, vowel duration is an effective cue to perception of vowel quality (Ainsworth, 1972; Crowther, 1993). In this experiment, stimuli varying in vowel duration (nine levels) and F1 offset frequency (three levels), were generated with a speech synthesizer (Klatt, 1980) and combined factorially to produce stimuli that ranged perceptually over the syllables [b 7 k] (``buck''), [bak] (``bock,'' the typical American English pronunciation of the name of composer J. S. Bach), [b 7 g] (``bug''), and [bag] (``bog''). Vowel duration, including formant transitions, ranged from 100 to 260 ms, in 20 ms steps. The F1 offset frequency levels were 300 (low), 450 (medium), or 575 Hz (high). A subject categorized each of the 27 stimuli (nine levels of vowel duration crossed with three levels of F1 offset frequency) as [b 7 k], [bak], [b 7 g], or [bag] 30 times. (Additional information regarding stimulus construction and the rationale of the experiment can be found in Crowther, 1993.) The raw data are plotted in Fig. 7. Each panel shows the number of trials on which the subject identified each stimulus as a token of [b 7 k] (upper left), [bak] (upper right), [b 7 g] (lower left), or [bag] (lower right). Vowel duration is plotted on the horizontal axis, and the curve parameter represents the level of the F1 offset frequency factor (low, medium, or high). It should be noted that in a number of cases only two or three of the response categories had appreciable counts for any given stimulus.

We offer the following substantive interpretation of the models. M1 , FLMP, assumes complete independence, i.e., that the vowel (A) and voicing (B) processes are independent (process independence) and that the vowel and voicing processes are a function only of the levels of the vowel duration factor and of the F1 offset factor, respectively (context independence). Model M2a assumes context independence, but process dependence, in that the probability of the voicing process is assumed to depend on the outcome of the vowel process. Likewise, M2b assumes context independence, but the opposite processing contingency of M2a . M3 assumes process independence, but symmetric context dependence (i.e., the A- and B-processes are sensitive to the levels of both factors). M4a assumes process independence, but asymmetric context dependence (the A-process is sensitive only to the levels of the vowel duration factor, but the B-process is sensitive to the levels of both factors). M4b also assumes process independence, but assumes the opposite context dependency of M4a (the A-process is sensitive to the levels of both factors and the B-process is sensitive only to

the levels of the F1 offset factor). Finally, M5 , like M3 , assumes process independence and context dependence, M5 unpacks and quantifies the effect of the factors in each decision, and assumes that both factors contribute independently to each decision. 

As is clear from Table 2, models M1 (FLMP), M2a , M2b , M4a , and M4b provided poor fits to the data, while M3 and M5 fit well. From the perspective of M3 , this suggests that the vowel and voicing processes were independent (i.e., process independence held), but that each process was sensitive to the levels of both factors (i.e., context independence failed).

From a statistical standpoint, it is important to note that M3 could have fit the data even if the functions were not so orderly. The orderliness is reflective of the fact that the levels of both factors are physically ordered. 

The next model, M5 , is intended to capture explicitly the orderliness apparent in the plot of M3's parameters. In particular, M5 may disclose the psychophysical relationship between the two acoustic factors and each latent process and whether the acoustic factors contributed independently in each process. 

Even though M5 is not identifiable, the nature of the nonidentifiability is such that we can make meaningful comparisons of relative parameter values. Hence M5's parameters are potentially useful for measuring the psychophysical relationship between the levels of both acoustic factors and each latent process. To draw any firm conclusions about the speech perception modeling results, it would be necessary to consider data and model-fitting results for more that one subject. However, this example demonstrates what can be gained by using this GPT family to model factorial categorization data. For example, had we been equipped only with FLMP (M1), and having found that M1 failed, we would have learned only that processing and�or context independence failed. By fitting other models in the GPT family, we were able to discover that, in fact, processing independence held, but that context independence failed. Further, having found that context independence failed, the good fit of M5 suggested that the two factors contributed independently to each decision. We were able to interpret M5's parameters as describing the psychophysical relationship between the response categories and the experimental factors, and also in terms of speech production data. Finally, each GPT model discussed has a coherent and reasonable (although not necessarily correct) interpretation as a component within a substantive model for speech perception. 

CONCLUSIONS 

In this paper, we have considered the two-factor, fourresponse paradigm in detail. We showed that the statistical structure of FLMP for this paradigm is easily written in the GPT model framework of Hu and Batchelder (1994). There are two reasons why this reformulation of FLMP is useful. First, it allows users of FLMP to utilize the statistical results of the GPT framework. In particular, software described in Hu and Batchelder (1994) can be used to conduct statistical inference for all GPT models. In fact, FLMP is sufficiently simple that closed-form expressions for MLEs and their asymptotic variances are obtained and presented in Table I. 

A second advantage of considering FLMP as a member of the GPT family is that it suggests generalizations that may account for data in cases where FLMP is found to fail. In a similar spirit, some efforts to generalize FLMP are also seen in Oden and Massaro (1978) and Massaro and Oden (1980). We constructed a new GPT model family with FLMP as a special case, and in an example showed how it could shed insight into speech perception data that were not accounted for by FLMP. 

Fortunately, the approach in this paper is not restricted to the two-factor, four-category paradigm. Considering just two factors, there are natural versions of our approach that involve two and three response categories. The key is to retain the idea that there are two dichotomous latent processes.
