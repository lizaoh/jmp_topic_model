As one of the originators of Mathematical Psychology, William K. Estes had a keen interest in the careers of scientists who worked in this field. For many in mathematical psychology, Bill was an intellectual father figure or grandfather figure. He read and commented on our papers. He faithfully listened to our conference presentations. He encouraged us to keep asking good questions. Also for 20 years, Bill was a close neighbor to the first author of this paper; Harvard University and Tufts University are about three miles apart. During that period there were numerous visits to share ideas. This paper is a belated outgrowth from several conversations with Bill about how hazard functions are a powerful alternative framework for understanding stochastic processes and from that perspective there were some new insights that can be gleaned about some classic problems. In this paper, hazard functions are used to reexamine Markovian processes for learning and memory.

The utilization of stochastic models to study learning and memory should be ranked very high among the many technical and conceptual contributions of William K. Estes. Stimulus sampling theory (SST) was one of the earliest models in this area ( Atkinson & Estes, 1963 ;  Estes, 1959a,b ;  Estes & Burke, 1953 ; and  Neimark & Estes, 1967 ). In the  Estes and Suppes (1974)  paper the axiomatic foundations for SST were delineated. Some of those axioms made explicit the properties that link SST to finite state Markov chain models, i.e., “ 
                         … 
                       we turn to the proof of what is probably the most important general theorem of stimulus sampling theory, namely, that under very broad conditions an appropriately chosen sequence of events is a Markov chain” ( Estes & Suppes, 1974 , p. 174.). Of course general Markov processes and Markov chains were already well established topics in probability theory prior to their use in psychology. The topic of Markov processes was developed in the first decade of the 20th century, and Markov models were prominently featured in the  Feller (1950)  textbook that had a strong influence on the early work in mathematical psychology. Markov models remain a fundamental technique in the conceptual toolbox of mathematical psychologists ( Brainerd, 1985 ). Markov models are discussed in virtually all the mathematical psychology textbooks (viz.,  Busemeyer & Diederich, 2010; Coombs, Dawes, & Tversky, 1970; Laming, 1973; Levine & Burke, 1972; Restle & Greeno, 1970; Roberts, 1976; Wickens, 1982 ).

                         There are other classic books of mathematical psychology that discuss random walk models, which are a form of a Markov chain (e.g.,   Luce, 1986; Townsend & Ashby, 1983 ).

The concept of hazard functions had a later introduction in the mathematical and psychological literatures. Hazard functions were originally developed in actuarial statistics ( Steffensen, 1930 ), but it took some time before hazard functions were discussed in general probability theory. For example, hazard functions were not discussed in either volume of the classic textbooks on probability theory by  Feller (1950, 1966) . Consequently, there was some delay before hazard functions were used in psychology.  Townsend and Ashby (1978)  first used hazard functions in psychology, but their application was in the context of information processing models for response time. Although the interest in hazard functions grew in the response time literature, the use of hazard function for the study of learning and memory developed slowly ( Chechile, 1987, 2006 ). Consequently, hazard functions were not well known tools at the time of the development of SST. There are now many established facts about hazard functions ( Chechile, 2003, 2006 :  Luce, 1986 ; and  Townsend & Ashby, 1983 ). Hazard functions also have a connection to Markov models, and this connection is explored in the present paper. In general Markov chain models impose some constraints on the hazard function, and conversely some hazard functions are not consistent with a Markov chain model.

In the next six sections of this paper, learning and memory will be reexamined in a way that links the two different mathematical perspectives, i.e. Markovian processes and hazard functions. In Section   2  the basic framework for Markov chains and general Markov models is developed with a particular focus on applications for theories of learning and memory retention. In Section   3  a framework is provided for reexamining learning data in terms of a hazard function perspective. Also in that section, a general form of a Markovian process is reexpressed in the form of hazard functions. In learning applications, hazard is associated with the conditional likelihood of establishing a new association in memory on a particular training trial. However, time and subsequent events can damage previously established memories. Hazard in the context of forgetting deals with the conditional likelihood of a memory loss at a particular point in time. The topic of hazard and memory retention is explored in Section   4 . A case is made, based on a theoretical and empirical utilization of a surrogate function, that the hazard associated with memory loss has a peaked shape. A surrogate function is a function that can be used to extract information about the empirical properties of the hazard function without directly fitting a hazard function. In Section   5  the  Chechile (2006)  two trace hazard (TTH) model is advanced as the appropriate description of the hazard changes caused by interference in the retention interval. This theory is contrasted with other memory models, including the  Estes (1997)  dual-trace model. Although Estes did not advance a Markov model for general memory loss, a Markovian model for memory loss can be nonetheless formulated. In Section   5  a Markovian version of the TTH model is developed, and in Section   6  a Markovian model is developed for the recent Implicit–Explicit Separation (IES) model by  Chechile et al. (2012) . The IES model is a multinomial processing tree (MPT) model that enables the estimation of four basic states for representing the knowledge that the subject has in memory associated with a target event. The four types of information storage are explicit, implicit, fractional, and non-storage. The Markovian framework for the IES model leads to a new interpretation of the relationship between explicit and implicit memory. Finally in Section   7  some concluding observations are discussed.

There are four subsections contained in this section. The first subsection defines key Markovian concepts and illustrates these ideas with four specialized models for either learning or memory retention applications. These models will be discussed in later sections of the paper. The second subsection covers key ideas from the hazard function literature and develops a general discrete Weibull model for learning applications. The third subsection deals with linking a general Markovian learning model to hazard values. In a similar fashion, the fourth subsection links a general Markovian forgetting model to hazard functions.

For some applications, like that of a one-dimensional random walk, the states correspond to all the discrete outcomes between the two end points. However, for other applications, there might only be two states for the Markov process, with both states representing extremes. For example, in a memory retention application, one state could be a target storage state and another state could be for the loss of target information. In probability theory, stochastic models that have a cumulative probability less than 1 are called subprobability models ( Bertoin & LeJan, 1992; Graczyk, 1994; Haydn, Lacroix, & Vaienti, 2005 ). Subprobabilities are mathematical measures like probabilities, but they are measures that are not scaled so as to add to 1. Subprobability theory is similar to standard probability theory. There are a number of theorems from probability theory that do not depend on the sum of all the probabilities being 1. Those theorems are also true for subprobability theory. From a hazard function perspective, a subprobability model is a stochastic system where there is some nonzero probability that the critical event never occurs over the support interval.

The  Chechile (2006)  Two Trace Hazard (TTH) model for memory loss is an example of a subprobability model. In the TTH theory, each new memory encoding is assumed to have two traces created. The first trace has monotonically increasing hazard. The hazard for just this trace is based on a standard Weibull hazard model with a shape parameter  
                            c 
                            = 
                            2 
                         . The integrated hazard for the first trace diverges, so this trace always fails over the lifetime of the individual. The second trace has monotonically decreasing hazard, and it is based on a subprobability hazard model. The second trace hazard model is based on a Weibull distribution where the density is multiplied by a constant  
                            b 
                          with  
                            0 
                            < 
                            b 
                            < 
                            1

                            c 
                          has been taken to be positive and less than 1. The system of the two traces, where either trace is sufficient for memory retention, has a hazard that increases for a short time, then reaches a peak which is followed by decreasing hazard over the remainder of the support interval. Only  
                            b

Theorem 1  clarifies why Eq.  (13)  is a flexible learning model. Depending on the value of the  
                               c 
                             parameter, the same equation can capture either increasing hazard, constant hazard, or decreasing hazard. By estimating a single parameter, we have a method for representing the learning hazard.

It is well known that the estimation of hazard is challenging and is subject to sizable statistical bounce. See  Chechile (2003)  for a more detailed discussion of the problems with empirical estimates of hazard functions. One way to circumvent these problems is to assume a flexible function such as Eq.  (15)  that can be easily fitted to data to obtain an estimate of a single parameter (i.e. the  
                               c 
                             parameter) that telegraphs the hazard properties across all the trials. To illustrate the utility of this approach let us consider several sequences of hypothetical learning hazard values that are choppy and show how the estimation of the  
                               c 
                             parameter in the Weibull model can represent the general trend of the hazard data. For example, suppose that the discrete learning hazard values for the first five learning trials are respectively:.30,.29,.22,.38, and.19. Except for the fourth trial, the hazard is decreasing with trial number. The estimate of the Weibull model  
                               c 
                             parameter is.9516 for these data. Because the estimate of  
                               c 
                             is less than 1 for this hypothetical example, it implies a decreasing hazard profile despite the spike in hazard on trial 4. As another example, consider the sequence of hazard values of.30,.32,.37,.38, and.19 for five learning trials. This sequence mainly is an increasing hazard profile except for a dip on trial 5. For this example, the  
                               c 
                             parameter in the Weibull learning model is estimated as 1.0803 and is consistent with the conclusion of an increasing hazard sequence. The correlation between the Weibull model predicted learning rate and the learning rate implied by the above stated hazard values is.9857 for the first example and.9981 for the second example. These cases illustrate the flexibility of the Weibull model to capture discrete hazard sequences regardless if the data roughly exhibit either an increasing hazard pattern, decreasing hazard pattern, or constant hazard.

Most of the experiments in the vast literature on learning were not done in a fashion that resulted in storage probability estimates as a function of training trials. Conventional dependent variables, such as percentage correct recall or percentage correct recognition, are influenced by a number of underlying cognitive processes such as storage, retrieval, and guessing. This issue of the entanglement of cognitive processes on the available dependent variables is a general problem that has led some researchers to argue for the necessity of modeling the latent processes in order to separate the confounded dependent variables ( Chechile, 2007; Chechile & Roder, 1998; Riefer, Knapp, Batchelder, Bamber, & Manifold, 2002 ). These papers also advance the benefits of multinomial processing tree (MPT) modeling as a suitable solution to the entanglement problem. This cognitive psychometric approach yields estimates of the probability of information storage.

Another methodological point that needs to be discussed deals with the basis for examining the learning data. One of the points stressed by  Estes (1956)  pertained to the misleading aspect of group learning curves. Estes argued for the feasibility of an all-or-none learning process that could result in a smooth learning curve when the data are grouped over individuals. This point is clearly correct; consequently it is necessary to study the learning hazard function on an individual basis in order to avoid a grouping effect.

Empirical hazard estimates are challenging because the estimates have substantial statistical error that grows in the right tail of the probability distribution. See  Chechile (2003)  for a more detailed discussion of the difficulties with hazard estimation. However, in some cases the hazard properties of a function can be ascertained without actually estimating hazard on a trial-by-trial basis for each individual. For example, the

A total of 80 subjects were tested in the experiment. The subjects learned a set of 18 paired associate items. The stimulus member of each pair was a consonant–vowel–consonant trigram selected from the 11%–18% meaningfulness range in the  Archer (1960)  norm. The response member of each pair was a two-syllable noun selected from the  Kucera and Francis (1967)  norm. The subjects received six training trials on the set of 18 pair associates. Between the study phase and the test phase of a training trial, there was a 20 s period in which the subjects shadowed random digits. In the test phase of the trial, a random set of six pairs were probed with a recall test probe and the other 12 pairs were tested on an old/new recognition test. If “VAJ clothing” were a study pair, then the recall test probe would be “VAJ-recall”; whereas if this unit were given a recognition test, then “VAJ” was presented either with the correct association (clothing) or with a word associated with another pair on the list. Following the yes/no response, the subjects were asked to provide a three-point confidence rating. Subjects were instructed to use the “3” rating for a certain response, a “2” rating for an educated guess, and a “1” rating for a blind guess. On each test trial, the 18 associates were presented with a random intermixing of the three test trials types (recall, old recognition, and new recognition), but over the six test trials each association was tested equally with each test type. See  Chechile and Ehrensbeck (1983)  for additional information about this experiment.

The frequencies were obtained for common response categories for the three test trial types, i.e. separate multinomials for recall, old recognition, and new recognition. Recall data were organized to a binomial for correct versus incorrect recall. Old and new recognition data were separately organized into four-cell multinomials for the response categories of (1) a high confident “no” (2) a lower confident “no” (3) a lower confident “yes”, and (4) a high confident “yes”. Estes (1956)  warned that there could still be a grouping artifact when learning is studied on an individual basis if there were a grouping over test trials. “Throughout this discussion we have spoken in terms of mean curves obtained from groups of organisms. Similar problems arise, and similar considerations apply, however, in the case of a curve whose points represent means of repeated measures on the same organism”. ( Estes, 1956 , p. 139.) For the current experiment it is possible that some CVC-word pairings might be more difficult to encode for a given subject. The lists were designed to be a homogeneous set of associates. Yet it is still likely that there is some idiosyncratic variation across the items on the list in terms of the ease of learning. Also for list learning procedures, like the paired-associate task, there is variability in the retroactive and proactive interference effects that occur over the study-test interval. Each item is presented in a random serial position and is tested in a random test position, so each association has a unique interference history over the course of training. Thus the estimated

                            a

                            a

Memory hazard functions can be computed for each of the forgetting functions.  Chechile (2006)  derived the hazard function for these functions. Qualitatively the hazard functions are either monotonically decreasing, constant, or peaked. By peaked shape, it is meant that hazard increases for a short time, reaches a peak, and then is decreasing.

The qualitative property of the forgetting hazard function is a proven outcome, so a model can be rejected if it is established that the hazard is contrary to its predictive property. This method of model comparison should provide strong evidence against some of the proposals. For example, if it is established that the empirical memory hazard function is peaked, then nine of the proposed functions can be rejected as suitable models for forgetting. Conversely if it can be established that the forgetting hazard function is monotonically decreasing, then between three and five models shown in  Table 2  can be rejected.

Given peak shaped hazard, the first nine models listed in  Table 2  can be rejected on the grounds that those models fail to capture a key qualitative feature of the memory hazard function. Among the models rejected are some ad hoc functions that were advanced without a strong theoretical foundation, e.g., the Hyperbolic and the PM-Hyperbolic models are functions without a theoretical basis. Yet among the rejected models are functions based on major cognitive theories. For example, among the rejected models is the famous multiple-store model (MSM) ( Atkinson & Shiffrin, 1968 ) that has been called the modal model of memory in many cognition textbooks (e.g.,   Goldstein, 2008 ;  Robinson-Riegler & Robinson-Riegler, 2004 ). By modal model, the textbook authors mean it is the prevailing memory theory of most cognitive psychologists. In the multiple-store model, the memory loss does not come from items that are initially encoded in the long-term store because items in the long-term store are retained forever. In this model, storage loss can only come if an item is only encoded into the short-term store, and without rehearsal these items will ultimately be lost. The interplay of some initial permanent storage along with some weakly encoded items that were left in the short-term store results in a monotonically decreasing hazard ( Chechile, 2006 ). Other criteria can further narrow the set of five candidate functions that exhibit peak-shaped hazard. For example, there is no strong rationale for preferring the original AS-Power model to the PM-AS-Power model because there is evidence that some memories are not forgotten.  Bahrick (1984)  found above-chance memory retention of information taught in high school some 50 years later. For example, list learning procedures have longer delays between trace formation and memory test. Without data in the short time frame, the researchers would miss observing a key property of the memory hazard function. Moreover, experimental procedures that do not enable the estimation of a storage measure are also uninformative. Behavioral dependent variables, such as percentage correct recall or percentage correct recognition, are sensitive to processes other than just the storage probability. However, the experiments reported above used test protocols that enabled the estimation of the storage probability via an MPT model. Storage did change substantially with retention interval, and the hazard function for storage losses showed a brief period of increasing hazard that was followed by a period of decreasing hazard.

System redundancy is a key notion in reliability engineering. In order for the memory of an event to be lost, both Trace 1 and Trace 2 must be lost. One of the consequences of having a Trace 1 representation, despite its short effective lifetime, is that it creates redundancy in the early time period after memory encoding. This redundancy extends the length of the memory span substantially.  Chechile (2006)  noted that without Trace 1, the memory span would not be in the typical range of 6±2 but would be only about 3.

Because Trace 1 plays an important role for increasing the reliability of a new encoding and it extends the duration of the immediate memory span, this trace should be advantageous in facilitating learning. Humans are quite remarkable in their ability to acquire new information. Consequently, it might be possible that Trace 1 is a capability particular to human or perhaps only some animals have a dual trace representation for encoding new information.

                          It is technically challenging to conduct the type of research to determine the memory hazard for nonhuman species. The test protocols would need to span many retention intervals with a task that enabled a model for obtaining a measure for storage. Although the TTH model has only been tested with human data, it should be pointed out that this is also true for all the other functions listed in  Table 2  because the animal memory studies lag behind the human research in terms of cognitive psychometric methods.

It should be stressed that Trace 1 is not a perceptual persistence trace, such as an iconic image ( Averbach & Coriell, 1961; Spencer & Shuntich, 1970; Sperling, 1960 ). The iconic image only lasts about 200 ms and can be eliminated altogether by a visual mask ( Spencer & Shuntich, 1970 ). The experiments reported above did not use a visual target, so the iconic image is not pertinent. But there is also evidence of a persistence of auditory targets, i.e. the echoic trace ( Efron, 1970; Massaro, 1970 ). Without auditory masking the echoic trace lasts about 250 ms ( Massaro, 1975 ). However, with the experiments reported previously with the Brown–Peterson paradigm, there was no delay between target presentation and the beginning of interpolated shadowing procedure. This procedure thus masks the auditory target.

The TTH model might seem to be similar to the classic multiple-store model (MSM) of  Atkinson and Shiffrin (1968) , but the models differ in a number of important ways. Despite the existence of multiple stores, there are not simultaneous representations for all items in both the short-term store and the long-term store with the MSM. Items in the long-term store are permanently stored.

Estes (1997)  also discussed memory retention in terms of a dual-trace framework. In the  Estes (1997)  model, sensory input leads directly to a “primary” trace that is the basis for a recognition response. The “secondary” trace comes from the subject’s reaction to the stimulus, and this trace is used for memory recall. The reaction to the stimulus would include elaborative encodings as well as the effects of reinforcement. Unlike the TTH model, the hazard properties of the two traces in the  Estes (1997)  dual-trace model are not specified, so it is difficult to directly compare the models.

All the candidate functions in  Table 2 , except the Exponential model, predict decreasing memory hazard for sufficiently long retention intervals. The question arises as to why there is a decreased risk of a storage loss as the memory ages? One possible answer to this question is memory consolidation theory ( Hebb, 1949 ). In the extreme form of the consolidation hypothesis, a new memory encoding is fragile like wet paint and needs time to harden into a permanent memory ( Bliss & Collingridge, 1993 ). In consolidation theory, the risk of storage loss is reduced because the trace becomes stronger with the passage of time. The consolidation hypothesis emerged from the neuroscience literature, but it has been also advocated as a cognitive model ( Wixted, 2005 ). However,  Chechile (2006)  pointed out that it is not necessary to postulate that the memory encoding is physically strengthened by the passage of time. All that is needed is to postulate that the interference is weaker towards old memories as opposed to recent memories. In the TTH model, both Trace 1 and Trace 2 are subject to possible loss because of the disrupting effect of new information that is being processed. But what memory traces does the new information disrupt?  Chechile (2006)  suggested that new information can cause a mild form of retrograde amnesia. With retrograde amnesia, it is the recent information that is most likely to be forgotten ( Ribot, 1881 ). Unless there is a special retrieval of the old memory, new information is most likely to disrupt other recent information. The study by  Misanin, Miller, and Lewis (1968)  is consistent with this interpretation and inconsistent with the original consolidation hypothesis. These investigators showed that electroconvulsive shock (ECS) did not damage a memory that was 24 h old, but it did disrupt new learning. However, if a well established and presumed “consolidated memory” was retrained prior to ECS, then it was damaged. That result should not happen to a truly hardened trace. Thus consolidation theorists hypothesized that additional training transformed a hardened trace back to a fragile trace ( Nader, Schafe, & Le Doux, 2000 ). This trace then needs to be reconsolidated. The reconsolidation hypothesis is paradoxical because it suggests that relearning is a trace weakening effect as opposed to a trace strengthening effect.

There is an alternative interpretation to the reconsolidation hypothesis. Instead of claiming that the memory trace is hardened or strengthened, it is sufficient to argue that the temporal context is important, and intervening interference is most vigorous towards prior memories that are in the current temporal context. As the memory trace survives to an older age, there is less overlap between the temporal context of the trace and the current temporal context. Consequently, the risk of a storage disruption is reduced for an older surviving trace. If the experimenter reintroduces information after a shift in the temporal context, then the old memory can be further strengthened, and it also resets the memory lag value because the time lag is measured from the last studied event. But the reintroduction of the item can also put the trace back into the crossfire of an ongoing storage interference process.

                               Brown and Lewandowsky (2010)  have advanced a more formally developed model that provides an alternative account to the consolidation hypothesis. With this model there is a similarity between items in memory, and time is a component of the similarity value.

From the perspective of the TTH model there is also an alternative interpretation of the role of sleep on memory. A number of researchers have discussed the augmentation of memory consolidation and reconsolidation from sleep. See the review of this literature by  Walker and Stickgold (2006) . These researchers have argued that sleep directly enhances the formation of the memory representation. However, in the TTH model, sleep should have two beneficial effects on memory. First, in the TTH model it is the number of interpolated events that occur in time rather than time itself that has a potentially disruptive effect on the memory representation. Sleep dissociates the variables of time and number of interpolated events. Without sleep the subject would encounter more events that can cause storage interference. Second, sleep causes a break in the temporal context. Such a break makes it less likely that the new interpolated events will dislodge an older memory.

Regardless of the theoretical interpretation of the declining risk to an older memory, the TTH model provides an accurate description of the changes in the conditional risk of a storage loss as a function of time. With this model, there is not a critical time when the risk of a memory loss goes to zero. According to this model, storage loss is not a constant, but it undergoes dynamic change.

In the Markovian forgetting model shown in Eq.  (25) , there are only two observable states, which correspond to sufficient storage and insufficient storage. However, it is possible to distinguish other states of knowledge. Chechile et al. (2012)  recently advanced an MPT model that separates four knowledge states for a memory of a target event. This MPT model is called the IES model where the name stands for the Implicit–Explicit Separation model. The motivation underlying the IES model is an important distinction in memory research between explicit and implicit memory. The concept of implicit memory emerged from clinical neurology because certain amnesic patients who exhibited dramatically impaired memory for recent events would, nonetheless, show priming effects based on earlier experiences ( Warrington & Weiskrantz, 1974 ). Explicit memory of an event is considered to be exemplified by the conscious recall or recognition of an earlier experience; whereas, implicit memory is thought to be an unconscious representation of the previous event that can influence guessing rates or lead to priming ( Roediger & McDermott, 1993; Schacter, 1987 ).

An explicit representation has all the information encoded such that the subject could reproduce the target entirely without error if the subject were to gain access to that representation. Nonetheless, if implicit memories were stored in a different brain region, then why does the rate of implicit memory increase in the early part of the retention interval?

The Markovian implementation in  Fig. 2  also reveals another interpretation for another effect that led some experimentalists to conclude that explicit and implicit memory are different systems. For the case examined in  Fig. 2 , the explicit trace has several advantages over the implicit representation. First, all traces start in the explicit state. Second, the explicit state has two traces via the TTH model; whereas there is only a single implicit trace. The hazard models for both the explicit memory (Trace 2) and the implicit representation are the same for the case in  Fig. 2 . The parameter coefficients are equal and both the explicit and implicit states are modeled with subprobability models that have the same ultimate asymptote. Nonetheless, note that for the longer  
                         n 
                       values in  Fig. 2 , there is a slower decline in the implicit probability in comparison to the explicit probability. This effect is caused by a number of factors. One factor is associated with the fact that the new implicit traces that are produced from the loss of an explicit trace have a greater temporal separation from the original encoding. Recall from the discussion of the TTH model, it was argued that the reduced hazard for long retention intervals is not due to a strengthening of the traces. Rather it is caused by the fact that storage interference is acting less vigorously on the old memories because of a shift in the temporal context. The new implicit memories are more removed from the original context of the target encoding, so there is reduced hazard for these newly created implicit traces. Moreover, the newly created implicit traces are also only a part of the full degradation of the explicit traces because some explicit traces degrade to either the fractional state or the non-storage state. Consequently, the slower rate of change of implicit storage relative to explicit storage occurs naturally in the Markovian model. A number of experimental studies have reported finding a slower rate of change for measures thought to be sensitive to implicit memory in comparison to the rate of loss for measures thought to be sensitive to explicit memory ( Heindel, Salmon, Shults, Walicke, & Butters, 1989; Light, La Voie, & Kennison, 1995; Nilsson, 2003 ). These investigators used this effect to argue that implicit memory and explicit memory are separate storage systems. But the finding of a different rate of loss is not sufficient for concluding that there is a separate storage system for explicit and implicit memory. The Markovian form of the IES model also produces a slower rate of loss for implicit traces.

With the SST framework William K. Estes showed that simple probability models provided a means for describing learning and memory processes. A Markov chain model is an elegant way to represent underlying processes in terms of a stationary transition matrix. Yet in this paper, the transition probabilities can be understood in terms of hazard. Moreover, the empirical study of hazard has generated strong evidence that the hazard functions are not a constant. In learning, changes in hazard can result if there is any variability in item difficulty. Any variation, regardless of how slight, in item difficulty for an individual is enough to cause the hazard to be altered from a constant. This result occurs because a mixture of constant hazard functions has monotonically decreasing hazard rather than constant hazard. In the case of memory retention, a strong argument is advanced that memory hazard also changes with the events in the retention interval and has a peaked shape. The  Chechile (2006)  TTH model provides a good description of the dynamic changes with retention interval. Consequently, a Markov chain is not suitable for a forgetting model. Nonetheless, a general Markovian model, where the trial number or the event number changes the transition probabilities, is a creditable model for both learning and memory retention. Also the four states of storage measured in the  Chechile et al. (2012)  IES model can be successfully modeled in terms of a Markovian model. The Markovian form of the IES model provides new insights about the qualitative differences between explicit and implicit memory.

Finally, prior to the work of William K. Estes and his colleagues in the 1950s, the topics of learning and memory were dominated by experimenters who did not use stochastic models as a tool for understanding underlying psychological processes. Although the early mathematical models have changed, as would be expected after more than 50 years of research, the idea of mathematical modeling of learning and memory processes has continued to advance and now occupies a strong position in this subfield of psychology. We are delighted to continue in the tradition initiated by Bill Estes of using mathematical tools to uncover the fundamental processes involved with learning and memory.
