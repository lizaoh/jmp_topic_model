Daily life confronts us with many choice situations in which three or more options are available. What should we do? Take the best? Eliminate all but one? There are numerous empirical studies and theoretical approaches on multi-alternative choice situations with very different settings and foci (e.g.,  Hensher et al., 2015; Louviere et al., 2004  for an overview of designs and models). The current paper is on best–worst scenarios only. In a best–worst task, a person when faced with three or more choice alternatives is required to identify the best  and  the worst choice option. This method received increasing theoretical and practical interest over the last decade because it has a number of advantages over the traditional choice tasks in which the decision maker is typically asked to choose only the best alternative ( Marley & Louviere, 2005  p. 464). It has been applied to choosing among consumer products, health services and treatments, perceptual stimuli, and more (e.g.,  Diederich et al., 2012; Hawkins et al., 2014a, 2014b; Soekhai et al., 2021 ). Note that, “best” and “worst” refer to any terms that define extremes on a subjective continuum, for instance, largest and smallest ( Louviere et al., 2015  p. 6).

The majority of theoretical best–worst approaches is based on random ranking and random utility, joint and sequential, and ratio scales ( Marley & Louviere, 2005  p. 465) and focusses on axiomatizations of best–worst tasks ( Louviere et al., 2015; Marley, 2020 ). There are hardly any models that take the dynamics of decision-making into account. An exception is the work by  Hawkins et al. (2014a, 2014b)  who extended the linear ballistic accumulator (LBA) model to best–worst choice data, called  parallel LBA model . The goal of this work is to introduce another dynamic model for the best–worst choice task as well as for ranking tasks that is based on a multi-dimensional diffusion process. The approach is a natural modification of the cube model that was previously studied in  Mallahi-Karai and Diederich (2019, 2021)  in the context of multi-alternative choice situations with choosing the best option only. Note the that primary goal here is to convey the basic concept and ideas.

The paper is organized as follows. Section  2  sets the notation and introduces the mathematical objects used in the remainder of the paper. In Section  3  we briefly describe the cube model and recall definitions of complete and partial rankings. In Section  4  we will give a detailed description of the cube model for ranking. Finally, in Section  6  we will discuss some problems related to the proposed model that will be studied in future work.

In this paper, we proposed two models for the best–worst choice task as well as for ranking tasks. Both models are based on the cube model, a model originally designed for multi-attribute choice alternatives. Different from most approaches for best–worst task, the current models are dynamic. They make predictions not only for choice probabilities, partial and complete rankings, but also for choice response times distributions — at least in principle. We conclude with some remarks and questions.

1. 
                            In Section  5  we outlined an approach for finding semi-closed formulas for the density of the stopping times associated with forming a complete ranking. The idea of independent races as shown there comes closest to the LBA model for best–worst tasks proposed by  Hawkins et al. (2014a, 2014b) . However, there are a number of profound differences between the two models. First, the races between the Best and Worst in our model are not independent. In the LBA model all races for Best and Worst are independent processes. That is, in the case of three alternatives, the LBA assumes 6 independent races. Second, the source of randomness in this model (the Wiener process) is quite different from the LBA model. Third, we (may) include  stochastic  dependencies between all “racers” which is not provided for in the parallel LBA model. It would be interesting to compare the distributions of stopping times and probabilities for our model and the LBA model. 
                         
                         
                            2. 
                            In Section  5  we dealt with independent noise processes (diagonal  
                                  Σ 
                               ). Can one also find a different mathematical approach to deal with dependent noise processes in our model? 
                         
                         
                            3. 
                            Can one find closed formulas (or approximate values) for first passage probabilities and first passage times for fitting the models to choice frequencies and choice response times for different rankings and Best–Worst choice and estimating parameters? If so, do the two models proposed in Section  4  make similar or different predictions?
