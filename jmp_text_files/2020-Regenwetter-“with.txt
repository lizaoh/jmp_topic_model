Oftentimes, scholars entertain theories according to which  the number of people in a target population, who will show a given symptom, behavior, or response, must increase (or decrease) with certain changes in an independent variable.  Unfortunately, rather than dictating suitable models and statistical methods, psychological theories are instead routinely subjugated to the analysis tools at the scholar’s disposal. General-purpose statistical methods will augment theories like the above with numerous extraneous assumptions and properties that are not inherently part of the theory itself. Not only will such a divergence from the genuine theory threaten to introduce bias and error into substantive conclusions, but it can also wreak havoc on replication.  Regenwetter and Robinson (2017, 2019)  and  Rotello et al. (2015) , and others have warned that various conceptual, theoretical, and methodological misspecifications can cause replication studies to perpetuate erroneous conclusions (see also  Hertwig & Pleskac, 2018  for a commentary). Furthermore, it is well-known that misspecified models can cause statistical rejections and/or cause replication failures, not because of a wrong core theoretical claim, but because of peripheral technical requirements that are only occasionally met (see also, e.g.,  Regenwetter & Davis-Stober, 2017  for a related tutorial review). Even under conditions of perfect model specification and perfect replicability,  Davis-Stober and Regenwetter (2019)  have warned that thinking of psychological theories as ‘predictions with exceptions’ leads down a logical rabbit hole, at the bottom of which everyone may become an exception to a given theory. This tutorial brings together these different lines of scholarship. It discusses a very general and powerful approach to theory formulation and theory building using a simple example. An illustrated case study shows how we can formulate a theory like the one above in mathematically succinct ways that solve the ‘paradox’ of converging evidence, according to which accumulating effects predicted by a theory may ‘paradoxically’ accumulate exceptions to the theory. The succinct theory formulations in this paper lend themselves to direct statistical analysis, rather than weighing the theory down with additional ballast from off-the-shelf statistical models and methods. The tutorial also builds on a framework developed recently by  Regenwetter and Cavagnaro (2019) . The general setting is an empirical paradigm that generates observed proportions of several binary variables, such as yes/no responses, presence/absence of a symptom, presence/absence of a certain behavior. These binary variables may measure the same construct for several different conditions, such as different discrete levels of an independent variable, different treatment groups, different time points, or different locations, or they may be binary variables measuring different constructs altogether. In our running example here, I focus on the case of a binary questionnaire response variable, for a single construct, measured at different discrete time points. The case study centers on a social-psychology paradigm, but can serve as a blue print for any number of research programs from almost any area of psychology.

Spielmann and Stern (2019)  were interested in perceptions of transgender individuals undergoing gender-affirming hormone replacement therapy (GAHT) and how shifts in their perceived gender typicality over time affect observer judgments of their sexual orientation. Specifically,  Spielmann and Stern (2019)  studied whether gender transition shapes perceptions of sexual orientation. They sought to evaluate whether and how the perception of sexual orientation is affected by the amount of time a person has spent in the gender transition process. I consider one of their theoretical expectations, namely that, as the appearance of a  target  person undergoing GAHT becomes more and more stereotypical of the desired gender, fewer and fewer people will eventually categorize the target as gay.  Spielmann and Stern (2019)  pre-registered their study and collected two separate samples, for each set of target images, on two different platforms, namely Amazon’s Mechanical Turk (henceforth mTurk) and Qualtrics Online Research Panel (henceforth Qualtrics).

Standardized images of three transgender individuals transitioning from female to male were obtained from the HRT Transgender Data Set ( Mahalingam & Ricanek, 2013; Mahalingam et al., 2014 ). The database contains images of transgender individuals from the day they started GAHT and every day thereafter for a year. Participants (mTurk, N  
                         = 
                       786; Qualtrics, N  
                         = 
                       916) were randomly assigned to view one picture of each of the three targets and were instructed to indicate the target’s perceived sexual orientation (binary choice between “gay” and “straight”). Participants knew neither that the targets were transgender individuals nor that they had been undergoing GAHT.

The mTurk and Qualtrics studies were nearly identical, but differed in minor ways. Here, I will employ them as a proxy for a replication. The biggest difference between the two studies was the  judgment type : The mTurk participants were asked to indicate whether the  average person  would say that the sexual orientation of the person in the photograph was gay or straight, whereas the Qualtrics participants were asked to indicate their own  personal  perception.  Spielmann and Stern (2019)  used a general linear model, whose independent variables were the months spent transitioning, the judgment type, as well as their interaction, and whose dependent variable was the perceived sexual orientation, which they specified as a binary logistic (coded as “gay”  
                         = 
                       0; “straight”  
                         =

General purpose parametric methods can introduce numerous extraneous assumptions and features in order to convert a verbal or conceptual theoretical claim into a formal testable hypothesis. For instance, some of the common assumptions inherent in popular methods include linear (or logistic) functions, normal distributions, and various independence properties, most of which tend to be extrinsic to the theory under consideration. The primary goal here is to avoid strong auxiliary assumptions, while also spelling out very specific and nuanced predictions that encapsulate several variations of the theory itself as genuinely and directly as possible. I later document that, besides being genuine to the theoretical claim, many of these predictions are also surprisingly parsimonious, hence easy to test (and potentially refute) with suitable empirical evidence. We will also walk through how to evaluate these predictions and how to carry out a competition among highly nuanced hypotheses. I first show how to translate the verbal theory into mathematically precise and parsimonious hypotheses. This crucial step of fully specifying one’s theory is the core purpose of this tutorial.

Two and half centuries of past research on the mathematics of consensus and voting methods teaches us that these types of aggregation ‘paradoxes’ could well be ubiquitous ( Arrow, 1951; Condorcet, 1785 ).

Davis-Stober and Regenwetter (2019)  recently warned against conceptualizing successful psychological theories as ‘predictions that hold by and large, with some exceptions’. They spelled out a ‘paradox’ of converging evidence, whereby it is a logical fallacy to believe that every additional study that shows an effect is adding further evidence in favor of a theoretical claim. Instead, every additional successful prediction further delineates the scope of the theory because exceptions to the theory have the opportunity to accumulate to the point where nobody satisfies the combination of predictions that a theory implies. Applied to our context,  Hypothesis  6, for example, gives 21 inequalities (not just the three of  Hypothesis  10), to which any given person could be an exception, even if, thanks for large samples, high quality data, and perfect replicability, one knew with high confidence that all 21 inequalities held at the population level. Simply put, the more predictions we derive, the more ways there are for individuals to violate at least some of those predictions, and become an exception to the theory. While it may be tempting to let everyone be an exception to some predictions made within a study, that research strategy torpedoes external validity: Short of developing a theory of exceptions, there is no telling how many real world situations would be exceptions, too.

One solution to this paradox is to state theories that pertain to  everyone  without exception ( Davis-Stober & Regenwetter, 2019 ).  Hypotheses  1–11 can be re-stated this way: For example, if  everyone  satisfies an individual level version of  Hypothesis  10, then, the response probability of a randomly selected respondent, because it is a convex combination of the individual response probabilities, will satisfy  Hypothesis  10 (but the reverse does not hold, as we have already established). Before I dig deeper into these nuances, I review the results for the other data sets.

The analysis for Target 2 yields different findings from those for Target 1. Here,  Hypotheses  1–4 are rejected by all three methods of analysis in both data sets. In the mTurk study, none of the hypotheses are worthwhile by Bayes factor criteria, but in the Qualtrics study,  Hypothesis  6 provides a good account by all three methods (albeit with the Bayes factor evaluating the supporting evidence merely as substantial, compared with an unconstrained model), and it offers the best account according to both DIC and the Bayes factor. Put in words, in a nutshell, it is difficult to derive a coherent story for Target 2 through the lens of these hypotheses with much confidence. In particular, whether we consider the mTurk and Qualtrix findings a successful replication depends quite strongly on the question we are interested in.

A pattern of findings like this can serve as input for developing more informative future studies and formulating more refined theoretical expectations. For example,  Spielmann and Stern (2019)  were also interested in how perceived gender typicality changes over time. While general-purpose models would impose a functional form on how gender typicality could function as a moderator, order-constrained hypotheses like the ones in this paper can be more nuanced: For example, one could predict that a sufficiently large increase in time and/or a sufficiently large increase in rated gender typicality implies a reduction in the response probability but one could avoid predicting how time and gender typicality trade-off with each other, say, when gender typicality decreases over time for a while. In situations involving two or more independent variables, a linear model would impose an additive trade-off between those variables that may go beyond the intended theory. Furthermore, some independent variables may not form interval scales.

Returning to our case study, for Target 3, our multifaceted analysis paints a different picture, depending on whether respondents reported the average person’s response or their own judgment. If one were to use one study as a replication of the other, such an analysis would allow the scholar to dig into the details of which substantive findings do or do not replicate. For Spielmann and Stern’s questions about the role of judgment type, our answers depend heavily on the specific hypothesis as well as on our statistical method. The frequentist findings are very closely aligned, but the Bayesian analysis is less consistent between the mTurk and Qualtrix data sets. For the inferred perception by an average person (mTurk), the performance for  Hypotheses  3–4 depends on the statistical criterion, whereas  Hypotheses  5–10 perform well by all criteria. It seems that the probability of “gay” responses declines with a moving window of five month delays, by DIC and based on a very impressive Bayes factor. For personal perception (Qualtrics), however, one can unambiguously conclude that the short term changes captured by  Hypotheses  1–5 are soundly refuted. Here, there appears to be a 7 month moving window associated with decreases in “gay” responses.

Before concluding, I review some of the nuance involved with computing and interpreting Bayes factors.  Tables 1–3  incorporate various tradeoffs associated with the fact that the Bayes factors are numerical approximations that rely on a sampling process and whose accuracy depends on the amount of computation one invests and on the rate of convergence. The rate of convergence depends on the number of parameters (the dimension of the parameter space), on the idiosyncratic mathematical properties of each hypothesis, as well as on the idiosyncrasies of the empirical data. As an illustrative example,  Fig. 5  shows convergence plots that  QTest  generated for  Hypothesis  3 on the Qualtrics data of Target 1, with two different random seeds and 1 billion draws. These plots are available from the  QTest  GUI by clicking the button labeled “Details …” below the “Results” window.  QTest  automatically scales the graph, in this case with 100 million draws per unit on the horizontal axis, and different units on the vertical axis to reflect the range of values it visited. On first inspection, the left hand side process generated Bayes factor values in a very narrow range that never exceeded 0.16, whereas the right hand side process only reached values up to about 0.12. For the purpose of classifying this Bayes factor as providing strong evidence against  Hypothesis  3, it almost did not matter how many iterations we used, since virtually the entire plot is in that range of values in both graphs. Had we run 100 million iterations, then our approximate Bayes factors would have been around 0.08 on the left and about 0.06 on the right. The Gibbs sampler based computation employed here shows some jumps that have to do with the underlying search algorithm.

On the one hand, having two different processes with different random seeds yield nearly the same value for the Bayes factor suggests that we have found a close approximation of the true value. However, as is clear from these graphs, we never have a guarantee that we have reached the correct value or that there could not be another discontinuity if we used a larger collection of draws. This uncertainty about convergence, together with the potentially slow convergence overall, is the main price to pay for having an algorithm that applies to a very broad collection of models, including models that combine inequality with equality constraints. For these hypotheses, none of which involve equality constraints, the user who requires very high accuracy and confidence in the Bayes factors can either supplement or replace this Bayes factor calculation with a draw-and-test algorithm that is currently available only in the  Matlab  code version of  QTest  (as well as the R package of  Heck & Davis-Stober, 2019 ).

The most parsimonious hypotheses, such as  Hypotheses  1 & 2 generate very small Bayes factors that unambiguously rate the evidence against these hypotheses as decisive in Targets 1 & 3, and strong in Target 2. Even if computation from different random seeds leads to values that differ, say, by a factor of two or even ten, these conclusions are typically unaffected here because all values are minuscule.  Fig. 6  provides an example for  Hypothesis  3 on the Qualtrics data of Target 3, with two different random seeds and 1 billion draws. In this figure, the left side graph is scaled about 20 times finer then the right. Both graphs yield only very small values, never exceeding 0.0035, across the entire spectrum of numbers of draws, thereby supporting decisive evidence against the hypothesis almost regardless of the sample size we use for the Gibbs sampler.

For the most complex models, such as  Hypotheses  10 & 11, several considerations are in play: First, the maximal possible Bayes factors are rather small, indicating that strong or decisive evidence is a mathematical impossibility for these models. In the extreme of  Hypothesis  11, where the highest possible value is a Bayes factor of 2, there is very little benefit in achieving an accurate value, especially since we already know from the frequentist and Bayesian  
                         p 
                       analysis that we have a perfect fit. Second, because the high empirical sample size in these data makes for a highly concentrated posterior distribution, the Gibbs sampler, which has to weigh this concentrated posterior against a very broad prior, has a hard time converging to precise values.

In all, with these data and the entire range of hypotheses we considered, most Bayes factors achieve unambiguous classification into the different evidence categories with 10 million to 500 million draws in the Gibbs sampler. In a few cases, most notably  Hypotheses  10 & 11, even a billion draws do not lead to narrow convergence, as evidenced by comparing the results from two or more random seeds and/or by values that exceed the theoretical maximum.

In general, the user who wants to compute Bayes factors, needs to weigh several considerations: First,  QTest  offers different algorithms for Bayes factor calculation, with the draw-and-test method only available in  Matlab . Second, whether one needs very high accuracy in computing a given Bayes factor depends on the scientific value of that precision. High accuracy becomes by far the most valuable when two important and distinct theories are both supported with decisive evidence in their favor, on the same data. In such a situation, the user may want to compute a Bayes factor between these two theories (by taking the ratio of their respective Bayes factors against the unconstrained model) in order to select the better theory and to assess the balance of evidence in its favor. Being able to quantify the relative evidence in favor of one theory versus another will require that the Bayes factors of both theories (against the unconstrained model) are sufficiently accurate to begin with.

This tutorial highlights several features that stand out in the case study.

1. 
                            These eleven hypotheses fundamentally permit huge substantive heterogeneity in the actual response probabilities among observers and some hypotheses can accommodate huge swings in behavior over short or even moderate time spans. In contrast to a common mindset in psychology, rather than thinking of participants as approximate clones of an average/prototypical person, give or take some noise, the approach allows the scholar to take genuine heterogeneity very seriously. 
                         
                         
                            2. 
                            The approach is extremely general: Nothing in this case study is tied to the social psychology paradigm, except for the structure of the theoretical claims. The theory could be from almost any area of psychology and the response proportions in  Fig. 1  could be proportions of choices, answers, symptoms, successes, or other binary observables. They also need not be indexed by discrete values of an independent variable like, in this case study, time spans measured in months. They can be a finite collection of distinct binary variables that may tap into distinct hypothetical constructs. So far, we have only used  QTest  for at most about 40 such variables, as the computational cost for some of its algorithms grows exponentially in the number of binary variables. 
                         
                         
                            3. 
                            This case study illustrates a common situation in psychological research, namely that commonly used methods can render theoretical claims far more specific than intended. Spielmann and Stern theorize that, generally, as targets transition throughout a year they become more gender typical and are less likely to be perceived as gay. By design, any general-purpose parametric analysis may translate the theory overly literally and in a very strict fashion. Most ready-to-use models will implement a far more specific mathematical model, such as a linear model. Standard methods would also add distributional assumptions that the theory does not specify. In particular, many standard methods, such as regressions, would be far more restrictive than  Hypothesis  1 and would imply all eleven hypotheses. Yet, at the same time, many standard approaches would lack the statistical power to reject any of these hypotheses. Our direct test of  Hypothesis  1 clearly refuted it in nearly all cases. The frequentist test therefore also statistically rejected all nested models implied by various standard parametric methods. It is important to note, however, that this rejection does not question Spielmann and Stern’s general verbal and conceptual theory itself: Their theory, itself, is far from being so restrictive as to actually literally imply  Hypothesis  1. 
                         
                         
                            4. 
                            The hypotheses in this paper provide an alternative route to translating a verbal and conceptual theory into very precise, testable hypotheses. The order-constrained approach lets the scholar specify exactly what predictions they make and lets them avoid technical assumptions they would rather not make. While standard parametric software packages tend to impose structure that is not inherent in a theory, order-constrained models like these allow the scholar to take full control of their theoretical predictions. On the downside, unless the scholar specifies theoretical expectations and predictions very precisely and explicitly, there is no suitable order-constrained model to test. 
                         
                         
                            5. 
                            Different substantive, verbal, or conceptual theories may vary in their specificity. This case study illustrates a situation where there are many ways to fully unpack a verbal/conceptual theoretical expectation. In this tutorial, we looked at eleven different hypotheses (ten for Target 2) and sketched numerous other possibilities. This comes with an important caveat: If a scholar lacks a sufficiently precise and well-motivated order-constrained hypothesis, it may be tempting to play around with a large number of possible models that encapsulate variations of a general intuition. This approach needs to be treated with similar care as other exploratory analyses in that the best explanation of a given set of data may be aleatory and may not perform well in explaining or predicting out of sample data. Even Bayes factors are not immune to overfitting. The scholar should also refrain from adding new models to a model competition after having looked at their data, as this will expend an unknown number of degrees of freedom in the data and thereby jeopardize the entire statistical methodology. 
                         
                         
                            6. 
                            Order-constrained models like these enable scholars to run sophisticated model selection among competing, and not necessarily nested, hypotheses of various levels of complexity and nuance. 
                         
                         
                            7. 
                            Statistical inference for these types of models is nontrivial, but the  QTest  public-domain open-source software can be applied in many cases (as can the R package of  Heck & Davis-Stober, 2019 ). As a rule of thumb, the frequentist  
                                  p 
                                value calculations require at least 20 observations per binary response variable. The Bayesian computations have no requirements regarding minimum sample size. However, small samples only provide limited evidence, hence prior distribution assumptions may exercise an outsize influence when using small data sets. This, in turn, could bias the substantive conclusions in favor of parsimonious models, regardless of their empirical performance. The application in this paper led to a different challenge, associated with very large empirical samples: The resulting tight posterior distributions place an additional burden on the Gibbs sampler to converge to very precise Bayes factors for the most complex models.
