Partial masking is conveniently studied in terms of matching functions which record the value of the intensity of a tone in  quiet required to match, in loudness, a tone of intensity x embedded in noise of intensity n. PARTIAL MASKING AND SHIFT INVARIANCE  Listening to a pure tone superimposed on a background of uniform broadband noise, one experiences the following three  phenomena:  (i) The intensity of the tone required for it to be just audible exceeds the corresponding value in quiet.  (ii) As the level of the tone is increased above its threshold value, it sounds less loud than a tone of the same intensity in  quiet.  (iii) As the level of the tone increases still further, the effect of the background noise diminishes. In fact, at  sufficiently high levels the loudness of the tone is unaffected by the noise.  The effect noise on the loudness of a tone, often called partial masking, is conveniently studied in the laboratory via a  matching paradigm. Let us use the following notation: we identify a tone by its intensity and a uniform, broadband Gaussian  noise by its total power. With this understanding we write z = O(x, n) if a tone z (in quiet) matches in loudness a tone x  partially masked by a noise n. The map (x, n) H z = O(x, n) defines the matching surface which equivalently may be thought of  as a family of matching functions 0x) = O(x, n). THE GAIN CONTROL REPRESENTATION  While shift invariance places severe constraints on any theory of partial masking it does not involve any assumptions as to  the manner in which a background noise produces its inhibiting effect on a pure tone stimulus. A model of masking is required  to progress beyond (9) or (10).  We assume that the auditory periphery behaves in a fashion similar to that of an automatic gain control device; that is, a  nonlinear device whose gain is adjusted by some long term property of the input signal, such as average power. We have shown that shift invariance, a property of empirical partially masked loudness  matching functions, in conjunction with a reasonable model of partial masking involving the notion of automatic gain control,  is sufficient to determine the entire matching surface in terms of a few parameters. A parameterc representation for the  matching functions is potentially useful in a clinical setting, and suggests new techniques for assessing certain types of  hearing loss. An extension of the ideas contained in this paper to a binaural paradigm appears interesting both from an  empirical and a theoretical viewpoint. Such an extension may be particularly useful in clinical applications since hearing  loss is not usually confined to a single ear.  Notwithstanding the intuitive appeal of the representation (1), it would be a mistake to conclude that fits of data to the  expressions (13), (14) constitute good evidence for a gain control representation. As we have emphasized, shift invariance by  itself reduces the determination of the matching surface to that of a single matching function. There are obviously many  different ways one might attempt to describe the functional form of a single matching function, and (1) is just one of these.  We can conclude only that the empirical matching data are not inconsistent with the representation (1). A sharper test of our  intuition concerning gain control involves considerations of dynamics, that is, temporal properties of partial masking. In  this connection, we have recently investigated partial masking in the context of a forward masking paradigm. The preliminary  results from this study are encouraging, and we shall report them in detail at a later date.  Certainly the most important aspect of this work is the singular role played by shift invariance. As we have emphasized  throughout this paper, homogeneity conditions are to be prized as readily verifiable constraints of great intrinsic power. We  have found examining a great number of psychophysical data as to their properties under change of physical intensity scale to  be a very useful heuristic in guiding the search for invariants and one which we suggest unearths fundamental properties of  intensity transduction.
