JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 19, 108-136 (1979)  A Diffusion Model for Discrimination of Temporal Numerosity  PAOLO VIVIANI  Department of Neurosensory Physiology, Laboratoire de Physiologie du Travail, CNRS, 41, rue Gay-Lussac, 75005 Paris, France  This paper presents a model for sequential discrimination between the temporal frequencies of two discrete events. The model  results from adjoining a theory for continuous estimation of temporal numerosity to a mathematical representation of the  response criterion. A simple set of assumptions is used to describe the time evolution of numerosity estimates by a stochastic  diffusion process. The predictions of the model are extensively compared to a body of results presented in a previous report,  with an emphasis on individual performances. It is shown that a number of response strategies, observed in actual experiments,  can be predicted by generalizing the classical notion of a single-valued response threshold to a stochastic, time-varying  process.  INTRODUCTION  In experiments on discrimination of temporal numerosity, the stimuli are sequences containing a random mixture of at least two  types of perceptually distinguishable events. After an observation period, the subject must decide which event has been (or is  being) presented more frequently. According to the paradigm most frequently found in the literature (Erlick, 1961, 1962;  Viviani and Terzuolo, 1972), the experimenter imposes a constant observation time which is then followed by a response.  Alternatively, one can leave the subject free to prolong the observation as long as he deems necessary to meet some subjective  response criterion. Such a sequential paradigm yields a rich crop of data because the performance is defined jointly by the  response latencies and probabilities. Results for this kind of experiment were presented in a previous paper (Viviani, 1979).  The purpose of this paper is to present a model of the sequential discrimination task. Any such model must contain two  logically distinct components: (1) a set of assumptions that define the process whereby the physical stimulus (i.e. the  sequence) is translated into a percept, and (2) the specification of a response mechanism. To represent the first component, a  strength theory for temporal numerosity was developed in a previous study (Viviani and Terzuolo, 1972). Although its  motivation was provided by the results of experiments with fixed observation times, the theory is only concerned with the  perceptual aspect of discrimination and should be applicable independently to the type of response mechanism imposed by the  experimental task. The generality of the theory is tested by extending it to sequential discrimination tasks.  The specification of the response mechanism is the task-dependent component of the model. A STRENGTH THEORY FOR TEMPORAL NUMEROSITY  The Perceptual Task  Whenever a sequence of events is presented at a rate that makes counting impossible, the numerosity of the sequence (the total  number of events actually presented) can nevertheless be estimated. Aside from the fact that numerosity cannot be apprehended  instantaneously like loudness, pitch, or brightness, there seems to be no a priori reason to consider the perception of  numerosity differently from the perception of these physical quantities. Actually, the results of discrimination experiments  are somewhat similar to those obtained with more familiar stimuli (e.g. Erlick, 1961). Absolute number judgments, however,  demonstrate that numerosity is a rather peculiar dimension. Forsyth and Chapanis (1958), using sequences of light flashes  presented between 2.5 and 30 stimuli per second over a wide range of retinal locations, found that mean judged number was  approximately proportional to the actual number. However, in accordance with other studies (Taubmann, 1950a, b; Cheatham and  White, 1952, 1954; Lawrence, 1971), they also found that the slope of the relationship was a sharply decreasing function of  the rate of presentation. Therefore, to each presentation rate there corresponds a different scale for numerosity. The process  of continuously updating perceived numerosity by integrating successive events apparently has a finite time constant which  makes it sensitive to the rate of presentation.  The same data of Forsyth and Chapanis shows that the presentation rate, itself, is a better behaved prothetic dimension. The  linear approximation used by these authors is:  h = y(v, a)n,  where n and n are the objective and estimated number of flashes respectively. The coefficient y(v, a) depends on the rate of  presentation v and on the angular displacement from the fovea a. Further information on the process of formation of temporal numerosity judgments comes from discrimination studies (Erlick,  1961; Viviani and Terzuolo, 1972; Viviani and Curry, in preparation). They consistently show that subjective difference  between the numerosity of two competing sequences of events is a negatively accelerated function of the sequence duration.  Since discrimination power tends to level off for relatively long sequences, it can be supposed that perceived numerosity also  increases less than linearly with sequence duration. Finally, discrimination experiments in which the presentation rate is not  uniform throughout the sequence (Erlick, 1961; Sanders and Ter Linden, 1967; Viviani and Terzuolo, 1972), in addition to  confirming the non-linearity of the number-estimation process, also show the presence of a strong recency effect. The simplest  explanation for this effect is time decay of a memory trace.  To summarize, the process of continuously translating objective number into perceived numerosity can be qualitatively  characterized as a non-linear, leaky integration whose time constant is such as to induce the type of rate sensitivity  illustrated in Table I. A MODEL FOR THE SEQUENTIAL TASK  The section presents a model for sequential estimation of temporal numerosity which joins the previously developed perceptual  theory with a simple and yet realistic representation of the response criterion. The discriminal information YD(t) is  represented by a diffusion process with a positive or negative drift, as in most models for Choice-Reaction Times. In line  with the basic intuition underlying these models, we assume that a response is elicited as soon as the discriminal information  exceeds one of two threshold values. Part of the observed variability is due to the stimulus sequences. However, even  when the same sequence is presented over and over, a residual variability is present which, in the context of the model, must  be accounted for by the randomness of the thresholds. Since delaying the response increases their latency variability (see  later) and since the variance of the discriminal process decreases with time, it is necessary to postulate an increase of the  thresholds' variance. A Comment on Independence  It must be stressed that assumptions on the thresholds are not independent of those on the discriminal process. Thus, aside  from the obvious indeterminacy of the scale factors (either 1/~ or the difference TG(O) - T,(0) can be fixed arbitrarily), the  assumed properties of the threshold variance are derived entirely from those of VD(t). Crediting to the threshold all the  variability present in the responses when the same sequence is given over and over, is an oversimplification because the  discriminal process is certainly noisy as well. However the decrease in the average criterion over time is independent of the  representation of the discriminal process. As seen later, experimental results imply that  TEMPORAL NUMEROSITY 119  such a decrease must occur, given the very sensible assumption that discrimination improves with increased observation time  (Sanders and Ter Linden, 1967; Viviani, 1979).  VALIDATION OF THE MODEL  This section compares the predictions of the model with a body of experimental evidence gathered in a previous investigation  (Viviani, 1979). The stimuli were sequences of colored flashes arranged in a random order, except for the following invariant  structure. Each sequence began with an initial segment of duration, d = 8, 10, 12, 14 or 16 sec, which contained an equal  number of green and red flashes. At the end of this segment an imbalance was suddenly introduced between the presentation  rates vG and vR of the two colors. The imbalance was characterized by the ratio r = v,Iv, which could take any of the five  values: .5, .666, l., 1.5 and 2. The overall rate v = VG -r PR was kept constant for all sequences in a given condition. Two  conditions, v = 3 p.p.s. (pulses per second) and v = 5 p.p.s. were considered. The observer was asked to identify the color  which was being shown more frequently by pressing one of two keys as soon as he felt confident about his judgment. Entering  the response immediately terminated the presentation. DISCUSSION  A previous paper (Viviani, 1979) argued that discrimination of temporal numerosity is based on continuous comparison between a  discriminal process and a time-varying criterion. This paper presents a model for the sequential discrimination task which  formalizes these two concepts. It also shows that the model duplicates most of the experimental results and parametrizes  individual differences in a fairly intuitive way.  Relationship between Response Latencies and Probability  Once a set of model parameters optimizes the fit to a basic performance profile, a number of other, unrelated aspects are also  predicted. Two of these, concerning the relationships between response latencies and probabilities, are particularly  interesting because they were not explicitly built into the model. The first is the bimodal latency distribution for errors,  which the model attributes to two concomitant sources (c.f. upper diagrams of Fig. (7)). The increased frequency of slow  errors is caused primarily by the progressive relaxation of the criterion while the fast errors stem from the presence of an  initial segment of the sequence which supplies inconclusive information. Since these early errors dominate in absolute  frequency (see Fig. (6)), the model attributes the fact that mean latencies for errors tend to be shorter than those for  correct answers to sampling of the informationless segment of the sequence. A similar explanation, sampling of the empty  pre-exposure field, has been previously advanced in the modeling of classical CRT experiments (Laming, 1968).  The other interesting prediction concerns color biases which the model can interpret as either a difference in the decay rates  or in the initial values of the thresholds. The first alternative emphasizes the role of the response strategy. The second  alternative is logically equivalent to a difference in sensitivity to the two stimuli and, as such, pertains more to the  perceptual aspect of the performance. These two interpretations are indistinguishable as long as one only considers color  biases of individual subjects. However, the two alternatives do not make the same predictions about the covariation of  response probabilities and latencies across a population of subjects with different biases (see Fig. (8)). The reasonably good  fit obtained by shifting the initial values of the thresholds by equal amounts can be taken to suggest that color biases are  due to perceptual asymmetries rather than to asymmetries in the response strategies. The perceptual processes were represented on the basis of three ideas: 1) each event in the sequence is individually taken  into account, 2) the observer forgets and 3) adding one more event to a large sum total has a smaller perceptual effect than  adding it to a small sum total. The same ideas may have been embodied in a function relationship different from Eq. (1). We  maintain, however, that the present formulation represents a variety of possible behaviors in a unified manner. For relatively  short observation intervals, the average state variable E{F(t)) estimates the absolute number of events since limt-o 9Ef  F(t)}let = v - ~. As time goes on, the same quantity turns progressively into a logarithmic estimator of the presentation  rate: lim,, E{F(t)) _ (1/0) log(] /99) (1/0) log v. Thus, according to the present theory, rate and number estimation are  limiting cases of a more fundamental perceptual mechanism which can be addressed directly only to get absolute number in short  sequences or average rate in longer sequences. In the complementary cases, number and -rate would have to be calculated from  an independent estimate of time, and would therefore constitute a form of derived measurement. Notice that this hypothesis is  fully consistent with our analysis of the data of Forsyth and Chapanis (see above) and, in particular, with the logarithmic  scaling of presentation rates. However, the coefficients a and b in Table I cannot be identified directly with 1 /0 log 1/~  and 1/0, respectively, because they would also include the process of time estimation.  As in the case of the discriminal variable FD(t) (c.f. Eq. (5)), also the evolution of the average state variable from a  number to a rate estimator is strongly dependent on the saturation constant 0: The smaller the value of this parameter, the  longer it takes for E{F(t)j to approach the asymptotic rate estimator. Thus the observer's capability of stressing either  aspect of the perceptual process-rate or number estimation-depending on the circumstance and the assignements, is interpreted  by the model by assuming the possibility of properly setting the value of the non-linear parameter 0. The extent to which this  control is actually possible still awaits experimental scrutiny.  If indeed objective numerosity is translated into a primary percept sharing the character of both absolute number and rate,  then discrimination experiments seem better suited than scaling experiments to investigate the psychophysics of such a  percept. The reason is that subjective numerosity, in the sense stated above, overlaps with such familiar notions as number  and rate, but is not itself an intuitive concept. It is consequently difficult to ask straight-forward questions concerning  absolute magnitude. Magnitude estimations of number and/or rate seem impractical too, since it is unclear how to untangle the  process of forming the primary percept (subjective numerosity) from that of perceiving the duration. In the case of  differential judgments, the observer can directly compare the magnitudes of the primary percepts, regardless of the  terminology used to define the task. Significantly enough, in both fixed-sample and sequential experiments, even those  subjects who were capable of very accurate discrimination did very poorly whenever (after a presentation) they were asked to  estimate the actual total number of red and green flashes they had just seen.
