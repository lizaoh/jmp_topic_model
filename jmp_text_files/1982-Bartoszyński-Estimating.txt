PURI  Polish Academy of Science, Warsaw, and Indiana University, Bloomington  Methods are presented for estimating inter-subject variability of the probability of a given event defined in terms of  subject's behavior (e.g., probability of a given choice in a discrimination experiment). The constraints consist of using no  more than two independent observations for each subject. Estimators are provided for assessing the inter-subject "variance" of  the analyzed probabilities; also, a method is given for testing the hypothesis that the average probability is the same for  two groups of subjects.  1. INTRODUCTION  A practical problem in which the results of the present paper may be useful is as follows. The experimenter is interested in a  certain event, say A, defined in terms of behavior of the subject (e.g., choice of one of the two displayed stimuli which are  to be discriminated). The experimenter needs the probability PS(A), where s denotes the subject. To estimate this probability  one should make a series of independent observations on the same subject, a procedure often not feasible for reasons such as  memory or learning effects, or simply subject's boredom. In such cases, one often makes the assumption that Ps(A) does not  depend on s, and proceeds to estimate P(A) using data for large groups of subjects, each testes once or twice. The crucial  issue in such a procedure is the inter-subject variability of PS(A). The present paper gives, among others, methods of  assessing this variability, and testing the hypothesis of equality of average PS(A) in two groups of subjects, under the  constraint that at most two observations are taken for each subject. In a sense, this paper constitutes a statistical  counterpart of an earlier paper written by one of the authors (see Bartoszyliski (1978)).  Here we study problems similar, but not identical, to those of analyzing interrater agreement by the so-called kappa  coefficient (see, e.g., Fleiss, 1981, Chap. 13). Phrased in such terms, in this paper  we have many observers, each assessing the same object (into categories A and A') and the problem lies in determining the  degree of agreement in such assessment.  The approach used here differs in some respects from the ones usually employed. Typically, one would treat the group of  subjects as a sample chosen from a larger population and the probabilities P,(A) as the values of some random variable (the  "true scores"). Thes true scores would, in turn, be subject to observation errors when one observes the occurrence or  non-occurrence of A for various s. This is the approach to the theory of mental test scores (see the monograph of Lord &  Novick, 1968), where various procedures are suggested for the estimation of the variance of error scores, in so-called  reliability theory. As regards equality of means of PS(A) in two groups, one would typically use the schemes of analysis of  variance and methods related to such schemes.  The disadvantage of the latter approach lies in the fact that the techniques of analysis of variance are based on assumptions  which are not met in the present case of binary random variables (occurrence or non-occurrence of A), The approach of the  theory of mental tests, on the other hand, is based on the presupposition that the subjects (hence their true scores) are  sampled from some larger population according to a certain distribution, which determines the variance of true scores. The  latter, being the primary concern in our case, is treated as a sort of "nuisance parameter" in the theory of mental test  scores, which concentrates mainly on estimation and control of the variance of error scores.  Our approach consists of treating the set of subjects under consideration as a population, characterized by a multidimensional  parameter O = {PS(A), s E S ~, and estimating some functions of O. We derive certain bounds for the variances of our  estimators, obtaining, in essence, conservative estimation or testing procedures, with the quality of these procedures  improving as n (the number of subjects) increases. APPLICATIONS  A typical application, for assessing the inter-subject variability of choice probabilities, has already been described in the  Introduction. Somewhat more generally, one may consider the following situation.  Suppose that the subjects are trained to perform some classification task, e.g., classify some objects into binary categories.  Taking one of these categories as "success," and classifying the same set of objects twice by the same group of subjects, one  obtains a variety of possibilities for applying the results of this paper. Let pi; be the probability that ith subject will  classify jth object to the chosen category, and let X;p YU be 0 or 1 depending on how the jth object was classified by the ith  subject on the first and second trials. Fixing i, and taking a large set of objects, one can estimate the variability of PU  under changes of j; if this variability is  184 BARTOSZYPISKI AND PURI  large, one may want to identify objects which are easier, or more difficult, to classify. On the other hand, fixing j, and  varying i, one can test whether there exists sufficient homogeneity among classifying subjects; one can also test the  hypothesis, using Propositions 3 and 4, that the two groups of subjects are equally well trained, etc.  If there are more than two categories in the classification, one can apply the same technique by fixing one category. However,  if one can ensure not two, but four independent classifications for each person, then one can define "success" as the event  with "identical classifications on two successive trials."  Letting PtUk, k = 1, 2,..., denote the probability that ith subject will classify jth object into kth category, the  probability of success in this case is  z  r.. Finally, observe that the above ways of testing  the quality of classification by human classifiers is independent of the concept of "true category" of the object, and applies  equally well to classification schemes in which such a concept makes no sense (e.g., grading students' papers, etc.).
