Let  Z ={ x , y , z , w ,…} or, alternatively,  Z ={1,…, n } be a finite set of  n ⩾3 elements. Interpreting Z as a set of stimuli, we consider a (two-way, one-mode) proximity matrix on Z in which  q ( x , y ) is a generic proximity. In the Shepard–Kruskal variety of multidimensional scaling (MDS), commonly called  non-metric  MDS, we seek a representation of such a matrix in which the  x ∈ Z  are represented as points in a metric space of minimal dimensionality such that the proximities, when taken as dissimilarities, are monotone with the interpoint distances to an acceptable degree of approximation ( Shepard, 1972 , pp. 7–8). In some such analyses the resulting configuration of points appears roughly circular. Well-known examples are the configuration much resembling the color circle obtained by  Shepard (1962)  in his famous reanalysis of  Ekman's (1954)  proximity matrix on a set of 14 colors, and the configuration of the Morse codes of the digits obtained by  Coombs (1964, p. 487)  from a proximity matrix based on data of  Rothkopf (1957) , [hereafter simply called  Rothkopf's (1957)  matrix]. Other examples are in  Shepard (1978) ,  Russell (1980) , and  Russell, Lewicka, and Niit (1989) . These results suggest to consider the stimuli as arranged in a cycle and, then, raise the question of how to discern that cycle in the proximities. Can we detect a circular structure in a proximity matrix directly, i.e., without first embedding the stimuli in a two-dimensional space?

Referring to the configuration obtained in his reanalysis of  Ekman's (1954)  matrix,  Shepard (1974, p. 383)  notes that ‘interpretation in terms of a circular dimension ... seems as inviting as interpretation in terms solely of rectilinear dimensions’. The latter interpretation suggests to consider the stimuli as tending ‘to be analyzed into perceptually distinct components or properties’ appearing in the dimensions whereas the former suggests to regard them as ‘homogeneous, unitary wholes’ which are taken as such ( Shepard, 1964, p. 80 ). Certainly in view of these contrasting interpretations it is unsatisfactory to characterize a configuration resulting from MDS as circular only by inspection. Also, if doing so, the question arises as to what the circularity of the configuration means. Does the circular order of the stimuli have any bearing on the structure of their proximities? For, if the configuration only tells us that its interpoint distances are approximately monotone with the ordering of the proximities, what is the point of the configuration being circular?

A proximity matrix is called  row ( column )- conditional  if its proximities are deemed comparable only within rows (columns). Here, we take  conditional  always as row-conditional. Experiments on recognition in which  q ( x , y ) is determined as, say, the proportion of the presentations of x to which y was responded, are a well-known source of such proximities. Other examples are in  Coombs (1964)  and  Coxon and Davies (1982) . Being typically not symmetric, a conditional proximity matrix is generally not fit for a spatial representation with symmetric interpoint distances. For analysis by a method of non-metric MDS, it is often first made symmetric (e.g. by averaging or summing the symmetrically related entries) and, then, treated as a symmetric matrix. Examples are  Coombs’ (1964)  and  Hubert's (1974)  analyses of Rothkopf's matrix. Alternatively, a conditional matrix is processed by a program of MDS in which the order relations among the interpoint distances are only constrained by the orders of the proximities within rows (e.g. the programs ALSCAL;  Schiffman, Reynolds, & Young, 1981, p. 76  or MINICPA;  Coxon & Davies, 1982 ). Yet, in whatever way it is obtained, a spatial representation with symmetric distances represents the ordered pairs ( x , y ) and ( y , x ) by a same distance and, so, is essentially a mapping of a set of unordered pairs { x , y } into a set of distances.

We interpret an inequality  q ( x , y )> q ( x , z ) on these proximities as the order { x , y }> 0 { x , z } meaning that, in some sense, x is closer to y than to z. Interpreting the proximities within a row as inducing an order, possibly a weak order, on the unordered pairs determined by that row, we obtain such an order of the unordered pairs from every row. Then, in the case of a conditional matrix, the conjunction of these orders may very well contain a circular order, e.g.

Such a circular order is not compatible with the transitivity of the order relation ⩾ among the interpoint distances in a representation as obtained in MDS (we take it that a trivial representation in which all interpoint distances are equal is unwanted). A circular order thus embedded in a conditional matrix may even comprise  all  unordered pairs { x , y }, x ≠ y  ( Bezembinder, 1991, p. 172 ).  Section 3  shows a conditional matrix on a set of 8 stimuli (the one represented by  Q 4 ( Table 1 ))  that comprises 2 circular orders of 8, and 2 circular orders of 4 unordered pairs while its rows are all monotone, in the sense specified below, with one (same) circular order of the stimuli. Such a matrix does not call for a spatial representation with a transitive order of the interpoint distances but motivates an examination of the underlying circular order of the stimuli.

We present an alternative approach for examining a circular order of the stimuli as underlying a proximity matrix. We formulate our approach for a proximity matrix that (i) is  row - conditional , and (ii) has in each row, at the diagonal, one (unique) maximum. Such a matrix induces a weak (possibly simple) order on the proximities and, thereby, also on the associated unordered pairs within every row. (Recall that, unlike a weak order, a simple order only allows equivalence classes of one single object (e.g.  Roberts, 1979 , p. 29): only a row whose proximities are all different can induce a simple order on the associated unordered pairs). We focus on what we call a  rowwise rank ordered proximity  ( ROP ) matrix that induces a simple order on the unordered pairs determined by, respectively, every row. We take no interest in the values of the proximities but only in their orders within rows. So, we accept that a matrix with tied proximities within rows may give rise to several ROP-matrices depending on the breaking of the ties. We will treat a matrix in which  all  proximities are deemed comparable, typically a symmetric matrix, also as row-conditional: we only consider the orders of its proximities within rows. In our approach it is not fruitful to use comparabilities of proximities across rows.

From now on, we will use ‘ cycle ’ and ‘ cyclic ’ when referring to a circular order of the stimuli, and ‘ circular ( ity )’ when referring to a circular order of unordered pairs. We define a  dyad  as an unordered pair { x , y } with  x ≠ y . We call the element that dyads from a same row have in common, the  reference . So, when we compare the dyads { x , y } and { x , z }, x  is the reference, and we use the proximities in row x for the comparison. Similarly, we use row y when comparing { x , y } and { y , z }. With a fixed reference x we can indicate the dyad { x , z } by the column index z while, when considering a cycle  C =(… vwxyz …), we can moreover indicate { x , z } by the  cycle position  (+2) of its column index on C w.r.t. x. Indeed, { x , z } and { x , w } have, respectively, the cycle positions 2 and −1 on C w.r.t. x since, starting from x, it takes 2 steps on C to reach z (going clockwise) and 1 step to reach w (going anti-clockwise). The only consequence of denoting C by (… zyxwv …) is that the signs of all cycle positions are reversed. (Changing from one notation to the other comes down to flipping the cycle and, thereby, interchanging left and right). When considering a cycle (1… n ), it is convenient (though not necessary) to order the rows and columns of the proximity matrix accordingly. In our examples we will do so.

The term ‘ circumplex ’ commonly refers to (i) a  symmetric  proximity matrix with a  circumplical pattern  in both rows and columns, i.e., when moving in a row or column away from the main diagonal, going around, and then approaching the diagonal from the other end, the proximities first do not increase and then do not decrease or, alternatively, first do not decrease and then do not increase, and (ii) a cycle comprising all stimuli ( Jöreskog, 1974 , pp. 43, 45;  Shepard, 1978 , p. 37). The circumplex was probably first studied by  Guttman (1954, 1966)  who obtained a correlation matrix with an approximately circumplical pattern from a cycle of mental tests that he christened ‘circumplex’. Our notion of circumplex pertains to a (row)- conditional  proximity matrix whose rows show a circumplical pattern. We do not use the term ‘circumplex’ for designating a cycle of the stimuli but speak of a  cycle  (of stimuli) as  underlying  a circumplex (a conditional matrix with a circumplical pattern in every row).

Let the stimuli be arranged in a (virtual) cycle, and suppose that proximities to x are ordered by the order in which we meet the other stimuli when, starting from x, we go along this cycle, clockwise or anti-clockwise. How does this constrain the proximities to x in row x of a proximity matrix? As an example, and to make clear the notions used in the following, let the stimuli  1,2, …, 9,0  be arranged in the cycle ( 12 … 0 ). Starting from, say,  3 , and going clockwise we meet, in this order,  4, 5, 6, 7, 8,  and row  3  of the matrix should show decreasing proximities in the corresponding columns  4, 5, 6, 7, 8 . Going anti-clockwise, we meet, in this order,  2, 1, 0, 9, 8,  and the proximities in row  3  should decrease in the columns  2,1, …, 8 . If its proximities decrease in this manner, we say that row  3  shows  unilateral monotonicity  ( UM ). If UM holds in all of its rows, we say that the matrix forms a  unilateral circumplex . We just regarded the proximities of the dyads { 3,5 } and { 3,9 } as incomparable since  5  and  9  are  bilateral  of  3 , i.e., on different halves of the cycle when ‘seen’ from  3 . However, when we assume that these halves are seen jointly, then the proximities in row  3  should also decrease when, for instance, we go through the columns  4, 1, 6, 9, 8  because, on the cycle, these are, respectively, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 steps removed from  3 . In fact, the proximities in row  3  should then decrease when we go through the columns in the order  2, 4, 1, 5, 0, 6, 9, 7, 8  or in an order we get from this one by interchanging  2  and  4  and/or  1  and  5  and/or  0  and  6  and/or  9  and  7  (pairs of dyads like  {{3,1},{3,5}}  are  equidistant ). If its proximities follow these constraints, we say that row  3  shows  circumplical monotonicity  ( CM ). If CM holds in all rows, we call the matrix a  non-equidistant circumplex . Only dyads in equidistant pairs are now considered incomparable. Finally, it is possible that the equidistant pairs show  iso - orientation  ( IO ) in the sense that always of two equidistant dyads { x , y } and { x , z } the first has a greater proximity than the second if on the cycle y is to the right of x while z is to the left of x (note that flipping the cycle interchanges right and left). All proximities within rows now being comparable, the matrix satisfies  full circumplicity  (FC), i.e., CM as well as IO, and describes a  full circumplex .

As the monotonicity required under UM and CM is strict, tied proximities (within rows) are not compatible with our approach. We accordingly defined the unilateral, non-equidistant, and full circumplex for a ROP-matrix. We call a circumplex on  Z ={ 1, …, n }  global  if it is induced by a cycle, say, ( 1 … n ), that comprises all elements of Z. A global circumplex may then be unilateral, non-equidistant or full. Unless n is prime, we may also partition Z into equal parts, and examine whether the corresponding submatrices of the ROP-matrix under consideration show a circumplex. Again, a circumplex then appearing in a submatrix may be unilateral, non-equidistant or full. In the case of such a partition, we call the structure as a whole a  local  circumplex.

Our purposes are the following. (i) We propose a procedure for deciding whether a ROP-matrix may be regarded as a circumplex with a specific, hypothetical, underlying cycle of the stimuli. Taking account of its violations of UM, CM, and FC, we will evaluate the fit of a ROP-matrix to a unilateral, a non-equidistant, and a full circumplex, respectively. (ii) The rows of a unilateral circumplex all have the same pattern: in the centered peak form, their proximities strictly decrease from either side of the peak. Also in a non-equidistant circumplex, all rows have a same pattern: expressed by the cycle positions of their column indices, the proximities decrease in the order 1  −1 2 −2…  except for, possibly, some interchanges of j and − j , for  j =1,2,… [i.e., the decrements go by the  partial order  
                      (1 −1)(2 −2)…  with j and − j  (in parentheses) being incomparable]. Actually, it will appear that each of the 3 circumplexes admits an automorphism that is induced by one,  unique , cycle of the stimuli that is determined by only the order relations among the proximities ( Theorems 5, 6 ). (iii) In the context of these notions we will examine  Ekman's (1954)  and  Rothkopf's (1957)  matrices for some global as well as local circumplexes. (iv) A full circumplex on 3 or ⩾5 stimuli, the strictest form of circumplex here considered, comprises ⩾1 circular order of n dyads as well as some circular orders of < n  dyads if n is not prime ( Theorem 7 ). We will argue that these circular orders are rather vulnerable by errors in the orderings of proximities.

Our approach is basically ordinal: we only consider orders of proximities within rows. However, a row in centered peak form should show, by UM, equal numbers of strictly decreasing proximities at either side of the peak and, by CM, a weak form of symmetry between the decrements of the proximities with positive and negative cycle positions (appearing in the partial order  (1 −1)(2 −2)…) . So, our approach is slightly stronger than ordinal. Yet, since what we analyze are the orders (not the values) of the proximities within rows, it is for our purposes appropriate and sufficient to represent a proximity matrix by (only) the orders of its proximities within rows. Thus going by  Coombs’ (1964, p. 4)  notion of data as ‘only … that which is analyzed’, we will typically represent a proximity matrix by (only) the orders of the off-diagonal proximities, i.e., the orders of the dyads, indicated by their column-indices, within rows. A full circumplex (also) constrains the order of the dyads in the equidistant pairs. Specifically, for every reference and for every equidistant pair, there should be a  tendency  towards the same branch. If the tendency is to the right [left], then, for every pair { i ,− i }, the dyad at cycle position  i [− i ] shows a stronger proximity than the one at cycle position − i [ i ]. We presented a method for analyzing a ROP-matrix under the hypothesis of a global or local circumplex. The underlying cycle  π  of stimuli is taken as a primitive and interpreted as a basis of similarity. This does not preclude that  π  be derived from substantive considerations nor that the similarities reflected in  π  be considered in the context of a model of similarity such as a feature model. We take  π  as a primitive only in the context of analyzing a ROP-matrix in order to test if it is circumplical under  π . Our method serves to establish or discard a (hypothetical) underlying cycle for a ROP-matrix arising from a conditional or a symmetric proximity matrix. When used under the hypothesis of a local circumplex, it also serves to establish a clustering of the stimuli.

Aiming at detecting a circumplex directly in ordered pairs of conjoint dyads, our approach is most appropriate for two classes of stimuli: (i) relatively unanalyzable stimuli that tend to be processed as unitary wholes or, by lack of preferred dimensions, do not elicit a processing by some fixed dimensions ( Shepard, 1964 , p. 80); these include the naturalistic stimuli typically examined by methods of MDS for finding underlying dimensions; examples: facial expressions, colors, speech sounds (references in  Shepard, 1964 , p. 80), emotions, nations, maternal behaviors ( Schaefer, 1959 ); (ii) inherently finite sets of stimuli (e.g., Morse codes, kin terms) or inherently discrete structures (e.g., conceptual or semantic systems, speech sounds, algebraic structures (references in  Shepard, 1974 , p. 412)).

Recall  D 
                      
                         x 
                       as a conjoint set ( Section 2 ). In a global circumplex under a (cyclic)  π  we require an ordered conjoint pair to be monotone with its step-distances on  π  if these are unequal, and allow it to be part of a circular ordering if they are equal. This is the core of our model of a circumplex. In its strictest variety the model requires an automorphism under  π  on the CP-digraph that represents a full circumplex and, thus, circular orderings for the equidistant conjoint pairs (the conjunction of the  Theorems 5 and 7 ). Now suppose that orderings of conjoint proximities on, say, colors or personality traits, are suspected to be induced by an underlying cycle of the stimuli. For the collection of the data we then suggest to see to it that the orders of the dyads within conjoint sets are independent so that their average correlation with the criterion order determined by the underlying cycle can be tested for significance (a necessary though far from sufficient condition for the existence of a circumplex). In general, all psychometric methods that establish such orders are here appropriate (e.g.,  Torgerson, 1958 ;  Hubert & Schultz, 1975 ;  Hubert, 1976 ;  Takane, 1981 ). However, inferring the desired orders from symmetric proximities (e.g., correlations) is only appropriate under the hypothesis that the underlying cycle constrains (the order in) unilateral pairs. As symmetric proximities determine a weak order on all dyads, circular paths of dyads then result from tiebreaks ( Section 2 ) or error. If the hypothetical circumplex is more strict (i.e., non-equidistant or full), it is  not  appropriate to infer the desired orders from symmetric measures since these determine the proximity of  xy  in jointly  D

                       and  D

The present circumplex model does not call for symmetrizing since it takes conditional and symmetric proximity matrices as inherently different: only the former can give rise to a CP-digraph that has circular paths and admits a non-trivial automorphism. Our approach is designed for a conditional matrix. For applying it to a symmetric proximity matrix, we need only consider the weak orders induced on the dyads within rows. If these weak orders reveal a circumplex for some ways of breaking their ties, we may consider to regard this circumplex as being embedded in the weak order of all proximities determined by the matrix as a whole. The fact of this weak order precludes circular orderings, an automorphism by  Theorem 8 , and, thus, (only) the strictest form of a circumplex.

If  Ekman's (1954)  proximities were on 15 instead of 14 colors we could have obtained cycles of lengths 3, 5, 15 instead of 7, 14. We comment on this in two points.

1. The constraints that UC ( Section 7 ) imposes on the comparisons of conjoint proximities do not so much arise from UC as a strategy of judgment but rather from the context in which it operates: a (supposedly) circumplical structure of simply ordered conjoint sets deemed invariant under the action of an underlying permutation of the (set of) elements composing the proximities. As appears from the  Theorems 5 and 9 , this context severely constrains the structures we may discern in the proximities. Here is the rub: structural properties of sets of objects, which may be particularly poignant in finite sets, can generally not be transferred to other sets.

2. The sets  Z 
                      14  of colors and  Z 
                      10  of Morse codes are samples from, respectively, a continuum of colors and a set of discrete Morse codes. The wave lengths of the colors in  Z 
                      14  ( Table 2 ) are unequally spaced on the color circle. However, the notion of spacing cannot be reflected in the notion of a step-distance along a permutation. Our approach is suited for studying similarities in finite sets which we deem of interest as such without inferences about their supersets. It stirs to study subsets rather than supersets.

Let Z be a set of  n ⩾5 stimuli, { x , y }, x ≠ y , a  dyad , i.e., an unordered pair from Z, and {{ x , y },{ x , z }} a  conjoint pair  (of dyads from Z). For a proximity matrix, let  xy → xz  mean that row x shows a stronger proximity for { x , y } than for { x , z }. This is represented by an arc in a digraph having the dyads as points. Let  π  be a cyclic permutation of Z.

(i) For some proximity matrices, multidimensional scaling yields a roughly circular configuration of the stimuli. Being not symmetric, a row-conditional matrix is not fit for such an analysis. However, suppose its proximities are all different within rows. We have  π  characterize two subsets of the conjoint pairs. If the arcs  xy → xz  between the pairs in such a subset are in a specific sense monotone with  π , the matrix determines  π  uniquely, and is, in that sense, a circumplex with  π  as underlying cycle.

Significance : The requirement of monotonicity provides an essentially ordinal analysis of, in particular, a row-conditional proximity matrix whose proximities are all different within rows. It suggests a procedure for deciding whether such a matrix can be regarded as a circumplex, possibly distorted by error, with a hypothetical cyclic permutation  τ  of the stimuli as underlying cycle. A proportion of the conjoint pairs in which the arc is the inverse of the one that is monotone with  τ  forms a natural criterion for this decision.

(ii) A circumplex is said to be  full  if its underlying cycle determines the arc in all conjoint pairs. The arcs  xy → xz  induced by a full circumplex form ⩾1 circular paths ( Theorem 7 ). As its proximities are all comparable throughout, a symmetric proximity matrix induces a weak order on all unordered pairs of stimuli and, so, cannot be a full circumplex.

Significance : When presumed to form a circumplex as here defined, a symmetric and a (row-) conditional matrix are inherently different. This makes symmetrizing a conditional matrix pointless. A symmetric matrix can be analyzed by multidimensional scaling but cannot be a full circumplex.
