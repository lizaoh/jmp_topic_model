A general theory of the relationship of binocular visual space to physical space is formulated within a conjoint measurement framework. Psychophysically motivated invariance relations induce various functional equations. Another class of functional equations arises if we additionally assume the validity of a different psychophysical theory that is based on a formula suggested by A. A. Blank. We solve and interpret most of these equations and point out an unsolved problem. The obtained results lead to a measurement-theoretic foundation of the psychophysical assumptions underlying the Luneburg theory of binocular vision. They also contribute to clarifying the relationship between the presented general theory and Blank's approach. � 1999 Academic Press 

1. BACKGROUND 

We perceive ourselves located in a unitary and stable perceptual space. The so-called visual space is distinguished from other perceptual spaces by its compelling phenomenological reality and its undoubted spatial character. In a highly sophisticated approach, Luneburg (1947) embodied visual space in a geometrically formulated perceptual theory. The Luneburg theory of binocular vision not only provides a geometrical characterization of the internal structure of visual space but also

It accounts for classical empirical phenomena, like the Helmholtz frontal horopter (Helmholtz, 1867) or the visual alleys (Hillebrand, 1902; Blumenfeld, 1913), which arise under viewing conditions isolating the effect of binocularity by excluding monocular cues and experiential factors. 2. THE LUNEBURG THEORY OF BINOCULAR VISION 

In the sequel, we will confine ourselves to observation of stimuli in the horizontal plane at eye-level. The following two postulates are at the core of the Luneburg theory of binocular vision: 

(L1) Visual space, which may be coordinatized with respect to perceived egocentric distance and perceived direction, is a Riemannian space of constant Gaussian curvature. Its metric determines the perceived interpoint distances, while its geodesics are the perceptually straight lines. 

(L2) The points lying on a Vieth�Mu�ller circle are perceived as being equidistant from the observer, and the hyperbolae of Hillebrand are perceived as radial lines of constant direction. 

Note. The Vieth�Mu�ller circles go through L and R (the rotation centers of the left and right eye, respectively). Each Hillebrand hyperbola branch goes through either L or R, except for the x-axis, which is also a (degenerate) hyperbola of Hillebrand. The point of intersection of a hyperbola branch and of a Vieth�Mu�ller circle, connected with that circle's point at the negative vertical axis, yields a straight line meeting the vertical axis under constant angle for a given hyperbola (see Fig. 1). Under idealized assumptions on the optics of the eye, the (uniquely determined) Vieth�Mu�ller circle through the fixation point is the locus of stimuli that are projected on corresponding retinal points (cf. Howard 6 Rogers, 1995). For this to be true, the center of rotation and the optical node have to be assumed to coincide with the center of curvature of a spherical eye. Then corresponding retinal points are congruent when the two retinas are superimposed so that the foveae, onto which the fixation point is projected, coincide. Accordingly, the hyperbola of Hillebrand is the locus of symmetric retinal points, which deviate to the same extent but in opposite directions from the foveae. 

By assumption (L2), the preimages of the visual coordinate lines under the psychophysical function are the Vieth�Mu�ller circles and the hyperbolae of Hillebrand, respectively. This psychophysical relationship is not most elegantly formulated by the Cartesian coordinates of the physical space, where the rotation centers of the eyes are located symmetrically to the origin on the y-axis and the stimuli are the points lying in the horizontal positive half plane x>0. Instead, Luneburg (1947) introduced bipolar coordinates :, ;, which are angular coordinates describing

7. CONCLUSIONS 

In what follows, we summarize and discuss the impact of the obtained mathematical results on the generalized psychophysical theory of binocular space perception which we presented in the first four sections of the paper. The basic supposition in this theory is that the loci of perceived egocentric equidistance and perceived equidirection can be characterized within a conjoint measurement approach. More specifically, it is assumed that the relational structure (S, P\ , P%) forms a binocular coordinate structure for which the representations (4) and (5) hold with strictly increasing functions f, g which are supposed to map I onto intervals. We determined all such f and g for which the relations P\ and P% are #-, .-, :-, and ;-shift invariant. While none of these guaranteed the linearity of f and g, pairs of different invariances did. 

Two aspects are of particular interest. First, an additional symmetry condition uniquely determined the Luneburg case (L2) (cf. (3)) among the relations of a binocular coordinate structure (S, P\ , P%) satisfying .- or :- or ;-shift invariance (#-shift invariance alone is not sufficient). Second, the connection of the presented generalized theory to another conjoint representation, based on the formula of Blank (1978) (here Eq. (2)) has been clarified. 

Equations (i) or (i$) formulate the constraints imposed on the functions f, g if P\ or P% , respectively, are #-shift invariant. We refer to the situation where both hold by saying that (S, P\ , P%) is #-shift invariant. To put it another way, the solutions of (i) or of (i$) characterize the case in which #-shifts (i.e., the transformations (:, ;) [ (:+{, ;&{)) constitute automorphisms of the binocular coordinate structure (S, P\ , P%). As already mentioned, the solutions f, g of both equations are identical under the given conditions and thus Theorem 1 provides the possible functional form of the representation in this case. The functions f, g are either linear or exponential of the type given in (I2). Analogously, Theorem 2 deals with the effect of assuming (S, P\ , P%) to be .-shift invariant, where besides the linear case a different type of exponential solution arises (see (II2)). The restrictions resulting from the invariances with respect to :-shifts and ;-shifts are given in Theorem 3. 

98 

We postpone the discussion of the exponential solutions and consider first the linear case, which is characterized by stating that the binocular coordinate structure (S, P\ , P%) is #-shift invariant and .-shift invariant (see Corollary 1) or, alternatively, :-shift invariant and ;-shift invariant (see Theorem 3 and the subsequent remarks). Since the conjoint measurement approach results in an interval scale representation, i.e., f and g in (4) and (5) are unique up to affine transformations (cf. Krantz et al., 1971), the ratio of the slope constants of f and g is meaningful. It may be interpreted as the factor by which the magnification of the lenses of the two eyes differ in optical aniseikonia. Its main effect on the loci of perceived equidistance and equidirection, which is exemplified in Fig. 4, consists in an asymmetry with respect to the x-axis that has frequently been observed empirically. 

All the considered invariance properties are necessary conditions for the Luneburg case but, together with the conditions assuring representations (4) and (5) (see Heller, 1997), they do not suffice to characterize representation (3). There have to be additional constraints imposed, like the symmetry property (36) (cf. (37)). As a consequence of the results of Subsection 5.3, a symmetric binocular coordinate structure (S, P\ , P%) can be represented according to (3) if it is .-shift invariant, or :-shift invariant, or ;-shift invariant. Since this characterization is completely in terms of qualitative properties of P\ and P% , the above statement provides alternative versions of the desired representation theorem. 

Notice that symmetry in particular eliminates the exponential solution (II2) as a generally valid functional form ((II2) may, however, provide a valid approximation of some particular data sets). Although the data exhibit some more or less marked asymmetry, any psychologically plausible solution should be able to capture the symmetric case. On the other hand, for the second, exponential, solution (I2) of Eqs. (i) and (i$), originating from #-shift invariances, symmetry implies only a=c. If fitted to the data of Foley (1966), however, the constant C appearing in the exponential function tends to be very small, so that, since e[C:] is approximately equal to 1+C:, the resulting predictions cannot be distinguished from those obtained in the linear case (I1). The representations (4) and (5) with linear f, g (not necessarily equal slopes), that is, (I1)=(II1), approximate the data of Foley (1966) and others better than (3) but there still remain systematic deviations for most of the observers. Figure 5 shows the predictions of the locus of perceived equidistance derived from (4) with linear f, g for two typical data sets. Subject 1 shows practically no asymmetries with respect to the x-axis, so that nearly equal slope constants for f and g are obtained, and the predictions are thus close to the Vieth�Mu�ller circle. The tendency toward physical equidistance that has been found in most of the empirical studies cannot be accounted for. However, the resulting skewed curves closely predict the data of Subject 10, which means that in this case aniseikonia provides a complete explanation of the locus of perceived equidistance. 

Thus there is room for further improvement, which might be achieved by finding invariance properties fitting the data even better than the :-, ;-, and .-shift invariances (and certainly better than the #-shift invariances). This may be the subject of further work.
