JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 27, 406-438 (1983)  Some Deterministic Models of Concept Identification  KAM PUI CHOW AND JOHN W. COTTON  University of California, Santa Barbara  It is shown that deterministic models can compete effectively with stochastic models in summarizing concept identification  behavior. Three groups of deterministic models are examined. Examination of individual learners' trial by trial behavior in a  concept experiment shows: (1) One person exhibited behavior consistent with a Hypothesis Permutation (HP) model despite being  a nonlearner who showed no evidence of improvement over a period of 24 trials. However, when all 50 persons studied in each of  two treatment groups were examined, only 22 members of one group and 10 of the other showed no inconsistencies with  deterministic local consistency assumptions. (2) Certain deterministic computer programs could find at least one satisfactory  order for predicting all responses by 18 of the 22 consistent solvers and 6 of the 10 consistent solvers, respectively, in the  two groups just mentioned. For these 24 persons, then, a less restrictive deterministic model is adequate than for the others.  (3) Those 38 original members of the first treatment group who met a stringent learning criterion were compared with respect  to predictions generated by stochastic and mathematized deterministic models. One deterministic model (RSS-U 9-state) is in  some respects the best of the models examined, but this success is a partial reflection of estimating eight parameters from  the data.  Prodded by deterministic theories in artificial intelligence, Cotton (1982) suggested ways to model concept identification  data without the assumption of random processes. Deterministic models are obviously preferable if they possess predictive  power and make fewer explanatory assumptions. The present paper shows that there are deterministic models consistent with  certain concept identification data previously thought to require probabilistic processes. In this regard, Tumblin and Gholson  (1981) have pointed out that, although probabilistic models of concept identification have been dominant in psychology, early  studies with humans (Bruner, Goodnow, & Austin, 1956) and much research with children emphasize systematic concept behavior  with little or no reference to random processes.  The class of concept identification models most relevant to the deterministic models of the present paper is called local  consistency theory (Trabasso & Bower, 1966, p. 168; Gregg & Simon, 1967, pp. 252-254 with Axiom S5Z; Trabasso & Bower, 1968,  pp. 52-57; Simon & Newell, 1974, pp. 106-113; Millward & Wickens, 1974, especially p. 53 and Axiom S2b, p. 56; Cotton 1974,  pp. 365-366). Our primary present interest is in the model of Gregg and Simon, simply restated by Simon and Newell, and in its  variant based on Restle (1962) and discussed by Cotton. The body of this article now begins, with a discussion of some deterministic models which nonetheless imply interindividual  variability in behavior by persons receiving identical treatment but differing in the priorities they give to different  hypotheses. Further variability of behavior is introduced by assuming specific variations in stimuli presented.  One way to think of the development below is to say that earlier theories assumed random generation of hypotheses whereas the  theories to be presented now assume a preordained ordering of hypotheses for each individual. Both kinds of theories appear to  generate random behavior prior to solution of the task. However, a reviewer of this article pointed out that a deterministic  model implicitly assumes that a very good CAT scan of the brain could show this preordained order of hypothese before a series  of randomly selected hypotheses could be thought to have been generated in response to prior errors. This analogy may be  helpful to the reader in suggesting some ultimate means of discriminating between two superficially different classes of  models which will be seen below to have very similar and sometimes identical behavioral consequences.  A crucial term below is stack, by which is meant a systematic pile or heap such as a stack of trays in a cafeteria, with one  tray at the bottom, another directly on top of it, etc., until the last tray is found directly on top of its predecessor. The  preordained order of hypotheses mentioned above will be called a stack of hypotheses even though there is no direct evidence  of a physical stack of hypotheses in the human brain.  410 CHOW AND COTTON  MODELS ALLOWING VARIED BEHAVIOR BY PERSONS WHETHER OR NOT TREATED IDENTICALLY  1. Random Stimulus Sequence Model with Unknown Stack Order (RSS-U Model)  This model is a basic deterministic model of learning for a set of individuals with a finite number of affirmative hypotheses  and with the stimulus for any trial selected randomly. What might be called a pure RSS model (See Appendix B) would apply to  an individual who started to learn a certain concept with the correct hypothesis stored in a certain position of a stack of  known, fixed order. Because any ordinary group of experimental subjects would be presumed to come to the experiment with  different response propensities, we need the RSS model for each person to have a unique ordering of the K pairs of hypotheses  being considered. The following are the assumptions of the RSS-U model, combining all individuals conforming to the RSS model  (1) There are 2K affirmative hypotheses (H's) in a person's stack, each represented once, where K is the number of binary  dimensions. For each dimension except Dimension 0 for the correct hypothesis, the two opposite hypotheses appear adjacent to  each other in the stack. This adjacency requirement will be eliminated later when the hypothesis permutation (HP) model is  presented.  (2) The correct hypothesis H, is at an unknown position in the stack. It will be convenient to assign it to Dimension 0 and  thus sometimes to refer to HC as Ho.  (3) When any concept stimulus is presented, the learner responds on the basis of the hypothesis currently at the top of the  stack. If that hypothesis is confirmed for the present trial, it maintains its position. Otherwise, it is removed from the  stack and is not used to control the response to the next concept stimulus. Note that this assumption implies that sampling  with replacement does not occur. Further note that the opposite of the correct hypothesis, H' =Ho, is never confirmed by any  feedback and so will never be held, except possibly on a first (blind) guess in an experiment, a possibility which will be  excluded later by invoking the notion of an example trial with the response ignored or nonexistent.  (4) An ordering such as: H,H2H,H,H, for K=3 can be so split that one person considers H0H'HZH;H, in right to left order and  another person considers 11, 112 in the same order, in effect having no H, in the stack or storing it on the left of the  correct hypothesis Ho. The reason for this split is to require four errors for the first person and three errors for the  second. Without this assumption i, the number of errors, would always be even.  To facilitate comparison with stochastic models, we need a system of state vectors, Pfor any Trial n, to apply within the  RSS-U model. Let Eo be the state in which no more errors will occur. Alternatively, this state is called the learned (L)  state. Then the person must hold Ho. In general, E; is the state in which i more errors will occur. If the K! possible orderings of dimensions in the stack are each equally likely, the initial vector will have a value  of 1/2K for each state except the zero error state, which will have a probability of 1/K.  (5) The stimulus presentation axiom P, given earlier applies here. Since this is an assumption about experimental procedure,  it can be changed and new prediction .generated without changing any psychological assumptions in the RSS-U model.  (6) An example stimulus and feedback cause the subject (or learner) to choose a locally consistent response on the next trial,  i.e., she will show a stimulus-response pairing consistent with at least one of the K hypotheses confirmed on the example  trial. As stated in assumption (3), the topmost position in the hypothesis stack is the one first considered by the subject  and successively lower ones are considered as needed, making the hypothesis chosen immediately after the example trial be the  topmost locally consistent hypothesis.  Since stacks are displayed horizontally in this article and hypotheses are considered in right to left order, one should think  of the right side of a display as its top. The reason for this convention is that it produces parallelism with the  conventional representation of Markov processes in psychology: The final (learned) state is on the far left of each vector  giving probabilities of being in a specific state; the learned state is the last one entered. For example, in Eq. (3), a  transition matrix is defined with the property that a person beginning an experiment in the rightmost state of the matrix and  also of its associated vectors will always either move one state to the left or stay in the current state on a given trial,  progressing from state to state until the leftmost state is reached. Similarly, our representation of learning processes as  movements through a stack in search of a hypothesis consistent with the immediately previous feedback represent the learner as  trying the hypothesis at the far right (or top) of the stack, discarding it if it fails, then trying the hypothesis just to  the left (or toward the bottom) of the original one, etc., until a series of trials has led the learner to employ the  hypothesis that will succeed on all trials of the current task.  (7) The learner presented with a stimulus on tresponds in accordance with the H for that trial. If correct, she uses that H  on the next trial. Otherwise, she chooses the opposite hypothesis if available. If the opposite hypothesis is not available,  the next hypothesis in the stack is chosen. Hypothesis Permutation Model (HP Model)  In the RSS models of the previous section, we only permute hypothesis pairs for the stimulus dimensions, i.e., in a  K-dimensional model we have K! different permutations. If we allow all the hypotheses to be permuted, we shall have (2K)!  different permutations since each dimension has 2 different possible hypotheses, including the one hypothesis that always  fails. This is the so-called hypothesis permutation (HP) model, which is otherwise identical to the RSS-U model. All people  having the same hypotheses in the same order will give the same response sequence to the same stimulus sequence. If the stack  order is known, the response sequence can be predicted perfectly. Note that when appropriate permutations are omitted, the HP  model becomes the RSS-U model again. Note that an HP deterministic model is in effect a local consistency model but one in  which resampling is alleged not to be random but rather to follow some (possibly unknown) prior hypothesis ordering held by an  individual person being studied.  2. EMPIRICAL EVALUATION OF DETERMINISTIC MODELS  Direct Identification of Hypotheses  In earlier research, identification of hypotheses has primarily employed sequences of orthogonal blank trials (Levine, 1969),  requests for display of values on one or more specific dimensions out of all those available (Millward Spoehr, 1973), or  ancillary introspective data (Berger, 1974; Cotton, 1975; Indow & Suzuki, 1972, 1973). To the extent that concept  identification studies support an assumption that more than one hypothesis is considered at a time, a question evaluated in  Brown's  418 CHOW AND COTTON  (1974) review of the general literature in this field, it seems inappropriate to consider the current deterministic computer  models to have potential empirical adequacy. Furthermore, Levine's (1969, p. 118) demonstration that only about 85% of  subjects perform according to local consistency predictions immediately after the first error trial of a concept  identification task also calls deterministic models into question; Levine's local consistency data after later error trials  are even worse. Notice, however, that Levine's (1969, p. 116) failure to obtain perfect processing (reduction in size of the  tenable hypothesis set as fast as cumulative logical analysis of previous trials would allow) is not necessarily inconsistent  with the predictions of a deterministic model with limited memory.  Despite the foregoing reservations, we have tried to identity the hypothesis held on each trial for each subject of the Cotton  (1971) Experiment 2, previously analyzed in the spirit of the Bower and Trabasso (1964) model. COMPARISON OF SAMPLING METHODS IN DETERMINISTIC AND STOCHASTIC MODELS  Further theoretical results can best be presented now when relevant empirical evidence is available.  Finite models. The two primary deterministic models, RSS-U and HP, clearly employ local consistency theory and thus assume  sampling without immediate replacement, a theory which, for stochastic models, has failed to give good fits to early empirical  data (Gregg & Simon, 1967, Table 2). Later evidence on this point has been mixed (Brown, 1974, p. 782). It seems reasonable to  assume that failure of local consistency theory or of _other theories assuming sampling without replacement would carry over  to cases of deterministic modeling with finite numbers of hypotheses and each hypothesis represented only once-line 6 of  numerical entries in Table 3 (showing poor performance of the RSS-U 5-state model) may be interpreted as confirming this  informal prediction.  A second reason for the poor performance in Table 3 of the RSS-U 5-state model may be that Assumption 2 for that model implies  stack adjacency of the two hypotheses for each dimension. This may be restated psychologically to mean that subjects reverse  their hypotheses whenever the reverse of the current hypothesis has not been previously excluded. Studies of reversal learning  in Bower and Trabasso (1964), Trabasso and Bower (1966, 1968) found no evidence for such automatic  424 CHOW AND COTTON  hypothesis reversal; Trabasso and Bower (1968, p.49) explicitly reject this possibility, advocating instead "local consistency  with local nonreplacement of the disconfirmed dimension."  Although Millward and Wickens (1974) outline very general cases and Trabasso and Bower (1968) provide a structure in which  appropriate analysis could be performed, earlier reports have not given detailed consideration of the effects of sampling one  element at a time from a pool of hypotheses in which some hypotheses are represented more than once. Line 7 of Table 3  indicates that good fits can be attained with such assumptions in a deterministic model (RSS-U 9 state), provided that all  initial vector elements are directly estimated from the data. Hence it appears that a finite deterministic model need not  necessarily fail because of sampling without replacement or pairing of opposite hypotheses.  Infinite models. The Appendix D proof that the RSS model for an infinite number of hypotheses and a certain initial vector is  mathematically equivalent to one version of the Bower and Trabasso model may be surprising in view of the fact that sampling  without replacement and pairing of opposite hypotheses are assumed by the former but not by the latter model. If pairing has  no effect in the infinite case, this means that the infinite HP model should also be mathematically equivalent to the Bower  and Trabasso model under appropriate circumstances. Such an equivalence is easily proved by showing (or assuming) identity of  corresponding initial vectors and transition matrices.  Equation 7 for the infinite RSS model defines a transition matrix of the same form as Eq. 3 for the RSS-U and HP models if we  allow the latter to have the number, 2K-2, of possible errors tend to infinity. Since (DI) defines the initial vector assumed  for the infinite RSS model to be equivalent to that for the Bower-Trabasso model, it is sufficient to assume that it holds for  the infinite HP model as well. But the infinite RSS and infinite HP models now have identical initial vectors and transition  matrices, so they make identical predictions in all cases for which both can be applied. Since the HP model does not assume  pairing of hypotheses, this shows that in the limit this pairing has no effect upon the mathematical predictions of these  deterministic models.  A slight generalization of the HP model. Heretofore, this model has assumed K dimensions and 2K hypotheses. For the finite  case, the same transition matrix given in (3) holds if there are 2K hypotheses but less than K dimensions because some  hypotheses occur more than once in the stack. More importantly, an infinite HP model with some or all hypotheses appearing  more than once and with a finite number of dimensions will have the same transition matrix (7) as for the infinite RSS model,  thus implying equivalence to the Bower and Trabasso model with c used as in (D 1) and with p = z . It may now be noted that a  strict interpretation of the infinite RSS model means that it may never be applied to a real life situation because an  infinite number of dimensions is impossible to use in the characterization of such a situation. This difficulty is removed  once we deal with the generalized infinite HP model, for now the number of dimensions can be finite and pairing of opposite  DETERMINISTIC MODELS OF CONCEPT IDENTIFICATION 425  hypothese need not be assumed and yet the overall model has been proved equivalent to the Bower and Trabasso model with  parameter values as stated above. This makes the generalized infinite HP model one of the most plausible deterministic models  of the article.  Conclusion. Finally, let us talk about how significant deterministic models are. Chaitin (1975, p. 49) discussed how to define  randomness formally, proposing that "a random series of digits is one whose complexity is approximately equal to its size in  bits." He also proved that given a number (series of digits), Gddel's incompleteness theorem implies that it is impossible to  prove that that particular series of digits satisfies this definition of randomness. Despite possible problems arising from  Chaitin's results, for a long time people have tried to apply probabilistic models to experimental data. Even in modern  physics, quantum mechanics gave up the classical deterministic properties of motion of a particle (its position and velocity)  and put in some kind of uncertainty. Einstein (1950, p. 110), who strongly preferred deterministic hypotheses in physics,  found himself forced to conclude: "Thus, it is probably out of the question that any future knowledge can compel physics again  to relinquish our present statistical theoretical foundation in favor of a deterministic one which would deal directly with  physical reality."  Bohm (1957) is one of the few remaining physicists who reject a probabilistic interpretation of quantum mechanics in favor of  a deterministic position (the assumption that so-called hidden variables of a deterministic sort operate to produce the  statistical phenomena observed in that field). Some form of Bohm's position may be compatible with present knowledge about the  physical world but, like the current psychological analysis, lacks a compelling crucial test of its specific assumptions.  In a further discussion of hidden variables in physics, Jauch (1973, pp. 63-65) has proposed that something may look random  unless one has developed through a process of abstraction a kind of filter that extracts a simple structure behind the  randomness. One may suggest that reality is itself nothing but the manifestation of one very complicated formal deterministic  system. If true, this means that everything operates deterministically but there exist a huge number of variables which are  out of our observation and control. Of course, whether the universe operates deterministically or not is still an open  question. Rohrlich (1983) has reviewed the current state of hidden variable theory, calling attention to recent empirical  disproofs of a special case, local hidden variables theory (Aspect, Grangier, & Roger, 1982; Aspect, Delibard, & Roger, 1982).  How much can this reality be imitated deterministically? What we proposed above is a set of deterministic models which fit  some experimental data, but whose results are usually compatible also with some probabilistic model. Everything appears to be  random until we establish a deterministic model to read it. We belive that there are always or almost always two different  views to look at experimental data (reality), deterministically and probabilistically.  426 CHOW AND COTTON  APPENDIX A A COMPARISON OF LOCAL CONSISTENCY MODELS SPECIALIZED FROM THE GENERAL MODELS OF RESTLE (1962) AND BOWER AND  TRABASSO (1964)  This appendix is partially intended to justify Gregg and Simon's (1967) conclusion that a local consistency assumption added  to the original Bower and Trabasso (1964) model implies a certain value for the expected number of errors, E(T), under stated  symmetry conditions.
