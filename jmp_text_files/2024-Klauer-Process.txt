Multinomial processing tree models (MPT models;  Riefer & Batchelder, 1988 ) account for the observed frequencies of categorical responses in terms of a finite number of processes arranged serially in a processing tree. They are tailored to a specific experimental paradigm and aim at modeling the major cognitive processes involved in the generation of the responses. MPT models have seen a wide range of applications in diverse areas of psychology (for reviews see  Batchelder & Riefer, 1999; Erdfelder et al., 2009; Hütter & Klauer, 2016 ). The fruitfulness of the MPT modeling framework is attested to by the fact that new models are regularly proposed (e.g.,  Chechile & Pintea, 2021; Gawronski et al., 2016 ) and new methodological tools developed (e.g.,  Klauer et al., 2015; Matzke et al., 2015; Wu et al., 2010 ), expanding the empirical scope, theoretical validity, and practical applicability of such models. Considerable research effort has recently been devoted to modeling approaches that bring together MPT models and process-oriented response-time modeling ( Hartmann et al., 2020; Hartmann & Klauer, 2020; Heck & Erdfelder, 2016; Heck et al., 2018; Klauer & Kellen, 2018 ).

A classical example of a simple MPT model is the  two-high-threshold model  (2HT) for recognition memory ( Snodgrass & Corwin, 1988 ) illustrated in  Fig. 1 . In a basic recognition-memory experiment, participants study a list of items in a learning phase. In a subsequent test phase, they are shown a succession of studied items and new items and their task is to decide for each item whether it is a studied or a new item. As exemplified for the 2HT in  Fig. 1 , MPT models often consist of several trees, which are typically associated with different types of trials. They model categorical data as a mixture of latent states, more precisely of probabilistic state-to-response mappings. The edges of the trees correspond to process outcomes and the interior nodes to mental states.

A number of recent methodological developments have aimed at integrating response-time data into MPT analyses—an endeavor that was pioneered by  Hu (2001) . In an approach proposed by  Heck and Erdfelder  ( 2016 , see also  Heck & Erdfelder, 2017 ), response categories are split into bins based on the speed of responses. A new branching at the end of each path of the MPT model then distributes responses generated along that path into the different bins. New parameters are added to the edges of the new branchings that can be estimated when identified. Their values provide information about the response-time distributions associated with the different processing paths.

A related approach has been proposed by  Heck et al. (2018) . Like in  Heck and Erdfelder (2016) , a different response-time distribution is estimated for each path, but unlike in  Heck and Erdfelder (2016) , the pathwise response-time distributions are constrained to stem from a specified parametric family of distribution (e.g., they must all be normal distributions). Both approaches are general in that they can in principle be applied to any model out of the large class of MPT models. The literature also contains several examples in which specific discrete-state models have been extended to account not only for choices, but also for reaction-time distributions (e.g.,  Donkin et al., 2013; Starns, 2018 ).

Here, we focus on another general approach proposed by  Klauer & Kellen  ( 2018 , see also  Hartmann et al., 2020; Hartmann & Klauer, 2020 ) to incorporate reaction times, termed RT-MPT. Like  Heck et al. ’s ( 2018 ) approach, it relies on distributional assumptions, but also aims to account for and estimate process-completion time distributions rather than only pathwise response-time distributions. In addition, like many evidence-accumulation models (e.g.,  Brown & Heathcote, 2008; Ratcliff, 1978; Usher & McClelland, 2001 ) it accommodates motor and encoding times, henceforth referred to briefly as motor times.

In a nutshell, a RT-MPT model assumes exponential distributions for process-completion times with different rate parameters for the different possible process outcomes. RT-MPT thus adopts a serial interpretation of the processing paths as describing a succession or at least a cascade of processing steps ( McClelland, 1979; Sternberg & Backus, 2015 ). In this latter respect, RT-MPT builds on the approach by  Hu (2001)  and follows the intuitions of many modelers in formulating MPT models.

As discussed by  Klauer and Kellen (2018) , integrating response latencies in this fashion brings a number of advantages. One of these advantages is that the use of RT-MPT makes it possible to discriminate between MPT models that differ in the order of processes as they occur in the tree, but are equivalent in terms of their predictions for response probabilities. For example, in  Fig. 3 , the 2HT is shown in two variants that are equivalent when only response frequencies, but not response latencies are considered; the detect-guess variant (also shown in  Fig. 1 ) and a default-interventionist variant, in which guessing precedes detection processes. Both variants differ in their predictions for response-time distributions, which makes it possible to adjudicate between them on the basis of observed data. Another related advantage is that unidentified MPT models will often become identifiable if the data are enriched by response latencies. These and other advantages come at the cost of specific auxiliary distributional assumptions regarding response-time distributions. This invites a number of criticisms, regarding (1) the suitability of the exponential distribution as a model of completion times for mental processes, (2) the absence of a simple mechanism devoted to accounting for speed-accuracy trade-offs, and (3) the role of the auxiliary distributional assumptions in substantive conclusions.

This means that “no matter how much time has elapsed, as long as processing is not yet complete, the expected remaining time needed to complete it is unchanged” ( Sternberg, 2018 , p. 106). This runs counter the idea that progress towards process completion should gradually increase as processing proceeds.

Second, the RT-MPT model has no simple mechanism devoted to accounting for speed-accuracy trade-offs. According to  Starns (2018) , “one critical test for any RT model is its ability to accommodate the speed–accuracy trade-off, the ubiquitous finding that encouraging people to make quicker decisions decreases the probability that they will make correct decisions. Speed-accuracy manipulations have played a large role in testing RT models, and correctly accounting for the speed–accuracy trade-off is a critical measurement property of a model”. (p. 2406; see also  Pleskac & Busemeyer, 2010 , Hurdle 1 in their  Table 1 ). Diffusion models ( Ratcliff, 1978 ) and related evidence-accumulation models (e.g.,  Brown & Heathcote, 2008 ) account for speed-accuracy trade-off in terms of threshold separation regulating the amount of evidence that must accumulate before a decision is made. Large threshold separation implies accurate, but slow decisions, whereas small threshold separation implies less accurate, but fast decisions. To be sure, RT-MPT models can accommodate data with manipulations of, or variation in, speed-accuracy trade-off (see also  Heck & Erdfelder, 2020 ), but there is no single simple mechanism that would enforce this negative correlation between speed and accuracy.

Third, the auxiliary distributional assumptions (exponential process-completion times and truncated normal for motor times) render a good fit of the model interesting inasmuch as it is the ensemble of assumptions (MPT model assumptions and auxiliary distributional assumptions) that, in the case of good fit, provides a satisfactory account of the data. A bad fit, on the other hand, is difficult to interpret: Does it reflect issues with the MPT model or does it go back to inappropriate auxiliary assumptions or both? Even for a good fit, the question remains to what extent substantive conclusions drawn on the basis of the analysis (e.g., that one process is faster than another process) are robust under alternative auxiliary assumptions; see  Jones and Dzhafarov (2014)  for a related discussion.

For such reasons, we developed a new version of RT-MPT that draws on alternative distributional assumptions. In this version, the outcomes and process-completion times for each outcome of a given process are jointly modeled by a diffusion model with a minimal set of parameters. In this model, observed response latencies are sums of first-passage times of processes encountered along the processing path that is followed plus a motor time. This development goes some way in addressing the above criticisms.

First, the first-passage time distributions that come out of the diffusion model are generally considered more realistic distributions for process-completion times than exponential distributions for a number of conceptual and empirical reasons. For example, unlike exponential distributions, they come with a neurophysiologically plausible interpretation, do not exhibit the questionable memoryless property, and make it possible to account for non-monotonic hazard rates of single-path response times (see  Appendix C ). Second, there is a mechanism accounting for speed-accuracy trade-off, namely the same as that for the diffusion model and similar evidence-accumulation models in terms of settings of the threshold separation. Third, being able to fit the same MPT model structure with a quite different set of auxiliary distributional assumptions allows one to assess the dependence of model fit on, and the robustness of substantive conclusions against, different auxiliary assumptions.

Like the exponential version, the diffusion-model version of RT-MPT is hierarchical with random effects for participants, which is advantageous in two different ways: ”First, it enables models to accommodate the substantial heterogeneity that usually stems from individual differences. These differences can be expressed in many ways such as accuracy, response bias, response speed, and speed-accuracy trade-offs, among others. Second, each participant’s parameter estimates are thereby informed by the other participants’ estimates, which is particularly valuable when the data per individual are sparse, and group-level estimates generalize across them ( Katahira, 2016; Klauer, 2010 )”. ( Klauer & Kellen, 2018 , p. 113).

The resulting models are grounded in and extend the large diffusion-model literature. For example, the distribution of response latencies generated by a minimal MPT model with only one binary branching combine a motor time and a first-passage time like in a simple diffusion model with four parameters as recommended by  Dutilh et al. (2019)  and  Lerche and Voss (2016)  although the RT-MPT also implements trial-by-trial variability in the motor time. More complex MPT models add structure in terms of a processing architecture assumed to underlie responses and response latencies.

Depending upon the area of application and the kind of process considered, the diffusion-model parameters estimated for a given process may lend themselves to substantive interpretations (e.g., drift rate and threshold parameter of a detect process may be tied to the speed of evidence accumulation and to the level of caution exerted, respectively, in making the detect decision). In other applications, the diffusion model may more appropriately be considered a descriptive model providing economic approximations of realistic process-completion time distributions in terms of a few parameters (e.g., it may be more difficult to interpret the drift rate estimated for a guessing process in psychological terms).

Notation and some of the following technical descriptions are adapted from  Klauer and Kellen (2018) . As already mentioned, each processing path represents a succession of processing stages for which process-completion times add up ( Sternberg & Backus, 2015 ). An additional additive component, the motor time, summarizes encoding and response-execution times. The diffusion processes attached to the nodes in the tree are assumed to be stochastically independent within each person (i.e., conditional independence is assumed;  Province & Rouder, 2012 ), and the diffusion processes of the same process are governed by the same person-specific parameters wherever that process occurs in the processing tree. Model estimation takes a hierarchical Bayesian approach, and the model therefore comprises a person-level model, prior (i.e., population-level) distributions, and hyperprior distributions.

MPT models usually consist of several trees. For example, in the 2HT, there are two trees, one for trials involving studied items, and one for trials involving new items. Each tree has internal nodes, referred to simply as nodes in the following, and leaves. The leaves correspond to observable response categories, which are mapped on actual responses such as left or right keypresses. For example, the “old” response categories of the 2HT may be mapped on the left response key, and the “new” response categories on the right response key (see  Fig. 1 ). Response categories for different trees are, however, considered different categories even if they give rise to the same response. For example, the category “old” is also indexed by the tree from which it stems and thus, to be precise, there are four response categories in the 2HT. Here, we consider only binary MPT models in which each node has two children. But note that non-binary MPT models can always be transformed into binary MPT models ( Hu & Batchelder, 1994 ).

RT-MPT modeling can in principle be applied to any of the many MPT paradigms that have been proposed ( Erdfelder et al., 2009; Hütter & Klauer, 2016 ). The only requirement is that response latencies can be meaningfully recorded in the paradigm. Unlike some of the traditional approaches to response-time modeling, RT-MPT models are therefore not restricted to tasks with two choices, but can handle paradigms with multiple response categories. However, where a multinomial model has been proposed for a two-choice task, RT-MPT models provide an extension of classical analyses in terms of diffusion-model analyses ( Ratcliff & Rouder, 1998 ) as exemplified above. It is useful to consider such cases in some more detail.

In such cases, diffusion models and RT-MPT models provide accounts that differ in breadth and depth. RT-MPT models assume that several processes are involved in the paradigm and provide accounts of how the processes interact in generating observed responses and response latencies. Diffusion models on the other hand assume that responses and response latencies are driven by one underlying process of evidence accumulation (setting aside encoding and motor processes that both model classes treat only summarily). In contrast, RT-MPT models add a structured processing architecture and thereby provide accounts of more  breadth  than diffusion models.

The exponential RT-MPT models, however, do not model the postulated processes in any more  depth  than by characterizing in a relatively direct fashion their contribution to outcome frequencies and latencies. In contrast, diffusion models provide deeper accounts of the postulated evidence-accumulation process: Threshold parameters account for speed-accuracy trade-offs in a natural fashion; starting points account for decision biases and correlations between response frequency and response speed induced by variations in them; drift rates account for the efficiency of evidence accumulation and correlations between response accuracy and response speed induced by variations in them. Diffusion RT-MPT models strive to combine the breadth of exponential RT-MPT models with the depth of diffusion-model analyses.

They do so by importing the diffusion model’s elegance in accounting for speed-accuracy trade-offs and decision biases via their diffusion-model kernels. For mental processes that can be conceptualized as evidence-accumulation processes, drift-rate parameters moreover have a natural interpretation in terms of the speed of evidence accumulation. For processes (such as perhaps guessing processes), for which this is not the case, the diffusion-model kernel still provides parsimonious descriptions of realistic process-completion time distributions by means of a few parameters.

Diffusion-model RT-MPTs thereby counter several critiques of exponential RT-MPTs: They have a simple mechanism for accommodating speed-accuracy trade-offs ( Pleskac & Busemeyer, 2010; Starns, 2018 ) and they use a more realistic model of process-completion times than the exponential distribution ( Burbeck & Luce, 1982; Matzke & Wagenmakers, 2009; Sternberg, 2018 ; see also  Appendix C ).

The more realistic process-completion time distributions implemented in diffusion RT-MPT models may account for the fact that the contribution of motor times was consistently smaller in applications than for the exponential RT-MPT models. This was expressed both in terms of smaller absolute estimates of mean motor times as well as in smaller individual variances for the motor-time component. In other words, the diffusion RT-MPT models attribute larger amount of the observed response latencies and more of the variance in them to process-related contributions rather than to encoding and motor contributions.

Another aspect of RT-MPT models illustrated by the applications is that including response latencies makes some models identified that are not identified when the data are restricted to response frequencies. Relatedly, RT-MPT models allow one to adjudicate between models that differ in terms of their processing architecture, but are equivalent in their predictions for response frequencies. The DG and DI versions of the 2HT from  Fig. 3  served as examples in the current paper.

These and other advantages of diffusion and exponential RT-MPT models come at the cost of strong auxiliary assumptions about the distributions of process-completion times and motor times. As explained by  Van Zandt (2002) , a complete model of response-time distributions needs such assumptions where limited data are available per participant. But the need for such assumptions raises the question to what extent fit or misfit of a model and substantive conclusions drawn from model fits and parameter estimates reflect auxiliary assumptions versus core assumptions about the architecture of processing embodied by the MPT model. One motivation for developing the diffusion RT-MPT model class was to have a means to fit RT-MPT models with two quite different sets of auxiliary assumptions, one based on exponential distributions, the other one based on first-passage time distributions coming out of the diffusion model.

Researchers who do not want to commit to either set of auxiliary assumptions will be more confident in conclusions that are robust against the set of auxiliary assumptions that is adopted and thus in conclusions that are shared by both exponential and diffusion RT-MPT analyses of the same data. For example, the experimental manipulations of item strength and baserate mostly influenced only detection processes and guessing processes, respectively, whether analyzed by means of exponential RT-MPT ( Klauer & Kellen, 2018 ) or diffusion RT-MPT. On the other hand, the preference for the DG versus DI architecture hinged on the set of auxiliary assumptions adopted: The DI architecture was preferred in the exponential RT-MPT analysis, whereas the DG architecture prevailed in the diffusion RT-MPT analysis. Taking a stance on the question of architecture thus entails committing to one or the other set of auxiliary assumptions.

The simulation-based calibration suggests that we succeeded in implementing the complex algorithm for fitting diffusion RT-MPT models correctly. The recovery study suggests that group-level parameters for thresholds and relative starting points will be well recovered even in relatively small datasets, whereas drift rates are estimated with more uncertainty. Recovery of standard deviations of person-specific parameters was surprisingly good despite the small simulated number of persons, whereas only the rank order, but not the absolute values of correlations between them could be estimated accurately in this situation. Thus, if the research interest focuses on correlations between diffusion-model parameters much larger datasets are probably required.

The current developments are available in the form of an already existing R package called  rtmpt  ( Hartmann et al., 2020; Hartmann & Klauer, 2020 ;  rtmpt ) where we added functions to fit models of the diffusion RT-MPT model family next to the exponential RT-MPT model family. 
                            11 
                         
                         
                            11 
                            The new functions are implemented in  rtmpt  version  2.0-0  or higher. 
                          Fitting a diffusion-model RT-MPT requires more time than fitting an exponential RT-MPT model; for the applications above, computation times on a standard PC with four cores ranged between 1 and 14 days per model fit. In our experience, convergence of the algorithm is aided by using the option available in the R package to base initial parameter values on maximum-likelihood estimates of the parameters for each person rather than on random values. But computing the maximum-likelihood estimates requires computation of the model’s likelihood function which in turn requires numerical multidimensional integration for each and every response in the data, which is very costly in terms of computation time. The same is true for the output option to compute and store trialwise likelihood values as needed for computing model selection indices such as DIC, WAIC, and LOO-IC.

The three main steps for fitting an exponential ( ertmpt ) or a diffusion RT-MPT model ( drtmpt ) are (1) defining a model file with the underlying MPT structure and use the  to_ertmpt_model() / to_drtmpt_model()  function to store it as a model object, (2) use  to_ertmpt_data() / to_drtmpt_data()  and the model object to transform a data file or object to a data object that is compatible with the model, and (3) use  fit_ertmpt() / fit_drtmpt()  with the model and data object to fit an exponential or diffusion RT-MPT model, respectively.

The study of reaction times has a long history in psychology and is one of the major approaches to studying mental processes ( Luce, 1986; Townsend & Ashby, 1983; Van Zandt, 2002 ). Although not as ubiquitous as reaction-time studies, multinomial processing tree models have provided another useful avenue to the study of mental processes in many fields of psychological inquiry. Their applications range from the study of memory and cognitive psychology ( Erdfelder et al., 2009 ) to social psychology ( Hütter & Klauer, 2016 ), from developmental psychology (e.g.,  Fazio & Sherry, 2020 ) to neuropsychology (e.g.,  Amodio, 2009 ), to name just a few.

The present article has contributed to recent attempts to bring together these two strands of process-oriented inquiry. The new approach can in principle be combined with any MPT model for which reaction times can be meaningfully obtained. An R package implementing the approach ensures that it can be easily used. We hope that the current addition to the field’s toolbox will be found useful and lead to many new insights.

The hazard rate of a continuous response-time distribution “gives the likelihood that an event [(i.e., the response)] will occur in the next [infinitesimally] small interval [...] in time, given that it has not occurred before that point in time” ( Van Zandt, 2002 , p. 465). Therefore, such a representation provides “the continually reconditionalized immediate likelihood” ( Chechile, 2003 , p. 478) of a response occurring, which has advantages for visualizing certain characteristics of a response-time distribution ( Colonius, 2016; Holden et al., 2009; Luce, 1986; Van Zandt & Ratcliff, 1995 ) — especially its right tail behavior.

Although accurately estimating response-time hazard rates from empirical data comes with some practical challenges ( Van Zandt, 2002 ), it is nevertheless well-established that human response-time data often show non-monotonic hazard rates (e.g.,  Burbeck & Luce, 1982; Green & Smith, 1982; Maddox et al., 1998; Smith, 1995 ). This is interesting insofar as some response-time models, such as the ex-Gaussian model ( Hohle, 1965 ; see also, e.g.,  Heathcote et al., 1991 ), predict hazard rates to be monotonically increasing, revealing a systematic misspecification of the model (see  Schwarz, 2001 ). The same criticism applies—at least to some extent—to the exponential RT-MPT model.

To see why this is the case, note first that the theoretical hazard rate is monotonically increasing if the associated probability distribution is log-concave ( Bagnoli & Bergstrom, 2005 , Corollary 2; see also  Luce, 1986 , Theorem 1.1). Furthermore, the exponential distribution is log-concave as is the Gaussian distribution and hence, trivially, the truncated Gaussian distribution used for the motor times in exponential RT-MPT models. 
                         13 
                      
                      
                         13 
                         In fact, the exponential distribution is both log-concave  and  log-convex everywhere on its support, which results in a constant hazard rate. 
                       Importantly, log-concavity (but not log-convexity) is preserved under convolutions ( Prékopa, 1973 ). It follows that the sum of exponentially distributed random variables and a truncated Gaussian variable has a distribution with a monotonically increasing hazard rate.

Note, however, that the exponential RT-MPT allows for multiple paths of the model (potentially of different length and involving different processing stages) to terminate in the same response category. As a consequence, the response-time distribution for each response category may in fact be a weighted mixture distribution (the same holds of course also for the diffusion RT-MPT; see Eq.  (3) ). Although the response-time distribution of each path is therefore log-concave, this must  not  necessarily be true for their mixture (see e.g.,  Boyd & Vandenberghe, 2004 ).

That said, if a response category is only associated with a single path of the model, it becomes impossible for the model to predict a non-monotonic hazard rate for response times associated with that particular category. By contrast, the hazard rate of the first-passage time distribution of a diffusion model is typically found to be noticeably peaked (see, e.g.,  Ratcliff & Van Dongen, 2011; Ratcliff et al., 1999 ). Thus, it seems reasonable to conjecture that modeling individual processing stages within the RT-MPT framework as a diffusion process will enable the model to account for hazard rates of empirical response times more accurately than the exponential RT-MPT.
