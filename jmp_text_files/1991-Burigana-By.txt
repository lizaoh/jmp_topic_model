JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 35, 345-370 (1991)  Organization by Rules in Finite Sequences  LUIGI BURIGANA  University or Padova  The overall content of a given sequence divides into a structural part, which is common to sequences with the same  organization, and a metrical part, which differentiates it from structurally equivalent sequences. Of particular interest here  are regularities in sequences, as notable factors of perceptual and/or cognitive organization. Accordingly, the structural  part or structuration of a sequence is conceived as a configuration of rules, each qualifying some structural unit.  Analytically, structurations thus defined amount to sets of relations between the components of a sequence, so that in a fixed  structuration those components are to a certain extent mutually dependent and involve redundancy; a suitable measure of the  efficiency of the given structuration may be derived from the number of degrees of freedom allowed by such a relational  network. Sections 3-6 of this paper present a formal investigation of dependence relations induced by structurations, relying  mainly on a direct correspondence between this type of relations and the algebraic notions of closure-operators and  closuresystems (Moore families). Last, the suggested principles of structural appraisal of sequences are illustrated by some  examples. a',; 1991 Academic Press, Inc.  1. INTRODUCTION  If treated and described at an analytical level a finite sequence amounts simply to an ordered set of elementary components;  but in psychological processing such a sequence is generally organized into a system of separate or nested structural units.  Let us consider, by way of example, Fig. 1. Here we have, in analytical terms, a sequence of 128 vertical lines, of various  lengths and locations. However, visually this array of "stimuli" organizes into a system of well-defined objects, a "flask"  with three "glasses." The next drawing, Fig. 2, if examined term by term, would be described as a row of 19 "trees" of  different sizes. If viewed as a whole the given drawing reveals some structure: it might be noted, for instance, that the row  of trees divides into two parts, one on the left, consisting of three pairs of trees of linearly increasing size, and one on  the right, symmetrical around a small tree. Figure 3 shows a run of 24 tones (the central part of the 95th bar from the  "Prelude and Fugue in D minor" (BWV 539) by J. S. Bach). As an auditory event this tonal sequence divides neatly into four  descending subsequences, each consisting of a doubled set of three ascending tones. One of these principles (indeed a  notable one) may be stated as follows: if the terms in a specific part of a given sequence comply with some simple and easily  detectable "rule," then that part will tend to constitute a distinct structural unit, and the rule itself will define a global  property of such a unit. It has also inspired, more or less directly, some remarkable "coding theories" on perception and  cognition (Glanzer & Clark, 1962; Simon & Kotovsky, 1963; Payne, 1966; Vitz & Todd, 1969; Restle, 1970; Simon, 1972; Greeno &  Simon, 1974; Deutsch & Feroe, 1981). In particular, the so-called "theory of structural information" concerns perceptual  organization according to the given principle (Leeuwenberg, 1968, 1971, 1978; Buffart, Leeuwenberg, & Restle, 1981; Buffart &  Leeuwenberg, 1983).  Wertheimer (1923, p. 325), in commenting on the factor of "good continuation" and the tendency to "good form," points out that  "...in this connection there are also certain purely mathematical and set-theoretical problems to be discussed, especially the  problem of global properties compared with mere local regularity". ILLUSTRATIVE DETAILS ON RULES, CONSISTENCY, AND STRUCTURATIONS  The model proposed here for organization in finite sequences is of conditional type, in that it depends on the assumed set -'  of structuring rules and the supposed relation >< of structural consistency; this twofold dependence is stressed in the  notation ~71.~.x() introduced for the set of admissible structurations of a given sequence . Let us now discuss these concepts  Structural consistency was introduced to take account of the fact that two located-rules, both valid in a given sequence, may  determine structural units which cannot be ascribed to the same structuration, in that they contrast with each other. But how  can such a relation be explicitly defined? This is a request for the definition  362 LUIGI BURIGANA  of consistency between located-rules in terms of their formal descriptions as pairs of rules and locations. We may assume, at  the start, that structural consistency is essentially a "topological" relation; by this we mean that consistency between any  two located-rules (R, S), (R', S') depends only on locations S= (s,, ..., s), S'= (s; , ..., s;) and not on rules R, R'.  We may also appeal to the evidence of a general prevalence in perception and cognition of structurations of hierarchical type,  and state that structural consistency between located-rules is tantamount to hierarchical consistency between their locations.  However, such locations are families of intervals in a linearly ordered set, and various ways may be conceived to specify  hierarchical relations between them; we could agree, for example, on the definition  (R, S)X(R',S')iff  for all i = 1, ..., m there is i' = 1, ..., m' such that s; s~; s;-,  or for all i' = 1, ..., m' there is i = 1, ..., m such that s;- 9 s;,  or for all i = 1, ..., m and i' = 1, ..., m', s; n s;- = QS, (23)  according to which any two located-rules are consistent if each segment in the location of one of them is "nested" in some  segment in the location of the other, or if all segments are mutually disjoint. Other definitions are possible, but we will  not go into further details; rather let us note that, in the search for the true form of structural consistency, these  abstract speculations cannot be separated from rigorous empirical testing.  Now for some comments on structuring rules. In Section 2 it was claimed that any structuring rule in a sequence applies to  some ordered set of disjoint segments of the sequence, called the field-of-action of the rule. Each component in the  fieldof-action has a definite place in it (segments are listed according to their arrangement in the sequence) and possesses a  specific content (segments are strings of letters in the assumed alphabet, thus describing definite concrete sequences). But  what are, in substance, structuring rules? We present the following tentative answer to the question: a structuring rule in a  sequence consists in a coherent correspondence (a sort of "homomorphism") between two relations on the set of components in  its field-of-action, specifically a "positional" relation which refers to the places of such components and a "qualitative"  relation which concerns their contents. The two relations and the connection between them must be simple, so that the rule may  be easily detected and become psychologically effective; the assumed "correlation" between places and contents is the basis of  the redundancy entailed by the structuring rule. Of course, it will be asked at this point what is meant by the "simplicity"  of the two relations and their connection. This is a complex question, discussion of which would be lengthy. We limit  ourselves here to pointing out that the simplicity of the qualitative relation demands that the components of the  field-of-action be similar to a certain extent, so that the aspects  ORGANIZATION IN FINITE SEQUENCES 363  which undergo an orderly change become prominent and easily recognizable. Thus, when comparing the segments in the  field-of-action of a rule we generally find that some of their aspects are constant (over the whole set or on part of it) and  that other aspects vary in an orderly way, while the remaining characteristics change freely; the rule itself rests on some  kind of balance between constancy and controlled and uncontrolled variability (in particular, the last category must be  contained, so that the first two are not obscured). CONCLUDING REMARKS  In Section 3 it was indicated that, with reference to a fixed inventory 9 of structuring rules, the description of a given  sequence ~ would be conceived as a pair (U, ~ \A), where U is an 9-structuration admissible for ~ and A is a subset of  coordinates sufficient for the whole set with respect to U. The length of that description is simply I U1 + I A 1, and the  minimum length of the possible -4-descriptions of ~ could be taken as a measure of the 1-complexity of the given sequence.  Indeed, in very general terms, the complexity of an "object" with respect to a "language" may be viewed as the inadequacy of  that language for that object, and is expressed by the length of the shortest descriptions of the given object in the accepted  language (Bar-Hillel, 1964; Payne, 1966). Similar ideas are found in MacKay (1950, 1951), who suggested that a "logon" part  (corresponding to U) and a "metron" part (corresponding to A) should be distinguished in the overall content of a "message";  in the "theory of algorithmic complexity" (Kolmogorov, 1968a, b; Zvonkin & Levin, 1970; Dies, 1976), in which complexity of a  "text" with respect to a fixed "decoder" is defined as the length of the shortest "code" sufficient for "recovering" the given  "text"; and in the "structural information theory" of perception (Leeuwenberg, 1968, 1971; Buffart & Leeuwenberg, 1983;  Collard & Buffart, 1983), in which the complexity of a figure described by some "primitive code" is quantified in terms of the  length of a minimum "reduced code," consisting of both symbols for  480/35/3-7  368 LUIGI BURIGANA  "coding rules" and values for suitable "parameters." In fact, the present study originated from a critical review of the  "structural information theory" (Burigana & Lucca, 1985); it was found possible to lead the theory to some mathematical  results by putting aside the intriguing procedure of "code reduction", devising a suitable characterization for structurations  (as consistent families of located-rules), and referring to set explications of rules and structurations.  In determining the informational load of any relevant condition, both the theories of "selective information" (the classical  theory started by Hartley, 1928, and Shannon, 1949) and "structural information" refer to the corresponding "explication,"  defined as the set of all "individuals" in a suitable "population" that satisfy the given condition: the larger the  explication, the weaker, in general, the informational load of the condition. But whereas the selective approach determines a  relative measure of the size of the explication, in terms of its "entropy" with respect to some probability measure on the  fixed population, the structural approach seeks for the explication some essential "parametrization," whose dimensionality is  taken as a measure of the amplitude of the explication itself. The structural approach looks plain, in that it does not  involve any reference to probability measures; but it may prove quite inadequate in dealing with certain conditions. For  example, a "monotonicity" rule may be defined along the lines of "linearity," in case there is an order in the given alphabet.  Clearly, "monotonicity" does not generally allow any reduction in the structural description of sequences and so, in the  structural view, such a rule should be considered as devoid of any information. However, this conclusion does not seem to be  acceptable.
