JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 35, 283-293 (1992)  Theoretical Note Ubiquity of Scalar Timing with a Poisson Clock  JOHN GIBBON  New York State Psychiatric Institute and Columbia University  A Poisson pacemaker/accumulator system for perceiving and remembering time is studied. It is shown that were the Poisson  process the only source of variance, the distribution of estimates of a remembered time would follow a compound gamma law.  However, when multiplicative, mnemonic variance is introduced via encoding and retrieval constants, the scalar property is  induced and dominates other sources of variance which may be present but invisible. Oc 1992 Academic Press, Inc.  INTRODUCTION  Counting and timing functions in the nervous system are commonly thought to be Poisson (e.g., Green & Luce, 1974). How is it,  then, that a Poisson system for an endogenous clock induces performances which appear to simply rescale for different times?  The scale transform property is ubiquitous throughout the timing literature on animal learning (e.g., Gibbon, 1977). It  represents a sort of Weber's law for time in a variety of different performances. An illustrative example from the "peak  procedure" is shown in Fig. 1 (Catania, 1970; Roberts, 1981; Church, 1984).1 On half of the trials, signalled by the onset of  the houselight, rats were reinforced for responding on a lever after 15 s had elapsed, but on the other half, no food was  programmed and the light simply remained on for a long time. Two other groups were exposed to this paradigm, but with food  primed on half the trials at 30 or 60 s. Typical performances on this procedure show a peak in responding near the time when  food is programmed, but this peak varies from trial to trial. An analysis of trials in which food was not programmed ("peak"  trials) identified the time of peak responding on each trial.' The relative frequency of these peak times are shown in the top  panel of Fig. 1. Most frequently peak responding occurs near the time when food is programmed, and when this time is longer,  the spread of these distributions increases. These  features, standard deviation and mean linear in reinforcement time, and superposition of the form of the distribution in  relative time, represent one example of a wide variety of performances conforming to what my colleagues and I have called the  scalar property (see Gibbon, Church, & Meck, 1984 or Gibbon, 1991, for a review). Indeed, the scalar property is evident in a  richer data set from the present experiment also. The entire response rate function, not just its peak, shows approximate  superposition in relative time (Gibbon, Church, & Meck, 1984). The peak times, however, provide a nice illustration of scalar  timing which lends itself to the analysis of estimates of a remembered time, that is pursued here.  This note considers how such estimates may conform to the scalar property when they are generated by a timing mechanism which  is essentially Poisson in character, as we expect in a neural pacemaker. I will show that scalar timing requires the addition  of constraints on the accuracy of memory and decision processes.  INFORMATION PROCESSING MODEL  Performances like those in Fig. 1 we have postulated to be the result of a pacemaker/accumulator system combined with a memory  for important, "to be  SCALAR TIMING WITH A POISSON CLOCK 285  remembered" times, and a decision mechanism comparing current and remembered time. In Fig. 2, such a system is schematized. A  pacemaker generates pulses which are accumulated during the timing signal in an accumulator, and it is this accumulation  process that is the "clock"-the representation of current elapsed time. The memory for the reinforced time is built up over  many trials by transfer of the accumulated number of pulses into a long-term store when reinforcement occurs. Thus the  "correct" time is represented by a number, n*, reflecting the accumulated count from the pacemaker/accumulator at  reinforcement time, i*. Subjects are assumed to monitor elapsed time until a comparison process indicates that the current  elapsed time is "close enough" to a remembered, reinforced time to warrant high rate responding. We can regard the process for  this simplified account as an estimate, T, of the reinforcement time, T*, indexed by a peak in the response rate functions at  time i, close to the criterion time (Fig. 1). CONCLUSION  The purpose of this note has been to show how the scalar property, a Weber law for time, may be seen in data reflecting a  Poisson pacemaker/accumulator system for the perception and memory for time. The results of the analysis show:  1. If the pacemaker/accumulator system is the only source of variance, then compounding variability in the clock with  variability induced by it in the memory results in a compound gamma, or modified Bessel function distribution with an atom at  zero, for estimates of the remembered, criterion time. This manner of mixing memory variance with clock variance does not  induce the scalar property. Rather, it simply doubles the Poisson variance.  2. Scalar variance is induced by randomizing the Poisson mean with bias, or encoding and retrieval variance in the memory  system.
