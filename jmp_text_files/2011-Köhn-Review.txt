Decisions that confront us with the choice among alternatives serving conflicting goals abound. When shopping for a new car we are tempted by flashy and fast models, but we are also concerned with gas economy. Although we may value luxury amenities, we also do not wish to spend a fortune. A vehicle does not exist having a 400 hp engine, running a thousand miles on a gallon, offering the highest level of comfort, and available for a price of $500. The antagonistic nature of our decision objectives poses a dilemma that we are forced to resolve by compromise—less hp, but higher economy on gas; trading luxury for a lower price; and so forth.

Multiple criteria decision making is a collective term for the study of rational strategies to identify the best possible compromise among conflicting objectives. It first gained wider attention within public planning (e.g., securing water supplies for a large city, determining the optimal location of a power plant, or devising a land-use plan for a community). Multiple criteria decision making developed into a highly active, interdisciplinary research area, with main contributions from mathematics and operations research, computer science, engineering, economics and psychology. Today, the most accepted categorization of the field distinguishes between multiobjective programming and multicriteria decision analysis. The latter is concerned with developing a rational calculus for maximizing the gain obtained for a decision problem defined by multiple objectives and explicitly stated alternatives, with respect to an idiosyncratic preference structure. Multicriteria decision analysis has strong ties with psychological utility and mathematical game theory (for the most recent developments, see  Ehrgott, Figueira, & Greco, 2010; Ehrgott, Naujoks, Stewart, & Wallenius, 2010 ). Multiobjective programming, in contrast, represents a discipline in operations research concerned with efficient mathematical algorithms for identifying optimal solutions to decision problems characterized by multiple objectives. The set of feasible alternatives is implicitly defined by constraints in the form of mathematical functions—the preference structure of a decision maker is of secondary importance.

The purpose of this paper is two-fold. A survey reviewing the literature on applications of multiobjective programming in quantitative psychology, without a rudimentary introduction to multiobjective programming, would probably have remained rather inaccessible. So, we first summarize theoretical key concepts of multiobjective optimization (omitting evolutionary multiobjective programming that has not been used in quantitative psychology—detailed presentations can be found in  Coello Coello (1999, 2000) ,  Coello Coello, Lamont, and Van Veldhuizen (2007) ,  Coello Coello and Romero (2002) ,  Deb (2001, 2010)  and  Zitzler, Laumanns, and Bleuler (2004) ). Applications of multiobjective programming to personnel selection, psychometrics, combinatorial data analysis, and the design of event-related functional magnetic resonance imaging experiments in cognitive neuroscience are discussed in the second part.

Adaptations of single objective combinatorial techniques such as dynamic programming and branch-and-bound can only handle problems of limited size. Meta-heuristics have become popular alternatives to traditional methods for generating the Pareto-optimal solution sets of multiobjective combinatorial programs (for an in-depth review, see  Ehrgott & Gandibleux, 2004 ). Multiobjective programming has been applied in quantitative psychology to optimize (i) personnel selection strategies; (ii) experimental designs in cognitive neuroscience employing event-related functional magnetic resonance imaging (ER-fMRI); (iii) item calibration/selection in psychometrics; (iv) structural data representations within the framework of combinatorial data analysis. We provide a survey of the work published in these areas (as a cautionary remark, preserving the original notation whenever possible might occasionally conflict with the definitions given at the very beginning; the specific context should help to resolve these ambiguities).

An employer is caught in the “selection quality-adverse impact” dilemma when pre-employment tests of established validity (e.g., for assessing cognitive ability, specific skills, and so on) reveal substantial effect sizes (i.e., standardized mean differences) by gender, age, race, ethnicity, and so on, either suggesting a relaxation of the hiring standards for minority groups (“selection quality”), or accepting disproportionately low selection rates for applicants from groups who differ in terms of these characteristics from the workforce majority (“adverse impact”). In the US, an employer is legally bound to abstain from recruitment procedures that are potentially biased against minorities. A common remedy to the selection quality-adverse impact problem uses a weighted combination of multiple job performance predictors, with varying effect sizes, so that a judicious choice of weights minimizes the adverse impact rate and preserves certain quality standards of selection. Suitable weights can typically be located only by trial and error.  De Corte, Lievens, and Sackett (2007)  propose a bi-objective (continuous nonlinear) program for identifying weights of predictor combinations yielding a Pareto-optimal trade-off to maximize selection quality and minimize adverse impact. The description of technical detail is sparse. Apparently, the weighted-sum-score method is used to identify the set of Pareto-optimal solutions. As an interesting technical detail, De Corte and his colleagues employ the normal boundary intersection (NBI) method ( Das & Dennis, 1998 ) for finding objective function weights that provide an equally-spaced Pareto-optimal front. As a potential weakness of NBI,  Miettinen  points out “that it may produce non-Pareto-optimal solutions to nonconvex problems” (1999, p. 113).

The stream of data collected during an ER-fMRI experiment can be condensed into various statistical estimates of parameters to infer the course of cognitive information processing. The efficiency of these statistics (i.e., the ratio of the Rao–Cramér lower bound to their actual variance) is directly affected by the complex interplay of specific features of the chosen experimental design such as timing, arrangement and patterning of stimulus presentation (e.g., rapid event-related versus block designs). In addition, stimuli must occur with a predetermined frequency; proper experimental practice demands that sequencing remain unpredictable for subjects to prevent habituation (e.g., through counterbalancing).  Wager and Nichols (2003)  propose a multicriteria optimization framework for devising ER-fMRI experiments integrating into a convex combination the criterion of maximal efficiency of parameter estimates and specific design requirements to control for unwanted psychological confounds.  Kao, Mandal, Lazar, and Stufken (2009)  further refine the Wager–Nichols approach by using a convex combination of four criteria (“estimation efficiency”, “detection power” for the signal-noise ratio, amount of counterbalancing, and desired stimuli frequency); the weights must be specified by the experimenter according to the specific focus of an experiment.  Wager and Nichols (2003)  as well as  Kao et al. (2009)  discuss highly complex procedures for quantifying these criteria based on a given design matrix. The actual search for optimal designs is carried out through a genetic algorithm started with an initial population of design matrices considered useful. For each candidate design matrix, the weighted sum of criteria gives a summary score. Neither  Wager and Nichols (2003)  nor  Kao et al. (2009)  mention the concept of Pareto-optimality explicitly; but, in a series of simulations,  Kao et al. (2009)  explore the effect of different criterion weights on the evaluation of 24 designs, essentially constructing a Pareto front.

The implementation of computerized adaptive tests requires the technical flexibility to choose a sequence of items from an item bank such that they constitute an optimal, individually tailored response to an examinee’s current ability parameter estimate (the latter is continuously updated during the course of testing based on the previously administered items). The selected items should be optimal in the sense that they are appropriate for an examinee’s most recent ability estimate (i.e., neither too easy, nor too difficult) to guarantee utmost accuracy for a subsequent update. The selection of items must be constrained by formal criteria such as difficulty, discrimination, susceptibility to guessing, and item information (i.e., the efficiency of the obtained ability parameter estimate). In addition, item content as well as properties of the entire test (e.g., total information; length, i.e., total number of items) must be taken into account. The concept of proximity subsumes similarity and its complement, dissimilarity. Any numerical measure of relationship between the elements of a pair from two (possibly distinct) sets of objects can serve as an index of proximity. Proximities are typically coded as nonnegative dissimilarities (where larger numerical indices pertain to less similar pairs of objects) and collected into a matrix with rows and columns representing the respective sets of objects. The goal of structural data representation is to display object relationship as a discrete or continuous configuration most faithfully reflecting the given data (these two representational modes correspond to distinct perspectives on how objects might be mentally represented). Combinatorial data analysis concerns the use of combinatorial optimization methods for identifying structural data representations, for example, in the form of an additive tree, a clustering/partition, seriation/sequencing or (multidimensional) scaling of objects (see  Arabie & Hubert, 1996; Hubert, Arabie, & Meulman, 2001, 2006 ). Combinatorial multiobjective methods have been applied to the optimization of structural data representations. Excellent presentations of multiobjective programming applications to seriation/sequencing and clustering/partitioning problems are given by  Brusco and Stahl (2005a, Chs. 6, 11) . In summary, for seriation/sequencing and scaling problems, two prototypical multiobjective programs can be distinguished to generate structural representations of (i) a single data matrix using multiple criteria; (ii) multiple data matrices in accordance with a single criterion.

The same distinction can be established among multiobjective programs for clustering and partitioning problems, as two early publications already document.  Ferligoj and Batagelj (1992) implement simultaneous hierarchical clustering of multiple dissimilarity matrices as a multiobjective program that, generally speaking, relies on the idea of modeling individual matrices as deviates from an aggregate of their central tendency.  Delattre and Hansen (1980)  develop a bi-objective clustering algorithm motivated by the observation that the ultimate goal of clustering–the generation of homogeneous and well-separated classes of objects–essentially entails two conflicting objectives that cannot be accomplished simultaneously by a single criterion algorithm. Delattre and Hansen combine  Johnson’s (1967)  single-link and complete-link method to remedy their respective deficits. The first seeks maximally separated clusters but is marred by the chaining effect, where very dissimilar objects can be clustered together because they are endpoints of a transitive similarity chain. Complete-link generates maximally homogeneous clusters that might suffer from the dissection effect, where very similar objects are assigned to different clusters. Multiobjective programming addresses many research problems at a level of complexity unheard of in the past. Special journal editions, numerous web-sites (for a compilation, see  Masud & Ravindran, 2009 ), and recent surveys on applications of multiobjective programming, for example, in engineering ( Marler & Arora, 2004 ) and computational biology ( Handl, Kell, & Knowles, 2007 ), point to its ever-growing importance and the apparent need for such methods in many academic disciplines. The applications in quantitative psychology surveyed provide evidence that the promise of refined solution strategies might not be an undivided blessing. Studies combining multiple criteria into a single program (e.g.  Brusco & Cradit, 2005 ) show impressive outcomes. Multiobjective programming for fitting multiple data sets has produced mixed results: the seminal contribution by  Brusco et al. (2010)  on three-way multidimensional scaling versus the study by  Brusco and Steinley (2008)  showing that increased complexity does not always lead to better solutions. What promising directions for future research can we identify? First, many data analytic and theoretical problems in quantitative psychology, with a genuine multiobjective structure, are still conceptualized and treated as single objective problems; examples abound in test construction, testing and assessment, as shown by the cited work. Second, validation studies on the merits of multiobjective strategies when applied to real-world data sets, with the one exception of  Brusco and Steinley (2008) , are not present in psychology but are definitely needed. Third, essentially all multiobjective programming applications in quantitative psychology use weighted-sum scalarization. Other solution techniques have not been used (with the exception of  Delattre & Hansen, 1980 ); closely related is the issue of nonsupported efficient solutions in applications of combinatorial multiobjective programming. Fourth,  Brusco et al. (2010)  develop a completely new perspective on the simultaneous analysis of proximity data collected from multiple sources. Traditional approaches to this data analytic task model individual variability as deviation from an aggregate of the central tendency of all data matrices. Open questions concerning the  Brusco et al. (2010)  approach refer, for example, to the specific solution method (scalarization versus alternative strategies for generating the set of efficient solutions), or whether other representational structures (e.g., unfolding, ultrametrics, or additive trees) can be extended to three-way data through multiobjective programming. Finally, ambitious latent variable models (e.g., as used in cognitive diagnosis) are richly parameterized but often difficult to estimate by maximum likelihood. Can multiobjective programming provide an alternative for fitting such models, for example, through implementing estimation as a simultaneous multiparameter search (as opposed to the common iterative “fix all others to identify one” approach)?
