MORRISON  Graduate School of Business, Columbia University, New York, New York 10027  A pure extinction process memory retrieval latency model is completely characterized by the number of items to be recalled n  and the recall rate A. In this paper A is allowed to vary across the population of subjects. Results are given for arbitrary  individual differences distributions on A. When A is distributed gamma the results are very simple and method of moment  estimators are easily obtained. In addition the shape parameter of the gamma individual differences distribution turns out to  be an index of the degree of heterogeneity of the recall parameter. The pure extinction process model is also appropriate for  certain collection and detection tasks.  1. INTRODUCTION  Consider a subject who has the task of recalling each item from an n item list. DISCUSSION  A memory retrieval latency model for the recall of n items has been generalized to allow for individual differences. The model  can have arbitrary distributions on the recall rate. However, when the individual differences distribution is gamma the  results are particularly simple. The model is then completely characterized by the gamma parameters r and a (assuming that we  know n, the number of items to be recalled).  A test of the model would be to use the observable histograms P(k; t) for different values of t. The expected histogram would  be obtained from (6) and the parameter estimates r" and a. If the model is correct the sample estimators for r and a will  remain stable for different values of t and the complete probability distributions derived from r" and a will fit the observed  histograms well. MORRISON  individual differences in recall rates are small and therefore the original homogeneous model proposed by McGill (1963) is  adequate. The model developed here is a natural generalization to consider when the McGill model doesn't fit or large  individual differences are expected on a priori grounds.  This paper has been written within the framework of memory retrieval latencies. However, the model has numerous other  potential applications as mentioned in the introduction. These applications include the time to complete the acquisition of a  set of n equally likely objects and job durations for groups of n individuals with identical A's within each group and  differing A's across the groups.  At this stage it would be nice to point to some published studies where the heterogeneous pure extinction model fits better  than the homogeneous model proposed by McGill. Unfortunately, none of the published literature on latencies or duration times  appears to be in the form of P(k; t). However, the contribution of this paper is not to present an empirically more accurate  model. The model development and parameter estimation procedures are, per se, of interest. In addition, this paper should  provide a stimulus for reanalyzing some sets of existing latency data and to stimulate the collection of latencies in areas  such as unaided brand recall where these latencies are not currently being recorded.
