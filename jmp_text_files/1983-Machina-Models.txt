MACHINA  Peter Fishburn is currently a member of the Mathematical Studies Department of AT & T Bell Laboratories. He received his Ph.D.  from Case Institute of Technology in 1962, and has been a member of the technical staff of Research Analysis Corporation,  Research Professor of Management Science at Pennsylvania State University, Visiting Professor on a Fulbright Grant to the  Technical University of Denmark, and a member of the Institute for Advanced Study in Princeton. He has published numerous  articles in the fields of utility and preference theory, social choice and voting theory, the theory of stochastic dominance,  and other topics in mathematical social science and applied mathematics, as well as the volumes Decision and Value Theory,  Utility Theory for Decision Making, The Theory of Social Choice, Approval Voting (with Steven Brams), and Mathematics of  Decision Theory.  The reviewer, Mark Machina, received his Ph.D. from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology and is currently Assistant  Professor of Economics at the University of California, San Diego. His primary research interest is decision making under  uncertainty.  Outside the field of geometry, where hundreds of years of vain attempts to "prove" the parallel postulate finally led to the  development of non-Euclidean geometry, I can think of few other fields where axiomatics have played a larger role in the  development, understanding, and misunderstanding of the topic than in the "expected utility" theory of individual behavior  toward risk first proposed by Bernoulli (1738) and rigorously formulated by yon Neumann and Morgenstern (1944, 1947, 1953). To  take but a few historical and current examples (see cited articles for details):  Samuelson's initial reaction to yon Neumann and Morgenstern was that the theory was "arbitrary" and that there was no reason  why rational individuals necessarily had to be expected utility maximizers Samuelson (1950, esp. p. 168). Shortly afterwards,  however, and as a result of his discovery that the yon Neumann-Morgenstern formulation had implicitly made use of a hidden  "independence assumption" or "independence axiom," he became convinced that the maximization of expected utility indeed was a  sine qua non of rational behavior under uncertainty (Samuelson, 1952a, 1952b).' In their classic paper on the application of the expected utility hypothesis to observed risk taking behavior, Friedman and  Savage (1948) presented an alternative statement of the expected utility axioms "designed to show that there is little  difference between the plausibity of [the expected utility] hypothesis and the [economist's] usual indifference curve  explanation of riskless choice" (Friedman & Savage, 1948, p. 288). Perhaps so, but as Samuelson subsequently noted, what  Friedman and Savage presented was not in fact equivalent to the expected utility axioms (they had gotten the independence  axiom wrong) and in particular was too weak to imply expected utility maximization (Stigler & Boulding, 1951, p. 71). More  recently, an attempt by Handa (1977) to axiomatize the psychologist's "subjective expected value" model of risk taking' by  means of a variation of the expected utility axioms was similarly shown to in fact imply something quite different from what  the author had intended (interestingly enough, it was Fishburn (1978), who among others, first pointed out this error).  Although Friedman and Savage subsequently claimed that the independence axiom (correctly stated) was "practically unique among  maxims for wise action in the face of uncertainty" (Friedman & Savage, 1952, p. 468) and Savage (1954) went on to develop one  of the most elegant and useful axiomatizations of expected utility, when asked by Allais to choose over two pairs of simple  lotteries in an example now known as the "Allais Paradox," Savage expressed personal preferences which in fact violated the  expected utility axioms. When this had been pointed out to him by Allais, Savage concluded upon reflection that it had been  his preferences, rather than the axioms, which had been in error! (Savage, 1954, pp. 101-104). Today, this issue of the  "rationality" of preferences which do not conform to the expected utility axioms is if anything being debated more than ever  (see, e.g., Allais, Amihud, de Finetti, Hagen, & Morgenstern in Allais & Hagen, 1979, as well as Jeffrey, 1983, Machina, 1981,  McClennen, 1983, and Tversky, 1975).  In a field where even the best minds have occasionally stumbled over the nature, content, and meaning of the axioms, the  extensive work of Peter Fishburn on the axiomatics of expected utility theory provides a model of intellectual rigor and  careful formal analysis. Indeed, the author has been so prolific in this area, with literally dozens of articles spread over  the psychology, mathematics, economics, and operations research literature, that the reader trying to keep fully abreast of  this material would find little time for anything else, and I am not the only person I know to have proven a "new" result in  expected utility theory only to have subsequently found that Fishburn had already done it. The present volume, which unifies  and extends the author's and others' works on this topic, thus constitutes a most welcome addition to the literature on  expected utility theory, and is without question the most complete and thorough treatment of the foundational aspects of this  field to date, or indeed, that can be expected for some time to come.  The value of the book lies not only in the care and rigor with which the author proceeds throughout, but also in its overall  organization. The short introductory first chapter, which outlines the development of the rest of the text, is itself one of  the most illuminating overviews of the logical structure of the various forms of the expected utility model that I have come  across, and the reader will Find that referring  `See, e.g., Preston and Baratta (1948), Griffith (1949), Edwards (1953, 1955), and Nogee and Lieberman (1960).  480/27/3-7  330 BOOK REVIEW  back to it serves perfectly to recall the overall structure of the "forest" when immersed in studying the more specific  structure of each of the "trees."  Each of the subsequent chapters develops a branch of the expected utility model in a way which emphasizes an important lesson.  Part I of the book (Chaps. 2-8) develops the theory of expected utility preferences over spaces of well-defined or "objective"  probability distributions. By beginning with a general axiomatic characterization of linear functions over convex sets,  Chapter 2 illustrates how most of the analytic structure of the "expected utility" axioms and the expected utility  representation theorem do not depend upon the objects of choice being probability distributions, or indeed involving  uncertainty in any way. Chapter 3 then explicitly considers the case of choice over probability distributions, developing the  slight additional requirements necessary to extend the expected utility representation theorem from simple probability  distributions (i.e., ones with finite supports) through to countably additive probability distributions-it is this material  which is what most researchers have in mind when they refer to "the" expected utility representation theorem.  Chapters 4 and 5 consider the role played in the theory by the continuity (i.e., "Archimedean") and transitivity axioms,  respectively. Once again we benefit from the author's rigor-rather than informally "assign" the various properties of the  expected utility model to each of these assumptions, the author illustrates the role played by each of them by logically  deriving the models which result from respectively omitting them. Dropping continuity results in a (possibly infinite)  "hierarchical" structure of preferences, and if we assume that such a hierarchy has a finite number of levels, we obtain the  "lexicographic expected utility" model where the utility of each outcome is an ordered n-tuple, and lotteries are ordered on  the basis of the expectation of the first component, or in case of a tie, on the basis of the expectation of the second  component, etc. Dropping transitivity yields the "one-way expected utility" model where a strictly greater expected utility  implies strict preference, but not necessarily the other way around. In each of these chapters, as throughout the text, the  author gives us a feel for the material by exploring the effects of variations in the axioms, often, for example, by showing  how the same model may be obtained by weakening one axiom while simultaneously strengthening another.  Chapters 6-8 offer a very concise and illuminating catagorization and analysis of the widely discussed area of  "multidimensional" or "multiattribute" expected utility theory, i.e., expected utility preferences over Cartesian product  spaces (see, e.g., the surveys of Farquhar, 1977 and Keeney & Raiffa, 1976, Chaps. 5, 6). Chapter 6 gives the precise  assumptions necessary for the von Neumann-Morgenstern utility function to be an additively separable function of the elements  of a Cartesian product space, making the crucial point that the mere combination of the expected utility axioms and the  assumption of separable ordinal preferences is not sufficient to obtain this frequently invoked functional form (this chapter  also derives conditions for the utility function to possess the "multiadditive" or "risk independent" form which implies that  risk attitudes toward one attribute are independent of the values of other attributes). Chapters 7 and 8 consider expected  BOOK REVIEW 331  spaces (e.g., Cartesian products of probability distributions) and accordingly provide a clear axiomatic foundation for the  standard assumptions on preferences in the theory of n-person games.  Part 11 of the book (Chaps. 9-12) develops the Savage-type theory of "subjective expected utility,"' where instead of  well-specified probability distributions, the objects of choice are "acts," i.e., mapping from "states of nature" to  consequences, and the axiomatic formulation generates both a von Neumann-Morgenstern utility function over consequences as  well as a "subjective probability distribution" over states of nature which together generate the individual's preference  ranking over acts. Once again the author clarifies the logical structure of these types of models by first deriving (in Chap.  9) a "proto-model" which demonstrates how most of their mathematical structure does not depend upon the notion of subjective  probability distributions (or indeed uncertainty) at all, and then showing the specific additional assumptions necessary to  obtain the regular subjective expected utility representation in the case of a finite number of states. Chapter 10 extends  this model to the case of an infinite number of states. Chapter 11 then examines the result of dropping transitivity and  obtains a subjective expected utility version of the one-way expected utility model of Chapter 5. Finally, Chapter 12  considers the alternative "conditional subjective expected utility" model, where rather than taking states of nature and  consequences as primitives and defining acts as mappings from one to the other, we take the set of states and the set of acts  as primitives, and define utility over act-state pairs, i.e., u(x, s) is defined as the utility of act x in state s.  It is important to point out that this book is not designed for the reader wishing an introduction to the expected utility  model or its behavioral applications-such readers would probably have a hard time following (and certainly appreciating) the  logical development of the book, and would be much better served by the expositions of Arrow (1970, Chaps. 2, 3), Hirshleifer  and Riley (1979), Raiffa (1968), Schoemaker (1982), or the author's own earlier volume (Fishburn, 1970, Parts II, III).  Rather, this book is for the reader who knows what expected utility is, has read some of the widespread and frequently  unrelated literature on the various manifestations of the model, and would like to see the foundational literature organized  and developed in a logical, motivated, and formal manner.  Where to from here? It is hard to see how the logical foundations of the expected utility model could develop much beyond the  synthesis presented in the current volume. The current volume is nevertheless important to both the  constructors and testers of such non-expected utility models-to the constructors in that it sets the high standards of  theoretical rigor to which such theorists must aspire if they are to convince their colleagues to abandon expected utility,  and to the empiricists because it helps separate the distinct building blocks of the theory, so that we might better test just  which assumptions are and are not violated. Not surprisingly, Fishburn himself has recently joined the forefront of these new  non-expected utility theorists, developing and exploring the implications of a new model of preferences (the "skew-symmetric  bilinear" (SBB) model) which weakens the independence axiom and can either include or not include transitivity.' An  up-to-the-minute description of his current work, however, would be beyond the scope of this review. Besides, the longer I sat  here and wrote about it, the further behind I would be falling.
