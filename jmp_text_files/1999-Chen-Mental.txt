A Mental Space Similarity Group Model of Shape Constancy 

Kathleen C. Chen

and

State University of New York, Brockport 

A group model of mental transformations based on the geometric model of P. B. Yale (1968, Geometry and symmetry, Holden�Day, San Francisco) was constructed for form recognition. The model consisted of nine characteristic subgroups of the similarity group in Euclidean space. With these subgroups, six series were formed, representing six visual paths for form recognition. Each series involved five characteristic subgroups. Six subframes were associated with nine characteristic subgroups in the model. These subframes were shape (angle measure), the sense, size (volume), verticality, uprightness, and position. The model was validated by an experiment, using reaction time as the behavior index. Since shape is the common invariant property of all subgroups of the similarity group, angle measure was not included in ordering of subframes. The findings show that the preservation of uprightness of a form provides the best condition for form recognition, followed by the preservation of sense and verticality of a form. While the effect of position is not strong, size has the weakest influence on space form recognition. ] 1999 Academic Press 

INTRODUCTION 

Shape constancy refers to the fact that observers see objects as having the same ``shape'' if the objects differ only in size, position, orientation, or sense or a combination of these. The psychological explanation of perceptual constancy dates back to early investigators like Hering (1878�1964) and Helmholtz (1856�1962), who suggested that in perceptual constancies, the flux of proximal stimulations contains some features that are invariantly related to distal objects. shape constancy involves reconstructing and size scaling of a three-dimensional Euclidean structure of an object from the external image after taking into account the object's orientation relative to the observer. 

Cassirer (1938�1944) extended the Erlanger program of Klein (1893) and explicitly introduced the mathematical concept of invariance over a group of transformations as a characterization of such perceptual constancies. Klein's Erlanger program specifies that one figure is equivalent to another figure in a given geometry if and only if there is a transformation in its underlying group that changes the first figure into the second. 

Following the illuminating analysis of perceptual constancy by Cassirer (1938�1944) was the work of Pitts and McCulloch (1947) on a neural network for computing group invariants. Despite these efforts, the idea of group invariants was not discussed in psychology until Gibson introduced his radical ecological approach to perception (Gibson, 1961, 1979). He presented penetrating discussions of transformations and the importance to perceptions of invariants over them. He construed the constancy problem differently from those psychologists in Helmholtz's tradition. He rejected the claim that the perception of form is based on the flux of sensations. According to Gibson, shape constancy is not based on object orientation. Instead, shape constancy involves projective invariants that are geometric properties of a shape that are preserved in any perspective or projective image of the shape. 

About the same period, Hoffman (1966, 1978) presented a formal theory of perception based on Lie transformation groups and Lie algebra. Also Caelli, Hoffman, and Lindman (1978a, 1978b) used the Lorentz group of relativity physics to explain real and apparent motion. Until then group approaches to perception were not widely understood in psychology. 

Since the eighties, more attention has been shown to such group approaches to perception. Chen and Chen (1982) established a series of plane isometry normal subgroups as models for form recognition. The results show that in a chain or a series the smaller an isometric group, the easier the recognition of the images by its transformations. 

Palmer (1983) presented a theory of perceptual organization, assuming the concept of local invariance over the group of Euclidean space similarity transformations. Based on a parallel systems related by similarity transformations, his theory accounts for the phenomena of shape constancy, motion perception, figural goodness, perceptual group, and reference frame effects. 

Another group approach to perception was carried out by Leyton (1986). The concept of group was used to describe the internal structure of reference frame and to identify an object by a set of invariant properties under geometric transformations. He postulated that human cognition involves an internal reference which is a successive decomposition of the input group of transformations into subgroups and eventually to the identity group by sequential removal of instable transformations. The groups Leyton used were plane groups: the affine group and the isometry group. In our present study, we used the three-dimensional similarity group which is larger than the three-dimensional isometry group, and also extended the concept of reference frames to multiple subframes. The similarity group contains the Euclidean group as a subgroup.

Carlton and Shepard (1990a, 1990b) used a symmetry group and a Lie Group to study rigid motions in the three-dimensional physical world. They regarded the geodesic path as the simplest psychological motion of an object from one position to the other. The rigid motions in their study were direct isometrics: translation, rotation, and screw displacement. In comparison, the group in our present study is a larger group which includes opposite isometrics and similarities. 

Since the eighties, research has been almost systematically restricted to the set of Euclidean transformations. The group of Euclidean transformations of the plane is generated by rotations, translations, and reflections, which all preserve distance. The group of similarity transformations involve manipulation of the distance between an object and its image. There are other transformations that do not involve parallelism between the object plane and the image plane. This is the group of affine transformations. When a planar figure is rotated around any axis and then projected orthogonally onto a fixed plane, the original figure is transformed into its image by an affine transformation. The affine transformation group is a supergroup of the similarity group. This group of affine transformations was explored by Wagemans, Vanden Bossche, Segers, and d'Ydewalle (1994). Their group model consisted of scalings, shears, and similarities. They used a 2_2 matrix over the real field to represent an affine transformation and the four elements of the matrix as parameters. Starting with four basic transformations��a scaling along the x-axis, another scaling along the y-axis, a shear along the x-axis and another shear along the y-axis��the authors formed 12 subgroups and eight chains of subgroups of the affine transformation group. They designated a chain of subgroups as a mathematically equivalent path and concluded that out of the eight paths only one seemed relevant for human perception. 

Supporting the contention that the perceptual system makes use of features of the projected image of the optic array that remain invariant under changes in viewpoint (e.g., Gibson, 1979), Van Gool, Moons, Pauwels, and Wagemans (1994) demonstrated that human observers were able to arrive at shape equivalence under affine transformations, even when provided with only minimal information of four distinct points. In another report, Wagemans, Van Gool, and Lamote (1996) argued for the visual system's use of invariants and stated that the frequently observed effects of parametric difference on the difficulty of matching task need not be taken as evidence for mental transformation and against the visual system's use of invariants. They reported an experiment in which observers were asked to give affine-invariant coordinates of a point located in an affine frame defined by three other points. The angle substended by the coordinate axes and the ratio of the lengths of their unit vectors systematically affected the measurement errors. They concluded from the above finding that the visual system's measurement of invariants need not itself be invariant. Further support of the invariants-based approach came from Biederman and Gerhardstein (1993), who argued that the effects of viewpoint might be caused by the occlusion of different parts of an object or by the disappearance of nonaccidental properties which are critical to determine the part category to which each part belongs (Tarr 6 Bu�lthoff, 1995). 

A distinction of perspective and projective invariants was made by Pizlo (1994). Perspective invariants differ from conventional invariants, which require that the

There is evidence suggesting that shape equivalence is not based on projective congruence. Niall and Macnamara (1990) used convex pentagons with variable three-dimensional orientations from which observers selected projectively matching patterns. The five-point cross ratios of the chosen comparison shapes differed significantly from those of the standard ones. Niall (1992) further confirmed his conclusion using shapes continuously rotating in depth. 

Another study by Niall (1997), using planar shapes pictured in depth, showed the importance of changes in invariants that can be measured in the picture plane by degree of compression due to perspective. He believed that observers are responsive to Euclidean properties of perspective views rather than the projective invariant properties of those views. 

An investigation (Wagemans, Lamote, 6 Van Gool, 1997) was carried out to tackle the issue of perspective and projective invariance on shape equivalence. The results suggest that the visual system does not deal with perspectivities in a categorically different way from projectivities. This is in accordance with the view that perspective transformations are only a subset of the group of projective transformations and that the invariants which they have in common constitute the basis of shape recognition. Therefore, the mathematical principles of group theory would seem to be more important than the optics of the eye. 

However, Wagemans et al. (1997) prefer to attribute their results to quantitative parametric differences rather than the fundamental qualitative difference between perspective and projective transformations. Another conclusion of their study shows that although compactness varies with parameter values, the observer's decision cannot be determined by a simple preference for similar compactness in shapes. 

Although the psychophysical investigations reviewed above support the view that shape constancy is achieved by the use of invariant properties of the projected image, the dominant belief in psychology seems to be that shape constancy is achieved via mental transformation (Jolicoeur, 1985; Shepard 6 Cooper, 1982) or normalization process (Tarr 6 Pinker, 1990; Ullman, 1989). 

The controversy between the two approaches is not easily resolved. SPACE SIMILARITIES 

A transformation in Euclidean space is a one-to-one map of the points in space onto themselves. The most commonly used Euclidean transformations are isometries and similarities. A space isometry is a transformation that maps points P and Q into points P$ and Q$ such that the Euclidean distance between P$ and Q$ is the same as the Euclidean distance between P and Q. The Euclidean distance between two points P(x1 , y1 , z1) and Q(x2, y2 , z2) is defined as PQ=- (x2&x1)[2] +( y2& y1)[2] +(z2&z1)[2] . Due to lack of standardization of the terminology in geometric transformations, the terms and definitions used in the present paper are from Transformation Geometry by Martin (1986). All six space isometries were identified by Euler in 1776. They are reflection, translation, rotation, screw displacement, glide reflection, and rotary reflection. A translation is a transformation that moves all points in space the same distance in the same direction. We may use a vector to represent both the direction and distance of a translation. We select the translation that moves all points zero distance in any direction as the identity transformation. A space rotation is the rotation of space about a line, called the axis, through a given angle. A space reflection in a plane, called the plane of reflection or the mirror, leaves point P fixed if P is on the mirror, and carries point Q to point Q$ such that the mirror is the perpendicular bisector of segment QQ$ if Q is not on the mirror. A screw displacement is a product of a rotation about a line and a translation through a vector parallel to the axis of the rotation. A glide reflection is the product of a translation and a reflection whose mirror is parallel to the vector that represents the translation. A rotary reflection is the product of a rotation and a reflection in a plane perpendicular to the axis of the rotation. A transformation is called an inversion in point C, if it fixes C and otherwise sends P to P$ such that C is the midpoint of segment PP$. 

A space similarity is the product of an isometry and a stretch. A stretch of positive ratio r>0 about� C is a transformation that fixes C and otherwise sends points P to P$ on ray CP such that CP$=rCP. Some authors use the term dilation for stretch. As an isometry preserves distances, a similarity preserves equal distances. The angle measure in degrees is a function m from the set of all angles into the � set [x: x is a real number and 0<x<180]. If a protractor is placed on VA of angle � MAVB, one reads the number x by VB and says that mMAVB=x%. While a similarity preserves angle measure of a form, an isometry preserves its angle measure as well as distance. As a result, a similarity preserves the shape of a form, while an isometry preserves the shape and volume of a form. In other words, a similarity maps a form to a similar form and an isometry maps a form to a congruent form. When the ratio of the stretch of a similarity is 1, the similarity is also an isometry. Hence, the set of space isometries, denoted by E, is a subset of space similarities, denoted by S. A transformation is a dilatation, if it sends a line to a parallel line. Translations, inversions, and stretches are all space dilatations.

We introduce a definition of similarity transformation on polyhedra here. Our definition requires the term ``space angle,'' which consists of the face angles of the same vertex. A face angle is the plane angle of a face at a vertex of a polyhedron. Two polyhedra are similar if there exists a one-to-one correspondence between the vertices of the two polyhedra, the space angles at corresponding vertices are congruent, and the lengths of corresponding edges are proportional. The second condition implies that corresponding face angles at corresponding vertices are congruent. When the above three conditions are satisfied, the two polyhedra are similar or have the same shape. A similarity transformation maps a polyhedron to a similar polyhedron. 

A space isometry is a product of at most four plane reflections. A reflection is the product of one reflection. A translation or a rotation is a product of two reflections. A glide reflection or a rotary reflection is the product of three, and a screw displacement is the product of four reflections. We call an isometry direct or opposite, according to whether the number of its reflection factors is even or odd, respectively. Thus the set of space isometries can be partitioned into the subset of direct isometrics which include translations, rotations, and screw displacements; and the subset of opposite isometries that include reflections, glide reflections, and rotary reflections. A similarity is the product of an isometry and a stretch with a positive ratio; it is direct or opposite according to whether its factor isometry is direct or opposite. Hence the set of space similarities may be classified as direct similarities and opposite similarities.

A wonder of human brains is to solve problems through mental simulation of real-world transformation, (Holland, Holyoak, Nisbett, 6 Thagard, 1983; Shepard, 1988; Shepard, 1994). A. Method 

EXPERIMENT 

Subjects. Thirteen subjects. all students at the Rochester Institute of Technology, were paid for their participation in the experiment which lasted approximately an hour. Nine of the subjects were male and four were female. All subjects majored in different fields, but had taken a course in Introduction to Psychology. They all had 20�20 vision or corrected 20�20 vision. 

Stimuli. The stimuli used for the experiment were two computer-generated perspective line drawings portraying seven rigid cubes of the same size fastened face-to-face. These stimuli are similar to those introduced by Shepard and Metzler (1971). The cubes form a three-dimensional structure that cannot be transformed into itself by any reflections. Since the generating solid of the forms is a cube, our forms are polyhedra. The two standard forms are shown at the bottom of Figs. 3 and 4. The two forms underwent the following transformations: 

3_4 spirals��three sizes of scales, 0.43, 1.00, 1.57, and four rotations counterclockwise about the z-axis with angular departure of 0, 60, 120, 180% from the positive xz-plane in a right-handed coordinate system; 

Four mirror reflections in the planes with a multiple of 60% angular departure from the xz-plane. 

Three translations��one along the y-axis, one along the z-axis and one moving into the second octant where y- and z-coordinates are positive and the x-coordinate is negative. 

Three glide reflections��the above three translations, each followed by a reflection in a plane parallel to the direction of the translation. 

Four screw displacements��the above four medium-sized rotations, each followed by a translation along the z-axis. 

Four rotary reflections��the above four medium-sized rotations, each followed by a reflection in a plane perpendicular to the z-axis. 

Only rotations undergo stretch�shrinkage in size. Other transformations were of medium-size displays. The above 30 transformations in two forms yield a total of 60 forms. The small, medium, and large displays subtended visual angles of approximately 2%34$, 8%20$, and 13%4$, respectively. The small size was approximately of height 0.75", length 1.44" and width 0.38"; the medium size of height 1.75", length 2.25", width 0.56"; and the large size of height 2.75", length 4.13", width 1.50". The viewing distance was approximately one foot. 

Procedure. This was a two-stimulus discrimination task introduced by Shepard and Metzler (1971). Each subject participated for a total of 180 trials. In each trial, 

421

a standard form and a test form appeared as a pair, with the standard form always appearing on the left. The experimenter instructed subjects that they would be shown two forms and that their task was to decide whether these two forms were intrinsically of the same shape, despite differences in the spatial orientations in which they were portrayed. The experimenter explained that ``intrinsically of the same shape'' meant that the two forms could be brought into a similar shape by rapidly moving and�or expanding�shrinking one with respect to the other in space, and that ``intrinsically different in shape'' meant that the two objects could not be brought into a similar shape by any rigid motion and a stretch in space. This point was concretely illustrated by using demonstration stimuli and physically rotating one of the two stimuli into a match with the other. Another illustration showed that the comparison stimulus, a mirror image of the standard stimulus, could never be brought into a similar shape by any rigid motion and a stretch in space. With these instructions, subjects were aware that the ``different'' trials were all made up of reflections and stretches and did not contain shapes that were completely unrelated to the standard form. 

Each subject participating in classifying 36 practice pairs was given immediately the feedback of ``right'' or ``wrong'' after each picture pair. No feedback was given for test pairs. For each test form, there were 11 test pairs of size scale 1 for which the correct classification was ``same'' (4 spirals, 3 translations, 4 screw displacements) and for 11 pairs it was ``different'' (4 reflections, 3 glide reflections, 4 rotary reflections). For the other two sizes of scale 0.43 and 1.57, there were eight pairs of rotations with ``same'' classification. Therefore there were a total of 60 transformations for the two forms. With 38 ``same'' responses and 22 ``different'' responses, these 60 transformations were repeated three times (three sessions) resulting in 180 trials. Subjects were not aware of the unequal distribution of ``same'' and ``different'' responses. As noted in their reports at the end of the experiment, they knew the termination of each session, because they had to type, for instance, ``Session I,'' and to press any key to start the next session. They also knew that each session was a replication of a previous one. Half the subjects pressed the right-hand key for similar forms and the left-hand key for dissimilar ones. The roles of the two hands were reversed for the other half of the subjects, in order to control for possible response biases. At the beginning of a trial, a cyan warning field was presented for approximately one second, followed by a test pair randomly selected by a computer. The computer presented all test pairs of stimuli and recorded reaction times and errors. Test pairs in which errors were made were presented again by the computer, in order to get a complete set of error-free data for the subject.

Derived models from the group model and their psychological implications. The group model in Fig. 5 is a scheme that can be used to order the reaction time needed to recognize the images by any pair of transformations. It is also a scheme used to describe perceptual organization for form recognition. In the present paper, there are three mathematical models: Yale's group model and Sequences (1) and (2). All these three models were independently validated by our experiment. We will show that Sequences (1) and (2) are the derivatives of Yale's group model. As defined in Table 1, I, T, R, SD, M, G, RM, ST, and SP denote the sets of the identity, translations, rotations, screw displacements, reflections, glide reflections, rotary reflections, stretches, and spirals respectively. 

From Fig. 8, we derive the following three principles of psychological implications: 

(a) The reaction time of a subgroup is shorter than that of its supergroup. 

(b) A composite transformation require a longer reaction time than either of its factor transformations. 

(c) Geometric properties may be ordered by their effectiveness in form recognition: uprightness, sense, verticality, position, and size. 

For practical purposes, we develop three rules from the three principles to order any pair of similarity transformations. 

(1) If two transformations belong to different subgroups on a path, the RT of the transformation of the subgroup is shorter than that of the supergroup. Similarly, if two composite transformations have a common factor transformation, the RT of the composite whose other factor transformation is in the subgroup is shorter than the RT of the composite whose other factor transformation is not in the subgroup but is in the supergroup. 

(2) If a transformation is a single transformation and the other one is a composite of which one factor transformation is of the same kind of transformation as the single transformation, the RT of the single transformation is shorter than that of the composite. 

(3) When two transformations belong to two different subgroups on different paths, the RT of the transformation of the subgroup with more effective geometric property is shorter than the RT of the transformation of the subgroup with less effective geometric property.

Theoretical issues and the group model. The two interdependent theoretical issues are those concerning the nature of internal representations that are internally transformed, and those concerning the internal transformations that are performed on those representations. To decide precisely what transformations are internally modeled, we need to determine what structural properties in the representation of the object are preserved during the transformation. 

Since the internal representation embodies important structural features of the three-dimensional object that are not manifest in the two-dimensional projection of that object on the surface of the retina, the structural features of three-dimensional objects in similarity groups in the present study and the corresponding perceptual properties of the internal model will be discussed. The invariants of the similarity groups are angle measure, volume, verticality, sense, uprightness, and position. The similarity group includes the isometry group as a subgroup which was studied by Chen and Chen (1993); many of these structural properties were already defined in that work. The only two additional properties are angle measure and volume. The property angle measure refers to the preservation of the measure of each face angle at each vertex, denoted by m(angle). This structural property of an object gives rise to the perceptual experience of shape. The other geometric property volume is defined with reference to the stimulus object which consisted of seven rigid cubes of the same size fastened face to face. Hence the volume of each cube with an edge e units is expressed as V=e[3] cubic units, and the volume of the stimulus is 7e[3] cubic units. Corresponding to volume is the perceptual property of size. Other isometric group properties are uprightness which preserves top�bottom of an object, verticality with no specification of top�bottom, sense which is related to the mirror image of the identity, and finally position which indicates the location of an object. 

Figure 5 shows the nine characteristic subgroups of the similarity group and their invariants. In the group model, the structure-preserving transformations are illustrated. The more invariants in the structure of an object or its isomorphic internal model, the easier it is to recognize the shape and size of the object. The object identity corresponds to the preservation of the entire structure in the object and hence to the greater restriction on the paths of possible transformations.

As discussed in the section on Group Models, while a normal or invariant subgroup N of a group G is globally invariant by each inner automorphism of G, a characteristic subgroup C of G is globally invariant by every automorphism of G. There are six series of characteristic subgroups in the group model and each series represents a visual path of form recognition. There are six perceptual properties corresponding to the six invariant geometric properties. Each path has five subgroups. With the exception of the translation group, each proper subgroup inherits the properties from its supergroups and brings out one additional property. The translation group brings out two additional properties, uprightness and another preserved property. In path I&T&E[+] &E&S, the translation group preserves uprightness and verticality. In paths I&T&V&E&S and I&T&V& D&S, the translation group preserves uprightness and sense. In path, I&T&D[+] &D&S, the translation group preserves uprightness and volume. 

From the group model, the group of geometric transformations and its preserved properties inherent in the three-dimensional structure of an object are used to explain form perception. The concept, reference frame, is used as a psychological correspondence to the invariant geometric property. When an inverse transformation is applied, an invariant geometric property emerges. The observer's visual experience of this geometric property is termed a frame of reference. In other words, frame of reference refers to an invariant perceptual property. Precisely what geometric transformation is internally modeled may be determined by examining what structural properties in the representation of the object are preserved during the transformation. A main frame with multiple subframes is postulated. The mainframe is composed of several subframes of reference, each indicating a perceptual invariant property such as shape, the sense, verticality, size, uprightness, and position. To achieve size and shape constancies, the effects of transformations must be removed. For instance, a similarity may be decomposed into the product of a reflection, a translation, a rotation, and a stretch. Each of these transformations induces changes in the standard form under identity in a specific way, resulting in ``instability.'' In the perceptual process of form recognition, the human visual system uses mental inverse transformations to remove instabilities from transformed images, to extract the identity group, and to restore them to its standard form, which is the main frame of reference. Pizlo (1994) claims that it is simpler and better to use the inverse transformation than a transformation itself to analyze the relationship between a shape and its image. 

The association between the transformational approach and reference frames lies in the relations among multiple subframes. Each possible Cartesian coordinate system is related to all others by some transformations of the Euclidean similarity group. Transformations can be seen as a way of moving from one subframe to another, and a way of organizing complex information structures consisting of multiple subframes. Subframes serve as ``landmarks'' on perceptual paths in search of standard forms. It should be pointed out that the selection of the characteristic series is not due to the observer's preference. When the observer intuitively notes certain instabilities, he�she selects appropriate inverse transformations to bring out subframes, which are largely determined by the structure of the form. Conclusions. The experimental results showed that reaction time increases linearly with angular differences in orientation for composite forms. Furthermore, the reaction time for image composite displays increases linearly with the number of mental transformations required for recognition of composite forms. Our findings showed that recognition of form depends on the manifestation of subframes by the subgroups of similarity transformations in the order of uprightness, sense, verticality, position, and size. Uprightness provides the best condition for an observer for shape recognition, followed by sense and verticality. While the effect of position is not strong, size has the weakest influence on space form recognition. 

REMARKS ON ECOLOGICAL RELEVANCE 

The ecological approach was articulated as the primary goal of the study of perception by Brunswik (1956) and J. Gibson (1950). They emphasized that as the organism, object, and sources of illumination move about in space, the changes in proximal stimulation bear little resemblance to the particular unidimensional changes of retinal size, wavelength, brightness, or duration that psychophysicists and psychophysiologists have typically manipulated in their laboratories. To Gibson, the notion that we construct our percepts by combining sensory cues reflects psychology's reliance on elementaristic, ecologically invalid laboratory experiments. He claims that in natural settings where we have visibility, free mobility, and persisting illumination, no inference is required because invariants in the optic array specify the layout of the environment. Followers of Gibson's ecological approach confined their studies to the identification and specification of the invariants that are sufficient for veridical perception of the physical environment under favorable conditions. They have not pursued problems raised by neurophysiology and artificial intelligence regarding the mechanisms that individuals use to extract invariants from the sensory displays. 

In striving, to understand the problem of mechanisms involved in extraction of appropriate invariants from the sensory layout, Shepard (1984, 1994) proposed an extension of the ecological approach to the realm of the organism's representation of objects that are not immediately affecting sensory arrays. He argued that we imagine external objects in the same way that we perceive them. Therefore, experiments can be carried out on both perception and imagery by probing individuals with appropriately selected external stimuli (Shepard, 1975, Shepard 6 Cooper, 1982). 

429 

A GROUP MODEL OF SHAPE CONSTANCY 

Shepard (1984, 1994) believes that the external constraints that have been most invariant throughout evolution, such as circadian rhythm, have become most deeply internalized. Because our internal constraints match so well with external constraints in our world, those internalized constraints operate only when external information is degraded or eliminated. The visual system is then guided more by internal constraints when the organism is less tightly controlled from without. The constraints of primary concern to Shepard are those of kinematic geometry (Hunt, 1978) which govern the relative motions of rigid objects, or of local parts of nonrigid objects during brief moments of time. These internalized constraints are demonstrated in experiments in which the two alternating presented stimuli are views of objects differing by complex transformations involving translation, rotation, reflection, expansion, and contraction, and different combinations of these (Bundesen 6 Larsen, 1975; Bundesen, Larsen, 6 Farrell, 1981; Shepard 6 Judd, 1976). Perception therefore cannot sufficiently be described simply as an individual act of picking up an invariant that is present in the particular stimulus. What is perceived is also determined by more general and abstract invariants that have been picked up genetically as a result of biological evolution and individual learning. 

Shepard (1984) further stated that objective performance in experiments requiring imagery indicates that while these images represent external objects and obey constraints on their transformations (Shepard 6 Cooper, 1982), they are quite schematic or abstract. Studies (Cooper 6 Shepard, 1978; Shepard 6 Cooper, 1982) on mental transformations demonstrated that the principles that constrain experienced real or apparent transformations also guide imagined transformations. The trajectories of imagined translations, rotations, dilations, and combinations of these seem to be exactly the occurring trajectories of corresponding rigid apparent motions (Shepard, 1978). 

We proceed with Shepard's idea of extending Gibson's ecological approach to perception into a realm of internal representation. We agree that the most deeply internalized invariants are those that constrain the possible transformations of all possible objects relative to the viewer and not those that characterize a particular object or layout. In our present experimental procedure, a standard form was presented together with a rotated�reflected image of the standard form, and the subject was required to respond to the same�different object by an appropriate - key press. 

In contrast to this type of discrimination procedure was the three-pattern configuration procedure used by Wagemans et al. (1997). They used two patterns below the standard pattern, and these two patterns were images of the standard form by perspective or projective transformations or of a new pseudo-random pattern. Their shape-equivalence procedure conforms more to the natural setting where the subject can observe the external patterns and make faster discrimination. 

In the same�different type of discrimination task, if an observer could directly visualize the inherent structure of an object without regard to point of view, then the observer could immediately perceive whether two subjects were of the same shape regardless of their respective orientations. However, such perceiving in our experimental task and in others' (e.g., Shepard 6 Cooper. 1982) did not seem immediate; the time taken to determine such sameness increased markedly with 

430 

CHEN AND CHEN 

variations in presented orientation. This type of discrimination task involving selfinduced geometrical transformations of the ``ambient optic array'' as Gibson (1979) observed are probably the most ubiquitous of the transformations with which the highly mobile human visual system must cope. Such apparent motion might be primarily governed by the more abstract and widely manifested constraints of kinematic geometry for three-dimensional space, and not by the principles of Newtonian mechanics (Shepard, 1984). Kinematic geometry is a branch of mathematics characterizing the motions that are geometrically possible and simplest given sets of objects and of constraints of their possible motions. 

According to Shepard (1994, p. 10), ``apparent motion may seem lacking in ecological validity in a world in which material objects do not go discontinuously in and out of existence. Yet, even in a natural environment, significant objects may be only intermittently visible��as when they are behind wind-blown foliage, for example.'' Apparent motions experienced in the laboratory where there is no external support are just the conditions that reveal the internalized kinematics of the mind and provide for the possibility of an invariant psychological law (Shepard, 1994).
