MCGILL  University of California San Diego, California 92037  Forced-choice psychometric functions are analyzed for the case of auditory energy detection in which a noise-filled interval is compared with a sine wave in noise. The psychometric functions are shown to have polynomial form. Probability of a correct choice is determined by the ratio of signal energy to noise power per unit bandwidth. The degree of the polynomial is fixed by the degrees of freedom of the noise. Special cases are computed and illustrated.  1. INTRODUCTION  The polynomial, so easily denounced as a shopworn empirical device suited only to curve-fitters and statistical consultants, has reappeared in psychophysics with new theoretical justification. The psychophysical application, as might be expected, turns out to be a highly specialized polynomial expression. Polynomial form does, however, seem to be nature's way in auditory energy detection, and the curve-fitters were perhaps not so far wrong after all.  Polynomial psychometric functions are found in auditory forced-choice. To see how they arise, we suppose that two brief, noise-filled intervals are presented to a listener who operates as a simple energy sensing device. In one interval a sinusoid is mixed with the noise; in the other, it is omitted. Our energy detector examines both intervals, selecting the larger sample of energy as the signal. This is the format of a standard forced-choice experiment. The first problem is to find the probability that the detector identifies the signal correctly, or more precisely, to find an expression (i.e., the psychometric function) relating detection probability to signal energy. Interest in such expressions derives from the view that auditory intensity discrimination, seeming in many respects to resemble energy detection, might then behave according to formula.  Auditory psychophysics has been preoccupied for more than a decade with the numbers characterizing forced-choice detectability of brief sinusoids in noise. A 1000 Hz tone, 100 msec in duration, and masked by a continuous white noise, has a detection threshold of approximately EIN, = 10, where E stands for signal energy, and No signifies noise power per unit bandwidth. The empirical value of the ratio is found to be indepedent of the background noise for all but the weakest noise levels.  1968 by Academic Press Inc.  369  4801513-1  370 MCGILL  Moreover, the shape of the psychometric function is also independent of the background noise level. Roughly speaking, forced-choice detectability seems to grow by about 5 % per decibel increase in signal intensity.  These data are strikingly simple and call for a simple explanation. The simplest current view is that they can be interpreted as phenomena of energy detection; see, for example, Green (1960), Pfafflin and Mathews (1962), Jeffress (1964, 1968), Green and Swets (1966, Chap. 8), McGill (1968). The present state of these explanatory efforts is as follows. Working via the principles of energy detection we can calculate the fixed threshold value EIN, = 10, and interpret the numerical constant as a bandwidth. We can also approximate the energy detector's psychometric function and confirm that its slope is just about what the data would predict. These arguments are presented in Green and Swets (1966, pp. 220-222). More recently an exact form has been developed for the signal-in-noise psychometric function (McGill, 1968). In McGill's paper the probability of a correct response from an energy detector operating in two alternative forced-choice is represented as the sum of an infinite series. The present paper attempts to refine our representation of the psychometric function still further by showing it to be a polynomial. The foregoing brief account of recent work on the energy statistics of short bursts of noise cannot pretend to convey either the subtleties of the problem or the agonies suffered by psychologists working in audition as they attempted to unravel their noise statistics. An extraordinary mismatch exists between the major interests of the engineering and mathematical literature on noise, and the requirements dictated by the apparent mode of operation of the ear. Communication engineers concentrate on the statistical properties of "instantaneous" amplitudes, largely because modern electronic equipment can easily isolate amplitude slivers of a random process. Mathematicians on the other hand are primarily interested in the limiting properties of noise over infinite time. The ergodic theorem is one good example. Noise spectra and autocorrelation functions are another.  Our noncentral chi-square argument (outlined above) happens to be a noise property ideally suited to energy accumulated over durations of the order of 100-200msec in the case of auditory stimuli. It is thus not ordinarily handled in mathematical papers on noise. Despite this, the essentials of the argument may be worked out by extrapolating from the classic paper of Rice (1944, 1945) on mathematical analysis of random noise. Rice's results, however, are given for the instantaneous properties of the noise envelope. It is rather unfortunate that the meaning of Rice's formulas, and their potential connection with the distribution of energy integrated over several oscillations of a noise waveform, were not fully appreciated until recently. Similar clues were also to be found in Peterson, Birdsall, and Fox (1954), a paper that psychophysicists frequently cite but seldom read carefully. Empirical psychometric functions for forced-choice detection of brief 1000 Hz tones in noise (see Green and Swets, 1966, p. 188, Fig. 7-3) are located near the theoretical curve for v = 100 in Fig. 1. The empirical curves also seem t0 have very nearly the same shape as those in Fig. 1. Since v = WT, our energy detection arguments permit estimation of the width of a critical band. The width proves to be roughly  376 MCGILL  700-1000 Hz for a test frequency of 1000 Hz. This figure should be compared with 60-150 Hz which is the commonly accepted range for the 1000 Hz critical band. Bandwidths determined via detection experiments may be (and probably are) inflated by effects due to imperfect memory or other transformations of the stimulus by the nervous system, as well as by adjustments produced when the ear attempts to match itself to a brief signal. The results are surprisingly good given the many points at which arguments like this might go off the track.  There is an irresistible temptation to deal with the psychometric functions in Fig. 1 as normal ogives, which they resemble. It would be difficult to say whether the normal ogive is as shopworn a device as the polynomial for fitting psychometric functions. Certainly they are close rivals. In view of such rivalry it seems only fair (and not in the least surprising) to note that the polynomial expression in Eq. 8 can easily be shown to have a normal limit as the degrees of freedom parameter v = WT increases. Hence polynomials become ogives and the humdrum is found to be clever and perhaps even beautiful if only the circumstances are chosen properly.
