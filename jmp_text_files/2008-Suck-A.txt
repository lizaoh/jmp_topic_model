Imagine a social scientist browsing the shelves of a book store hitting upon a book entitled ‘Media theory’. He is likely to pick it, open it at a randomly chosen page and if it is the book under review he will soon close and replace it. No, these hieroglyphic formulae, this assemblage of definitions and theorems are not what he expected and what he deems to add to his insights in the media. On the other hand a mathematician sauntering along the same shelves in search of new mathematical publications will with high probability leave this book unopened, not even skimming through it. In doing so she will miss a chance to learn about a new mathematical structure with interesting applications. Both scenarios are sort of a lost opportunity because both could have profited from reading this book. We will explain in this review why.

A medium in the sense of this book has two ingredients: a set of states and a set of tokens which somehow transform the states into other states. For each token there is a reverse, i.e., a token which reverses the result of applying the first. This transformation of states is ruled by two conditions: Firstly, one can go from any state to any other state by repeatedly applying tokens to the initial state; secondly, if a sequence of tokens is applied to a state such that the original state is recovered, then for each token in this sequence the reverse is also in it. Below we give these conditions more formally and more precisely.

We venture another example: Imagine some disease which a patient can have in various stages. The stages are the states of this medium, tokens are therapeutic measures which transform a patient from one state to another (hopefully better) state; omitting a therapeutic measure may also transform some of the states. A therapy is a sequence of tokens (therapeutic measures). Possibly for some diseases this structure obeys the rules [Ma] and [Mb], thus modelling it by a medium in the sense of this book.

In Chapter 5 which follows  Doignon and Falmagne (1997) , several well-known and important families of relations are shown to be well-graded families. Consequently they can be represented as media and are therefore important examples of this structure. The families investigated are the partial orders, the biorders, the interval orders, and the semiorders. All of these are familiar to readers of this journal; there are many papers in measurement and scaling which utilize these families. This chapter ends with an interesting and non-trivial counter-example: a family of relations — the almost connected orders — which are shown not to form a well-graded family. Thus, it seems that there is some ‘meta-theorem’ behind these special examples telling apart by structural conditions the well-graded families from the others. Perhaps this observation can be constructed as an open problem. So far there don’t seem to be candidates for necessary or sufficient conditions in the ballpark.

In applications a medium is naturally finite. Some of the examples given in the book are infinite; all are discrete. This flavor of the theory seems to be in tune with the intentions of the authors although so far nothing prevents us from invoking a continuous structure on a medium. A converse of this theorem is also proved: For each mediatic graph a token system can be constructed which is in fact a medium. The correspondence between mediatic graphs and media is bijective.

The aim of the next two chapters of the book under review is to embed the graph of a medium into something more familiar, something which one can handle in a more leisurely way, perhaps closer to intuition than the given medium and its graph. The structures which turn out to provide an adequate background are the graphs of hypercubes and integer lattices. In the course of this development the theory attains a more geometric character. As already mentioned, the purpose of these representations of media is to obtain a tool for visualization of large media. The two-dimensional concepts are characteristics or coefficients describing the complexity of a medium. Investigating their properties amounts to honing the tool’s edge. The cutting comes with the Chapters 10 and 11 dealing with algorithmic aspects of media. The authors utilize the fairly well-known and intensively studied graph algorithms, because of their close relation to graphs. However, they are skilfully adapted to the specific properties of mediatic graphs. The authors study input parameters (number of states or vertices, number of tokens and number of edges) and–very technically–input representations. The final chapter of the book details two quite different applications of media theory: learning spaces and analysis of opinion polls. In both cases the theory of random walks on media is extensively utilized.

The theory of knowledge spaces (learning spaces form a subset thereof) was developed during the last 20 or so years by Falmagne and Doignon (cf. Doignon and Falmagne (1999)). It is a mostly discrete theory with a potential to replace the classical theory of psychological tests and item response theory. At present it can be very successfully applied to ability and achievement assessments. In the course of streamlining the theory to make its application to relatively large item sets possible and for pedagogical reasons, well-graded knowledge spaces played a major role. We know already that well-gradedness is just one way of representing media. It seems that these problems were the incentive to develop media theory, at least for Jean-Claude Falmagne. A knowledge space is a union of a closed collection of subsets of a given set  
                         Q 
                       (of problems or questions) containing  
                         ∅ 
                       and  
                         Q 
                      . These subsets are interpreted as the possible knowledge states that a person can be in. They are partially ordered by set inclusion. Actually, they form a lattice, with union being its supremum.

A learning space is also a collection of subsets of  
                         Q 
                       with  
                         ∅ 
                       and  
                         Q 
                       which satisfies two conditions. The first says that if the state of a student is included in another state then he or she can reach this state by learning one item at a time. The second condition stipulates that if a particular item is learnable from a given state then it can be reached from any state above the given state by learning more items. Clearly, both conditions can be formulated more formally and unambiguously in terms of subsets and items. But their meaning is essentially captured in these words and it is kind of obvious that they are sound pedagogical requirements for a field of knowledge or a part of a school or university curriculum. Thus, they have an intuitive appeal for the intended applications.

It is shown (Section 4.2) that learning spaces essentially characterize the well-graded knowledge spaces. This result is adapted from  Cosyn and Uzun (submitted for publication) . The subsets of the space are the states of a medium and, in this interpretation, the possible knowledge states of individuals who have to learn some subject at school.

The construction of a suitable knowledge space or, preferably, learning space is an arduous task. For  
                         n 
                       items the collection of subsets among the

                               n 
                            
                         
                       subsets has to be found which describe possible knowledge states. (Think of  
                         n 
                       as some number between 20, say, and 250.) The method mostly applied for constructing a knowledge space (a so called querying procedure) fails to go directly for learning spaces. It constructs either a too narrow class of spaces (ordinal spaces) or — with much increased effort — a better fitting knowledge space which is often not well graded. To add to the difficulty, the ordinal spaces have often too many states which is a drawback when it comes to assessing the state of a student. It seems that in this area a lot of problems await theoretical and practical solutions. The algorithms for visualization of media described in Chapter 11 are used as a tool in representing learning spaces.

When the knowledge space is constructed (which is always a demanding enterprise) it can be utilized to assess the knowledge state of a student. This task is solved as a form of adaptive testing, drawing on the Markov chain results on media.

In summary, the sections on this application allow a glimpse into the working kitchen where theoretical innovation and practical sophistication nicely blend.

The second application deals with data gathered during campaigning for the 1992 presidential election in the US. This campaign saw three candidates (Clinton, Bush, Perot) and a random sample of the population of potential voters were asked twice within a short time interval for their preferences among the candidates. In fact the data were such that a weak order of them could be inferred at each questioning. The model for these data was a medium whose states are the 13 labelled weak orders of three elements (the candidates). It was assumed that for each candidate two tokens existed, one pushing him up and one down one step (these tokens were assumed ineffective in a state where this candidate could not be pushed up or down). From the data the number of respondents was determined whose preference weak orders were consistent with two neighboring weak orders in this medium (neighboring in the sense that the action of one such token transforms the first order into the second. According to the asymptotic theorem, continuous random walks on a medium are applied and the parameters estimated. With these estimates the expected numbers are calculated and compared. The results are not particularly striking, but they point to a number of refinements. The presentation of this example is mainly for illustration; the details and the very interesting discussion are published in the literature (cf.  Regenwetter, Falmagne, & Grofman, 1999; Hsu, Falmagne, & Regenwetter, 1993 , and others)).

The book takes its readers a long way from motivational examples at the beginning to the formal definition, algebraic, combinatorial, and geometric representations, to algorithmic problems, to several intuitive ways of visualizing media, and finally to serious applications. En route, one may find some obstacles and it is not always easy to follow, although the authors invest a lot in explaining their ideas. However, it is always rewarding to take the trouble to understand the mathematics behind everything because it deals with a fascinating subject. Furthermore, the authors lead the reader over or around every stepping stone that may lie on the way.

The exhaustive mathematical development can even be construed as a drawback of the book: After reading most of it I asked myself: Where are the open problems? Indeed, unsolved questions are hardly mentioned. The reason for this may be that there are none left. Given the authors’ skills one may think so. But all questions answered would be a rare situation in mathematics.

I want to mention a few places where, on my understanding, further research might be worthwhile. The first was already mentioned above: to find conditions for families of relations to satisfy well-gradedness. The second is the problem of querying experts efficiently for a well-graded knowledge space.  Koppen (1998)  discusses this question. According to the material presented in this context in the book it is still unsolved. Next, there seems to be a possibility to exploit the Shannon information on the probability distribution on the set of tokens. The final item on my problem list is the question of characterizing media as matroids. An independence system is a family of sets closed under subsets. Essentially, it is a matroid when an exchange condition (known in linear algebra for linear independent vectors under the name ‘Steinitz exchange property’) is satisfied. The authors mention tangentially a relation between independence systems and media in connection with well-graded families. It is quite obvious (via mediatic graphs) that media can be associated with matroids. But how exactly they can be separated from ‘non-mediatic matroids’ seems non-trivial.

We would like to follow our two fictitious customers in the book store from the very beginning of this review, pat them on their shoulders and urge them to have a bit more than a cursory look at this book. The mathematician will find a nicely expounded theory with many ramifications. The theorems and proofs are clear and exhaustive, well balanced between rigor and intuition. They combine and draw on quite different parts of mathematics. For the social scientist we must be a bit more tutorial. Unless he is well trained in mathematical thinking he will have difficulties following this tour through media theory. But the applications show that rather different areas of his research fields can profit from this book. Media can be a model for many subjects. So the best we can do for both scientists is to make them join forces and work together. This book has the potential of a script and score for this joint work.
