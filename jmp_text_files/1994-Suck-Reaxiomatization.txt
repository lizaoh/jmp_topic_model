A Reaxiomatization of Portfolio Theory 

REINHARD SUCK AND ASTRID GETTA 

University of Osnabriick 

An axiom system is developed which casts Portfolio Theory in the framework of conjoint measurement. According to these axioms a set of gambles with uncertain outcomes can be represented with aspects of riskiness and expectation as components which can be used to predict or explain preferential choice. The axioms are qualitative, the representation is numerical. The system is based on similar conditions proposed by van Santen (1978, Journal of Mathematical Psychology, 17, 14-20) which are shown to be insufficient to yield the representation characteristic of Portfolio Theory, © 1994 Academic Press, Inc. 

1. INTRODUCTION 

In Portfolio Theory aspects of riskiness of a decision and expected gain or loss are combined to describe or explain preferential choice data, as shown in Fig. 1. 

To be more precise, Portfolio Theory is concerned with three weak orders <,, <,, <, denoting the ordering of the elements of a set G of gambles with respect to expected value, riskiness, and preference. The latter two are thought to be provided by the subjects in a suitably run experiment. Alternatively the model can be used to get the risk order by unfolding the preferences. 

The description given in Coombs (1969, 1975) of how risk and expectation interact to influence the preferences of the subject has come under criticism (cf. Lopes, 1984; Luce, 1980). Despite this criticism the theory has influenced empirical research and seems in some respects more flexible than competing theories; in particular, the concept of conjointness in which the gambles are composed of components which account for their different features seems to be unique to Portfolio Theory. In the present paper this topic plays a major role. One of the characteristic tenets of Portfolio Theory is the “single peakedness” of the curves of preference indifference. It has been investigated theoretically and empirically by Aschenbrenner (1981, 1984) and Coombs & Avrunin (1977a, 1977b), among others. 

Van Santen (1978) presented a set of qualitative axioms which guarantee the representation of (G, <,, <., <,) as a structure of the form depicted in Fig. 1. However, this axiomatization is not really sufficient for a representation (we give a counterexample). Despite this shortcoming van Santen’s (1978) idea is interesting
