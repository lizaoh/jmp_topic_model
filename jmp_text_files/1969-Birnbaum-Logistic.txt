JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY: 6,258-276(1969)  Statistical Theory for Logistic Mental Test Models with a Prior Distribution of Ability'  ALLAN BIRNBAUM2  New York University, New York, New York 10012  In previous work the logistic model of a mental test was introduced, and statistical inference methods were developed without  the assumption of a prior distribution of ability. Such a distribution is assumed in the present paper, and corresponding  statistical inference methods and distribution theory are developed. Results include detailed investigation of posterior  distributions, given observed response patterns; pointestimation of ability, and its mean-squared error; and distributions of  test scores and their moments. Some computational methods and detailed examples are given.  1. INTRODUCTION  A general logistic test model was introduced by the present writer in Chapters 17 through 20 of Lord and Novick (1968), where  statistical theory and techniques were developed for classification and estimation of an examinee's unknown ability level 0,  for the logistic and other test models. Those methods were not based on an assumption of a probability distribution of  abilities 0.  When such an assumption is in fact made, it is appropriate and efficient to make use of it in the formulation and solution of  problems of classification and estimation of abilities and related problems of test design. The present paper presents in  selfcontained form some statistical theory for such a case. This is given in terms of the logistic test model, which has  advantages of mathematical tractability over the usually considered normal ogive model. MOMENTS OF THE DISTRIBUTION OF SCORES  We have had no occasion thus far to use moments of the distribution of scores. Such moments are not required in either the  exact theoretical, or the approximate numerical, developments of the distributions of scores in the preceding Sec. 4 and in  the following Sec. 6 (which is independent of the present section). (The approximations of Sec. 6 make heavy use of moments  and cumulants of VI,k.a, but not those of X.) Likewise, the derivations in Secs. 2-3 of the distribution g(B I x), its  moments, and the moments of the estimator J(X) required no use of moments of X.  However, there are other problems which give interest to derivations and computations of moments of test scores. Indeed, weak  true-score theory (e.g. Lord and Novick, 1968, Chs. 3-11), in contrast with strong true-score theory of which the present  paper is a part, is characterized mathematically by its use just of low-order moments and mixed moments of scores and  abilities.
