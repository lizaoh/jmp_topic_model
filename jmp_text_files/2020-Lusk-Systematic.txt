Although fundamental differences in the timing of sub- and supra-second durations have been proposed (e.g.,  Buhusi & Meck, 2005; Cordes & Meck, 2014; Gibbon, Malapani, Dale, & Gallistel, 1997; Ivry & Spencer, 2004; Lewis & Miall, 2003a, 2003b; Mauk & Buonomano, 2004; Wiener, Turkeltaub, & Coslett, 2010 ) there are clear examples where the accuracy and precision of timing appear to be very similar across these time scales (e.g.,  Gupta, 2014; Melgire et al., 2005 ). Numerous task dimensions including attention, memory, decision-making, and response strategies can differentially affect performance across multiple time scales (e.g.,  Coull, Cheng, & Meck, 2011; Coull, Vidal, Nazarian, & Macar, 2004; Gupta, 2014; Harrington, Zimbelman, Hinton, & Rao, 2010; Koch, Oliveri, & Caltagirone, 2009; Livesey, Wall, & Smith, 2007; Pouthas et al., 2005 ) making it difficult to isolate the underlying timing mechanisms. One psychophysical procedure used to examine these potential differences is the temporal bisection procedure (e.g.,  Allan & Gibbon, 1991; Church & Deluty, 1977; Meck, 1983; Penney & Cheng, 2018 ) which produces sigmoidal functions that vary in form and location based upon task parameters. Of particular interest is measuring accuracy and precision of the sigmoidal function. The point of subjective equality (PSE), or the duration a subject is equally likely to categorize as ‘short’ or ‘long’ (P(long)  
                         = 
                       0.5) provides a measure of accuracy. The difference limen (DL), a measure of variability, is calculated from the sigmoidal function by subtracting the signal duration at which 25% of responses were ‘long’ responses from the signal duration at which 75% were ‘long’ responses, and then dividing this value by 2. The Weber fraction (WF), a measure of relative variability used to compare precision across different conditions, can be obtained by dividing the DL by the PSE ( Ng, Tobin, & Penney, 2011; Paule et al., 1999; Raslear, 1983, 1985 ).

While the PSE, DL, and WF are the “gold standards” for evaluating temporal bisection data, a recent meta-analysis of temporal bisection in humans revealed that parameters such as the separation of the anchor durations and spacing of intermediate probe durations alter both the PSE and WF ( Kopec & Brody, 2010 ). Interestingly, these authors proposed a model that can account for variations based on task parameters, however they did not consider how these variations may have arisen from other sources. For instance, the Kopec and Brody study did not examine the effects of duration range or model type on the obtained psychometric functions. As we will demonstrate below, crossing through duration ranges, initial model choice, or individual vs. group value calculation can drastically alter results. Consequently, we designed a more systematic study exploring the strengths and weaknesses of the most commonly used models for fitting temporal bisection functions. We sought to isolate differences between sub-second and supra-second time scales on the form and location of the bisection function while holding other parameters such as anchor ratio, stimulus type, probe spacing, and probability of reinforcement constant. Moreover, by implementing a within-subject design alternating daily between sub-second (200 ms vs. 800 ms) and supra-second (2 s vs. 8 s) training sessions we were also able to control for performance variations related to temporal order and contextual calibration effects, which may occur when presenting the different signal ranges in separate blocks of sessions (e.g.,  Cordes & Meck, 2014 ) or intermixing blocks of different ranges within the same session (e.g.,  Gu, Jurkowski, Lake, Malapani, & Meck, 2015; Jazayeri & Shadlen, 2010 ).

Our selection of models included (1) a traditional  logarithmic four parameter sigmoid (L4P) model  which often provides excellent fits to the data and reasonable approximations of the PSE, DL, and WF. Such atheoretical fitting, while providing a useful means of quantification, provides little explanatory power in relation to specific timing mechanisms (e.g.,  Penney, Brown, & Wong, 2014; Penney, Gibbon, & Meck, 2000; Pleil, Cordes, Meck, & Williams, 2011 ). (2) The  sample known exactly (SKE) model  which posits that the bisection task is accomplished by the subject comparing the current trial’s clock reading to memory distributions for the learned ‘short’ and ‘long’ anchor durations. Variables in the SKE model are useful for assessing the probability of attention and response bias as well as clock and memory variability for the anchor durations ( Allan & Gibbon, 1991; Gibbon, 1981; Meck, 1983; Penney et al., 2000; Penney, Gibbon, & Meck, 2008 ). (3) The  pseudo logistic model (PLM)  which uses parameters that approximate the PSE and WF, while allowing contributions from scalar, non-scalar, and constant variability (e.g.,  Brown et al., 2011; Es-seddiqi, El Massioui, Samson, Brown, & Doyère, 2016; Höhn et al., 2011; Killeen, Fetterman, & Bizo, 1997 ). The PLM is particularly useful to tease apart the contributions of different sources of variability in sub- vs. supra-second timing. (4) The final model studied is the  drift–diffusion model (DDM) . The DDM model describes the accumulation of evidence using a noisy integration process that moves toward either the upper response threshold or the lower response threshold. As soon as the decision variable crosses one of the thresholds, the corresponding response is initiated, and the threshold-crossing determines the reaction time, which is an integral part of the DDM analysis not taken into account by the other models (e.g.,  Akdoğan & Balcı, 2016; Balcı& Simen, 2014 ).

Taken together, these four methods/models represent the state-of-the-art in the field in terms of analyzing psychometric functions for temporal discrimination. Although other methods and/or models have been developed to fit temporal bisection data under various conditions (e.g.,  Allman, DeLeon, & Wearden, 2011; Brown, McCormack, Smith, & Stewart, 2005; Droit-Volet & Wearden, 2001; Miller & Ulrich, 2001; Penney, Yim, & Ng, 2014; Wearden, 1991; Wearden & Ferrara, 1995, 1996 ) they may be considered variations on these approaches. Consequently, our goal is to determine the extent to which these models vary in their ability to fit temporal bisection data obtained from subjects tested using sub- and supra-second durations ranges while holding non-temporal stimulus properties constant (e.g., intensity, modality, probability, and spacing). Moreover, through an exploration of different timing and decision models we aim to provide a general-purpose framework to guide investigators looking for the most efficient and robust methods for analyzing temporal bisection data.

Twenty-four experimentally-naïve male Sprague–Dawleyrats ( 
                            ∼ 
                         9 weeks old, 300–320g) served as subjects. Rats were housed in pairs in a temperature-controlled room, under a 12 h light/dark cycle; all experiments took place during the light phase of the cycle. Rats were provided free access to water in their home cages and were maintained at 85% of their ad lib weight by restricting access to food. Experiments were conducted in accordance with standard procedures approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee at Duke University.

The apparatus consisted of 8 standard lever boxes (Model ENV-007, MED Associates, Inc., Albans, VT) housed in light and sound attenuating cubicles (Model ENV-019, MED Associates, Albans, VT). Each lever box is equipped with two response levers situated on the front wall of the box on either side of a food cup, attached to a pellet dispenser (45 mg precision food pellets, Research Diets, Inc. New Brunswick, NJ). A noise amplifier/speaker system is installed in each lever box, mounted on the opposite wall from the levers.

A Windows XP (Microsoft, Redmond, WA) based computer system running MED-PC Version IV Research Control & Data Acquisition System software (Med Associates, St. Albans, VT) attached to an electronic interface is used to control the experimental equipment and record the data. Signals consisted of white noise stimuli presented at a volume of 20 dB above background noise.

Rats first received combined magazine and lever training in 30 min sessions. During each session, one food pellet is dispensed automatically once each minute and in addition, every lever press produced a pellet. One lever is extended into the chamber at a time until 10 presses were made (producing 10 food pellets) and the lever retracted and the other lever is extended until 10 reinforced presses were made. When all rats lever-pressed at least 100 times within the session, they were transitioned to two-signal discrimination training.

Rats were trained to discriminate between ‘short’ and ‘long’ anchor durations that alternated daily between a sub-second (200 ms vs. 800 ms) and a supra-second (2 s vs. 8 s) range. During these 2hr training sessions, one lever is consistently associated with the ‘short’ anchor duration (200 ms or 2 s) and the other lever is consistently associated with ‘long’ anchor duration (800 ms or 8 s) with lever associations counterbalanced across subjects. Trials began with the onset of a white-noise signal that lasted for one of the two anchor durations, randomly selected with equal probability. Upon offset of the signal, the levers were extended into the box and subjects were allowed to make one lever response. Correct responses (e.g., a ‘long’ lever response preceded by a ‘long’ signal duration) were reinforced with a food pellet, whereas no reinforcement followed incorrect responses. Failure to respond within 8 s resulted in retraction of the levers and the start of a random duration intertrial interval that varied between 15–35 s (geometrically distributed). Once rats achieved  
                            > 
                         80% correct responses for both ‘short’ and ‘long’ durations they were transitioned to seven-signal training and testing. Rats were trained for a median of 24 sessions.

Upon reaching criterion performance for two-signal discrimination training subjects were transitioned to the test phase where 50% of trials were unreinforced probe signals of intermediate duration that were geometrically spaced between the reinforced anchor durations. Sub-second probe durations: 260, 320, 400, 500, and 640 ms. Supra-second probe durations: 2.6, 3.2, 4.0, 5.0, and 6.4 s. No reward is given for any lever presses following probe durations and is only delivered for correct responses following anchor durations. Subjects presumably classified these intermediate probe durations as being closer to the ‘short’ or the ‘long’ anchor duration — thus measuring their ability to discriminate between the anchor durations. The duration that subjects classified as being ‘short’ (or ‘long’) 50% of the time is referred to as the point of subjective equality (PSE) or bisection point and reflects the subjective middle between the anchor durations ( Church & Deluty, 1977; Meck, 1983 ). Rats were tested for 10 sessions.

Sessions with greater than 40 omissions were rare with all animals receiving between 10–12 testing days. As the models used contained varying numbers of parameters, a standardized quantitative assessment across models was necessary. To investigate the amount of information loss in each model, a derivation of the Akaike Information Criterion (AICc) was applied, which accounts for the small sample size to parameter ratio. However, as the DDM fits a different set of data than the other three models, it was most appropriate to separate the analysis into two sets, decision models (i.e., DDMs) and timing models (i.e., SKE, PLM, and L4P). As there are multiple versions of the SKE and PLM models, for comparison we used the models that produced the lowest AICc value for each duration. Though the adjacent S values may seem to provide a contradictory interpretation of the fits, it should be noted that the S values provide a measure of the sampled data while the AICc values are reflecting the strength of evidence for a model given the data. That is, if the population was to be modeled rather than a sample, the AIC and S values would converge on the same model.

Though the PLM provides an impressive amount of explanatory power in terms of understanding the location of the PSE, the location of the unbiased PSE at the HM conflicts with most of the current timing literature, which places the PSE at either the GM or trending toward the AM ( Kopec & Brody, 2018 ). A possible explanation is that if one were to time all intervals perfectly, the HM would be achieved, but because task components such as signal detection and attention to the “to-be-timed” signal are variable most likely resulting in time “loss”, subjects will nearly always display a rightward shift in their psychometric functions which would place the PSE at the GM for relatively small deviations from unbiased timing to the AM for larger deviations.

The ability to perceive time only becomes useful if temporal information can be used to make decisions that aid fitness and survival ( Bateson, 2003 ). Moreover, these decisions are the sole observable output of the timing system, thereby making it essential to incorporate response rules into timing models (e.g.,  Balcı& Simen, 2016; Freestone & Church, 2016 ). Decision stages represented by DDMs have been used to effectively model a variety of timing tasks across multiple signal modalities (e.g.,  Akdoğan & Balcı, 2016; Balcı& Simen, 2014, 2016; Luzardo, Ludvig, & Rivest, 2013; Matthews & Meck, 2016; Simen, Rivest, Ludvig, Balı, & Killeen, 2013; Simen, Vlasov, & Papadakis, 2016 ), implying a common underlying decision process for timing. Yet, to our knowledge a direct comparison of DDMs across both sub- and supra-second duration ranges has not been tested, and would strengthen the claim for a unified evidence accumulation process for interval-timing behavior.

In order to model choice responses in the temporal bisection procedure, the diffusion model analysis toolbox (DMAT) in Matlab was used to fit psychometric functions across sub- and supra-second duration ranges ( Vandekerckhove & Tuerlinckx, 2008 ). Only responses with latencies  
                            < 
                         3 s were analyzed because responses with longer latencies have been shown to not be well controlled by signal duration ( Maricq, Roberts, & Church, 1981 ). In addition to evaluating three different DDMs, we also used the invcdfdif function to compute the response latencies shown in  Fig. 6  ( Vandekerckhove & Tuerlinckx, 2008 ).

The DDMs have a potential of seven free parameters, but at most three were allowed to vary across signal durations. The key DDM parameters include non-decision time ( Ter ), drift rate ( v ), and starting point ( z ) and are designed to account for reaction time and choice proportion in the temporal bisection task.  Ter  represents the time that is not directly involved in making the decision such as sensory-motor processing required of the task. The amount of time required to make a decision is determined by the  v  which accounts for the direction and rate of evidence accumulation. Therefore, a negative drift rate will move toward a ‘short’ response and a positive drift rate will accumulate toward a ‘long’ response. Similarly, a large absolute value of drift rate will generate a faster response, as a threshold is reached sooner. This evidence accumulation has a starting point (z) and result in accumulation toward a lower or an upper threshold ( a ) or decision boundary. The starting point lies between the lower boundary (0) and the upper boundary ( a ). As we are using a 2nd stage decision model, evidence has already been accumulated for the duration of the signal, therefore we allow  z  to vary in order to reflect this accumulation ( Balcı& Simen, 2014 ). It is important to note that this does not reflect an inherent response bias in timing, but rather a change in response likelihood resulting from the evidence. Therefore, we expect  z and v  to be correlated, reflecting connected decision processes during and after signal presentation. We do not allow  a  to vary as a function of signal duration because it implies that the animals know the signal duration prior to the decision being made. That is, they have evidence about the signal and knowledge of where the threshold is which would suggest they could make a decision without further evidence accumulation.

These DDMs restricted trial-to-trial variability in the standard deviation of drift rate (ETA), range of starting points (sZ), and range of non-decision time (sT), such that they were all set to 0.0001. The models differed in terms of whether or not they allowed  Ter  (DDM1),  z  (DDM1, DDM2), or neither (DDM3), to vary across signal duration ranges. In our DDM models  v  was always allowed to vary across signal duration ranges and was assumed to reflect a decision process determined by the encoded signal duration. With these parameter restrictions we were able to examine how the subjects were making decisions based on accumulation of evidence, and non-decision time across sub- and supra-second duration ranges.

The most noteworthy finding is that both sub- and supra-second duration ranges showed the best fit with DDM1, which allows the same free parameters ( v ,  z, Ter ) to vary as shown in  Fig. 5 . Moreover, in both cases the drift rate increasing nearly linearly as a function of signal duration, reaching a value of zero in close approximation of the PSE. It is even more interesting that drift rate can account for the major differences observed between the sub- and supra-second duration ranges. This occurred without requiring an initial response bias for either duration range, suggesting a similarity in the way signals are timed across duration ranges. None of the parameters were fixed between sub- and supra-second duration ranges, based on the assumption that different neural circuits may contribute in varying degrees for sub- and supra-second timing ( Petter, Lusk, Hesslow, & Meck, 2016 ).

Other models (not shown here) where  Ter  as opposed to  z  were allowed to vary did not provide as good fits as DDM2. DDM3 allowed only  v  to vary and while not explaining the data as parsimoniously as the other two models it perhaps reflects evidence accumulation through  v  is the most essential, as no other single parameter model (not shown here) is able to perform as well as DDM3. The rest of the discussion will largely pertain to DDM1 as it is the most parsimonious model.

Consistent with previous findings, the intermediate durations within a range seem to be best predicted by the DDMs, compared with the anchor durations (e.g.,  Akdoğan & Balcı, 2016; Balcı& Simen, 2014 ). This may be the result of training differences between the reinforced anchor durations and the unreinforced probe durations. The ability of drift–diffusion to achieve this level of precision in modeling choice suggests that rodents and humans are engaging in similar evidence accumulation processes using noisy signals to drift toward a decision threshold. Moreover, the successful application of DDMs to interval timing suggests important commonalities between temporal cognition and other domains of information processing and decision-making (e.g.,  Matthews & Meck, 2014; Meck, Doyère, & Gruart, 2012 ).

In addition to  v  there is also a systematic variation in  z  when it is allowed to vary in DDM1 and 2.  z,  is correlated with drift rate and reflects evidence accumulated during the signal duration. In this way longer signal durations should push  z  closer to the upper threshold, whereas shorter durations result in z closer to 0, or the lower threshold. Therefore, as expected  z  for supra-second durations follows a steeper slope, suggesting more evidence can be accumulated during longer durations.

The other DDM parameter we allowed to vary was non-decision time which showed a relatively flat trend for sub- vs. supra-second durations decrease as signal duration increased in the sub-second range but not the supra-second range. As the supra-second durations increase there is an initial dip in  Ter  followed by an increase. Such a trend may be governed by having high  z  and  v  rate for longer durations, but also having fairly high reaction times for short responses during these longer durations. Therefore, the only way to account for a shift in reaction times is through  Ter .

A major component of these timing models involves reaction time. DDMs provided reasonable approximations of response time. The duration categories with fewer responses had poorer fits, for instance the predicted RTs were excellent models for ‘long’ responses on ‘long’ anchor durations, but poorer models for ‘long’ responses on ‘short’ anchor durations, which is likely due to the lower number of incorrect responses at these durations. Our data showed increasing response times for short responses as a function of signal duration and decreasing response times for long responses. Therefore, response times were shorter for correct vs. incorrect responses. Response times generated by DDMs showed a more pronounced trend for supra- compared to sub-second duration ranges.

It is interesting that although we could model population data well with our models, there were some questionable fits for some of the individual animal data (i.e., non-positive definite Hessian matrices). One reason for this may be because there is limited data for a single subject (especially at the anchor durations) for the fit to be optimized. Other problems could potentially arise from using evidence-accumulation models based on reaction time, to model perceptual timing tasks that do not have an incentive for fast responses. The trials are not self-initiated and the inter-trial intervals are randomized to reduce anticipation. Therefore, a response on the current trial, from the point of view of the subject, really will only affect reward on this trial, as opposed to maximizing reward across trials. Keeping a fixed inter-trial interval or titrating the reward size based on response time may allow a more successful implementation of the DDM. Besides the lack of incentive, there are additional confounds affecting reaction time. One instance is the subject’s location relative to the lever. In studies with rats and mice, this may vary on a trial by trial basis increasing variability in reaction time.

It is also essential to consider how such processes may be represented in the brain. Evidence accumulation is a well-established concept in decision-making and generally suggests that there should be an underlying ramping signal that will predict when a decision is made (e.g.,  Narayanan, 2016; Shadlen & Kiani, 2013 ). Recently, however, it has been shown that neural activity may occur in discrete steps rather than through ramping ( Latimer, Yates, Meister, Huk, & Pillow, 2015 ). At the population level, however, these steps would be expected to occur at different times which would result in gradual ramping activity as opposed to a step function ( Jazayeri & Shadlen, 2015; MacDonald, Cheng, & Meck, 2012 ). Considerably more research is required in order to determine if there is a functional evidence accumulator in the brain ( Brody & Hanks, 2016 ;  Hanks et al., 2015 ). Interestingly step-like behavior can also be observed at the behavioral level. Single trial analysis of animals performing a peak procedure shows discrete low and high response rates ( Church, Meck, & Gibbon, 1994 ). These high states are centered around a mean duration in close approximation to the criterion duration, and there is no observed ramping of responses that is evident when peak trials are averaged together. Based on observed steps in neural activity and behavior the precise role of evidence accumulation needs to be further elucidated.

One way to do this may be exploration of other integration models such as the urgency gating model (UGM) ( Carland, Thura, & Cisek, 2015; Thura & Cisek, 2014 ). This model has many similarities to the DDM but integrates information using a low pass filter with a short time constant. The integrated information is then combined with a growing urgency signal. More in depth exploration ( Srivastava, Holmes, & Simen, 2016 ) and comparisons of DDM and UGM are required, but initial comparisons suggest that the UGM may outperform when using dynamic stimuli ( Hawkins, Wagenmakers, Ratcliff, & Brown, 2015; Thura & Cisek, 2016 ). Despite these difficulties DDMs are still capable of capturing choices and reaction time across animals. Moreover, these models provide some of the smallest S values of the choice data while also modeling reaction time. Further being able to calculate parameters for each trial type (e.g. signal duration) DDMs demonstrate great flexibility in their ability to account for varied data. For instance, they should not have any difficulty in accounting for the reversal phenomena ( Penney et al., 2008 ), or other potential anomalies that may arise. This being said, caution should be used to impose appropriate theoretical restrictions on the parameters, as in some cases they should not all be allowed to vary freely (e.g. decision boundary for this task). Overall these models are ideal for understanding underlying psychological processes and are therefore extremely useful for tasks where reaction time is a key component.

The current findings show that although subjects bisect close to the GM for both sub- and supra-second time scales, they exhibit a significant rightward shift in the PSE for sub-second durations relative to the GM. These results generally agree with those reported by  Cordes and Meck (2014) , with the supra-second PSE not being significantly different from the GM (L4P, SKE, PLM, or DDM) and the sub-second PSE lying between, yet significantly different from the GM and AM for the L4P, PLM, SKE. Despite general agreement across model types, there were two notable deviations. First, the sub-second PSE for the DDM was the only one found to not significantly differ from the AM and second, the SKE was unique in that Weber Fractions calculated from individual fits were not significantly different across sub- and supra-second conditions.

An important note is that the theoretic based timing models used here only address one possible application. Specifically, since its derivation ( Gibbon, 1981 ) the SKE model has been further developed to account for additional aspects of timing behavior such as bisection reversal around putative temporal boundaries ( Penney et al., 2008 ). As this paper only addresses the ability of these models to account for the differences exhibited between sub- and supra-second durations, expanding the models through their entire parameterization may provide further insights into their competence in describing the many nuances of temporal cognition. This is an ability lost on generic models such as the L4P, as the parameters are tied only to the shape of the functions and not components or stages of interval timing (see  Allman & Meck, 2012; Allman, Teki, Griffiths, & Meck, 2014 ).

Out of the three qualitative models that we evaluated (DDM, PLM, and the SKE model), the DDM was able to most accurately represent our sample data with S values

                       4.0% across both temporal duration ranges. The AIC analysis of timing models found strong support for the PLM, some support for the SKE in sub-second durations, and no support for the L4P. This finding is somewhat contrary to the S values we obtained, but as all models provided good fits, the PLM and SKE models showed much greater parsimony. These findings provide support for a reassessment of the overwhelming reliance on generalized logistic functions in the fitting of timing data. Along with the above mentioned concerns, the AIC indicates that the slight increases in fit obtained by having increased parameters does not proportionally increase the amount of information that the function is extracting. Nevertheless, L4P models remain an effective way to obtain PSE, DL, and WF measures in order to evaluate group differences in temporal bisection (cf.  Droit-Volet, Meck, & Penney, 2007; Pleil et al., 2011; Schirmer, Ng, Escoffier, & Penney, 2016 ). For the decision based DDMs, DDM1 was found to provide the greatest explanatory power for sub- and supra-second duration ranges. This reflects that v, z, and Ter are all essential components in modeling temporal decisions. While direct comparison between interval-timing and decision-making models would have been ideal, their use of distinct data sets made that impossible with the AIC.

The DDM and the PLM which explained the greatest amount of variance in the timing data possess similarities that help explain temporal processing. Both of these models provide insight into behavioral strategies and potential underlying neural mechanisms used in temporal bisection tasks. Unlike the SKE model, neither the DDM nor PLM requiresusing a generic response bias to account for variation between duration ranges. The DDM uses  v  which increases nearly linearly as a function of signal duration. The  v  parameter is directly related to the strength of the decision being made and may be represented through ramping activity of neural populations ( Hanks et al., 2015; Shadlen & Kiani, 2013 ). Similarly, the PLM shows a distinct contribution from sources of scalar variability and accounts for the rightward shift seen in the sub-second duration range. This shift may be caused by sources of constant variability related to signal detection and delays in attentional resource allocation ( Buhusi & Meck, 2009 ) and is evidenced in the significant lowering in AICc accompanying the inclusion of constant variability for sub-second durations. Specifically, the addition of constant variability provides no additional information for supra-second fits as would be expected for this type of temporal error ( Breukelaar & Dalrymple-Alford, 1999 ). Decision mechanisms fundamental to determining the PSE within the framework of the PLM are dependent on the anchor ratios, and sources of temporal variability, but not probe signal spacing, which is similar to the reliance of DDMs on decision boundaries established by anchor durations. In the PLM, this results in the unbiased PSE occurring near the point of intersection of memory distributions for ‘short’ and ‘long’ anchor durations (see  Allan, 2002; Allan & Gerhardt, 2001; Levy et al., 2015 ). While this seems like a logical point for a duration that would be equally likely to elicit either response, using the parameters obtained for our supra-second duration range, the chance that this value came from either distribution is less than 9% (i.e., 1.34% chance from the ‘short’ and 7.2% chance from the “long”). This makes it doubtful that subjects believe probe durations to be sampled from the anchor distributions as they are likely aware that probe durations around the PSE are distinct from either of the anchors. Instead, they pick the option they find most similar through a secondary calculation as they are likely aware both options are unlikely. This low probability of a middle duration being either anchor duration coincides with the DDM finding that the starting point (z) will be furthest from either decision boundary at the PSE with the similarity calculation is being reflected in the increased RTs around the middle probes.

Depending on the goals of a particular study, any of these fitting approaches may be reasonable and prudent, although preference should be given to theoretically based models when appropriate. For our purposes, all of the models were able to effectively capture differences across sub- and supra-second duration ranges. When we used the models with the lowest AICc values they all provided similar findings for the PSE and WF. However, when models were not fit for each duration range based upon AICc value (data not shown) found discrepant findings in PSE and WF across model classes. Due to the qualitative nature of the DDMs, PLM, and SKE model, further studies holding parameters constant across duration ranges could provide substantial insight into the degree in which sub- and supra-second timing engage interconnected timing processes (e.g.,  Allman et al., 2014 ;  Teki, Grube, Kumar, & Griffiths, 2011 ). It is important to note that these model parameters have different estimates and impacts in explaining timing behavior for sub- vs. supra-second timing. For instance, subjects are able to accumulate information more precisely for supra-second durations compared with sub-second durations, and constant variability has a larger impact on sub-second timing compared with supra-second timing. These differences imply that there may be different neural components that initiate sub- vs. supra-second timing, and further work is required to separate the different neural underpinnings of these interconnected yet dispersed timing systems ( Lusk, Petter, MacDonald, & Meck, 2016 ). While we did not systematically manipulate all temporal parameters in the current study (e.g., anchor duration ratios, inter trial intervals), we believe that the versatility displayed by the reviewed models across sub- and supra-second duration ranges demonstrates the validity of this approach in the study of temporal cognition.
