ARNOLD  Iowa State University, Ames, Iowa 50010  The classical urn scheme is further extended to permit application to simple learning situations with arbitrary response and  reinforcement spaces. The assumptions incorporated are those which, in the classical situation, are equivalent to (1)  permitting the total number of balls added per trial to depend on the preceding reinforcement and (2) allowing the probability  that a ball is drawn to depend on the time the ball has been in the urn. This results in a flexible family of models whose  behavior is analyzed in the case ononcontingent reinforcement.  1. INTRODUCTION  The model to be described is appropriate for simple learning situations of the following nature. On each of a sequence of  trials the subject makes a response which consists of selection of a member of a specified response set. Each response is  followed by a reinforcement which consists of selection of a member of a specified reinforcement set. In many examples the  response set and the reinforcement set coincide. The subject's responses are presumed to be governed by a probabilistic  mechanism which depends on the preceding sequence of reinforcements. The model to be discussed is a generalization of one of  the earliest stochastic models for learning, the urn scheme.  The urn scheme, for the case where the response and reinforcement sets coincide and consist of two alternatives, may be  described as follows. The subject's responses, which may be numbered 1 and 2, are governed by selection of a ball from an urn  of variable composition. At time zero the urn contains qI* red balls and q$* white balls. If response i is reinforced on a  trial, then qli red and qsi white balls are added to the urn (i = 1, 2). Drawings are assumed to be with replacement, and the  response of the subject is determined by the color of the ball drawn (response 1 if red is drawn, 2 if white is drawn).  Such models are described in, for example, Audley and Jonckheere (1956) and more recently by Wolter and Earl (1969). Arnold  (1967) generalized this model by making the probability of drawing a ball depend on the length of time that the ball has been  in the urn. SUMMARY  Much of the theory for urn schemes remains tractable when the total number of balls added on a trial is allowed to depend on  the preceding reinforcement, provided attention is restricted to simple noncontigent schedules. This permits certain  reinforcements to have greater salience in determining subsequent responses. We, thus, obtain a more flexible family of models  without introducing undue complication in the asymptotic response distributions. In addition, the theory carries over without  difficulty to general response and reinforcement spaces.  GENERALIZED URN SCHEME 239  REFERENCES  ARNOLD, B. C.
