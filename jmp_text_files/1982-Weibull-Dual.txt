WEIBULL  Royal Institute of Technology, Stockholm  The paper examines Ramsey's proposition that preferences among uncertain prospects may be represented in terms of subjectively  expected subjective values. While the von Neumann-Morgenstern approach presupposes probabilities and derives values, the  present approach does the reverse: it presupposes values and derives probabilities. Necessary and sufficient conditions are  presented for such representations for a fairly wide range of preference structures, including discrete as well as continuous  spaces of states-of-nature. An operational procedure is suggested for constructing the subjective values and probabilities.  1. INTRODUCTION  In most decisions, the decision maker has to choose among prospects which are to some degree uncertain. Such uncertain or  risky prospects are, in their simplest form, characterized by two components: the (objective or subjective) value which  results when a certain act is chosen and a state-of-nature occurs, and the (objective or subjective) probabilities of the  states-of-nature occurring. Models of decision behavior may be classified according to these four possible combinations of  objective or subjective values and probabilities respectively. In all of these models, the decision maker is assumed to behave  as if he ranked the prospects according to some appropriate notion of expected value. These four types of model are summarized  in Table 1 (cf. also Archibald (1959) and Menges (1979) for this classification).  The EV (expected value) model is the classical gambling model where, usually, the values are monetary and the probabilities  are objective a priori probabilities. In the EU model, the monetary values.are replaced by their subjective value or utility  for the individual. This model is the predominant decision model in the economics literature, especially since the celebrated  axiomatic formulation by von Neumann and Morgenstern (1947). However, a number of empirical studies indicate that the EU model  is inappropriate for description of actual choice behavior, even in very simple decision problems where objective a priori  probabilities are specified for the individual (cf. Edwards, 1961, 1962; Tversky, 1967; and Allais, 1979). These findings have  given rise to a long controversy in the literature (cf. Allais and Hagen, 1979 for a comprehensive discussion). SEV and SEU models recognize the fact that in most real-life situations no such probabilities are available. Thus, there  models instead hypothesize that the individual behaves as if he assigned subjective probabilities over the states-of-nature.  In the SEV (subjectively expected value) model the values are objective, while they are subjective in the SEU (subjectively  expected utility) model. The SEV model has received but little attention from theoretical researchers (cf. Ramsey, 1964;  deFinetti, 1964; Koopman, 1940; Marschak, 1955). Far more attention has been paid to the more general SEU model. This model  was given a famous axiomatic formulation in Savage (1954) and the model has later been elaborated by Fishburn and others (see,  e.g., Fishburn, 1969, 1970). In the previously mentioned empirical studies of actual choice behavior, the SEU model and even  the SEV model generally outperformed the EU model, even though objective a priori probabilities then were specified for the  individuals (cf. Edwards, 1961; Tversky, 1967).  To the author's knowledge, F. P. Ramsey was the first to suggest an axiomatic construction of the SEU model. In essence, he  proposed to first derive subjective values on the basis of an even-chance values (cf. Ramsey, 1964). von Neumann and  Morgenstern (1947) reversed Ramsey's procedure by first assuming (objective) probabilities and then deriving subjective  values. The more general approach adopted by Savage (1954) draws on these prior ideas of Ramsey and von Neumann and  Morgenstern as well as those of de Finetti (1964). In his axiomatization of the SEU model, Savage first derived probabilities  (obtaining a structure much like that of von Neumann and Morgenstern), and then derived subjective values based on these  probabilities. See Fishburn (1970, Chap. 14) for a precise and clear statement of Savage's theory. Thus, Savage too reversed  Ramsey's procedure.  The present paper proposes an analytical framework within which the procedure originally suggested by Ramsey is formalized and  examined. While Savage's approach gives only sufficient conditions for the existence of both subjective values and  probabilities, the present approach gives both necessary and sufficient conditions for the existence of subjective  probabilities for given subjective values.' In this sense,  ' In the most general axiomatic formulation of the SEU model, Fishburn (1969) establishes sufficient conditions involving  extraneous probabilities for SEU representations in terms of finitely additive probability measures.  the approach taken here may be viewed as dual to that of von Neumann and Morgenstern: while they presuppose probabilities and  deduce values, we here presuppose values and deduce probabilities. A rationale for this dual approach is the observation that  in many situations probabilities are not available while subjective values are relatively easy to establish (see Section 3).  The material to be presented is organized as follows: First, a fairly general representation theorem is given in Section 2 for  preference structures over a topological state space. Two corollaries for standard spaces are given and a third corollary, for  SEV representations, is given and related to a similar result in Suppes and Zanotti (1976). An operational procedure for  construction and testing of the model is discussed in Section 3.  2. This  approach will be adopted here.
