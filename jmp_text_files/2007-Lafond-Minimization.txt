Feldman (2003a) 's catalog of Boolean concepts provides a formalism to explain the observed difficulties of human participants in learning various basic categorization problems. Part of this catalog (41 concept types) was used in a concept learning experiment reported by  Feldman (2000) . For the purpose of the experiment, simple visual stimuli (fictitious amoebae) were created using different combinations of either three or four binary features. Different concepts were defined by assigning these stimuli to one of two exclusive categories: members and non-members of the concept. Feldman used the minimum length of a Boolean algebraic formula representing the classification task as a quantitative indicator of the complexity of the task. The number of literals (symbolic variables) in the minimized proposition was taken as a measure of its length. This procedure is analogous to finding the Kolmogorov complexity of Boolean concepts (see  Li & Vitányi, 1997 ). Various researchers have suggested that the process of complexity minimization mirrors a basic economic principle of the mind ( Chater, 2000 ;  Chater & Vitányi, 2003 ).

The present paper first reports a recalculation of the Boolean complexity of the six classic concept types (Types I–VI) introduced by  Shepard, Hovland, and Jenkins (1961)  and compares the results with those from  Feldman (2000) . We describe two solutions of lower complexity and discuss the implications of these results for Feldman's theory. The second part of this article presents an exhaustive revision of the minimal formulae listed in the catalog of Boolean concepts ( Feldman, 2003a ). To do this, we used a logic minimization program intended for digital circuit optimization. The third part reports the results of an experiment in which we tested whether participants categorized stimuli using rules of minimal complexity. It presents a method based on complexity minimization that identifies Boolean propositions describing the classification rules used by individual participants.

The following revision of the catalog of Boolean concepts was motivated by our observation ( Lafond & Lacouture, 2003 ) that two of the classic concept types studied by  Shepard et al. (1961)  have minimal forms shorter than those presented in  Feldman (2000, 2003a) . The six SHJ (the acronym for the names of the authors) concept types are defined by different assignments of the same eight stimuli into two categories of equal size. These stimuli take every possible value combination of three binary attributes.  Fig. 1 
                       illustrates the six problem types. Different classification problems are of the same type when they are logically isomorphic regardless of the specific assignment of the dimensions ( Shepard et al., 1961 ).

The SHJ types (Type I–VI) have been the subject of many empirical studies (e.g.,  Feldman, 2000 ;  Nosofsky, Gluck, Palmeri, McKinley, & Glauthier, 1994 ;  Shepard et al., 1961 ;  Smith, Minda, & Washburn, 2004 ). These concept types define various classification problems that vary in difficulty as a function of stimulus assignments to each category while using the same set of eight stimuli and the same category size. The task of the participants is to learn to discriminate Category A and Category B stimuli. Feedback on the correct category at the end of each trial allows learning to take place. In the experiments reported in these papers, the level of difficulty was measured by the number of trials it took a participant to reach a learning criterion or by the proportion of correct recall. The general results showed a difficulty ordering for Types I–VI, expressed by the ranking I<II<[III,IV,V]<VI. One particularity is that Types III, IV and V are considered approximately equal in difficulty. There is a distinct common element to these three concept types in that they can all be described by the same kind of logical rule: an  if–then  rule applied to a single feature of the stimuli complemented by two memorized exceptions. These three rules  and  their exceptions are formally expressed by Feldman's corresponding minimal formula for each type of concept:  a ′( bc )′ +ab ′ c  for Type III,  a ′( bc )′ +ab ′ c ′ for Type IV and  a ′( bc )′+ abc  for Type V. In the present notation,  ab  stands for “ a and b ”,  a+b  stands for “ a or b ” and  a ′ stands for “ not a ”. Let  a/a ′ stand for white/black,  b/b ′ stand for square/triangle and  c/c ′ stand for small/large. For instance, the Type III concept illustrated in  Fig. 1  can be logically defined by Feldman's minimal formula as “Category A if  black  and not ( square  and  small ) or if ( white  and  triangle  and  small )”. The fact that the minimized formulae for Type III, IV and V concepts are of the same length (six literals) successfully explained the approximately equal difficulty in learning the concepts. It was thus very surprising for us to find shorter minimal formulae for Type III and Type IV tasks. In fact, the minimal proposition of complexity 4 for SHJ Type III is also reported in  Aiken et al. (1951)  and  Mathy and Bradmetz (2004) . Both of these revisions have also been found and discussed by  Vigo (2006) .

The next logical step was to verify whether these new minimal formulae better corresponded to the learning difficulty. With these new minimal propositions, Type III and IV concepts have complexity levels of 4 and 5, respectively, not 6 as reported in the catalog of Boolean concepts. The rank ordering by difficulty of the six types, I<II<[III,IV,V]<VI, was originally explained by Boolean complexities of 1, 4, 6, 6, 6 and 10, respectively. The revised Boolean complexity of Types I–VI is 1, 4, 4, 5, 6 and 10, respectively. The difficulty ordering suggested by the revised complexity is I<[II,III]<IV<V<VI. Here, Type II and III concepts should have the same level of difficulty, whereas Type IV should be more difficult to learn than Types II and III, and Type V should be more difficult to learn than Type IV.

Empirical results consistently show that  Shepard et al. (1961) 's Type III, IV and V concepts are approximately equally difficult to learn ( Nosofsky, Gluck et al., 1994 ;  Shepard et al., 1961 ;  Smith et al., 2004 ). Thus, Feldman's formulae for Type III and IV problems, although not minimal, better correspond to human performance than the minimal formulae described here. This result suggests that Feldman's complexity minimization principle has to be reformulated to explain why, in some cases, humans find a concept more difficult to learn than that indicated by the complexity level of the minimal formula.

To account for the empirical results described above, we first assumed that the  definition  of the learned concept in the participant's mind corresponded to a Boolean representation. We propose that this representation can be verbally reported by the participant, allowing the categorization rules to be identified and expressed using Boolean concepts. For instance,  Shepard et al. (1961)  classified verbal reports of individual participants’ categorization rules into six rule types: single factor, two factor, single factor with exception(s), miscellaneous, enumeration and error. They reported the percentage of participants that used each type of rule (see  Shepard et al., 1961 ;  Table 3 ). The verbal descriptions of the rules typically reported by the participants to describe how they classify the stimuli for the six SHJ tasks generally correspond to Feldman's minimized formulae. For example, the minimized Boolean representation for  Shepard et al. (1961) 's Type V tasks is  a ′( bc )′ +abc.  This Boolean function can be expressed verbally as a  single factor rule with two exceptions:  “If the stimulus has feature  a ′ ( not a ) and does  not  have features  bc  ( b  and  c ), or if it has features  abc  ( a  and  b  and  c ), then it is a member of category A, otherwise it belongs to category B (which is implicitly stated).” Removing the  redundancies  in a DNF thus reveals the logical relationship that unites members of a category and distinguishes them from non-members.

The conclusion by  Shepard et al. (1961)  that Type III, IV and V problems are about equally difficult to learn is not the end of the story. For each concept type used in their Experiment 1,  Shepard et al. (1961)  reported examples of the simplest rules that were verbalized and reported by the participants. For Type III problems, a single-dimension rule with  partially  memorized exceptions (of complexity 5, not 6) was reported as solution IIIa, whereas solution IIIb was described as a  two-factor  rule. Solution IIIb actually corresponds to the revised minimal formula for Type III problems (of complexity 4, not 6).  Table 3  in  Shepard et al. (1961)  shows that for Type III problems  most  participants reported having used a single-factor with exception rule but that a smaller proportion of participants did report using the minimal two-factor rule. Apparently, some participants may find and use the revised minimal rule, even though it does not fit the main pattern of responses.  Shepard et al. (1961) 's Type IVa solution is a single dimension rule with partially memorized exceptions (of complexity 5, not 6) that actually corresponds to our revised formula for this type of concept. In comparison, for Type V problems, a single dimension rule with memorized exceptions corresponding to Feldman's unrevised formula (of complexity 6) is reported in  Shepard et al. (1961) 's  Table 3  as the most common verbal rule used by the participants. Another type of strategy, involving counting, was also described for Type IV and VI problems (solutions IVb, VIa and VIb).

The apparent equality in the  average  learning difficulty for Types III, IV and V problems may be much less informative about psychological processes than the different strategies or rules used by different participants in learning and performing the task. Clearly, these latter strategies are best described and studied at the individual level.  Nosofsky, Palmeri, and McKinley (1994)  did just that, presenting a rule-plus-exception (RULEX) model of category learning based on the idea that averaged classification data consist of the aggregation of idiosyncratic rules. The learning principle behind RULEX can, in fact, explain  both  the tendency to use minimal rules in general  and  the precedence of non-minimal rules in cases like Type III and IV concepts.

An important aspect of RULEX is its postulate that, when learning a concept, humans first use simple rules that are applied to distinctive features. If a simple rule based on one feature is insufficient to categorize the stimuli, RULEX tries to perform the task using a single-dimension rule and memorizing some or all the features of the exceptions. This second processing level works, providing the number of exceptions is small. The exceptions can either be viewed as instances that are memorized using a complementary categorization process (e.g.,  Erickson & Kruschke, 1998 ;  Palmeri & Nosofsky, 1995 ) or simply as subsidiary rules. In this case, the distinction between rules and stored exceptions breaks down ( Feldman, 2003b ). An exception is part of the Boolean rule, and each exception increases the complexity level of the definition of the concept used by a participant. If the second processing stage (based on a single-dimension rule plus memorized exceptions) fails, RULEX tries to perform the task using conjunctive rules based on multiple features, followed by a search for an exception if no exact rule works. With RULEX, there is also some probability (usually low) that the search for a conjunctive rule begins right after the search for a distinctive feature. This possible sequence of search processes does not account for the non-minimal results obtained for Type III concepts.

Nosofsky, Palmeri et al. (1994)  showed that such a multi-stage process provides a very good fit to behavioral data. For  Shepard et al. (1961) 's Type III problems, the most economical strategy (in terms of Boolean complexity) requires a two-factor rule that involves the disjunction of two conjunctions (our revised formula of complexity 4). Using the RULEX algorithm, the first processing level (a search for an exact single-dimension rule) would fail. The second step would be to try an imperfect single-dimension rule and attempt to store exceptions. If RULEX (or the participant) succeeds in categorizing the stimuli at this stage, then the most economical (two-factor) solution, which may have been found in later processing steps (conjunctive search, followed by an exception search), may never be considered.

The Type IV non-minimal rule that better fits the observed data is a single-dimension rule with two memorized exceptions (of complexity 6). The revised minimal rule (of complexity 5) is also a single-dimension rule with two exceptions. However, one of the exceptions is only partially memorized (i.e., one of its features is not listed). The exception search described in RULEX may explain why participants generally used the non-minimal rule. Basically, RULEX samples the features of each exception (with a given probability) and attempts to memorize them. This process takes into account that working memory has a limited capacity, that is, successfully storing exceptions becomes increasingly difficult as the number of previously memorized exceptions and the number of memorized features increases. For Type IV problems, the number of exceptions  and  the number of features of the stimuli are small. Memory capacity is not overloaded and participants can memorize the exceptions with relative ease.

While the discussion on the hypothesis-testing sequence and its constraints helps provide a qualitative account of the use of non-minimal rules, it is important as well to conduct rigorous simulations at some point. One inherent difficulty in using the RULEX model for fitting the observed data is that it requires extensive computations due to the stochastic nature of the search process. Note that  Navarro (2005)  successfully formalized many aspects of the RULEX model using a more tractable algorithmic method.

Having found two Boolean formulae of lower complexity than reported in  Feldman (2003a) , we attempted an exhaustive revision of the catalog of Boolean concepts. Feldman noted that minimal formulae are approximations due to the fact that computing a globally minimal formula is intractable. Minimized algebraic expressions (MAE) are derived using heuristic techniques. The term “minimization” used throughout this paper thus refers to procedures that do not necessarily lead to globally minimal formulae.

Aiken and the staff of the Harvard University Computation Laboratory (1951) developed a technique for minimizing Boolean functions. Like  Feldman (2003a) , they catalogued the solutions they found (see  Aiken et al., 1951 , Tables 1.3 and 3.3) for all the  switching  functions of two, three and four variables. While  Aiken et al. (1951)  were interested in optimizing switching circuits, their objective was essentially to find minimal Boolean formulae. The switching functions in  Aiken et al. (1951)  correspond to the classification problems listed in the catalog of Boolean concepts ( Feldman, 2003a ), except that they also include all complementary concepts. Complementary concepts are defined by a DNF that lists the negative examples of the category.

We compared the Boolean complexity of the minimal formulae presented by  Feldman (2003a)  and  Aiken et al. (1951)  and found that Feldman's minimization algorithm did not always provide the simplest solution. Since the Harvard minimization method is executed manually, it is considerably less practical than a computational algorithm. These findings motivated our current search for a minimization algorithm that could match or improve on the simplest formulae from either  Feldman (2003a)  or  Aiken et al. (1951) .

SIS 1.3 is a multilevel logic minimization program developed at UC Berkeley ( Sentovich et al., 1992 ). The purpose of the software is to lower the implementation cost of combinatorial and sequential digital circuits. Combinatorial circuits implement Boolean functions in either two-level or multilevel logic. While two-level logic functions can be implemented in a two-layer circuit, multilevel logic functions generally require fewer literals but do require multiple circuit layers. In this technology-independent stage of multilevel logic synthesis, cost is usually measured as the number of literals in the minimized Boolean function. The minimized Boolean propositions in the catalog of Boolean concepts use a type of representation called  factored forms  in the logic synthesis literature: 
                            A factored form is a representation of a logic function with some attractive properties. Like Bryant's differential form, the factored form represents both a function and, by duality, its complement. For multilevel logic synthesis, it often estimates how the logic function may be implemented better than other representations. […] The factored form represents better the complexity of this function ( Brayton, 1987, p. 187 ).

Clearly, switching circuit theory has much in common with the basic categorization problems used in psychological research. Using SIS to search for new minimal propositions thus seemed appropriate.

We compared different scripts and primitive SIS operations for their ability to minimize Boolean concepts, searching for a unique procedure that would give best results for all concepts studied. Finding no such procedure, we developed our own script by trial and error and succeeded in finding one that consistently gave the best results. Our script, called script.elements, is presented in  Appendix A .

Revising Feldman's catalog with SIS (using script.elements), we found 148 minimized formulae (of a total of 253 concept types) that are shorter than reported. Using SIS, we succeeded in finding minimized formulae of equal or lower complexity than the best corresponding minimized proposition presented in either  Feldman (2003a)  or  Aiken et al. (1951)  for all two-dimension, three-dimension and four-dimension Boolean concept types.

A concept family D[P] is defined by the number of Boolean features D describing the stimuli coupled with the number of positive examples of the concept P placed within square brackets (e.g., the SHJ concept family is defined as the 3[4] family, which stands for all concepts having three features and four positive examples). All concepts belonging to a family are divided among a number of concept types. Concepts of the same type (within a given family) are said to be  congruent , that is, isomorphic in their logical structure ( Feldman, 2003a ). Each concept also has a complementary version with essentially the same logical structure, but with inverted category labels.

The  case  indicates the specific concept type (in bold) within a concept family (in subscript). For example, SHJ types are denoted as case 1 3[4]  to 6 3[4] . The catalog of Boolean concepts identifies 253 cases, each representing a unique logical structure corresponding to two exclusive groups (of varying size) having two, three or four Boolean attributes.

The DNF and the revised minimal formula are presented for each case. The DNF is the uncompressed algebraic formula that lists the exemplars of a given concept. It describes the features of each positive instance (the concept  members ) in the form of a conjunction of feature values while the instances are connected by disjunctions. The last column in  Table 1  indicates the complexity of the revised minimal proposition.

To summarize, the concepts listed in Feldman's catalog had an average complexity of 10.2 before the revisions. Using the best formulae reported in  Aiken et al. (1951)  and  Feldman (2003a) , the average complexity was 9.2. Using SIS, we produced a revised catalog with an average complexity of 8.9. The Boolean complexity of each revised formula was on average decreased by 2.2. There were no cases in which SIS did not find the simplest known algebraic expression.

Vigo (2006)  performed a revision of the minimal formulae of the 41 concept types listed in  Feldman (2000) , which constitute a subset of the 253 concept types in the catalog of Boolean concepts.  Vigo (2006)  developed a minimization procedure called the QMV method, which is based on the Quine–McCluskey method ( McCluskey, 1956 ;  Quine, 1955 ), a very popular two-level logic minimization technique in digital circuit design. Vigo observed that the minimized formulae in the so-called “sum-of-products” form (i.e., two-level logic) could be further reduced by using factorization (i.e., producing factored forms in multilevel logic). The QMV method thus incorporates a factorization step after applying the QM method. Furthermore, the QMV method involves two “complementary” minimization procedures, one that simplifies the “positive DNF” (the one listing the positive category members) and one that simplifies the negative DNF (and then negates the entire minimized expression to obtain the Category A definition). This two-fold minimization approach involves selecting the best of the two minimized propositions and is identical to the strategy we used in script.elements with the “invert” command (see  Appendix A ).

Both SIS and QMV thus attempt to find the simplest factored form, and the two procedures take advantage of the opportunity to minimize both the positive concept and the negative concept in order to select the simplest Boolean formula. To our knowledge, this extremely useful strategy (minimizing a Boolean function and its dual) has been completely ignored in the logic synthesis literature, with the exception of  Rau, Chen, and Chang (2000)  and  Mintz and Golumbic (2005) .

The results obtained by the QMV algorithm clearly converge with those found with SIS. For the 41 cases listed in  Feldman (2000) , both procedures found revisions to the same nine concept types. The nine revised formulae found by QMV and SIS also have the same complexity level, even though the minimized propositions are not necessarily identical. Both procedures agree that the revised Boolean complexity of SHJ Types I–VI is 1, 4, 4, 5, 6 and 10, respectively, which predicts a difficulty ordering of I<[II,III]<IV<V<VI.

Feldman (2000)  reported an experiment that studied the level of difficulty of categorization tasks corresponding to the first 41 cases in the catalog of Boolean concepts. The results were explained by two orthogonal factors: Boolean complexity and parity. The parity effect relates to the increased difficulty in learning a concept when the group of positive examples is larger than the group of negative examples. Any concept has, by definition, a complementary concept with inverted labels. These two concepts are called the  up  and  down  versions of a concept-learning task. When the number of members is not equal to the number of non-members, concepts tend to be easier to learn with the  up  version of a task, this is when the smaller group is chosen as the set of positive examples ( Feldman, 2000 ).

                         2 =.2062) and 29% ( R 
                         2 =.2881) of the variance, respectively ( Feldman, 2003b ).

Of the 41 concept types studied in  Feldman (2000) , there are nine cases in which a formula of lower complexity was found. Using these new complexity values, we can recalculate the proportion of variance accounted for by Boolean complexity when predicting proportion correct at recall. Much of the variance unaccounted for by Feldman's combined factors comes from the  down  versions of the task in the higher dimension concepts (those varying along four attributes, not three). We believe that part of the variance that is unaccounted for may in fact be due to the experimental procedure. Despite the inverted labels and positions on the screen (examples and counter-examples are presented simultaneously), some participants may have discovered that it is easier to focus on the counter-examples and learn the complementary concept, which is the same as learning the  up  version of the problem. This could explain the greater variability observed in the  down  versions. For this reason, we will focus on the complexity effect in the  up  versions only.

There are three concept families used in  Feldman (2000) 's experiment for which new minimal formulae have been found. SIS and QMV found two revisions for the 3[4] family, one revision for the 4[3] family and six revisions for the 4[4] family. Considering solely the  up  versions, the proportion of variance ( R 
                         2 ) explained by the original complexity values is .88, .64 and .54, respectively. The recalculated  R

The revised complexity values corresponding to the new minimal formulae thus provide an overall worse fit to the data. This is in line with our earlier conclusion that non-minimal algebraic descriptions might, at least in some cases, be better indicators of the complexity of Boolean concepts.

A surprising twist occurred at this point. A closer inspection of  Feldman (2000) 's experimental results for the 3[4] concept family revealed that the difficulty ordering of the SHJ concept types actually differed from the classical results. The average number of correct responses (of a total of eight stimuli) was 7.6, 6.7, 6.7, 6.2, 6.5 and 5.8, respectively, for Type I–VI concepts. In particular, Type II and III seemed to be equally difficult, rather than Type III–IV–V. This unexpected result is intriguing in light of the complexity 4 minimal formula found for Type III concepts, which may explain why Type II and III were about as difficult.

The complexity values listed in the catalog of Boolean concepts for each of the six SHJ types (1, 4, 6, 6, 6, 10) predicted a difficulty ordering that matched the classical observed ordering: I<II<III–IV–V<VI. The revised minimal algebraic expressions listed here (1, 4, 4, 5, 6, 10) predict a different difficulty ordering: I<II–III<IV<V<VI. Although, this revised MAE ordering seems to match the  Feldman (2000)  data on the approximately equal difficulty of Type II and III concepts, it does not match the observed ordering of Type IV and V concepts (it predicts the opposite order of difficulty).

Feldman (2000)  provided no evidence that the difficulty ordering he observed is actually reliable, given that he did not report paired-comparisons of proportion correct for the SHJ concept types. We thus performed a graphical analysis of within-participant confidence intervals (see  Masson & Loftus, 2003 ) based on additional data provided by Feldman.  Fig. 2 
                          presents the proportion of correct responses and 95% confidence intervals for each SHJ concept type. A visual assessment of the confidence intervals suggests that Type I concepts were easier (i.e., had a higher proportion correct) than the other five concept types, and that Type II, III and V concepts where easier than Type VI concepts. There was no reliable difference between the means of Type II, III, IV and V concepts. Nonetheless, the approximately equal difficulty of Type II and III concepts shows that the observed difficulty ordering in the data from  Feldman (2000)  is definitely different from the classical ordering.

The experimental procedure used in  Feldman (2000)  may have produced results that are unlike those from previous studies because it is based on a different measure of difficulty (proportion correct at recall) and a different training procedure (simultaneous time-limited presentation of all examples and counter-examples). Perhaps the hypothesis-testing process is different when learning from sequentially presented versus concurrently displayed exemplars. Specifically, a visual scanning process may be involved when the stimulus set is presented all at once.

The following experiment was designed with three closely related objectives in mind. The first objective was to show that logic synthesis provides a simple method for identifying classification rules that are consistent with experimental results at the individual level. This method involves simplifying a DNF that matches a participant's empirical classification response pattern. The second objective was to vary the number of correctly classified stimuli and examine the impact of this factor on the classification of new stimuli (inductive generalization). The complexity of the rules derived from each classification pattern should vary as a function of proportion correct. The third objective was to test whether participants would learn minimal classification rules or unnecessarily complex ones. We examined a specific contrast between a minimal two factor rule (or “double conjunction”) vs. a non-minimal “single-dimension rule with exceptions” to see whether the results would converge with those predicted by the standard hypothesis-testing sequence described in RULEX.

The category structure designed for this experiment is presented in  Table 2 
                         . Sixteen different stimuli (schematic faces) vary along four binary attributes (mouth height, nose shape, eye separation and ear size). This “ill-defined” category structure has two particularities: (1) any single-dimension rule (based on any of the four attributes) allows eight of ten training stimuli to be classified correctly. There are always two exceptions, one in Category A and one in Category B; and (2) a disjunction of two conjunctions (similar to the SHJ Type III two-factor rule) of lower Boolean complexity than a single-dimension rule plus exceptions can be used to classify all ten training stimuli correctly. In sum, either a single-dimension rule with two “memorized” exceptions (e.g., Category  A=a ′( bcd )′ +ab ′ c ′ d ′), or a simpler “double conjunction” (e.g., Category  A=a ′ b ′ + c ′ d ′), may be used to classify all training exemplars correctly. This contrast is similar to the two rules described earlier for SHJ Type III but with stimuli varying along four rather than three binary features.

The general purpose of this experiment was to analyze the empirical classification pattern of participants who learn to correctly classify eight, nine or all ten training stimuli, using logic synthesis to identify categorization rules that “make sense” out of the various response patterns. It is difficult, however, to devise an experimental condition that guarantees that a participant has learned to correctly classify  exactly  eight, nine or all ten training stimuli. We anticipated that there would be a chance that the level of proportion correct could jump (e.g., due to a new hypothesis) in a way that the learning criterion was not only attained during a learning block, but surpassed. We decided to set a  minimum  criterion of either 80% or 90% proportion correct, expecting that a participant who learns to use a single-dimension rule may sometimes learn how to classify correctly one or both of the exceptions within the same learning block, thus exceeding the targeted minimum. Three groups, labeled 8/10, 9/10 and 10/10, were thus obtained using a quasi-experimental paradigm. We manipulated the learning criterion (a minimum of 80% or 90% correct) by randomly assigning half the participants to each one. Only after data collection did we divide the participants into three groups based on the proportion of correct responses they actually achieved (80%, 90% or 100%).

Based on the hypothesis-testing sequence of the RULEX model, we expected participants to use a single-dimension rule (Group 8/10), a single-dimension rule with one learned exception (Group 9/10) or a single-dimension rule with two learned exceptions (Group 10/10). We wondered whether any of the participants would discover the minimal two-factor rule.

Sixteen students from  Université Laval  participated in a single experimental session. The task of the participants was to learn to correctly assign the stimuli to either Category A or B. The instructions clearly identified the possible feature values and did not suggest any particular classification strategy. Participants were informed that they would have to start by guessing the correct category and that feedback would help them succeed in the task. They were instructed to prioritize accuracy and that response times would not be measured. Each participant received a compensation of $5. Each was randomly assigned to one of two conditions: a minimum learning criterion of either 80% or 90% correct classifications during three successive blocks of trials.

The stimuli were 16 schematic faces composed of four binary features. The four binary features were mouth height (low/high), nose shape (diamond/square), eye separation (wide/narrow) and ear size (large/small). For coding purposes, we assigned the value 0 or 1 to each of these feature properties. Stimulus feature assignment was counter-balanced across participants. The schematic faces had a diameter of about 7 cm and were presented at a distance of approximately 60 cm in the upper-half of the screen. Sample stimuli are shown in  Fig. 3 
                            .

Ten training stimuli were assigned to either Category A or Category B (five stimuli in each category) to obtain the abstract category structure shown in  Table 2 . The six test stimuli had no predetermined category assignment. The task of the participants was to answer either Category A or B in response to each schematic face. Feedback allowed the participants to learn the correct classification of the exemplars and reach the level of proportion correct specified by the learning criterion.

There were three phases in this experiment. In addition to the usual training and transfer phases, there was an intermediate  consolidation  phase. The training trials presented Stimuli 1–10 sequentially in random order. A dialog box below the stimulus asked “Is this stimulus a member of Category A?” Participants entered their response by clicking a yes/no box displayed below the stimulus. After each trial, the stimulus remained on display and feedback on the correct category was presented. The training phase ended when the learning criterion (80% or 90% correct in a same block) was achieved. Successfully reaching the criterion in one given block was not sufficient to begin the transfer phase. We required participants to maintain a criterion level of proportion correct for two additional blocks. Our rationale was that this might allow a rule to be consolidated in memory. To prevent further learning in these two blocks, it was essential that no feedback about the correct category be provided. If the criterion was not met during the consolidation phase, the training phase (with feedback) was resumed. In the transfer phase, all 16 faces (i.e., the ten training stimuli and the six test stimuli) were randomly presented three times. No feedback was provided at the end of each trial in the transfer phase.

A classification pattern is defined by the category response given most often (the mode) for each stimulus. We assume that individuals who use rules apply them in a deterministic fashion [choice probabilities for each stimulus must be close to 1 (Category A) or 0 (Category B)].  Lamberts (2000)  and  Cohen and Nosofsky (2003)  reported individual choice probabilities that are polarized in this way. For example, if a stimulus has a choice probability of .03, it is assigned to Category B (for defining the classification pattern) and the 3% Category A responses observed are taken as errors relative to the use of a specific rule that matches the classification pattern. This proportion of errors can be seen as noise in the decision process and can be attributed to occasional lapses of attention, inadvertent button presses and mistakes in applying the rules and exceptions ( Nosofsky, Palmeri et al., 1994 ).

We attempted to find a logic minimization procedure for analyzing empirical response patterns that would not assume that all training stimuli are correctly classified and that would not assume that participants always learn minimal concept definitions based on the training stimulus set. We opted for a descriptive approach based on the observed classification pattern for the complete stimulus set (i.e., using both training and test stimuli). The DNF corresponding to a specific classification pattern is defined by the set of stimuli assigned to Category A. The minimal equivalent formula can be obtained using SIS (with script.elements) or by manual minimization using the axioms and identities of Boolean algebra. One limit of this approach is that, in some cases, there may be multiple minimal formulae that accurately describe a classification pattern.

The logical analysis of classification response patterns revealed how Group 8/10, Group 9/10 and Group 10/10 differed essentially in the number of subsidiary rules that were used to correctly classify the exceptions. Learning to correctly classify one or two exceptions also affected the generalization pattern. This analysis showed that exceptions were not entirely memorized and that some test stimuli were actually consistently classified according to subsidiary or exception-rules.

In general, the exception-rules for Group 9/10 and Group 10/10 (see  Table 3 ) included only the minimal set of features necessary to correctly classify the exceptions. This contrasts with the SHJ Type III and IV non-minimal rules discussed earlier, whose exceptions were apparently memorized by most participants, even if it was possible for some participants to partially memorize the exceptions (solutions IIIa and IVa in  Shepard et al., 1961 ). The hypothesis that people tend to memorize exceptions as long as working memory capacity is not overloaded may explain the partially memorized exceptions identified for Group 10/10 (memorizing would bring Boolean complexity to 8). However, Group 9/10 participants did not memorize all the features of the learned exception either, but the complexity of the rules they used was much lower (since only one exception was learned). The experimental procedure, which stopped providing feedback after the criterion was met, possibly prevented participants from sampling additional features of the exceptions.

A key objective of this experiment was to test whether Group 10/10 participants would fail to use a minimal two-factor rule and would rather use a more complex single-dimension rule with memorized exceptions, as seen for SHJ Type III. The task could have been accomplished by using one of three minimal rules:  Category A=a ′ d ′ +b ′ c ′ , Category A=a ′ c ′ +b ′ d ′ or  Category A=a ′ b ′ + c ′ d ′. None of these rules matched the classification pattern observed for any of the participants in this experiment. The complexity of the minimized formulae for Group 10/10 participants was either 5 or 6 compared to the minimal two-factor rule of complexity 4.

Like SHJ Type III, the use of a single-dimension rule with (partially) memorized exceptions superseded the use of a minimal two-factor rule. Again, this result can be explained by the hypothesis-testing sequence in RULEX ( Nosofsky, Palmeri et al., 1994 ).

Two earlier studies ( Medin, Wattenmaker, & Michalski, 1987 ;  Nosofsky, Clark, & Shin, 1989 ) have shown that participants may tend to learn non-minimal rules. The inductive generalizations observed in these studies matched rules that generally included more than the minimum number of attributes needed to discriminate between categories. These rules usually had a greater specificity than the most simple and general discriminating rule, suggesting that rule induction is guided by more than simplicity or parsimony.  Medin et al. (1987)  suggested that this bias towards specificity protects rule induction systems from developing over-generalizations (i.e., making general rules out of exceptions). Rules that are too complex and specific risk over-fitting the data, while rules that are too simple and general risk over-generalizing. Learning simple but non-minimal rules seems to balance these two risks.

Nosofsky et al. (1989)  studied rule induction using stimuli varying along two continuous dimensions. They performed a series of experiments designed to contrast the predictions of the generalized context model (a very successful exemplar model) and a rule-based model that relied on the simplest rules that could correctly categorize all stimuli. When instructions required the participants to use a specific minimal rule, the rule-based model provided a better quantitative account of results. In free-strategy conditions, however, the generalized context model provided a superior fit to the data. It is nevertheless possible to interpret these results as corresponding to various non-minimal rules, some of which were identified by the authors.

Our paper presents revisions to the catalog of Boolean concepts, to provide the simplest known formulae that describe Feldman's concepts types, along with new complexity indices. The 148 revised formulae found by SIS are approximations based on a heuristic procedure, not the result of an exhaustive search for globally minimal formulae. Simpler formulae may in principle be identified using another technique. This procedure can nonetheless be considered very good since it always found algebraic expressions of equal or lower complexity than those in both  Feldman (2003a)  and  Aiken et al. (1951) . Furthermore, SIS found revisions with the same complexity values as the QMV minimization method for the subset of 41 concept types studied in  Vigo (2006) .

Although Boolean complexity minimization is undeniably a very useful theoretical construct, our analysis did not lend support to the idea that participants necessarily learn and use minimal rules when performing a categorization task. First, we showed that for Type III and IV problems, a non-minimal Boolean proposition better describes the difficulty ordering of Types I–VI and matches the verbal rules  mostly  reported by participants in  Shepard et al. (1961) . Nonetheless, a smaller proportion of participants did report using the minimal rules. Second, we showed that revising nine minimal formulae for the 3[4], 4[3] and 4[4] concept families did not improve the proportion of variance accounted for by Boolean complexity (for proportion correct) in the experiment reported by  Feldman (2000) , but actually worsened it. Third, we obtained experimental results in which the pattern of individual responses suggested that participants who correctly classified all training stimuli in the task used non-minimal categorization rules. Fourth, we identified a minimal “two factor” rule (of complexity 4) for the classic 5–4 category structure ( Medin & Schaffer, 1978 ) and showed that, for two studies having used this category structure ( Cohen & Nosofsky, 2003 ;  Lamberts, 2000 ), none of the individual classification patterns identified matched the use of the minimal rule. Rather, the complexity of the various rules identified for these participants ranged from 5 to 9 (except for two participants who learned incomplete rules and made errors on two of the training stimuli). These non-minimal rules could, for the most part, be interpreted as single-dimension rules with two (partially) memorized exceptions, a result that clearly converges with those from the experiment reported in this paper.

Our analysis of the data from the present experiment and from these two additional studies demonstrated how the logic underlying each individual classification pattern was identified using Boolean minimization, without assuming that participants used the simplest possible rule. Logic synthesis applied to each individual classification pattern made sense of the different transfer patterns, not in terms of varying attentional weights (as in exemplar and prototype models) but in terms of varying logical rules. We suggest that a Boolean function can be used to describe the concept definition abstracted by a participant doing a supervised classification task with binary-featured stimuli. This representation is obtained by minimizing the complexity of a DNF that corresponds to the actual pattern of response for each participant. Using this method, one does not get a generic rule or a universal complexity level, but a much more tailored description of the rules that may have been used by each participant. This ad hoc descriptive analysis will be used in our future work in conjunction with decision trees as the basis for modeling individual categorization strategies. This will hopefully be a useful approach for modeling different variables such as response times, eye fixations (see  Rehder & Hoffman, 2005 ), classification of partial stimuli and crossover effects under time pressure (see  Lamberts & Freeman, 1999a, 1999b ).

Using a minimized Boolean function to define a categorization rule bears much similarity to the notion of feature lists described in the  classical  view of categorization ( Bruner, Goodnow, & Austin, 1956 ; see also  Smith & Medin, 1981 ). The “classical theory” assumes that categories are represented by a set of necessary and sufficient features. To categorize a stimulus, a participant must retrieve the feature list of previously learned categories (i.e., Category A and Category B) and test whether the stimulus features match the criteria in one list.  Smith and Medin (1981)  explained how these feature lists serve as concept definitions that strictly include  individually  necessary and  jointly  sufficient features. This theory does not, however, put much value on the use of disjunctive concepts (e.g., Category A if small  or  large-and-round).  Bruner et al. (1956, p. 181)  relied on a primitive notion that “[…] common effects have common causes. The disjunctive category is a violation of this classical conception.” Based on their empirical findings, they qualified human strategies for learning disjunctive concepts as clumsy and inelegant.

The classical approach was dismissed long ago essentially because it was  incomplete : it could not represent a class of Boolean concepts referred to as “ill-defined” category structures ( Ashby & Maddox, 2005 ;  Smith & Medin, 1981 ). Many concepts cannot be expressed using a list of necessary and sufficient features, while people can indeed learn these “ill-defined” or disjunctive concepts (e.g.,  Martin & Caramazza, 1980 ;  Salatas & Bourne, 1974 ,  Ward & Scott, 1987 ). However, Boolean expressions are a type of feature list or concept definition that does not suffer from this limitation. The approach is  complete  in the sense that it can express any Boolean concept. In our opinion, Feldman's catalog of Boolean concepts stands as an expanded version of the classical approach.

The formalism used in  Feldman (2003a)  has definite merits without necessarily assuming that humans always learn minimal classification rules. Various minimization methods can be used for different purposes, like describing the simplest known MAE that can be learned, finding non-minimal expressions that better fit human data or providing a synthetic description of an observed classification pattern. These types of feature lists are a useful way of representing both categorization rules and exceptions, minimal or not, and may serve purposes that go beyond calculating Boolean complexity.

Based on the evidence for non-minimal rules reported in this paper, the minimal algebraic descriptions listed in the (revised) catalog of Boolean concepts seem to be, in some cases (but not all), too “compressed” to correspond to human learning. This means, paradoxically, that many of these formulae may not be psychologically relevant descriptions. Nevertheless, cataloging these minimal formulae is essential to identify which observed rules are in fact non-minimal. Moreover, even in cases for which a non-minimal rule seems to be used by most participants, minimal rules are psychologically relevant to the extent that a small subset of participants may be able to learn them (e.g., Type IIIb and IVa solutions in  Shepard et al., 1961 ). We should add that a large number of cases listed in the catalog of Boolean concepts are probably too complex to be learned using a verbal rule. Identifying these cognitive limitations will require further investigation.

A more substantive issue about the role of non-minimal rules in human learning is that, since Boolean minimization is intractable, it would be truly strange if human learning did correspond to globally minimal forms. This would imply that humans have access to a polynomial algorithm for an NP-complete problem, which it not considered possible. It should be no great surprise then that human learning corresponds to some type of approximation that often leads to non-minimal forms. Hence, there is a clear theoretical justification for exploring the constraints on rule learning, namely the computational necessity of using approximations when attempting to learn Boolean concept definitions.

Feldman (2006)  developed a concept algebra that can be applied to categories whose exemplars vary along Boolean or discrete features. First, he defines a new measure of subjective difficulty called  algebraic complexity . Second, he proposes a definition of the  algebraic typicality  of exemplars of a concept.  Feldman (2006)  provides empirical support for these two new indices and shows that the proportion of variance accounted for by these two measures compared favorably with that of ALCOVE, a well-known exemplar model ( Kruschke, 1992 ). Note that a virtually identical proportion of variance was accounted for by algebraic complexity ( R 
                         2 =.5037) and Boolean complexity ( R 
                         2 =.5017) in the  Feldman (2000)  data set (parity effect included). One of Feldman's key motivations for developing this new algebra of concept learning was that Boolean propositions seemed implausible as mnemonic representations because they suggest an all-or-none category representation. This contradicts the generally accepted notion that category membership is represented in a graded fashion and that performance tends to increase with exemplar typicality.  Feldman (2006)  used proportion correct as a proxy for measuring typicality. We advise caution here since this would assume that category membership tends to become all-or-none as proportion correct approaches  one . It would be preferable to use subjective ratings based on a typicality scale (e.g.,  Martin & Caramazza, 1980 ).

A unique feature of this concept algebra is that it assumes that human learners are biased towards linear concepts (concepts with spectral power at degree one or less; see  Feldman, 2006 ).

                          This bias leads to simplified or regularized interpretations of the set of category exemplars:  
                            The interpreted world would exhibit a more coherent structure than the world itself […] Such an interpretation would reflect the observer's bias towards a more structured interpretation–a bias strong enough to override some degree of disagreement with the data itself. ( Feldman, 2006, p. 346 ).

The “linearity hypothesis” introduced in the concept algebra adds a constraint on learning that gives it an inductive power and reduces the risk of over-fitting the data. However, this restriction to linear concepts implies that not all possible concepts can be expressed or learned. This is markedly different from  Feldman (2000, 2003a) 's approach based on minimized Boolean propositions, which provides a complete expressive power but no inductive capability. While they are clearly related, these two approaches seem to follow different objectives. Minimized Boolean functions are essentially descriptive. They provide a logical definition of a concept and have the attractive property to naturally translate into verbal rules. This approach must rely on models of category learning (e.g., RULEX) to explain how these concept definitions may have been learned. Conversely,  Feldman (2006) 's concept algebra is mainly focused on modeling constraints in human concept learning, rather than expressing the precise category assignment. Boolean propositions seem to be the appropriate choice for representing individual classification patterns, while the concept algebra seems more appropriate for modeling general tendencies in human category learning.

The empirical results discussed throughout this paper concern only the classification of separable-dimension stimuli, in contrast to integral dimension stimuli, which are perceived as relatively unanalyzable wholes.  Nosofsky and Palmeri (1996)  tested the SHJ concept types using integral-dimension stimuli and observed a specific inversion in the difficulty ordering, namely that SHJ Type II was now more difficult to learn than Types III–V. The selective attention mechanisms hypothesized by exemplar models can explain why separable and integral-dimension stimuli result in different difficulty rank orderings.

Smith et al. (2004)  studied the SHJ tasks both with humans and rhesus monkeys using separable-dimension stimuli. The I<II<[III, IV, V]<VI difficulty ordering was replicated in the human group. In the monkey group, the relatively slower learning of the Type I problem and the observed difficulty ordering (I<[III, IV, V]<II<VI) are well explained by similarity-based processes. Note the inversion in the rank ordering of Type II concepts, identical to the inversion observed by  Nosofsky and Palmeri (1996)  when using separable versus integral-dimension stimuli.

Smith et al. (2004)  also studied the backward learning curves for Type I and II problems. They illustrated how Type I and II tasks are gradually learned by monkeys, but not by humans. The backward learning curves showed how proportion correct changes quite suddenly in the human group (and remains high thereafter), allowing the point where the rule is discovered to be isolated. These results “underscore the theoretical interpretation that the human categorization system transcends associative or similarity-based processes” in these tasks ( Smith et al., 2004, p. 409 ).

Martin and Caramazza (1980)  hypothesized that participants learn classification rules and sequentially test feature values to assess category membership. The sequence of feature tests is represented using decision trees. Symbolizing the rule verification process using decision trees is, in an important way, emblematic of the feature lists that were used as concept definitions in the classical theory of categorization. Decision trees are more elaborate than feature lists in the sense that they include not only the criteria for category judgment but also an explicit serial decision process. Unlike necessary and sufficient feature lists, decision trees readily express disjunctive concepts. Most importantly, decision tree models can explain why particular stimuli are categorized more quickly than others in that response time tends to increase with the number of sequential feature tests required to reach a given decision node, or leaf, in the tree ( Martin & Caramazza, 1980 ).

Nosofsky, Palmeri et al. (1994)  detailed the stages in a hypothesis-testing strategy for learning classification rules (and exceptions) and related this learning process to the formation of a decision tree. However, classification decisions in RULEX are not described as a sequence of individual feature tests. Category judgments are made by sequentially verifying stored rules and exceptions, going from the most specific (exceptions) to the most general (single-dimension rules). This process terminates when a match is found. First, RULEX tests whether the stimulus matches a stored exception. If not, it checks for a stored conjunctive rule, then a single-dimension rule. RULEX makes a random guess if no matching rule is found. Clearly, the RULEX model could be extended to account for response times on the basis of this decision process.

Martin and Caramazza (1980)  developed a framework using decision trees to specify how Boolean rules might be tested when classifying a stimulus. The number of feature tests required to reach a decision node in the tree was used to explain the relative response times of the stimuli. Martin and Caramazza did not, however, attempt to provide a quantitative account for response times. Surprisingly, this had already been achieved by  Trabasso, Rollins, and Shaughnessy (1971) .  Trabasso et al. (1971)  introduced formal decision tree models (Models 1a, 2 and 3) that show good fits to response times when testing simple classification rules stated by the experimenter.  Lafond and Lacouture (2005)  attempted to use  Trabasso et al. (1971) 's models to explain response times and choice proportions for the individual participant results reported by  Lamberts (2000) . To use these models, Lafond and Lacouture first identified the best-fitting decision tree for each participant, which allowed the equations for each stimulus to be specified. These models showed very promising fits, even though there was no systematic method for identifying the best-fitting decision trees. One important limitation of Boolean rules is that they define category membership as all-or-none ( Feldman, 2006 ). We suggest that while minimized Boolean formulae cannot represent concept membership in a graded manner, decision trees representing the verification process of a Boolean proposition could be used to indicate which exemplars are regular members of a category (those higher in the tree) and which are more irregular category members (those lower in the decision tree). Decision tree models may thus provide a quantitative account for typicality judgments and response times, two variables that did not initially appear to be within the reach of a rule-based view of categorization.
