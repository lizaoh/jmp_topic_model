Despite the popularity of maze paradigms such as the Morris water maze and the radial maze for the analysis of learning and memory functions ( Astur, Tropp, Sava, Constable, & Markus, 2004; Paganelli et al., 2004 ), the statistical analyses of maze learning data are typically restricted to the application of  
                         t 
                      -tests and ANOVA, thereby leaving out potential sources of information inherent in the learning curve.

The purpose of our paper is to provide a mathematical model of maze learning, and to test it in a three-arm maze. Applications to group and individual data are also presented. In addition, we will provide a review of previous literature and discuss how our model differs from other models such as that developed by  Rescorla and Wagner (1972) .

The mathematical analysis of learning uses two different approaches. The first fits empirical equations to the learning curve. The second deals with rational equations developed from certain basic theories that represent relationships found in the experimental data. In the latter case, the shape of the learning curve is the consequence of finding the solutions to the rational equations.

The Rescorla–Wagner model has been influential across many fields of animal and human learning as well as many other areas of psychology ( Miller, Barnet, & Grahame, 1995; Siegel & Lorraine, 1996 ). Over time, weaknesses were noted that led to the development of other models and many of these new models were based on the principles laid in the original Rescorla–Wagner model. In particular, we want to mention the Pearce–Hall model, designed to accommodate some subtle aspects of blocking and latent inhibition. The Pearce–Hall model differs from Rescorla–Wagner in two important respects: it assumes that the salience of the conditioned stimulus is not a constant value and proposes a separate process for the accumulation of negative associative strength ( Mercier, 1996; Pearce & Hall, 1980 ).

The Rescorla–Wagner model was not a real-time model. Parameters such as the duration of a conditioned stimulus, time between unconditioned stimulus presentation, and duration of a trace interval were not explicitly defined. As these factors have strong effects on the course of conditioning, their absence was always a serious limitation and led to the development of time-derivative models ( Balkenius & Morén, 2000; Klopf, 1988; Schmajuk & DiCarlo, 1992; Sutton & Barto, 1981 ). Since the time variable is absent in most maze learning experiments (as well as in memory tests in humans) we do not consider these real-time models here.

We mentioned that many empirical equations based on different mathematical functions fit most learning curves. The question arises what criterion should be used for the choice of appropriate mathematical function. Our answer is to use system analysis, based on methods for system identification. System analysis has long existed and is widely used in engineering ( Eykoff, 1974; Sage & Melsa, 1971 ), biology ( Grodins, 1963; Milsum, 1966 ) and the ergonomic assessment of labor skills ( Towill, 1976 ).

A system under identification is treated as “a black box”, its internal structure is unknown, and behavior of the system is analyzed in the terms of input and output signals that are connected with each other by a transfer function. The simplest input signal is the step function, which is equal to zero up to the moment of the first input and then becomes equal to a constant value, for simplicity equal to 1, until completion of the input signal action ( Grodins, 1963; Milsum, 1966 ).

During maze learning, the reinforcement or reward acts as a stepwise input signal, because it invariably elicits an unconditioned response on every trial. The subjects were male Wistar and albino rats from the Rappolovo nursery in St. Petersburg Russia. The animals were between six to seven weeks of age (weight of 160–170 g) and housed in groups of 3–4 animals in plastic cages. They were maintained on a standard 12:12 h light–dark cycle with lights on at 8:00 AM. Behavioral testing was during the light phase. Animals were allowed continuous access to food (standard pellets and fresh vegetables), and water ad lib before the beginning of the experiment.

The training apparatus was a 3-arm radial maze constructed of opaque dark brown plastic. To prevent escape it was enclosed with three sliding covers made of clear Plexiglas ( Fig. 1 
                         ). Each arm was 460 mm long, 210 mm high and 210 mm wide. All arms converged at an equal

The length of the startbox was 248 mm ( Fig. 1 .A, 2), and the length of the goalbox ( Fig. 1 .A, 4) was 200 mm. Every arm functioned as the runway and goal box. Food served as the reward and was placed via the opening in the cover of the goal box.

Eight Wistar and seven albino rats were randomly selected from the laboratory colony one week before the start of training, and were housed in individual plastic cages with water continuously available. Their weight was 180–200 g at the beginning of the experiment.

Rats were taught to visit a bright goalbox to get food — a small piece of cheese “Rossiyskiy”™weighting 35–45 mg and being of homogenous structure without air cavities.

The experiment consisted of three stages. The first stage — pretraining sessions, the second stage — the initial training, the third stage (for Wistar rats only) — the re-acquisition of training 7 weeks after the first training session (i.e., a repeat of stage two). Both initial and re-acquisition training consisted of 5 consecutive training sessions, one session per a day.

From day 1 of pretraining, animals were placed on a restricted diet (about one half of usual food supply) with free access to water. All animals received 3 pretraining sessions during 3 consecutive days with one session per a day. A rat was placed in the startbox of arm A and allowed to freely explore all three arms. The goalbox in every arm contained a piece of cheese. To access the goalbox, a rat had to push a door ( Fig. 1 .B, 6) in the partition ( Fig. 1 .A, B, 3). Each pretraining session lasted 15 min or until a rat consumed 20 pieces of cheese. The purpose of the pretraining phase was to train animals to access the goalbox and to adapt to the maze. All animals received food after the pretraining session.

The same food deprivation protocol (about one half of usual food supply with free access to water with access to food after the training session) is used during the second stage. The second stage — initial light/dark discrimination training — lasted for five consecutive days. Each training session lasted until 20 pieces of cheese were eaten. Experiments were run at 10 AM.

After the end of the second stage, Wistar rats were returned to their full standard diet. This feeding routine was supported during the interval between the second and the third stages. The third stage–re-acquisition of training–was the same as the second stage but occurred 7 weeks later. To increase motivation and memory of stage two training, each Wistar rat was placed on a restricted diet three days prior to the start of stage three and received 5 g of “Rossiyskiy”™ cheese. When stage three commenced, the same food deprivation protocol (about one half of the usual food supply with free access to water and assess to food after the training session) was begun.

The details of training apply to both stage 2 and stage 3. Before the start of each training session, a piece of cheese was put into the distal box of every arm. Next, an animal was placed in the startbox of arm A, so that arm A was always the starting location and was illuminated. The B and the C arms were dark. The doors in arms B and C were fixed, and the door in arm A released. In this way the rat could obtain food only in the goalbox of illuminated arm A. As a rule, an animal began to explore the maze. If the rat ran out from the A arm and into a dark B or C arm, it could not open the door to obtain the cheese and the animal returned to illuminated arm A. Here the animal could freely come into the distal box of arm A through the released door and get food. This piece of the cheese was considered the “starting piece” and the session lasted until the next 20 pieces of cheese were eaten. Training began from the moment an animal received the “starting piece” of cheese.

In general, the strategy to select which arm to illuminate next consisted in illuminating the arm that was just visited before the animal entered the currently illuminated arm. If a rat, placed into starting arm A, entered into the distal box with food, the C arm was illuminated and the door was unlocked. The light in the A arm was turned off when the animal was eating the cheese, and next arm was illuminated. The door in the distal box, where the rat ate the cheese, was fixed when the rat had left the box. The animal could run from the A arm clockwise and first visit the dark B arm and, moving further in the same direction, reached the illuminated C arm where it received free access to cheese. If the animal ran in a counter-clockwise direction, it first reached the illuminated C arm and ate the cheese there. Behavior of animal was recorded on a magnetic type recorder in the form of a verbal report.

Analyzing the shape of the learning curve attracts attention from both a theoretical and practical point of view. Theoretically, it contributes to the development of general learning theory. In a practical sense, it adds additional information on memory in animals and humans and on the influence of internal and external factors on memory processes. Analysis of the shape of the learning curve becomes effective if it is based on adequate quantitative mathematical models. Maze paradigms currently in use do not include such models. Moreover, the California Learning Memory Test (CVLT), widely used in clinical practice, does not use any mathematical model for the assessment of the learning curve ( Delis et al., 1987 ).

As we mentioned in the introduction, an enormous literature exists regarding the analysis of the learning curve. The problem faced by the researcher is to decide, which mathematical model to use. In the case of our 3-arm radial maze light–dark discrimination data, we have shown that the learning curve could be more or less equally well approximated with the hyperbola, the arc cotangent, the logarithmic or exponential transfer function. On the other hand, the Rescorla–Wagner model of classical conditioning also results in the same negatively accelerated learning curve, and absolutely coincides with the transfer function model.

The relationship of our model with the Rescorla–Wagner model gives us confidence that the transfer function for the first order linear system can easily and comfortably be applied to learning paradigms ( Balaban & Stepanov, 1996; Stepanov, 1983; Stepanov & Abramson, 2005; Stepanov et al., 1988a,b ). Examples of successful applications of the transfer function model include food avoidance in land snails ( Balaban & Stepanov, 1996 ), human eye blink conditioning ( Lokhov, Stepanov, & Vartanian, 1990 ), and the memorization of pictures in the memory recognition test in humans ( Stepanov & Abramson, 2005 ).

An opinion was expressed that an averaged curve cannot be considered merely a reflection of the individual curves ( Brown & Heathcote, 2003; Estes, 2002; Gallistel et al., 2004; Merrell, 1931; Wixted, 1997 ). We found no significant difference between the mean values based on the individual curves coefficients and the values of coefficients after fitting of averaged experimental data sampled from animals whose individual learning curves were fitted with the proposed mathematical model.

Our results are in agreement with the point of view of Brown and Heathcote that “An average is functionally isomorphic only when it has the same mathematical form as its components” ( Brown & Heathcote, 2003 ). Therefore, the application of a regression analysis, coupled with the use of the proposed mathematical model can be used to assess individual learning curves for control as well as experimental animals or participants. Subgroups of animals with significant exponential learning curve can be used for further analysis to obtain averaged values of the model’s coefficients. In other words, our model is valid for fitting averaged group data, if averaging is applied to a subgroup of subjects possessing individual exponential learning curves.

In our future work, we want to draw attention that the 3-arm radial light–dark discrimination task can be used in a variety of experimental tasks with novel nootropics and other pharmacological drugs. That our mathematical treatment of the learning curve can model the behavior of simple learning tasks in both humans and animals, suggests that the model can be used to compare the effects of drugs under various conditions of memory formation, not only in animal experiments, but also in clinical practice.
