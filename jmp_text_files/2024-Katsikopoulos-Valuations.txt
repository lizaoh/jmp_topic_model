The priority heuristic ( Brandstätter, Gigerenzer & Hertwig, 2006 ) is a model of decision making under risk. It aims to predict the majority response of people in choices between gambles. In this heuristic, gamble attributes—such as the minimum outcome, the probability of the minimum outcome, and the maximum outcome—are compared between gambles and combined in a lexicographic form by employing numerical thresholds, leading to semi-orders ( Drechsler, Katsikopoulos & Gigerenzer, 2014 ). Unlike expected utility theory and its modifications such as prospect theory ( Kahneman & Tversky, 1979 ;  Wakker & Tversky, 1993 ), the priority heuristic does not transform or integrate outcomes and probabilities. The heuristic does not have free parameters; the order in which attributes are inspected and the thresholds used in attribute comparisons are fixed.

Despite any apparent lack of sophistication, the priority heuristic has several merits. It predicts, out-of-sample, majority choices more accurately than benchmarks such as prospect theory ( Brandstätter et al., 2006 ). Such empirical results have been rationalized by showing that the heuristic implies major violations of expected utility theory ( Katsikopoulos & Gigerenzer, 2008 ). Additionally, a parameterized version of the priority heuristic has been axiomatized ( Drechsler et al., 2014 ). Of course, the heuristic has shortcomings too. It cannot account for individual differences and intricate patterns of choice ( Birnbaum, 2008 ,  2010 ). Furthermore, hybrids of the priority heuristic and prospect theory ( Erev et al., 2017 ), and other ensembles ( He, Analytis & Bhatia, 2022 ), as well as neural networks ( Bourgin et al., 2019 ), have been developed that predict individual choices much more accurately in some environments.

This note focuses on an important purported shortcoming of the priority heuristic: Because the heuristic is designed to only output a choice between gambles, it seems that it cannot produce valuations of gambles. If this were so, it would reduce the scope of the heuristic; it could not, for example, be applied to key effects such as the Saint Petersburg paradox ( Hayden & Platt, 2009 ) or the equity premium puzzle ( Mehra & Prescott, 1985 ). Indeed, the heuristic has been assumed to be silent on such findings. The contribution of this note is twofold. First, I point out that  Rieger and Wang (2008)  have derived the certainty equivalent of a gamble for the priority heuristic. This fact opens the door for the heuristic to be applied to effects involving valuation, such as the Saint Petersburg paradox and the equity premium puzzle. By making simple auxiliary assumptions and calculations, I demonstrate that the priority heuristic can explain these two key effects, arguably more parsimoniously and plausibly than standard approaches.

The next section provides a concise definition of the priority heuristic. The section after that outlines the heuristic's merits and shortcomings. The certainty equivalent of a gamble for the heuristic is presented next. Then, valuations are made by the priority heuristic and used to explain the Saint Petersburg paradox and the equity premium puzzle. In a brief conclusion, insights generated by the heuristic are discussed.

In other words, the heuristic implements a lexicographic order where in its first step the attribute inspected is the minimum outcome, in a second step the probability of minimum outcome, and in the third and final step the attribute inspected is the maximum outcome. Between-gamble comparisons for the first two steps employ positive thresholds, both equaling 0.1 of the upper bound for the corresponding attribute value.

Brandstätter et al. (2006)  tested the priority heuristic and prospect theory, and another 12 models including the transfer-of-attention-exchange model ( Birnbaum & Chavez, 1997 ) and security-potential/aspiration theory ( Lopes & Oden, 1999 ), using well-known published data from four laboratory studies, involving 260 choices. This exercise measured predictive accuracy because the parameters of models were fixed based on data other than the data in which models were tested. The average, across the four datasets, accuracy in predicting the participants’ majority choice was 90% for the priority heuristic and 74 % for prospect theory, and the heuristic was more accurate in all datasets. This result fits with the fact that the heuristic logically implies major violations of expected utility theory—which partly comprise the data—such as common consequence effects, common ratio effects, and the four-fold pattern of risk attitude ( Katsikopoulos & Gigerenzer, 2008 ).

On the other hand,  Birnbaum (2008 ,  2010 ) presented conditions under which the priority heuristic could not empirically account well for people's choices. For example, most people violated three properties of lexicographic semi-order models, such as the priority heuristic—non-compensatoriness ( Martignon, Katsikopoulos & Woike, 2008 ) and patterns of attribute integration or interaction ( Birnbaum, 2010 ). More recently, BEAST, a hybrid of the priority heuristic and prospect theory, boosted with the sampling of gamble realizations and the computation of expected values, outperformed its component models in predicting 750 individual choices of 125 Technion students, which encompassed 14 effects ( Erev et al., 2017 ). Ensembles with more models did even better across 19 datasets involving more than 800 participants ( He et al., 2022 ). Finally, a big training dataset (185,000 data points) generated by BEAST was used by  Bourgin et al. (2019)  to train a neural network that was then fine-tuned by human data and halved the mean squared error of BEAST (0.0053 versus 0.0099).

There are more thought experiments than empirical evidence on how much money people offer.  Hayden and Platt (2009)  ran a survey with 220 participants, including  The New York Times  readers and Duke University students. The median valuation of the gamble was 1.5 dollars, which coincides with the true median. The modal offer, made by 68 participants, was 1 dollar and only 28 participants valued the gamble at 8 dollars or more.

Bernoulli's explanation for the Saint Petersburg paradox is that people have a utility function that exhibits diminishing sensitivity, an idea that spawned the revision of expected-value theory into expected utility theory. Another explanation is that people might doubt that very large sums could or would be paid out. But quantitative versions of both explanations overestimate the observed valuations, implying offers around 20 dollars ( Hayden & Platt, 2009 ). It is interesting to develop models that are more accurate than the state-of-art and predict more old and new effects, but surely there is more to modeling? Box's maxim that all models are wrong but some are useful reminds us that we must seek theoretical insight. The priority heuristic attempts to provide insight by specifying the processes underlying choices between gambles, and this feature has stimulated empirical and conceptual efforts ( Johnson, Schulte-Mecklenbeck & Willemsen, 2008 ;  Pachur, Hertwig, Gigerenzer & Brandstätter, 2013 ;  Katsikopoulos, 2023 ).

The simple steps of the priority heuristic can interact and generate complex effects such as the four-fold pattern of risk attitude ( Katsikopoulos & Gigerenzer, 2008 ), without transforming and integrating outcomes and probabilities. Such results suggest an intriguing possibility: The concepts of diminishing sensitivity, risk and loss aversion, probability weighting, sampling and expected value computation—while having their rightful place in the study of human decision making—are not always necessary to model explicitly, at least not to capture choices ( Friedman, Issac, James & Sunder, 2014 ;  Katsikopoulos, 2023 ). This note demonstrates that there might be more to this possibility, because it can apply to valuations too. The priority heuristic has empirical shortcomings, yes, but it can be theoretically useful.
