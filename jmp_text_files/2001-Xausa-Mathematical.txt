A Mathematical Model of Depth Displacement with a Contracting Bar 

E. Xausa, L. Beghi, and L. M. Zanforlin 

Universita�di Padova, Padua, Italy 

When a vertical line segment contracts at both ends according to a (decelerating) time law of hyperbolic type, it appears to rotate around its midpoint. The phenomenon is quite surprising from the point of view of projective geometry as the segment should rather appear to recede along the sagittal plane. Apparent displacement in depth is however obtained when the bar simultaneously contracts and is displaced laterally on the frontal plane. But, here again, projective expectations are contradicted, because apparent displacement in depth occurs whether movement of the bar in two dimensions is hyperbolic (decelerating), uniform, harmonic (accelerating), or a mixture of the three. It is suggested here that the visual system operates in such a way as to minimize the differences between the lengths of the velocity vectors of all points of a moving configuration. The mathematical model derived from this hypothesis allows qualitative and quantitative predictions that are in good agreement with experimental results. � 2001 Academic Press 

INTRODUCTION 

The study of visual perception of three-dimensionality from moving stimuli displayed on a plane in front of an observer has a long tradition and many unexpected results. 

One example is offered by a contracting bar. A rigid bar receding along the sagittal plane projects onto the retina an image that contracts with a movement of hyperbolic decelerating type, while a bar rotating around the horizontal axis produces an image of a bar that contracts with a movement of harmonic type. 

But when a vertical line segment contracting at both ends is presented on a uniform frontal plane to an observer, whether its contraction is hyperbolic or harmonic it appears, in both cases, to rotate around its midpoint (Tampieri, 1959). 

Obviously, disregarding the speed of contraction, the apparent rotation of the hyperbolically contracting bar is also projectively compatible; the problem arises of why only the rotation appears and never the movement in depth or a mixture of the two. 

One may suppose that the nondisplacement in depth of the bar is due to the fact that the line should also shrink in width, as does the retinal projection of a receding real solid object or a rectangular figure.

However, although there is no change in width, when the contracting bar is simultaneously displaced laterally on the frontal plane, whether the contraction is hyperbolic or harmonic, it appears to move in depth. Tampieri (1959, 1964), who first observed the phenomenon, was unable to explain it. 

Indeed, there are no reasons, in projective geometry, for why lateral displacement on the frontal plane of a contracting bar with a constant width should induce the impression of movement in depth. Both Wallach and O'Connell (1953) and Johansson (1964) observed that to obtain apparent depth displacement, movement in two dimensions is necessary. But Johansson (1964) was referring to the simultaneous vertical and horizontal shrinking of a rectangular figure, and he considered it a basic law of visual perception in accordance with projective geometry. 

However, even when a rectangular figure contracts in both dimensions, there is another surprising result: the amount of displacement in depth is far shorter than that expected from projective geometry for a rigid object (Marmolin, 1973; Gogel 6 Eby, 1997). 

Various other noncorrespondences of perceived depth with projective geometry of rigid objects have lead recent authors to question the prevalent ``rigidity assumption'' (Ullman, 1979, 1984) in structure reconstruction from motion and to hypothesize that the visual system only records properties of the 3D objects that are invariant under affine transformations (Todd 6 Bressan, 1990; Todd 6 Norman, 1991; Koenderink 6 van Doorn, 1991; Borjesson 6 Lind, 1996). 

In general, the artificial intelligence models of structure reconstruction from motion such as those of Ullman (1984), Hoffman and Bennet (1985, 1986), Bennett and Hoffman (1985), Norman and Todd (1994), and Pollick (1994) aim at establishing under which conditions and assumptions a real 3D object can be recovered from its 2D retinal image. This is equivalent to establishing the properties of an ideal visual system. When this ideal system is compared with the performance of the human visual system the limits and the few advantages of the latter can be pointed out but not explained. 

Given that the human visual system is not ideal, the problem arises of how it works. From this point of view the problem is not one of recovering the real object (the visual system has other ways of recovering the real world besides movement), but that of explaining the percepts produced by any moving retinal image whether they do or do not correspond to the real object. In particular, the present paper attempts to explain how an ambiguous retinal image produces only those particular percepts and not others, and how an image that in an ideal system should be unambiguous produces a 3D image different from the real object.[1] 

1 For example, a rigid bar rotating at a uniform velocity around an axis parallel to the image plane with its midpoint moving along the axis at uniform velocity should produce a 2D image from which the real object can be unambiguously recovered under the assumptions of Hoffman and Bennet (1986). But, as we will see later on, all the subjects faced with such a 2D image will perceive a rotating bar whose midpoint moves in depth and not along the frontal plane. Moreover, as Pollick (1994) has pointed out when considering the silhouette of an ellipsoid rotating around a vertical axis, the real object cannot be recovered if the angular velocity of the real object is not known: it can be a short object moving quickly or a long one oscillating slowly. The same consideration holds for the contracting line segment that can be assimilated to a very thin ellipsoid. So even the most recent theories of structure from motion (Pollick, 1994; Norman 6 Todd, 1994) cannot explain why a contracting line segment or bar appears to rotate and not to move in depth when it contracts with hyperbolic velocity or why it appears to move in depth when it is displaced laterally even if its contracting velocity is harmonic.

It is suggested here that the visual system operates in such a way as to minimize the differences between the lengths of velocity vectors of all points of a moving configuration. 

The hypothesis, derived from the gestalt theory of minimum principle (Koffka, 1933; Musatti, 1931);[2] Hatfield 6 Epstein, 1985), has already been successfully applied to other phenomena concerning the recovery of structure from motion (Beghi, Xausa, 6 Zanforlin, 1991a, 1997b; Beghi, Xausa, De Biasio, 6 Zanforlin, 1991b; Beghi, Xausa, Tomat, 6 Zanforlin, 1997a; Xausa, Beghi, 6 Zanforlin, 1997; Zanforlin, 1988; Zanforlin 6 Vallortigara, 1988, 1990). 

The absolute values of velocity differences between any two points moving on the frontal plane can be minimized or equated by adding (or subtracting) to the velocity vector of each point a component in the third dimension in such a way that the sum of the components has equal length for all points or has a minimum difference; i.e., when two points move at different velocities on the frontal plane, by equalizing the absolute values of their velocities one of the two will appear to move in depth. When a point of the configuration has zero velocity value it will act as an ``anchor'' point and it will appear to be stationary. 

The mathematical model derived from this hypothesis not only explains why the lateral displacement of contracting lines produces the apparent movement in depth, but it also predicts three new aspects of the phenomenon not previously observed. First, that lateral displacement of a bar has far less effect than contraction on the amount of depth perceived. Second, that the apparent depth displacement of a bar, no matter what its type of contraction, will always be associated with an apparent rotation or an elastic shrinking. Third, that the amount of apparent rotation of a bar moving in depth will not be very different from that of a line with no lateral displacement. 

The predictions are confirmed by the experimental results here presented.

EXPERIMENTAL CONTROL OF THE HYPOTHESIS 

The mathematical model presented here, besides explaining the known phenomena produced by a contracting bar, as described in the Introduction, predicts some new aspects not observed before. 

The first regards the demonstration of the existence of an apparent rotation and of a simultaneous displacement in depth produced by a bar periodically contracting and undergoing a lateral displacement on the frontal plane according to a hyperbolic time law and also according to a uniform or a harmonic one. 

The second regards the quantitative evaluation of the amount of apparent depth displacement of this contracting bar, for which no experimental data could be found in the literature.

For technical reasons, Tampieri (1959, 1964) (who used a mechanical device to produce the stimuli) could produce only uniform lateral displacements of contracting bars. He did not report the simultaneous apparent rotation jointly with the depth displacement of bars with contractions of hyperbolic type, nor did he present quantitative data for the various phenomena observed. 

As regards the estimation of the amount of apparent depth displacement for contracting figures, we have found in the literature only the work of Marmolin (1973) and Gogel and Eby (1997), who both used rectangular figures simultaneously contracting in height and width, with no lateral displacement. As we have not considered here two-dimensional figures, experimental control of the hypothesis will regard only movements of the contracting bars. 

The third prediction concerns the amount of the apparent angular rotation of contracting bars with or without lateral displacement, for which no data were found in literature. 

APPARATUS AND STIMULI 

In order to validate our models, we presented to 13 subjects on a computer screen 4 different groups of stimuli (situations a, b, c, d). 

(a) To check the effects of different amounts of contraction of bars with simultaneous lateral displacement, 3 bars 6.5 cm long and 1 mm wide were presented. The bars contracted and expanded with a movement of hyperbolic type and at the same time moved 3.6 cm laterally according to an hyperbolic law. The three bars contracted respectively to lengths of 4.3, 3.2, and 2 cm. 

(b) To check the effects of different amounts of lateral displacement on contracting bars, 3 bars with the same size as the previous ones were presented; they contracted to a length of 3.2 cm and moved laterally 2, 3.6, and 5.3 cm, respectively. Both contraction and lateral movement were of hyperbolic type. 

(c) To check the effects of different types of contraction movement, two bars of the same size as the previous ones were presented, one contracting with a motion of harmonic type and the other with a motion of uniform type. Both bars contracted to a length of 3.2 cm and moved 3.6 cm laterally with uniform time law. 

(d) To check the amount of apparent rotation of bars with no lateral displacement, 3 bars of the same size as the previous ones, contracting at both ends to a length of 3.2 cm, were presented: one of them contracted with uniform time law, one with hyperbolic time law, and the last with harmonic time law. 

All the bars continuously contracted and returned to the original size and position with an inverted movement until the subjects gave their evaluations. 

The moving bars were presented in randomized order at a distance of 1 m from the subjects, so that the bars at maximum length subtended visual angles of approximately 3.7 degrees in length and 0.006 degrees in width. 

Lateral movement speed was of 1.6 cm�s; contraction speed depended on the amount of simultaneous lateral displacement.

The experimental room was dimly illuminated in such a way that the stimuli and the computer were clearly visible and the subjects could easily perceive their distance from the screen. 

PROCEDURE 

The subjects were seated in front of a computer with their head at the same height as the screen and not supported by a chin rest. 

They were asked: 

(1) to observe the moving bar with one eye only (they covered the other with a hand) and describe the apparent movement for each bar; 

(2) to estimate the amount of apparent movement in depth of the midpoint of the bar, and 

(3) to estimate the apparent rotation in degrees. 

To facilitate the estimation of the length of bars and of depth displacement, a ruler was positioned horizontally along the side of the computer. The apparent rotation of the bar was estimated by asking the subjects to position a clock hand on a graduated disk in such a way that it corresponded to the maximum apparent slant in depth of the bar. 

The subjects were allowed to continuously observe the moving bars until they were sure of their impressions and estimations. 

The 13 subjects were university students with normal or corrected to normal sight.

Of the various projectively compatible percepts, i.e., rotation and�or displacement in depth, all subjects reported perceiving only rotation. For all three types of contraction the bars appeared to describe a rotatory movement in depth around their stationary midpoint, thus confirming the results reported by previous authors and the predictions of our model. 

The amount of apparent rotation does not show a statistically significant difference among the three cases. The general average is 47.9% (s.d. 15.14) and it is within two standard deviations from that predicted by the model. 

This average is not statistically different from the average reported above for apparent rotation of bars with equal contraction and lateral displacement, which also appeared to move in depth. That the apparent rotation in the two cases should not have been very different was predicted by the model. 

CONCLUSIONS 

The qualitative predictions of the mathematical model are fully confirmed by the experimental results here reported. 

(1) Contracting bars with no lateral displacement appear to rotate around their midpoints for all velocity types of contraction. 

(2) When contraction is combined with simultaneous lateral displacement, bars appear to move in depth as well as rotating around their midpoint. This combination of movements, predicted by the model, was not previously reported. 

(3) Although lateral displacement is necessary to induce an impression of movement in depth of the midpoint of bar, the amount of depth displacement perceived depends mainly on vertical contraction of the bars and very little on lateral displacement, a further prediction of the model that had not been previously observed. 

(4) The absolute amount of displacement in depth perceived in all situations is quite close to that predicted by the model and far shorter than what would have been predicted by the projective geometry of rigid objects: for instance, a rigid bar perceived at a distance of one meter, whose retinal image shrinks to half its initial size, should be perceived as moving to a depth of one meter and not to a depth of 20 cm as estimated by the subjects. 

(5) The values of perceived rotation in all situations are also close to those predicted by the model. 

(6) A particularly interesting and unexpected effect of the contracting line is that the amount of perceived rotation is not very different whether the line appears to rotate around its stationary midpoint or rotate while moving in depth. A bar whose retinal image shrinks by half its size should appear to rotate in depth at an angle of 60% and not of 47% as reported by the subjects. Moreover, one would expect the perceived rotation to be significantly less when the bar appears to move in depth as well, because part of the contraction would be attributed to movement in depth. Instead, the apparent rotation reported by subjects in the latter case is only slightly less than in the previous situation. This small difference in apparent

Although the values obtained by the model are lower (35.2% and 29.8% respectively) than the experimental ones (47.9% and 44.6%), no comparison can be made between these values and those obtainable from the projective geometry of rigid objects, owing to the fact that both movements are compatible with the conic projections on the frontal plane. Any subdivision between the two movements is possible, as long as the two sum up to the full contraction of the retinal image. 

In spite of the fact that the qualitative predictions of the model are fully confirmed by the results and the quantitative predictions show the same trend and are quite close to the experimental data, the values obtained from the model are lower than the experimental ones. It may be that this discrepancy is due to the fact that the model does not take into account the frame of reference nor the distance of the observer from the moving bars. A further development of the model will check this hypothesis. 

However, the predictions derived by the present model appear, in general, well supported by the experimental results. 

Unlike various models in artificial intelligence, the present model does not aim to establish conditions and assumptions necessary to recover real 3D objects from their 2D projections. The relative velocity differences minimization hypothesis is intended to explain the actual performance of the human visual system, i.e., the various percepts produced by moving retinal images whether or not they correspond to the real objects that have produced the images. 

So far the velocity minimization model has been applied to various other phenomena (Beghi et al., 1991a, 1991b, 1997a, 1997b; Xausa et al., 1997; Zanforlin, 1988; Zanforlin 6 Vallortigara, 1988, 1990) besides those here presented, and it appears more valid in explaining the perceived structure from motion than any of those based on ``rigidity assumption.''
