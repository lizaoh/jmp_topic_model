An important distinction in contemporary memory research is that between explicit and implicit memory. Explicit memory is considered to be a memory associated with conscious awareness. Explicit memory is exemplified by the situation when a person accurately recalls what they ate for dinner last evening or when a person correctly recognizes a friend who has been away for several months. In contradistinction to explicit memory, implicit memory is considered as a residue from prior experience that is not capable of supporting conscious recall or confident correct recognition. The residue might be the remains of a memory trace that has undergone dramatic weakening. Although implicit memory is a weak residue, it is still capable of influencing above chance recognition guessing or is capable of producing priming.

The distinction between explicit and implicit memory emerged from early work in neuropsychology. For example,  Warrington and Weiskrantz (1974)  found that if amnesic patients read a word earlier, then later they would be more likely to complete an ambiguous word fragment with that word even though they reported being unable to remember seeing the word previously. Initially, memory researchers attempted to link implicit and explicit memory to specific tasks or dependent variables. The assumption was that certain tasks tapped only a single type of memory. This assumption or hypothesis is called the process purity approach.  Roediger and McDermott (1993)  and  Schacter (1987)  reviewed many experimental studies that were conducted under the assumption of the process purity approach. Here the memory researcher typically assumes that some available dependent variable taps only implicit memory while some other variable taps only explicit memory. Yet it is difficult to know, and unreasonable to assume, that there are cognitive tasks that influence only a single process. It is more likely that any specific dependent variable involves a mixture of underlying cognitive processes. Multinomial processing tree modeling (MPT) is ideally suited for disentangling the latent cognitive processes that are involved in experimental tasks.  Batchelder and Riefer (1999)  and  Erdfelder et al. (2009)  provide excellent reviews of many of the MPT models in psychology. Unlike the process purity approach, MPT models are tree models of the underlying psychological processes that are involved in a specific task. In some cases, novel experimental procedures are used by the MPT modeler in order to obtain the information needed to estimate the latent psychological processes of interest. In essence the MPT model makes clear how the latent cognitive processes map onto the available dependent variables without assuming the existence of a simple one-to-one mapping between a dependent variable and a latent process. In fact,  Chechile (2007)  and  Chechile and Roder (1998)  have argued that a mathematical model is generally required in order to measure underlying cognitive processes because dependent variables from experiments do not reflect the influence of only one psychological process.

Jacoby (1991)  invented an experimental task and a model that ostensibly provided a means for obtaining separate measures for explicit and implicit memory. An improved extension of the  Jacoby (1991)  model was advanced by  Buchner, Erdfelder, and Vaterrodt-Plünnecke (1995) . These models and the experimental task on which they are based have come to be called the process-dissociation method. The process-dissociation procedure relies on an instructional manipulation. In a typical process-dissociation task, the participants receive memory training on two sources of information, such as a first and a second list. Later the participants are given either inclusion instructions or exclusion instructions. The inclusion instruction directs the subject to respond yes to a test item if the item matches any of the items from either list; whereas the exclusion instruction directs the subjects to only use the yes response for test items believed to be from the second list. The process-dissociation models have been widely used in cognitive psychology (e.g.  Buchner et al., 1995 ,  Jacoby, Toth, & Yonelinas, 1993 ,  McBride & Dosher, 1999 , and  McBride, Dosher, & Gage, 2001 ) as well as in social psychology (e.g.  Buchner & Wippich, 1996 , and  Gaunt, Leynes, & Demoulin, 2002 ).

Although it is advantageous to use a cognitive psychometric approach for measuring explicit and implicit memory, there are many problems with the process-dissociation procedure ( Dodson & Johnson, 1996; Yu & Bellezza, 2000 ). Rather than retracing the criticisms of  Dodson and Johnson (1996)  and  Yu and Bellezza (2000) , we will instead focus on some additional concerns with the process-dissociation procedure that motivated the development of an improved method for separately measuring explicit and implicit memory. Although it is possible to use saturated models, it is also advantageous to have a model that can provide a strong test of the model’s suitability in each experimental condition.                        There is also a third problem with the process-dissociation procedure related to the possibility that the instructions themselves might cause a criterion shift in a signal-detection sense. For example, given a level for familiarity strength, there could be a difference in the criterion for responding yes in the two instructional conditions. In the process-dissociation model, it is assumed that the familiarity process has the same tendency to result in an affirmative response regardless of the instructional condition. But, at a fixed familiarity level, a shift in the criterion to respond yes, caused by the instructional manipulation, would alter and contaminate the estimates of explicit and implicit memory.

Given these difficulties with the process-dissociation procedure, it is advantageous to develop a new model for measuring implicit memory that is: (1) not a saturated model, (2) a model that deals with fractional information storage, and (3) a model that does not rely upon an instructional manipulation. In this paper a new model is advanced for obtaining separate measures of explicit and implicit memory. The model is called the Implicit/Explicit Separation (IES) model. The model is based on a specific experimental task. The framework for the task is developed in Section  2 . A description of the model and parameter estimates are provided in respectively, Sections  3 and 4 . In Section  5  Monte Carlo sample studies are described for studying the accuracy of the estimates as a function of sample size. In Section  6  three experiments are provided that demonstrate how the new model can be used to examine a number of important effects in memory research. Experiment 1 addresses the effect of encoding time; whereas Experiment 2 deals with the effect of generative encoding, and Experiment 3 examines the effect of retention interval. Finally in Section  7 , there is a general discussion of the IES model in light of the experimental findings and a comparison to other models for memory.

It makes sense to differentiate four distinctively different states for target information: (1) explicit memory, (2) an implicit representation of the whole target, (3) fractional memory, and (4) a null state of a complete lack of target information. This four part taxonomy is the key conceptual idea for the IES model, and as such it warrants further elaboration. The notion of explicit storage is the same idea as the concept of sufficient storage, which was advanced in the  Chechile and Meyer (1976)  and the  Chechile (2004)  MPT models. The purpose of those models was to measure separate storage and retrieval components of recall. Sufficient storage was defined as having enough information in memory about the target event that the person would be able to recall the entire target correctly without cuing, provided that the person could gain access to the encoded information. Also, if a target item is in this state of sufficient storage, then the subject is assumed able to confidently recognize the target on an old recognition test.

A few examples might help to clarify the distinction between fractional and implicit storage. Fractional storage means that there is some information about the original encoding that enables a person to reject some foils but not enough information for him to reject all the foils. For example, suppose that a subject was shown a triad of letters, but the subject was only able to retain the first letter of the triad. In this case the subject, when given a list of possible items to choose on a multiple-choice test, would only be able to reject some items, but could not reliably reject all the foils. Implicit storage is different from fractional storage. With implicit storage, the subject might state that he does not know the target but, if asked to select the target from a list of items, then the subject correctly selects the item.

A method has already been advanced for measuring the probability of a fractional representation and for measuring the probability of non-storage by using a contingent series of forced-choice recognition tests ( Chechile, 1998; Chechile & Soraci, 1999 ). The testing procedure includes an initial four-alternative forced-choice test (4 AFC) encompassing a single target along with three foil items. If the subject is correct in choosing the target on the 4 AFC test, then testing for that target is completed. However, if the subject selects instead one of the foils, then feedback is provided, and the incorrect item is removed from the list of alternatives. In this case, the subject is asked to select again, i.e. the task becomes a three-alternative forced-choice task (3 AFC). If the subject is correct on the 3 AFC task, then testing is complete for that target. However, if the subject is again incorrect, then the incorrect alternative is removed, and the subject is asked to choose again between the last two alternatives, i.e. there is a final 2 AFC test.

However, the  Chechile and Soraci (1999)  experimental task did not include test trials where only foils were presented on the 4 AFC test in order to correct for the possibility of a high-confidence error on the forced-choice test. Without that correction, it might be the case that the different storage value found by  Chechile and Soraci (1999)  for the forced-choice method was just the result of an overly confident forced-choice decision. This procedural limitation will be rectified in the new procedure introduced in this paper. However, before discussing the new procedure, it is important to discuss in more detail the measurement of non-storage and fractional storage.

With the contingent forced-choice procedure used by  Chechile (1998)  and  Chechile and Soraci (1999) , there is the possibility that a subject is incorrect on the initial 4 AFC test as well as on the followup 3 AFC and 2 AFC tests. This case is precisely what would be expected if there is absolutely no storage whatsoever for the target event remaining in the memory system. Items that degrade to this degree are indistinguishable from a novel foil. However, if there were partial information available, then at least one of the three foils could have been eliminated from consideration. Consequently, with the contingent forced-choice testing procedure, it is possible to obtain measures for fractional storage and non-storage. Moreover, the measures would have high construct validity because if there is fractional storage, then at least one of the foils should be eliminated, but if there is no storage, then it is possible to get all the tests incorrect.

Finally, let us consider the important possibility that the target information is stored implicitly. When there is implicit storage, it would not be the case that the subject can confidently recognize an item on a yes/no test, but it would be the case that the subject is correct on a followup 4 AFC test.

It should be pointed out that the present theoretical framework differs from the dual-process view of recognition memory advanced by  Yonelinas (1994) . The two processes in the dual-process model are recollection and familiarity. Correct old recognition is assumed to be based on a recollective component as well as a familiarity component such as with a signal detection model for recognition memory; however, foil recognition is assumed to be based on familiarity alone. In contradistinction to the dual-process model, the IES model also has a knowledge-based process in foil recognition.

                            The original  Chechile and Meyer (1976)  model also used, for both old and new recognition, two pathways to a correct response; one based on stored information available at test and a secondary pathway based on guessing. The  Chechile (2004)  models provided different rates for the initial knowledge-based process for old and new recognition tests. 
                          Moreover with the IES model, if information is not explicitly available at the time of test, then there is not a single familiarity process. Instead with the IES model, there are three distinctly different states when information is not explicitly stored, i.e. (1) non-storage (2) fractional storage, and (3) implicit storage. With non-storage it is possible to have all incorrect responses on the contingent forced-choice testing procedure. With fractional storage the subject would be able to avoid the case of all errors on the forced-choice tests. With implicit storage the subject would be either uncertain or incorrect on a yes/no recognition decision, but would still be correct on a 4 AFC task. The detailed tree analysis of the IES model is discussed in the next section.

The task used by  Chechile and Soraci (1999)  was nearly satisfactory because it had a straightforward way for obtaining estimates of fractional and non-storage. In this task the participants are initially presented items to be learned—these target items are later tested after some retention interval. At test, the subjects are first provided with a set of four items and are asked if any of the items were previously presented. The subjects are instructed to give a yes or no response. The subjects are also asked to provide a three-point confidence rating, where a rating of 3 is for a certain response, a 2 rating is for an educated guess, and a 1 rating is for a blind guess. Although there are three confidence levels for the yes/no decision, the two low confidence ratings are grouped together, so the confidence judgment is treated here as either high or low. For target-present trials, one of the four items is a target and the other three items are foils; whereas on target-absent test trials, all four items are foils. Consequently, for the target-absent trials there are four response outcomes for the yes/no confidence judgment, i.e. no high confidence, no low confidence, yes low confidence and yes high confidence (see cells 17–20 shown in  Fig. 2 ). On target-present trials, the yes/no confidence task is followed up with a series of forced-choice recognition tests. For target-present trials, the participant is asked to select the target from the four candidates displayed, i.e. a four-alternative forced-choice task (4 AFC). If the participant is correct on the 4 AFC task, then that target is not tested further, and the recorded response is one of the cell categories from cell 1 to cell 4 from  Fig. 2 , depending on the yes/no confidence response. But if the participant is wrong on the 4 AFC task, then error feedback is provided and the incorrectly selected foil is removed from the candidate list. The participant is then instructed to select again, i.e. the task becomes a 3 AFC task. If the participant is correct on the 3 AFC task, then testing for that target is stopped, and the response would be recorded in one of the cell categories from 5 to 8 in  Fig. 2  (again depending on the yes/no confidence response). If the participant is wrong on both the 4 AFC and 3 AFC tests, then error feedback is again provided and the person is directed to do a 2 AFC task. The results of the 2 AFC task will be a response in either cells 9–12 (if the participant is correct on the 2 AFC task) or in cells 13–16 (if the person is wrong on the 2 AFC task).

For the MLE values, a hybrid approach is used where there is both an empirical parameter search combined with a constrained maximization of the likelihood function based on the candidate values for the searched parameters. The ML solution is the combination of parameter values that maximize  (1) . The MLE is regarded as approximate because only a finite number of search parameter values are assessed. This hybrid procedure is described in detail in the  Appendix .

Subjects . A total of 48 students from Tufts University participated as subjects in this experiment. The participants were enrolled in either Introductory Psychology or Introductory Behavioral Statistics and signed up as subjects to satisfy a research participation requirement associated with those courses.

Design, materials, and procedure . The stimuli were 144 words taken from the  Kucera and Francis (1967)  word norm. The words were randomly selected from the subset of words between four to seven letters long and with a frequency count of 50 or greater. Another 504 words were also randomly selected in the same way for use as foils during the test phase.

The stimuli were grouped into 24 lists of six words each. Across the 24 lists, eight lists were presented at the rate of 45 ms per word, eight lists were presented at the rate of 100 ms per word, and eight lists were presented at the rate of 1000 ms per word. The lists were presented visually on a microcomputer. Each presented word was preceded by and followed by a masking field of “#” signs. The duration of the forward and backward masking field was 12 ms. Across the set of 48 subjects, the words were counterbalanced with regard to encoding time, so that each word served equally in the three encoding time conditions. The within-subject design and counterbalancing of lists assured that there would not be any systematic differences among the three presentation time conditions caused by either subject or item effects.

The subjects were tested individually after receiving instructions along with an informed consent form. The entire experiment consisted of a series of 24 blocks where each block involved the presentation of six words and was followed immediately by six test trials. The Latin square design of the experiment also counterbalanced the three presentation time conditions across subjects for each within-block testing order as well as for each learning serial position. For the presentation phase, all the words on the six-item list were presented at the same rate, which was either 45 ms, 100 ms, or 1000 ms. Across the set of 24 blocks, there were eight blocks for each encoding time, and these eight blocks were distributed evenly across the 24 blocks. Prior to the presentation of the six word list, there was a ready signal that alerted the subject that the list would begin in 2 s. The list began with a 12 ms masking field of a string of “#” signs, followed by the presentation of the first word. The words were presented separately in the same spatial location. The masking field was displayed for 24 ms between consecutive words, i.e. the masking field was on for 12 ms before the first word of the list, and on for 24 ms before each of the subsequent five words of the list. After the presentation of the sixth word of the list, there was a 12 ms masking field followed by a cue saying that there would be a series of memory tests for the preceding list. The subjects were given six test trials. Three of these tests were target-present trials, where one of the four candidate words was a target word. The other three tests were target-absent trials, i.e. all four candidate words were novel foils. The subjects were cued to respond with either a yes or a no response, where a yes decision indicated that one of the four words was a target. Following this yes or no response, the subjects were asked to provide a confidence rating from 1 to 3, where 3 denoted a certain decision, 2 indicated an educated guess, and 1 indicated a blind guess. The definitions of the confidence rating categories were visually present on all yes/no tests. For target-present test trials, the subject followed the confidence rating with a 4 AFC decision as to which word was on the presentation list of six items. If the subject was correct on the 4 AFC test, then that trial was concluded. However, if the subject was incorrect, then the selected foil was removed from the list, and the subject was cued to respond again, i.e. it became a 3 AFC task. If the subject was correct on the 3 AFC test, then testing for that target item was concluded. But if the subject was again incorrect, then the incorrect foil item was removed from the list, and the subject was cued to select again, i.e. a final 2 AFC test. For target-present tests, only words in the list serial position of two to five were examined.

Another way to vary the encoding quality of information in memory is to manipulate the generative requirement. The generation effect refers to the memory retention advantage for items that required a greater degree of engagement on the part of the subject during the initial learning, e.g.  Hirschman and Bjork (1988) ,  Roenker, Wenger, Thompson, and Watkins (1978)  and  Slamecka and Graf (1978) . The control condition for a typical generation effect experiment is the standard memory encoding condition where the subject reads intact target items during the learning stage. This condition is called the “Read” condition. The other condition is the “Generation” condition. In a typical generation condition, a letter from each target word might be missing. Although the missing letter is an easy puzzle to solve, it still results in better memory retention for the memory targets even after many controls are implemented to equate for the total study time and the target words themselves. There have been a number of researchers who have hypothesized that explicit and implicit memory are separate memory systems (e.g.  Cohen & Squire, 1980 ;  Graf & Schacter, 1987 ;  Johnson, 1983 ;  Parkin, 1993 ;  Schacter, 1989 ;  Schacter, Chin, & Ochsner, 1993 ;  Squire, 1986 ;  Squire, 1987 ;  Tulving, 1983 ;  Tulving, 1985 ;  Weiskrantz, 1987  and  Weiskrantz, 1989 ). In fact,  Schacter et al. (1993)  hypothesize separate explicit and implicit memory systems that are controlled from different brain regions. Proponents of separate memory systems typically report finding a dissociation between either variables or tasks that seemingly can be accounted for in terms of the differential roles of two separate memory systems. For example,  Parkin (1993)  argued that implicit memory develops in childhood prior to explicit memory; whereas with aging populations, implicit memory is preserved, but explicit memory undergoes a decline. Yet other developmental researchers find a similar developmental timetable and debate the necessity of postulating a separate memory system ( Rovee-Collier, 1997 ). Proponents of a separate memory system have assumed the process-purity approach. However, critics of the multiple memory systems hypothesis have stressed that tasks and tests differ in regard to the role of explicit and implicit information, and separate systems are not required to handle experimental dissociations ( Roediger, 1990 ).

Also from the cognitive psychometric perspective of the current paper, it is premature to conclude that there must be a separate memory system to process implicitly stored information. For example, with the previous two experiments, the explicit memory component changed with encoding quality, but the implicit memory component did not change reliably with encoding quality. Although there are different patterns for the implicit and explicit measures, it does not necessarily mean that there are separate memory stores for these measures. Perhaps in time information that was explicitly stored degrades to either an implicit representation or to a fractional trace. In fact  Chechile (1987, 2006)  provided evidence that there were dynamic changes in the probability of sufficient storage that are detectable with MPT models. In a number of studies there has been a consistent finding of a large loss in storage with increasing retention interval ( Chechile, 1987, 2004, 2006, 2010b; Chechile & Meyer, 1976; Gerrein & Chechile, 1977 ). From the perspective of a dynamic memory framework, the independent variable of retention interval should be very revealing. As the time subsequent to encoding is increased, it is expected that explicit storage should decline dramatically. As explicit memory degrades, it might be possible to see the implicit storage rate increase, because memory traces that were once explicitly stored might have degraded to the point that the traces are implicitly stored. The finding of an increase in the proportion of implicitly stored traces with increasing retention interval would be a finding that is difficult to reconcile with an independent memory systems framework. If some items were originally stored in a separate implicit memory system, then the proportion of traces in that system should not increase with increasing memory lag. However, if implicit storage represents a state of a memory trace, then a trace might degrade, with memory lag, from an explicit representation to an implicit representation. Consequently, the variable of retention interval should be particularly informative in regard to the hypothesis of separate memory systems for explicit and implicit memory.

Subjects . Fifty-eight undergraduate students from Tufts University served as subjects in this study. The subjects participated as part of a requirement for a class in either Introductory Psychology or Introductory Behavioral Statistics.

Design, materials, and procedure . The stimuli consisted of 120 letter triads selected randomly from the moderately low meaningfulness range of the  Witmer (1935)  norm. Another set of 420 triads were also selected in the same fashion to serve as foils during testing.

The stimuli were spoken triads and were presented via headphones. Following the auditory presentation of a target, there was a spoken series of digits presented at the rate of three digits per sec. The gender of the speaker of the digits was different from the gender of the speaker of the triads. For half of the trials, the triads were articulated in a female voice, and for the other half of the trials, the triads were presented in a male voice. The order in which the triads were presented to participants was randomized so that no participant received the triads in the same order, i.e. each triad-shadow-test presentation was randomized across all subjects.

The subjects were instructed to verbally shadow the digits that followed the memory target. After the digit shadowing, the subjects were tested for their knowledge of the target for that trial. The presentation of the testing information was visually displayed on a microcomputer screen. The sound files and testing protocols were all handled by a program written in E-prime 2.0. The testing procedure was the same as described in the previous two experiments, i.e. an initial yes/no test followed by a three-point confidence and, for target-present tests, the subject was given the contingent forced-choice task as described previously.

The biphasic profile for the implicit storage measure is particularly difficult to reconcile with the hypothesis that explicit and implicit memory are separate systems. If an item is not in the hypothesized separate implicit memory system, then why should it be in that system after a brief period of shadowing digits? Proponents of a separate memory system have hypothesized that items in the presumed separate implicit system can be spared from the loss caused by retention interval; whereas explicit memory undergoes a loss with retention interval ( Parkin, 1993; Weiskrantz, 1987, 1989 ). But it is another matter to find that the rate of implicit storage increases with increasing retention interval. Thus the inverted-U retention function for the implicit memory is inconsistent with the separate memory systems view.

It is more plausible to describe the loss of information from an explicit storage state to one of the other possible states, i.e. implicit memory, fractional memory, or non-storage. However, these three other alternatives to explicit storage should not behave in the same fashion in terms of the retention interval variable. For example, once an item degrades to the state of non-storage, then it is not expected that there will be an improvement with even longer retention intervals. However, information in the implicit storage state might degrade further to be in either the fractional state or the non-storage state, but it is also possible that a longer retention interval might also cause additional items to degrade from an explicit state to the implicit state. Thus, the rate of implicit storage can increase with longer retention intervals. From this perspective, implicit storage is “fed” by the loss from explicit memory, but items that are stored implicitly can also undergo further degradation to become either fractionally stored or lost altogether. Consequently, the peaking of the implicit storage rate with an intermediate retention interval is a reasonable finding.

Heretofore, researchers have used tasks that were thought to be sensitive to implicit memory as a way to study implicit memory across different experimental conditions. However, the case is made here that experimental tasks generally tap more than one cognitive process. Without a psychometric model to disentangle the latent processes, researchers cannot unambiguously identify condition differences in terms of the latent cognitive memory components that changed between conditions. For example, if a person is able to guess above chance, then that is not sufficient reason for concluding that there is an implicit memory. The improvement over chance could also be caused by some explicit memory or fractional storage. However, if a person can guess above chance and a model like the IES model is used to correct for the amount of explicit storage, fractional storage, and the non-storage guessing rate, then the remainder does qualify as implicit storage. Tasks, such as priming, fragment completion, or relearning savings, are all potentially sensitive to explicit, implicit, and fractional memories, so none of these tasks should be considered as a pure measure for any one of these memory types.

With explicit storage the subject can recall the target event, if the subject can gain access to the memory encoding. The subject is also correct (with high confidence) on a yes/no lineup recognition test and is successful in selecting the target on a 4 AFC test. However, fractional storage is different than explicit memory because it is characterized as a level of knowledge where only some of the items on a forced-choice test can be eliminated. With only fractional storage the subject is at risk of making errors on both the yes/no test and the 4 AFC test. With fractional storage only one or two foils can be rejected; whereas with non-storage, the subject is at a pure chance level on a forced-choice task. In contradistinction to these three memory states, implicit memory is conceptualized here as a memory residue that is complete enough to guide correct forced-choice recognition, but it is not vivid enough or strong enough to support confident and correct yes/no recognition on a memory lineup test. All the present experiments showed that there is a nonzero value for this memory residue. Even after estimates are made for explicit storage, fractional storage, and non-storage, there remains something else that we identify as implicit memory. Previously reported studies on implicit memory in the psychological literature did not provide corrections for explicit memory, fractional memory, and non-storage, so the case for the existence of implicit memory was debatable from those studies. However, the experiments examined here with the IES model strengthen the support for the concept of implicit memory because all the necessary corrections have been implemented.

The IES model has some similarity and important differences with other theories of recognition memory. One comparison that needs to be discussed is with the dual-process model ( Yonelinas, 1994 ). While the  Yonelinas (1994)  model has attracted the most attention in the memory literature, there actually are a number of different models that identified two processes in recognition memory (e.g.  Atkinson & Juola, 1974 ;  Chechile, 2004 ;  Chechile & Meyer, 1976 ;  DeCarlo, 2002 ;  Jacoby et al., 1993  and  Mandler, 1980 ). 
                         8 
                      
                      
                         8 
                         Although all of the cited papers delineate two important processes involved in recognition memory, there are important conceptual differences among the models. For example, the  Atkinson and Juola (1974)  model did not stipulate that there is a stochastic mixture for the processes, but rather used two thresholds on the usual signal-detection framework of two overlapping, continuous distributions. The other papers are mixture models for only the target-present trials; whereas the  Chechile (2004)  and the  Chechile and Meyer (1976)  models have mixtures for both target-present and target-absent tests. Also  Atkinson and Juola (1974)  and  Mandler (1980)  hypothesize an initial familiarity process followed by a more deliberative secondary process; whereas, the other models hypothesize a recollective or knowledge-based process as the initial process for recognition. 
                       However, for most contemporary memory researchers, the term “the dual-process model” is most likely to be thought of as the  Yonelinas (1994)  model. Consequently, the discussion here of the IES model and the dual-process model is limited to a comparison with the  Yonelinas (1994)  version of the dual-process model.

For the  Yonelinas (1994)  dual-process model, recognition memory for target-present trials is conceptualized as involving an initial recollective process that has some probability of success. In the case of recollection, there is an immediate yes response. However, if this initial process fails, then there is a secondary familiarity process that is modeled by a signal-detection strength-axis framework. Like signal-detection theory, it is assumed that target and foil distributions are overlapping, continuous distributions. Clearly the initial recollective process for target-present tests is thought of as a conscious memory, and as such it is similar to the explicit storage measure in the IES model. But in the dual-process model, there is an undifferentiated state for the targets that are not explicitly stored. Hence, the dual-process model only has two states for the representation of target information. If one stipulates that this other state, based on familiarity processes, is implicit memory, then the dual-process model would only have two states for information, i.e. explicit and implicit. In contrast, the IES model postulates that there are four states for representing target information. The dual-process model has largely been discussed as a way to account for properties of the receiver operator characteristic (ROC) curve. An important comparison of the dual-process model is the standard signal detection model for recognition memory. However, the IES model is advanced as a memory measurement model. From this perspective, it is more important to measure latent psychological processes over which there is a stochastic mixture than it is to deal with the profile of the hit rate versus the false alarm rate. Consequently, a MPT representation is used for representing this mixture. In general MPT models can provide a creditable account for the ROC curve, depending on how the secondary guessing and rating processes are modeled. However, these processes are viewed here as nuisance processes. The primary focus is on the measurement of the important unconditional, first-branch processes in the MPT model.

There are also important similarities and differences between the IES model and the process-dissociation model ( Buchner et al., 1995; Jacoby, 1991 ). The process-dissociation model provides a three part decomposition of information, i.e. target information is either consciously stored (explicit memory), unconsciously stored (implicit memory), or in a null state. However, with the IES model, information that is neither explicit nor implicit can be in two different states, i.e. fractional storage and non-storage. One feature of the IES experimental task is not required. It is possible to obtain a tractable and identified model with a different number on the initial yes/no recognition lineup test. It is only required that the number on the lineup must be greater than two. For example, a tractable model can be achieved with three items on the lineup test. The contingent forced-choice test would, in that case, begin with a 3 AFC task. This altered task results in one less parameter (the

                               f 
                               2 
                            
                         
                       parameter), and the data structure has fewer degrees of freedom across the response categories. With only three alternatives, it is not possible to distinguish between the two types of fractional storage, i.e. the case when partial knowledge eliminates one alternative, and the case when it can eliminate two alternatives. Yet the resulting MPT model is still identifiable and has well defined ML and PPM estimates for the parameters. It is also possible to have a more complex version based on a five-alternative lineup. The fixed number of alternatives on the recognition task can in principle be any number equal to three or more. Yet with more than four alternatives there is an increased number of foils needed, and it lengthens the time to test a subject with the contingent forced-choice procedure. Consequently, practical considerations may effectively limit the number to the case of either three or four alternatives.

It is should be pointed out that the separation of explicit and implicit memory measured by the IES model is based on the data from a specific experimental task. Neither the task nor the model require making the assumption that implicit memory is an unconscious memory. According to the IES model, if the target is explicitly stored, then the person will give a confident correct yes decision when asked if a target is in the lineup list, and the person will correctly select the target out of the lineup. If the target is stored as an implicit memory representation, then the person will either be unconfident or inaccurate in their initial yes/no decision to the four-item lineup test, but when asked to select the target the person will still be able to pick the target. This difference in behavioral patterns might be linked to conscious versus unconscious processes, but there is no assumption made here about implicit memory being unconscious. A more apt characterization of an implicit memory representation is that it is a faint memory residue from prior experience that is too weak to support recall or confident correct recognition. Yet this residue is capable of influencing performance on memory and cognitive tasks.

Given the importance of implicit memory in cognitive and social psychology, the IES model developed in this paper should be a valuable tool. The experimental results already provide a challenge to several hypotheses held by some researchers. First, the finding of a four-way dissociation of measures is a clear demonstration that it is imprudent to assume that any task is a pure test of a single memory process. A mixture of item types should be assumed, and a MPT model used to measure the rates for the various memory components. Second, the findings from the retention interval experiment are problematic for the hypothesis that implicit memory resides in a separate memory system or brain region. If implicit memory were in a separate brain region or in a separate memory system, then the proportion of targets in that hypothesized system should not have an inverted-U shaped function in terms of the retention interval. Surely other important effects remain to be discovered with the use of the IES model.

The above hybrid method is a novel procedure for obtaining the MLE values. For many other MPT applications, software like that described in  Hu and Phillips (1999)  has been used to obtain MLE values. This software is based on the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm developed by  Dempster, Laird, and Rubin (1977) . In order to see the accuracy of the hybrid method, both the hybrid method and the EM algorithm for the IES model were studied in a test case with perfect data that are 100% consistent with a known MLE solution. When implementing the EM algorithm to MPT models, each tree pathway is treated a having a separate frequency value, but some of the pathways have missing data that are augmented in the expectation or E step. The maximization or M step of the algorithm yields an estimate of the likelihood based on the augmented data structure. The MLE solution is taken as the convergent values that result after many cycles of the E and M steps. For the IES model there are a total of 45 pathways, but 38 of these pathways have missing frequencies that require estimation in the E step. The initial estimation of the augmented data structure was based on the PPM parameter estimates. The model fits for the hybrid approach and the EM approach are shown in  Table 7 
                      . Neither the hybrid method nor the EM algorithm found the true solution exactly. In the case of the hybrid approach, the error in finding the true MLE is understood to be due to the limitation of considering only a finite number of values when searching a six-parameter space. In the case of the EM algorithm, the error is due to a limitation with the EM algorithm when there is a large degree of data augmentation required (such as in this application). In such cases the EM algorithm is sensitive to the initial estimates, and it might overshoot a local maximum and stabilize on a non-optional solution ( Arslan, Constable, & Kent, 1993; Vaida, 2005 ). Nonetheless, the hybrid and EM solutions are very close, and as a practical matter the error is small. In this example, the hybrid solution is exactly correct for all the unconditional first branch probabilities of the IES model, but it exhibits some small error on eight of the nine nuisance parameters. The EM solution does better overall by being better with the nuisance parameters, but there is a small error of 0.001 for four of the five first branch probabilities. Overall the close correspondence between the two solutions illustrates that the hybrid model can be effective in finding a maximum (approximately) of the likelihood function.
