When we watch moving objects, normally their shape does not appear to change. Our general impression is that the shape and size of objects remain constant: for example, we do not see people grow or shrink as they walk towards us or away from us. The notion that vision provides us with a stable percept of the environment has led many researchers to believe that there exists also some stable internal representation often denoted visual space. However, visual estimates of equidistance, collinearity and parallelity have been shown to deviate considerably from physically correct. For instance, it was shown that when two rows of faint luminous lights extending depthwise appear to lie along two parallel lines (parallel-alleys), the actual positions are located along physically curved lines ( Blumenfeld, 1913 ;  Hillebrand, 1902 ). Furthermore,  Blumenfeld (1913)  found that these apparently parallel rows of lights do not appear to be equidistant; to be perceived as equidistant they must be arranged on differently curved lines, the so-called distance-alleys.  Luneburg (1947)  noted that in curved geometries parallel lines are no longer equidistant and that shape constancy is preserved only in Riemannian spaces of constant curvature. Together with the observation that distance-alleys lie outside the parallel-alleys, this led Luneburg to propose that visual space is a Riemannian space of constant negative curvature. Many investigators have tried to establish the curvature based on this model (see  Indow (1991)  for an overview of this literature), but the results have been ambiguous. Moreover, direct tests of the constant curvature assumption revealed that this assumption generally does not hold (e.g.  Foley, 1963, 1972 ;  Higashiyama, 1984 ;  Koenderink, Van Doorn, & Lappin, 2000 ). Experiments conducted out of doors showed that, in these circumstances, visual space is described more accurately with a Euclidean geometry and a simple distance relation ( Battro, Pierro Netto, & Rozestraten, 1976 ;  Battro, Reggini, & Karts, 1978 ;  Wagner, 1985 ). So the following question remains: what is the geometry of visual space and is it metric?

In a previous study ( Cuijpers, Kappers, & Koenderink, 2000b ) we examined the structure of visual space with a parallelity task. In that experiment subjects were asked to adjust the orientation of a test bar until it appeared parallel to a reference bar. The bars were placed horizontally at eye height and rotated about the vertical axis through their centres. The locations of the bars and the orientation of the reference bar were varied systematically. The deviations from parallel were found to increase linearly with the separation angle between the bars. The deviations ranged from 10° to 40° for the maximum separation angle of 60° depending on the subject and were independent of the distance of the bars from the subject. Note that, despite these large errors, subjects were unaware of them and were convinced that the bars had approximately the same physical orientation in space. The size of the deviations was independent of the orientation of the reference bar except when the reference bar was aligned with or perpendicular to the orientation of the main axis of the experimental room; for these orientations the deviations were much smaller or even zero depending on the subject.  Cuijpers, Kappers, and Koenderink (2001)  showed that this ‘oblique effect’ was caused by external references. They found evidence that without external references the deviations would not vanish and would be completely independent of the orientation of the reference bar.

Very similar results were obtained for the haptic modality by  Kappers (1999)  and  Kappers and Koenderink (1999) . In these studies subjects were blindfolded and they adjusted the orientation of a test bar manually until it  felt  parallel to a reference bar. Both bars were located in a horizontal plane at waist height. The results are surprisingly similar: the deviations increase linearly with the lateral distance between the bars. The deviations ranged from 8° to 90.5° for the maximum lateral distance of  120 
                         cm 
                       depending on the subject (see  Kappers, 2002 ). Note that subjects were unaware of these enormous errors and felt disbelief when their settings were shown to them afterwards. A quick attempt to improve their performance revealed the same errors, however. For non-oblique orientations of the reference the deviations are smaller for most subjects; for subjects with very large deviations a reversed oblique effect was observed ( Kappers, 2002 ). A change of the distance in depth of the bars from the subject had no effect on the deviations.

The parallelity tasks were designed bearing in mind that the haptic and visual perceptual spaces may not be metrical spaces. Parallelism is a non-metrical property that one can define without the need of a concept of length. This yields the so-called  affine  spaces. It appears that the structure of visual space is affine ( Todd, Oomes, Koenderink, & Kappers, 2001 ). In differential geometry, the approach is rather different because all the structure is defined locally and needs to be integrated to obtain the global structure. As a consequence defining parallelism locally does not imply the existence of globally parallel lines. The simplest example is the surface of a sphere: all meridians intersect the equator at right angles but they intersect each other at the poles, so they are locally parallel at the equator but not globally parallel. Local parallelism is defined by means of the so-called  affine connection  (the word  affine  here has no relation to the aforementioned term). The main goal of this paper is to demonstrate how the results from the parallelity tasks can be used to establish the affine connection of the corresponding perceptual spaces.

The use of differential geometric constructs is not new in the literature. To begin with,  Luneburg (1947)  postulated that visual space is a Riemannian space of constant curvature. Also, the much weaker assumption of affinely connected spaces has been proposed before. For example,  Shimbo, Yamanoi, and Kawaguchi (1978)  and  Yamazaki (1987)  showed how the affine connection can be determined from the classical alley experiments and developed a (non-Riemannian) model for visual space.  Levin (2000)  extended the application of affinely connected spaces to any continuous stimulus space. In this study, the affine connection is determined experimentally from perceptually equivalent transformations at various stimulus levels: for example, an equivalent rise in pitch at different levels of loudness and pitch. These transformations are needed to obtain estimates of parallel “vectors”. However, in our parallelity tasks, the parallel vectors are obtained directly by identifying the orientation of the bars with vectors of unknown length. This is a novel method for obtaining the affine connection and we will explain it in detail.

There are several other mathematical models that, in principle, could be applied to our data. Recently,  Dzhafarov and Colonius (1999)  proposed a technique to establish Finsler metrics for continuous stimulus spaces. This technique requires psychometric functions describing the discrimination probabilities among stimuli. Although it is an elegant extension to Fechner's original empirical theory, it presupposes the existence of a metric which, in our case, is undesirable. Furthermore, we would need a lot more data to determine the required psychometric functions. Indeed, the experiments would become rather cumbersome, if we did try to measure them. Another approach is the use of Lie transformation groups ( Hoffman, 1966 ;  Dodwell, 1983 ). The affine connection defines a Lie transformation group so these methods are related, but the approach is rather different and we will not pursue it any further.

If the affine connections of haptic and visual space are sufficiently well behaved, one can derive the corresponding Riemannian metrics. These metrics impose a unique relation between concepts such as distance, straightness and parallelity. As a consequence, one can predict the frontoparallel plane horopters, parallel- and distance-alleys and egocentric distance relations. In this study, we will determine the affine connections and derive the metrics for the haptic and visual modalities. We will restrict ourselves to the case where the deviations are independent of the reference orientation, and thus will not take the oblique effect into account. Despite this restriction, our models are still relevant, at least for the visual modality, because many visual experiments described in the literature were conducted in environments without external references. Consequently, the predictions derived from our model can be compared directly to the results reported in the literature.

The assumption that visual space is a metrical space is valid if and only if some sort of distance relation can be defined.  Blank (1958)  proved that a sense of length ordering (i.e. which distance interval is longer) and a sense of alignment (i.e. are three separate points collinear) are, in principle, sufficient for the existence of such a distance relation and, thus, a metric. The assumption that visual space is Riemannian is even more restrictive ( Stoker, 1969 ). In a Riemannian space all geometric concepts such as parallelism and straightness follow from its metric. However, these concepts do not require the existence of a metric: the so-called affinely connected spaces are equipped with concepts of parallelism and straightness without presupposing the existence of a metric. Note that every Riemannian space is also an affinely connected space, but that the reverse is not true. The simplest example of a non-Euclidean space that is affinely connected and Riemannian is the surface of a sphere. We will use the spherical geometry as an example for introducing the basic equations of differential geometry ( Stoker, 1969 ).

Suppose a ship is sailing the seas without deviating either left or right. In that case it will trace out a great circle (provided the earth is spherical). So a great circle is the equivalent of a straight line in spherical geometry. However, great circles always intersect each other in two anti-podal points, so straight lines cannot be parallel globally. Clearly, careful definitions of straightness and parallelity are needed in curved spaces. This can be done by making use of the parallel-transport of a vector along a curve. Classically, the parallel-alleys were constructed by arranging pairs of dim lights in the dark in a horizontal plane at eye height until they appeared to form two parallel and straight lines symmetric about the median plane ( Blumenfeld, 1913 ;  Hardy, Rand, Rittler, Blank, & Boeder, 1953 ;  Indow, Inoue, & Matsushima, 1962 ;  Zajaczkowska, 1956b ). The most distant pair of stimuli was always fixed and the other pairs were added successively at increasingly closer distances from the observer. The separation between the nearer pair was adjusted while all previously adjusted pairs remained visible. Subjects were often allowed to readjust their settings after completing the alley. Typically, the heads of the subjects were fixed and free eye movements were allowed. Examples of the obtained parallel-alleys are shown in  Fig. 4 .  Instead of lights in the dark,  Hillebrand (1902)  used black threads against a white background in dim room lighting conditions and for various fixation strategies. The egocentric distance  d ( r ) given in  Eq. (27)  is independent of the direction. As a result, points which appear equidistant from the observer lie on circles centred on the observer. It seems logical that if an internal representation of the world exists, haptically and visually perceived structures should eventually lead to the same representation. Clearly, this is not the case: for example, lines which appear haptically parallel do not appear visually parallel, etc. (cf.  Figs. 3 and 6 ). On the other hand, the intrinsic structures of both visual and haptic space are flat. This leads to the possibility that the visual space and haptic space can be matched without stretching or bending. In that case a single (internal) representation is possible but with the distinction that the corresponding physical structures depend on the type of perception. Visual and haptic space could be identified as follows: 
                         
                            (37) 
                            u=aξ−bη+c,v=bξ+aη+d, 
                         
                      with  a , b , c  and  d  arbitrary constants corresponding to an arbitrary scaling, rotation and translation. Substituting  Eqs. (34) and (25)  yields a mapping from physical to physical space such that the visual appearance of one structure is mapped to another structure with the same haptic appearance.

The affine connections of visual space and haptic space have been determined from the data of the parallelity experiments. Hereby, it was assumed that the visually and the haptically perceived spaces are torsion-free. Subsequently, the Riemannian metrics have been derived from the affine connection. Neither model incorporates the experimental observation that the measured deviations are often significantly smaller for non-oblique orientations of the reference bar. In the case of the visual experiments, this oblique effect was due to external references. Thus, the current model for visual space is restricted to environments without external references. This might also be true for the haptic model. Apart from the cases where the deviations are significantly smaller due to the oblique effect, both models provide a good description of the experimental data.

Luneburg (1947)  proposed that visual space is a Riemannian space of constant negative curvature and based his model on the observation that the parallel-alleys lie inside the distance-alleys. On the basis of the parallelity task we find a zero curvature for visual space and, as a consequence, the parallel-alleys and distance-alleys should coincide. However, the measured differences between parallel-alleys and distance-alleys are often very small for distances larger than  1.5 
                         m 
                       (see  Battro et al., 1976 ;  Hardy et al., 1951 ; Indow et al., 1962;  Indow & Watanabe, 1984 ;  Zajaczkowska, 1956a ). Moreover, the differences between the parallel- and distance-alleys could have arisen from the different methods in constructing them ( Ehrenstein, 1977 ). The various examples of the parallel-alleys reported in the literature can be fitted well by our equations with only one free parameter. The fact that our model is based directly on experimental data is a distinct improvement on Luneburg's model.

The visually perceived egocentric distance in our model is isotropic and always decelerates at a constant rate. Our model is in reasonable agreement with the experimental data obtained outdoors by  Gilinsky (1951)  and  Wagner (1985) . However, in previous studies for a pointing task ( Cuijpers, Kappers, & Koenderink, 2000a ) and a collinearity task ( Cuijpers, Kappers, & Koenderink, 2002 ), we found that the egocentric distance function should have an inflection point: namely, it should have a transition from an accelerating part in near space to a decelerating part in far space. This is in conflict with the present model. Luneburg's distance function does have an inflection point, but nonetheless it is inconsistent quantitatively. The actual locations of apparently equidistant points were measured by  Foley (1966)  and lie in between the equidistance circles predicted by our model and by Luneburg's model. Luneburg's model was modified by  Heller (1997)  to incorporate Foley's results.

The visual frontoparallel horopters predicted by our model are always concave towards the observer and their curvatures decrease with distance. The inversion from concave to convex horopters, as reported in the literature, is not predicted. However, the variability between the shapes of the horopters reported in the literature is considerable and in many cases an inversion from concave to convex is not observed (see  Shipley (1959)  for an overview).

Reports on the structure of haptic space are very sparse. An exception is a study by  Blumenfeld (1937)  who measured haptic parallel-alleys and frontoparallel horopters. The predicted horopters from our model are in surprisingly good agreement with the findings of Blumenfeld. Unfortunately, the predicted parallel-alleys are not in good agreement but the reason is unclear. On the other hand, Blumenfeld's conclusions about the haptic alleys were not very consistent with his own data. The structure of haptic space was also measured using a pointing task and a collinearity task ( Kappers & Koenderink, 1999 ). The results were consistent with the parallelity task ( Kappers & Koenderink, 1999 ;  Kappers, 1999 ) and, therefore, consistent with our haptic model.

Both haptic and visual space are distorted in relation to physical space but in a different way. So a given physical structure will have a different appearance when perceived haptically or visually. On the other hand, the intrinsic geometries of both visual and haptic space are Euclidean and both spaces can be matched without bending or stretching. Although our results apply only to the horizontal plane, it was found earlier that visual space is also Euclidean in the frontoparallel plane ( Indow & Watanabe, 1988 ;  Indow, 1991 ). If visual and haptic space are Euclidean, a mapping exists relating the visual shape of an object to an equivalent haptic shape. The corresponding physical shapes will be different but with the same appearance.
