JOURNAL OF MATHEMATICAL PSYCHOLOGY 26, 1-12 (1982)  The Bias of an Estimate of Coupled Slack in Stochastic PERT Networks  RICHARD SCHWEICKERT  Purdue University  An earlier paper presented a method for using response times to construct a PERT network representing the mental activities in  a task. A key role in this method is played by parameters called coupled slacks. The magnitudes of the coupled slacks are used  to test the validity of a PERT model and to deduce aspects of the structure of the network. In a stochastic PERT network, the  usual estimate of coupled slack based on the mean completion times can be biased. Distribution-free upper and lower bounds are  derived for the bias, and it is shown that the bias approaches zero as the probability of relatively long prolongations  increases. It is also shown that the expected value of the estimate of coupled slack between two activities is negative only  if the two activities are in a Wheatstone bridge. Finally, an estimate of the increase in completion time due to prolonging an  activity, given that the prolongation did in fact increase the completion time, is derived.  Suppose a subject executes a number of mental activities to perform a task. If the activities are partially ordered, they can  be represented in a directed acyclic network called a PERT, or critical path, network (Fig. 1). In previous papers  (Schweickert, 1978, 1980), 1 presented a method, Latent Network Theory, for using reaction times to construct a PERT network  representing the mental activities in a task, if one is applicable, or for concluding that no such network is possible. To use  this method, some of the mental activities are prolonged, as in Sternberg's (1969) additive actor method. In those papers, the simplifying assumption was made that the duration of each activity was a fixed real number, rather than a  random variable. One of the problems in analyzing stochastic PERT networks is that the natural estimate of the coupled slack  between two activities (see Eq. (5)) can be biased. The variance of the estimate is important also, but we will not discuss it  here because in principle the experimenter can decrease the variance by increasing the number of observations. Unfortunately,  the bias in the estimate of coupled slack cannot be reduced by increasing the number of observations, so other means of  reducing the bias must be sought. In this paper I will derive upper and lower bounds for the bias, and will show that the bias  approaches zero as the prolongations of activities become large. I will also show that the expected value of the estimate of  the coupled slack for activities p and q is negative only if p and q are arranged in a Wheatstone bridge (see Fig. 2).  BASIC CONCEPTS AND PREVIOUS WORK  Each arrow in a PERT network represents an activity, which has a starting point and a terminating point. None of the  activities starting at a point can begin until all of the activities terminating at that point are completed. A path from a  point u to a point v is a sequence of activities p, , p2, ..., psuch that u is the starting point of p, , v is the  terminating point of p, and the terminating point of pi is the starting point of pi,,, for i = 1,..., n - 1. The duration of  each activity pi is a random variable X; whose values are nonnegative real numbers. A value of X; will be denoted by xi. We  define the duration of a path to be the sum of the durations of the activities on it. A point preceded by no activity is  called a source, and a point followed by no activity is called a sink. In a reaction time task, the presentation of a stimulus  will usually be represented by a source, and the making of a response by a sink. We will suppose there is one source, denoted  s, and one sink, denoted r. A path of maximal duration from the source to the sink is called a critical path. The duration of  a critical path is the completion time of the task, which we will call the response time. We will sometimes call the response  time the reaction time, but to conform with common  ESTIMATE OF COUPLED SLACK  usage, we will do so only if a stimulus is presented at the source, and the response to it is made at the sink.  An approximation to the expected response time (completion time) can be obtained by associating with each activity its  expected duration, and finding a path from the source to the sink such that the sum of the expected durations of the  activities on the path is as large as possible. If this path were a critical path on every trial, this procedure would yield  the correct expected completion time. But if the activity durations vary from trial to trial, sometimes one path will be  critical, and sometimes another will be. Then the procedure just described underestimates the expected completion time  (Fulkerson, 1962), because the expected value of the maximum path duration is greater than or equal to the expected value of  the duration of any one path.  Fulkerson (1962) developed a closer, although not exact, estimate of the expected completion time. Devroye (1979) provided  distribution-free upper bounds for the mean and variance of the completion time in terms of the means and variances of the  activity durations. The problem of deriving the density function of the completion time from the density functions of the  activity durations was studied by Charnes, Cooper, and Thompson (1964), Martin (1965), Hartley and Wortham (1966), and Ringer  (1969). Most of these papers lead to solutions which must be approximated by numerical methods, because the exact expressions  are intractable. Fisher and Goldstein (in press) have produced a straightforward procedure for deriving an exact, tractable  expression for the mean and variance of the completion time, if the activity durations have general-gamma distributions  (McGill and Gibbon, 1965), or exponential distributions as a special case. The exponential case is of interest because Ashby  and Townsend (1980) have produced evidence that some mental activities have exponential distributions.  Most of these papers begin with knowledge of the arrangement of activities in the network and of the distributions of their  durations. In a reaction time experiment, this information is usually unknown, although we can obtain information empirically  about the distribution of the completion times, i.e., the response times. The problem of deriving the distribution functions  of individual serial activities from the response time distribution has been investigated by several people including Christie  and Luce (1956), who used Laplace transformations, by McGill and Gibbon (1965), who studied the general gamma distribution, by  Bloxom (1979), who used cubic splines, and by Ashby and Townsend (1980), who used Fourier transformations. Q.E.D.  The Wheatstone Bridge. Two directed graphs are homeomorphic if both can be obtained from the same directed graph by inserting  points with exactly one incoming and exactly one outgoing arrow. In other words, if two directed graphs yield the same  directed graph when all arrows in series are replaced by single arrws, the two directed graphs are homeomorphic. I have  previously shown (Schweickert, 1978) that k can only be negative if the task network contains a subnetwork homeomorphic to a  Wheatstone bridge, with p preceding q as in Fig. 2, or with the positions p and q interchanged. If p and q are not in a  Wheatstone bridge, only nonnegative values of K are possible. Fortunately, if p and q are not in a Wheatstone bridge, E[IC] >  0, as can be seen from Table 1. Therefore, although the values of K may be negative for a particular set of observations, with  large sample sizes K will not tend to be negative unless p and q are in a Wheatstone bridge. Then in the deterministic case, if p, q, and u are critical when prolonged by Ax, dy, and dz,  respectively, individually and together,  dt(dx, Ay, Az) = dt(dx, 0, 0) + k(p, q) + dt(0, dy, 0)  + k(q, u) + dt(0, 0, Az), (8)  where dt(dx, 0, 0) = t(dx, 0, 0) - t(0, 0, 0), etc. A test of the analogous equation in the stochastic case requires having an  estimate of  E[T(AX, AY, AZ) I p, q, u are critical when prolonged].  No unbiased estimate of this parameter is known to the author, but if the bounds of the theorem for the estimates of E[K(p,  q)], E[K(p, u)] and E[K(q, u)] are small a reasonable estimate is simply T(AX, AY, AZ). Estimates of the other relevant  quantities in Eq. (8) have been discussed above. Then the stochastic analog of Eq. (8) would be expected to hold.  There are many problems which remain in the synthesis of stochastic PERT networks. Better estimates of coupled slacks would be  desirable. But the theorem  12 RICHARD SCHWEICKERT  presented here shows that even in the worst case, if the arrangement of the activities in the network and the distribution of  activity durations are unknown, a network model can be tested with stochastic data, and precise estimates of the parameters  can be made.
