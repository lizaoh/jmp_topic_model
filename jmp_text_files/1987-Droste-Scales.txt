INTRODUCTION AND RESULTS  In the theory of measurement, a consistent assignment of real numbers to the elements of an empirical structure is usually  called a scale for this structure. Many of the empirical structures occurring in the real world have some kind of natural  ordering, and scales reflecting this ordering are usually termed ordered scales. Three different classes (types) of ordered  scales are most considered and in use, depending on the kind of empirical structure being measured: ratio, interval, and  ordinal scale types. In this paper, we deal exclusively with ordinal scale types, i.e., we assume that only the order of the  underlying structure is known (or relevant), and we study how two different measurement scales of such a structure can be  systematically compared. We will show that the set of all scales of such an ordered structure has, if endowed with the right  comparison criterion for scales, a very rich structure theory. For classical background information on this topic, we refer  the reader to Stevens (1946, 1951), Suppes and Zinnes (1963), Pfanzagl (1968), and Krantz, Luce, Suppes and Tversky (1971).  More recent research in this area is contained, e.g., in Roberts (1979), Narens (1981a, 1981b, 1985), and Luce and Narens  (1983, 1985).  Before proceeding, let us fix our mathematical terminology. A strict partial order (A, < ) (i.e. < is an asymmetric transitive  binary relation on A) is called a weak order (or weakly ordered set) if whenever a, b, c E A with a < b, then either a < c or  c < b. Such a structure may be envisaged as a linearly ordered set of slices of pairwise incomparable elements, and, in fact,  (strict) linear orders are the most important type of weak orders.
