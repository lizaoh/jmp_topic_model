GREENO  University of Pittsburgh, Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania 15213  A goodness-of-fit test based on the maximum likelihood criterion is derived for use in evaluating models of choice reaction  time that predict choice probabilities and means and variances of latency. Special cases of the test involving models that  predict only one or two of these statistics are considered and shown to be asymptotically identical to the traditional  goodness-of-fit tests appropriate for these special cases.  This paper is concerned with testing predictions derived from models of psychological processes. Predictions about frequencies  of response are easily tested using chi-square statistics or likelihood ratios, and these methods are widely used. However,  tests of predictions about latency are more awkward. A test of goodness of fit to a complete theoretical distribution can be  carried out using available methods, but this is impractical unless there are many observations (probably on the order of 100)  in each experimental condition, and it is impossible if the model's assumptions are not detailed enough to imply complete  descriptions of the distributions. It also may be of interest to determine whether the predictions derived from a model  regarding summary statistics such as means and variances of latencies are consistent with data, even if a model's predictions  about complete distributions are false. We will present statistics for testing a variety of hypotheses concerning the fit of a  model to observed means and variances of latency, as well as observed response frequencies. TESTING SIGNIFICANCE OF EXPERIMENTAL EFFECTS  Null hypotheses of no difference between experimental conditions in choice probabilities or properties of latency  distributions are special cases of the kinds of hypotheses considered here. The hypothesis of no difference in choice  frequency would be tested by a standard chi-square or likelihood ratio test for difference between proportions. A significance  test for experimental effects on mean latency can be obtained using Eq. (12), finding a single value of p( for all  conditions. This test avoids the need to assume equal variances among conditions. A test of the null hypothesis that  experimental conditions were equal in means and variances of latency is obtained using Eq. (11), restricted to a single value  of p( and one of a( for all the conditions, and a test of no difference in means and variances as well as choice  probabilities can be obtained by using Eq. (4).  ASSUMPTIONS OF THE TEST  The tests derived here all depend on a common set of assumptions which will now be examined. First, the assumption is made  that the latency distributions are  LATENCY AND CHOICE  normal. This assumption is approximately correct in many situations, though latency distributions are often skewed toward  longer latencies. In practical applications, of course, the obtained latency distributions can be examined for severe  departures from normality. The normality assumption determines the basic form of the likelihood function (Eq. (2)), and in  principle there is no reason why similar methods could not be worked out for other latency distributions. The robustness of  the present methods with respect to departures of the latency distribution from assumed normality is considered using Monte  Carlo methods in Example 2, and the results are encouraging. The present tests would be expected to be fairly robust, since  the sample means and variances are sufficient statistics for all the tests we have presented. Thus the tests will be valid  whenever the distributions of sample means and variances approximate those taken from the normal. A comparison between the methods derived here and an alternative test statistic was made, using data reported by  King and Anderson (1976). In their experiment subjects memorized a set of propositions and were then given test items, each  involving a verb-object pair, with the question whether that pair had appeared in a single proposition. King and Anderson  assumed that the propositions were represented in memory as networks, with concepts and propositions represented as nodes and  relations  8 MILLER AND GREENO  represented by labeled arcs. They assumed that the process of answering a question involved activation of the nodes  corresponding to the verb and object mentioned in the question, and that activation spreads from those nodes to others  connected to them. In this model, a positive response occurs when activation spreading from the verb node intersects  activation spreading from the object node. The fact that nearly identical results were obtained with the two methods is encouraging, since it involves both the question  of convergence toward equivalence of methods and the question of equivalent power of different tests. King and Anderson also noted that the model, although it could be rejected,  gave a remarkably good fit to the data. They pointed out in support of this contention that the model explained 95% of the  variance in reaction times and 69% of the variance in error rates. CONCLUSIONS  The proposed likelihood ratio approach to evaluating psychological models deserves consideration in a variety of applications.  The major virtue of the approach is that it allows simultaneous consideration of the fit of a model to three measures: mean  latency, variance of latency, and response frequency. Special cases of the test can evaluate models predicting a subset of  these measures. Preliminary Monte Carlo evaluations suggest that the test has good power, and that it is reasonably robust  with respect to violations of distributional assumptions. Further evaluation of the power and robustness of the test is  needed. In particular, investigation is needed to determine the robustness of the test when used with models not meeting the  conditions of Rao (1965).  REFERENCES  CHANDLER, J. P. Program STEPIT.
