The concept of  S-multiplicativity is related to Goodman's concept of quasi-independence and generalizes Falmagne's concept of a  multiplicative confusion matrix, where S consists of the cells lying outside the main diagonal (i7-multiplicativity). In the  present study, the notion of S-multiplicativity is applied to the analysis of visual identification performance. The confusion  matrices obtained from three identification experiments are tested for predictions of S-multiplicativity derived from the  multicomponent theory of perception. If the stimuli do not overlap, i.e., different stimuli have no features in common, then  the theory predicts a D-multiplicative confusion matrix. Alternatively, in the case of overlapping stimuli the theory predicts  S-multiplicativity with respect to a more severely restricted subset S. These predictions are confirmed by the data.  INTRODUCTION  Let (pi;) be a stochastic matrix (Feller, 1968) and suppose that pi; is interpreted as the conditional probability of some  response (numbered) j given some stimulus (numbered) i. This interpretation arises in a variety of situations in learning,  perception, choice behavior, etc. A simple model for such stochastic matrices is that the conditional probabilities can be  analyzed into a product of two functions, one depending on the stimulus, the other depending on the response. The matrix is called S-independent iff SCI and there exist two  real-valued functions a and b defined on its rows i and columns j such that the probability of the (i, j)-event is represented  by the product aibi whenever iSj. The notions of S-multiplicativity and S-independence are to be distinguished if the values  of a and b are interpreted as the marginal probabilities of the row- and column-events, which seems to be implied by  Goodman(1968, pp. 1096-1097). In that case S-independence is a special case of S-multiplicativity where u is a constant  function (Falmagne, 1972, pp. 215-216).  The concept of S-multiplicativity represents a generalization of Falmagne's (1972) concept of a multiplicative confusion  matrix. According to the latter concept the subset S includes all incorrect stimulus-response pairings, that is, all off  diagonal elements (i, j) e I; and the sequel, I shall refer to this as to D-multiplicativity.  Falmagne (1972) and Townsend (Note 2) have shown some connections between D-multiplicativity as a formal attribute of a  stochastic square matrix and several process models of perceptual identification and choice behavior, which predict  D-multiplicative confusion matrices. Among these models the all-or-none model (Townsend, 1971) is of particular interest here.  According to the all-or-none model, the presented stimulus i is either identified with probability pi or no relevant  information about the presented stimulus is available and the subject guesses some response j with probability b; ; thus the  all-or-none model predicts the confusion matrix of a perceptual identification experiment (where the response i is the unique  correct response to the stimulus i) by  pii = pi + (1 - pi) b? , i = jr  _ (1 -pi) b? , i =A j,  and ~ 1 bi = 1. Clearly, Eq. (1) implies D-multiplicativity. However, later on it will be shown that confusion matrices  obtained from perceptual identification experiments need not be D-multiplicative and, correspondingly, the all-or-none model  is not generally an adequate model of perceptual identification.  In the present study, the confusion matrices obtained from three visual identification experiments (Wandmacher, Note 3;  Keeley, Doherty, & Bachman, 1970; Rumelhart, Note 1) will be tested for D-multiplicativity. This property will be shown to be  supported in some cases, but rejected in others. At the same time, the aim of this paper will be directed to provide an  explanation for this state of affairs. More precisely, it will be shown that the multicomponent theory of perception (MCTP)  developed by Rumelhart (Note 1) may be applied successfully, such as to predict in which experimental situations  D-multiplicativity holds and in which ones it is doomed to failure. Actually, for some sets of stimuli MCTP reduces to the  all-or-none model ,i.e., it predicts D-multiplicativity. For other sets of stimuli, however, the theory demands that  multiplicativity only holds for a more severely restricted subset SCI. This clearly points to the importance of  S-multiplicativity for theories of visual identification. I shall begin with a brief exposition of some consequences of the  definition of S-multiplicativity given above. Following the presentation of the actual data, the connections claimed to hold  betweenS-multiplicativity and MCTP will be worked out in detail. DISCUSSION AND RESULTS  As was indicated in the Introduction, the results will be discussed in terms of the multicomponent theory of perception  (MCTP). MCTP predicts the proportions of correct responses obtained from whole report, partial report, and backward-making  experiments (Rumelhart, 1970), the confusion matrices of Experiment III presented above, and the recognition of briefly  displayed three-letter stimuli (Rumelhart & Siple, 1974). A brief outline of MCTP follows. A stimulus (si) is represented  psychologically as a set of features (si), a feature being specific with regard to orientation and location (cf. Fig. 1). A  feature consists of components, the elementary perceptual units, which are also assumed to be specific in orientation and  location. A component is detected in an all-or-none fashion and independently of the perception of other components in the  display. A feature is perceived when at least one constituent component has been detected and, consequently, the perception of  a feature is likewise all-or-none and independent of the perception of other features in the display. The presentation of si  leaves the observer in some perceptual state F C si ; thus the set of possible perceptual states equals the power set of the  respective feature set si . A given perceptual state F is processed into a response decision as follows: A candidate set,  C(F), of possible response alternatives (r1) is collected such that C(F) _ {r; I F C st and N(s;) - N(F) < c}, where r; is the  unique correct response to s; , N(s;) and N(F) are the numbers of features in s; and F, respectively, and c is a nonnegative  criterion number. The first condition for an r; to be in C(F), F C s; , effects the exclusion of response alternatives which  are incompatible with the given perceptual state F. As to the second condition, the smaller the criterion number c, the more  responses will be excluded from C(F), and in particular, if c = 0 and if F is not equal to any s; , then C(F) = 0. FINAL REMARKS  While S-multiplicativity has been applied to confusion matrices obtained from visual identification experiments and formalized  theories of identification, the concept oS-multiplicativity may play an analogous part in other psychological domains such  as learning, social interaction and judgement behavior. For example, Smith (1973) has discussed D-multiplicativity in social  judgement behavior, yet remember that the range of S-multiplicativity is not restricted to a quadratic stochastic matrix.  Thus, whenever a psychological theory predicts an n X m stochastic matrix and the corresponding contingency table can be  observed, theoretical considerations may lead to the hypothesis of some S-multiplicativity, and this prediction can then be  tested independently of the assumptions concerning the occurrence of other events outside the respective subset S.  Furthermore, in earlier stages of theorizing an observed contingency table can be tested for multiplicativity with respect to  one or more subsets of the matrix, and the results thus obtained may help to suggest and possibly to falsify assumptions about  the psychological processes which have generated such a matrix.
