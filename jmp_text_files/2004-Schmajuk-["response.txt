Although  Blaissdel (2003)  found that there is much to commend about my book “Animal learning and cognition: a neural network approach,” he also raised some important questions. Some of the questions refer to data collected after the book was written. Other questions led us to study how the models behave. In the following paragraphs, I address those issues.

1. Does the book describe one single model or a suite of models?

The book describes several, and different, neural network models of classical conditioning, operant conditioning, timing, spatial navigation, and cognitive mapping.

Some of the individual classical conditioning models have been integrated in one large network. That was indicated in the text, in the diagrams of the networks, and in the boxes included in each chapter that briefly describe the model presented in that chapter. In Chapter 6, I present a model of classical conditioning that combines the properties of the attentional model presented in Chapter 5 with the configural model presented in Chapter 6.

2. Does associative learning reflect changes in the efficacy of synapses?

This is a common assumption in the connectionist approach. It does not exclude underlying intracellular and molecular mechanisms.

3. In the book, is extinction treated as unlearning?

Unlike the Rescorla and Wagner (1972) theory, the configural model presented in Chapter 6 does not treat extinction as unlearning. In a detailed application of the model to occasion setting ( Schmajuk, Lamoureux, & Holland, 1998 ), we showed how the model describes extinction in low- and high-salience contexts. Whereas in a low-salience context the conditioned stimulus–unconditioned stimulus (CS–US) association is mostly unlearned, that is not the case in a high-salience context. The simulations approximate very well  Jarrard and Davidson's (1991)  data and show that the CS–US association barely decreases over nonreinforced trials in the high-salience context. Instead, extinction is mediated through increasingly inhibitory hidden units—US associations. Interestingly, our account of extinction is similar to  Bouton's (1994)  suggestion that acquisition and extinction are mediated by separate, context-controlled mechanisms.  Renewal . The configural model presented in Chapter 6 explains renewal ( Bouton & Bolles, 1979 ). In the case of renewal, excitatory CS–US associations are preserved as inhibitory contextual configural associations are formed. Return to the original training context reduces the contribution of those inhibitory configural associations, resulting in the reemergence of responding.  Spontaneous recovery . The attentional-associative model described in Chapter 5 successfully describes spontaneous recovery ( Pavlov, 1927 ), by simulating the time between testing trials as time in the training or home context. The presentation of the context in the absence of the CS increases Novelty, which in turn increases attention to the CS when presented again, thereby increasing the magnitude of the conditioned response.

External desinhibition . Again the attentional-associative model successfully describes external desinhibition ( Pavlov, 1927 ). The presentation of a novel CS in the context produces also an increase in Novelty that results, as in the previous case, in an increased CR.

The combined attentional-configural model briefly described in Chapter 6 deals with the aspects of extinction mentioned above.

4. Do the models require the use of a lot of mathematics?

The models require some basic knowledge of differential equations. Although the mathematics might look complex, conceptually they are relatively simple (see below).

5. Do the models have many parameters?

Some of the models use many parameters. P. 73 (Chapter 4) shows the 19 parameters we use to simulate values an attentional model presented by  Grossberg (1975) . Two observations regarding the number of parameters. First, what is important might not be the number of parameters but the relationship between parameters and number of paradigms each model can correctly describe (see p. 304 on Evaluation of the models). Second, models based on very simple concepts (e.g., attention and association in Chapter 5) might still require complex mechanisms to implement them. For example, an adding machine implements a very simple mathematical operation that, nevertheless, requires a rather complex machinery to carry out the process.

6. By changing parameters or connections, can the models be reworked to explain almost any experimental outcome?

No. Although the models are powerful enough to explain many experimental results, they cannot explain almost any experimental outcome. Each chapter contains a list of the experimental outcomes the models cannot explain. Besides, we run our simulations without changing the initial parameters of the models. We keep doing that in all subsequent papers. So, the models explain many experimental outcomes without being “reworked.”

7. Do you believe that each distinct area within the brain is multifunctional and contributes to many processes?

Absolutely. Box 14.2 (Some general principles of brain–behavior interaction), states that 1(a) A brain regions might participate in different psychological functions, 1(b) A psychological function might be computed by several brain regions.

8. Can the models adequately account for recovery of latent inhibition, overshadowing, and blocking?

The attentional model presented in Chapter 5 successfully describes some experimental results that have been previously explained by the comparator hypothesis ( Denniston, Savastano, & Miller, 2001 ). These results include recovery from latent inhibition (shown on p. 101 in the book), recovery from blocking ( Blaisdell, Gunther, & Miller, 1999 ), recovery from overshadowing ( Matzel, Schachtman, & Miller, 1985 ), and the mutual cancellation between latent inhibition and overshadowing ( Blaisdell, Bristol, Gunther, & Miller, 1998 ). Our attentional model explains these results in terms of the increased attention derived to the CS as a consequence of the procedures that follow conditioning. This increased attention results in the recovery from latent inhibition, blocking, and overshadowing.

In sum, the models seem to satisfactorily pass Blaisdell's important challenges. Many aspects of learning and cognition seem to be correctly described by our neural nets.
