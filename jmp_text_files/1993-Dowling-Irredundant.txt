On the Irredundant Generation of Knowledge Spaces 

CORNELIA E. DoWLING* 

Technische Universitat Braunschweig 

Algorithms for the computer aided assessment of students’ knowledge frequently operate on a structure called a knowledge space. The generation of a knowledge space often requires computing the closure of a family of subsets of a given set under union; unfortunately, however, the number of unions thus generated may grow exponentially with the number of subsets. It follows that algorithmic efficiency is of paramount importance. In this paper we present an algorithm based on specific members of a family of subsets of a finite set that eliminates redundant computation, thereby obtaining optimal efficiency. This algorithm is called the irredundant algorithm. The irredundant algorithm was applied to generating knowledge spaces from two different branches of knowledge, and to determining the size of these spaces. It is shown that the size of a knowledge space is an important characteristic of the structure of knowledge in a given area. For the same field of knowledge, in practice, we may obtain different knowledge spaces. It is shown that the irredundant algorithm can be used to intergrate different knowledge spaces into a common knowledge space. This procedure is illustrated with the example of two knowledge spaces on elementary reading and writing abilities. © 1993 Academic Press, Inc. 

1. INTRODUCTION 

Algorithms for the computer aided assessment of students’ knowledge in a given field are frequently controlled by a collection of knowledge states (cf. Doignon & Falmagne, 1985; Degreef, Doignon, Ducamp, & Falmagne, 1986; Falmagne & Doignon 1988a, b). A knowledge state is defined to be a subset of items from the field of knowledge under consideration that can be answered correctly by some student. These items, for example, can be relevant examination questions, or well defined skills from the branch of knowledge. It is important to remark that, in most applications, not all possible subsets of items turn out to be knowledge states. Often the number of knowledge states is far smaller than the number of possible subsets of items. This is illustrated with an example from a continuing project for primary school pupils on the field of elementary reading and writing.

Doignon and Falmagne have suggested concentrating attention on particular sets of knowledge states. If % is a family of subsets of a finite set QO with the following two properties: 

(i) the empty set and the total set Q are members of the family #, and

(ii) the family # ts closed under union,

then the family % is called a knowledge space on Q. Assumption (i) makes sense for many applications. Considering the set Q of our introductory example, we agree immediately that there may be a pupil who possesses all skills in the set Q, and that there may be a child with none of these skills. Assumption (ii) is also plausible for applications where we can assume that for any pair of knowledge states there will be a pupil whose knowledge state corresponds to the union of these two states. For example, we can assume that some pupil with the knowledge state {g} in Example 1.1 will eventually learn the skill e and will thus advance to the knowledge state { g,e}; we can also assume that some pupil with the knowledge state {d, f, g} will learn skill {e} and arrive at knowledge state {d, e, f, g}. The set of knowledge states from Example 1.1 is a knowledge space. Investigating the relationship between knowledge spaces and certain types of expert judgments, Koppen and Doignon (1990) and Miller (1989) have given further reasons for the assumption that a family of knowledge states be closed under union. Each knowledge space can be partitioned into two subsets of knowledge states, the states which are equal to the union of their strict subsets, which are also members of the knowledge space, and the states which are not equal to the union of other members of the knowledge space. The subset of members of a knowledge space which are not equal to the union of other members of the knowledge space is called the basis of this knowledge space (cf. Doignon & Falmagne, 1985). In the diagram of Example 1.1, the members of the basis of the knowledge space are printed in bold letters. The basis of a knowledge space is the smallest subset of the knowledge space from which the knowledge space itself can be reconstructed by closing the members of the basis under union. 

We have already mentioned that a knowledge space can be obtained from the judgments of an expert; another way of determining a knowledge space has been suggested by Falmagne (1989). Koppen (1993) has developed a procedure for deriving a knowledge space from an expert’s judgments. In practical applications, however, it may happen that the questioning must be terminated after only a subset containing the basis of the knowledge space, but not the knowledge space, has been determined. 6. DISCUSSION 

The irredundant algorithm has been applied to generate knowledge spaces from their bases. The number of knowledge states in the three knowledge spaces on the 48 reading and writing abilities is sufficiently small to suggest the feasibility of using 

60

such structures for the assessment of pupils’ knowledge as they learn how to read and write. 

There is no known procedure for counting the states of a knowledge space without generating them. As far as the author is aware, the size of a knowledge space can be determined without generating its states only in special cases of knowledge spaces as, for example, in the case where the knowledge space is also closed under intersection. Since the irredundant algorithm helps counting the knowledge states, it is useful even in applications where the resulting knowledge space is too large for the assessment of knowledge. 

The number of states in a knowledge space is an important characteristic of the relationships between the items. It indicates the degree to which the items are “structured.” A knowledge space can be regarded as a structure representing prerequisite or antecedent relationships between the items. For simplicity, we only refer to the prerequisites of items even though a knowledge space is also a structure for representing antecedent relationships (cf. Falmagne et a/., 1990). 

For a knowledge space % on the set Q of items, for each ge Q, there is at least one state X € # which contains g, and which is minimal with respect to inclusion. Such a state X is called minimal for q (cf. Doignon & Falmagne, 1985). The members of a knowledge space which are minimal for some géQ constitute the basis of the knowledge space. A state which is minimal for an item g can be interpreted as a set ofprerequisites for this item. In particular, each item can be regarded as a prerequisite of itself. As an example, we recall the basis
