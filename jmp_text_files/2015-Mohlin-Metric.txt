For the case of a finite set  
                         X 
                      , a number of metrics or ‘similarity indices’ have been proposed in the literature on classification and cluster analysis ( Denœud & Guénoche, 2006 ). In particular, a number of metrics and similarity indices have been constructed on the basis of counting the pairs of elements of  
                         X 
                      , on which the two partitions agree ( Johnson, 1968; Mirkin & Cherny, 1970; Rand, 1971 ), or disagree ( Arabie & Boorman, 1973 ): Two partitions of a finite set disagree on a pair of objects if one of the partitions puts the two objects in the same cell whereas the other partition puts the two objects in different cells. In the context of decision trees,  López De Mántaras (1991)  defines a metric, which amounts to a symmetrized version of conditional information. It can in principle be applied to measurable partitions of an infinite set  
                         X 
                      . It has been used in cluster analysis and decision tree algorithms.

When  
                         X 
                       is finite and the counting measure is used, the metric proposed in this paper reduces to twice the number of “pairs of disagreement” (Section   3.1 ). When a probability measure is used and partitions are finite (and  
                         X 
                       may be finite or infinite), the proposed metric is structurally similar to the metric of López de Mántaras (Section   3.2 ).

A metric such as the one proposed in this paper has a number of applications: It can be employed in the construction of decision trees or classification trees ( Breiman, Friedman, Olshen, & Stone, 1984; Quinlan, 1986; Ripley, 1996 ). Specifically one may use the metric to evaluate, and select among, different trees, by comparing the partitions they induce, and measuring the distance between the induced partitions and the correct partition of a training or validation sample.

In game theory and economics it is frequently assumed that people share a common prior defined on a shared state space. Differences in the agents’ information can be represented by assuming that they have different partitions of the state space. The proposed metric can used be to quantify such informational differences.

                         Metrics defined on sub-sigma-fields, such as the metric of   Boylan (1971) , have been employed by   Allen (1983)  and others to measure differences between information structures. As long as information is represented by countable partitions, the metric proposed in this paper is arguably both simpler to apply and more intuitive, than the metrics for sub-sigma-fields.

In the final section of the paper (Section   4 ) the proposed metric is applied to the study of the psychology of colour categorization. The metric can be used to measure how far observed natural language colour categorizations are from theoretically predicted categorizations. It can also be employed to quantify the relative importance of universal and culturally relative forces in shaping the way colours are categorized.

In the literature on the psychology of colour categorization, there is a long-standing debate regarding the degree to which natural language colour categorizations are mainly determined by universal constraints, or mainly the product of forces that are relative to culture. The modern debate was initiated by   Berlin and Kay (1969)  who proposed that universal principles guide the structural development of colour categorizations. Their theory has been further developed e.g. in   Kay, Berlin, Maffi, and Merrifield (1997) . Relativist arguments have been put forward e.g. by   Davidoff, Davies, and Roberson (1999) , and  Roberson, Davidoff, Davies, and Shapiro (2005) .

In recent years there have been a number of attempts at evaluating the universalist predictions using quantitative and statistical methods. The metric described in this paper may contribute to this research programme in two ways. First, it can be used to quantify how far observed natural language colour categorizations are from theoretically predicted categorizations. Second, it can be used to evaluate what factors that determine whether two natural language colour categorizations will be similar or not.

In relation to the first application   Regier, Kay, and Khetarpal (2007) , building on   Jameson and D’Andrade (1997) , argue that colour categorizations employed by different cultures all share the feature that they are optimal in the sense of minimizing the sum of within-category dissimilarity and between-category similarity (in CIELAB colour space). 
                         5 
                      
                      
                         5 
                         
                            Komarova, Jameson, and Narens (2007)  provide a related model of evolution of colour categorizations. On a more abstract level,  Mohlin (2014)  studies partitions (categorizations) that are optimal in the sense that they minimize expected prediction error. 
                       They evaluate the claim by calculating this measure for the actual categorizations as well as for distorted versions of the actual categorizations, showing that the former obtains a lower score. In contrast the proposed metric allows for direct quantification of the discrepancy between actual categorizations and optimal categorizations.

Regarding the second application, differences in colour categorizations have been quantified in many ways but not in the way done here.  Kay and Regier (2003)  measure distance between elicited focal colours, or the implied centroids of the elicited extensions of categories. They ask whether the focal colours, or centroids, of different languages cluster together.  Jäger (2012)  and  Lindsey and Brown (2006)  measure the distance between the extensions of pairs of colours. This allows them to use cluster analysis, and related techniques, in order to let the data reveal commonalities in how different languages tend to cluster different colours together. They partly confirm, and partly add new insights to, earlier universalist proposals (see   Vejdemo-Johansson, Vejdemo, & Ek, 2014  for further discussion on suitable choices of metrics). Instead of measuring  distance between focal colours/centroids , or measuring  distance between extensions of colours , one may use the metric proposed in this paper to directly measure the  distance between colour categorizations .

As an illustration, we consider the original data of   Berlin and Kay (1969) . They asked 20 informants with 20 different mother tongues to give colour names to Munsell chips, as depicted in  Fig. 2 
                      . This data set has several limitations, and in order to overcome them the World Color Survey (WCS) Database was later assembled. Hence, a full-scale implementation of the methodology suggested here should focus on the WCS database. 
                         6 
                      
                      
                         6 
                         The World Color Survey (WCS) Database, described in  Cook, Kay, and Regier (2005) , contains information on the colour naming practices of native speakers of 110 different unwritten languages. Both the WCS data and the original data from   Berlin and Kay (1969)  are available online at:  http://www1.icsi.berkeley.edu/wcs/data.html . 
                       However, in this paper I focus on the smaller original data set for simplicity.

The distances between the colour categorizations in   Berlin and Kay (1969)  were calculated (by a program written in  Fortran ) for all language pairs. Since the number of named chips differed between languages/informants, the distance between two languages was only based on the set of chips that was named by both informants. These distance calculations were based on the counting measure, thus inducing the metric to coincide with the number of pairs of disagreement. However a more complete analysis may use the individual within-language variation in naming practices. Such weighting is straightforward given that the proposed metric is defined for arbitrary measures.

In order to interpret the calculated distances a number of techniques could be employed. One approach is to let the distances determine a grouping of languages. To this end a hierarchical clustering was produced (using Ward’s method, implemented in  R ). The result is illustrated in  Fig. 3 
                      .

This clustering may be used to test whether languages grouped together in ways that reflect proposed universal principles.  Table 1 
                       summarizes some relevant features. There are a few languages for which the number of chips is very limited and hence the results for these languages may be less reliable. Languages with less than 100 named chips are written in smaller text in  Table 1 . Disregarding those languages, it can be noted that languages within clusters (delineated by solid lines in  Table 1 ) tend to have the same number of colour terms. This is in line with universalistic proposals. However, it can also be noted that in those cases where languages with different numbers of terms are clustered together they tend to share a common language family (e.g. Korean and Japanese, or Cantonese and Mandarin). This is more in line with relativistic accounts. A complete analysis of these patterns is beyond the scope of this paper (and should focus on the WCS data set). Others are invited to pursue such questions. The data on distances between categorizations will be made available from the author’s webpage.

This paper has proposed a metric for countable and measurable partitions, and some of its theoretical properties have been investigated. It is hoped that it will prove useful in machine learning, in particular decision trees, or in the psychology of categorization.
