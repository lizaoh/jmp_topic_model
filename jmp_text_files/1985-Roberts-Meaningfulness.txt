INTRODUCTION  Among the most useful concepts in the modern theory of measurement is that of meaningfulness. In this survey paper, we briefly  describe the formal concept of meaningfulness, and then give a variety of practical applications. The applications we present  deal with average performance, importance ratings, statistical tests, indices of consumer confidence, psychophysical scaling,  block modeling of social structure, structural modeling in decision making, and the analysis of order and matching experiments  when matching is not transitive.  Suppose a subject is asked to rate alternatives on a 3-point scale, with 3 meaning favorable, 2 meaning neutral, and 1 meaning  unfavorable. No one would want to assert that if alternative x receives a rating of 3 and alternative y a rating of 1, then x  is viewed 3 times as favorably as y. This seems meaningless. Similarly, in a survey of the value of alternatives, suppose 100  alternatives are arbitrarily assigned identifying numbers from 1 to 100, and alternatives x, y, and z receive the numbers 10,  11, and 79, respectively. No one would want to assert that x and y are closer or more similar than x and z. This too seems  meaningless.  We shall be interested in the meaningfulness of assertions which use numerical scales. Let us first recall what we mean by a  numerical scale. We shall adopt the notation and terminology of Roberts (1979a) here. What makes the statements about 3-point scales and about identifying numbers meaningless is that they are dependent on the  specific numerical scales chosen. Their truth or falsity can change if the numerical scales used are replaced by other equally  acceptable ones. For instance, if we used a 3-point scale with numbers 1, 3, and 5-this certainly gives an equivalent  scale-then the assertion that alternative x is viewed three times as favorably as alternative y changes from true to false.  Similarly, in the case of identifying numbers, we could easily relabel the alternatives so that x gets 88, y gets 3, and z  gets 89. Then the assertion that x and y are closer than x and z changes from true to false.  In general, we shall say that a statement involving numerical scales is meaningful if its truth value is unchanged when every  scale is replaced by another acceptable one. This rather vague definition has its roots in the work of Suppes (1959) and  Suppes and Zinnes (1963). The definition is closely related to the concept of invariance which has played an important role in  classical geometry (cf. the Erlanger Program of Felix Klein; see Falmagne and Narens, 1983, for a discussion). The defnition  is also closely related to the notion of invariance which is called the "principle of theory construction" in a very  influential paper by Luce (1959). See the follow-up papers by Rozeboom (1962a, 1962b), Luce (1962, 1964), and Osborne (1970).  To illustrate the definition, we note that it is meaningful to assert that I weigh more than the elephant in the zoo. This is  meaningful because it is false under all acceptable scales of weight. Meaningfulness is not the same as truth. On the other  hand, it is meaningless to assert that the temperature of this room is twice the temperature outside. For this might be true  under one scale of temperature, e.g., Fahrenheit, while false under another scale, e.g., Centigrade. Yet, it is meaningful to  assert that I weigh twice as much as the elephant. For if this statement is true in pounds, it is also true in grams,  kilograms, etc.  As Falmagne and Narens (1983) point out, the general definition of meaningfulness we have given is imprecise, and subject to  possible misinterpretation  THEORY OF MEANINGFULNESS 313  unless care is taken in its application. Moreover, it is not even clear what is meant by a "statement involving numerical  scales." This imprecision has led to a variety of alternative formulations of the definition, all in the same spirit, by such  authors as Adams, Fagot, and Robinson (1965), Falmagne and Narens (1983), Luce (1978), Narens (1981a), Pfanzagl (1968),  Roberts (1979a, 1980), Roberts and Franke (1976), and Robinson (1963). We discuss the alternative formulation by Falmagne and  Narens below.  A variety of authors have tried to make the notion of meaningfulness more precise by speaking of admissible transformations of  scale. STATISTICS  The analysis in the previous two sections suggests that certain descriptive statistics, e.g., mode, median, arithmetic or  geometric mean, are appropriate only for certain measurement situations. This idea was originally put forth by Stevens (1946,  1951), and was widely popularized in statistics for the behavioral sciences by Siegel (1956). Using the principle that  statements involving descriptive statistics should be invariant-i.e., meaningful in our sense-Stevens argued that medians are  appropriate descriptive statistics for ordinal scales, (arithmetic) means for interval scales. Over the years, Stevens' basic  ideas have also come to be applied to inferential statistics. The general principle which has been widely accepted is that it  is improper to use parametric statistics (e.g., t test, Pearson correlation, analysis of variance) unless the numbers in  question form at least an interval scale. For ordinal scales, nonparametric statistics (such as Mann-Whitney U,  Kruskal-Wallis, or Kendall's i) can be used. A more detailed discussion of the application of meaningfulness ideas to  statistical tests can be found in Pfanzagl (1968) and Adams et al., (1965).  Not everyone agrees that meaningfulness considerations should influence inferential statistical analysis. For instance,  Anderson (1961) argues that statistical tests concern numbers, and the type of scales defining these numbers are irrelevant.  For a discussion of this point, see Adams et al. (1965), Stevens (1968), and Luce (1967). For more recent work relevant to  this point, see Armstrong (1981, 1984), Gaito (1980), Knapp (1984), Spencer (1983a, 1983b) and Townsend and Ashby (1984).  5. THE POSSIBLE SCIENTIFIC LAWS  Implicit in our discussion of the previous section, and in our whole discussion so far, is the idea that scientific laws  should be meaningful. Must this be so? Certainly the scientific community has a strong preference for assertions that are  meaningful, and scientific laws are usually stated without mentioning units. A law which depends on some arbitrary choice of  scale would seem to be just an artifact of this choice. To the extent that scientific "laws" (of economics, political science,  etc.) are used in making decisions, stating a "law" which depends on the choice of units seems wrong. For if two different  sets of numbers were both legitimate as input for a decision making problem, and if these sets of numbers could lead to  different decisions, then there would be chaos.  As Krantz, Luce, Suppes, and Tversky (1971, Chap. 10) point out, at least the standard physical laws are unaffected by  systematic changes of units. They are dimensionally invariant. In the literature of science, no one has been able to explain  this precisely because no one has been able to define "law" precisely. However, Luce (1978) makes an attempt to define  dimensionally invariant laws and then shows that the dimensionally invariant laws correspond exactly to the set of meaningful  relations on a certain relational system. Also see Osborne (1978).  8. SUMMARY  We have seen that the theory of meaningfulness has a wide variety of applications. These applications can affect the way we  analyze data (for instance in using geometric means rather than arithmetic means). They can help us to understand what claims  we can make on the basis of empirical evidence (as with performance measures). They can help us avoid pitfalls (such as using  improper index numbers or correlations). They can help us to build models describing the outcomes of experiments (as in the  psychophysical situation). They can help us to analyze models (as in pulse processes). Finally, they can help us understand  the nature of scientific laws (as in dimensional invariance). The potential applications of this simple idea of meaningfulness  are only beginning to be made.  REFERENCES  ADAMS, E. W., FAGOT, R. F., & ROBINSON, R. E. (1965). A theory of appropriate statistics.
