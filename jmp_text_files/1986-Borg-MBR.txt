Schonemann's (1982, Bulletin of the Psychonomic Society, 19, 317-319; 1983, Journal of Mathematical Psychology, 27,  311-324) MBR metric is designed to account for distortion effects in distance estimates caused by upper bounds in the response  scales. To test the MBR hypothesis, given data have to be transformed first into the half-open interval [0, 1). This is  achieved by defining the greatest distance estimate as the upper bound, and then dividing all values by this bound plus some  "small constant" e, which ensures that the interval is open. How e should be picked is left open. It is shown here that very  small differences in the value chosen for e have massive effects on the fit of the MBR model. Moreover, if e -. 0, then the  fit will eventually get bad for all realistic data. 1986 Academic Press, Inc.  Schonemann (1982, 1983) and Schdnemann and Kienapple (1984) introduced a metric for bounded response scales, termed MBR  metric, and defined as  dr(a+b)/(l+ab), 0<a,b<l, (1)  for the 2-dimensional case, with intra-dimensional differences a, b. They compare this metric to the familiar Minkowski family,  dr=(ar+br)1lr, (2)  whose special cases, the city-block distance (r=1), the Euclidean distance (r=2), and the dominance distance (r -. oc) have  been the focus of much theoretical and empirical attention (e.g., Coombs, Dawes, & Tversky, 1970; Krantz & Tversky, 1975; Borg  & Leutner, 1983a, 1983b).  If distance estimates are collected for stimuli which differ with respect to two dimensions (such as rectangles, for example),  one often finds that (2) is an inappropriate model to explain them, because larger distances are consistently underestimated.  This distortion may be caused by the existence of an upper bound in the response scale, so "that subjects will have to employ  some contraction if they want to produce city-block-like numerical responses" (Schonemann, 1983, p. 316). The MBR rule is a  model for this contraction rule. If e shrinks, however, then the greatest data value is enlarged more and more. This has eventually a disastrous nonlinear  distortion effect, since all values with a distance of at least 0.1 from the upper bound stay almost put, while those closer  to the upper bound are blown up without limits. Hence, choosing very small e values will have similar effects not only for the  particular data considered above, but for all data which form at least three classes. Only for dichotomous data the choice of  e is irrelevant, since then the MBR and the Minkowski model are indistinguishable within the linear scaling context.  In any case, e remains an unspecified parameter, and since it is not irrelevant how it is specified, one may ask how this  should be done. It is conceivable to treat it as r in (2) was treated by Kruskal (1964), i.e., to set e equal to a variety of  values and then choose that e which leads to the lowest stress. However, one might argue that stress is really not the most  appropriate loss function in all cases, in particular in scaling experiments, where one usually investigates whether the MDS  configuration is some simple transform of a physical design configuration. With respect to the rectangle data, one finds that  relatively small e-values lead indeed to a reduction of the logarithmic compressions described in Borg and Leutner (1983b),  inspite of massive increments in stress. So, another fit criterion may be the fit of the MDS configuration to the predicted  structure. Yet, when e is reduced further, then this correspondence gets destroyed too.  Finally, it should be pointed out that the problem in transforming data into an open or half-open interval arises in many  other contexts too. A reviewer wrote that "in measurement problems, it is not at all uncommon to encounter situations in which  the data must be in an open interval. The logistic transformation, log[p/(1-p)], is one example. In these cases, it is common  to add a small constant at some point in the process (e.g., for the above transformation we must add a small constant if p = 0  or p = 1). The effect of adding this small constant is rarely considered."  84 BORG AND STAUFENBIEL  The logistic transformation is used, for example, in general Fechnerian scaling (see, e.g., Baird & Noma, 1978), where  dominance probabilities p(x, y) are mapped into scale value differences s(x) -s(y) via f -'[p(x, y) ], with f a strictly  monotone function over (- oo, + co ). In Thurstone's (1927) Case-V version of his "law of comparative judgment," f is the  cumulative normal distribution function. Obviously, if p(x, y) is either 0 or 1, no finite difference results. The standard  approach to handle this problem is to skip over such boundary probabilities in the scaling process (Gulliksen, 1956) rather  than change them by some small constant to 0.00..1 or 0.99..9, respectively. This approach certainly removes ambiguities, and,  as long as there are more non-boundary than boundary probabilities in each row of the probability matrix, yields differences  unique up to an additive constant (Schdnemann, 1970). However, eliminating data raises new questions and does not strike us as  a generally satisfactory answer to the problem of mapping data values into an open or half-open interval either. Thus the  problem pointed out here impinges on a methodological problem that exists in several diverse areas. It clearly is a difficulty  that deserves a more adequate solution than has been offered in the past.  REFERENCES  BAIRD, J. C., & NOMA, E. (1978). Fundamentals of scaling and psychophysics.
