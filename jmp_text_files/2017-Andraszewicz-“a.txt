Budescu and Bo (2017, henceforth BB)  point out that the discussion of the  standardized covariance  by  Andraszewicz and Rieskamp (2014, henceforth AR)  only demonstrates its properties, but does not provide a statistical context of the measure. BB show how the  standardized covariance  is related to the family of four association coefficients for metric scales, proposed in two seminal papers by  Zegers and ten Berge (1985)  and  Zegers (1986) , and it is equivalent to the  coefficient of additivity . The remaining three measures are  coefficient of proportionality ,  coefficient of identity  and  coefficient of linearity . The latter is equivalent to Pearson correlation coefficient and is used as a benchmark for the remaining measures.

BB derive mathematically how the standardized covariance measures the magnitude of the additive relationship between two lotteries. They conclude that “one can think of […] the standardized covariance as a simple measure of disparity between ranges of outcomes offered by two lotteries”. This interpretation is in line with the properties of the standardized covariance and experimental findings outlined by AR.

Whereas BB provide the statistical context for the standardized covariance, AR map the values of the standardized covariance to its cognitive interpretation. They provide examples of gambles whose outcomes are highly correlated, as measured with the Pearson correlation (i.e. have high linear dependence) but have low level of similarity as measured with the standardized covariance (i.e. have high distances between corresponding outcomes). In AR’s experiment, 20 participants were presented with a pair of gambles characterized by a high coefficient of linearity but low coefficient of additivity (a.k.a. standardized covariance) and a pair of gambles characterized by high coefficient of linearity and high coefficient of additivity. The participants were asked which of the two pairs they found more similar to each other and all participants chose the pair with the high standardized covariance. This experimental evidence shows that people perceive the similarity of two choice options in an additive rather than in a linear way.

Additionally, AR experimentally showed that people make more consistent decisions when presented with gambles characterized by high standardized covariance than when presented with gambles characterized by low standardized covariance, although both types of gambles were characterized by the same level of linear correlation. This result can make the omnipresent linear correlation inappropriate for characterizing decision situations. AR’s finding aligns with the prediction of cognitive theories of decision making, which assume that people do not evaluate choice options independently of each other but compare their respective outcomes when making decisions. Thus, the key aim of AR was to show the practical applicability of the standardized covariance and its relevance for the prediction of decision theories.

Furthermore, we think it is important to distinguish the concept of association and similarity, a distinction that is given less attention in BB,  Zegers and ten Berge (1985)  and  Zegers (1986) . AR point out the differences between these two concepts on a cognitive level and at the level of interpreting the standardized covariance (see Section 1 and Fig. 1 of AR). In short, association measures the dependence between two choice options, whereas similarity refers to “the number of features that two choice options have in common […] or to a metric distance between these features” ( Andraszewicz & Rieskamp, 2014 ). Additionally, AR bring the concept of co-riskiness, which measures the similarity of riskiness (defined as variance) of two gambles. Co-riskiness is strictly related to the fact that the standardized covariance reflects the relation between the variances of two random variables. Future investigation of the four measures proposed by  Zegers and ten Berge (1985)  should empirically test whether the three other measures also relate to the perceived co-riskiness of choice options.

In sum,  Budescu and Bo (2017)  stated that “future work should explore the merits of the various coefficients in this context”. BB’s exposition is very clear and substantially contributes to the point raised by AR that measures of association and similarity need more attention and investigation in the scientific community. Relying solely on standard measures such as linear correlation may lead to inappropriate interpretations of experimental results. The work of  Zegers and ten Berge (1985)  and  Zegers (1986)  shows how the associations between two variables can be measured beyond the standard linear correlation. AR’s work contributes to the understanding of how the statistical structure of a decision context can enrich our knowledge about how people make decisions in different decision environments.
